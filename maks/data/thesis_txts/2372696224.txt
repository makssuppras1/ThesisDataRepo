Abstract
Scheduling of employees occur in many diﬀerent areas and can be a complex and time-
consuming task to do manually. A special area of interest is the scheduling of nurses at
hospitals.
This thesis analyses the potential of introducing automatised and optimised scheduling
for nurses at an emergency department in Region Zealand. The analysis takes both the
cost and the quality of the schedule into account.
The problem is formulated for a general scheduling horizon, assuming that no excep-
tions occur within the scheduling horizon, and is solved with both direct methods and a
decomposed formulation utilising column generation.
The direct methods are able to generate high quality schedules within a reasonable exe-
cution time and with possible savings of over 40 % compared to 2014. Furthermore, the
best obtained cost-optimised solution is less than 4 % from a theoretical lower bound.

Chapter 1
Introduction
One of the main concerns in all healthcare organisations is to provide high quality services
for the patients while maintaining a low cost. In Denmark, every ninth person goes to
the hospital every year, [38]. When people stay at the hospital they deserve to get the
care that they need in a patient-centered way without wasting resources.
In 2015, Denmark spent 10.4 % of the GNP on healthcare which for other OECD coun-
tries is on average 9 %, [27]. Furthermore, Region Zealand had the highest healthcare
expenses per citizen in 2016 compared to the other regions in Denmark, [18]. In 2015, the
National Audit Department of Denmark published a report on the utilisation of personnel
in hospitals in Denmark. The report states that the staﬀand salary expenses for hospitals
are approximately 45 billion DKK annually and that such a large area in the public sector
requires a particular focus on whether the resources are used cost-consciously, [34].
This thesis focuses on nurse scheduling at an emergency department in Region Zealand
and how it can be improved by applying operations research techniques. Currently, the
schedules are generated manually by the planning nurse in the department. The schedul-
ing is a diﬃcult and time-consuming task due to the various requirements that have to be
satisﬁed. The requirements include, among others, the hospital’s demand, the contractual
requirements and the collective agreements for the nurses. Not being able to meet the
requirements for the scheduling of the nurses can cause signiﬁcant negative impact on
patient outcomes and in worst case mortality. It is a complex task to manually assess all
the requirements and obtain a high quality schedule for the nurses while lowering the cost
of the schedule. Scheduling can be done faster by fully or partially automating the task.

Furthermore, the time that would be used for scheduling can be used for other tasks.
The scope of this thesis is to analyse what the potential of introducing optimised and
automatised scheduling would be for the emergency department. The thesis deals with a
single emergency department in Region Zealand; the exact emergency department is not
revealed due to conﬁdential reasons. All data, for setting up a schedule for the nurses,
have been provided by Region Zealand. The data includes the current shift structure at
the department, data about the nurses, the requirements at the department and the cost
of schedules used by the department in 2014. The aim of the scheduling of the nurses
at the emergency department is to assign nurses to a sequence of shifts and days oﬀ
during the scheduling period of 16-weeks while minimising the cost of the schedule and
maximising the quality of schedule.
Operations research techniques will be used to generate various schedules on the basis of
the data provided by Region Zealand. This thesis is an analytical project with a focus on
understanding and analysing the requirements for a schedule such that an optimisation
model can be formulated. Furthermore, this thesis will focus on the mathematical formu-
lations, setting up and describing a number of solution methods and on analysing which
methods are appropriate for solving the problem. The focus is not on the implementation
of the models or the development of any programs or software that could be used for the
planning by Region Zealand.
Due to conﬁdential reasons, the cost of the various schedules generated will not be ex-
plicitly stated. However, the cost will be compared to the cost of the schedules in 2014.
Throughout this thesis the schedules are generated for the entire scheduling period such
that the nurses would know in advance which shifts they are working and which days
they have oﬀ. This thesis does not deal with how the schedule can be changed during the
scheduling period.
In operations research literature, the problem of assigning nurses to shifts are known as
the nurse scheduling problem (NSP) or the nurse rostering problem (NRP). The problem
at the emergency department diﬀer from most problems in the literature since it has a
longer scheduling horizon. Furthermore, the requirements for the schedule diﬀer from
other countries which most problems in other papers are based on. For the emergency
department, this thesis contributes with methods to obtain high quality schedules that
beneﬁt both the patients, the nurses, the hospital, and the department as a whole while
obtaining a large saving in the cost.
In the ﬁrst part of this thesis the problem speciﬁcations at the emergency department will
be analysed and described and a direct mathematical model will be formulated, taking
both the cost and the quality of the schedule into account. In addition, several solution
methods for solving the direct mathematical formulation will be presented and discussed.
In the second part of this thesis a decomposition method will be utilised to obtain an
alternative formulation of the problem. Furthermore, a column generation process will be

Chapter 2
Literature Review
Personnel scheduling problems arise in many diﬀerent areas and have been studied exten-
sively in the last few decades. However, personnel schedules are often very case speciﬁc
and must be speciﬁcally created and adjusted to a given problem. This also includes
nurse scheduling problems which are often too complex for general scheduling models to
be applicable. Furthermore, hospitals work around the clock unlike most other organi-
sations. However, nurse scheduling is a well-known problem within operations research,
and a wide range of models and solution methods have been developed. See [5] and [11]
for comprehensive overviews on papers on nurse scheduling. Note that there is a big dif-
ference in the legal regulations depending on diﬀerent countries which makes it diﬃcult
to directly compare diﬀerent models.
Nurse scheduling is an interesting academic problem. Most nurse scheduling problems in
the real world are NP-hard and have been considered more complex than the travelling
salesman problem, [8]. From a practical point of view, optimised and automatised nurse
scheduling is also very useful for the hospitals as it is a complex and time-consuming task
to manually generate a schedule that satisﬁes all the requirements. Furthermore, it is
desirable to lower the cost of the schedule while still maintaining a high quality.
In the majority of the literature describing nurse scheduling problems, the goal is to assign
a sequence of shifts and days oﬀto be worked during the scheduling period. Two diﬀerent
types of schedules are distinguished, cyclic schedules and non-cyclic schedules.
Cyclic schedules are schedules covering a shorter time period than the actual scheduling
==PAGE:16==
6
Literature Review
horizon.
The cyclic schedule is then repeated to cover the entire scheduling horizon.
However, the precise deﬁnition of cyclic schedules is ambiguous. In a number of papers,
for example [10], [25] and [26] cyclic schedules comprise schedules that is rotated between
the staﬀ. This means that each nurse cycles through all the diﬀerent schedules generated.
The advantage of such a cyclic schedule is that everyone goes through the same schedules
and therefore the fairness of the schedule is high. However, these schedules are not feasible
if the nurses do not have the exact same competences and working hours.
In other papers, for example [35], a cyclic schedule consists of an individual schedule for
each nurse which is not rotated among the nurses but simply repeated for each nurse. A
disadvantage of such a schedule can be the fairness of the schedule since if a nurse has
unfavourable shifts these will add up through the entire scheduling horizon. However, an
advantage of such cyclic schedules is that individuality of the nurses can be taken into
account. This form of cyclic schedules can take into account that not all nurses have the
same competences and working hours. Furthermore, individual preferences of the nurses
can also be taken into account.
Cyclic schedules require, in general, lower computational eﬀorts due to the shorter schedul-
ing period.
Non-cyclic schedules are generated for the entire scheduling period which, for longer
horizons, increases the level of complexity compared to cyclic schedules. However, non-
cyclic schedules can include time dependent constraints such as vacation. Furthermore,
the fairness of the schedule can be handled as an optimization criteria or by introducing
speciﬁc constraints ensuring a certain fairness.
The purpose of personnel scheduling is to make a plan that fulﬁls a certain demand.
Within personnel scheduling, demand refers to the number of employees needed to cover
a number of shifts. However, the speciﬁc requirements for the schedules diﬀer not only
from country to country and from hospital to hospital but also from department to depart-
ment. In 2004, Poulsen [33] set up a mixed integer programming model for three diﬀerent
departments at The State University Hospital in Copenhagen. The objective for all three
departments was to minimise the unfairness of the schedule. Since the requirements and
the meaning of a good schedule diﬀer for each of the departments, it was concluded that
the three departments cannot be described by one model without the model being too
general and therefore losing applicability.
The varying requirements for one application to another has resulted in a large range
of NSP models, and consequently, a wide range of solution approaches developed for
these models.
Even though the models diﬀer there are a number of constraints and
objective functions that recur in diﬀerent variants. The constraints are usually divided
into hard constraints and soft constraints. The hard constraints usually include demand
requirements, contractual requirements, collective agreements for nurses and requirements
for days oﬀ. The soft constraints usually include the cost of the schedule or the quality of
the schedule seen from the nurses’ perspective. The main goal is always to schedule the

7
nurses in order to meet the hard constraints while aiming at a high quality with respect to
the soft constraints. See [11] for a list of commonly occurring constraints and references
to papers where the constraints are described. The characteristics of an NSP is that it is
usually a highly constrained problem.
The objective of NSP models also diﬀer depending on the speciﬁc instance. Most instances
deal with cost and/or nurse preferences as objective functions. Typically the cost includes
salary, beneﬁts or any other extra expenses the hospital pays the nurses.
The nurse
preferences usually implies maximising the fairness of the schedule. However, the fairness
of the schedule is often not uniquely deﬁned but it often includes preferences like an equal
number of unfavourable shifts, good allocation of days oﬀ, good shift patterns and an
equal number of personal wishes fulﬁlled.
The deﬁnition of an optimal schedule depends not only on the speciﬁc instance but also on
the diﬀerent nurses and on the management for which criteria to include in the objective
function. However, taking into account all criteria for an optimal solution will result in an
unrealistic multi-criteria problem. Furthermore, some of the criteria might be conﬂicting.
Most of the literature focus on a single-objective when modelling the NSP.
2.1
Solution Methods
Since the 1960s many diﬀerent solution approaches to computerised nurse scheduling
have been published. However, many work scheduling problems are still solved entirely or
partially in a manual manner. Besides fulﬁlling the demand, the work schedules need to
include preferences and requests from the employees. The process of generating a schedule
manually is very complicated and time-consuming. The goal for hospitals is to be able to
quickly generate a high quality schedule that satisﬁes all hard constraints and as many
soft constraints as possible.
The diﬀerent solution methods varies from exact methods to a large number of heuristics.
However, the solution methods are highly dependent on the speciﬁc nurse scheduling
problem.
Mathematical programming has been used in order to ﬁnd exact solutions to NSPs. How-
ever, in 2004 Burke et al. [5] concluded that mathematical programming solutions are not
appropriate for the enormous and complex search spaces that are represented by mod-
ern nurse scheduling problems. Note that this paper is from 2004 and that MIP solvers
have been developed extensively since. In 2014, Burke and Curtois [6] concluded that a
mathematical programming approach may be able to solve some instances to optimality
extremely quickly but on other instances it may take infeasible amounts of time or use
too much memory.
==PAGE:18==
8
Literature Review
Other solution approaches include metaheuristics, artiﬁcial intelligence methods and lin-
ear and integer programming methods such as column generation and branch-and-price.
Heuristic approaches can often generate satisfactory results in a reasonable time.
In
2008, Burke et al. [7] describes a hybrid variable neighbourhood search algorithm that is
particularly eﬀective on medium and small sized instances with less than twenty nurses.
In 2004, Dowsland and Lane [15] describes a genetic algorithm for a nurse scheduling
problem. The approach use an indirect coding based on permutations of the nurses, and
a heuristic decoder that builds schedules from these permutations. The method shows
good results on instances including up to 30 nurses. Metaheuristics such as simulated
annealing, [4], and tabu search, [9], have also been used.
In 1998, Jaumard et al. [20] proposed an exact solution approach applying column gen-
eration and branch and bound. The master problem ﬁnds a conﬁguration of individual
schedules to satisfy the demand constraints. The sub-problem is formulated as a resource
constrained shortest path problem where each possible shift is represented by a node. The
model is ﬂexible to changes in the scheduling environment and can be used for a realistic
nurse scheduling problem. The method is tested for a six-week scheduling horizon where
the same network is used for the 41 permanent nurses of the unit. A fractional linear
relaxation optimal solution is obtained in less than 40 minutes. A branch and bound
approach yields a good integer solution in 16.5 hours.
A column generation approach is also described in 1998 by Mason and Smith [24] which
solved a scheduling problem with 86 nurses, 7 diﬀerent competences, 5 diﬀerent shift
types per day and a planning horizon of 28 days in less than 5 minutes. In [24] an exact
branch-and-price algorithm for solving the nurse scheduling problem is presented. The
algorithm incorporated multiple objectives and diﬀerent branching and pruning strategies
are discussed.
In 2013, Dohn and Mason [13] proposed a generic programming implementation of the
column-generation algorithm.
The sub-problem is formulated as a three-stage nested
shortest path problem with resource constraints. The sub-problem is solved by combin-
ing shifts into on-stretches, combining on-stretches and days oﬀinto work-stretches and
creating schedule-lines by sequencing work-stretches. These three stages are formulated
as resource constrained shortest path problems and are solved with dynamic programming
using label setting.
The approach, presented in [13], creates a ﬂexible modelling framework that is suited to
handle a large variety of the problems. The framework is tested on three instances. The
ﬁrst instance has 85 nurses and 5 diﬀerent shift types, the second instance has 28 nurses
and 4 diﬀerent shift types and the last instance has 40 nurses and 18 diﬀerent shift types.
All three instances have a scheduling horizon of 4 weeks. The approach captures the
features of all the instances and results in high-quality solutions within 15 minutes.
A similar approach is used by Burke and Curtois in 2014, [6]. Results are presented for

benchmark nurse rostering problems and it is shown that the branch and price method
can solve some instances very eﬀectively while other instances are restricted by time and
resources.
In the literature, the scheduling horizon is usually 2-6 weeks which is signiﬁcantly less
than the 16 week scheduling horizon for the NSP at the emergency department. Fur-
thermore, the diﬀerent solution methods proposed in the literature are based on diﬀerent
instances and, therefore, it is diﬃcult to compare the quality of the diﬀerent solution
methods and evaluate which one is appropriate for a new problem. Inserting a math-
ematical formulation of the problem in a mathematical solver such as GAMS (General
Algebraic Modelling Software) is relatively straight forward and can solve some instances
to optimality quickly.
However it is a big problem that some instances are solved quickly while others take infea-
sible amounts of time or use too much memory. Furthermore, due to the long scheduling
horizon of 16 weeks it is unlikely that an optimal solution can be generated for the en-
tire scheduling horizon within a reasonable time. However, the goal is not necessarily
to be able to generate an optimal solution for the entire scheduling horizon. Therefore,
MIP solvers might still be useful to generate, not optimal schedules, but good enough
schedules by breaking the problem down in schedules that cover shorter time periods and
then combining these schedules to cover the entire scheduling horizon, for example cyclic
schedules.
In order to analyse the full potential of introducing automatised and optimised scheduling
at the emergency department, the solution with the minimum cost for the entire schedul-
ing horizon is necessary. An approach to solve the problem to optimality would be to
decompose the problem and utilise a column generation approach. The column genera-
tion approaches described in [20], [24], [14] and [6] yield good results for various instances
and, therefore, a similar solution approach is promising.

The period, for which the schedule has to be planned, is called the scheduling period. For

no transition between two scheduling periods. This means that two scheduling periods

periods are guaranteed to be satisﬁed.
The schedule includes nurses that work either full-time or part-time. In Denmark working
full-time corresponds to working 37 hours per week. The nurses who are employed part-

the nurses’ contracts does not have to be exactly fulﬁlled every week. However, over the
scheduling period the nurses should on average work the amount of hours stated in their
contracts. Therefore, the hours can diﬀer between the weeks, as long as the average is
obtained at the end of the scheduling period, and no other regulations are violated.
The number of hours for a scheduling period of 16 weeks is for a full-time nurse
16 weeks · 37 hours/week = 592 hours
(3.1)
For nurses employed part-time the amount of working hours during the period will be
reduced proportionally. There are no restrictions on the amount of hours per week for
a full-time or a part-time nurse. This means that nurses employed part-time can work
full-time some weeks as long as the nurses work less other weeks such that the average
over the entire period is satisﬁed.
Making a schedule where every nurse has the exact amount of hours at the end of the
scheduling period can be very challenging and even infeasible. Therefore, it is allowed
for a nurse to work less than the amount of hours stated in his/her contract. However,
Region Zealand still pays a ﬁxed monthly salary even though a nurse works less than

3.7
Pay Supplements
At the emergency department a feasible schedule ensures that all shifts are covered by
nurses with the appropriate competences at all times, while still satisfying the various
other requirements presented in Section 3.2, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5 and 3.6. Subject to all of these
requirements, the overall goal for Region Zealand is to minimise the total cost of pay
supplements for the nurses. Region Zealand does not have an upper bound on the cost,
they simply wish to generate the schedule that will be the most proﬁtable. Therefore, the
pay supplements are formulated as a soft constraint. However, a schedule that is generated
by optimising solely on cost will most likely result in a schedule that is undesirable on
various other parameters deﬁning a good schedule, see Section 3.8.
The nurses’ pay supplements include a certain percentage of their hourly salary when
working evening, night or weekends. The cost for a given schedule is deﬁned by these pay
supplements. This means that optimising on the cost will not result in Region Zealand
having to dismiss any of the nurses. Rather, minimising the cost will result in the nurses
with the lowest hourly salary preferably working the evening shifts, night shifts and
weekend shifts.
The nurses can choose to either get time oﬀas compensation or get paid the supplements.
Throughout this entire thesis it is assumed that the nurses get the supplements paid.

• Working in the evening from 18.00 to 23.00 gives a pay supplement of 27 % of the

• Working at night from 23.00 to 06.00 gives a pay supplement of 32.5 % of the hourly
salary.
• Working in the weekends from Saturday at 06.00 to Sunday at 24.00 gives a pay
supplement of 42 %.

are given per hour and are calculated for each 30 minutes begun.

3.8
A Good Schedule
The schedule with the lowest cost might lack considerable quality when it comes to other
parameters. In this section it will be described what deﬁnes a good schedule with regard to
other parameters than cost. The requirements presented in this section are soft constraints
and can therefore be violated if it is proﬁtable or if it is necessary in order to satisfy some
of the hard constraints. However, a planned schedule with a slight increase in cost and
an increase in the quality might turn out to be the cheapest schedule due to various
exceptions during the scheduling horizon.
The nurses can be stressed due to too many changes in day shifts, evening shifts and nights
shifts, and therefore have many sick leaves which will result in higher salary expenses. A
cost optimal schedule might therefore be within a certain percentage of the minimum cost
of a schedule. However, the uncertain exceptions, that might occur within the scheduling
horizon, cannot be known beforehand and therefore the exact cost of the schedule cannot
be determined. It is therefore Region Zealand that has to make a decision on how much
cost they will devote in order to increase the quality of the schedule.
==PAGE:33==
3.8 A Good Schedule
23
A good schedule is deﬁned by numerous requests from the nurses, in order to ensure
their job satisfaction, as well as the hospital, in order to ensure the quality of the nurses’
work. The requests from the nurses do not include individual requests but only common
requests. The common requests will generate a schedule with an overall high quality for
the nurses. This will ensure good working conditions which will help increase the nurses’
job satisfaction. Furthermore, this can help retain the staﬀand be an attractive place of
employment for job seeking nurses.
The rest of the subsections in this section deﬁne factors that inﬂuence the quality of a
schedule for the emergency department. Of course, there will always be more factors that
could impact the quality of the schedule. Some of these factors might even be conﬂicting
when generating the schedule. However, the factors that are presented in this section are
essential requests from the emergency department.
3.8.1
Experience
One of the requirements, that the emergency department would like to have satisﬁed, is
that at least one nurse with considerable experience should be at work at any time. A
nurse with considerable experience is deﬁned as a nurse who gets a bonus for 10 years of
experience. The nurses working the shifts coordination, triage and treatment generally
have more working experience as those shifts are more advanced tasks and the nurses
have been speciﬁcally trained for those. Therefore, if all shifts are considered when trying
to satisfy this requirement, it is highly likely that at least one nurse working has 10
years of experience, and therefore implementing that as a preference might not have any
signiﬁcant eﬀect. Therefore, it is only the two basic shifts hospitalisation and emergency
room that are included. That way the focus is on maintaining experience in the tasks
that the nurses need less speciﬁc training for.
Furthermore, always having a nurse with considerable experience allocated for a shift with
only 1 demand, see Table 3.3, might not help the experience level. If this nurse is busy
the entire shift, during for example the telephone tasks, none of the other nurses will be
able to beneﬁt from the experience of that nurse.
3.8.2
Distribution of Shifts
The next requirement, that the emergency department would like to fulﬁl, is to evenly
distribute the diﬃculty level of the shifts among the nurses. As discussed in Section 3.1
it can be diﬃcult to distribute the diﬃculty well as the nurses’ competences diﬀer a lot.
Furthermore, this requirement does not seem of high importance to the nurses at the
emergency department and it will therefore not be implemented as a speciﬁc require-
ment in the model. However, it is partly included in the preference of experience, see
==PAGE:34==
24

Section 3.8.1, as more experienced nurses are requested to cover the more basic shifts as
hospitalisation and emergency room.
3.8.3
Distribution of Working Hours
As noted earlier it is assumed that no exceptions occur within the scheduling period. In
practice it is necessary to take into consideration that the nurses are not always available
because of illness, vacation and so on. Therefore, there will be a surplus of hours available
from the nurses compared to the shifts that they have to cover. This surplus of hours will
give a number of unused hours available from the nurses.
In practice there will not be an excessive amount of hours unused and therefore it will be
less applicable if some nurses work much less than stated in their contracts. It is there-
fore desirable if the surplus of hours is equally distributed among the nurses. However,
since the nurses’ working hours diﬀer from 16 hours per week to 37 hours per week, the
distribution has to be proportional to the number of hours stated in the nurses’ contracts.
A good distribution of the surplus of hours among the nurses will be an advantage if the
schedule has to be modiﬁed. For example if the schedule is extended with vacations, illness
and other exceptions occurring within the scheduling horizon it is easier to distribute shifts
among the nurses when all the nurses have hours available.
3.8.4
Distribution of Flow-coordinator Shifts
As explained in Section 3.4 the nurses, who are employed as ﬂow-coordinators, should
cover the ﬂow-coordinator shifts 50 % of a full-time position. However, the number of
ﬂow-coordinator shifts do not correspond to the number of hours available from the nurses
employed as ﬂow-coordinators. It will be desirable if the ﬂow-coordinator shifts are equally
distributed among the nurses employed as ﬂow-coordinators. The nurses, that are em-
ployed as ﬂow-coordinators, should work 50 % of a full-time position as ﬂow-coordinators
not depending on whether they are employed full-time or part time. Therefore, the ﬂow-
coordinator shifts are not distributed depending on whether the nurses are employed
full-time or part-time.
As in Section 3.8.3 it will be less applicable if some of the nurses, who are employed as ﬂow-
coordinators, are working every ﬂow-coordinator shift while some of the other nurses, who
are also employed as ﬂow-coordinators, do not work any of the ﬂow-coordinator shifts.
Furthermore, it will be easier to modify the schedule to various exceptions during the
scheduling horizon. For example if one of the nurses, employed as a ﬂow-coordinator,
has most of the ﬂow-coordinator shifts and then needs 3 weeks vacation, the schedule
would have to be modiﬁed considerably. However, if there were an equal distribution the
==PAGE:35==
3.8 A Good Schedule
25
ﬂow-coordinator shifts could easily be distributed among the rest of the nurses who are
employed as ﬂow-coordinators.
3.8.5
Changes between Shifts
One of the common requests from the nurses is the distribution of day shifts, evening
shifts and night shifts. The nurses would like to have less changes between day shifts,
evening shifts and night shifts. For this request it is assumed that the ﬂow-coordinator
shifts are included in the day shifts. The request implies that it is less preferable if a nurse
has a day shift on day 1 and then a night shift on day 2 rather than two consecutive day
shifts or two consecutive night shifts. Not only shifts between two consecutive days are
undesirable for the nurses. For example if a nurse has a night shift on a day, no shifts the
following day and then a day shift on the third day is also an undesirable shift pattern.
However, it is assumed that changing between shifts are not a disadvantage if there are
two days with no shifts in between.
3.8.6
Allocation of Rest Days
Another common request from the nurses is the allocation of rest days. As described in
Section 3.6.2 the nurses are required to have 2 rest days every 7 days. It is desirable if
these two rest days are given as a long rest day, i.e. as two consecutive rest days, rather
than two short rest days.

it takes each of the requirements at the emergency department and formulates it as a

advantage of the penalty points is that for a given schedule it is relatively straight forward
to see where the schedule lacks quality and how it can be improved. Furthermore, the
penalty points are comparable. For example if a nurse, who has 10 years of experience,
has to work a day where there is a lack of experience then z2 will decrease. However,
this might add to undesirable changes between shifts and therefore z5 will increase. In
this way it is easy to see whether a change will increase or decrease the total number of
penalty points for the quality of the schedule.
Generating a schedule by solely optimising on cost is not unique, hence there might exist
several schedules with the same cost but diﬀerent qualities. This means that given the
minimum cost it is important to optimise on the quality in order to ﬁnd the best schedule
with the minimum cost. Furthermore, if a certain quality should be maintained the cost
can be minimised within an upper bound on the penalty points for the quality. Let ¯ptot
denote a chosen upper bound on the penalty points for the quality. In order to ensure
a certain quality of the schedule, the following constraint sets an upper bound on the
penalty points.
ptot ≤¯ptot
(4.80)

model in Appendix B will generate a schedule solely optimised on cost. For the model
in Appendix C the quality of the schedule is taken into account. The cost is minimised
subject to an upper bound of the penalty points of the schedule.

Implementation of the Direct
Mathematical Formulation
The direct mathematical model from Chapter 4 will be implemented in order to solve
the NSP for the 16-week scheduling horizon at the emergency department. The model is
solved as a Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) problem. The literature does not suggest
that it is possible to solve the problem for the entire scheduling horizon, see Chapter
2. As the literature suggests other methods including breaking the problem down into
shorter periods and combining these to cover the entire scheduling horizon can be useful.
In this chapter two approaches will be described, see Section 5.1 and 5.2.
By breaking the problem down into smaller periods, the optimal solution will not nec-
essarily be found for the entire scheduling horizon. However, the goal at the emergency
department is not necessary to be able to generate an optimal schedule but rather a good
enough schedule within a reasonable execution time.
The two approaches will ﬁrst be described generally and then the detailed modiﬁcations
to the direct mathematical formulation will be presented.
5.1
Cyclic Schedules
One approach for solving the direct formulation of the NSP is to generate a cyclic schedule.
A cyclic schedule is a schedule that is repeated throughout the entire scheduling period.
==PAGE:62==
52
Implementation of the Direct Mathematical Formulation
For example for a scheduling horizon of 16 weeks, a 2-week cyclic schedule should be
repeated 8 times. Thereby, the ﬁnal solution for the 16 week period will consist of the
same 8 cyclic 2-week schedules.
In a number of articles, [10], [25] and [26] cyclic schedules comprise schedules that is
rotated between the staﬀ. However, this is not possible at the emergency department as
the nurses have diﬀerent competences and working hours. Therefore, the cyclic schedules
presented here consist of a cyclic schedule for each nurse that is repeated for the same
nurse.
In order for the cyclic schedule to satisfy the constraints for the entire scheduling horizon,
a number of changes to the direct mathematical formulation have to be made. In the
rest of this section these modiﬁcations will be presented. The sets, parameters, constants,
variables, constraints and objectives that are not mentioned in this section are not changed
and will remain the same as in Chapter 4.
In order for a recurring cyclic schedule to establish a schedule for the entire scheduling
horizon it is necessary that the number of weeks in the cyclic schedule divides the number
of weeks for the entire scheduling horizon. Recall that the scheduling horizon for the
emergency department is 16 weeks. Therefore, a cyclic schedule of either 1-, 2-, 4- or
8-weeks could be used in this case.

5.3
Pros and Cons
In this section the advantages and disadvantages of a cyclic schedule and the two iterative
methods will be described.
5.3.1
Cyclic Schedules
With the changes presented in Section 5.1 it is possible to implement the direct math-
ematical model as a cyclic schedule. However, the changes will have an impact on the
ﬁnal solution to the NSP.
An evident advantage of the schedule is that the schedule can be extended to an arbitrary
number of weeks.
In Section 3.3 it was described how it is assumed that two scheduling periods are totally
separated and that no requirements regarding days from diﬀerent scheduling periods are
guaranteed to be satisﬁed. However, this is not the case for cyclic schedules as a cyclic
schedule can be repeated for more weeks than the scheduling horizon and still satisfy the
requirements regarding diﬀerent scheduling horizons because of the circular lead for the
constraints.
Another advantage of a cyclic schedule is that since the schedule will be repeated, the
schedule for the entire scheduling horizon will result in more structure at the department.
A cyclic schedule will, therefore, be an advantage for nurses who prefer structure, routine
and a less capricious schedule.
Given a cyclic schedule, the individual schedules for the nurses can be varied between
==PAGE:70==
60
Implementation of the Direct Mathematical Formulation
some of the nurses.
At the emergency department almost all of the nurses have the
same competences and working hours as another nurse, some more than others. A cyclic
schedule could therefore be rotated among the nurses with the same competences and
working hours. However, blindly rotating the schedules might result in violation of the
resting periods. For example if a nurse has no rest days the last 5 days of a cyclic period,
and then the schedules are rotated such that the nurse gets a new schedule where there are
no rest days the ﬁrst two days of the next cyclic period. In this case the schedule becomes
infeasible since the nurse does not have at least two rest days every 7 days. In general,
the transition between two diﬀerent schedules can relatively often result in infeasibility
with respect to the constraints about resting periods.
Another advantage of a cyclic schedule, that is not rotated between the nurses, is to
use the schedule as a basis schedule. The basis schedule could then be extended with
vacation, sick leaves and other exceptions by minimising the changes with respect to the
basis schedule.
As described in Chapter 2 the execution time for a cyclic schedule is in general lower than
for a schedule covering the entire scheduling horizon. Therefore, it is more promising to
be able to experiment with diﬀerent objective functions and various other scenarios.
One of the disadvantages of a cyclic schedule is the working hours for the nurses. In the
model it was only required that the amount of hours for each nurse should be less than the
amount of hours stated in their contract for the entire scheduling period. However, for a
cyclic schedule it is required that the amount of hours cannot exceed the amount stated
in the contract for the weeks the cyclic schedule is generated for. This means that there
is less freedom to work less some weeks and more other weeks, i.e. varying the amount of
working hours over the entire scheduling horizon. A cyclic schedule therefore reduces the
ﬂexibility which the full scheduling period oﬀers. This also implies that a cyclic schedule
is not guaranteed to generate the cheapest solution for the entire scheduling horizon.
Another disadvantage of the cyclic schedule can also be found in the cyclic pattern of
the schedule. Since the schedule is repeated for each of the nurses, some of the nurses
might be stuck with unfavourable shifts or patterns leading to an unfair schedule. For
example as described in Section 5.1.5 an unfair distribution of hours adds up over the
entire scheduling horizon. Furthermore, some nurses might prefer a more varying schedule
than a cyclic schedule can oﬀer.
Recall that it is assumed that the scheduling is done for a general scheduling horizon,
i.e. no vacations, bank holidays or exceptions are occurring within the scheduling period.
However, in practice the nurses will have days and weeks oﬀ. A cyclic schedule cannot
include any planned vacation by ﬁxing a number of x-variables to 0 since this will result
in the nurses having the days oﬀfor the entire scheduling horizon. Furthermore, if bank
holidays occur, the cost for a weekday being a bank holiday can increase and make the
schedule more expensive than necessary.
==PAGE:71==
5.3 Pros and Cons
61
In practice a cyclic schedule is not directly applicable as not all the nurses will be available
throughout the entire scheduling horizon.
Therefore, the schedule would have to be
adjusted which would compromise on the cost of the schedule and the other advantages
of a cyclic schedule.
5.3.2
Iterative Methods
For the iterative methods the problem is solved for the entire scheduling horizon which
results in a number of advantages.
Compared to a cyclic schedule there is a higher degree of freedom for the distribution of
the working hours. It is possible that the nurses work more some weeks and less others.
However, for the two iterative methods presented in Section 5.2.1 and 5.2.2 there is still
an upper bound on the number of working hours in order to ensure feasibility.
Furthermore, the fairness of the schedule, generated by one of the iterative methods,
can be higher as unfavourable shifts or patterns are not repeated throughout the entire
scheduling horizon.
Another advantage, compared to solving directly for the entire scheduling horizon, is the
execution time. By breaking the problem down into smaller problems, it might be possible
to obtain a feasible schedule for the entire scheduling horizon within a reasonable time.
Another vital advantage is that time dependent constraints can be taken into account.
Planned vacations, long-term illness or leave of absence can be incorporated by ﬁxing the
x-variables to 0 in the time span that the nurse is not able to work. Furthermore, the
actual cost of a bank holiday can be taken into account by increasing the pay supplements
for speciﬁc days in the scheduling period.
The schedule generated for the entire scheduling horizon by one of the iterative methods
are actually applicable in practice as it can be modiﬁed to also work for a non-general
scheduling horizon. Of course there can occur some day to day diﬀerences due to for
example illness. However, this cannot be planned in advance.
There are also some disadvantages of the iterative methods. Firstly, the methods are not
guaranteed to solve the problem to optimality. Secondly, the models might take longer
to solve compared to a cyclic schedule since several models have to be solved throughout
the iterations.
An advantage of both the cyclic schedules and the iterative methods is that they are
relatively simple and are implementable from the direct mathematical model with small
modiﬁcations.

From the variables describing the solution, xi,s,d and yi,d, it can be diﬃcult to create a
good overview of the solution. Therefore, in order to present the results from the methods,
the cost, i.e. pay supplements, and the quality of the schedules will be reported. For a
number of tests it is not possible to generate a feasible schedule before the program runs
out of memory. These cases are reported as ”Out of memory”.
The results in this chapter are compared to manually generated schedules used at the
emergency department in 2014. The cost for the schedules from 2014 are given for the
entire year. In comparison to the cost in 2014 it will be easy to get a good overview
of the solutions and whether the cost can be reduced markedly by utilising the models
and methods presented in this thesis. Throughout this entire chapter, the costs of the
schedules are not given explicitly but rather as a percentage of the cost in 2014 due to
conﬁdentiality.

6.1.1
Exact Solution
Solving the direct model for the entire scheduling horizon is not possible. The program
runs out of memory and is not able to ﬁnd any feasible 16-week schedule for the emergency
department. This is the case both optimising on cost and quality. However, this is as
expected since other instances found in the literature are in general only solved for 2-6
weeks.
Since it is not possible to ﬁnd a solution for the entire scheduling horizon, it is necessary
to look into other approaches for obtaining a schedule. The next two sections present the
results for cyclic schedules and iterative schedules.

6.2
Discussion
In this section the results, presented in Section 6.1, will be discussed.
Recall from the problem description, Chapter 3, that the scheduling is done for a gen-
eral scheduling horizon. This means that all nurses are available throughout the entire
scheduling horizon and no holidays or other exceptions are occurring within the scheduling
period. However, all the results are presented as relative to the cost used for scheduling
at the emergency department in 2014. The scheduling in 2014 was mainly done manually.
Note that the cost for 2014 is the cost for the entire year, i.e. for example Christmas and
the summer holidays where the assumption about a general scheduling is no longer valid.
This means that the cost in 2014 includes some expensive periods which are not taken into
account in the results, presented in this chapter. However, this thesis is about analysing
the potential of introducing automatised and optimised scheduling and not necessarily
calculating a realistic saving.
Furthermore, recall from Section 3.1 that the data used for the scheduling is from 2015.
This will give an uncertainty in the comparison to the cost from 2014 as changes could
have been made to the emergency department in the meantime.
Since it was not possible to obtain a schedule for the emergency department for the full
scheduling horizon by the direct model it was necessary to look into other approaches.
Overall the cyclic schedules give good results for the emergency department. However,
the execution time is very sensitive to longer time periods or setting the quality of a
schedule.
The iterative methods have shown good results as well.
It was possible to
obtain a schedule for the full scheduling horizon both taking the cost and the quality
into account.
The schedules should not only be evaluated on the cost and quality of
the schedule but also on the realisability. Here the advantages and the disadvantages of
the schedules should be taking into account, see Section 5.3. One important diﬀerence
between the cyclic schedules and the schedules generated by the iterative methods is
the ability to take planned vacation and other planned exceptions during the scheduling
==PAGE:83==
6.2 Discussion

horizon into account.
Cyclic schedules would have to be modiﬁed either manually or
by setting up a new model in order to incorporate planned exceptions whereas in the
iterative methods the x-variables for speciﬁc days and nurses can be ﬁxed to 0 before the
schedule is generated. Therefore, the iterative methods are a preference when it comes
to the realisability of the methods.
The factors inﬂuencing the overall quality of the schedule at the emergency department
could be varied for a cyclic 2-week schedule and an iterative method ﬁxing 2 weeks at
a time. Impressive results with 0 penalty points for poor quality of the schedule was
obtained within a slight decrease in the cost. In reality such a trade-oﬀcan probably not
be obtained since not all nurses will be available in practice. Furthermore, recall that
it was only the common factors, deﬁned in Section 3.8, inﬂuencing the quality of the
schedule. Others factors such as an equal distribution of nigh shifts and weekend shifts
could also be interesting to test. In addition, no individual preferences of the nurses were
taking into account.
For both the cyclic schedules and the iterative schedules it is impressive that such good
results can be obtained for a fairly big problem with 70 nurses and with 20 diﬀerent shifts
per day. In the literature, see Chapter 2, most instances have less nurses or less shifts.
However, the instances are not directly comparable due to the varying requirements.
The execution time for generating the results varies between a few seconds to several
hours and a few are not solvable. This shows that the execution time of the methods are
not very robust to changes in the instances. This supports the statement in [6] saying that
mathematical programming may be able to solve some instances to optimality extremely
quickly while others take infeasible amounts of time or use too much memory.
Even
though it is desirable to obtain a good solution in a reasonable time, an execution time of
a few hours are acceptable if the schedule only has to be generated once every 16-weeks
which is the case at the emergency department.
In Appendix D the cost-optimised cyclic 2-week schedule is shown as an example of a
schedule for the emergency department.
The models formulated and the presented methods show good results for the NSP at
the emergency department. It would be interesting to see whether similar results could
be obtained for other instances. However, some of the constraints would likely have to
changed since they are speciﬁc for the emergency department, especially the constraints
regarding the resting periods and the quality of the schedule.
The obtained results indicate that there is a great potential for reducing the cost by intro-
ducing automatised and optimised scheduling for the nurses at the emergency department.
It has been shown that theoretically Region Zealand can save over 40 % compared to 2014
by a diﬀerent allocation of the shifts to the nurses. However, the optimal solution is not
found and it is therefore not possible to state the full potential or evaluate if the cost
can be reduced further. In order to evaluate whether the cost can be reduced further

Chapter 7
Schedule-based Formulation
and Solution Method
In this chapter a modiﬁed formulation of the NSP at the emergency department will be
presented. Recall the problem speciﬁcations described in Chapter 3. The problem will
be formulated with an objective only measuring the cost of the schedule; the quality of
the schedule will not be taking into account.
In Chapter 6 it was seen that good solutions for the NSP at the emergency department
could be obtained within a reasonable execution time. However, the optimal solution were
not found and it was not evaluated if the objective could be reduced further. In Chapter
2 a number of diﬀerent solution methods for other NSPs were described. One possibility,
that have shown good results for other instances, is to decompose the problem by Dantzig-
Wolfe decomposition and use a column generation solution process. The Dantzig-Wolfe
decomposition is appropriate for the NSP at the emergency department since it is a highly
constrained problem.
The formulation, presented in this chapter, will be based on feasible schedules for the
individual nurses working at the emergency department, and will be modelled as a set
covering problem in Section 7.2. Furthermore, an appropriate solution method utilising
column generation will be presented in Section 7.3. The schedule produced as output will
consist of schedule-lines, which is deﬁned as a sequence of shifts, rest days and days oﬀ,
worked by the nurses during the scheduling horizon.

A ﬁnal solution to the NSP contains one schedule j ∈Ji for each nurse i ∈I; this is
stated in (7.6). Constraint (7.7), (7.8) and (7.9) ensure that all the demands are met.
In [19] a single constraint for the demand is formulated. However, in Section 3.5 it was
described how the demands for the ﬂow-coordinator shifts depend on whether it is during
the weekdays or weekends, and therefore the demand constraints are separated into three
diﬀerent constraints. Recall from Section 4.2.3 that the hospitalisation shifts, D1, E1 and
N1, can be overstaﬀed while for the rest of the of shifts, the demand should be satisﬁed
exactly. However, in the schedule-based formulation, the constraints are set to greater
than or equal to. In the solution approach for the schedule-based formulation it can be
advantageous to slack the constraints. After a solution is generated, shifts can manually
be removed if over-staﬃng occurs. Lastly, the domain (7.10) ensures that a schedule-line
j ∈Ji is either chosen or not chosen for a nurse i ∈I.
Compared to the direct formulation of the problem, presented in Chapter 4, the schedule-
based formulation has more variables but fewer constraints. This is an advantage since
the direct formulation of the NSP at the emergency department is a highly constrained
problem. The objective function, (7.5), corresponds to the objective of the direct for-
mulation as stated in Section 4.3.1, (4.49), and the demand constraints, (7.7), (7.8) and
(7.9), corresponds to the constraints from Section 4.2.3.
All the constraints from Chapter 4 that are not directly speciﬁed in the schedule-based
formulation are incorporated in the feasible schedule-lines j ∈Ji for i ∈I, i.e. the main
complexity of the direct mathematical formulation is included in the schedule-lines. A
schedule-line has to satisfy the 11-hour-rule, the 24-hour-rest and the competences and
working hours of the nurse. Given all the feasible schedule-lines for every nurse i ∈I the
optimal schedule for all the nurses can be found by solving the model on schedule-based
form. However, there will be exponential many schedule-lines. In the following section
a solution method for generating the schedule-lines during the solution process will be
presented.

7.3
Column Generation Approach
In this section a column generation approach, for solving the problem in schedule-based
form, will be presented. In order to solve the problem, it is decomposed into a master
problem and a sub-problem, see Section 7.3.1, which is modelled as a resource constrained
shortest path problem and solved using a multicriteria label setting algorithm, see Section
7.3.1.1.
In general, column generation is a method for solving LP-problems with a huge number
of variables, typically appearing by reformulating the original problem through Dantzig-
Wolfe decomposition. Since only a small set of the variables will be in basis, column
generation makes it possible to add variables to the problem as needed during the LP-
solution process, without explicitly knowing the complete problem. Column generation
has shown to perform well on rostering and routing problems, [14]. The NSP schedule-
based formulation presented in Section 7.2 comprise the schedule-line sets Ji for every
i ∈I. These sets are prohibitively large to solve the schedule-based formulation directly.
In [20], [13], [23] and [6] column generation has been shown to be a suitable solution
approach since only a proportion of the variables λi,j, the schedule-lines, also denoted as
columns, are considered during the solution process.
In order to use column generation, the problem is decomposed into a master problem and
a sub-problem. The master problem is an alternative formulation compared to the direct
formulation presented in Chapter 4. The problem is formulated by schedule-lines and
combining these to satisfy the demand. In this case the master problem corresponds to
the schedule-based formulation, presented in Section 7.2, in LP-form. The sub-problem
handles the generation of feasible schedule-lines which might contribute to the ﬁnal sched-
ule, see Section 7.3.1. This decomposition is suitable for the problem at the emergency
department since the majority of the constraints from the direct formulation are speciﬁed
for each of the nurses, i.e. the majority of the constraints, the main complexity of the
direct formulation, is moved to the sub-problems.
Since the schedule-lines will be generated during the column generation solution process,
only a subset of the schedule-lines will be available at any stage during the solution process.
Let the set J ′
i contain the schedule-lines for nurse i ∈I generated at a point during the
solution process. Replacing Ji with J ′
i in the master problem gives the restricted master
problem (RMP). Since column generation is based on linear programming, the binary
constraints, (7.10), are relaxed in order to create the linear programming master problem,
also known as the relaxed master problem. Combining the restricted master problem and
the relaxed master problem yields the restricted relaxed master problem (RRMP) which
will be solved at each step of the column generation solution process.
During the column generation solution process the RRMP is solved iteratively. After
solving the RRMP, the column generation process attempts to identify new schedule-
lines, a column, to add by iteratively calling the sub-problem. Adding a schedule-line

7.3.1
Sub-problem
In this section the sub-problem will be described and modelled as a resource constrained
shortest path problem. In Section 7.3.1.1 the resource constrained shortest path problem
is solved by a multicriteria label setting algorithm.
The sub-problem, also referred to as the pricing problem, generates new columns for the
restricted master problem.
Since diﬀerent rules apply to each nurse, a separate sub-
problem exists for each of the nurses. The objective of the sub-problem for nurse i ∈I is
to ﬁnd feasible schedule-lines with negative reduced cost or to prove that no schedule-line
with negative reduced cost exists.
In [20], [6] and [13] the sub-problems are modelled as shortest path problems. In [13] the
sub-problems are modelled by combining shits into on-stretches, combining on-stretches
and days oﬀinto work-stretches and creating schedule-lines by sequencing work-stretches.
However, this approach is not directly applicable for the problem at the emergency de-
partment. Recall that there are 21 diﬀerent shifts per day. Combining these shifts would
result in a huge number of possible on-stretches. Furthermore, it would be diﬃcult to en-
sure 2 rest days every 7 days when combining on-stretches and days oﬀ. The modelling of
the sub-problem suggested in [13] are therefore not chosen for the NSP at the emergency
department.
In [20] and [6] the sub-problems are modelled as resource constrained shortest path prob-
lems and solved using a dynamic programming approach. This approach is more appli-
cable for the NSP at the emergency department since feasibility checks can be included
in the dynamic programming approach.

7.3.1.1
Solving the Resource Constrained Shortest Path Problem
In this section a dynamic programming approach to solve the resource constrained shortest
path problem will be presented. The resource constrained shortest path problem is the
problem of ﬁnding the shortest path between two vertices in a network where the traversal
of an arc consumes certain resources and the resources consumed along the path must lie
within a given interval, [1]. In this case the network corresponds to a speciﬁc case of the
general network presented in Figure 7.1, and the resource constraints are the constraints
imposed by the working hours and the rest days. The problem can be associated with
the problem of a traveller with a budget, a multidimensional vector of resources, who has
to reach a given destination as quickly as possible within the constraints imposed by his
budget, [1].
A resource constrained shortest path problem is usually solved with a label correcting
algorithm or a label setting algorithm which builds on the concept of dynamic program-
ming, [14]. A label correcting algorithm can handle negative cycles in the graph whereas
a label setting algorithm cannot, [17]. Since the graph considered in this case is a DAG, a
label setting algorithm will be used. In general, the label setting algorithm makes labels
for each vertex representing partial paths. The labels are then compared by utilising dom-
inance and unpromising labels are eliminated along with infeasible labels. The labels that
are not eliminated are extended to longer partial paths and lastly to full paths through
the graph, [14].

approach the paths are extended into all feasible directions.

Dominance
Dominance rules are established in order to reduce the solution space
by adding new constraints to it, [21]. In this case in order to eliminate non-promising
schedule-lines.

In terms of reaching the vertex faster, the working hours and rest days have to be more
promising for further extending the path.

The eﬃciency of the label setting al-
gorithm depends on the size of the graph and the number of labels generated at each
vertex.
An upper bound on the number of labels for each vertex can be derived. The maximum
working hours at the emergency department is 37 hours, i.e.
on average this yields
37 · 16 = 592 hours for the entire scheduling horizon. At the emergency department some
of the shifts are 8.25 hours. This means that the amount of hours worked by any nurse
does not have to an integer. Therefore, an upper bound on the total number of diﬀerent
working hours that is possible at the emergency department is 592 · 4 = 2.368. Let this
bound be denoted by T. Recall that the two criteria for the rest days denote when the
allocation of the last two rest days were. In order for the path to be feasible there has to
be at least 2 rest days per every 7 days. This means that the number of days that the
rest days can be allocated is bounded by 7. Let this bound be denoted by U.
Given a number of labels in a vertex. Then for every value of working hours, last rest
day and second-to-last rest day there can at most be one cost. If there were several costs
for the same values of the rest of the criteria, the labels would have been dominated and

Space : |V| · T · U 2

Given the upper bound on the number of labels, the complexity of the algorithm can be
determined. The complexity of the algorithm is determined by looking at the diﬀerent
statements in Algorithm 1.
The algorithm starts with a topological sorting of the vertices in V. The running time for
topological sorting is
Topological sorting: O(|V| + |A|)
(7.21)
The algorithm then has a triple nested loop with two if statements calling the feasibility
procedure and the dominance procedure.
The ﬁrst for-loop, step (4), is executed for all the vertices, i.e. |V| times. In the second
for-loop, step (6), the algorithm executes for all the ingoing arcs for the vertex vj. This
means that the ﬁrst two for-loops take all vertices and all ingoing arcs to these vertices.
Since all arcs are only considered once through the label setting algorithm, the number
of executions of the ﬁrst two for-loops are bounded by |A|. The third for-loop, step (7),
is executed for all the labels in the adjacent vertices. The number of labels in a vertex
is bounded by K = T · U 2. The ﬁrst if-loop, step (8), checks the feasibility which is
done in constant time. The second if-loop, step (10), checks if the label is dominated. In
the dominance check the worst case is to go through all the labels, which is bounded by
K = T · U 2, to compare the labels. Adding, step (11), or skipping, step (14), the label is
done in constant time. In step (19) the dominated labels are deleted. In order to check
which labels are dominated, all labels are gone through, i.e. K = T · U 2.
The total complexity for the triple for-loop is then
O(|A| · K · K + |V| · K) = O(|A| · K2 + |V| · K)
(7.22)

since it is dependent on the speciﬁc instance that the column generation process, and

8.3
Discussion
In this section the results, presented in Section 8.2, will be discussed.
Through the result section it can be seen that it was not possible to improve the solutions,
obtained in Chapter 6, through the column generation process for the NSP at the emer-
gency department. From Table 8.1a it can be seen that it was only for 1.(c),2.(c) that
the column generation process was able to improve the objective of the relaxed master
problem. However, the solution to the non-relaxed master problem was not improved.
Furthermore, the objective of the relaxed master problem was improved to 61.92% which
is still above all the cost-optimised start solutions.
The lack of improvement for the NSP at the emergency department when using the column
generation process can be due to a number of reasons. From Table 8.1b it can be seen that
several columns with negative reduced cost are found and added to the master problem.
However, none of these columns are actually able to improve to solution. This indicates
that the algorithm has a hard time ﬁnding the right columns that would actually result
in an improvement. One thing that could have an impact on this, is the inclusion of the
dominance rules. As the problem is only solved with 1.(b) and 1.(c) it is not guaranteed
that the column with the most negative reduced cost is added to the master problem in
==PAGE:121==
8.3 Discussion
111
each iteration. This means that the dual values are not used as adequately as possible
which can be a hindrance to the convergence of the column generation process.
Another problem lies in the problem structure; it can found in the composition of the
nurses and the scheduling period. From Figure 3.1 it can be seen how many nurses have
the competence for each of the shift types. From the ﬁgure it can be seen that each
shift has several nurses that can cover the shift. Furthermore, each shift occurs 2 or 3
times a day during the entire scheduling period. Since a general scheduling horizon is
considered there are a surplus of hours. This means that the bounds on the working
hours are not very limiting for most of the nurses. These factors can have an impact on
the enormous amount of possible combinations for a schedule. This means that many
schedules will be slightly diﬀerent but basically the same schedule. For example if two
nurses with the same working hours and competences simply swap shifts. This kind of
symmetry can also occur throughout the scheduling horizon since most of the days have
the exact same requirements. The symmetry could result in the column generation process
generating schedule-lines that are variants of the same schedule-lines as the start solution
and therefore not improving the solution. More feasible combinations give more columns
and therefore the convergence is slow. This also leads to the start solutions already being
really good solutions that are diﬃcult to improve.
Furthermore, the values of the dual variables can aﬀect the convergence of the column
generation. As can be seen from the plots of the dual values, see Figure 8.3, 8.5 and
8.6, the dual variables ﬂuctuate a lot. Especially during the ﬁrst 75 iterations. A way
to overcome this issue is to use dual stabilisation which tries to reduce the ﬂuctuation
of the dual values.
It has been shown that stabilisation can be used to improve the
solution time for diﬃcult instances and to solve larger ones, [16].
From Table 8.5 it
can be seen that the sub-problem is time consuming, especially for 1.(b), and therefore it
would be beneﬁcial to be able to ﬁnd better dual values earlier in return for saving column
generation iterations. Furthermore, if dual stabilisation could help to improve the dual
values and therefore generate schedule-lines that would actually be part of an improved
solution, the unnecessarily large growth of the master problem could be avoided.
Another problem could be that the algorithm is not continued for enough iterations before
usable schedule-lines are found. As seen in Section 8.2.1 the execution time of the master
problem can be decreased by adding less columns to the master problem in each iteration.
However, it might mean that some useful columns are disregarded. It would be very useful
to be able to decrease the execution time of the sub-problem, especially with the inclusion
of the dominance rules 1.(a) and 1.(b).
One way of decreasing the execution time of the sub-problems is to combine some of
the sub-problems for the individual nurses. A number of nurses have the exact same
competences and working hours and therefore the same sub-problem is solved for each of
them. Combining the sub-problems would mean that less sub-problems would have be
to solved in each iteration of the column generation process. Another way of reducing
the execution time of the sub-problems is to look into more eﬃcient ways to solve the
==PAGE:122==
112
Results using the Schedule-based Solution Method
resource constrained shortest path problems. Improvement in the execution time of the
sub-problem could also beneﬁcial as all the dominance rules, 1.(a), could be included and
it would be guaranteed that the column with the most negative reduced cost would be
added to the master problem in each iteration.
The graph representing the sub-problem is very large for the nurses at the emergency
department. This is due to the long scheduling horizon and the large number of shifts
including rest days and day oﬀ. Furthermore, it was necessary to introduce three diﬀerent
vertices for the rest days per day. Overall this means that the graph is huge and, therefore,
there is a lot of partial paths to extend during the label setting algorithm. In addition,
the sub-problem has to be solved for all 70 nurses employed at the emergency department.
Overall the schedule-based formulation and solution method was not able to improve
the solutions from Chapter 6. However, it has been shown how the problem could be
formulated for a column generation solution approach. The method might be useful for
similar problems where good solutions could not be generated by a direct model. Since
the column generation process, presented in Chapter 7, is developed speciﬁcally for the
emergency department, the schedule-based formulation and solution method would have
to be modiﬁed in order to accurately model the speciﬁcations for another instance.
It could be interesting to see how the schedule-based solution method would work for
other instances, for example other departments at the hospital. It is likely that the column
generation process would be better suited for smaller problems such that especially the
execution time of the sub-problem could be decreased. A decrease in the execution time of
the sub-problem would require a smaller graph, i.e. a shorter scheduling horizon and/or
less shift types. Furthermore, less nurses would mean that the sub-problem would have
be solved less times.

Chapter 9
Discussion
In this chapter the diﬀerent solution methods and obtained results will be compared.
The realisability, execution time and adequacy will be taken into account in order to
recommend which methods are promising to implement for the scheduling at the emer-
gency department. The potentials for cost savings and maintenance of the quality of the
schedule will be evaluated. Recall that the results are only for the emergency department
instance. The cost of the obtained schedules will be compared to a lower bound which
will be derived in Section 9.1. At the end, suggestions for further research are presented
in Section 9.2.
In Chapter 6 it was seen that good solutions could be obtained for the emergency depart-
ment by breaking the problem down into smaller scheduling periods and adjusting the
direct models such that a schedule could be generated for the entire scheduling period.
A cyclic 1-week or 2-week model could solve the problem extremely fast by optimising on
cost. However, when the quality of the schedule were taken into account, the execution
time increased. A cyclic 2-week schedule was shown to be adequate to obtain a large
saving while maintaining a high quality of the schedule. One of the main disadvantages
of a cyclic schedule is the lack of realisability. After a cyclic schedule is generated, it
would have to be adjusted, either manually or by setting up a new model, in order to
take exceptions during the scheduling period, for example planned holidays, into account.
The realisability of the iterative methods is better since exceptions can be added to the
model before it is solved and the obtained solutions would have incorporated the planned
holidays and other exceptions when they are generated. Therefore, after the schedule is
generated no further modiﬁcations are needed. The results from the iterative methods
==PAGE:124==
114
Discussion
also show large savings in cost and a possibility to maintain a good quality of the schedule.
Furthermore, the schedules are solved within reasonable execution times.
Recall that it is only the factors, deﬁned in Section 3.8, that inﬂuence the quality for the
models tested. For a schedule, generated by any of these models, to be directly applicable
at the emergency department, more factors should probably be taking into account. This
could for example include an equal distribution of evening shifts, night shifts, and weekend
shifts, and individual preferences for the nurses.
It was not possible to prove optimality for the models or solve the model directly for the
entire scheduling horizon by the direct formulation. In order to improve the solutions, a
set covering problem was formulated for the problem and a column generation algorithm
was implemented. However, no improvement was obtained for the considered instance.
Even though similar methods have shown to be useful in the literature, see Chapter 2,
it was not applicable for the problem at the emergency department. It seems that the
method is not suﬃcient for as big as a problem as the one at the emergency department.
The good results, obtained by the direct formulation, might have been a coincidence and
small changes in the problem might result in the direct formulation not being able to
generate any good or even feasible solutions. Therefore, the schedule-based formulation
and the column generation approach might be put to good use for other instances or
similar problems. Furthermore, the column generation process could be modiﬁed such
that it could generate cyclic schedules. In this way the column generation process might
become more applicable in terms of the execution time as smaller resource constrained
shortest path problems would have to be solved in each iteration.
In light of the tests conducted in this thesis, it is apparent that it is very promising to im-
plement the iterative method with 2-week ﬁxed x-variables at the emergency department.
This method shows a high saving in the cost, over 40 %, for a cost optimised schedule.
Furthermore it is possible to solve the problem with a lower bound on the quality with a
saving just under 40 % and with small modiﬁcations it is possible to incorporate vacation
and other planned exceptions throughout the scheduling horizon. In addition, a schedule
can be generated within a reasonable execution time by this method. Recall that the
results presented in this report assumes that all the nurses are available throughout the
scheduling horizon. This will not be the case in practice. Furthermore, the data are from
2015 while the cost for comparison is from 2014. However, a great potential with automa-
tised and optimised scheduling have been shown which indicates that there is room for
improvement of the schedule at the emergency department. In the report of The National
Audit Department of Denmark published in 2015, [34], it is stated that the resources can
in general be utilised more eﬃciently within the health care. This statement is conﬁrmed
by the studies in this thesis.
Note that the saving of over 40 % only requires a diﬀerent allocation of the shifts to the
nurses. It maintains the number of hired nurses and the structure at the department. A
restructuring at the department might result in an even bigger possible saving. However,
this requires an in-depth analysis of the needs at the department in order to maintain a

method was not able to ﬁnd a better solution for the considered instance. Furthermore,
it indicates that it will not be a big advantage to be able to solve for the entire scheduling
period and utilising the appertaining freedom of allocation of the shifts over the entire
scheduling period. The solutions found are not optimal, however, based on the lower
bound they are not far from it.
9.2
Further Research
In this section a number of suggestions for further research will be presented.
To further test the realisability of the proposed models and solutions, it could be inter-
esting to test how robust the models are to changes in the data for example the number
of nurses, demand for each shift or addition or removal of some shifts. These tests could
help determine if it is still possible to use the same models and methods when changes
are made at the emergency department.
Furthermore, it could be interesting to test the models for other instances to see whether
a large saving in the cost could still be obtained and whether the execution times could
be maintained. However, as the models and methods in this thesis are directly develop
for the emergency department, this would require a remodelling of the formulations and
the solution methods.
Other factors, that would increase the realisability of the schedule, could also be incor-
porated. As described in Section 4.3.3.1 weighting can be introduced in order to take
individual preferences of the nurses into account. Furthermore, it could be interesting to
==PAGE:127==
9.2 Further Research
117
include more common preferences for the nurses in order to analyse the potential when
these factors are incorporated. The preferences would have to be determined in collab-
oration with the nurses in order to ensure a truly meaningful high quality schedule that
would improve their job satisfaction.
If the direct mathematical formulation and solution methods are not able to handle some
variations of the problem or other instances, it would be interesting to modify the schedule-
based formulation and the column generation process to solve for example a 2-week cyclic
schedule. In the column generation it could also be interesting to incorporate the quality
of the schedule and test whether the method can handle a minimum required quality.
It would also be interesting to see how the column generation approach could be developed
further to solve the problem at the emergency department for the entire scheduling period.
This could for example be done by using another solution method for the sub-problem,
for example develop a heuristic to generate columns. Furthermore, the column generation
could be extended with branching such that branch-and-price is utilised to ﬁnd the optimal
solution.
Furthermore, the impact of assuming that the scheduling is done for a general schedul-
ing period could be analysed. It would be interesting to analyse how much the saving
would decrease when exceptions occurring throughout the scheduling period are taken
into account. Many of these factors cannot be known beforehand and will change from
time to time, e.g. how many nurses are on vacation, on a sick leave and so on. Therefore,
stochastic programming could be used to estimate the potential for savings.
Another interesting scheduling problem could arise during for example Christmas and the
summer holidays where there must be a high demand for getting days oﬀ. A model min-
imising the number of requests not met while still satisfying the demand of the department
could be formulated and tested.

Discussion
==PAGE:129==
Chapter 10
Conclusion
The models, formulated in this thesis, successfully solve the real-life problem at the emer-
gency department taking into account all requirements regarding the department and the
nurses. The possible savings, and the quality that can be maintained within these savings,
were further evaluated.
The long scheduling horizon of the NSP at the emergency department diﬀer from most
other problems in the literature which is why it was necessary to break the problem
down into shorter time periods. However, a direct mathematical formulation with small
modiﬁcations results in high quality schedules that can cover the entire scheduling horizon.
In order to improve the solutions further, an alternative formulation of the problem was
considered utilising column generation as the solution method. The alternative formula-
tion was a decomposition of the original problem into a master problem and a sub-problem.
The master problem was formulated as a set covering problem and the sub-problem was
modelled as a resource constrained shortest path problem and solved using a multicriteria
label setting algorithm. However, the alternative formulation and solution method did
not succeed to improve the solutions for the emergency department further.
The best cost-optimised solution was obtained by an iterative method solving 3 weeks at
a time and ﬁxing 1-week variables in each iteration of the method. A similar iterative
method makes it possible to require a certain quality of the schedule. Both models are
solved within a reasonable execution time and are directly applicable in practice since it,
with small modiﬁcations, can incorporate planned exceptions in the scheduling period.
==PAGE:130==
120
Conclusion
A lower bound on the cost of the schedule is derived and it is concluded that there is not
a lot of room for further improvement of the solutions obtained.
It is estimated that Region Zealand can save over 40 % compared to 2014 by introducing
automatised and optimised scheduling at the emergency department. The savings are not
directly applicable in practice since all nurses will not always be available. However, it
shows that simply allocating the nurses diﬀerently has a great potential. It is shown that,
with a slight increase in cost, the quality of a schedule can also be taken into account which
is likely to result in a higher satisfaction with the schedules among the nurses. A higher
job satisfaction among the nurses can beneﬁt the patients and, therefore, introducing
automatised and optimised scheduling can result in high quality schedules that can beneﬁt
both Region Zealand, the nurses, and the patients.
It would be interesting to test the methods on other instances in order to see whether the
good solutions and the execution times of a direct mathematical model can be maintained.
Furthermore, it would be interesting to test whether the column generation approach
could improve the solutions.
