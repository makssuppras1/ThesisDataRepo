Optimization of well-based diﬀuser design
Abstract
Well-based diﬀusers have greatly developed since the introduction of Schroeder diﬀusers forty years
ago.
Nevertheless, common designs found nowadays still have a lack of eﬃciency, especially at low
frequencies when arranged in arrays.
The present study therefore aimed at proposing a method to
design optimal well-based sound diﬀusers. Based on simulations performed with the boundary-element
method, the diﬀerent eﬀects of the parameters determining the geometry of a well-based diﬀuser were ﬁrst
investigated so as to identify the key variables to take into consideration. According to these ﬁndings, an
optimization strategy was adopted and optimal designs were suggested, which were then compared with
a chosen reference diﬀuser. Finally, a method to improve the poor performance of one of the optimal
designs in a speciﬁc frequency band was introduced.
Keywords Sound diﬀusion, well-based diﬀusers, phase-grating diﬀusers, optimization, orthogonal ar-
rays

Chapter 1.
Introduction
1.1
Study motivation
The study of room acoustics is of crucial importance for spaces such as performance halls and re-
hearsal rooms naturally, but also for classrooms, lecture halls, work places and social gathering venues.
Besides providing a pleasant listening experience in the case of music, good acoustic conditions can re-
duce the noise level and make speech easier to understand. Less noise and less eﬀort in understanding
then leads to an enhanced quality of study and work or improved social interactions according to the
situation. To control acoustic conditions in a room three phenomena can be taken advantage of when
sound waves encounter a surface: absorption, reﬂection and diﬀusion. Absorption allows the reduction
of the total acoustic energy either by transforming it into heat or by transmitting it to another space.
It is widely used and sometimes even overused, making rooms sound ‘dead’ and uncomfortable. Re-
ﬂection in the present case is meant as specular reﬂection, or mirror-like reﬂection. It helps redirecting
sound in a desired direction by placing reﬂectors at the right positions and angles. Diﬀusion consists
in spreading sound in all directions. Perfect diﬀusion occurs when a surface reﬂects the same amount
of acoustic energy in all directions (including the specular direction) regardless of the wave incidence.
On the contrary, a total lack of diﬀusion is equivalent to a perfectly specular reﬂection. Hence diﬀusion
should not be confused with scattering, which is the property of reﬂecting sound in other directions than
the specular one with no importance given to uniformity. Diﬀusion helps creating more homogeneous
acoustic conditions between diﬀerent positions in a room, hence making the sound ﬁeld more diﬀuse. It is
particularly relevant because the prediction of the main parameters used in room acoustics are based on
the assumption of a diﬀuse sound ﬁeld. It is also an eﬃcient way to break up standing wave patterns and
focusing eﬀects, two major phenomena to avoid in a room. Besides, diﬀusion can inﬂuence perceptual as-
pects for listeners related to spaciousness and early reﬂections, increasing the feeling of being surrounded
by sound. Of course, diﬀerent rooms with diﬀerent purposes require diﬀerent acoustic conditions, and
absorption, reﬂection and diﬀusion rarely occur alone. The key to room acoustics engineering is thus to
ﬁnd the right balance between these three treatments.
May it be intentional or not, some diﬀusion can be introduced in rooms by the diﬀerent pieces
of furniture present.
Geometric shapes like hemispheres and pyramids can also be implemented on
the walls and ceiling to reﬂect sound in diﬀerent directions, but it is not always suﬃcient to get the
desired acoustic conditions. Sound diﬀuser design methods were therefore established based on a diﬀerent
approach. Rather than considering geometry to control the directions of the reﬂected sound, impedance
is varied along the surface to induce phase diﬀerences between neighboring reﬂective zones. The resulting
interferences then create a diﬀuse reﬂection at a distance far enough from the surface. Later, optimization
processes were performed in an attempt to take full advantage of this new technique. Unfortunately
these processes did not account for all the frequencies and incidences a diﬀuser can encounter, making
the results only partially optimized.
After design methods followed new performance evaluation procedures, which have now become
international standards.
As a result, two parameters are currently used to evaluate a diﬀuser: the
diﬀusion coeﬃcient and the normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient. The diﬀusion coeﬃcient indicates the amount
– 1 –
==PAGE:12==
of diﬀusion created in the far ﬁeld by a sample without any ﬂange or backing surface, and its normalized
version compares it to the diﬀusion created by a thin ﬂat plate with same height and width; hence,
the diﬀraction eﬀect being removed. While most currently commercialized sound diﬀusers exhibit good
performances in terms of diﬀusion coeﬃcient, they often suﬀer from a lack of eﬃciency at low frequencies
when the normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient is considered. This can be a problem when sound diﬀusers are
embedded in walls or arranged in arrays, as in those cases the diﬀraction created by a single unit does
not signiﬁcantly contribute to the diﬀusion.
1.2
Previous works
Using Fraunhofer approximation to predict the sound ﬁeld reﬂected from a surface, Schroeder ﬁrst
suggested in 1975 arranging wells of diﬀerent depths to vary the impedance of the reﬂective surface
along one direction and create diﬀusion [1]. Assuming an inﬁnitely hard material for the structure, wells
with quarter-wavelength depths were associated with zero-depth sections to yield reﬂection coeﬃcients
respectively equal to -1 and 1. The exact arrangement of wells was then chosen by following a maximum-
length sequence. Such sequence having a ﬂat power spectrum, the same amount of energy should be
reﬂected from the surface in all directions. A method to turn a one-dimensional sequence into a two-
dimensional diﬀuser was also given, but a new and better one was introduced by Schroeder and Gerlach
in 1976 [2]. The principle was to break up a long sequence into short equal segments, with each row of the
diﬀuser following one of these segments. However, the maximum-length sequence design is only eﬃcient
around a speciﬁc frequency, making Schroeder and Gerlach use quadratic-residue sequences in 1977 in
order to extend the eﬃciency bandwidth of Schroeder diﬀusers [3]. These sequences indeed lead to well
depths ranging (discretely) between zero and half a desired wavelength. With this new sequence they
derived the expression for two-dimensional diﬀusers in 1979, using a two-dimensional quadratic-residue
array instead of transforming the one-dimensional sequence [4]. In 1979 Berkhout et al. proved that
Schroeder’s assumption of local reaction yielded incorrect results, but practical evidence was provided
the same year by Schroeder et al. and Schroder to show that this approximation could be made without
having a strong impact [4, 5, 6]. Schroeder diﬀusers based on quadratic-residue sequences then became
and still remain the reference in sound diﬀusers.
In 1984 D’antonio and Konnert gave a more detailed explanation of the design parameters of
quadratic-residue diﬀusers and their eﬀects on the ﬁnal performance [7].
They also introduced the
primitive-root sequence to determine well depths. One advantage of this sequence over the quadratic-
residue one is its asymmetry, which can be useful when implementing diﬀusers in arrays.
However
it produces a cancellation or attenuation of the specular reﬂection, making it a better scatterer than
diﬀuser, even if it can achieve good diﬀusion. In 1994 Cox performed an optimization process on well-
based diﬀusers starting from wells with random depths [8]. However, the optimization was performed
for normal incidence only and frequencies between 100 Hz and 1250 Hz. The diﬀuser thereby obtained
could only achieve a performance level similar to a quadratic-residue diﬀuser for conditions out of the
optimization scope. Reviews of state-of-the-art knowledge about sound diﬀusers were made by Cox and
D’antonio in 2003 and 2004, with an updated edition of the latter in 2009 [9, 10, 11]. Another sound
diﬀuser optimization was done in 2013 by Yang et al., considering normal incidence only and performing
several single frequency processes [12]. It resulted in diﬀerent designs for diﬀerent frequencies, hence a
lack of practicality for applications.

1.3
Scope of the study
The present study aimed at providing general guidelines and deﬁning a method to design optimal
sound diﬀusers. It implied investigating the diﬀerent parameters that can aﬀect a diﬀuser’s performance,
choosing an appropriate optimization strategy, and assessing the resulting diﬀusers both in optimization
and standard conditions. Only one-dimensional sound diﬀusers were considered, and contrary to pre-
vious works, the optimization process was performed for a wide frequency range and several incidence
directions.
To reach the objectives stated previously, elements of pipe radiation were explained and the theory
behind Schroeder diﬀusers was described, with its assumptions and limitations.
Examples of sound
diﬀuser performances were later given to illustrate the problems of current designs. Once the boundary
element method model selected to perform the optimization was validated, the diﬀerent parameters
involved in the design of a well-based diﬀuser were investigated. Finally, the optimization process was
performed and the resulting designs were assessed.

Chapter 2.
Theoretical background
2.1
Unﬂanged pipe radiation
When a sound wave is incident on a pipe, only a part of the acoustic energy enters it as reﬂection
occurs at the entrance. For wavelengths much larger than the dimensions of the pipe, the reﬂection
is even largely dominant, but it becomes minimal at resonance frequencies. In all cases, the energy
that enters the pipe can then be re-emitted in the open space. The sound radiation of a cylindrical
unﬂanged pipe was theorized by Levine and Schwinger in 1948 [13]. They described the radiation in a
given direction relatively to the axis of the pipe with the power-gain function G(θ), which is the radiated
sound power ratio between the pipe and an isotropic point source. If G(θ) = 1, the pipe radiates as much
power as the point source in the direction θ, but if G(θ) > 1 the pipe radiates more sound power in that
direction than the point source (and inversely). One of the main ﬁndings of their study was that the
maximum of G(θ) always occurs for θ = 0◦, and the value of G(0◦) increases with ka, a being the radius
of the pipe. On the contrary, G(θ) tends to 1 when ka tends to 0, that is at very low frequencies or for
very narrow pipes. Thus, the lower ka the more isotropically a pipe radiates; and inversely, the higher
ka the more directive the pipe. However, when transverse modes propagate in the pipe (ka > 1.841),
local maxima can appear in G(θ) in addition to the global maximum at θ = 0◦. In addition to that,
they also determined the exact value of the end correction ∆l. The eﬀective length of a pipe is indeed
greater than its geometric length l by leff = l + ∆l, thus aﬀecting its resonance frequency. In the low
frequency limit, it was proven that ∆l = 0.6133a; hence, the end correction being larger for a wide pipe
than for a narrow pipe.
In the two-dimensional case, a rectangular pipe of width 2a can be considered to behave similarly to
a cylindrical pipe of radius a. The same discussions about radiation and end correction can therefore be
made. Nevertheless, the limit for single-mode propagation still remains diﬀerent, with transverse modes
propagating when ka > π/2 for the rectangular pipe.

2.2.2
Limiting assumptions
Schroeder diﬀuser design method relies on several simplifying assumptions. The sources and receivers
should be situated in the far ﬁeld, the diﬀusers are approximated to be made of a rigid material and the
separations between wells to be inﬁnitely thin. Furthermore the wells are assumed to be locally reacting,
implying that pressures in neighboring wells do not aﬀect each other. This assumption holds as long
as only plane waves propagate in the wells. The upper frequency limit of the theory is thus set to the
cut-oﬀfrequency of the wells. It corresponds to the point when wavelengths become short enough to
allow the propagation of transverse waves, i.e. when the width of the wells w becomes larger than half
the wavelength. Nonetheless diﬀusion is still generated above this limit in practical cases.

2.2.3
Importance of the near ﬁeld
Schroeder established his theory with the assumption that both source and receivers are situated
in the far ﬁeld of diﬀusers [1]. Moreover the international standard regulating the assessment of sound
diﬀusers also requires the same far ﬁeld conditions [15]. This far ﬁeld can be deﬁned as the space where
the sound ﬁeld reﬂected from the diﬀuser follows a spherical propagation. By opposition, near ﬁeld can
be deﬁned as the space where far ﬁeld criteria are not satisﬁed. It comprises both hydrodynamic and
geometric near ﬁelds.
A receiver can be reached by sound coming from all the wells of a diﬀuser, thus three criteria can
deﬁne the far ﬁeld depending on whether the diﬀuser is considered as a monopole, an array of sources or
a surface source. Given a source located inﬁnitely far away, these criteria are respectively [16, 17, 18, 19]:

2.3.3
Sound diﬀuser arrays
Even with high values of diﬀusion coeﬃcient, a sound diﬀuser always has a tendency to reﬂect more
sound energy in one or several preferred directions.
If an array is made by implementing the same
diﬀuser repeatedly, the preferred directions will then be reinforced and the diﬀusion coeﬃcient of the
array will decrease compared to the single unit. An example of this phenomenon is illustrated in Figure
2.3, based on data from Cox and D’Antonio and RPG Diﬀusor Systems Inc. commercial documentation
[11]. The comparison between a single QRD 734 unit and an array made of 6 identical units shows that
the two samples have approximately the same dependence on frequency, with their normalized diﬀusion
coeﬃcient curves following the same variations. However, the single unit’s coeﬃcient values are almost
twice larger than the array’s ones for most frequencies above 400 Hz, conﬁrming the performance loss in
periodic arrangements of sound diﬀusers.
One solution to this problem consists in using two diﬀerent sound diﬀuser units and arranging them
according to a pseudo-random binary sequence minimizing periodicity [20]. A simple way to implement
this solution is to have an asymmetric sound diﬀuser as the ﬁrst unit and ﬂip it upside down to get a
second diﬀerent unit, thus allowing to create an aperiodic array out of a single diﬀuser design. Two
examples of this solution are given in Figure 2.4, with the ﬁrst array made of quadratic-residue diﬀusers
with 5 and 7 wells, and the second array made of Modﬀusors (data from Cox and D’Antonio and RPG
Diﬀusor Systems Inc.)[10]. It shows that the performance loss can indeed be compensated for above
500 Hz, which is the frequency above which the normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcients of the single units take
values greater than 0.4. Nevertheless, the diﬀusion coeﬃcient below 500 Hz decreases dramatically when
the diﬀusers are arranged in arrays. This is due to the fact that no diﬀusion is created by these designs at
low frequencies, as seen with their normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcients. Consequently, when the diﬀraction
eﬀect decreases with the increasing total width of the array, the diﬀusion of the array also decreases.

widths wn, the separation width wsep, and the edge radius a. Before separating these parameters into
ﬁxed parameters and design variables, a qualitative study of their diﬀerent inﬂuences was performed to
understand how they aﬀect the performance of a diﬀuser.
4.1
Eﬀects of geometric parameters
4.1.1
Practical constraints
As mentioned in Chapter 2, the total width has an inﬂuence on the frequency below which the ﬂat-
plate behavior signiﬁcantly contributes to diﬀusion. This happens for f < c/L, when wavelengths are
larger than the total width. However, diﬀusers are usually implemented on large surfaces and arranged
in arrays. In such cases, it is the width of the array that determines the limit for ﬂat-plate behavior,
which can be below 100 Hz if the array is larger than 3.5 m. The total width of diﬀuser units L then does

not signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the performance of the array. Besides, since this parameter depends on the
number of wells, their widths, and the separation width, it was decided to set it as a constraint rather
than a ﬁxed value: a diﬀuser unit should not be wider than 1 m in order to facilitate its implementation.
This value was thought to be appropriate to let a diﬀuser ﬁt in various situations and potentially be
arranged in arrays.
The total depth of a diﬀuser also has a strong impact on its low frequency behavior because it limits
the maximum value well depths can take, and the deeper the wells the broader the eﬃciency bandwidth
towards the low frequencies. Nevertheless, implementing diﬀusers in a room should preserve its usable
volume as reasonably as possible, which can be challenging in small spaces. Such cases can be handled
with folded well designs, which allow wells to be eﬀectively deeper than the total depth of the diﬀuser
at low frequencies [22]. Well-based diﬀusers currently commercialized having usually a total depth of
0.23 m, straight wells with depths up to 0.3 m were investigated in the present study.
4.1.2
Number of wells
The inﬂuence of the number of wells is not as simple as the total width or the total depth. Figure
4.2 illustrates the case where all wells were given the same depth of 0.12 m with a total depth of 0.23 m, a
constant well width of 0.07 m, a separation width of 0.012 m, and ﬂat edges. In this ﬁgure, the normalized
diﬀusion coeﬃcient of diﬀusers with diﬀerent numbers of wells was plotted to remove the eﬀect of the
total width. It appeared that with 6 wells, a high diﬀusion was achieved at 315 Hz, followed by a lack
of diﬀusion at 500 Hz. A negative normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient indeed indicates that a ﬂat plate is
more diﬀusive than the sample. When the number of wells was increased, both the peak and dip in
diﬀusion shifted towards low frequencies while their amplitudes decreased. With 11 wells for example,
they respectively appeared at 200 Hz and 400 Hz. Hence, the diﬀuser tended towards a diﬀusion similar
to that of a ﬂat plate when having a large number of wells.
Naturally, the wells of a sound diﬀuser should not all share the same depth to yield good perfor-
mances. A large number of wells can then lead to many diﬀerent well depths, and thus good diﬀusion
for a broad frequency range. However, a small number of wells can result in high diﬀusion at speciﬁc
frequencies and possibly cancel out the diﬀusion dip from one well with the peak from another one.
Both small and large numbers of wells having their advantages, diﬀerent values were selected for the
optimization process, but it was set to a traditional value of 7 wells for the rest of the parameter study.
4.1.3
Well depths
Well depths are the central variables in the design of Schroeder diﬀusers and were discussed in
Chapter 2. When the sound ﬁelds emanating from all wells sum up in the far ﬁeld, the diﬀerent reﬂec-
tion phases introduced by the wells combine in either constructive or destructive interferences. These
reﬂection phases depend on the depths of the wells, and a right combination of well depths is needed to
create good diﬀusion. Generally, the depth of a well is tuned so that its resonance frequency corresponds
to the frequency at which diﬀusion is desired. Therefore well depths are closely linked to the frequencies
at which diﬀusion is achieved or not.

4.1.4
Well widths
The width of a well inﬂuences several aspects of its behavior. Firstly, it dictates the end correction
and modiﬁes the eﬀective depth of the well. It means that two wells with the same depth but diﬀerent
widths have diﬀerent eﬀective depths, and hence contribute diﬀerently to diﬀusion (as explained in the
previous paragraph). Besides, for a given frequency where only plane wave propagation occurs in the well,
a narrow well radiates more closely to an isotropic point source than a wide well which is more directive.
When the cut-oﬀfrequency of the well is exceeded, lobes appear in the radiation angular pattern, making
it more complicated to describe. In all cases however, the direction with most power radiated is the on-
axis direction, with this maximum being more or less pronounced depending on the situation. For this
reason, wells with diﬀerent widths have diﬀerent radiation patterns and could compensate each others’
lacks of uniformity at certain frequencies.

4.1.5
Separations between the wells
In Schroeder’s theory, the separations between the wells of a diﬀuser were assumed to be inﬁnitely
thin. Following his work, the diﬀusers designed until now generally had separation widths much smaller
than their well widths. For example, there is almost a factor 6 between the width of the wells and the
width of the separations in the diﬀusers designed by RPG Diﬀusor Systems Inc. (0.07 m for 0.012 m). To
investigate the inﬂuence of the separation width on the performance of a diﬀuser, simulations were per-
formed on the same reference design as previously with three diﬀerent values for wsep: 0.012 m, 0.024 m,
and 0.036 m. The outcome of these simulations is displayed in Figure 4.7 with the normalized diﬀusion
coeﬃcients of the three cases. Two diﬀerent tendencies emerged from the results. On one hand, the
performance of the diﬀuser decreased at low frequencies when its separation width was increased. On
the other hand, the peaks in the normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient became broader with a larger separation
width, yielding smaller variations between the local maxima and minima. The widths investigated in
the present case were very small compared to the wavelengths of the incident sound (around 0.069 m
at 5000 Hz), it is therefore unlikely that diﬀraction from the separations played a signiﬁcant role in the
diﬀusers’ performances. An explanation for the results obtained could be that increasing the separation
width leads to a larger distance between the wells. As a result, a part of the sound radiated from one
well that could previously enter the neighboring wells now encounters a ﬂat reﬂective surface. In other
words, the interactions between neighboring wells might have been reduced because of the larger distance
separating them.

to generalize the results obtained from this particular case and decide which parameters would be chosen
as design variables.

4.2
Selection of design variables

limited number of runs. Based on the qualitative study, a mixed-level orthogonal array with 14 four-level
variables and 2 two-level variables was selected [25]. 4 equally spaced levels were allocated to the well

4.2.1

important. The ﬁrst one was its overall value, meaning that the diﬀusion coeﬃcient should be high to
get a good performance. The second aspect was the variation of the diﬀusion coeﬃcient with frequency.
One of the objectives was indeed to design a diﬀuser that could be eﬃcient over a broad frequency range,
implying that the diﬀusion coeﬃcient should reach similar values at all frequencies. Two responses were
thus selected to represent the performance of a diﬀuser: the root-mean-square error RMSE(d, 1) between

better the performance of the diﬀuser.

Chapter 5.
Optimization
After determining an objective function and the design variables of a well-based diﬀuser, the opti-
mization process itself could take place. The ﬁrst strategy considered in this study was based on Taguchi
methods for robust design, which are traditionally used in combination with orthogonal arrays, but the
complexity of the system representing diﬀusers forced a search for alternatives.
5.1
Taguchi methods
Taguchi methods rely on the assumption that the interactions between the design variables are
limited or negligible. When looking at the present design variables however, it seemed that strong in-
teractions between all the depths and widths could be expected. In order to test if Taguchi methods
could be applied to the case of well-based diﬀusers, their reasoning was tested on the 64 runs obtained
during the parameter study. For each design variable, the runs were ﬁrst separated into groups cor-
responding to the diﬀerent levels of the design variable. Secondly, the mean of the relative closeness
coeﬃcient was calculated within each group to determine which level yielded the best results. After
repeating these steps for all design variables, the performance of the resulting sound diﬀuser (later called
Taguchi diﬀuser) was simulated. The selection of the best level of each design variable is detailed in
Table 5.1. Since the separation width and edge radius were found to be insigniﬁcant in the parameter
study, the fact that no diﬀerences were made between their two levels did not impact the result. By
following this method, it could be noticed that when taken individually, the widest wells always yielded
better diﬀusion performances. This might be due to the fact that wider wells lead to larger end correc-
tions, and potentially larger eﬀective depth variations among the wells if the other ones had diﬀerent
levels. It should indeed be remembered from the parameter study that a sound diﬀuser’s performance
was signiﬁcantly worse when the well width was the same for most of the wells. The best result from
the original 64 runs of the orthogonal array, i.e. the run with the highest relative closeness coeﬃcient,
was then selected for a comparison with this Taguchi diﬀuser in the optimization conditions (3 source
locations, frequencies between 250 Hz and 2500 Hz), as shown in Figure 5.1. The standard deviations of
the alternative diﬀusion coeﬃcients for the best run and the Taguchi diﬀuer were respectively 0.095 and
0.142; regarding the root-mean-square error, it was found that RMSE(dalt, 1) = 0.363 for the best run,
and RMSE(dalt, 1) = 0.422 for the Taguchi diﬀuser. These results indicated that Taguchi methods were

Chapter 6.
Conclusion
6.1
Parameters governing the performance of a diﬀuser
The qualitative study on the eﬀects of the diﬀerent geometric parameters provided some useful
information on how the performance of a diﬀuser can be aﬀected.
It was ﬁrstly found that the number of wells inﬂuenced the variations of the diﬀusion coeﬃcient with
frequency. The more wells in a diﬀuser the more constant its diﬀusion coeﬃcient, but also the lower the
maximum value of this coeﬃcient. An intermediate number of wells is then probably the best solution
to ﬁnd a compromise between a high diﬀusion coeﬃcient at certain frequencies and small variations of
this diﬀusion coeﬃcient with frequency.
Secondly, it was seen that the depths of the wells determined the frequencies at which good diﬀusion
is achieved or not. Hence, the performance of a diﬀuser could be shifted towards low frequencies by scaling
up its well depths, or shifted towards high frequencies by scaling them down.
Contrary to the well depths, the widths of the wells exhibited a more complex inﬂuence on the
performance of a sound diﬀuser. Wider wells lead to lower cut-oﬀfrequencies, and as a result, the high
frequency behavior can be completely changed. Wider wells also have a higher directivity when only a
single mode propagates. Consequently, the normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient was found to decrease at low
frequencies. Besides, the width of a well determines its end correction; hence, the eﬀective lengths and
resonance frequencies of the wells can be modiﬁed by their widths. With a constant well depths, this
eﬀect was mostly seen in the mid-frequency range, where the normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient appeared to
increase in the case studied. Furthermore, attributing diﬀerent widths to the wells of a diﬀuser seemed
to combine the advantages of both narrow and wide wells.
The width of the separations between the wells was also studied. It was found that the normalized
diﬀusion coeﬃcient was more constant when the separation width was larger, with the prominences of
local maxima and minima being reduced. It was probably due to the fact that larger distance between
the wells caused a reduction of their interactions.
Finally, the edge radius was investigated. Given that the dimensions of the separations were much
smaller than the wavelengths involved in this study, it was not expected that diﬀraction from the edges
played an important role in the total diﬀusion. This was conﬁrmed when diﬀusers with straight edges
and semicircular edges were found to have almost identical diﬀusion coeﬃcients.
After studying the inﬂuences of the diﬀerent geometric parameters of a well-based sound diﬀuser,
the technique for order preference by similarity to ideal solution (TOPSIS) was applied to determine
what parameters have a signiﬁcant impact on the diﬀusion coeﬃcient.
The results showed that the
performance of a diﬀuser was signiﬁcantly poorer when most of its wells have the same depth. The same
ﬁndings were obtained for the well widths, as the diﬀusion coeﬃcient of the diﬀusers with most of their
wells having the same width was signiﬁcantly lower. Regarding the separation width and edge radius,
the TOPSIS results indicated that both these parameters did not have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the
performance of a sound diﬀuser. As a consequence, the depths and widths of all the wells were selected
as design variable, and several optimization processes were performed with diﬀerent numbers of wells.

==PAGE:60==
6.2
Use of orthogonal arrays to obtain optimal designs
Orthogonal arrays with numbers of levels equal to the numbers of wells tested were selected to
conduct the optimization, allowing the wells of the diﬀusers to virtually all have diﬀerent depths and
widths. Among all the designs simulated with a given orthogonal array, the best diﬀuser resulting from
the TOPSIS method was considered an optimal design. The idea was that orthogonal arrays ensured
that a representative sample of the possible designs was simulated with a minimum computational eﬀort.
Optimizations were performed for designs with 5, 7, 8, and 9 wells and diﬀerent depth ranges. It was
found that the optimal designs with 8 wells and 9 wells showed the most constant alternative diﬀusion
coeﬃcients, hence an agreement with the previous qualitative study. However, the optimal design with 8
wells did not perform as well in standard conditions and was ﬁnally evaluated as a worse design than the
reference chosen. Regarding the optimal designs with 5 wells and 7 wells, they both performed better
than the reference design, although the optimal diﬀuser with 5 wells had a low normalized diﬀusion
coeﬃcient in the 630 Hz 1/3-octave band.
A method to improve a diﬀuser for a speciﬁc frequency range was applied to the optimal design
with 5 wells. A 6th well with a resonance frequency within the 630 Hz 1/3-octave band was thus added.
The 6-well design thereby obtained was eventually found to have better performances than the optimal
design with 5 wells, both in terms of diﬀusion coeﬃcient and normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient.
In order to compare two designs with the same number of wells, the improvement method was
applied once more to obtain an improved design with 7 wells. It was done by adding a 7th well to the
improved design with 6 wells, the resonance frequency of this 7th well lying in the 800 Hz 1/3-octave
band. After ranking all the designs obtained by using the TOPSIS method, it appeared that the im-
proved design with 7 wells performed better than the optimal design with 7 wells when considering the
normalized diﬀusion coeﬃcient. When the diﬀusion coeﬃcient was taken into account, it is the optimal
design with 7 wells that performed better. Overall, all the designs with 5 wells, 6 wells, and 7 wells were
found to perform better than the reference.
Two optimization methods relying on orthogonal arrays could therefore be suggested in this study.
The ﬁrst method consists in setting the desired number of wells and selecting the diﬀuser with the best
performance among all the designs simulated according to the orthogonal array. In the second method,
the number of wells is set to a smaller number than what is desired at the start. The best diﬀuser in
these conditions is found with the ﬁrst method, and this design is improved by adding wells. This allows
to solve some problems that the design might have for speciﬁc frequencies. Wells are then added in this
way until the desired number of wells or the desired performance is achieved.
6.3
Recommendations for future work
As a direct continuation to this work, experimental measurements should be performed to verify
the results obtained numerically. To do so, prototypes of the optimal diﬀusers designed should be made,
and their performances assessed according to the ISO 17497-2:2012. Although some discrepancies might
appear between the diﬀusion coeﬃcients simulated and the ones measured, the same ranking of perfor-
mance should be obtained by experimental means. Such results would then conﬁrm that the optimization
methods described in this study are reliable.

==PAGE:61==
An issue has brought some limitations to the discussion in this study when comparing the diﬀusion
coeﬃcients of two diﬀerent sound diﬀusers. Indeed, it is not well known how much diﬀerence in the
diﬀusion coeﬃcient creates a signiﬁcant change in the perception of the reﬂections by listeners. It is
thus recommended to carry out some investigation to ﬁll this gap, for example by determining the just
noticeable diﬀerence (JND) related to the diﬀusion coeﬃcient similarly to what exists for the scattering
coeﬃcient. This information would be valuable when designing new diﬀusers to know whether or not the
improvements obtained are signiﬁcant, but also when deciding what diﬀuser(s) to implement in a room.
Finally, it can easily happen in practical cases of implementation that listeners are situated in the
near ﬁeld of well-based sound diﬀusers.
However, there is a lack of knowledge about the near ﬁeld
properties of these diﬀusers. As of now, it is simply assumed that good far ﬁeld diﬀusers also perform
well in the near ﬁeld. Although it is a complex sound ﬁeld to study, it would be useful to assess the
performances of sound diﬀusers in these conditions as well, and new design strategies might emerge
depending on what could be found.
