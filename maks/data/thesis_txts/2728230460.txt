Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive techno-economic assessment of a Power-to-Ammonia
(P2A) project using hydropower as an energy source. The study focuses on the hydropower
resource of 7e lake Tasersiaq in West Greenland in an off-grid and remote setting. Through
the use of the SpineOpt energy system modelling framework, the feasibility of the system
is demonstrated and the optimal size and configuration of the P2A system is determined.
The study addresses key factors that influence the feasibility of the P2A system, including
component eﬀiciency and water inflow. In addition, financial assumptions, including the
weighted average cost of capital (WACC), have a significant impact on the levelized cost
of ammonia (LCOA) and return on investment [%] (ROI). To account for the uncertainty
of future water inflows three scenarios in the form of dry, normal and wet years are derived
and the uncertainty of these water inflows is included in an extended stochastic model.
This results in two investment scales: a 670 MW hydropower plant for shares of less than
30% in wet years and a 862 MW hydropower plant for shares of 30% or more in wet years,
showing that while there is an increase in the LCOA due to higher investment cost, larger
increases in profits outweigh these additional investments.
The results of the study reveal important insights.
Firstly, intermediate storages for
hydrogen and nitrogen are not recommended, as the water storage itself acts as a flexible
storage system. Secondly, constant operation to ensure a high utilisation rate is preferred,
with deviations only justified in case of extreme differences in ammonia market prices.
The project’s impact, evaluated through a triple bottom line impact assessment, reveals
potential benefits in social, economic, and environmental aspects. Recommendations for
future research include conducting simulations over several years to extensively evaluate
the performance and potential of the system.

Introduction
Climate change is a pressing global challenge, necessitating urgent action to mitigate it.
The Paris Agreement and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDG), both formulated in
2015, highlight the importance of global cooperation in ensuring a sustainable future for
both humanity and the planet. To achieve this, particular attention must be given to the
energy sector, as it plays a pivotal role in reducing greenhouse gas emissions [1, 2].
Renewable energy sources such as wind, solar and hydropower offer promising opportu-
nities to mitigate climate change.
Hydropower in particular offers several advantages,
including the installation of large capacities, stable energy production, long lifetime and
relatively low levelized cost of electricity (LCOE), especially for large power plants. How-
ever, some issues with hydropower projects is that they are often associated with high up-
front investments, long construction times and the complexity of managing unique project
requirements [3]. This has made it challenging for continued development of hydropower,
especially in the arctic region with Greenland being a country of high interest where a
large potential for hydropower projects can be identified.
Currently, Greenland has significant unexploited hydropower resources, with only a limited
number of hydropower plants currently installed [4]. A challenge with further development
of large-scale hydropower in Greenland is the separated energy grid that limits energy
transport and the low total energy demand compared to the potential of renewable energy
sources [5].
This is also due to the region being characterised by remote settlements,
with some smaller towns scattered throughout the area.
Given the surplus of usable
renewable energy resources that exceeds local demand in Greenland, the use of Power-to-
X (P2X) technology seems to be a viable solution for energy storage and transport, though
converting electrical energy from an energy source to carbon-neutral synthetic fuels. The
through water electrolysis generated hydrogen can undergo additional processing for the
production of carbon- (C) or nitrogen- (N2) based hydrofuels, including methane (CH4),
methanol (CH3OH) or ammonia (NH3), serving as chemical energy storage, which can
then be shipped [6].
Carbon for these hydrofuels can for example be sourced through CO2 capture, directly
from the air or from biogas plants [7]. The currently available technologies for a large
scale carbon supply are either limited through high cost or a small industry in Greenland.
Therefore, ammonia serves as a more attractive option for hydrofuels as it is based on
nitrogen, which can be extracted from air through cryogenic destillation [8]. This process
describes the separation of the element in the air by utilizing their different boiling points,
which in the case of air are at cryogenic temperatures. Consequently, ammonia is the
preferred hydropower fuel for the P2X system in this study, which will now be referred to
as P2A.
The government of Greenland has recently issued multiple tenders for the development
of hydropower resources in West Greenland, including the notable hydropower resource
known as 7e lake Tasersiaq (shown in Figure 1.1). The government has welcomed the
participation of project developers in shaping the specific tender description, which has
been deliberately designed to encompass a wide range of possibilities.
The tendering
process began in May 2022 and operation is planned for 2030. The overarching goals of
the government entail ensuring equitable and substantial financial returns for Greenland,
selecting a project that aligns with the unique characteristics of Greenland and fosters

1.1
Formulation of the problem
The broad and non-specific nature of the formulated tender description underlines the need
for a thorough techno-economic assessment of the hydropower resource 7e lake Tasersiaq.
The lack of a clear decision-making framework from the project developer’s perspective
makes it diﬀicult to determine the project scope and financial feasibility. Consequently, it
is essential to carry out a comprehensive techno-economic assessment, which can provide
valuable guidance for the potential project developers, but also for the Greenlandic gov-
ernment in its decision-making process. However, the unique characteristics of the project,
including the large-scale implementation of a P2A system in an arctic environment and
the limited availability of data, pose a major challenge for the assessment.
The hydropower potential of 7e lake Tasersiaq, and a potential hydropower plant with the
intention of supplying energy to an aluminum smelter, has been evaluated by ALCOA in
a pre-feasibility study (PFS), which estimated the firm power output to be 500 MW [10].
The results of the PFS can serve as the basis for this project due to its comprehensive
findings about the cost structure, applied technologies and necessary construction works.
In contrast to the connected aluminum smelter in the PFS, this thesis focuses on a P2A
configuration utilizing the 7e lake Tasersiaq hydropower resource, wherein a P2A plant
located near Maniitsoq is connected to the hydropower plant via a transmission line. The
system is comprehensively modelled to incorporate pertinent components, as depicted in
Figure 1.2.

The report starts with an introduction that gives an overview of the research objectives,
motivation and research questions. This section sets the groundwork for the following
sections and establishes the context for the study.
The conducted literature review specific to this thesis examines the current state of re-
search in terms of relevant technologies, reference reports and aspects of the market and
transport in the context of the project in Greenland. The literature review helps assessing
appropriate technologies and understanding of system behaviour, and therefore provides
input for the data analysis and model sections.
The section data analysis includes a comprehensive comparison, selection and evaluation
of the collected data. More specifically, the data analysis results in specific parameter
values that serve as important input for the subsequent modelling phase model.
The model section involves a careful selection of modelling tools and supporting tools for
preparing inputs, running optimisation models and analysing results. Assumptions are
made to define the report scope and ensure reproducibility of the results. The base model
is implemented and relevant scenarios are derived from it. Following this, an extended
stochastic model is integrated to account for uncertainties of water inflow.
The combined information collated from the literature review, the data analysis and the
model sections are discussed in the section discussion. This section focuses on contextu-
alising the report in a TBL approach and serves as a basis for the reflection and outlook
section.
The section reflection and outlook builds on the discussions as it contains a subjective

2.1.1
Hydropower plant
Hydropower is a crucial source of low-carbon electricity generation, contributing nearly
half of the world’s total renewable energy supply. As of 2020, it accounted for one-sixth of
global electricity production, ranking as the third largest source following coal and natural
gas [12].
Hydropower plants can be categorized into three types: (1) reservoir hydropower plants,
which use a large reservoir to store water for later use; (2) pumped storage facilities,
which pump water to an elevated reservoir during off-peak hours and release it to gen-
erate electricity during periods of high demand; and (3) run-of-river hydropower plants,
which generate electricity using the natural flow of rivers without the need for a large
reservoir. When managing reservoir hydropower plants, factors such as surface elevation
of the reservoir, downstream water usage, and habitat maintenance in the lower river
environment must be taken into consideration [13]. Hydropower plants that incorporate
reservoirs are known for their flexibility in electricity generation. They can quickly ramp
up or down their output and store energy in their reservoirs, making them adaptable to
a wide range of power generation needs (e.g., grid services) [12, 13, 14]. An example of a
reservoir hydropower plant is shown in Figure 2.1.

2.1.3
Nitrogen synthesis and storage
The Haber-Bosch synthesis of ammonia relies on the utilization of pure nitrogen as its
primary input. To achieve the required level of purity, an air separation unit (ASU) is
employed. The ASU employs cryogenic distillation to separate ambient air into its con-
stituent components, namely nitrogen, oxygen, and argon. It does not produce usable
heat, but it effectively supplies nitrogen with a remarkably high level of purity (>99.9%),
which is essential for the Haber-Bosch process [24]. The research by the Danish Energy
Agency (DEA) has shown that a considerable number of theoretical studies and investi-
gations have examined the energy demand of green ammonia plants with an electrolysis
unit and ASU [25].
The energy demand of these plants differs significantly from that
of conventional ammonia plants, with the electricity consumption of the electrolysis unit
accounting for a substantial proportion of the total energy consumption of the plant, in
contrast to the ASU. The total energy consumption of the ASU is expected to be about
1% to 3% of the input required to produce 1 tNH3. The energy consumption of ASUs (200
- 400 kWh/tN2) depends on several factors, such as the capacity of the plant, the degree of
integration and the need to produce a high proportion of liquid N2 for back-up [24]. Both
[25] and [24] assume an average energy consumption of 250 kWh/tN2 and 240 kWh/tN2,
respectively. It is worth noting that only a limited amount of literature is available on the
optimisation of ASU or their integration into P2X systems. This is primarily due to the
fact that electrolysis accounts for the majority of electricity consumption and CAPEX,
dwarfing the role of ASUs [24]. The cost of an ASU is estimated by [6] to be approximately
$20151.45 M/tN2/h. In their study, [26] utilise an optimization model to explore the dy-
namic operation limits of an ASU. The design of the plant has a substantial influence
on its dynamic performance. ASUs are susceptible to variations in customer demand and
electricity prices. Effective management of these frequently encountered fluctuations can
greatly impact the operational eﬀiciency and economic viability of the plant. As the model
under study is off-grid, the operations remain constant, thus avoiding possible fluctuations
in demand or electricity prices. Therefore, it is considered reasonable to assume a single
operating point of the ASU.
Furthermore, constant operation results in predictable production, leading to the conclu-
sion that intermediate storage may not be necessary. Intermediate nitrogen storage, both
cryogenic and compressed gas, is not considered in other P2A applications and the nitro-
gen is fed directly to the ammonia synthesis cycle [6, 27]. In contrast, a highly dynamic
application of P2A with wind and solar energy investigated by [28] included a nitrogen
buffer with a cost of 1,900$2015/tN2.
An optional nitrogen buffer is integrated into the model to account for e.g., downtime
of the ASU. Since there is hardly any literature dealing with nitrogen storage for P2X
applications and the expected dynamics through this off-grid project are low, the nitrogen
storage is included as an optional element in the model for the sake of completeness. Given
the current state of the art of large-scale electrolysers and the significant uncertainties
regarding their eﬀiciencies, the ASU is expected to play only a minor role in the modelling
of the energy model. Consequently, the small impact of the ASU, together with the limited
availability of operating curve data, has led to its eﬀiciency being assumed as a constant
point in the model.

Ammonia synthesis
Ammonia can be synthesized by using the thermo-chemical Haber-Bosch process, which
creates ammonia from pressurized elemental hydrogen and nitrogen under high tempera-
ture. The standard process is methane fed and consumes 35.2 GJ/t and causes emissions
of 1.9 tCO2/tNH3 to 2.1 tCO2/tNH3 [6], that could be compensated by carbon capture
[41]. While other technologies for ammonia synthesis exist, namely electro-chemical syn-

cycles [8], this report solely considers the Haber-Bosch process, due to its maturity and
availability in large scale.
Combining hydrogen from water electrolysis using renewable electricity with the Haber-
Bosch process is called P2A [6]. The process loop is changed, consequently not requiring
methane for hydrogen production heat production and thus emitting 75% less CO2 [27].
It is expected to consume less energy in total than the methane-fed process in future
applications [27]. The Haber-Bosch process´s main components are a multi-stage feed
compressor, a reactor, a condenser, as well as a refrigeration and recycle compressor. Am-
monia is extracted through the condenser, and leftover hydrogen and nitrogen is recycled
to be re-used in the reactor [27].
In a dynamic application, this Haber-Bosch process has a minimum operating point of
about 25% [42]. The operating temperature of modern systems is about 400°C to 450°C
with a pressure of 100 bar to 150 bar [6]. Typical plant sizes are stated as 229 tNH3/day
and estimated to 916 tNH3/day in 2040 by the DEA.
An electricity consumption of

consumption of 0.64 MWh/tNH3 output. High performance Haber-Bosch processes can
reach up to 8.6 kWh/kgNH3 and 9.5 kWh/kgNH3 [43].
The process is producing heat, which can be exploited for e.g., district heating.

2.2.1
Renewable energy system models
Energy system modeling encompasses a variety of approaches and objectives that differ
significantly depending on the case. In general, however, (energy) system modeling is an
iterative process that requires constant efforts to validate and verify model behavior [44].
The objectives underlying energy system modeling can include a wide range of factors,
such as investment or operation or cost- and demand-based optimizations. In addition,
the characteristics and underlying assumptions can vary widely, such as temporal and ge-
ographic resolution, technologies employed, and off-grid or on-grid scenarios. In addition
to linear optimization programs, alternative approaches such as machine learning exist
for modeling energy systems.
It should be noted that the optimal choice of modeling
technique depends on the specific context and requirements of the study [45]. There are
several software tools available for modelling energy systems, such as PyPSA, Balmorel,
TIMES, or TEMOA. Each has different advantages over the others and may include spe-
cific functionalities such as stochasticity or modeling of specific technologies (e.g., a water
reservoir) [46]. Modelling energy systems requires a trade-off between the degree of sim-
plification and the realistic representation of the system under study. For example, in the
case of water to electricity conversion, a linearisation technique for the eﬀiciency curve
can be used to reduce the computational complexity as well as avoiding a mixed-integer
programming (MIP). Also, if choosing a too low temporal resolution, relevant informa-
tion might get lost [47]. Here, the tool SpineOpt offers a functionality to include different
temporal resolutions in a model [46].
In the study conducted by [48], an analysis of a hydropower plant was conducted focusing
on different objectives within the hydropower generation schedule. The study found that
different objectives, such as minimizing water loss or meeting demand, have an impact on
the optimal solution. The researchers noted that a change in the optimization problem
can be achieved by adjusting the input data without having to reprogram the model itself.
It should be noted, however, that the choice of modeling and solution approaches depends
on the specific scope of the study.
When considering models to optimize the invested capacity, it is common to use repre-
sentative years that map the characteristic features of the system under study (e.g., a
representative year of wind yield), as shown by [49], which examines the purchase of a
wind turbine at a small U.S. university. As illustrated, there are many different ways to
model energy system models. This thesis focuses on a linear optimization model using the
software SpineOpt. The advantages of SpineOpt are discussed in more detail in Section
4.1.

Hydropower plant projects and models
Different types of hydropower plants, such as run-of-river systems and those with reser-

the flexibility of hydropower generation. The dependence on head and turbine eﬀiciency
curves are among the physical and technical constraints that limit the production capa-
bilities of a run-of-river hydropower plant, and which can also be applied to hydropower
plants with reservoirs. It is concluded that a hydropower model that does not take these
constraints into account tends to overestimate the flexibility of hydropower and, conse-

quently, to overestimate its potential contribution to a renewable energy system.
According to [50], another notable challenge identified is the lack of comprehensive lit-

great interest to explore simplifications that can enable the implementation of a detailed,

As a summary, important factors need to be considered when modeling a hydropower
plant, e.g., the resolution and head height. A few of these factors are neglectable, because
of this project´s unique characteristics.

P2A used to be applied in combination with hydropower plants until the middle of the

gasification got more economical and caused the green ammonia plants to get decommis-

Examples of historical P2A plants are found in Norway, Egypt and Peru. One of the

called KIMA was 400 tNH3/day to 500 tNH3/day and it was decommissioned in 2019 [51].
Another example is the 1962 built renewable ammonia plant near Cuzco, Peru with a rated
electrolysis capacity of 18 MW. This leads to a total production of up to 41 tNH3/day
[52]. This plant is the last renewable ammonia plant in operation in 2023.

plant of its kind at such a large scale, reaching a GW capacity [55].
These three projects show that there is significant development in both technology (dy-
namic production) and scale (Gigawatt). It also implies that investigating the P2A pro-
duction for lake Tasersiaq with a scale of several hundred megawatts is reasonable. A
hydroelectric powered P2A plant of this scale has however not yet existed historically.

Market and transportation
Ammonia has many applications, with more than 70% of its production used for the
fertiliser industry [56]. The remaining share is used in various industries such as plastics
or synthetic fibres.
In addition, ammonia has the potential to serve as a low-carbon
energy source in the future. Promising developments are expected in the use of ammonia
as power, heat or transport fuel. The maritime sector in particular is one of the key areas
which is poised for significant market expansion. While current markets are expected to
contribute significantly to ammonia demand growth this decade mainly due to population
growth, energy markets are expected to show a much faster growth rate after 2030 [55].
The need to reduce the high CO2 emissions associated with conventional ammonia pro-
duction has underlined the growing importance of green ammonia. As CO2 prices rise,
the cost of traditionally produced ammonia is expected to increase. Consequently, green
ammonia has attracted increasing interest due to its potential to decarbonize various sec-
tors of existing ammonia applications and potential new applications, as mentioned earlier
for the maritime sector [55]. The maritime sector serves as an illustrative case in point.
Shipping activities currently account for approximately 3% of global CO2 emissions [57].
To address this challenge, ammonia emerges as a promising alternative marine fuel with
no current preferred propulsion technology still showing the associated uncertainty for
ammonia as a transportation fuel in the maritime industry [58]. The key drivers for a suc-

establishment of green ammonia production for trade and its development of appropriate
infrastructure incl. the availability of bunkering in ports [57]. Forecasts of the develop-

Data analysis
Data is a critical part of any energy systems model, as valid results can only be obtained on
the basis of high-quality input data. Therefore, data has to be critically evaluated before
feeding it to the model.
Careful documentation of all data collection and assessment
steps is an important part of an energy systems modeling process, especially for projects
with scarce and uncertain data. This chapter introduces a framework for qualitative data
assessment and summarises the applied data and the results of their assessment. The data
are summarised and described in the next sections.
3.1
Data quality assessment and validation of data
methodology
In the context of model validation, data validation is the process of verifying that a set of
values matches an acceptable and expected set of combinations. Data validation is essential
for building the conceptual model, validating the model, and performing experiments with
the validated model. Data validity is often diﬀicult, time-consuming, and costly to obtain,
and data problems can often hinder attempts to validate a model. Without validated
data, high model confidence cannot be obtained [44]. The main purpose of data validation
is to ensure a certain level of quality of the final data by assessing its plausibility and
identifying and preventing erroneous data to guarantee reliable model results. A positive
data validation result is not a guarantee of data accuracy, while a negative result indicates
erroneous data [61].
As part of the data validation process, the potential input data for the energy system
model are assessed against a qualitative framework.
This framework is based on the
qualitative and quantitative dimension of data quality presented in [62]. Therefore, the
data are mainly assessed in five qualitative categories:
1.
Timeliness: It is important to research, when the data was collected and published
to avoid using outdated data.
2.
Reliability: The correctness of the data is crucial. Therefore, the data are compared
with values found in the literature to allow an estimate of the uncertainty of the
data and to avoid outliers.
3.
Applicability: Since a renewable energy project in Greenland is exposed to a harsh
arctic setting and most likely remote location, the applicability of data to the project
needs to be assessed. The applicability under certain conditions can be rated.
4.
Transparency: A data source is transparent, if the underlying origin of the data or
the composition of the data is provided.
5.
Resolution: For time series data it is important to assess, if the available resolution
is suﬀicient to be applied to the project.
This framework is applied to the three categories of data described in the next chapters.
The three categories include hydrological data, hydropower construction data from the
PFS and technology data for the P2A system.

Evaluation
The data presented from Section 3.2 to 3.4 are evaluated qualitatively with the framework
given in Section 3.1. The main findings and resulting uncertainties regarding data are
summarized and discussed.
A general problem with the data from the PFS is its timeliness, as the cost calculations
are based on 2008 resource and labour costs, 15 years prior to this project’s evaluation in
this report. However, the data are used and adjusted for inflation as the PFS is the most
detailed and tailored data source for this hydropower construction. As the characteristics
of hydropower plants are very individual, comparing the data with data on other projects
or general hydropower plant data is very complex.
Hydrological data, especially hydropower discharge data, are only available at a monthly
resolution, which is just suﬀicient for this model to optimize the investments due to the
large reservoir capacity. Again, the timeliness of the discharge data is critical, as it does
not include data from 2008 to 2023 that could show trends and be used for improved
future forecasting. A high degree of reliability on the accuracy of the data is provided by
measuring runoff over several decades. The derivation of the 2020 runoff series based on
a synthetic series is described in detail and thus transparent in the PFS.
The technology data provided by the DEA has the advantage of being standardized,
comparable, complete and transparent. Its reliability is ensured through a comparison with
other sources collected in the literature review 2.1. This comparison shows an alignment
with other sources, with CAPEX estimates for the components being rather low. Due to
the large scale of this project, the lower electrolysis CAPEX is not considered as unrealistic,
especially because the data are given for a scale of 100 MW. Nitrogen synthesis and
storage data are partly taken from other sources. The maturity of the ASU technology
and comparably low CAPEX support the reliability and applicability of the data to this
project.
Concluding, the main finding from the data evaluation is that the main uncertainty is due
to scarcity of data on hydrology and the cost of the hydropower plant, since this project
has to rely on the slightly outdated PFS. Due to a large range of values for P2A technology
data provided by literature, it is important to assess the influence of uncertainty in the
parameters through a sensitivity analysis. It is decided to continue the project with AEC,
since according to the DEA the CAPEX of AEC is lower than for PEMEC in the 2030.

4.1
Choice of modeling tool
Various solutions for energy systems modelling software currently exist. Multiple criteria
are relevant for the decision making about the choice of a suitable modeling tool [48].
These criteria mainly are specialization and modelling capabilities. Another important
aspect, specifically for the academic community is, whether a tool is open source. In-
cluding uncertainties and details in long-term planning in models are current challenges,
that if successfully implemented, enable integration across energy sectors. This results in
increased model complexities that require advanced and flexible energy systems modeling
tools [46].
SpineOpt is a powerful open source modeling framework that includes the Spine Tool-
box and the Spine Model [48]. Based on Python, the Spine Toolbox allows users to define
energy systems using parameterization, input data management, and object-oriented visu-
alization capabilities. In addition, the Spine Model integrates seamlessly with the toolbox
and performs optimization tasks in a Julia environment. This comprehensive software
package provides the flexibility to create commodity flow models by combining modular
building blocks and setting constraints that link node and flow variables [48].
The Balmorel energy system model serves as a comparative example of a generic open-
source energy model that is commonly used in hydropower applications. However, it is
important to note that the Balmorel model is still based on GAMS, which is not open
source. Another commonly applied tool is HOMER, which can optimize operations in an
energy system, if the capacities are predefined. A concise comparison between SpineOpt
and other modeling tools is presented in Section 2.2.1 [48].
The choice of SpineOpt as a modeling tool is mainly due to its advantages, which are
based on [46] and will be briefly explained here. SpineOpt does not assume a specific type
of power system, but is adaptable for many applications. In particular, the generic data
and the fully flexible spatial and temporal structure are advantages over other modeling
tools. The flexible stochastic structure allows uncertain parameters to be accounted for
with scenario-based stochastic programming, which is a widely accepted method. Finally,
computational requirements are reduced by integrating the decomposition of investment
problems. In practice, the tool convinces with its graphical user interface for visual model-
ing and standardized output generation, which allows convenient analysis of output data.
A model file can continuously be extended with alternative parameters to create a range
of scenarios.
SpineOpt has been validated through several case studies in which the various functions
offered by SpineOpt are used. For example, [48] shows the automated handling of the
temporal resolution of the optimization model for the Skellefte river in Sweden as a single
system. Additionally, [47] developed a multi energy system model for the simulation of
hydropower plants in Sweden and Norway. It is also shown how different objectives for
hydropower generation, such as minimizing water discharge, affect the results.
Lastly, the use of SpineOpt may face some challenges, such as teething problems of the
fairly new tool and keeping the model runtime low. An incomplete documentation of all
functionality might cause further ambiguities. A detailed description of how SpineOpt is
applied is given in Section 4.2.1.

4.2
Approach for the model implementation
Figure 4.1: Overall energy systems modelling methodology for this project.
The overall approach to modelling energy systems is shown in Figure 4.1. It describes
the workflow that includes the phases from input data preparation through modelling to
output data analysis. The input for this workflow consists of the data presented in Section
3.
1. Data preparation in MS Excel: The data preparation process includes formatting
conversion rates, annualizing costs and other relevant variables to meet SpineOpt’s
input requirements.
In addition, a separate Excel spreadsheet is maintained to
provide a comprehensive overview of the data used.
2. Energy systems modeling with SpineOpt: SpineOpt, in conjunction with the Spine
Toolbox, serves as the primary modelling tool for the energy system. The model is
extended step-by-step through the iterative integration and validation of function-
alities.
3. Execution of the model on the DTU cluster: Due to the high computing demand,
the model is run on DTU’s Linux server. For example, the basic model requires 48
GB RAM and has a runtime of 2 hours, while the extended model requires 96 GB
RAM and has a runtime of 12 hours on the cluster.
4. Exporting standardized results using Spine Toolbox: The Spine Toolbox allows the
export of standardised output databases to .csv files.
These files can be further
specified and allow a thorough evaluation of the results obtained.
(a) Importing .csv files into PowerBI: Power BI, an open source tool, is used for
comprehensive analysis of the extensive data from the .csv output files. This
tool offers intuitive drag-and-drop functions that enable fast and customisable
analysis of operational behaviour and bug detection.
(b) Financial calculations using Excel: Excel is used to perform financial calcula-
tions based on input and output data. Different investment strategies can be
tested and key performance indicator (KPI), such as the LCOE and LCOA,
can be determined to inform decision makers.
Step 2 of the above-mentioned modelling approach includes the comprehensive consid-
eration of all essential and relevant components of the hydropower plant and the P2X
system. This approach follows the following methodology in which the model is refined
and extended through successive iterations.
1. Step-by-step implementation of a base model: The modelling process begins with
the step-by-step implementation of a “test” model, initially considering a short time
Power-to-X Technologies Using Hydropower in Greenland
25
==PAGE:38==
horizon and simplified input parameters. Individual functionalities are added and
validated iteratively.
2. Base model with realistic input data: Once the functionalities in the ”play” model
have been validated, they are implemented in the base model. The base model is
refined by the functionalities. It contains realistic input data and a time horizon
of one year. This allows an accurate representation of the system dynamics and
operational performance.
3. Model verification and validation: During the development of the base model, vig-
orous verification and validation procedures are applied to ensure the accuracy and
reliability of the model.
4. Sensitivity analysis: To gain insight further insights into the model, a sensitivity
analysis is performed.
In this analysis, the input parameters are systematically
varied to determine their impact on the model results.
5. Scenario derivation: Building on the base model, scenarios regarding the water inflow
are derived to explore different operational and external conditions.
6. Stochastic modeling based on scenarios: Stochastic modelling techniques are used to
account for inherent uncertainties and variability within the system. By integrating
probabilistic elements derived from the scenarios, a stochastic model is developed to
produce probabilistic forecasts and assess the resilience of the system to uncertain
conditions.
7. Creation of a decision basis: The results derived from the base model and the stochas-
tic scenarios serve as the basis for establishing a decision-making foundation. This

4.2.2
Investment methodology
In order to assess the financial feasibility of a project, it is crucial to discuss the applied
investment methodology. It should be mentioned at the outset that a general investment
strategy can become very complex and varies greatly depending on the company or project.
The objective of this section is to provide an overview of the investment methodology
applied, the KPIs used, and the importance of the various measures in assessing the
feasibility of a project. The investment methodology presented aims to answer the question
of whether the project is financially feasible.1
The literature often emphasises the significant impact of CAPEX on the LCOE.
For
renewable energy projects, CAPEX represent a significant portion of the project’s life cycle
costs. In many cases, differences in CAPEX, rather than differences in solar irradiance or
wind resources, are the primary cause of differences in LCOE across countries. Conversely,
fossil fuel electricity generation is less affected by CAPEX because a significant portion of
the life-cycle cost are fuel costs, which do not require upfront financing [65].
Especially in academia, researchers face the challenge of obtaining appropriate empirical
data because the financial information of many projects, often realized through project
financing structures, is confidential [65]. Therefore, the main objective of this report is
to show the relevance and impact of the applied investment methodology on the results.
While general recommendations in this thesis can be given based on the results, it is
important to note that no final decision can be made due to uncertainties and lack of
knowledge about, for example, a project developer’s access to the capital market.
The following measures are used to evaluate the feasibility of the project and allow its
comparability. Their equation is provided after presenting the definition of the measure.
In financial modelling, the discount factor is a decimal number used to discount future
cash flows to their present value.2 The discount factor is closely associated with the level
of risk associated with a company or project’s cash flows. This principle acknowledges
that the future value of 100 € is lower than its present value due to the consideration
of the opportunity cost associated with investing that 100 € immediately, which could
potentially result in additional returns over time. For instance, assuming an annual return
of 10%, 100 € today would be worth 110 € in the following year.
Because the study
focuses on an investment model from the perspective of a company, investor, or project
developer, it primarily uses financial analysis rather than economic analysis. Financial
analysis evaluates the profitability and financial viability of the investment, while economic
analysis considers broader social and environmental factors. The cost of capital is typically
higher in a financial analysis than in an economic analysis [66].
The cost of capital for a company is the opportunity cost for investors in a diversified
portfolio and represents the return required to justify the cost of a capital project. If
the ROI exceeds the return achievable on the open market, the investment is justified.

4.3.1
Verification of the basic model
Model verification and validation (V&V) is a crucial methodology that enables the devel-
opment of computational models capable of making engineering predictions with quan-
tifiable confidence. Verification is a critical process that ensures that the implementation
of a model accurately reflects the conceptual description of the model and the proposed
solution. The accuracy of models and their outcomes is of concern to various stakeholders,
including project developers and decision-makers. Validation, on the other hand, is the
process of determining the extent to which a model is an accurate representation of the
real world concerning the intended uses of the model. It is important to emphasize that
while V&V cannot demonstrate the correctness and accuracy of a model for all potential
situations, it can establish that the model is accurate enough for its intended purpose [69].
Furthermore, there is an extended approach called independent verification and validation
(IV&V) proposed by [44] that involves the participation of an independent third party to
evaluate the simulation model’s validity. Figure 4.3 shows the relationship between the
different steps of model validation.
The problem entity represents a real or proposed system, which is achieved through the
conceptual model that is the mathematical representation. The conceptual model is cre-
ated through an analysis and modelling phase. Then, the conceptual model is implemented
on a computer referred to as the computerized model. Furthermore, the process of con-
ceptual model validation involves ensuring that the underlying theories and assumptions
are correct. In contrast, the computerized model verification entails assuring the accuracy
of the programming and implementation of the conceptual model. The process of oper-
ational validation involves assessing whether the model’s output behaviour is suﬀiciently
accurate for the intended purpose, while data validity pertains to ensure that the data
used in the model is reliable, representative and accurate [44]. This process shall be seen
as iterative to ensure highest validation confidence. Documentation pertaining to model
verification and validation is typically crucial in persuading users regarding the accuracy
and reliability of a model and its outcomes, and therefore, it should be incorporated into
the simulation model documentation. The presented approach has been incorporated in
this study.
Verification of computerized models is essential to ensure the accuracy of both program-
ming and the implementation of the conceptual model. The utilization of a specialized
simulation language is recommended as it typically results in fewer errors compared to a
general-purpose simulation language [44]. It is noteworthy that SpineOpt has undergone a
comprehensive process of verification and validation. This involved comparing its perfor-
mance with other simulation tools through the examination of several case studies, where
the outcomes were critically assessed and compared, resulting in statistically significant
outcomes that were found to be similar [46, 48, 47].
Several techniques can be employed to ensure the verification of a computerized model.
The following approaches proposed by [70] have been used to verify and validate the model:
use of general good programming practices such as modular programming, iterative imple-
mentation approaches, documentation to facilitate traceability and checking intermediate
and final simulation outputs through tracing. In addition, animation in form of PowerBI
plots have been utilized to verify the dynamic system.
One of the most effective techniques employed in this study is extreme testing. Several
tests were conducted to assess the model’s resilience by subjecting it to individual extreme
values. These tests focused on various parameters, including water inflow, CAPEX of
P2A components, CAPEX of the construction, eﬀiciencies of system components and a

4.3.3
Sensitivity analysis of parameters of the basic model
The results of the previous sections in the base case are susceptible to uncertainties aris-
ing from the underlying assumptions and data used. This section attempts to measure
sensitivities to facilitate decision making and highlight data points that, if qualitatively
improved, would provide more reliable and robust results. The sensitivity analysis per-
formed at any time takes the base case as a reference point, with the center of the Figures
4.4 and 4.5 representing the value used in the base case.
Sensitivity analysis and model validation are two interrelated methods for evaluating the
effectiveness of a model specification and establishing the robustness of the derived con-
clusions. While model validation assesses the adequacy of the model for a given dataset,
sensitivity analysis is an essential tool for gaining confidence in the primary results of
the analysis to derive conclusions for e.g., decision-makers. Sensitivity analysis pertains
to a set of techniques utilized to quantify the correlation between the uncertainty in a
36

model’s output and the variability of its input parameters. Essentially, sensitivity analy-
sis examines the degree to which a model is responsive to changes in its constituent data,
parameters and assumptions. Such an analysis can prove significant in various phases of
the modelling process, including but not limited to identifying model errors, facilitating
the calibration of model parameters, and providing insight into the broader association
between the model inputs and outputs. The fundamental principles that form the basis of
a sensitivity analysis encompass three key aspects. Firstly, the analysis aims to facilitate
the quantification of the inherent level of uncertainty present in a given model. Secondly,
a secondary experimental design is employed to enable investigators to test the model of
interest. Finally, the outcomes derived from the secondary experimental design are then
utilized to gauge the overall sensitivity of the model of interest. By adhering to these
underlying principles, researchers can gain valuable insights into the effectiveness and ro-
bustness of a model’s performance, allowing them to refine their findings and improve the
accuracy of their predictions. If validation and sensitivity analyses yield unsatisfactory
results, the model needs do be revised or a new one developed [44]. To enhance the com-
prehensiveness of the analysis, [61] proposes exploring the interplay between inputs by
not only scrutinizing the effect of a single parameter, but also varying two, three, or more
inputs simultaneously, with the aim of estimating potential interactions among them. Vi-
sualizations shall be used to examine the results. All above-presented techniques have
been successfully applied.

4.4
Extended model
The basic model could be extended by various capabilities.
This section explores two
potential approaches that could extend the functionality of the model: incorporating un-
certainty through stochastic programming and converting it into MIP model. One way to
convert the linear model into a MIP model is, for example, to include the exact scaling
of the components through the investment in stacks or the consideration of economies of
scale in the scaling of the components of the P2A system. Due to the large scale of the
plant and the scarcity of data for such large systems, an implementation does not seem
reasonable, particularly because of the individualised nature of the Haber-Bosch plant.
Furthermore, the capture of uncertainties with regard to the intended application of the
model gains in importance and relevance. Consequently, it is imperative to develop an
extended model that adequately captures the uncertainty.
Stochastic programming is an established approach used to model optimization problems
that incorporate uncertainty. While deterministic optimization problems rely on known
and unchanging parameters, the majority of real-world problems involve variables that are
unknown at the time a decision must be made. This inherent uncertainty poses a challenge
in finding optimal solutions [71]. In this study, three scenarios that introduce uncertainty
into the optimization model in terms of water inflow, namely dry, normal, and wet years,
are encountered, each following a distribution range of 0% to 50% for dry and wet, and
0% to 100% for normal .
The use of a two-stage stochastic programme with recourse provides a structured frame-
work that facilitates sequential and adaptive decision-making in the presence of uncer-
tainty. The underlying principle of two-stage stochastic programming is that (optimal)
decisions should be based on the data available at the time of decision-making, without
relying on future observations. In the context of the project under study, the consider-
ation of a two-stage stochastic programme with recourse is a possible approach. In the
first stage, non-anticipatory decisions are made without knowledge of the values of the un-
certain parameters. These decisions are formulated solely on the basis of the information
available at that time, regardless of the knowledge of the random variables. Subsequently,
42
Power-to-X Technologies Using Hydropower in Greenland
==PAGE:55==
in the second phase, recourse decisions can be made once the randomness of the uncertain
parameters has been established. These recourse decisions aim to correct any sub-optimal
outcomes resulting from the decisions made in the first phase. By incorporating the ob-
served randomness, adjustments can be made to improve the overall effectiveness and
performance of the decision-making process [71].
However, the consideration of two-stage stochastic programming is not pursued in this
study. That is mainly due to the reason of computational complexity when running a
sequence of years. The applied methodology in this study focused solely on the distribution
of dry/wet/normal years, which has been implemented as a stochastic tree within the
SpineOpt framework. This allows to consider the probabilistic nature (distribution) of
the uncertain parameters (water inflow) and their potential impact on the optimization
process (scaling). But this methodology solely focuses on the overall distribution without
incorporating the reservoir’s capacity to function as a large-scale battery over multiple
years. Consequently, the here-and-now-solution will deliver the optimal scale of the system
considering the distribution of the dry, normal and wet years. It is not seen as value-
adding to calculate the wait-and-see solution. This type solves each scenario separately
and takes the expected value of their respective optimal objective values. This approach
is unrealistic as it determines the optimal scale for each scenario individually and then
computes a weighted average [71]. However, this would require the assumption that there
is always enough water in the reservoir and that the water inflow corresponds to the
weighted average. This approach does not take into account the sequence of years, e.g.,
consecutive dry or wet years, which can lead to different results than alternating wet and
dry years. Therefore, this method is not comparable to the here-and-now solution.

Discussion
The following sections discuss the energy systems modeling approach and contextualize
the project’s sustainable impact, generalizability, and global significance.
5.1
Energy systems modelling approach
In the following, the applied modelling approach for energy systems is discussed. Assump-
tions are discussed in both the Sections 5.1.2 and 5.1.3, some concerning the methodology
and others covering the uncertainties and risks arising from the methodology.
5.1.1
Limitations of the modelling tool SpineOpt
The SpineOpt platform has remarkable strengths, as already explored in Section 4.1.
Nevertheless, due to the early stage of development and the regular monthly inclusion of
additional features, there are certain weaknesses and initial challenges associated with its
use.
The possibilities of direct output analysis within SpineOpt are considerably limited. To
address this limitation, Excel models and PowerBI have been used to automate the analysis
process as much as possible by using the standardized output function from SpineOpt.
In addition, the rapid growth of the tool has resulted in incomplete and/or outdated
documentation. However, monthly meetings and the use of GitHub have proven valuable
in providing timely support, with response times of no more than four days.
Furthermore, the use of open source solvers leads to longer calculation times. For example,
using JuMP instead of Gurobi, the extended model required more than 72 hours to find
a solution, while Gurobi solved the problem within 24 hours on the DTU cluster. Based
on the experience, it is recommended to run the model due to its complexity and time
resolution on a cluster or server to meet the computational demands.
Moreover, modelling non-linear physical relationships is a major challenge. For example,
capturing the non-linear relationship between dam height and reservoir volume is not
feasible within a single unit given the inherent structure of SpineOpt. However, a feasible
approach is a step-wise implementation with multiple units and nodes, which has been
successfully demonstrated in the applied model. Furthermore, as a relatively new tool,
SpineOpt encounters certain smaller problems. An illustrative example is the inability to
apply OPEX directly to invested units.
In summary, SpineOpt’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses, especially when compared to
alternative modelling software solutions as discussed in Section 4.1.
5.1.2
Applied methodology
When discussing the data collection, it has proven advantageous to utilize fully stan-
dardized data obtained from a single source, namely the DEA. This approach ensures
comparability, as the DEA data were frequently used in previous studies. In addition,
the data obtained is consistent with the results of other scientific work. The financial
modeling perspective has been adopted from the standpoint of project developers and
investors. However, it is worth noting that the parameters can be easily adjusted within
the SpineOpt framework if another point of view is of interest. Given the available data
and its inherent uncertainty, the results obtained can only serve as a recommendation
rather than for definitive decision-making. In the context of hydropower modelling, it is
Power-to-X Technologies Using Hydropower in Greenland
47
==PAGE:60==
important to recognise that alternative methods may not have access to a comprehensive
PFS. The PFS provides important data on the hydropower plant and construction costs,
which are of utmost importance due to the unique characteristics and cost estimates of
hydropower projects.
The modeling approach is backed-up by literature. The performed data preparation has
been carried out similar to other projects as presented in [46]. Commonly used methods
such as model verification and validation, as well as an iterative implementation approach
have been applied as commonly done in the literature. In addition, annualized data and
representative inflow data for a given year were used, similar to the approach taken by [49]
in assessing the feasibility of a wind turbine generator based on its annual energy yield.
This method has the advantage that the model horizon can be reduced to a single year,
as opposed to several years. However, it should be noted that this approach does not take
into account temporal variations or stochastic considerations relevant for this project, such
as the specific sequence of different inflow conditions in the form of dry, normal and wet
years. Like [46], case studies from the literature include a sensitivity analysis to assess
the impact of parameter changes. However, not all of them include uncertainty analysis
through stochastic modelling.
In this report, uncertainty has been addressed through
scenario-based approaches that allow a comprehensive examination of potential outcomes
under different conditions.
The investment methodology is particularly sensitive to WACC and the distribution
of upfront investment costs, which is also shown in [49].
A key difference with other
studies is that the general methodology in academia considers upfront investment costs as
”overnight” costs, i.e., construction time is not considered. This is an oversimplification
and has significant implications for project feasibility. However, the methodology presented
is still based on fundamental assumptions such as unlimited access to the capital market,
unchanged WACC, etc., which may also be an oversimplification. It has been shown that
the project is feasible from the project developer’s point of view.
However, the exact data input and/or cannot be given due to confidential information
that are not shared by the industry.
Further, the issue of fixed costs for developing
larger hydropower plant than proposed in the PFS might need to be revised. They were
considered as fixed based on the estimated cost of the PFS, but not taking into account
the scale deviations of the electro-mechanical components.

•
The flexibility of the hydropower plant and electrolyser, influenced by stack consid-

the same degree of flexibility, especially in arctic conditions, and the consequences

•
The inflation-adjusted CAPEX resulting from the PFS may not accurately reflect
the real situation.

Uncertainty in the capital market as the financial feasibility of the project remains

•
Uncertainty in market development, especially in relation to factors such as the price

•
Uncertainty regarding inflow, with the availability of time series data limited to a
monthly basis based on the PFS. Current data would be valuable for monitoring
trends related to increased inflow due to global warming and ice melt.

The PFS identifies five main risks that could lead to delays and CAPEX escalation.

project [63]. These are as follows:
–
Challenges in building the infrastructure and logistics.
–
Challenges during construction works.
–
Unfavourable weather conditions, including seasonal change. While material

Risks associated with the transmission line, including crossing fjords and glaciers

Environmental concerns, such as possible delays by non-governmental organ-

The decision to invest in a larger scale beyond 30% wet years raises questions regarding

are no estimations in the PFS.

Decision framework proposal
One of the objectives of this report is to provide a decision-making framework for the fea-

namely financial feasibility, sustainability and realisability. The focus of the model used
in this study is primarily on the assessment of financial feasibility. Detailed descriptions
of the methods used can be found in Sections 3 and 4. The aspect of sustainability, on the
other hand, was analysed in the previous Section 5.2, where a corresponding framework
is presented.
In addition, the aspect of realisability emerges as a newly identified component that is seen
to be of great importance. It encompasses several factors, such as the recognition that even
if the project has favourable outcomes in terms of financial and sustainable feasibility, it
may still face barriers to realisation. These challenges could arise from deficiencies within
the P2A component supply chain, as highlighted by [80], or from a lack of skilled labour for
construction. It is critical to consider these potential constraints to ensure a comprehensive
assessment of the feasibility of the project.

5.4
Generalizability
This section addresses the generalizability of methods, the resulting model and the results
of this report. The model itself represents a generalized reservoir hydropower plant to
which an off-grid P2A system is connected. Its versatility allows for convenient application
to similar systems by adjusting parameters such as water inflow and conversion rates.
However, to connect the model to a grid, an implementation in SpineOpt would still
be necessary. The applicability to other projects is also achieved through accurate data
processing in the beginning, comprehensive sensitivity analysis, and iteration between

all phases. Generally the main sensitivities found in the sensitivity analysis need to be
carefully considered when applying the model to other projects. However, the study’s
heavy reliance on the PFS compromises direct transferability to other projects and the
accuracy of financial results due to the detailed cost estimation in the PFS.
The modelling tool and the applied methodology is generalizable where starting with a
qualitative input data validation is an important step. In addition, the methodology of
iterative testing the model through validation, verification and sensitivity analysis is highly
applicable. SpineOpt allows an abstract implementation of a commodity flow model, i.e.,
no specific function for a hydropower reservoir is necessary and the nodes can represent
any type of storage, which makes the tool highly flexible and applicable.
The results and particularities of the project are less generalizable than the model and
methods. The case studies results are highly influenced by the uniqueness of the project
and the off-grid location. Although the results can be considered accurate due to the
comprehensive applied methodology, the generalizability of the results is diﬀicult, due to
the uniqueness of the hydropower plant with its large storage volume, comparably large
head height and seasonal inflow. Results of the P2A system are more generalizable, because
it represents a state of the art system for the year 2030 and thus could be used for other
studies. Due to the projects unique characteristics such as the off-grid location and its
continuous operations, the results do not include a unit commitment problem, which also
means that a grid connection would be likely affect the realizability of a similar project.
The resulting profitability of the project is highly dependent on the WACC, which also
means that the feasibility of the project could be affected if applied to projects in different
countries with different access to capital markets. In this study, the possible costs caused
by the particular location were taken into account by including an arctic factor in CAPEX
of the P2A components.
All in all, the generalizability of the applied methodology, the model and SpineOpt as a
modeling framework is high. In contrast, the generalizability of the results is limited due
to the uniqueness of the hydropower plant.
5.5
Global significance of the project
As a final part of the discussion, the global significance of this project is evaluated. The
significance as a hydropower plant is particularly high due to its unique characteristics.
Compared to other hydropower plants, the storage volume is very large in relation to the
dam height and length due to the georaphic conditions of lake Tasersiaq. In addition,
the large head results in a higher energy production compared to hydropower plants with
similar discharge. The resulting competitive LCOE makes the project significant, even
though it is remote, off-grid, and located in the arctic region.
The project would also be the largest facility to date to combine hydropower and P2X
and would also be one of the largest P2X projects at this scale. Its realization would
demonstrate that a P2X or P2A project is feasible in the arctic region and that hydropower
still plays a competitive role within renewables. For the arctic region, a realization would
demonstrate the feasibility of renewable energy projects with an energy storage option
through P2X in a region with low energy demand and population density.
The ammonia production of 400,000 to 600,000 t/year make a minor contribution to the
current global ammonia production of 183 Mt/year (2020), which is expected to continue
to increase further until 2050 [55]. In terms of export markets, the ammonia produced
could currently account for about 10% of the total imported ammonia in Europe and

Reflection and outlook
This section includes a critical analysis of the project work and provides an outlook on
potential further research. The examination of the methodology is integrated into Section
5.
Firstly, it is important to acknowledge that the quality modelling options and of output
data improves as the amount of available data increases. In addition, it is essential to
ensure that the available data match the desired level of detail in the model. In other
words, including characteristics for which data are not available would not be beneficial.
The presence of a collaborative partner would have facilitated access to a wider range
of data, allowing for more diverse modelling options. For example, it would have been
possible to carry out piecewise investments in system components using an MIP approach,
but due to the lack of data in academia this was not possible. Such cooperation existed
at the beginning of the project, but was then terminated for various reasons. As a result,
access to data has been limited and there is an increasing reliance on potentially outdated
information, such as the PFS.
Secondly, increased computational power plays a crucial role in developing an ideal model.
Ideally, the optimization of a 25-year period with random wet, dry, or normal inflows,
without cyclic conditions, would be pursued to optimize both the scale of the dam height
and the hydropower and P2A system. This limitation showcases the need for reasonable
assumptions that have been taken.
If the project was to be restarted, certain adjustments in approach would be warranted.
Specifically, greater emphasis would be placed on the minimum viable product (MVP) and
initial outcomes, rather than becoming entrenched in less significant issues. For instance,
concerns about potential energy as a function of head height could have been minimised
at the outset. As it turned out, there were other features that had a greater impact on
the outcome. Therefore, it is essential to set the right priorities to address the significant
challenges.
In addition, having experience and knowledge of using (industry-specific) software such
as Excel and Power BI in an academic context has proven to be extremely beneficial.
These software applications have proven their usefulness in a variety of analytical tasks,
including sensitivity analysis and financial modelling.
Finally, the implementation of the relatively new SpineOpt framework was a success.
The framework was successfully adopted, underlining its practicality and effectiveness in
real-world applications. Of particular importance is the integration of stochasticity into
operational processes, which is an innovative approach and, to the authors knowledge, has
not been practised before in SpineOpt.
Further research opportunities can be explored in several areas. First, an in-depth environ-
mental study in the form of an environmental impact assessment, including a comprehen-
sive assessment of the carbon footprint associated with the construction of the system, is
worth exploring. Secondly, there is the possibility of reducing the complexity and changing
the temporal resolution of the model in SpineOpt to increase its eﬀiciency. Additionally,
gathering additional information regarding the cost implications of dam height can aid in
implementing a stepwise investment strategy for optimizing the reservoir volume.

Conclusion
In summary, this research paper aimed to investigate the feasibility of a P2A system, fo-
cusing on the case study of hydropower resource 7e lake Tasersiaq in Greenland. The main
objective was to evaluate the feasibility and determine the optimal size and configuration
of the energy system. This was achieved through a thorough review of the literature, col-
lection and analysis of data, modeling of the energy system with SpineOpt, and in-depth
discussion of the results.
The initial literature review and data analysis provided crucial inputs for the optimization
model in the SpineOpt framework. SpineOpt was deemed a suitable choice due to its ad-
vantageous features, including a graphical user interface and a flexible stochastic structure.
In addition, the ability to generate standardised outputs allowed for convenient analysis
of the output data in Power BI and Excel. The basic model revealed that the most im-
portant drivers of the system are the CAPEX, the availability factor and eﬀiciency of the
components, the water inflow, and the chosen investment methodology. To incorporate
the uncertainty of future water inflows, different scenarios were derived from the PFS and
applied in a stochastic model. It is noteworthy that the resulting invested capacities have
two different scales; The model investing in deterministic normal year capacities for wet
year shares below 30% (hydropower plant capacity 670 MW) and the model investing in
deterministic wet year capacities (hydropower plant capacity 862 MW) for wet year shares
of 30% or more. While there is a gap between 20% and 30% indicating increasing LCOA
for larger investments, the corresponding larger absolute profit gain outweights the LCOA
differences. Consequently, LCOA alone is not a comprehensive indicator for comparing
energy systems.
With regard to the obtained results, several important findings have emerged. Firstly, the
optimal model results indicated that intermediate storages for hydrogen and nitrogen are
not recommend, as the water reservoir itself serves as a flexible storage system. Therefore,
investments in additional storages cannot be triggered based on maintenance intervals and
market prices. Secondly, the optimal setup revealed a preference for constant operations
to ensure a high load factor. This means that deviations from constant operations can
only be justified by extreme differences in ammonia market prices, as retaining water in
the reservoir until a high market price is reached becomes a compelling reason. Thirdly,
the LCOA is heavily dependent on financial assumptions and WACC, yet even with a high
WACC of up to 11.28% in the basic model with a market price of 500 €/t3, the financial
viability of the project is demonstrated. Overall, the financial profitability, as measured
by the ROI significantly relies on the chosen investment methodology. In addition, the
influence of uncertainty in the CAPEX of the P2A system components and the ammonia
market price is primarily observed in the ROI and its threshold for different WACC. These
factors can impact the financial performance of the project, highlighting the importance
of robust financial planning and risk management.
To incorporate the goal of the tender process to create benefits for the region and the
greenlandic society, the project impact is evaluated through a triple bottom line impact
assessment, that reveals potential benefits in social, economic and environmental aspects.
However, it is important to note that further evaluation and consideration of these factors
is warranted. Lastly, the financial feasibility of the project is affected by the volatility and
uncertainty of future water inflows. Despite this challenge, the project remains financially
58

viable, even if the share of dry years exceeds 50%. With that said, from a project devel-
oper’s perspective, it is crucial to compare the project against other ventures within their
portfolio, as alternative projects may offer higher ROI.
Going forward, there are several important considerations for future research and analysis
as discussed in Section 6. Although this study focused primarily on a techno-economic
assessment rather than an in-depth environmental study, for example in the form of an
environmental impact assessment, it is crucial that such assessments are carried out to fully
evaluate the environmental impact of the project. Furthermore, it is worth considering
the utilization of the model results to run simulations over multiple years to offer a more
robust evaluation of the system’s performance and potential. This approach can provide
valuable insights to validate the scaling parameters and determine the optimal dam height
and volume without relying on the cyclic condition, i.e., having the same water level at
the beginning and end of the optimized, representative year. To build a comprehensive
business case and assess the financial feasibility of the project in more detail, it is advisable
to conduct an analysis that includes different investment methods.
Exploring various
investment methods can provide a more comprehensive understanding of the financial
implications, risks and potential returns associated with the project. This analysis can
help decision-makers make informed decisions and optimize the financial feasibility of the
proposed system.
In summary, this report demonstrates the eﬀiciency of SpineOpt as a powerful optimi-
sation framework for conducting a techno-economic assessment of an off grid renewable
energy system and confirming the feasibility of harnessing hydropower resource 7e in West
Greenland with a connected P2A system, contributing to the achievement of SDG 3 (Good
health and wellbeing), SDG 4 (Good education), SDG 7 (Affordable and clean energy),
SDG 9 (Industry, innovation and infrastructure), SDG 13 (Climate action), SDG 15 (Life
on land) and SDG 17 (Partnerships for the goals) in case of project implementation.

A.2
Assumptions for the basic model and the scenarios
Technological assumptions
1. Location of dams, tunnels and other infrastructure from the feasability study are
still applicable in 2030.
2. Eﬀiciency of turbines and generators of the hydropower plant from the PFS can be
used for the model.
3. The headheight is fixed to the minimum reservoir level of 680m, this means the
worst-case of potential energy is implemented in the model and the potential energy
of the water is calculated from the lowest possible reservoir operating point.
4. The losses in the transmission line are approximated to the feasability study, that
states approximately 95,8% eﬀiciency [63].
5. The electricity distribution at the P2A site does not cause any losses.
6. No diffusion or losses of any fluids in the system.
7. The tunnel causes a 1% energy loss, based on the stated headheight loss in the
feasability study [63].
8. AEC are used for the model.
9. Fresh water for the electrolysis can be taken from a lake on the Maniitsoq pensinsula
10. The heating value of hydrogen is assumed to be 33,33 MWh/t and the heating value
of ammonia is assumed to be 5,22 MWh/t
11. System boundaries: modeling the hydropower plant and P2A system, that means
from water inflow to ammonia output.
12. A constant eﬀiciency is assumed for all components.
Operational assumptions
1. Temporal resolution of the model: representative year with an hourly resolution.
2. The year starts with the wet season on the 1st July with an empty reservoir and
ends on the 30th June the next year with an empty reservoir (cyclic condition).
3. The minimal reservoir water level of 680m stated in the feasability study is used
for the state empty, as this is the lowest level, where a flow of water through the
reservoir is possible.
4. The water inflow to the reservoir in the model equals the measured reservoir outflows
from the feasability study. Historical data of the observed discharge 1970-2008 is
used for the validation and sensitivity analysis of the model.
5. The minimal reservoir level is used as the headheight for the model. No increase of
potential energy through a raised reservoir level is calculate in because of constraints
in SpineOpt and minor impact on the potential energy of the stored water.
6. The system operates entirely off-grid and is independent from external influences
that could occur in a grid connected environment.
7. Ramp up and ramp down are neglected because of the flexibility given through the
hydropower plant, electrolysis and the data scarcity for Haber-Bosch synthesis of
similar scale.
66

8. The fixed operation and maintenance cost (FOM Cost) in SpineOpt cannot be ap-
plied to units, where the capacity is optimized by the model. Therefore, this cost is
added to the CAPEX of each unit when entering into the model.
9. The system operates entirely off-grid and is independent from external influences
that could occur in a grid connected environment.
Financial assumptions
1. All assumptions regarding the CAPEX and OPEX of the applied technologies, as
well as the investment model (see Table 4.1).
2. The fix cost for the reservoir and infrastructure are given by the feasablity study
and the model optimizes the reservoir capacity without concrete cost assigned to
it. This assumption has to be taken because of data scarcity in regards to the cost
of dam height. (this is later verified by the fact that the FS volume is larger than
necessary). Possible variable cost for hydropower turbines can be neglected, because
it sums up to less than 20% of the total construction cost.
3. Cost for P2X components are taken from the DEA.
4. The following investment methodology is applied to the base model.
5. The investment horizon of 25 years is equivalent to the lowest lifetime of components
in the system.
6. The WACC is set to 6%.
7. All costs are given in €2015.
8. The cost input into the model are constant annuities.
9. An arctic factor of +10% is applied to all Power-to-Ammonia component cost.
10. The O&M cost for the ASU and H2 compression units are assumed to be 2%.
11. The lifetime of all components in the system exceeds the time in operation of the
entire plant if not stated differently in table 3.4.
12. The data for total plant sizes of 100MW given by the DEA is also applicable to
larger plants up to 500MW.
13. The ammonia market price is 500 Euros.
