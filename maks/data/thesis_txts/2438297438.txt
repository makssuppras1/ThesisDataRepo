Abstract 
This project covers theoretical and experimental investigations of the influence of torsional 
vibrations and drill length on the tool life in a drilling operation. Alongside this it is examined if 
there is a relation between the length of the drill and the torsional vibrations.  
The torsional vibrations are examined, as it is believed that they cause a variation in cutting 
speed, and thereby can have a large impact on the tool life. 
The experimental investigation is carried out by doing a series of tool life tests on a Cincinnati 
Sabre 750, and after every 50th hole, the wear is measured, and a singular hole is drilled for force 
and torque measurements, using a Montronix AT100 dynamometer. It is not done more often than 
that, since the collet chuck of the Montronix dynamometer has a very large runout, and this could 
risk ruining the lifetime tests, due to an unstable drilling operation. 
 
For tool life, the following two criteria were set: 
 
• 
Maximum cutting length: 8 m 
• 
Critical failure by breakage or unable to cut further 
Once these were reached, the tool life was considered to be over. 
The torsional vibrations are modelled based on torque measurements during the drilling operation, 
and the torque is then related to the rotational speed of the drill, and then the variation in cutting 
speed during the operation is examined, and related to the tool life. 
The results are inconclusive, as the torsional vibrations are present, and do seem to have an 
influence on the cutting speed, but the tool life in this case seems to be much more influenced by 
other factors, such as runout and too long drills that cause bending during drilling. Further work 
is possible with the short drills of a length 93 mm to reach a clearer conclusion. 
Even though the results are inconclusive, there is an indication that the torsional vibrations do 
have an influence on tool life, however it is so small that for conventional drilling it can be ignored.

1. Introduction 
Material removal processes like turning, milling or drilling have a workpiece and a tool which cuts 
the workpiece (sometimes multiple tools can work at the same time on the workpiece). During 
the material removal process the workpiece and tool experience different forces throughout whole 
process, and these forces influence the quality of the finish product and on the tool life. In order 
to improve the tool life lubrication can be applied during the process and it can additionally also 
have an effect on the quality of the finished product. However, the use of a cooling fluid and 
lubrication is expensive compared to just tool cost. Lubrication and cooling fluids are estimated 
to be 7 – 17 % of the manufacturing cost compared to the tool cost, which is 2 – 4 % [1]. In addition 
to high manufacturing cost, the handling of the cooling fluid and lubrication require special waste 
management since it is toxic waste. These reasons make optimizing the process that much more 
important, as it can either reduce the cost, or increase the tool life of each drill.      
In industrial drilling operations, just as in any other machining operation, there are many 
noticeable factors, which influence the tool life of each drill. These factors, such as material 
selection, choice of lubrication, or the length of the drill can have a large impact on tool life [2]. 
These noticeable factors can be changed and examined individually, as the combination of each 
of these can create rather large differences in the quality of the drilling operation, and the tool 
life of the tool. 
However, other less visible influences, which also have some impact on the drilling operation, 
such as vibrations, also exist. It is well known both in industrial drilling and in oil well drilling, 
vibrations can have an impact on the tool life and quality of the drilling operation [3]. Despite the 
influence the vibrations have on the tool life of the drill, there is not much available research on 
modelling the actual vibrations, but rather a focus on dampening the vibrations [4], in order to 
reduce its influence. There are some attempts at modelling the operations, however mostly in oil 
well drilling [5]. However, due to the large difference between oil well drilling and industrial 
drilling in metals, it is unclear if the vibration models for oil well drilling can be used when scaled 
down to industrial drills with a much smaller aspect ratio. 
During this project, the goal is to examine how the length of the drill affects the tool life of the 
drill, and alongside to also examine how the vibrations affect the tool life of the drill. With a lot 
of different types of vibrations, the focus area in this project will be on how the torsional 
vibrations affect the actual speed of the drill, which has been proven to have a strong impact on 
the tool life of the drill [23], regardless of any other factors. 
==PAGE:10==
 
2 
 
How the length and vibrations impact the tool life of the drill will be modelled during the project, 
and the models will then be verified by a series of tests, which will attempt to reduce the impact 
of all the other parameters, such as material choice or lubrication, by keeping these factors under 
control, while examining the effect of the variations of the length of the tool, and how the 
vibrations act on the tool and workpiece during machining. 
1.1. 
Project Goal 
The goal of the project is to conduct a theoretical and experimental investigation on the influence 
of torsional vibrations on the tool life in drilling. The focus on torsional vibration is due to the 
possibility that the torsional vibrations cause an increase in cutting speed, which theoretically 
should greatly increase tool wear. A theoretical model will be produced for the torsional vibrations 
on the twist drill, and a relation between the torsional vibrations and the drill length will also be 
examined. 
An experimental investigation will be carried out, by doing a series of tool life test, with drills of 
different lengths, and the wear and torsional vibrations will be measured at a determined interval, 
tool life criterions will be set based on the researched literature, and once a tool life criterion is 
reached the tool will be considered to have reached its end of life. 
1.2. 
Limitations 
This project has been dedicated a finite amount of resources from DTU to make the project 
possible. Restrictions to the project are necessary due the limit of resources available such as 
time, manpower and available material. 
 
It was necessary to limit the amount of drilling due to the cost of the drills and workpieces. The

plates. 
In terms of manpower and time, drilling was time-restricted as operating the machine required

2. Theory/Literature Study 
2.1. 
Drilling operation 
A common way to make holes in a material is done in two ways: punching or drilling. The latter is 
used the most in the hole making process when manufacturing holes. A large selection of drilling 
operations exists, such as conventional drilling (CD), counter boring, reaming, tapping etc. These 
operations have different types of drills associated to the operations, and for CD operations a twist 
drill is the most commonly used type of drill. 
 
Figure 1 shows the geometry and important characteristics for a twist drill. 
 
 
Figure 1. A sketch of the geometry of a twist drill can be seen and the different parts are marked 
and named [7] 
 
Each drilling operations serve a specific purpose. First a hole has to be drilled, afterwards a few 
different options are available such as a core drilling which will make the existing hole larger or a 
counterboring and countersinking which can be used to shape the top of the hole to adjust it for 
screws or bolts. Another option for making a hole could be the step drilling operation which 
manufactures a hole with two or more different diameters during one operation.

Depending on the hole design and purpose it might need an addition operation to fulfil it. The hole 
might need to be within certain diameter or must have a specific roughness tolerance, and to 
manufacture such a hole a reaming process can be used to make the hole the designated tolerance 
size and to give a finer hole surface quality. Often the object, that the hole has been drilled into, 
might have to be mounted or connected to another object, a mounting method could be done by 
using bolts. A tapping operation can be performed to make sure that the bolts are tightly secured 
into the object., This is done by cutting an internal thread into the workpiece so a bolt can be 
screwed into the workpiece and hold it together. Figure 2 above illustrate the different drills and 
the operations they are used in.

drill press is used for making multiple holes on a workpiece. Figure 3 above is an illustration of a

material and tool material are similar, adhesives wear can happen as well.  On Figure 4 below an

The drill, when it is worn down, can be honed to somewhat regain its sharp cutting edges, but it 
can be less effective when dealing with tools which have been hard coated. Cutting conditions 
also have an effect on the tools life and by adjusting and optimizing the cutting condition an 
increase in tool life can be achieved, these conditions are primarily cutting speed and feed rate 
which are exponentially correlated. 
Another way to improve the tool life and quality of the drilled hole can be done by using lubrication 
or coolant fluid to combat the rising temperature [9] that occurs during the drilling of a series of 
holes. It can also help increase the number of holes each drill can make in the operation before it 
needs to be changed [10]. The use of lubrication is an attractive subject of investigation, since 
current political interest demand more lubricates which are less harmful for the environment, and 
because the manufacturing cost of adding lubrication is rather high [7]. Therefore, new 
manufacturing methods are being tested, and older methods are being optimised to avoid or 
reduce the use of lubrication. 
When a hole needs to be drilled many parameters need to be taken into consideration, such as 
work piece material, the drill geometry and the machining parameters. The machining parameters 
have an importance for the tool life and quality of the hole, and to decide the best parameters 
and to estimate the optimal cutting speed and tool life, a Taylor curve can be used. Figure 6 below 
shows a taylor curve for a single point cutting tool and how the cutting speed has an enormous 
effect on tool life time. Even though figure 6 is for a single point cutting tool there are still many 
physical aspects that are the same for the twist drills and many comparisons can be drawn between 
the two.

2.2. 
Modelling of vibrations 
When considering the drilling operation, three types of vibrations are often considered: lateral, 
torsional and axial vibrations. Where lateral and axial vibrations have a more visible effect, as it 
can clearly be seen, if there is a change in the hole diameter or length, the less visible torsional 
vibrations can have a much larger impact, on both tool and machine lifetime. 
There is a general consensus that torsional vibrations have an impact on the tool life, and the 
approach to handling these vibrations is similar in most cases, as the vibrations are considered 
occurring near the natural frequency of torsional vibrations. This is seen in research done by 
Ahmadi and Altintas [3] and by Ma et al. [11], where the natural frequency of the torsional 
vibrations is modelled. 
Zhao [4] however, focuses on dampening the vibrations. The difference may be due to the research 
being done in different areas, as Zhao’s focus is in oil well drilling, while the other model is done 
in industrial drilling. The different aspect ratio might influence how big of an impact the torsional 
vibrations or torsional chatter have on the drilling operation. 
It is however concluded in the work of Ahmadi and Altintas [3] that the dampening of the vibrations 
changes the dominant mode of vibration, meaning that an undamped system, is highly affected 
by torsional-axial vibrations, and a that damped system is instead highly affected by the lateral 
vibrations. Lateral vibrations on the other hand are also affected by the supporting walls, of the 
workpiece, so after some drilling depth is achieved, the effect of the lateral vibrations is greatly 
reduced, and the torsional-axial vibrations become the dominating modes. 
 
The vibrations in the work are measured by using a table dynamometer and measuring the forces 
in X, Y and Z direction of the machine, meaning that similar methods should be a viable solution. 
The models made by Voronov et al. [12]  can be used, to determine the dynamics of the drill. The 
models focus on the torsional-axial vibrations combined, which represent the dynamics of the 
operation, both for the torsional part, which will be used here, but also the axial part, which will 
not be used in this case. The twist drill is simplified to a two-degree of freedom system, which 
vibrates in the axial and torsional direction, as seen in Figure 7 below.

4.1.1. 
Drillling area for force/torque measurement 
Depending on the application, the two dynamometers have different pros and cons. For instance, 
the Montronix dynamometer is on a collet chuck and can drill or ream a larger quantity of holes 
than the Kistler dynamometer, as the Kistler dynamometer is restricted to drilling on the small 
fixture. However, since the Kistler dynamometer is on a fixture it has a much larger array of 
application since it is not connected to a collet chuck and does not require a spindle to operate.  
The Kistler dynamometer has the highest accuracy when the measurements are centred on the 
dynamometer, since the torque is measured between the top plate and the bottom fixture of the 
dynamometer, so any workpiece put on top of the dynamometer must be centred, for the 
dynamometer to correctly transfer the torque from the workpiece to the piezoelectric sensor.

4.4. 
Drilling 
It has been proven that lubrication can improve the tool life and is has therefore been used for a 
long time, since dry drilling has proven to have reduced tool lives as low as a third of the durability 
of lubricated tools [9] and [10]. However, the choice now depends on whether to use minimum 
quality lubrication (MQL) or flooded, here [9] [10] has shown that they yield approximately the 
same result. The usage of lubricant can reduce the vibration happening during the drill process, 
and it has therefore been deemed that lubrication would not be used during the experiments. In 
==PAGE:38==
 
30 
 
addition, all the drills were cleaned with tissues and ethanol to make sure that there was no 
lubricant left from the manufacturing of the drill.         
In terms of the drilling method it should be considered if the drilling will be done exclusively by 
twist drills, or if a center drill should be used to assist the twist drill, by starting with a pilot hole, 
and thereby giving better control and stability of the twist drill while it starts drilling the hole, 
and reducing the wear during the initial drilling. This should give more consistent results, as the 
twist drill will not experience wear on entry, which may cause inconsistencies in the determination 
of tool life, as the cutting edges might not enter each hole the same way every single time. 
The drawback of having to use the additional center drill is that it would greatly increase the time 
required to conduct the experiments, since there are two issues. First, the tool has to be changed 
during the operation. This time can be reduced, by using the center drill, to drill all the entries 
initially, and then change to the twist drill. However, a second issue would then arise in aligning 
the twist drill with the holes for further drilling. With a CNC machine, this issue would be reduced, 
but the uncertainty of the machine would have to be noted, and taken into consideration.  
The final decision on which method will be used, will be made after the pilot-test. 
For the tests with the twist drill and measurements of the vibration, the use of lubrication was 
discarded as it reduces friction and therefore would also reduce the magnitude of the vibrations. 
So all drilling with the twist drills was decided to be done dry, for consistency during drilling. The 
twist drills had recommendation for the different drilling parameters from the manufacturer, but 
it was not completely clear if the recommendation were for lubricated or non-lubricated drilling. 
Based on basic knowledge of the use of lubrication in drilling, it was assumed to be drilling with 
lubrication and new drilling parameters had to be estimated for the drilling test. 
Since it was decided to use no lubrication during the testing, there was then the concern for Build-
up edges (BUE) and the effect it has on the tools life to consider. Due to this concern it was quickly 
decided that the drill needed to be removed after each hole. So the drilling was stopped after 
each hole was made to remove the BUE from the drill and remove any chips on the plate to reduce 
non-drilling related wear on the twist drill during drilling of the following holes. To remove the 
BUE in the twist drill a small aluminium rod was used, to softly scrape off the BUE. Since the rod 
is made of aluminium, it will cause minimum abrasive wear on the tool due to the softer nature 
of aluminium compared to steel. The ideal abrasive hardness protection is when the surface is 1.3 
times harder than the contacting abrasive element, based on Møller’s textbook [29]. Kalpakjian 
[15] show the difference in hardness on page 72.

4.5. 
Preparation and zeroing 
Before the drilling can commence, the CNC-Machine settings are adjusted, so the x-,y- and z-axis 
are aligned and zeroed with the steel plate. The x- and y-axis are aligned by using a 3D-taster that 
came with the Cincinnati saber 750, which is used to find the two sides of the plate, that are then 
vise parallel to the x- and y-axis of the machine. 
Next, the z-axis is aligned to the top of the plate, by inserting the drill into the machine, and 
manually moving the drill towards the surface of the plate. In order to avoid damaging the plate 
or the drill, a thin aluminium foil strip of 10 μm is placed beneath the drill as it is near the surface. 
The drill is then slowly lowered until the resistance can be felt when attempting to remove the 
strip. At this point, the drill is considered to be positioned at the surface of the plate. 
 
Next the length of the drill must also be determined within the machine. This can be done using 
a HOLEX fine zero setter, see Figure 17 below, which was originally used to calibrate the zero of 
the machine. The drill is lowered onto the zero setter, until zero is reached, and the coordinate 
can then be subtracted from the z-coordinate of the surface of the plate. This difference is then 
the distance from the 0-point in the z-axis to the surface of the plate. 
 
 
Figure 17. HOLEX fine zero setter. This is used to set the zero of the machine in the z-axis. The 
zero setter is placed on the base-plate of the machine, and the drill is then lowered onto it, until 
the zeroing position has been reached. The coordinates can then be saved on the Cincinnati, and 
used as a reference for the machine.

4.6. 
Pilot-Drilling  
The aid of lubrication was added to the plate for the pilot-drilled holes and the lubrication was 
thoroughly cleaned afterwards, with the use of ethanol and disposable tissues. 
Since the drills used are completely new, the cutting edges are rather sharp, and with dry cutting 
being a volatile condition, the even small irregularities during the entry may affect the results of 
the tool life for the experiments. To prevent this, the hard-coated stub drill4, henceforth the 
pilot-drill is used, so the twist drill can enter the designated drilling point and reduce some of 
this volatility along the periphery of the drill and make sure that all the drilling operations are 
conforming, in terms of wear during the process. The aid of WD-40 as lubrication is added to the

4.8.3. 
Procedure 
The procedure in which the final experiment is conducted is the following. First, the Cincinnati is 
turned on and will be put into zeroing position. Next, the position of the workpiece, the drill 
height, and the runout of the drill are measured. When the length and runout are measured and 
set the Cincinnati will be running for 1 hour at 1000 rpm to warm up the machine. 
First, pilot-drilled holes will be made for the necessary holes. Then one series of five drills will 
start doing 50 holes for each drill, the series could for instance be the L93 CS 15. When the drill 
has drilled 50 holes it will be taken out of the collet chuck and the wear will be measured on a 
LOM, light optical microscope.

After the wear has been measured for the five drills in one series, the drills are put into the 
Montronix collet chuck and will drill one hole into vibration block that is placed and secured on a 
Kistler dynamometer plate, to investigate the forces and torque during one drilling with two 
different dynamometers, so values can be compared to each other to give more reliable results. 
New pilot-drilled holes are then made, and the drills are then remounted on the standard collet 
chuck without a strain gauge. Then the same series of twist drill will drill 50 new holes and 
dismount, and the wear, forces and torque will be measured again. This procedure is repeated 
until the five drills in the series reaches 8 meters of cutting length or if a critical failure happens. 
Then the next series starts.

5. Methods and influential factors in measurements 
When conducting scientific experiments, it is important to know as many of the factors that can 
influence the results and outcome of the experiment. These factors should also be limited when 
possible. In this section, a method will be described for wear measurements and for check the 
repeatability of the test. An environmental temperature variation error test will also be performed 
for the Cincinnati Saber, with slightly altered parameter. 
5.1. 
Procedure for wear measurement method 
The measurement of wear on drill has no standard from ISO and the only few proposals found are 
in the works of Kanai [22] and IVF [24] which are from 1978 and 1970, and it begs the question if 
it is still reliable for today’s technology, but since no newer publications have been made these 
guidelines will be used. These proposals tried to make a guideline for quantifying tool life and 
wear for drills. The old guidelines suggest using outer corner wear, flank wear and force/torque 
for quantifying the tool life for drills. But they mainly suggest that outer corner wear and 
force/torque is the best indication to use for tool life. 
5.1.1. 
Method of measuring of wear.  
To measure the outer corner wear on the drill, a special holder was designed in Solidworks, which 
could hold the drill at an angle of 60° to a horizontally plane surface, and was 3D-printed with an 
Ultimaker 2. When the drill is placed in the holder ii will be put on top on an inverted LOM, 
Olympus GX 41, as seen in Figure 23 below set to either optical of x5 or x10 zoom depending on 
how worn the drill is. Thereafter pictures will be taken of the outer corner wear with the mounted 
camera, Leica DFC450 C. It was however necessary to divide the sections of the drill into multiple 
parts to get pictures of the whole drill. The pictures are named after the position shown in the 
Figure 24, where the CE stands for chisel edge and the OC stand for outer corner. The resolution 
of the pictures is 2560x1920 pixels where a length of 294 pixels correspond to 100 and 200 μm 
depending on the optic, and the black numbers on Figure 25 below are length in pixels and can 
easily converted into microns.

Method of measuring for force and Torque 
To measure the force and torque two dynamometers were used as mentioned in section 4.1, where 
only the data from Montronix dynamometer will be used for the results. The Kistler was used to 
calibrate the Montronix dynamometer since the signal output is in volts, and needs to be converted 
into newton and newton-meter. 
The method used to measure the force and torque is based on the principles of Mullers work [25] 
and on Figure 26 below shows some of the important information for measuring the force and 
torque. First points A and B are identified on the graph in an area where the force and torque 
reaches its nominal value and is stable. Then the mean value will be calculated with all the points 
between A and B, known as the Window span. If no noticeable irregularities are present on the 
graph, the window span will be set the length of 2 seconds, since it is drilling of a Ø 6 mm blind 
==PAGE:53==

hole will take around 2.4 seconds for cutting speed 15 m/min and 1.44 for CS 25 m/min. On Figure 
26 two red circles on the graph show when the drill reaches the workpiece and starts to drill and 
when the drill no longer has contact with the workpiece.

5.4. 
Operating machine temperature variation error  
In the industry it is normal practices to warm up the machine before operating, to make sure it 
has the smallest drift during operation. Therefore, a modified test of the ISO 230-3 is done to 
determine the influence of the temperature increase, while still suiting the experiment 
parameters better. Instead for running the machine at 2/3 of the maximum rotational speed, the 
machine was set to rotate at 1000 rpm to simulate this experiment’s rotational speed for both 
cutting speeds of 15 and 25 m/min, since it is the average rotational speed of the two tests, and 
only differs with 250 rpm for each series of drills. The test runs for four hours where position and 
spindle temperature was measured every 5th minute of the operation. When the operation stops 
the mounted Ø 40 mm mandrel is programmed to stop at the same place where it started spinning. 
When the machine stops and places the mandrel in the starting position the fixture surrounding 
the mandrel will have three linear displacement sensors for each coordinate axis. The sensors 
have small vacuum pumps which connect to the mandrel and measure the difference from their 
zeroing position. Figure 27 shows a standard setup for this test. When the displacement is noted, 
the temperature is measured, with a Fluke 52 thermometer with a TMC unit 12 probe, on the x,y-
plane of the spindle and will measured for 30 seconds to make sure that the temperature 
measurement has stabilized. The location of the spindle can be seen on (4) in Figure 9 in section 
4.1 and the coordination system for the spindle and machine can been seen on Figure 28 below.

more worn down and eventual it should start making loud coarse noises the more worn it becomes

Something which was noted in the torque measurements above is that when Δt≈0.048 s, which is

vibrations are not occurring in a single mode. For the smaller drills, seen in Figure 48, it shifts

resemblance to a sinus curve than the larger vibrations. For the larger drills, seen in Figure 49, it 
is more difficult to see whether that is the case or not. 
For both cases, the modes occur periodically, which means that it is not just chatter, but 
something which can be examined further.

An in-depth analysis will not be made in this work, but the results will be analysed briefly by using 
Fourier Transformation, to determine if the eigenfrequencies determined theoretically align with 
the ones which affect the experiments. Fourier Transformation is used since it transforms the 
measured results from a time domain, to a frequency domain where the influence of the different 
frequencies can be seen. 
Using MATLAB, it is possible to use a Fast Fourier Transformation algorithm, which is a variation 
of Fourier Transformation, based on the same method of transforming the results measured by the 
Montronix AT100 dynamometer from the time domain to the frequency domain. 
The transformation is done, since the results seem to have a sinusoidal shape, but without any 
clear indication of the Amplitude, angular frequency or the phase of the wave. This may be due 
to the fact that multiple modes of vibrations occur during the drilling process, and these modes 
can be decomposed into multiple sinusoids, with different angular frequencies.

7. Discussion of results 
The discussion of the results can be split into three parts. One regarding the Tool life and wear 
measurements, a second regarding the vibrational analysis, and a third about the practical aspects 
of the experiment. 
7.1. 
Tool life and wear 
After the experiment was done, the analysis of the results could begin, however working through 
the results it became clear that they did not indicate anything about the tool life or the quality 
of the hole, which was the hypothesis. One of the factors why the force and torque measurement 
did not indicate anything for the tool life could be that it only cut one diameter deep instead of 
the four times the diameter, but it was necessary due to the large runout of the Montronix chuck, 
that it can intentionally cause more wear to the drill than what it would have gotten in a more 
stable chuck. For the wear measurement using a LOM might have been a good idea for precise 
measurement, but it seemed to be unwise to use due to the time needed for measuring with that 
equipment, it might have been better to use a toolmakers microscope like a single point cutting 
tool and how they did it in Kanai [22]. 
For this type of experiment a shorter interval between measuring sessions could have given a 
better picture of how the was experiment going, it was however unfortunate that it would be 
more time consuming and may cause a higher chance of mistakes happening. 
Another thing which is still unclear when it comes to tool life is the noise, because for all the long 
drills, L139, a clear sound was present each time they were about to break, which persisted until 
they broke, however for the standard drills, L93, no noise was there to indicate if the drill was 
reeling and about to break. Therefore, a microphone for acoustic would perhaps be a better and 
more reliable tool to tell if drill is about to break, since it is only a tool used in the industry for 
this purpose, instead of relying on the human hearing.

7.2. 
Vibrational analysis 
When looking at the results from the vibrational analysis, the very first thing that springs to mind 
is the instability of the torque and thereby the variation in cutting speed, which is present on the 
graphs. 
The instability could be due to the choice of drilling without lubrication, which can exacerbate 
the vibrations due to the increased friction of dry drilling, which would then lead to uneven 
drilling. The uneven drilling could occur by the stick-slip phenomenon [30], where the drill will 
stick to the edge of the hole during drilling, and build up kinetic energy by twisting, followed by 
the release of kinetic energy by increasing the cutting speed for a brief moment, so the drill can 
rotate back to a non-twisting state. 
 
The uneven drilling could also occur by non-uniform drilling, where the drill would then remove a 
larger chunk of material in the hole, which then leaves a short period of time, where the drill does 
not have contact with the workpiece, in this period of time the torque would then be greatly 
reduced, until contact is established once more. 
Both cases are reasonable explanation, and both indicate that the problem could be solved by 
reducing the friction by adding lubrication to the process.  
Since a choice had to be made in analysing the unstable results, reducing the timeframe to a 
window small enough to have a stable set of measurements had to be done. The choice was made, 
since most of the results had longer stretches, with stable results, which could easily be plucked 
out for further examination. Additionally, the timeframe for a single revolution was 0.048 s and 
most of the stretches vary between 0.15 and 0.4 seconds, so the results would cover more than a 
single revolution. This means that any results with large variation in torque during the entire 
process, would be poorly represented, as only a section would be considered, rather than the 
entire process. 
A second method was considered, where the parts of the results which were unstable would be 
removed, and then the points before and after the unstable part of the results could be used as 
one set of measurements. The benefit of this method compared to the previous one is that a larger 
timeframe would be achieved. There were however two reasons that made this method less 
inviting. 
 
First, for most of the results the vibrations do not stay as a stable sinus curve with a certain offset 
from 0. The offset varied as well, which can clearly be seen in Figure 44 in section 6.4. This would 
in some cases lead to some disjointed connections between the two stable sections of the process.  
==PAGE:85==
 
77 
 
Second, this method removes some of the results, and even though removing the outliers may 
prove useful for analysing the torsional vibrations, it means disregarding all other influences which 
may influence not only the vibrations but also the tool life. 
Overall it was deemed that removing results was a worse alternative than having to settle with a 
short timeframe, which lead to the first choice being the best method of handling the results that 
were not stable and simple to analyse. 
In terms of the results and what they show, both the theoretical and experimental model show 
that the torsional vibrations do cause a variation in cutting speed, which can be seen by the 
oscillations in the torque measurements. Contrary to the hypothesis that the torsional vibrations 
are impactful on the process, it seems that the torsional vibrations only have a very small impact 
on the tool life, as the results imply that the variations in cutting speed based on the measured 
torque do not have a relation to the tool life. Despite no relation between the torsional vibrations 
and the tool life being shown, the tool life still varies greatly. In fact, the results have such a large 
variation that the results in some cases show that the larger drills actually have better tool life 
than the shorter drills. 
This is the contrary of what was expected, and may mean that some unexpected factors came into 
play. Since the L139 drills are the more unstable of the two sets, it may seem that these drills 
were too long for the hole depth which had to be drilled. This could also explain why the slower 
speed performed much better in terms of tool life, as it was mentioned that drills which are too 
long can have their tool life increased by reducing the cutting speed. 
 
It needs more research to confirm anything, but it is very possible that the length of the drills had 
an influence on the tool life. 
Based on the results from the Fourier analysis, it seems that the assumption of a fixed cylinder for 
the drill seems like poor choice, since the eigenfrequencies are much lower than expected based 
on the models used. This is most likely connected to the runout, since some of the drills were 
rather poor in this regard. 
The runout did not show any relation to the tool life, but since the runout was only measured once 
for each drill it may be possible that it varied for each remounting, and thereby had a larger 
influence than measured in this work.

7.3. 
Experiments 
With great instability in the results, and unverified models, it seems that a better approach could 
have been taken during the experiments. Specifically, the choice of using a dynamometer was 
deemed a questionable decision, as the use of an accelerometer would have given better results, 
and would also have given less room for error during modelling of the cutting speed. The 
dynamometer was initially chosen, since it gave the possibility of analysing both the force and the 
torque, and thereby the possibility to analyse both the torsional but also the axial vibrations was 
present. 
But since the axial vibrations were disregarded, the choice of using the dynamometer is in 
hindsight not the optimal approach. 
Additionally, since the Fourier analysis showed a great reduction in terms of rotational frequencies 
for the torsional vibrations, a sampling rate of 50 kHz might have been too high, since the torsional 
vibrations occurred below 2 kHz, so a lot of unwanted noise might have been present. The sampling 
rate should at least have been 10 kHz, but could also have been set at 20 kHz. Both cases would 
have been sufficient in order to pick up the required frequency for the torsional vibrations. 
With a sampling rate at 10-20 kHz, the vibration measurements could also have been extended. 
With the current setup, the vibrations are measured for a cutting depth of 7.5 mm, which is one 
drill diameter added to the initial pilot-drilled hole. This would have meant that the measurements 
could possibly have covered the entire drilling process, which it was not able to do, since the 
cutting depth was restricted based on the time-frame, in which the software could measure the 
vibrations at 50 kHz. This timeframe could be increased, since the sampling frequency could have 
been decreased.

8. Conclusion and Recommendations 
The goal of the project was to conduct a theoretical and experimental investigation on the 
influence of vibrations on the tool life in drilling. The focus area was torsional vibrations, as these 
generate a variation in the cutting speed, which is expected to increase tool wear. A theoretical 
model is produced for the torsional vibrations of a twist drill, which relates the torque to the 
cutting speed and the relation between cutting speed and tool life is examined. Alongside this, a 
study of the drill lengths influence on the tool life and torsional vibrations was also conducted. 
An experimental investigation was carried out, by doing a series of tool life test, with drills of 
different lengths, and the wear and torsional vibrations were measured after every 50th hole, and 
two tool life criteria were set, and once these were reached the tool was considered to have 
reached its end of life. The criteria were: 
 
• 
Maximum cutting length: 8 m 
• 
Critical failure by breakage or unable to cut further 
These limitations are set as 8 meters is considered the largest cutting length which is used 
industrially, while upholding the quality of the drilled holes, and critical failure has to be 
considered, in case the drill does not withstand 8 meters of drilling. 
Both the theoretical and experimental model agree that the torsional vibrations do cause a 
variation in cutting speed, which can be seen by the oscillations in the torque measurements. It 
however seems that the torsional vibrations have a very small impact on the tool life, as the results 
imply that the variations in cutting speed based on the measured torque do not have a relation to 
the tool life.  
The variation in speed not having as large an influence could be due to the possibility that multiple 
vibrational modes were present during the operation. 
 
The tool life on the other hand seems to be influenced more by other factors as well. Especially 
the drills with a length of 139 mm seem to have very large variations in cutting speed, which do 
not comply with the expected results. This is most likely due to the fact that the drills were too 
long for the specific type of holes drilled. This may cause larger fluctuations due to the runout, or 
due to slight bending during contact, which was clearly visible for the even larger drills with a 
length of 205 mm. 
 
==PAGE:88==
 
80 
 
The smaller drills did not seem to consistently wear down, which may be due to the precautions 
taken in terms of cutting speed and cleaning during the dry cutting process.  
This also made it very clear that the shorter drills had a longer tool life than the longer drills, and 
the results also clearly show that the longer drills experience torsional vibrations of a larger 
magnitude than the shorter drills.

9. Future work 
Based on the conclusions drawn so far, and the unclear results in terms of the vibrational analysis, 
different approaches can be taken for further work on the subject on torsional vibrations influence 
on tool life in drilling. 
The first possibility is to continue the current work, by adding more repetitions with the shorter 
L93 drills with a length of 93 mm, in order to have a better statistical representation of how the 
vibrations affect the tool life. It is also possible to continue the work with even shorter drills, but 
drills longer than the 93 mm are discouraged for future work, as the torsional vibrations seem to 
be wildly inconsistent with the theory, which is explained earlier to be either due to bending or 
the runout of the drills. 
 
Alternatively, the L93 drills can be used at a higher cutting speed, to reduce the tool life, to easier 
reach critical failure and have a more well represented relation between tool life and torsional 
vibrations. 
The model used to verify the torsional vibrations also did not seem to be a suitable choice, as the 
assumptions made, caused the model to exert a much larger initial torque than expected, which 
was then damped to a level which may or may not be correct. Further work could be done to prove 
the hypothesis that the model used in this report is correct. 
Something which was learned at a later stage of the project was that the use of an accelerometer 
would have been a great improvement over using the original dynamometers, in terms of modelling 
the torsional vibrations, as this would give access to the angular acceleration, which would have 
made modelling much simpler, rather than having to set up large matrixes in order to get results 
which were unable to be verified. 
Next, it is believed that adding a lubricant and coolant will greatly stabilise the very volatile 
oscillations in the torque measurements, and also allow for a higher cutting speed, which should 
in term give more clear results in terms of vibrations. 
For work which is not directly related to the research done in this report, a further investigation 
of the possible different vibration modes in section 6.4.1 could be examined further, to determine 
if the torsional vibrations are the only type which occur during contact. 
 
 
==PAGE:90==
 
82 
 
Next, a study could be done solely on researching the runout and determining it’s influence on 
the results in this work. Both the drill life and the torsional vibrations are very likely largely 
influenced by the runout, as a larger runout should greatly change the torsional vibrations by 
reducing the eigenfrequency due to the additional lateral vibrations. It should also reduce the tool 
life since the runout should both increase the diameter of the hole, and thereby remove more 
material, but it should also change the entry angle, causing possible bending in the drill and 
thereby also adding a cyclic load to the drilling process, which could be a larger influence on drill 
breakage than tool wear. 
When measuring the outer corner, flank or chisel wear a more detailed and developed guide for 
the tool life measurements and criteria for the drills are needed. This would greatly improve the 
understanding of the results and allow for better comparison between different drill results. Tool 
life test could be improved by developing a standardised measuring procedure, explanation of the 
equipment used for measuring wear and other factors, and explanation of how the tool is secured 
while measuring. The first step would be creating a standard that can give a good repeatability 
and reproducibility for measurements of the different types of wear on the drill.
