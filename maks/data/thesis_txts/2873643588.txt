Abstract
Measles remains a challenge in Europe despite high immunisation and elimination of en-
demic transmission. This thesis investigated the stochastic dynamics of measles outbreaks
in populations where transmission cannot sustain itself, with a particular emphasis on
how heterogeneous population immunity, rather than homogeneous susceptibility, drives
measles transmission and shape outbreak size distributions.
This was analysed using
a multi-method approach incorporating European TESSy outbreak data. The study ad-
dresses the persistent challenge of measles outbreaks caused by imported cases in countries
near transmission elimination, and uncovers novel implications for public health strategies.
A theoretical outbreak size distribution model by Jansen and Stollenwerk, grounded in
stochastic process theory, was used to assess transmission potential under the assump-
tion of homogeneous mixing in populations where transmission is self-limiting.
The
framework was extended to accommodate censored surveillance data of this thesis. Fur-
thermore, an agent-based model (ABM) implementing the susceptible-infected-recovered
(SIR) framework was developed to investigate outbreak size distributions of heteroge-
neous populations. The SIR model demonstrated that localised immunity gaps, despite
high overall vaccine coverage, substantially increased both the probability and scale of
large outbreaks, thus destabilising herd immunity of populations. An empirical analysis
of multi-country surveillance data from 2009–2025 (Austria, Spain, Ireland, Poland, and
the United Kingdom) revealed that observed outbreak size distributions significantly ex-
ceeded the homogeneous-mixing predictions (p < 0.01). These deviations from theoretical
predictions, combined with ABM demonstrations of clustered susceptibility effects, ex-
posed heterogenous clustering of susceptibility masked by aggregate national transmission
metrics, leading to underestimated outbreak risks under homogeneous assumptions. An
age-stratified binomial model identified children aged 0–4 years as primary contributors
to residual transmission within the countries. This paediatric dominance presented the
paradox that, despite high vaccine uptake among 1–2 year olds (91–97%), elevated risk
persists in this age group due to subnational susceptibility clusters concentrating unvac-
cinated children.
Collectively, this study demonstrates that national vaccination metrics mask localised sus-
ceptibility gaps, which can drive large-scale outbreaks even in populations meeting WHO
coverage targets. These findings advocate for targeted elimination efforts that identify
under-immunised communities and high-risk age groups, coupled with the enforcement of
subpopulation vaccine coverage thresholds to avoid escalation of outbreaks. Additional
measures include optimising childhood vaccination schedules, implementation of manda-
tory national vaccine requirements, and development of responsive containment plans.

Introduction
Measles is a highly contagious viral disease that infected 90% of exposed non-immune
individuals in the pre-vaccine era. Historically endemic within populations, measles dis-
proportionately affected children and represented a major childhood illness [1]. Since the
1960s, measles-containing vaccines (MCVs) have fundamentally altered the disease’s epi-
demiology, drastically reducing its burden and preventing an estimated 60.3 million deaths
globally between 2000–2023 [2]. Despite this progress, measles remains a leading cause of
vaccine-preventable childhood mortality worldwide, causing 107,500 deaths in 2023 alone,
predominantly among children under five and immunocompromised individuals [2]. In
Europe, endemic transmission has been eliminated in most countries, yet frequent out-
breaks persist despite overall high immunisation coverage [1]. Alarmingly, the European
region reported its highest incidence in 25 years in 2024 [3]. This resurgence coincides
with a global decline in first-dose MCV coverage since 2019, reversing the progress to-
ward WHO’s measles elimination goal [2]. Large ongoing outbreaks in the United States,
combined with rising infection rates in Europe, further underscore the persistent threat
of measles [3, 4].
This epidemiological challenge reflects a shift to non-sustaining transmission dynamics,
where outbreaks depend primarily on imported cases and undergo stochastic extinction,
rather than progressing to sustained endemic or epidemics [1]. Conventional homogeneous
population mixing models predict rapid outbreak extinction when population susceptibil-
ity falls below the herd immunity threshold due to stochastic fade-out [5]. However, the
recurrent large-scale outbreaks in Europe challenge these theoretical predictions, suggest-
ing that factors such as population immunity heterogeneity may govern transmission in
settings, which are near elimination of measles [3].
To characterize self-limiting outbreaks, stochastic models are essential. The theo-
retical outbreak-size distribution model by Jansen and Stollenwerk (2005) offers a foun-
dational approach, deriving probability distributions of finite outbreaks where infection
chains terminate stochastically [6]. However, its applicability to multinational European
populations, with diverse immunity profiles and population structures, is still inadequately
tested against empirical surveillance data.
Understanding these complex measles dynamics requires integrated knowledge of distinc-
tive virology, transmission pathways, and vaccination mechanisms, all essential factors
shaping outbreak patterns in contemporary European populations.
Measles Epidemiology
Measles spreads through respiratory droplets that remain infectious in the air for several
hours and on surfaces for up to two hours [1]. The virus infects the respiratory epithelium
and then spreads through the body. After an incubation time of 10-12 days, symptoms
manifests as fever, runny nose, cough and a rash all over the body. Full recovery can usually
be expected 8-12 days after symptom onset, establishing lifelong immunity [1]. There is
no specific antiviral treatment against measles, only complicating bacterial infections can
be treated. Nevertheless, bacterial infections are the main cause of mortality from measles
and six out of ten measles associated deaths are due to pneumonia. The case fatality rate
in Europe is 1–3 per 1,000 reported cases [1].

MCV contains live attenuated measles strains that induce immunity corresponding to that
acquired through natural infection with the wild virus. The incubation time of the vaccine
virus is shorter than the wild virus, and can therefore be administered upon exposure to
infected individuals within 72 hours to prevent clinical disease [1].
Newborn children acquire maternal antibodies against measles if the mother was im-
mune. This provides immunity to the infant until approximately 6 months of age where
the antibodies begins to fade. In the European Union (EU) and European Economic Area
(EEA) it is recommended to schedule two doses of MCV to children, with the first dose
(MCV1) administered within the second year of life and the second dose (MCV2) at an
older age, which differs between countries [1]. The recommended scheduling of MCV1 ad-
ministration is a balance between risking infection and vaccine failure. Maternal antibodies
are namely the most common cause of primary vaccine failure of MCV1. Administering
the MCV1 at 12 months of age provides at least 95% effectiveness, and administering the
MCV2 later will improve the effectiveness obtaining close to 100% [2]. WHO recommends
a 95% vaccine uptake with two MCV doses as a goal for national vaccination programs to
achieve herd immunity [3].
1.1
Aim
The continued occurrence of large measles outbreaks across Europe represent a persis-
tent public health challenge despite implemented vaccination programs. This thesis aims
to investigate how heterogeneity in population immunity, rather than homogeneous sus-
ceptibility, can drive measles transmission in non-sustaining transmission settings, where
outbreaks depend entirely on imported cases. Using theoretical frameworks and multi-
country surveillance data from 2009–2025, we assess the impact of heterogeneous immunity
on outbreak size distributions.
Specifically, we:
• Assess the applicability of the homogeneous outbreak size distribution model by
Jansen and Stollenwerk to empirical European data.
• Quantify the impact of subpopulations with low vaccination coverage on outbreak
sizes using agent-based modelling, testing scenarios where high national coverage
masks subpopulation susceptibility.
• Estimate country-specific transmission dynamics in Austria, Spain, Ireland, Poland,
and the United Kingdom through empirical outbreak analysis and assess the fit to
theoretical homogeneous mixing predictions.
• Uncover age-structured transmission drivers, by analysing age-group co-occurrence
patterns and per-case transmission risks, in relation to children’s vaccine uptake.
This integrated approach combines theoretical models, stochastic simulations, and multi-
national surveillance data, aiming to unravel the impact of localised immunity gaps on
measles transmission, ultimately forming strategies for outbreak prevention.

Mathematical Foundations of
Homogeneous Epidemic Modelling
Mathematical modelling provides essential tools for understanding transmission dynam-
ics of infectious diseases like measles [5]. Such models enable the prediction of outbreak
patterns, evaluation of control strategies, and exploration of fundamental epidemiological
mechanisms that are challenging to observe empirically [7].
This chapter establishes the theoretical foundation for simulating and analysing measles
outbreaks focusing initially on homogeneously mixed populations which have eliminated
endemic transmission, as observed in many European populations. From the deterministic
Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) model framework, we will derive the core epidemi-
ological metrics of reproductive numbers and herd immunity thresholds. The foundation
is then extended to stochastic approaches, which are useful for characterising self-limited
outbreaks under herd immunity, where stochastic processes dominate disease transmis-
sion [6].
Central to this extension is a probability distribution of sizes of self-limited
outbreaks by Jansen and Stollenwerk [6].
We conclude this chapter with a review of population mixing paradigms in measles
modelling, contextualising our homogeneous framework within current literature.
This theoretical foundation supports our later agent-based explorations of heterogeneity,
and analysis of European measles surveillance data, bridging theoretical dynamics to real-
world data application and assessments.
2.1
SIR Model
The SIR model is a classic compartment model within epidemiology that is used to simulate
outbreaks of infectious diseases (e.g., measles) over time [8]. It describes the dynamics
of disease transmission within a population, where people move between the three states
susceptible (S), infected (I), and recovered (R). The classic SIR formulation does not
account for births or deaths, due to the typically short timescale of outbreaks.
The
population size is thus assumed to be constant throughout the modelling process (N =
S +I +R). The model further assumes that the population is homogeneously mixed. The
transition from compartment to compartment (S →I →R) is determined by transition
rate constants β (transmission) and γ (recovery).
An infected individual causes new
infections with the rate βS, where β reflects the combined rate of potentially infectious
contacts per unit time and the probability of transmission per contact. Higher β will cause
more transmissions. An infected individual recovers from the infection with rate γ, which
is the inverse of the average infectious period ( 1
γ ). Higher γ causes faster recoveries [5].

(ODEs), here normalised to a population size of N = 1, as

2.2
Reproduction Numbers
Using the SIR framework established in Section 2.1, we now introduce core epidemiological
metrics for evaluating transmission dynamics. Reproduction numbers are used to quantify
the transmission potential of infectious diseases.
Estimating reproduction numbers of
pathogens such as measles can provide critical guidance in public health strategies. They
give insight into outbreak dynamics and disease control measures such as vaccination
programs [7].
The basic reproduction number, R0, is a theoretical metric defined by the average number
of secondary infections arising from a single infected individual in a fully susceptible popu-
lation. While this idealised scenario of a fully susceptible population is rarely observed due
to immunity from prior infections and vaccinations, R0 serves as a foundational modelling
construction for quantifying transmission potential. R0 is affected by numerous factors
such as behaviour, biology, and environment that all affect disease transmission [9].
The R0 of measles is notably high, usually estimated at 12-18 [1]. This reflects that measles
is highly contagious, compared to diseases like the common cold with R0 estimates at 2-
3 [10]. Within the normalised SIR models (N = 1), R0 is mathematically defined as the
ratio between the transmission rate (β) and the recovery rate (γ) in a fully susceptible
population (S ≈1) [7]. That is

Herd Immunity
The effective reproduction number defined in Equation (2.5) enables practical applications
in public health strategies, most notably the concept of herd immunity. Herd immunity

2.3
Branching Processes in Disease Transmission
Self-limiting outbreaks (Re < 1) can be analysed through stochastic frameworks that fo-
cus solely on infection events derived from the SIR model framework. For this, branching
processes provide a valuable tool for modelling early outbreak dynamics where stochastic
effects dominate. Here we will introduce an event-based discrete-time branching process
with constant transition probabilities simulated as a random walk to model measles out-
break in a population.
2.3.1
Continuous-Time Birth-Death Process
In a population with a suﬀiciently large susceptible group and with Re < 1, the disease
will only infect a limited part of the population, and it can be assumed that the susceptible
population is unchanged during an outbreak. The dynamics from the ordinary SIR model
(Section 2.1) can be reduced to a continuous-time birth-death process where only the
infected population I is recorded. This can be formulated as a continuous-time Markov
process with an infection (birth) rate b = βS = γRe per infected individual, and a recovery
(death) rate d = γ per infected individual, governing the state I. State transitions occur
as I →I + 1 for infections or I →I −1 for recoveries, with transition times exponentially
distributed with rate b + d = γ(Re + 1) per infected individual [7].

2.7
Population Mixing Paradigms in Measles Modelling
Research on measles transmission in subcritical populations near elimination often relies
on homogeneous mixing frameworks derived from the SIR framework. In Europe, most
countries are in a subcritical regime and the simple model proposed by Jansen and Stol-
lenwerk offers a foundational approach for quantifying outbreak size distributions under
assumptions of uniform susceptibility and contact patterns [6].
In a 2003 study by Jansen and Stollenwerk they apply the model to investigate the de-
cline in vaccine uptake and increasing number of large outbreaks in England and Wales [14].
They estimated the effective reproduction numbers from outbreak size distributions of two
subsequent time periods (1995-1998 and 1999-2002), before and after a controversy of in-
correct linking between the Measles, Mumps, and Rubella (MMR) vaccine and autism [15].
They conclude that there is a significant difference in the effective reproduction number
between the two time periods, though constrained by homogeneous assumptions.
Historically, theoretical measles modelling has often prioritized analytical tractability
through homogeneous frameworks, explicitly ignoring demographic structure to derive fun-
damental epidemiological measures [5]. A study by Keeling and Grenfell (1997) exemplifies
this approach, applying stochastic SIR models with homogeneous mixing to derive critical
community sizes for measles persistence [16]. The assumption of complete population-wide
14

==PAGE:23==
uniformity in both susceptibility and contact patterns, enables elegant closed-form solu-
tions, but creates an idealized representation where potential heterogeneity in transmission
dynamics is unaccounted for.
Beyond homogeneous approaches, other studies implicitly account for heterogeneity. A
study by Herzog et al. (2011) researches the association between vaccination coverage
and measles incidence in Germany with a multivariate time-series model [17]. The study
considers the variation in vaccination coverage between federal states and thus models ge-
ographical heterogeneity. The results indicate negative correlations between vaccination
coverage and disease spread, while emphasising that measles transmission across Germany
is not homogeneous.
Multiple recent studies further indicate that social and spatial structures significantly
influence transmission in European contexts. For example a more complex approach by
Munday et al. (2024) investigated the measles transmission between schools in the Nether-

large outbreak observed in 2013, obtaining significantly improved predictions of outbreak
size compared to alternative homogeneous models. They found that the large outbreak
was mainly driven by pupils. However, the study remains context specific, restricted to a
single outbreak and neglecting external transmission.
Collectively, current studies on measles transmission emphasise the importance of imple-
menting heterogeneity in models to reflect real world dynamics in contemporary popu-
lations where transmission is subcritical. Spatial- and social networks also seem to have
an impact on measles transmission like proposed by the study in the Netherlands [18].
However, these approaches remain context-specific, and to our knowledge neither of these
studies have applied their model to different countries and thereby different population
structures.
Our study addresses a gap in the existing literature by investigating how local het-
erogeneity shape measles outbreaks across multiple European countries, while addressing
limitations of using homogenous models on surveillance data.

3
The Distribution of Outbreak Sizes in
Subcritical Populations
In countries with high vaccine coverage, where measles transmission is subcritical (Re < 1),
outbreaks are inherently self-limited, yet stochastic variability can result in occasionally
large outbreaks.
Transmission dynamics are regulated by contact patterns and levels
of vaccine coverage, which determine the effective reproduction number. The size and
frequency of outbreaks are influenced by these factors, carrying important implications
for public health policy and strategies.
This chapter presents a preliminary investigation comparing stochastic simulations of out-
break sizes in subcritical populations with the analytic probability distribution model
proposed by Jansen and Stollenwerk (Section 2.4), addressing two key questions: How
do variations in effective reproduction numbers (Re) influence outbreak-size distributions?
And how does sample size affect uncertainty in maximum likelihood estimation of Re?
3.1
Methods
For this analysis, a Galton-Watson branching process is used to simulate outbreak-size
distributions, which are then utilised to benchmark against Jansen and Stollenwerk’s model
and to assess the uncertainty of the MLE of Re with varying sample sizes.
3.1.1
Stochastic Outbreak Simulations Using a Branching Process
Outbreak sizes are simulated using a stochastic Galton-Watson branching process as de-
scribed in Section 2.3.2. Each outbreak begins with a single infected individual (I = 1)
and terminates when no infected individuals remain (I = 0). The course of each outbreak
is determined by discrete events of infections and recoveries. At each step, a uniformly
distributed random variable is drawn on the interval [0, 1]. If the value is smaller than
the probability of infection i = Re/(Re + 1), the next event is an infection, and otherwise
a recovery. When an outbreak has terminated, the final outbreak size is calculated using
the event-based method defined in Equation (2.9). To estimate distributions of outbreak
sizes, we simulate 100,000 outbreaks for each Re ∈{0.1, 0.2, . . . , 0.9}. The relative fre-
quencies of outbreak sizes collectively provide an estimate of the outbreak size probability
distribution.

The Jansen and Stollenwerk Model
Outbreak size distributions for Re ∈{0.1, 0.2, . . . , 0.9} are computed using Equation (2.15),
which defines the probability mass function q(x) proposed by Jansen and Stollenwerk [6].
Probabilities will be evaluated for outbreak sizes x ranging over integers from 1 to 1,000.
Furthermore, the same values of Re and x are used to compute the cumulative distributions
defined in Equation (2.21).
At criticality (Re = 1), the cumulative distribution of outbreak sizes is approximated by a
power law (Equation (2.23)) for larger outbreak sizes. However, for smaller outbreak sizes
the exact solution q(x) provides a better approximation and ensures that the cumulative
probability equals one at x = 1, though application with Re = 1 falls outside the theoretical
assumptions for the model. For visualisation purposes, the cumulative size distribution

3.3
Implications for Outbreak Risk and Parameter
Estimation
The frequency distributions showed that the simulated outbreak sizes follow the theoret-
ical distribution q(x) for Re < 1 (Figure 3.1), demonstrating that the model effectively
captures the stochastic balance between infections and recoveries in subcritical transmis-
sion regimes. This validates the utility of Jansen and Stollenwerk’s model for investigating
the distribution of outbreak sizes within the underlying assumptions of homogeneous mix-
ing and infinite susceptible population. The minor observed variations at larger outbreak
sizes reflect the finite number of simulations, where large outbreaks are underrepresented
due to their low probability.
As Re approaches 1, the probability of larger outbreaks increases (Figure 3.1). For Re =
0.9 one case leads to an outbreak of size 100 or larger with a probability of 2%, and the
probability distribution exhibit a longer tail, highlighting the risk of large outbreaks near
criticality. This suggests that maintaining an effective reproduction number just below 1
does not prevent large outbreaks. To effectively minimise the risk of large outbreaks, Re
should be maintained well below 1. Increasing Re shifts the distribution of outbreak sizes
towards power law scaling at criticality, exemplifying classic phase-transition behaviour,
where scale-free dynamics amplify outbreak potential.
In order to reliably estimate the effective reproduction number with the q(x) model
through MLE, a sample of at least 200 outbreaks is necessary to achieve a 95% Fisher
CI. Smaller sample sizes (n < 100) inflates the uncertainty of ˆ
Re and the CI coverage is
reduced due to finite-sample effects. The bootstrap method additionally fails to account
for the left-skewed outbreak size distribution when estimating ˆ
Re, shifting CIs away from
the true Re. The Fisher SE and CI incorporates the likelihood function’s curvature, and
since the simulated data originates from the distribution behind the likelihood function,
the parametric Fisher method performs better than the non-parametric bootstrap method.

tional assumptions. These findings emphasise the importance of suﬀicient data to reliably
estimate effective reproduction numbers and outbreak risks.
Key Findings of this Chapter:
• This analysis shows that Jansen and Stollenwerk’s probability distribution model de-
scribes outbreak risks in line with simulated outbreak sizes from branching processes
in subcritical populations.
• While Re < 1 ensures outbreak termination, large outbreaks can still occur with a
non-negligible probability. This highlights the need to keep Re as far below 1 as
possible.
• Reliable estimation of Re using MLE depends critically on sample size. Data drawn
from the Jansen and Stollenwerk distribution requires a sample size n ≥100 to min-

Agent Based Model of Heterogeneous
Vaccine Coverage
While Chapter 3 examined outbreak distributions under homogeneous mixing assump-
tions, real-world populations are often far more clustered. This chapter investigates the
impact of heterogeneous population structure and vaccine coverage on the magnitude of
measles outbreaks. Agent based modelling (ABM) is utilised in this chapter to simulate
outbreaks under varying vaccine coverage distributions.
Unlike branching processes (Section 2.3), ABMs can capture heterogeneity in contact
networks and vaccine coverage. ABMs simulate a finite number of individual agents and
trace their interactions. The dynamic of the system arises from the interactions between
agents. Each agent is characterised by a set of attributes that influence their interactions,
allowing ABMs to represent heterogeneous populations and capture stochastic processes
such as disease transmission under certain conditions. In epidemiological contexts, ABMs
often incorporate information about each agent’s immunity status and contact network.
Through these simulations, we address the core question: How do heterogeneous dis-
tributions of vaccinated individuals affect outbreak size and transmission patterns in pop-
ulations achieving 95% global vaccine coverage? The simulations will generate relevant
insights to develop and evaluate control strategies.
4.1
Methods
An agent based SIR model is utilised to track the status of each agent as either S (sus-
ceptible), I (infected) or R (recovered or vaccinated) throughout a measles outbreak. The
simplified framework captures the essential dynamics of measles outbreaks while omitting
complex real-world transmission details such as incubation time and age-dynamics.
4.1.1
Population Initialisation
A population of 10,000 agents is created and divided into subpopulations with distinct
vaccine coverages and sizes. These subpopulations may represent geographical areas or
social groups. Crucially, agents interact preferentially within their own subpopulation.
Agents, that are vaccinated prior to an outbreak, are assigned the status R, while the
rest of the population are assigned the status S. A single initial infection is introduced by
randomly selecting one agent from the susceptible population, assigning the status I and
an infectious period, as described in the following section.
4.1.2
Transmission Dynamics
The parameters of the model are chosen to reflect typical measles outbreaks. Therefore, the
basic reproduction number is set to R0 = 15 (Section 2.2). An average infectious period of
five days is implemented in the mean recovery rate γ = 1/5 [19]. When susceptible agents
become infected, their infectious period is drawn from a gamma distribution with mean
1/γ and shape parameter 2 to reflect moderate individual variation.

Implications of Heterogeneous Vaccine Coverage
The simulations of the three different scenarios show the effect of heterogeneity in vaccine
coverage on outbreak dynamics, when global 95% vaccine coverage is obtained.
4.3.1
Outbreak Dynamics in Three Vaccination Scenarios
Scenario 1 shows an example of a population with homogeneous vaccine coverage and
thereby uniform herd immunity. This scenario generally produces small outbreaks (Fig-
ure 4.2) with rare spread of infections between subpopulations (Figure 4.3a). Both globally
and locally Re < 1 is obtained (Table 4.1), and the population therefore doesn’t have the
potential to develop to an epidemic. This is in contrast to Scenario 2, which is an exam-
ple of a population with heterogeneous vaccine coverage. Here, two of the subpopulations
have an Re < 1, while one has an Re ≈1 and the last one has Re > 1. Having an effective
reproduction number ≥1 gives rise to a potential epidemic in the relevant subpopula-
tion, and eventually the entire population, as an Re > 1 is obtained globally (Table 4.1).
So, even though Scenario 1 and Scenario 2 both have a similar distribution of the sub-
populations and a vaccine coverage of 95% for the entire population, the distribution of
vaccinated agents have a great impact on the general dynamic of infection transmission
in a population. This effect is evident in Scenario 2, which shows a higher probability
of large outbreaks (Figure 4.2) and exhibits more pronounced variation in transmission
patterns both within and between subpopulations (Figure 4.3b).
Scenario 3 is an example of one small subpopulation with low vaccine coverage and a large
subpopulation with high vaccine coverage, where the entire population obtains an overall
vaccine coverage of 95%. However, the smaller subpopulation seems to destabilise the herd
immunity of the population, resulting in an Re ≈3.35 for the entire population, which is
almost three times greater than in Scenario 2. Both of these scenarios have subpopulations
with Re > 1, but demonstrate completely different dynamics of infection transmission.
Outbreaks in Scenario 3 exhibit a bimodal distribution, typically resulting either in rapid
extinction or in large-scale epidemics (Figures 4.2 and 4.4).

agents in the large subpopulation, resulting in a substantial number of infections among

4.3.2
Strengths of the Agent Based Model
The ABM captures interactions and social dynamics within and between subpopulations,
enabling the exploration of both homogeneous and heterogeneous vaccine scenarios that
approximate real-life scenarios. The entire population may be interpreted as representing
a country with subpopulations corresponding to cities or regions.
In Scenario 1, vac-
cine coverage is uniformly distributed across all subpopulations, illustrating an idealised
situation in which herd immunity is achieved locally as well as globally. However, such
uniformity is rarely observed in the real world. Scenario 2 presents a more realistic setting,
where vaccine coverage varies between subpopulations due to local differences in uptake.
These local disparities can undermine overall herd immunity, even if national coverage
appears suﬀicient. Scenario 3 is designed to investigate the effects of vaccine scepticism
and hesitancy concentrated within minority groups. It demonstrates that insuﬀicient vac-
cine coverage in minority groups can initiate outbreaks that extend beyond the group
itself, affecting the larger population despite their overall herd immunity. The outcomes
of the two heterogeneous scenarios, particularly Scenario 3), emphasise the importance of
uniform vaccine coverage to effectively maintain herd immunity at a global level.
The ABM incorporates stochastic processes, enabling simulations of social groupings
and interaction patterns, beyond deterministic model approximation.
This enables an
analysis of outbreak dynamics in both homogeneous and heterogeneous populations, ac-
counting for variability and non-uniformity in social structure. The contact rates, defined
in the β-matrix, can for instance be adjusted by the scaling parameter ϵ to increase or
decrease interactions within and between subpopulations.
4.3.3
Model Limitations and Simplifications
It is important to acknowledge that the ABM relies on several simplifying assumptions and
does not fully capture the complexity of real-life disease dynamics. The model assumes
homogeneous contact rates both within and between subpopulations, while actual social
networks are highly structured and heterogeneous, and could potentially lead to an under-
estimation of outbreak clustering. Additionally, the model assumes 100% vaccine eﬀicacy,
which does not reflect real-life conditions, and it does not account for behavioural changes
during infection such as self-isolation or reduced mobility, which are likely considering the
symptoms of the disease [1]. Furthermore, interactions between subpopulations are as-
sumed to be uniformly distributed, implying that each individual has an equal probability
of interacting with individuals from any of the other subpopulations. In reality, factors
such as geographic distance and social connectivity can significantly influence intergroup
interaction rates. Although simplified, the model effectively illustrates how heterogeneous
vaccine coverage influences outbreak dynamics compared to a uniformly vaccinated pop-
ulation.

4.3.4
Public Health Implications
Global averages of vaccine coverage may obscure the instabilities that manifest locally due
to uneven distribution of immunity. Subpopulations with strong internal social cohesion
combined with low vaccine uptake pose a significant risk of large outbreaks. This could
be communities defined by ideological, religious, or ethnic aﬀiliations.
Achieving herd
immunity requires more than reaching a target average; it necessitates assessing whether
unvaccinated individuals are socially clustered or dispersed throughout the population.
The simulations emphasise the critical importance of homogeneous vaccine coverage to
sustain herd immunity and to prevent localised epidemics.
Key Findings of this Chapter:
• The ABM reveals that heterogeneity in vaccine coverage significantly affects outbreak
dynamics. Even when the average vaccine coverage is high, localised pockets of low
immunity can lead to large-scale transmission.
• Achieving and maintaining global herd immunity requires vaccine coverage to be
homogeneously distributed across all subpopulations. Targeted public health inter-
ventions are necessary to close local immunity gaps.
• The origin of the outbreak strongly influences its potential spread. In heterogeneous
settings, outbreaks are more likely to escalate into widespread epidemics when they
begin in poorly vaccinated subpopulations.

Theoretical models provide essential frameworks for characterising measles outbreak dy-

agent‐based simulations to explore how heterogeneity in vaccine coverage alters outbreak
sizes. Although these theoretical simulation studies illuminate fundamental transmission
mechanisms, empirical validation is needed to assess whether real-world surveillance pat-
terns align with these theoretical predictions.
This chapter marks a transition from theoretical investigations to the analysis of empirical
measles outbreak data from European surveillance systems. We address a central question:
Do empirical outbreak sizes align with the predicted outbreak size distributions of the Jansen
and Stollenwerk model, and what do observed patterns in the outbreak data reveal about
country-specific transmission dynamics?
Our analysis unfolds in two integrated phases. First, a descriptive examination of the
dataset outlining its structure and summarising key trends in outbreak reporting, age‐group
involvement and vaccine status. Secondly, we analyse the outbreak size distributions of
countries with suﬀicient data, employ MLE to derive country-specific effective reproduc-
tion numbers, evaluate their fit to the theoretical Jansen and Stollenwerk distribution,
and investigate whether countries exhibit statistically distinct transmission dynamics.
Throughout, we explicitly account for critical surveillance data constraints.
By confronting theoretical predictions with empirical data, this chapter reveals distinctive
measles transmission dynamics across European countries and demonstrates the value of
theoretical frameworks for interpreting complex surveillance data.

To determine whether the differences in ˆRe across countries are significant and thus epi-
demiologically meaningful, we perform pairwise comparative hypothesis testing. While
exploratory in nature, this analysis has a pre-specified focus on country pairs exhibiting
the largest differences in ˆRe. To asses whether transmission dynamics differ between two
countries (A and B), we test the null hypothesis H0 : Re,A = Re,B.
We apply three statistical methods to evaluate differences in ˆRe between countries:
• Log-Likelihood Ratio Test (LLRT): Tests whether the outbreak data from two
countries share the same transmission dynamics (H0) by comparing likelihoods of
individual ˆRe estimates to a pooled data estimate (Section 2.6.3). This test evaluates
differences across the full outbreak size distribution.
• Wald Test: It computes a Z-statistic using the difference in ˆRe values divided by
the pooled standard error derived from Fisher information (Section 2.6.2), assum-
ing asymptotic normality of the MLE. This test assumes that the data follow the
theoretical q(x) distribution making it sensitive to deviations like overdispersion in
empirical data.
• Two-sample Bootstrap Hypothesis Test: Assuming equal transmission dy-
namics (H0), outbreaks from both countries are pooled. For each of 1,000 repli-
cates, we resample nA outbreaks (simulating country A) and nB outbreaks (sim-
ulating country B) from the pooled dataset.

Implications and Limitations
This section assesses the observed patterns of the multinational surveillance data analysis,
while evaluating methodological and structural limitations of the analysis that might affect
the results.
5.3.1
Empirical and Theoretical Distribution Mismatch
The empirical outbreak size distributions exhibit systematic and significant deviations
from the Jansen and Stollenwerk distribution model (Table 5.3). The empirical data dis-
plays an overdispersion, specifically with higher frequency of large outbreak sizes than
predicted by the theoretical model (Figure 5.2).
This overdispersion could arise from
surveillance artifacts, such as under-detection of small outbreaks, reducing their empir-
ical frequency.
Conversely, larger outbreaks could incorrectly be reported as multiple
smaller clusters if epidemiological links are not established, potentially inflating frequency
of smaller outbreaks. Since outbreaks of size 1 (stand-alone cases) are excluded in data,
the risk of potential distortion from biased reporting of single-cases is reduced.
Overdispersion of outbreaks can indicate super-spreading events or spatial clustering
of outbreaks inconsistent with the homogeneous mixing assumptions of the Jansen and
Stollenwerk framework. While this challenges the direct application of such idealised mod-
els to real-world data, it also underscores the models’ value as theoretical benchmarks for
identifying deviations from homogeneous transmission dynamics.
5.3.2
Reproduction Number Uncertainty and Interpretation
The MLEs of Re derive from a distributional model q(x), which the data systematically
deviate from. The mismatch between model and data challenges the reliability of the
ˆRe estimates interpretation as precise, true effective reproduction numbers. Nevertheless,
the estimates retain value capturing key transmission dynamics, and ˆRe provides a useful
metric for comparing transmission differences across countries.
The limited number of available outbreaks in data substantially constraints the re-
liability of the estimates. This is exemplified by Poland’s wide CI for ˆRe, complicating
its use for meaningful comparisons with other countries. The consistently broader boot-
strap CIs relative to parametric Fisher CIs reflect the empirical variability that is not

Age-Specific Transmission Dynamics
in Measles Outbreaks
Preventing large measles outbreaks requires identification of transmission drivers in pop-
ulations where localised susceptibility gaps seed outbreaks. Understanding age-specific
transmission dynamics can be critical for designing targeted public health interventions.
Despite high national vaccine uptake in many European countries, immunity heterogeneity
persists across age groups due to historical variability in vaccine uptake and unvaccinated
subpopulations. Such factors can create age-structured transmission dynamics that can
sustain outbreaks even in populations with relatively high overall vaccination coverage.
This chapter investigates the age-specific transmission dynamics in measles outbreaks
across five European countries analysed in Chapter 5, addressing two key questions: Are
all age groups involved equally in outbreaks? Does the presence of one age-group predict
the involvement of other age groups? Finally, we assess whether childhood vaccination
uptake aligns with the observed transmission patterns.
To address these questions, we employ a binomial model estimating per-case transmission
probabilities by age group while accounting for outbreak sizes. Subsequently, we anal-
yse age group co-occurrence patterns using conditional probabilities to identify clustering.
The analyses will adapt to constraints in the TESSy dataset, including binary age group
reporting in outbreaks lacking case counts. These methods provide a probabilistic frame-
work to gain insights on age-specific transmission, that generate hypotheses with valuable
perspectives about transmission contexts, despite the surveillance data limitations.

6.4.1
Age-Structured Transmission Risks Insights
The binomial model revealed systematic age-specific transmission risks across all countries
(Figure 6.1), with the 0–4 age group exhibiting the highest age-normalised per-case trans-

inance, the countries exhibit distinct age-specific transmission patterns. Spain uniquely
demonstrated elevated risk among the 26–50 age group, suggesting higher susceptibility
in adults compared to the other countries.
The model’s assumption of independence between age groups, while necessary given the
data constraints, ignores potential age clustering in real-world transmission settings. This
simplification might particularly underestimate clustering of ages in large outbreaks, where
multiple age groups are reported as present, and the model can not distinguish whether
one group dominates a large outbreak or not. The systematic underestimation of P

The persistent dominance of age group 0–4 in normalised transmission risks, despite high

outbreaks involving 0–4 year olds rarely lead to spillover to adults, indicating age-isolated
transmission. This non-homogeneous mixing likely restricts the outbreaks within paedi-

remains notable given measles’ high transmission rate.

Discussion
This section integrates the empirical findings and theoretical insights to characterise
measles transmission dynamics within subcritical regimes in relation to the role of vacci-
nation coverage and population heterogeneity. We address the persistent paradox of large
outbreaks occurring in populations with high national vaccination coverage. Following this

7.1
Interpretation of Major Results
Chapters 3-6 examined the impacts of homogeneous and heterogeneous conditions using
different simulation and modelling approaches. The outbreak size distribution model in-
troduced in Section 2.4 was applied to empirical data from five European countries to
evaluate transmission criticality and to examine population dynamics, with emphasis on
vaccination coverage and age-specific transmission patterns. Now, we will synthesise the
major findings across the chapters and reflect on their unified contribution to understand-
ing measles epidemiology.
7.1.1
The Role of Heterogeneity in Outbreak Dynamics
Galton-Watson simulated outbreaks consistently followed the theoretical probability dis-
tribution function q(x) for Re-values at subcriticality in Chapter 3. Meanwhile, empirical
data on outbreak sizes from five European countries all deviated from the theoretical cu-
mulative probability distribution function q(x) in Chapter 5.
A primary reason for this systematic deviation is the violation of the homogeneity
assumption fundamental to the theoretical q(x) model. The model assumes a homoge-
neously mixed population, characterised by a single effective reproduction number for the
entire population. However, real world populations at national scale are far more complex
in structure. Transmission patterns are shaped by both social networks and behaviours,
such as clustering of vaccine hesitancy. Chapter 4 demonstrated, with an ABM, how local
differences in vaccination coverage changed the population outbreak dynamics despite of
constant global vaccination coverage. The simulations of the two heterogeneous scenarios
resulted in higher global effective reproduction numbers compared to the homogeneous
scenario, despite identical overall vaccination coverage. Comparing the cumulative proba-
bility outbreak size distributions for the ABM simulations (Figure 4.2) and the empirical
data (Figure 5.1), reveals that the empirical distributions are consistently right-skewed
relative to the theoretical predictions. This resembles the distributions observed in the
heterogeneous Scenario 2 and Scenario 3 of the ABM simulations. These findings thus
indicate that the empirical data originates from heterogeneous populations.
Heterogeneous susceptibility clusters emerge when people with shared vaccine-related be-
liefs or barriers form distinct local communities. Such clusters require systematic lower
immunity within a distinct social network. Such communities can be driven by factors
as resources, religion, ethnicity, political ideology, education, etc. It is important to note
that heterogeneity does not arise from individual random omissions, it requires systematic
lower immunisation within social structures.

an MCV1 coverage of 60%, while nationally the MCV1 coverage was > 95%. A similar
epidemic in the orthodox protestants community occurred in 1999-2000, with minimal
cases reported in the years between and up until now [24, 25]. This suggests that epidemic-
scale transmission temporarily depleted the susceptible pool within this subpopulation.
This aligns with Scenario 3 from the ABM simulations (Chapter 4), where transmission
either resulted in epidemics driven by the low-immunity population or small outbreaks
with rapid termination.
Not all heterogeneous populations manifest as pronounced as in the Dutch example, and
outbreaks do not necessarily lead to depletion of susceptibles. Spain and multiple other Eu-
ropean countries have had recurrent outbreaks involving Roma communities characterised
by low vaccine coverage [26]. In the UK, there are examples of local vaccine decline in
some London boroughs, where childhood vaccination coverage with both MMR doses is
down to 60% [27]. These are examples of subtler and more persistent transmission drivers
that do not cause depletion of susceptibles. The permeable large unvaccinated clusters in
London boroughs sustain transmission through frequent viral introductions and within-
group spread. The ABM Scenario 2 reflects these dynamics of moderate heterogeneous
populations that can still produce large outbreaks.
7.1.2
Age as an Axis of Heterogeneity
The binomial model of Section 6.1 revealed that the 0-4 year age group consistently exhib-
ited the highest age-normalised per-case transmission probability, across the five analysed
countries. This age-group dominated the outbreaks, representing between 37% (in the
United Kingdom) and 52% (in Ireland) of the relative transmission risk. The co-occurrence
analysis further demonstrated limited spillover from children to older cohorts, particularly
to the 26-50 year age group. The results indicated age-segregated transmission patterns.
This segregation likely contributes to the lower transmission risks observed in age group
26-50 compared to younger age cohorts where inter-group transmission is more frequent.
Age-specific settings such as daycare or schools might sustain transmission within unvac-
cinated paediatric clusters.
Despite the notably high national vaccine uptakes across the countries, the 0-4 age
group still exhibits the largest transmission risk. Under assumptions of homogeneous vac-
cine uptake and social mixing, these findings contradict expectations, and appear inconsis-
tent with the observed elevated national effective reproduction numbers. This divergence
indicates localised immunity gaps within paediatric subpopulations, that national metrics
fail to capture.
The pattern of paediatric dominance in measles transmission, despite high national vac-
cination uptake and declared elimination of endemic transmission, is not unique to our
findings. Recent data show that 30% of US cases (Jan-May 2025) and 43% of European
cases (2024) were of age-group 0-4 years [21, 4]. Parents of young children (born post 2010)
may be susceptible due to historical immunity gaps and vaccine hesitancy, and assumably
these parents would get infected from their infected children. However, our analysis and
WHO findings present a paradox of limited parental infections from children.
Our co-occurrence analysis illustrates that children (0-4, 5-14) and adults (26-50) do
not mutually amplify one another’s involvement in outbreaks. Potentially, daycare centers
function as transmission hubs, sustaining outbreaks through age-specific settings among
unvaccinated children. Meanwhile, their parents in age group 26-50 benefit from more
homogeneous susceptibility, due to mixed immunity from vaccines and natural exposure
before vaccination programs were introduced. Susceptibility in this era, before endemic

Another possible explanation of the disproportionally high infection risk of the 0-4

7.2
Implications for Measles Control
The findings of this study address a persistent paradox in measles epidemiology; despite
relatively high national vaccination coverage in many European countries, large outbreaks
continue to occur due to heterogeneous immunity distribution and not from minor short-
falls in aggregate national coverage. Our ABM simulations (Chapter 4 Scenario 3) demon-
strate that achieving the WHO’s 95% national coverage target remains insuﬀicient when
subpopulations exhibit significantly lower vaccination coverages. These susceptibility clus-
ters function as epidemic incubators, enabling large outbreaks even in populations meeting
national herd immunity thresholds. Consequently, eﬀicient transmission monitoring neces-
sitates identifying possible outbreak-driving subpopulations. In identified heterogeneous
transmission settings, effective reproduction numbers should be estimated locally rather
than nationally to yield strategic insights.
Although multiple factors contribute to heterogeneity in immunity and contact patterns,
age structure represents a substantial axis of heterogeneity. Children aged 0-4 years rep-
resent a high transmission risk group, demanding targeted interventions. Proactive mon-
itoring of paediatric settings (as daycares and schools) in high-risk regions, is essential for
detecting potential susceptibility gaps. Notably, the variation in MCV1 administration
schedules across countries (e.g., 9–12 months in Austria versus 12–14 months in Poland),
indicates a need for further research optimising dosing schedules, ensuring sustained immu-
nity coverage between maternal antibody decay and MCV1 administration [30]. Further-
more, optimising the MCV2 administration schedule (which varies widely across studied

countries) could reduce vulnerability in the 0–4 age group, by immunizing the 5% of
children that fall into an immunity gap due to MCV1 failure [23, 1].
Effective measles control requires a strategic focus on susceptibility clusters rather than
reliance on national coverage averages. Although the majority of European countries have
achieved elimination of endemic measles transmission, outbreaks continue to occur with
importation of cases [2]. Whether imported cases lead to limited or large outbreaks de-
pends on the local heterogeneity in immunity. Current large outbreaks in Europe and the
U.S. elevate cross-border risks, particularly for countries with subnational susceptibility
clusters [31, 21]. Beyond case importations, travel and immigration may introduce sus-
ceptible individuals into populations, posing additional transmission risks. Immigration
from regions with lower vaccination coverage thus threatens the immunity of countries
with subcritical national transmission, especially if susceptible immigrants cluster within
localised immunity gaps (as seen in Spain) [29].
Addressing these risks could involve strengthening vaccination rules for immigration or in-
ternational travel, or exploring feasibility of proof-of-vaccination requirements specifically
for travel from high incidence regions. Other measures could be implementing mandatory
vaccination for school enrolment. Case importation is largely inevitable in an intercon-
nected world, thus public health efforts must prioritise reducing the population-wide sus-
ceptibility. Reactive measures, such as breaking transmission chains by contact tracing
and post-exposure vaccination, have proven effective in countries with sporadic, limited
outbreaks (e.g., Denmark), where containment is achievable [32]. However, in countries
experiencing higher transmission, such as those in this analysis, reactive measures become
more challenging and require more resources. Proactive prevention yields better results in
such settings; refining the vaccination goal from 95% national coverage to ensuring 95%
coverage in all population subgroups, will substantially enhance outbreak prevention.
7.3
Methodological and Conceptual Limitations of the
Study
While the individual chapters addressed model-specific constraints, this study faces broader
limitations affecting the reliability, generalisability, and interpretation of its findings.
The empirical analysis was constrained by significant data limitations beyond censor-
ing issues, which have been discussed continuously in previous chapters. Data complica-
tions as under-reporting of smaller outbreaks may have biased the outbreak size distribu-
tions by truncating the left tail (Chapter 5). Further, public health interventions might
suppress large outbreaks with interruption of transmission chains. These surveillance ar-
tifacts could influence the observed frequencies in each end of the distribution.
Chapter 3 revealed that the accuracy of the ˆ
Re was improved substantially with increasing
sample size. The simulated data indicates that a minimum of 200 outbreaks are required
to achieve 95% coverage of the 95% Fisher confidence intervals. However, the country
sample sizes in the TESSy dataset constrained the selection of countries for analysis, lim-
iting the study to five countries. Yet, none of these countries reported more than 200
uncensored outbreaks, and only the UK reported over 100. A minimum threshold of 20
uncensored outbreaks per country was applied, which inevitably compromises the accuracy
of the confidence intervals and, thereby, the reliability of ˆ
Re. Moreover, restricting the
analysis to five countries limits the generalisability of the findings, particularly to regions
with differing social structures and transmission dynamics.

The limited amount of outbreak data further necessitated the aggregation of all re-
ported cases over the available years. Consequently, the analysis included outbreak data
from 2009 to 2025, with specific time spans varying across countries. This aggregation
inevitably obscures potential changes in transmission dynamics.
For instance, during
the COVID-19 pandemic, only a limited number of outbreaks were reported, likely due
to either reduced transmission or under-reporting. Furthermore, the resulting long-term
estimates of effective reproduction numbers were compared against averaged childhood
vaccination uptakes in the same years, representing a critical simplification.
Inclusion
of extended time periods potentially masks temporal epidemiological patterns, yet is a
necessary compromise to ensure suﬀicient sample sizes for statistical robustness.
The theoretical frameworks employed in this study presents conceptual limitations. Jansen
and Stollenwerk’s model is insuﬀicient to capture outbreak risks through reproduction
numbers in heterogeneous populations due to the homogeneous mixing assumption.
The divergence of empirical data from the theoretical model undermines the validity
of the Re estimates as accurate representations of the transmission potential in the popu-
lations, since the deviations likely reflect heterogeneity. In a study from 2003, Jansen and
Stollenwerk applied their model on transmission shifts in the UK to investigate the impact
of a declining vaccination uptake [14]. They reported statistically significant increase in
Re, from 0.47 to 0.83, despite a minor decline (less than 10%) in childhood vaccination up-
take over a three-year period. These results are inconsistent with the models homogeneous
assumptions, under which Re = R0S depends on overall population-level susceptibility. A
10% drop in vaccination uptake over three years would add to the population-level suscep-
tibility with under 1 percent. This paradox suggests that the estimated Re shifts are likely
driven by an increasing susceptibility heterogeneity, rather than by a uniform increase in
transmission across the population, which invalidates the interpretation as national re-
production numbers. The same limitation applies to this study, as the Re estimates do
not reflect national transmission dynamics, making them uninterpretable intermediates
between population and subpopulation dynamics.
A more fundamental limitation concerns depletion of susceptibles. The Jansen and Stollen-
werk model assumes an infinite number of susceptibles, enabling arbitrarily large outbreaks
as transmission dynamics move towards criticality. Realistically, epidemics in subpopula-
tions could cause depletion of local susceptible pools. This would establish a temporary
herd immunity and reduce the outbreak frequency until new susceptibles accumulate in
the subpopulation. The consequence would be skewed outbreak size distributions caused
by a reduction in large outbreaks following major epidemic events. However, the outbreak
sizes in the empirical data are relatively small, suggesting that depletion of susceptibles is
unlikely to be a distorting factor in this study.
The analysis of age-related transmission risks and co-occurrence patterns was particularly
constrained by the data structure. Without access to case counts and accounting for out-
break sizes, the binary co-occurrence analysis obscured the reconstruction of transmission
chains and likely introduced biases due to inflated clustering effects. The employment
of age as a proxy for transmission heterogeneity ignored critical dimensions like socio-
economic status, ethnicity, and belief systems. This may cause over-interpretation of age
as the source of heterogeneity. Though, the age-based analysis generated valuable hy-
potheses, the absence of case-level outbreak data limits the ability to distinguish genuine
transmission drivers from artifacts arising from data structures.

Novelty and Contributions
This thesis advances the understanding of measles transmission dynamics in populations
with high vaccination coverage through the contributions of
• Unique synthesising of theoretical homogeneous models (the Jansen and Stollenwerk
model), agent-based simulations capturing vaccination heterogeneity, and empirical
analyses using multi-country ECDC surveillance data, offering a comprehensive per-
spective on the drivers of measles outbreaks.
• Development of a censored MLE framework addressing a key limitation of the Jansen
and Stollenwerk model by enabling robust estimation of effective reproduction num-
bers under any data censoring thresholds. Independent re-derivation of the Jansen
and Stollenwerk outbreak‐size model (including correction of a previously overlooked
condition error) to ensure mathematical consistency and demonstration of back-
ground and assumptions.
• Demonstrating that localised clusters of low vaccination coverage, using an ABM
(Scenario 3), can destabilise herd immunity, providing an explanation for why the
95% national vaccination coverage target by WHO [21] fails to fully prevent large
outbreaks.
• Empirical analysis of surveillance data from Austria, Ireland, Poland, Spain, and
the UK revealed substantial country-specific variation and consistent heterogeneity
in measles transmission dynamics across countries.
• Uncovering consistent over-representation of the 0-4 year age group in outbreak data
across Austria, Ireland, Poland, Spain, and the UK, despite high national childhood
vaccine uptake, indicated susceptibility gaps among young children.
7.5
Generalisability to Vaccine-Preventable Diseases
While this thesis focuses on measles, our findings illuminate fundamental principles gov-
erning outbreaks of any vaccine-preventable disease. The destabilisation of herd immunity
through heterogeneous immunity applies broadly to any disease requiring high population
immunity thresholds. Crucially, measles’ extreme transmissibility (R0 ≈12–18) makes
it more sensitive to immunity gaps compared to other diseases [3]. Measles can thereby
function as an early indicator of susceptibility clustering and vaccine coverage failures.
Diseases with lower R0 such as mumps (R0 ≈4) exhibit similar dynamics but tolerate
larger susceptibility clusters before outbreaks escalate [33]. This explains why global MMR
programs more readily eliminate mumps and rubella (R0 ≈6-7) than measles transmis-
sion [34].
Methodologically, the homogenous Jansen and Stollenwerk model (Chapter 3), our
censored MLE framework (Chapter 5), and age-structured binomial model (Chapter 6)
provide methods to quantify transmission in near-elimination settings of diseases (e.g.,
rubella).
While these frameworks assume subcritical dynamics, our ABM framework
(Chapter 4) can be applied broader to infectious diseases beyond elimination context

Ultimately, the disease elimination strategy of vaccinating subpopulations is not re-

7.6
Future Research
This study has uncovered several avenues for future research relevant to measles elim-
ination. A natural next step would be to utilise case-level outbreak reports to enable
a thorough analysis of the transmission chains. Detailed empirical data could facilitate
deeper insights into age-specific transmission dynamics, socio-geographical clustering, and
local vaccination coverage, that were beyond the scope of this study. Given the persis-
tent transmission observed in the 0–4 age group, a deeper investigation of optimal vaccine
administration schedules of MCV should also be considered for further research to ensure
sustained immunity following maternal antibody decay and potential primary vaccine fail-
ure.
Furthermore, the development of more sophisticated hybrid transmission models could
improve estimation of the effective reproduction number in real populations. Our anal-
ysis revealed that none of the investigated outbreak distributions observed in European
countries actually fit the model proposed by Jansen and Stollenwerk, likely due to the
simplifying model assumptions [6]. Incorporating dynamic contact networks, that reflect
demographic heterogeneity such as age-structuring, and include more disease-specific pa-
rameters, like incubation time, across European populations could provide a universally
applicable model, offering a better understanding of measles transmission criticality in
Europe. Such models could be used in cost-benefit analyses to compare the impact of
subpopulation-targeted (e.g., daycare-focused campaigns) versus nationwide vaccination
strategies, and would be particularly valuable for public health policies.
To advance understanding of vaccine heterogeneity, future work should investigate the
epidemiological impact of susceptibility cluster permeability, by quantifying how social
isolation regulates outbreak risk. Our ABM assumed fixed between-group mixing (contact
heterogeneity parameter ϵ = 0.1), but real-world clusters can exhibit variable integration
with broader society. Systematically varying the level of permeability of the less immu-
nized cluster could identify thresholds where local Re > 1 emerges despite high national
coverage. Theoretically, such thresholds could be empirically validated through mobility
data from clustered populations (e.g., London boroughs or religious minorities) to cor-
relate cluster isolation with outbreak severity, and could establish measurable isolation
metrics to assess real-world cluster risk.
Another promising direction is the investigation of imported measles cases into coun-
tries that have obtained subcritical transmission dynamics. In countries with non-endemic
measles, the number of outbreaks is critically linked to the frequency of imported cases,
raising concerns when large outbreaks occur as recently seen in the U.S. [4]. High rates of
imported cases may surpass the effects of internal transmission dynamics, so understand-
ing the relationship between importation and internal heterogeneity could significantly
influence the prospects of measles elimination in Europe.

Conclusion
This thesis demonstrates that in European subcritical populations the size of measles out-
breaks is predominantly determined by localised immunity gaps arising from heterogeneity
rather than by homogeneous susceptibility or minor shortfalls in national aggregate vac-
cination coverage.
Our empirical analysis of outbreak data from Austria, Spain, Ireland, Poland, and the
United Kingdom, combined with agent-based modelling and theoretical model frameworks,
revealed key insights into measles dynamics.
The empirical distributions of outbreak sizes systematically deviated from the homo-
geneous predictions of the Jansen and Stollenwerk framework (p < 0.01), a divergence
that ultimately reinforces this thesis’ core insight that subnational heterogeneity in im-
munity enhance outbreak sizes in non-endemic subcritical countries. ABM simulations
demonstrated that clusters of low vaccination coverage destabilises herd immunity, en-
abling large outbreaks in populations with overall 95% vaccination coverage. In hetero-
geneous populations, national effective reproduction number estimates masks substantial
local variations. While heterogeneity in population immunity has many origins, age con-
sistently emerged as a critical factor driving transmission risk. Our analysis identified the
0-4 age group as exhibiting the highest age-normalised per-case transmission risk across
all five countries. The elevated risk indicates immunity gaps within the cohort, despite
high national childhood vaccine uptake.
Distinct national transmission profiles were identified across the five countries.
Spain
displayed an elevated adult transmission risk and a heavier outbreak tail compared to the
other countries, while Ireland exhibited the most pronounced paediatric dominance and
the lowest estimated effective reproduction number. Besides country-specific variations,
the consistent theme across Austria, Spain, Ireland, Poland, and the United Kingdom is
the fundamental role of heterogeneous immunity in facilitating measles spread.
Large ongoing outbreaks in the U.S., combined with rising infection rates in Europe, are
expected to increase case importation, underscoring the need for European countries to
strengthen population immunity. Public health strategies should move beyond national
vaccination targets by ensuring 95% coverage in all population subgroups to prevent large
measles outbreaks. This necessitates prioritised monitoring of high-risk groups (e.g., chil-
dren aged 0–4 years), implementation of subpopulation-targeted vaccination policies, and
rapid containment protocols for identified susceptibility clusters.
