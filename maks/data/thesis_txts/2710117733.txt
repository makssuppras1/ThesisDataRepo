Abstract
The project presents the state-of-the-art in injection moulding technology and process sim-
ulations in the area as a foundation for the subsequent challenges.
The project studied
the effect of back-pressure on injection moulding process parameters and its influences on
product consistency and efficiency. The increment of back-pressure increases the plasticizing
time while the metering performance decline. Therefore the production capacity decrease.
The project work concludes that the injection processing parameters are uniquely connected
as a function of back-pressure. In a statistical view, as a function of back-pressure, specific
parameters exhibit linear relationships as mean value, and certain parameters exhibit third-
degree polynomial relationships as in variation percentage while others contain both.
The study focuses on combining the polymer behavior of SABIC PP 576S with scientific
injection moulding principles. The study evaluated raw data from a sophisticated injection
moulding machine ENGEL e-motion 80 and used a statistical analysis application JMP/SAS
to analyze the raw data output.
The project discovers that the polypropylene SABIC PP 576S has a dependent viscosity
behavior on the processing setting back-pressure. The condition state of the polypropylene
during injection moulding processing affects the produced product quality and consistency
production and is dependent on the back-pressure process setting.
Moreover, it was observed that the polymer melt state as a function of back-pressure exhib-
ited three regions; "stable," "unstable," and "stabilizing" region. Additionally, as a function
of back-pressure, several injection processing parameters interacted consequently when the
viscosity value was changed as a "Domino-effect." Hence, the viscosity change as a function
of back-pressure is discovered to be the most significant effect on component quality and
consistency production.
Additionally, the project introduces a process "fingerprint," stating that energy consumption
of the transition barrel zones can reveal the polymer melt’s viscosity state. By monitoring
the energy consumption of the transition barrel zone, the viscosity state is predicted, and
the ideal back-pressure value for consistent product quality and production energy efficiency
can be determined.

1.1
Introduction
For many years, injection moulding has been a solid manufacturing technique for creating
various plastic goods and components. Injection moulding covers the needs of a wide range of
industries and is undergoing rapid change due to new technology in various vertical markets.
Injection moulding is unquestionably an energy-intensive process, as the entire process is
based on heating polymers, shaping them, and then cooling them again. The mentioned
processing requires a significant amount of energy to be added and then retaken. The most
challenging aspect in injection moulding is heating the plastic[16]. Not just because a signif-
icant amount of energy must be provided during the phase, but it is critical to maintaining
the molten plastic’s homogeneous quality.
Product quality is fundamental for modern plastics manufacturers and particularly in high-
tech areas such as medical and pharmaceutical industry, which require high technical spec-
ifications and stringent quality standards [17]. A detailed mapping of the process and an
improved optimization method can thus not only save energy and CO2 emissions, but also
ensure the highest possible product quality for clients, while the knowledge gained from
such an analysis saves the manufacturer energy and cost for every time new productions are
started.[18].
Since 2015, plastics manufacturers have reduced annual energy use by approximately 17,500
MWatt per hour, or slightly more than 5,000 tons of CO2. Now, the Danish Energy Agency
and the Plastics Industry have intensified their collaboration to reduce energy usage through
process optimization and much more.[19]

Project background
At any point along the screw, a particular temperature distribution is current. The temper-
ature change throughout the melt will affect the polymer flow during mould filling. Variance
analysis in the temperature distribution between shot-to-shot will affect the filling, resulting
in cycle-to-cycle variation for the produced components. It is well established through prac-
tical experience and data gathered from injection moulding that back-pressure affects the
weight variation of the part and that a third-degree polynomial function can approximate
the correlation between back-pressure and variation.
However, no investigation has been conducted to substantiate the model’s assumption. This
idea should be supported by a more extensive investigation and analysis of screw flow during
plasticization. The current method utilizes variance in part weight as a product fingerprint.
Weight is equivalent to volume, product quality, and consistency. Hence, weighing compo-
nents require an operator’s presence and a weighing scale and have measurement uncertainty
like all measurement devices. Suppose process monitoring metrics from the injection mould-
ing machine can be used as process fingerprints. In that case, it will be conceivable to reduce
the uncertainty and allow the operator to engage in other productive activities. That, in
turn, will increase both qualities of production and the productivity of operations.

Goal and objective
The goal of the MSc Project is to take the mentioned above project background topic to be

• Analyze the effect of back-pressure on part variation and determine process fingerprints

part weight variation and find correlated effects.

1.6
Research questions and motivation
Today, with the current knowledge, injection moulding processing can be optimized with
98 % accuracy. However, the 2 % left uncertainty can be optimized further with additional
knowledge and investigations. Sometimes, injection moulding engineers base their process
setting on "gut feelings and experience" without the solid support of scientific processing
concepts and the relation to material science engineering. The absence of the necessary
undiscovered scientific knowledge methodology for solving the 2 % uncertainty left leads the
moulder engineers to confusion and frustration. Furthermore, it will result in an unsustain-
able, inefficient optimization approach, leading to wasted production trails, more significant
energy consumption, and unnecessarily long time spent.
If a pre-guide design approach
supported by scientific knowledge processing or process "fingers print" is given before the
optimization procedures, many of the mentioned issues can be solved.
Some of the current plasticizing optimization approaches that moulder engineers use today
are based on temperature settings and material supplier recommendations. Furthermore,
the switch-over point and the hold pressure are used and optimized by the visual inspection
on shot analysis. These mentioned optimization approach results in significant and stable
processing output with an expected weight variation in the final part of about 1-2 %
Additionally, a plasticizing optimization approach mainly used in the medical and mobile
phone industries utilizes the processes setting of back-pressure. The back-pressure process
settings approach will provide a variation of better than 0,5 % and approximately 0.2 %.
Moreover, if the barrel temperature settings combined with screw rotational speed and back-
pressure are optimized, in that case, the variation is expected to be even better, hence the
going from 98.7 to 99 or 99.9 % optimization accuracy.
However, not many theories and studies are available for the optimization approach of the
back-pressure process setting. Therefore, the motivation of this study is to investigate this
gap and gain a deeper scientific understanding of the field. Moreover, to do comprehensive
research and implement experiments to accomplish a process "fingerprint" and a pre-guide
design approach that will benefit moulding engineers in the future to increase their produc-
tion efficiency work. Hence, to save time and energy and compensate for wasted production
trails while maintaining sustainable production trails and part quality products.

2.2
Injection moulding technology introduction
The most common techniques used in manufacturing plastic products are injection moulding
and extrusion. The Hyatt brothers came up with the idea of injection moulding plastics to
make billiard balls. The concept was based on a technology for injecting metal castings by
John Smith. Plastic injection moulding has come a long way since then. Because of the
simplicity of the concept, the efficiency of production, and the ability to produce precision
parts with fine details, the technology became a popular approach to implementing plastic
parts. Because of advancements in Science and Technology, the criteria for moulded parts
have become increasingly stringent. Demand for tighter tolerances and more complicated
parts has grown and continues to grow.[20]

Sizing the injection unit
Primarily and optimally, the injection unit should be sized to hold material for two complete
cycles. In other words, each time a cycle is finished, 50% of the injection cylinder’s contents
should be drained. Because material shoots into the mould during the injection phase, this
emptied capacity is referred to as the quantity of shot a machine requires for each cycle.
While the 50% rule is optimal, shot size should never be less than 20% of cylinder capacity
or greater than 80%.
The shot size is determined by the heat sensitivity of the material being moulded. Certain
materials are extremely heated sensitive and readily burn, whereas others are significantly
less heat sensitive and can endure extended exposure to elevated temperatures. Heat sensi-
tivity indicates how long a substance can remain within a heated injection cylinder before
degrading. Degraded materials are incapable of producing high-quality products.[1]

2.3.2
The heating cylinder
The heating cylinder is the injection unit’s heart, often known as the barrel. Barrels are of-
ten constructed in the shape of a long, circular tube and are made of a low-cost steel grade.
The inside of the tube is lined, often with a thin sleeve of high-quality hard tool steel that is
abrasion-resistant and capable of withstanding the abrasive nature of the injection process.
Typically, the sleeve has a significant amount of chromium.
The barrels are integrated with heating bands. The bands are electrically actuated and are
evenly spaced along the barrel’s length. In Figure 2.2, three heater zones are depicted: Rear,
center, and front. Each zone has three or more heater bands (depending on the injection
cylinder’s length), and each zone is independently controlled by an electrical unit housed
in the machine’s control panel. Each temperature control device is supplied with temper-
ature data via a thermocouple inserted into a hole in the wall of the heating barrel in the
zone it is controlling. The control unit then determines whether additional heat is required
and, if so, activates the zone’s heater bands. When the selected temperature is attained,
the thermocouple notifies the control unit, which then ceases supplying power to the heater
bands until the temperature drops below the selected value, at which point the cycle restarts.
The control unit’s minimum and maximum temperature restrictions determine whether the
heater bands should be powered or de-energized. Each heating zone is allocated a single
control unit and thermocouple. However, because each zone contains three or more heaters,
each control unit effectively manages three or more heaters simultaneously. Because all of
the heaters are connected, anything the control unit does to one heater affects all of the
heaters in that zone.[1]
The injection screw design
The screw is critical in generating a homogenous melt. The screw generates shear heat,
which aids in melting and mixing homogenizing melt. Additionally, it assists in precisely
measuring the volume of shots to be injected into the mould. Numerous screw designs have
been designed to accommodate various materials and their requirements. The most often
used screw is named a General Purpose screw, or GP screw.Figure 2.3 illustrates the design
of a GP screw.[2]

The screw is constructed in the manner of a shank with flights wrapped around it. Typically,
only one ribbon is used to produce the flights.
Outside diameter is the diameter of an imaginary cylinder formed by connecting the
flights’ outside areas. The exterior diameter is constant and slightly smaller than the inte-
rior diameter of the barrel. .
Root diameter is the diameter of the shank. The diameter of the screw’s root varies from
back to front, depending on the screw segment.
Channel depth is the difference between the outside diameter and the root diameter is
the feed depth. Since the root diameter changes, the feed depth also changes from the back
to the front of the screw.
Feed zone is the segment of the screw that picks up material from the feed aperture, which
is located at the bottom of the hopper and begins to soften the polymer as it is transported.
The root diameter is the smallest and most stable. Additionally, depths remain consistent;
they are referred to as the feed depth. The material that enters the feed zone is picked up
and softened during the screw’s rotational motion. The substance must never be entirely
molten in this part due to the Screw slipping. Screw slipping is a word that is frequently
used to describe a phenomenon where the melt rotates in lockstep with the screw, preventing
the screw from returning to pick up additional material and construct the next injection shot.
At the transition zone , the root diameter steadily rises, decreasing the channel depth.
The root diameter of the zone begins identical to the root diameter of the feed section and
steadily grows till the section finishes. As a result, the feed depth gradually decreases. As
the screw rotates, the lubricant becomes softer. Compression of the pellets begins, and the
air and any other volatiles are driven out between them as the depth of the feed decreases.
The plastic is transferred with the assistance of heat generated by the external heater bands
and shear generated by the screw’s rotation. The plastic becomes brittle and begins to melt.

Metering Zone is the final zone and is located closest to the machine’s nozzle. The channels
in this portion are shallow in comparison to the other two. Because the root diameter remains
constant, the channel depth remains constant. Moreover, the shot is constructed by reversing
the screw until it reaches a predetermined linear position (shot size); the metering depth must
be kept to a minimum to minimize fluctuation in the amount of melt between consecutive
shots. When the metering depth is increased, the amount of material fed in front of the
screw can change, resulting in inconsistency. However, as depth decreases, shear increases,
increasing the danger of material degradation, particularly for shear-sensitive materials such
as PVC. [2]

Screw tip and check ring
The mounting of a typical screw tip on the face of an injection screw is illustrated in Fig-
ure 2.5. The tip is placed via a check ring and seat to prevent molten material from spilling
back over the screw flights during the injection. The tip is frequently left-handed threaded to
counterbalance the screw’s natural spinning motion. A right-hand thread tends to unscrew
due to the screw’s spinning action. The angle and length of the screw tip are determined by
the viscosity of the moulded plastic.
Figure 2.5: Screw tip and non-return valves [1]
Nonreturn valve
The check-ring nonreturn valve mechanism illustrated in Figure 2.5 is used to prevent molten
plastic from leaking back over the screw as it advances, as a plunger to inject material into
the mould. The check ring is then permitted to advance while the screw augers advance
fresh material in preparation for the following cycle. The check ring’s operation allows the
material to advance forward of the screw point. The series of nonreturns is as follows:
1. The screw advances, injecting molten material into a mould.
2. The check ring is pressed against and seals against the screw tip seat, preventing
material from going back over the screw.
3. The screw stop to press and begins to revolve (providing new material).
4. The check ring advances forward as a result of the pressure increase.
5. Molten plastic flows into the gap between the screw tip and the screw body.[1]

Screw diameter and L/D ratio
The L/D ratio is the ratio of the screw’s length to its diameter. The greater the L/D ratio,
the more homogeneous the melt is.[2]
The clamping unit
The clamping unit secures the mould, holds it closed during injection, opens and closes the
mould quickly, allowing for part ejection, and protects the mould closure. The clamp has a
fixed and movable platen; because the stationary platen supports the mould’s core, it has
a hole for the nozzle to meet the sprue bushing. Typically, a locating ring surrounds the
sprue bushing and helps align the mould with the nozzle. The stationary platen is frequently
cooled with water when the mould has a hot manifold. [21]
Electric clamp unit
There are no hydraulic pumps on an electric machine. A servomotor extends and retracts
the toggles in the clamping unit, and a reduction drive gear is employed to create the ap-
propriate forces. Because there is no hydraulic system to generate heat, the clamps are
much more stable, and the servomotors allow the exact movement of the machine compo-
nents. Additionally, it is significantly more hygienic to operate than other types of moulding
equipment. All-electric machines are the preferred method of manufacturing the majority of
medical products.[21]
Clamp specification
A clamping unit’s key parameter is its maximum clamp force, which is the force required to
keep the mould closed during injection and packing. When the clamping force is insufficient
to overcome the injection and packing forces, the mould can be pushed open during injection
or packing and resulting in molten polymer or flash leakage.
Control system
The control system ensures consistency and reproducibility in machine operation. It con-
tinuously monitors and regulates processing factors such as temperature, pressure, injection
rate, screw speed, and position. Process control directly affects the quality of the finished
item and the economics of the process. Process control systems can range from a basic on/off
relay control to a highly sophisticated closed-loop microprocessor-based control.[22]
Mould system
The mould system is comprised of guide pillars, stationary and moving platens, and moulding
plates that contain the cavity, sprue and runner systems, ejector pins, and cooling channels.
The mould is simply a heat exchanger through which the molten thermoplastic hardens into
the desired shape and dimensions determined by the cavity. The moulding system moulds
the plastics contained within the cavity and ejects the moulded object.
The stationary
platen is secured to the machine’s barrel side and is linked to the moving platen via guide
pillars. Generally, the cavity plate is fixed to the stationary plate and contains the injec-
tion nozzle. The core plate moves in lockstep with the platen, guided by the guide pillars.[22]

A typical moulded system comprises two components: the delivery system and the moulded
part. The delivery system, which transports molten plastic from the machine nozzle to the

Cooling System design
The mould can be considered a heat exchanger, with the circulating coolant transferring
heat from the hot polymer melt. Mould cooling can account for more than two-thirds of
the entire cycle time when manufacturing injection-moulded thermoplastic parts. See Fig-
ure 2.12. A well-designed cooling circuit cuts down on cooling time, which improves overall
output. Furthermore, uniform cooling enhances part quality by decreasing residual stresses
and preserving dimensional precision and stability. [23]
Hot runners
The hot runner system connects the injection moulding machine to the mould cavities and
functions as a melt flow system. It is integrated into the mould and forms a part of the
mould. Except for hardening runners in conventional moulds, the thermoplastic melt "re-
mains" molten inside the system for at least one injection cycle [24].
The mentioned is
referred to as "sprueless moulding" [25].
Many benefits come with hot runners;
• The elimination of runners reduces the projected plane, which results in a reduction
of the clamping force.
• Hot runner technology enables the most creative geometric design of gates possible.
• With the exception of sprues and runners in conventional moulds, hot runner systems
do not require cooling, and low pressure requirements can be met even at extremely
low flow rates.
• Expanding the cross section of the channel in the hot runner system (while maintaining
the maximum permitted living duration) reduces the melt’s shear stress.[26]

2.4
Polymer and materials sciences
The primary source of concern for most manufacturers of plastic components is a lack of
specific information about the more than thirty generic families of plastic resins. There are
hundreds of different product types contained within these generic families. They range
from pure polymers (homopolymers) through copolymers (blended polymers) to mixes and
alloys of various polymers and additives (e.g., glass, minerals, carbon, elastomers, nucleators,
lubricants, processing aids, and flame retardants) and color pigments. Any modification to
the concentration of any of these ingredients will also result in a change in the product des-
ignation. These changes are less noticeable in the base polymers, as changes in molecular
weight are reflected in viscosity differences, limiting their processing ease.[27]
Polyolefins
Polyolefins are the simplest type of hydrocarbon, containing both carbon and hydrogen
atoms.The most widely injection-moulded polyolefins are polyethylene PP and polypropy-
lene. Polyethylene PP is a widely utilized material because of its superior electrical insulating
qualities, chemical resistance, impact resistance, and cheap cost. Polyethylene’s characteris-
tics can be adjusted to meet specific application requirements.
Temperature molecular weight phase diagram for crystalline polymers
Polymers such as polyethylene lack the common external form associated with crystals, and
closer examination reveals that they share some properties with crystalline materials. They
exhibit distinct X-ray diffraction patterns and differential scanning calorimetry melting pat-
terns (DSC). Although the precise nature of this crystallinity is unknown in some cases,
it appears that polymer segments pass through zones with a highly ordered molecular ar-
rangement. These zones, in some ways, act as knots or crosslinks, combining the individual
molecules of these materials. The effect of heating crystalline polymers from low tempera-
tures is more complicated than heating amorphous polymers from low temperatures.
At first, the material is rigid and difficult to work with; as the temperature approaches, the
glass transition temperature, Tg. Lightly crystalline materials soften slightly and take on the
appearance of leather, whereas highly crystalline materials retain their properties relatively
well. Additional heating causes the crystals to melt, frequently over a wide temperature
range, and the polymer becomes rubbery. Whether it melts or decomposes first depends on
the type of polymer and its molecular weight. [3]

2.4.1
Polymer morphology
The morphology of a polymer refers to the arrangement of its molecules.
Polymers are
classified into two types based on the various configurations of their molecules: amorphous
and crystalline polymers. The molecules in amorphous polymers are randomly distributed
without any structure or arrangement. This sample would resemble a large bowl of cooked
spaghetti under a high-power microscope. In the case of crystalline polymers, certain regions
of the sample contain highly ordered and structured molecules. Each of these areas is referred
to as a crystallite. No polymer is completely crystalline, and there will always be areas where
molecules are distributed randomly. Thus, a polymer is truly semi-crystalline in nature, with
crystallites coexisting with amorphous regions. A molecule can contain both amorphous and
crystalline regions. The degree of crystallinity indicates the concentration of crystallites in
a sample. Figure 2.7 illustrates the distinction between the two morphologies.[2]
Figure 2.7: Molecule arrangement in amorphous and semi-crystalline polymers [2]
A polymer can be amorphous or crystalline for two primary reasons: the geometric regularity
of the polymers and the strength of the intermolecular forces between them. Geometric reg-
ularity refers to the arrangement of the main chain’s groups. Stereoregular polymers contain
monomer segments in the same regular configuration along the main chain. This regularity
enables the chains to pack together more compactly and easily, much like the blocks in the
Tetris game.[2]
Role of morphology in injection moulding
Crystalline and amorphous polymers have distinct moulding properties. Crystallites form
due to strong molecular attraction and the possibility of the chains forming the bond un-
hindered. Occasionally, the mere presence of another molecule or a side chain is sufficient
to prevent crystallization. To process the melt, it is necessary to dissolve the crystallites,
separate the chains to reduce the viscosity, and inject the melt into the mould. This funda-
mental property of crystallite formation and dissolution is what dictates the differences in
processing and melt behavior.[2]

Pressure-Volume-Temperature (PVT) diagrams for polymers
It is observed that the PVT properties of polymers play an essential role in both numerical
simulation and process control for injection moulding. For injection moulding engineers,
understanding the PVT properties of polymers could be the fundamental topic.[4]
Data on polymer PVT is becoming increasingly valuable in material research. There are
several important areas where PVT data is being used heavily.[4]
• Miscibility between polymers can be predicted.
• Utilizing free volume concepts, predict the service performance and life of polymeric
materials and components.
• Properties of materials in systems that come into touch with solvents or gases.
• The nature of phase transitions is being investigated.
• Optimizing processing parameters rather than developing them through trial and error.
• The surface tension of polymer melts calculation
Some defects, such as shrinkage, warpage, and sink marks, generate thermally induced
stresses in the injection moulding process, affecting both dimensional accuracy and long-
term dimensional stability. By providing quantitative predictions based on accurate data,
software like Moldex3D (CoreTech System Co., Ltd., Taipei, China) aims to lessen the risk of
producing parts with shrinkage or warpage in plastic components. PVT data is a key input
data set to software programs to simulate the injection moulding process numerically. On
the other hand, process evaluations have indicated that using PVT diagrams in combination
with computer control of the process can result in optimal processing conditions. As a result,
more accurate PVT data is required for more accurate prediction, evaluation, optimization,
and calculation.[4]

Description of injection moulding process with PVT diagram
Injection moulding is a cyclic process consisting of four phases: filling, packing, holding, and
cooling, as shown by the typical PVT diagram in Figure 2.9(a), see also section 2.5.
The filling process starts at point A. The cavity pressure signal begins at point B, where
the melt plastics touch the pressure sensor for the first time, and then the pressure increases
steadily as the filling proceeds. The filling phase is complete at point C, where the melt only
volumetrically fills the cavity without being compressed. The packing process then embarks,
and the pressure rises rapidly to the peak value at point D. At point D, the injection pressure
switches over to the holding pressure and the holding pressure control sets in. After that, the
melt within the cavity is maintained at an assigned pressure during the holding phase, when
additional plastic melt can be packed into the cavity to compensate for the plastic shrinkage
caused by cooling to have the mould wholly filled. This process continues until the gate
is frozen, as marked at point E. Point E is the end-point of the holding phase. Beginning
at point E, a phase of constant volume is maintained (isochoric phase).
This isochoric
phase is especially important because one strives for a minimum of orientation, residual, and
distortion. This phase is the result of the dimensional accuracy of the moulding. Uniformly
reaching point F is decisive for the constancy of weight and dimensions of the moulding.
After point F, the moulding cannot be influenced anymore. It shrinks unaffected, usually
down to ambient temperature.[4]

Viscoelastic behaviour of polymers
Viscoelastic materials combine viscous and elastic properties. Viscoelasticity, also known as
anelasticity, is a property of systems that exists when they are deformed. Viscous materials
such as honey and polymer melt resist shear flow (shear flow is the gradient of a shear stress
force passing through a solid body) and strain, or the deformation of materials caused by
stress, increases linearly with time when a stress is applied. Shear stress is a type of stress in
which the stress is parallel or tangential to the material’s face, in contrast to normal stress,
which is perpendicular to the face. Shear stress is denoted by the variable, which is defined
as:
τ = V Q

(2.1)
for shear stress in a beam, where V denotes the shear force, Q denotes the cross section’s
first moment of area, t denotes the thickness of the material perpendicular to the shear, and
I denotes the cross section’s second moment of area.[31]
The nature of viscoelasticity
At all temperatures, polymers are viscoelestic. Moreover, they are not simple elastic solids,
and the effect of temperature on the response of viscoelastic systems to perturbation pro-
vides an interesting trend. When T = Tg, the strain against time remains nearly constant.
As the temperature rises, the strain variation with time increases in a square root profile
that becomes larger as the temperature rises.[31]
Polymer’s can transition from one state to another due to temperature changes. This is one
of the most critical thermodynamic variables to consider when describing and applying a
polymeric material. In general, polymers are classified in the solid-state according to their
thermal properties, which include melting point, crystallization point, and, in the case of
semi-crystalline polymers, glass transition temperature (Tg), which is a critical property of
the polymer due to its general amorphous nature. Additionally, when considering the gen-
eral behavior of polymers, the molecular weight, crystallinity degree, and branching degree
of the polymer should be considered. The majority of polymeric materials used in industry
are obtained via radical polymerization; thus, these materials are not only amorphous but
also have a broad molecular weight distribution, which influences the polymer’s behavior
from a variety of angles. For example, the glass transition temperature (Tg) of a polymer is
dependent on its molecular weight, particularly for low molecular weight polymers. On the
other hand, the degree of crystallinity and branching of the polymers may be responsible
for the polymeric materials’ thermal behavior. As a general observation, the mechanical
behavior of polymers is insignificantly temperature-dependent.[31].

Molecular theory
Polymers, due to their long chemical structure, retain their solid state even when discrete
sections of the chain undergo Brownian motions and move from one location to another via
diffusional jump processes. This fundamental distinction exists between elastic solids and
polymers.[31]
When stretched, elastic materials undergo instantaneous strain and quickly return to their
original state once the stress is removed. Viscoelastic materials combine both of these prop-
erties and exhibit time-dependent strain as a result. Whereas elasticity is typically caused
by bond stretching along crystallographic planes within an ordered solid, viscoelasticity is
caused by atoms or molecules diffusing within an amorphous material.[31]
Due to the high temperature dependence of the viscoelastic properties, the maximum tem-
perature should always be specified and taken into account. At room temperature, polymers
behave in a variety of ways, including as hard solids, elastic liquids, and rubbers.[31]
The response of a material can be classified as linear, non-linear, on the change in strain
rate versus stress. When a material exhibits linear response, it is classified as Newtonian.
When the material is Newtonian, the stress is proportional to the strain rate linearly. When
a material’s response to strain rate in non-linear, it is classified as a non-Newtonian mate-
rial. Additionally, there is an intriguing case in which the viscosity decreases as the rate of
shear/strain remains constant. When the stress is independent of the strain rate, this type
of material is referred to as thixotropic deformation.[31]
In certain instances, viscoelastic materials behave similarly to rubbers. Indeed, rubber elas-
ticity is observed in a variety of polymers, particularly those with crosslinking. These systems
exhibit hysteresis, stress relaxation, and creep.[31]
When a load is applied and then removed, a viscoelastic substance loses energy. Hysteresis
is observed in stress−strain curves when the area of the loop equals the energy lost during
the loading cycle. Due to the fact that viscosity is defined as the resistance of a material to
thermally activated plastic deformation, a viscous material will lose energy during a loading
cycle. Plastic deformation results in energy loss, which is not typical of the response of a
purely elastic material to a loading cycle. [31]

Plastic drying
The majority of plastics absorb moisture when exposed to humidity.
Polymers absorb
moister, whether in the shape of a pellet, before processing or finished product.
When
polymer parts absorb moisture, they expand in size, causing variations in the part dimen-
sions. Moisture in the plastic resin should be reduced to a suitable level before moulding
to ensure a high-quality component. Every plastic has a limited moisture content that can
cause melt processing issues if exceeded. Therefore, moisture levels should be less than their
defined limit before processing. [32]
Problems in melt processing related to the presence of moisture
The presence of moisture in the polymer melt during processing may result in several issues:
Degradation of plastic
Degradation in the polymer melt can occur if moisture is present during injection moulding
processing. The degradation occurs due to a chemical reaction called hydrolytic degradation
that damages long-chain molecules. The water molecule initiates the degradation, which
acts as a catalyst. The degradation process can produce additional water, speeding up the
reaction rate. Due to the degradation, the final produced product loses characteristics and
may contain defects. [32]
Dimensional variation
Vent plugging might result in dimensional changes in the part. Plastic pressure in the cavity
has a direct relationship with part dimensions. As a result, the cavity pressure must be
maintained throughout each injection moulding cycle to provide consistent quality. If the
vents are clogged, the air and gasses cannot escape, and the cavity’s internal pressure rises,
resulting in a component with varying dimensions. [32]
Inconsistency in shot control
The presence of moisture during the shot build-up can hide the actual volume of the plastic,
resulting in a reduction in the amount of necessary plastic. The screw will always reach the
shot size or produce the appropriate injection volume. However, the shot volume necessary
to make a good quality component may not always be theoretically ideal with the presence
of moisture. As a result, each shot will vary, resulting in inconsistency from shot to shot. [32]

2.5
The injection moulding process cycle
To comprehend how process control ensures component quality during the injection moulding
cycle, exploring the manufacturing methods and processing factors connected with moulded
parts is necessary. The injection moulding cycle utilizes reciprocating screw injection mould-
ing equipment to convert the powdered resin to a molten state. The molten plastic is then
pumped under pressure into a mould chamber, where it solidifies and then ejects the part.
The controls and settings for the moulding machine’s process parameters are determined
by the processing conditions of the resin, mould, and support equipment. Each resin has
unique processing parameters and settings that vary by machine. When changing moulding
machines, the parameters must be recalculated to accommodate the new machine’s process
variables. These new settings refer to the first machine’s configuration. No two machines
are ever identical due to wear.[5]
Each plastic resin will have supplier-recommended processing conditions, including barrel-
zone temperature settings, heater band, softening point temperature, injection speed profile,
injection pressure time, melt viscosity range, and mould temperature settings. The setup
operation converts the data to machine settings for the moulding cycle. To achieve successful
moulding and manufacture, it is necessary to establish and adhere to a machine setup and
operation process.
Setting the injection moulding cycle entails the following procedures:
1. The feed hopper gate of the moulding machine is opened, and pre-dried resin is fed
into the slow-moving screw.
2. Set the screw to operable RPMs with the nozzle backed away from the mould’s sprue
bushing as the resin is transported forward via the feed, transition, and into the meter-
ing section. The material absorbs heat from the barrel during this phase and begins to
melt. The moulding machine has been transformed into an extruder, which melts the
resin by conduction, barrel heat, and barrel/screw shear heat. The shallowing screw
flights compress, melt, and pump the resin forward in front of the screw tip as it goes
down the barrel. The melt is extruded from the nozzle, and air shots are fired until
the molten resin reaches operating temperature. When the melt quality appears to
be consistent, the melt temperature is determined. If the moulding parameters are
reached, the nozzle of the machine is advanced into contact with the sprue bushing
of the mould, the mould is closed, clamp pressure is applied, and the moulding cycle
begins. Now, the screw is being pushed back by the rising volume of molten resin
accumulating ahead of its tip at the barrel’s front. The amount of melt produced in
front of the screw is determined by the weight of material required to fill the mould
and runner system. A minor overflow volume provides approximately 13 millimeters of
melt cushion in front of the screw to maintain packing pressure following the injection
stroke. The amount of resin that accumulates is regulated by a limit switch that, when
tripped, halts the screw’s rearward motion and rotation. If the melt temperature is
out of range, the machine is adjusted until within the specified zone.
Page 27 of I
==PAGE:37==
Optimization IM for robustness and reduce resource consumption
April 17, 2022
3. If difficulties in obtaining a good melt are encountered, hydraulic screw-back pressure
can be applied against the screw’s rearward motion to generate more screw-shear heat.
This may be necessary if frequent press cycling results in insufficient travel to melt the
resin, if unmelted pellets appear, or if the screw is ineffective at providing a uniform
melt temperature for the resin. Pressure should be applied sparingly to heat-sensitive
and glass-reinforced polymers. This is because increased backpressure results in in-
creased glass fiber breakage, which results in a deterioration of the moulded part’s
physical qualities.
4. The moulding cycle is completed by injecting resin in front of the screw. The screw
is then stopped from rotating, and the injection cycle begins with the screw being
propelled forward by hydraulic injection pressure.
The screw now acts as a ram,
forcing the resin into the cavity of the mould. The check ring at the tip of the screw is
pushed rearward, thereby closing off any material backflow over the screw. The resin is
ejected into the mould at a predetermined first-stage injection pressure and speed. The
injection pressure and speed settings can be customized to match the desired resin and
mould filling characteristics. The trapped air is evacuated through the cavity venting
system as the resin is injected.
5. After filling the mould to between 90% and 95% in the first stage, the high decreased in-
jection pressure is dropped by half to a lower second packing pressure stage to complete
the cavity filling. Without a rapid reduction in injection pressure, the mould cavity
would flash. This packing pressure is then maintained in the cavity until the gates
close and no additional material can be put into it. The part cools and shrinks during
second-stage packing before the cavity gate freezes off. As a result, pressure must be
maintained on the melt to ensure that the maximum amount of resin is incorporated.
This compensates for the shrinkage of the resin during cooling.
6. After freeze-off of the gate, the cavity is sealed, and the screw can begin revolving to
build up the resin volume for the next shot. The portion, which is cooling in the cavity
at the moment, continues to shrink in accordance with its molecular structure. The
part must now cool sufficiently to become rigid enough to be expelled from the cavity
without warping and without the ejector pins penetrating the surface and distorting
the part. The cooling time for a part is determined by the type of resin, the thickness of
the part, the crystallinity of the resin, the temperature of the mould, and the intricacy
of the part. This is often the cycle’s longest time period.
7. The mould then opens, ejecting the part. The mould is then closed using the maximum
clamp pressure, the screw injects additional resin, and the cycle is repeated.

Scientific processing and scientific moulding
Numerous parameters contribute to the success of the moulding process, considering the

of the scientific concepts underpinning each parameter and their application to provide a
robust procedure and consistent part quality. Scientific processing encompasses the entire

exits as a finished product. A robust process is one that is capable of withstanding reasonable
natural fluctuation or a slight deliberate change in an input while still producing consistent
output. Consistency refers to moulding pieces with the least variance in their quality. The
term "part quality" refers to the size, look, and weight of the part, as well as any other factor
critical to the part’s shape, fit, and function. The fluctuation should be due to a particular
cause and not to any natural cause. Variations with a special cause are those that are caused
by an external influence.

3.3
The types of consistencies required in injection moulding
The objective of establishing a moulding process should be to create stable procedures that
require no process adjustments once established. Consistency in processes results in incon-
sistency in quality. Consistency is measured in three ways: cavity-to-cavity consistency,
shot-to-shot consistency, and run-to-run consistency. Consistency between cavities is neces-
sary for multi-cavity moulds to ensure that one cavity is of the same quality as the others.
Shot-to-shot consistency indicates that each subsequent shot is identical to the prior one
or that the first shot is identical to the final shot of the production run, with the process
parameters being constant throughout. When the process settings for two distinct runs are
identical and yield identical parts of the same quality, this is called run-to-run consistency.
With a single defined process, robust and stable procedures always produce consistently
high-quality products.[34]

the screw is at the shot size.
To get the screw-recovery time back down below the set

melt-temperature increase results when rpm is increased. Hence the confusion of cause and

Screw decompression
Decompression is a small backward movement of the screw a few millimeters used to draw
the molten resin back into the nozzle; it is timed to occur when the mould opens. Except
that it draws air into the nozzle, this method successfully prevents drool. The nozzle heater
band and its controller should be inspected to determine whether there is an issue maintain-

Decompression is crucial because variations in check ring location affect cavity filling by al-

is maintained consistently for each shot, instability is minimized. Because the back-pressure
is reduced during decompression, the ideal decompression setting will naturally depend on
the pressure setting, which is part of the investigation in the testing.
3.8
Screw speed
Because plastic is a poor conductor of heat, the inner layers of the plastic closest to the
screw do not receive enough heat from the heater bands. While the plastic is molten, the
melt is not uniform. The rotating screw’s shear generates the energy required to meet these
inner layers and adds to melt homogeneity.[8]
Screw speed setting is crucial, mainly when working with crystalline materials. The shear
generated by the rotating screw gives a substantial amount of energy to the plastic melting
process. When the material is processed through the barrel during the machine cycle, the
heat generated by the heater bands alone is insufficient to melt the crystallites. Additional
heat is generated due to the shear energy generated by the screw’s spinning. High screw
speeds produce significant shear, which contributes to the melting of crystallites.[8]
The material supplier typically specifies the recommended screw speeds. However, the val-
ues are expressed in revolutions per minute (rpm); this is extremely misleading. Depending
on the screw diameter, a given rpm value will result in considerable shear for big diameter
screws and minimal shear for smaller diameter screws. Therefore, screw speeds should be
expressed in terms of distance per time, such as meters per second.[8]
Screw design also significantly impacts the melt homogeneity of various materials, depending
on their shear sensitivity. Cycle times may be extended in some circumstances because the
available screw on the moulding machine cannot provide sufficient shear heat to melt the
plastic quickly enough.[8]

as seen Figure 3.5.[6]
Figure 3.5: Hold cushion. [6]
The cushion’s thickness cannot be less than 0.3 g since anything less is impossible to man-
age precisely. There is a significant risk that the cushion will go to zero at random due to
inconsistencies in the melt’s specific gravity. If the cushion reaches zero (or bottoms out), no
pressure is applied to the material in the mould, and the moulded item may deform, crack,
or simply fail to fill. [6]
Cushion values should not be too large. Rather than that, there should be just enough
cushion to aid in applying the desired pressures. There are two reasons why a small cushion
value is desirable. First, the plastic melt is compressible like a ball of rubber bands, and
a large cushion can absorb some of the applied pressure, lowering the pressure inside the
cavities. This is critical for high-viscosity or highly filled materials and processes on the
verge of being pressure-limited. An oversized cushion can result in shot-to-shot fluctuation,
and reduced process capabilities in these instances [7], see Figure 3.6.

Shot size
The maximum volume/weight of material that the screw can inject in a single operation cycle
is shot capacity. A product’s shot weight is the amount of material that must be injected
into the mould to produce the product, including the feed system (if not, hot runners are
included). The shot capacity is always less than the shot weight. It is determined by elements
such as the density of the material and the diameter of the machine screw. Maximum dosage
is also a factor in deciding the machine’s maximum shot weight. The "injection stroke" or
shot size refers to the backward/refilling position in the barrel to fill the given shot. [38]

Setting of the melt temperature
The material manufacturers provide processing data sheets that include information about
the material’s melt temperature. The suggested melt temperatures for amorphous materials
are quite broad, but the range for crystalline materials is relatively small. The mean of these
proposed temperatures must be chosen as the goal melt temperature as a starting point.
The desired melt temperature must be the actual melt temperature of the plastic, not the
temperature specified on the barrel temperature controller of the moulding machine. The
melt temperature will vary throughout designing a moulding process, and the end process
may have a different melt temperature. A melt pyrometer must be used to determine the
temperature of the melt.[8]

The temperature settings on the machine barrel are adjusted to achieve the optimum melt
temperatures for amorphous and crystalline polymers. The barrel of a moulding machine is
divided into at least three heating zones, each of which can be controlled independently. As
a result, each barrel has a unique temperature profile, and each screw section has a distinct
role. The screw’s base makes contact with the plastic pellets first. The feed throat is the
portion of the barrel where pellets are transmitted and then softened. The plastic must not
melt at the feed stage since it will stick to the barrel and the screw, preventing additional
material from being transported. As a result, temperatures in barrels closest to the feed
opening must be set at the lower end of the acceptable range.[8]

increase is frequently larger than amorphous plastics. However, because crystalline polymers

polymers, which take less energy to soften and can remain in a heated barrel for extended
periods of time. The successive zones have higher temperatures, resulting in what is known
as a conventional profile. Figure 3.8. illustrates the various profiles.[8]

3.11
Barrel zones and nozzle-temperature measurement and
control
Temperature control is crucial, and the plastic temperature should be maintained shot to
shot, run to run, and machine to machine. Moulders tend to fail to distinguish between the
actual plastic temperature and the temperature reported or displayed on the temperature-
control screen. They are not identical and can vary largely. Hence, during production, it is
difficult to determine the actual melt temperature. [39].
Even today, there is no universally accepted and trustworthy method for temperature mea-
surement during injection moulding processing. [40]
Controlling the nozzle temperature is crucial for processing crystalline polymers. The noz-
zle’s temperature must be kept within the processing range, but it should also be kept within
a significantly tighter temperature range than the processing range. This is critical during
the static periods of the plastic flow, which follow the holding phase of one cycle and precede
the injection phase of the following cycle. Lower temperatures cause the plastic in the nozzle
tip to freeze, rendering the nozzle tip incapable of injecting the next shot. All crystallites
will have dissolved at higher temperatures, resulting in an extremely low viscosity and what
is frequently referred to as nozzle drooling. There are some new nozzle tip designs available
on the market that can prevent this issue. [8]
Temperature regulation is crucial throughout the process, from the feed throat to the nozzle
tip. Despite advancements in machinery, this remains a challenge for Moulders.
The classifications of electric, hydraulic, and hybrid injection moulding machines continue
to evolve. Without a doubt, significant advancements have occurred. The new moulding
machines are more energy-efficient, quicker, and equipped with advanced software and con-
trollers, excellent news on efficiency and speed. [41]
The bad news is that several fundamental issues remain unaddressed, such as on-stream melt
temperature detection and, more specifically, temperature management of the nozzle body.
Temperature detection in the stream is difficult, but temperature management of the nozzle
body is another matter together. [41]
The proper thermocouple location on the nozzle body and tip is essential for nozzle tem-

delivering a melt temperature that is even close to accurate. If the nozzle-body temperature

Optimization of the injection phase
Non-Newtonian melts are inherent in all plastics. This means that their viscosity is not
constant throughout a variety of shear speeds. In the strictest sense, a plastic’s rheological
behavior is a mix of non-Newtonian and Newtonian activity. The plastic is Newtonian at
extremely low shear rates, which are rarely seen in injection moulding, but as the shear rate
increases, the plastic begins to exhibit non-Newtonian behavior. Interestingly, as shear rates
increase, the plastic tends to behave more Newtonian after a rapid initial viscosity drop.[8]
In Figure 3.9 the graph demonstrates that the change or decrease in viscosity is substantially
greater at low shear rates than at high shear rates. This occurs as a result of the polymer
molecules becoming untangled and aligning themselves in the direction of flow when the
shear rate increases. This results in a decrease in the resistance to flow (the viscosity). At
greater shear rates, the plastic tends to become more Newtonian[8]
Page 42 of I
==PAGE:52==
Optimization IM for robustness and reduce resource consumption
April 17, 2022
Figure 3.9: Orientation of the molecules in the direction of flow of different injection speeds [shear
rates] [8]
Typically, crystalline materials have a flatter region than amorphous materials due to their
lower viscosities, which facilitate orientation.
During the injection phase of injection moulding, the material is subjected to severe shear
stresses. Shear rate is directly proportional to injection speed. If the shear rates are low and
the curve is initially non-Newtonian, then minor changes in the shear rate result in a big
shift in the viscosity. Because natural variance exists, the mould filling will vary, resulting
in shot-to-shot inconsistency. However, when injection speeds are increased, the viscosity
typically remains steady. Shear rates are high at high injection speeds, and the effect of
shear rate on viscosity is not as strong as it was at low injection speeds. Small adjustments
in injection speed result in little or no change in the melt’s viscosity.[8]
John Bozzelli established the process for determining the viscosity curve. The fundamental
idea is similar to that of the melt rheometer, which is used to determine the viscosity of
plastics. Additionally, the injection moulding machine can be viewed as a rheometer, with
the nozzle orifice serving as the rheometer’s die and the screw serving as the piston. The
screw is used to apply hydraulic pressure to the molten plastic. The force required to move
the screw at a predetermined speed is measured.[8]

Determining the pressure-drop
As plastic flows through the various sections of the nozzle and mould, the applied pressure
is lost at the flow front of the plastic due to drag and frictional effects. Additionally, as
the plastic contacts the mould walls, it cools, increasing the plastic’s viscosity, requiring
additional pressure to push the plastic. The plastic skin that forms on the inside walls of
the runner system reduces the cross-sectional area of the plastic flow, resulting in a pressure
drop as well. Depending on the moulding machine’s pump capacity, the maximum amount
of pressure available to push the screw at the chosen injection speed is limited. The pres-
sure required to advance the screw at the specified injection speed should never exceed the
maximum available pressure. If the required pressure is increased, the screw will never be
able to sustain the stated injection speed during the injection phase, and the process will be
classified as pressure limited.[8]
The pressure loss in each portion of the flow path assists in determining the overall pressure
loss and the areas with the largest pressure losses during process development. The mould’s
runner system can then be changed to minimize this pressure loss and provide a more uni-
form flow. Sudden pressure drops between channels are likewise undesirable. If the process
is pressure limited, the mould must be adjusted to minimize this pressure decrease. Sections
with abrupt pressure changes must also be adjusted to provide a more continuous flow.[8]
Determining the process window
Plastic injection into the cavity is separated into two distinct stages. The first stage is the
injection phase, which involves entirely filling the mould cavity with molten plastic. The
melt volume is equal to the cavity volume. The second process is the packaging and holding
phase. The holding pressure must be increased to compensate for the volumetric shrinkage
induced by cooling when the plastic comes into contact with the cold mould walls. The
packing pressure, holding pressure, packing time, and holding duration are the many factors
that must be regulated throughout this phase. In most circumstances, packing and holding
are combined into what is referred to as the holding phase.
The packing includes filling the cavity with plastic material equal to the part’s theoretical
weight. The theoretical weight is equal to its solid density multiplied by its volume. Any less
plastic results in an under-packed component, whereas any more results in an over-packed
component. Parts that are not correctly packed exhibit faults such as sinks and internal
voids. These components frequently display significant post-mould shrinking. Over-packed
components may have inherent tensions often released during the moulding process, result-
ing in flaws like warpage or early mechanical failure. The optimal pack and hold pressures
are established by examining the mould’s process window. The process window is estab-
lished by varying two process variables: the holding pressure and melt temperatures. Due
to the small melt temperature range of crystalline materials, the melt temperature is fixed
to a value in the middle of the suggested values, and the two variables selected are hold-
ing pressure and mould temperature. Holding pressure typically has the greatest effect on
the part’s quality since it directly affects the part’s specific volume and thus its dimensions.[8]

aesthetically pleasing components are moulded. The greater the size of the window, the
more robust the process is. [8]

To ensure the process is robust, dimension data must be collected and the mould steel

Unmelted plastic may cause problems below the melt temperature limitations,

With low mould temperatures, the plastic may lack sufficient energy to produce crystallites.
On the other hand, if the mould temperature is higher than the crystallization temperature,

Injection moulding simulation - Theory and practice
4.1
Chapter Introduction
A gap between the practical injection processing and the computer-aided simulation software
processing of injection is always present. To have the most optimal and realistic simulation
output, it is essential to understand how the numerical simulation programs are coded and
the software’s capabilities and limitations. This is critical to achieving a good estimation
compression between manufactured and simulated components. While there is always some
variation between practical and simulated injection moulding, the differences between the
two are quite distinct. When this variation is expressed statically, it allows for a more real-
istic validation comparison between manufactured and simulated components.
Why simulate the injection moulding process?
"A computer simulation is any computer-implemented method for exploring the properties
of mathematical models where analytic methods are unavailable". [42]
In other words, computer simulations are the numerical computation of the solution of equa-
tions and their associated boundary conditions that control a mathematical model. If an
analytic solution is not available or is too complex, they are applied to these mathematical
models. Simulations are commonly used in any engineering discipline since physical and
mathematical models exist for several difficulties in engineering and technology. Structural
assessments, meteorology processes, fluid dynamics, logistics, and electric and electronic cir-
cuits, to name a few, are all covered by simulations.[43]
In the field of injection moulding, the following general reasons for running simulations were
determined as being effective and applicable.[44]
• Simulations allow in-depth examination of systems when observations, in reality, are
impossible due to practical reasons (too quick, too slow, inaccessible parts, the system
does not yet exist, etc.), such as in the case of climate change or the filling phase in
injection moulding.
• When changes to the observed system are too expensive, too laborious, or simply
impossible in reality, as in the case of material exchange in construction or newly
manufactured plastic parts, simulations allow for quick and easy manipulation.
• When visualization or implementation of a system is the reality too expensive or unsafe,
as in the case of combustion inside an engine or plastic flow inside a steel mould,
simulations allow you to visualize and increase the understanding of the underlying
system.[43]
Page 47 of I
==PAGE:57==
Optimization IM for robustness and reduce resource consumption
April 17, 2022
These considerations can be utilized and brought to the field of injection moulding. For
simulations of conventional injection moulding, the following objectives can be generated.[45]
• Assist in the choosing of materials.
• Minimizing or removing the necessity for physical prototyping and tests.
• Optimizing the part and mould design, including the manufacturing process parameter
settings.
• Reducing the time, it takes to develop and promote a new product.
• Assistance with component design, particularly at an early point in the process when
moulds or equipment are not yet available.
• Avoiding costly mould design errors and the associated re-engineering actions required
to correct them.
• Obtaining a better understanding of the process characteristics, also visualizing the
process, and examining the end product for air entrapment, warpage, shrinkage, weld
lines, and other issues.
In conclusion, simulations are widely used and considered as useful and effective tools in
the plastics sector. Process simulations of injection moulding are effective in terms of part
quality. [43]
However, there are several disadvantages to conducting simulations. It’s important to re-
member that, no matter how realistic or exact the results appear to be, the underlying models
usually include idealizations, approximations, and assumptions. Furthermore, regardless of
how well the real data are recreated, the underlying model must be understood, and there
must be evidence to believe in its application and validity.[46]
Modeling
The complete injection moulding machine’s characteristics, including the actual part, the
feed system, the inserts, the mould block, the cooling and heating system, the deployed ma-
terial, and the deployed injection moulding machine, should be analyzed and incorporated
into the simulation model. [47],[48]. A solid technique for constructing the simulation model
improves the quality of the accuracy of the results. [49],[50].

Mesh
The mesh is the geometric discretization of the geometry under investigation. The mesh is
essential for the simulation’s accuracy and the model’s spatial resolution [55]. It impacts
how well the original geometry is replicated, the precision of the numerical findings, and the
amount of time it takes to compute. In general, decreasing individual element edge length
or increasing mesh density increases numerical accuracy and computing time. The amount
of elements per volume or surface area is known as mesh density. Depending on the settings
of the multiple meshing parameters, the resultant mesh for the same geometry can appear
extremely different.[43]
The mesh density is locally modified according to the meshed geometry and entity size in the
multiscale mesh technique. The multiscale mesh technique assures an acceptable mesh size
for all included components, reducing computation time and providing a balance between
the required fine mesh for small entities.[43]

A stability study of the IM Engel was accomplished for Experiment #02. The approach
distinguished how many sample shots should be produced before setting new back-pressure
input. It is common knowledge that IM needs some time to run before stabilization.
In Appendix D, the process parameters, such as the injection pressure at switch over [bar],
the plasticizing time [s], and the feed zone temperature [C°], were tracked to see when sta-
bilization is current as the back-pressure is changed. As a result, it was concluded that the
temperature at the feed zone needs at least 30 shots before stabilization. The approach gave
an overview of how many shots should be produced before collecting the sample tests. It was
then decided to produce 50 samples between every back-pressure to ensure proper system
stability and to gain accurate data output.

To start with, the polymer melt state as a function of back-pressure is grouped into three

1. Stable region
As noticed in Figure 7.20, the processing parameter’s variation from back-pressure 20

processing parameters variations have increased consequently. The same domino effect

2. Unstable region
At back-pressure interval 80−110 [bar], the viscosity change and the variations of the
processing parameters are increased significantly and unstable. As a consequence, the
sample weight variations are also significant.
3. Stabilizing region
The viscosity mean value and variation value decrease at back-pressure interval 120−

the sample weight variations decrease. This indicates a stabilizing behavior.
The presence of the three region;
As mentioned in subsection 3.12, the semi-crystalline material behavior is a mix of non-
Newtonian and Newtonian activity. It indicates that the viscosity is not constant through-
out various shear rates. At high shear rates, semi-crystal materials exhibit non-Newtonian
behavior. This explains why the viscosity change in percent as the mean value has a linear
R-squared value of 0,86 and not accurately 0,99. See Table 7.3.
The viscoelastic behavior may put the polymer melt into a non-equilibrium state, affect-
ing each sample shot differently and causing variations in the system of the IM machine.
The temperature fluctuates during phase transition at the location of a non-equilibrium
back-pressure point due to heat infusion released from the polymer melt, see subsubsec-
tion 2.4.3. The fluctuating temperature behavior makes it more difficult for the injection
machine system to stabilize, as it has a domino effect on several IM processing parame-
ters. The temperature-molecular weight phase diagram of crystalline polymers is shown in
Figure 2.6; the diagram demonstrates that as the temperature increase, the plastic melt
transitions from Tg (Temperature Glass) to Tm (Temperature Melt) rubbery and finally to
a Viscoelastic zone.
At back-pressure intervals 20-70 [bar], the viscosity behavior seems Newtonian since the ma-
terial exhibits a linear response to the change in strain rate versus stress. When a material’s
response to strain rate is nonlinear, it is classified as a non-Newtonian material behavior.
This is noticed in the viscosity mean value from back-pressure 80-130 [bar]. As the shear
stress increases due to increased back-pressure to 80-130 [bar], the viscosity mean value
does not have a linear behavior; hence, the material’s response to strain rate is nonlinear,
which indicates a non-Newtonian material behavior state. See also Figure 3.9 and subsub-
section 2.4.4.

At back-pressure 20 [bar], it is observed that the energy consumption of the barrel tran-

The observed tendency can be related to the natural behavior of semicrystalline materi-
als. The intermolecular forces in crystalline polymers are extremely strong to start with;
therefore, a significant amount of energy is required to separate the molecules, see subsub-
section 2.4.3. As the back-pressure increases, the polymer melt in the barrel transition zone
compacts against the injection screw’s surface, allowing for increased shear stress in the melt
and increasing the temperature due to friction heat release. A loop then communicates the
temperature increases to the barrel regulator; as a result, the regulator reduces the barrel’s
energy consumption, as illustrated in Figure 7.10.
When a load is applied and then removed, a viscoelastic substance loses energy; since viscos-
ity is defined as a material’s resistance to thermally induced plastic deformation, therefore,
the PP melt will lose energy during the loading cycle due to plastic deformation [31]. The
mentioned relationship underlines the maintained low energy consumption of the barrel tran-
sition from 50 to 130 [bar], as observed in Figure 7.10. In terms of energy consumption, as
the internal energy of the PP material is utilized, no additional friction heat release will be
present in the polymer state.
The back-pressure interval 20-50 [bar] can be considered a "buffer zone" since the viscosity
change in mean value, and variance percentage value is stable and low; additionally, the
produced sample weight variations are also stable and low. After back-pressure 50 [bar],
the internal energy of the polymer is deficient. The low internal energy explains why the
domino effect occurs at back-pressure 60 [bar]. Since the internal polymer energy is low,
the polymer melt will be in none-equilibrium, resulting in larger variations due to instability
state as observed in Figure 7.20.
Because the internal energy loss of the polymer melt is greatest at 50 [bar], the probability
of a transition state is high. When polymer materials are deficient in energy, the polymer
structures are in a non-equilibrium state. In order to compensate for the non-equilibrium
state, a transition in the polymer structure occurs, which means the polymer in the phase
diagram changes position. Interestingly, after the transition at 60 [bar], the polymer melt
becomes stable at 70 [bar]; thus, the variation in viscosity change decreases while the sample
weight variations also decrease.
The observed discussions lead to a process "fingerprint" stating;
The optimal back-pressure input for robustness monitoring, energy efficiency,
and consistent product quality can be identified by tracking the energy
consumption of the barrel transition zone.
During processing set-up, the process "fingerprint" can be accomplished by tracking the
energy consumption in the transition zone, distinguishing at what back-pressure input the
energy consumption peaks and stabilizes. For instance, that point would be at
back-pressure 50 [bar] of this project experiment.

Conclusion
It was possible to gather injection processing studies and theories and connect the gained
knowledge to the project investigation study area. By performing injection moulding
simulations, manufacturing experiments, process analysis, and material characterization, it
was possible to validate and define a manufacturing-oriented product design approach,
more precisely the accomplishment of gaining a broader knowledge of the relationship
between the back-pressure effect on product quality and consistency. The following sections
are the accomplishments and conclusions.
8.1
Experiments
In general, it can be concluded that the effect of back-pressure on process parameters
influences the injection moulding production efficiency. More precisely, it was observed
that when back-pressure is increased, the Plasticizing time, Torque force, and Metering
performance decline, resulting in decreased production capacity, and increased production
time, as mentioned in subsection 8.1.
Additionally, the project concludes that the plasticizing optimization approach for weight
analysis utilizing the back-pressure process settings is a very sensitive investigation method.
Because the project study aims at optimizing the remaining 2 % uncertainty, as mentioned
in subsection 1.6, minor experimental errors will influence the experimental resulting data
output, see subsection 7.4. By the accomplishments of experiments, it is concluded that;
• No interruption should be present during the experiment.
• The decompression must be set constant throughout the experiment with a constant
shot volume.
• The polymer grade should not be mixed with the previous production grades. The
injection screw must be cleansed before the experiment because different polymer
grades have different material characteristics and properties that affect the
âcleannessâ of the experiment’s raw data output.
• It is encouraged to apply PID (proportional integral derivative) to improve
temperature variation in the barrel zones. It is also encouraged to conduct a stability
analysis of the injection machine.
• It is essential to store the resin bags in suitable atmospheric conditions to avoid
moisture absorption and to maintain resin quality.
By implementing the above-mentioned experiment procedures bullets, Experiment #02
improved, the R-squared values improved, and a better overview of the results was

8.2
Simulations and back-pressure
Simulation software has limitations in several areas. As for the limitation of including the
back-pressure processing parameter, see subsection 5.4. Even though the simulation
software can still be beneficial for gaining a deeper understanding of what cannot be seen
in the physical eye, with the help of the physical-mathematical models, including fluid
dynamics and thermomechanics that the software initiates, therefore, the injection
moulding simulations enabled in-depth investigation of observations that are physically not
possible; as to distinguish the density distribution, the filling pressure, and the filling shear
stress of the polymer melt at the current three-dimensional distribution, see subsection 5.5.
Furthermore, it was possible to implement short-shot simulations, as illustrated in
subsection 5.3, that was necessary for the simulation experiment aim, hence, to have
identical and realistic simulation results compared to the practical experiment.
8.3
Injection moulding parameters and back-pressure
The project work concludes that the injection processing parameters are uniquely
correlated. As a function of back-pressure, specific parameters exhibit linear relationships
as mean value, and certain parameters exhibit third-degree polynomial relationships as in
variation percentage while others contain both. The project concludes that the injection
process parameters interact uniquely as one system; their behavior is compatible with
Newton’s third law, which asserts that every action has an equal and opposite reaction.
For instance, several injection processing parameters interacted consequently when the
viscosity value was changed as a "Domino-effect" as discussed in subsection 7.10 and
observed in Figure 7.20. Moreover, it was observed that the polymer melt state as a
function of back-pressure exhibited three regions; "stable," "unstable," and "stabilizing"
region. The three regions’ presence is discussed and related to the nature of Polymer
Material Science, Polymer Morphology, Thermal Transitions in Polymers, and Viscoelastic
Behaviour of Polymers.

8.4
Plastic material behaviour and back-pressure
The natural behavior of polymers is dependent on their atmospheric current condition
state. To fully grasp the effect of viscosity on sample weight variations, it is necessary to
have a solid understanding of Polymer Material Science. The Science behind polymer
morphology, viscoelastic behavior, and the thermal transition are among other fields that
have to be considered in the analysis. The change in viscosity is concluded to have a
"domino effect" on several processing injection moulding parameters. Hence, the viscosity
change as a function of back-pressure is concluded to be the most significant effect on
sample weight variation.
Additionally, as the process "fingerprint" states, the barrel zones’ energy consumption can
reveal the polymer melt’s viscosity state. By monitoring the energy consumption of the
transition barrel zone, the viscosity state is predicted; as a result, the ideal back-pressure
input for consistent product quality and energy efficiency can be determined, see
subsubsection 7.10.2

8.5
Summary
As mentioned in subsection 1.6, the current knowledge in injection moulding processing
can be optimized with 98 % accuracy. With additional knowledge and investigations,
injection moulding processing can be optimized up to 99 % and 99.9 %; hence, this project
work has observed and accomplished the mentioned accuracy.
The plasticizing optimization approach of back-pressure process settings for Experiment
#02 provided a sample variation down to 0.39 % at back-pressure 20 [bar] while at
back-pressure 50 [bar] a variation down to 0.47 %, hence an process accuracy of 99.61 %
and 99.53 %, see Figure 7.11, while the energy consumption is maintained low at 50 [bar],
as discussed in subsubsection 7.10.2 and observed in Figure 7.24.
Whereas for Experiment #01 the sample variation was obtained down to 0.002 % at
back-pressure 10 [bar] and 0.24 % at back-pressure 30 [bar], hence an process accuracy of
99.9 % and 99.79 %, see Figure 7.11. However, the experimental procedure for Experiment
#01 is not identified as for Experiment #02, and possible noise factors were present as
concluded in subsection 8.1. Moreover, as mentioned in subsubsection 7.5.1, the variation
may have increased in Experiment #02 because the resin bags were not optimally stored
during the period between Experiment #01 and Experiment #02.
However, it was interesting to see that the R-squared value of the third-degree polynomial
regression was improved for Experiment #02, hence the "S-curve". Suppose the resin was
in good condition as for Experiment #01, the sample weight variation of Experiment #02
would also have improved.
Today, some injection moulding engineers base their process settings on "gut feelings and
experience" to obtain a significant 99.9 % accuracy production quality. Conceivably, by the
gained knowledge from this project investigation work, injection moulding engineers may
now base their process settings on a process "fingerprint" instead, supported by scientific
processing concepts and material science engineering.
Moreover, by the process "fingerprint," the measurements of produced samples will be
eliminated since the back-pressure production consistency value input can be predicted
during processing. Additionally, wasted production trails can be saved by determining what
back-pressure value the "unstable region" occurs. See subsection 7.10 for the description of
the presence of three "three regions" and their relation tendencies to back-pressure.
However, the process "fingerprint" hypothesis requires several testings with different
polymer grades, injection machines, and component geometries to be fully supported and
approved.

8.6
Future work
It was not possible to simulate the back-pressure process parameter in the software
programs, Moldex3D and Screwplus, as mentioned in subsection 5.4. Therefore, other
alternatives for the project study can be investigated. The decompression effect on sample
weight variations is another area of investigation in the injection moulding industries. As
mentioned in subsection 6.2, the decompression effect also corresponds to the third-degree
polynomial regression, see Figure 6.3. Therefore, simulating the decompression effect would
be very beneficial for future work since the simulation methodologies allow for the
integration of Design Of Experiments (DOE) and Monte Carlo simulations.
Moreover, it would be interesting to study the "S-curve" shifting position as a function of
back-pressure; if the temperatures in the barrel zones were changed in a manner illustrated
in the Figure 8.1 below.
Figure 8.1: Future work investigation area
Changing the temperature as illustrated will, in theory, shift the "S-curve" position to the
larger back-pressure scales. As if the "Domino-effect" illustrated in Figure 7.20 will result
in shifting position from interval 60-70 [bar] to interval 80-90 [Bar]. The mentioned is
theoretically possible since less temperature is applied in the polymer melt; the polymer
will, therefore, store a more significant energy amount that will be compensated in the
larger back-pressure values.
Another area of investigation for future work is to study the quantitative energy effect as a
function of back-pressure. ENGEL A/S offers customized software programs that contain
the necessary calculation formulas to provide quantitative data output for energy
consumption. Having the mentioned tool will help this project for future work to
understand the relationship between different back-pressure inputs and the energy
consumption.

SABIC® PP 576S
PP homopolymer for Injection moulding
Description:
SABIC® PP 576S is a PP homopolymer grade that has good flow properties, excellent demoulding properties and 
a narrow molecular weight distribution. Products made from SABIC® PP 576S typically have low warpage, high 
stiffness in combination with moderate impact strength at room temperature and a high gloss.
Application:
SABIC® PP 576S is typically used in caps & closures and thin wall packaging both in food and non-food 
segments, and appliances.
