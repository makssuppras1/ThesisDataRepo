Abstract 
The increased interest in physical inactivity among the city residents all around the world 
caused greater focus on urban parks as spaces that can contribute to the improvement of 
physical activity and therefore, the public health in cities.  With this in mind, this study 
performs a Post Occupancy Evaluation (POE) of an urban park in Copenhagen 
(Superkilen), with main focus on physical activity. The study aims to clarify how local 
residents use Superkilen for physical expression, examining whether its design has 
managed to promote the activity of local residents. Then formulate a series of 
recommendations to improve Superkilen, which may be applied for the future urban 
development 
projects, 
which 
also 
aim 
to 
promote 
physical 
activity.                              
The main research question is: 
How is Superkilen used by local residents and do the park promote local residents’ 
physical activity?  
The research employs POE study of Superkilen, mainly done by field work, which consists 
of regular observations of the park and distribution of questionnaire. In addition, interviews 
with two experts were done to support the POE, highlighting the areas that are worth to 
focus on during the POE. 
Moreover, the study combines POE method with a scientific study of urban parks by 
performing a literature study of urban parks in relation to physical activity. 
The results show that Superkilen is a popular local urban park that is used among groups 
from different social and cultural background. However, the study indicates that only few 
users manage to be physically active by using the provided facilities. The vast majority of 
the users use the facilities only for recreational purposes. 
Moreover, the study specifies that the distance factor is the most important factor to 
encourage people to use Superkilen, where broadly all users live close to the park. 
Regarding the use of the park, the study shows that walking and relaxing are the most 
preferable activities among the users.   
Research study reveals that not all social groups can manage to use multifunctional 
facilities to be physically active, some groups need to have traditional and clearly assigned 
facilities, which do not require a high level of creativity to use. As Superkilen mainly 
consists of multifunctional facilities, it allows confusion for some of the users’ groups and 
as a result only the creative users can manage to use them actively. Moreover, the study 
concludes with a remark that it is not always a reasonable goal to encourage all various 
groups of users to perform physical activity in local urban parks, due to cultural reasons. 
What must be accepted is that not all of the users will be physically active in local parks. 
 
Keywords: Post Occupancy Evaluation (POE), Urban parks, Physical activity, 
Superkilen, Social disadvantaged district.

Summary  
Den stigende interesse i fysisk aktivitet blandt by beboerne rundt omkring i verdenen 
forårsager et stigende fokus på byparker og deres evne til at bidrage til en forbedring af 
folks fysiske aktivitet og således opnå en bedre folkesundhed. På baggrund af dette 
undersøger dette studie en bestemt bypark i København (Superkilen, der består af tre zoner; 
grøn, sort og rød) ved at udføre en Post Occupancy Evaluation (POE) med fokus på 
brugernes fysiske aktivitet. En POE er en metode, der bruges til at evaluere et bestemt 
design eller en konstruktion fra brugernes synspunkt. Formålet med studiet er at klarlægge, 
hvordan lokale beboere bruger Superkilen til fysisk udfoldelse, og om hvorledes Superkilen 
har formået at fremme den fysiske aktivitet blandt lokalbeboerne. Studiet vil derefter 
formulere en række anbefalinger til forbedring af Superkilen samt forslag til andre 
fremtidige byudviklingsprojekter, der ligeledes har til formål at fremme lokalbeboernes 
fysiske aktivitet. 
Studiets forskningsspørgsmål er: 
Hvordan er Superkilen anvendt af lokale beboere, og fremmer parken de lokales fysiske 
aktivitet? 
Der er flere årsager til at Superkilden er en interessant bypark at undersøge. Først og 
fremmest har parken opnået en stor national og international opmærksomhed ved både at 
være nomineret samt vinde prestigefyldte priser. Det er en park beliggende på Nørrebro. 
Det Københavnske område Nørrebro er præget af mange sociale problemer, og hvor 26% 
af beboerne er af anden etnisk herkomst end dansk fra mere end 50 forskellige nationaliteter 
(Streets without Cars 2014). Derudover er forventningslevealderen hos Nørrebros beboer 
mindre end i resten af København (Københavns Kommune 2016). Det er derfor interessent 
at undersøge hvorledes Superkilen har formået at gavne de lokale beboers aktivitetsniveau, 
som var intentionen fra Københavns Kommune. 
 
POE undersøgelsen af Superkilen er hovedsageligt udført af feltarbejde, i form af 
regelmæssige observationer af parken samt spørgeskemauddeling. Observationen af parken 
har foregået 20 gange – fordelt mellem ugedage og weekender. Observationen bestod i at 
observere brugerne og deres opførsel i parken samtidig med, at der blev udfyldt et Excel 
ark. Excel arket giver mulighed for at dokumentere antal brugere, køn, alder, etnicitet og 
aktivitet. Hvad angår spørgeskemaet er den udført på tre forskellige sprog: engelsk, dansk 
og arabisk. Dette giver mulighed for at nå frem til flest mulige potentielle brugere af parken. 
Spørgeskemaet er derudover både uddelt manuelt i parken og via sociale medier. Derudover 
gør undersøgelsen brug af to eksperters viden inden for byparker ved at interviewe dem og 
høre deres mening om byparker og fysisk aktivitet samt om Superkilen specifikt. 
Dette studie kombinerer POE undersøgelsen af Superkilen med et litteraturstudie om 
byparker med fokus på fysisk aktivitet. Denne kombination gør det muligt at bruge 
resultaterne fra POE med resultatet fra litteraturstudiet for så at komme frem til en endelig 
konklusion. 
Med hensyn til litteraturstudiet er begrebet ’livability’ en væsentlig faktor, når det gælder 
tiltrækning af folk til bestemte byparker. Ifølge Byplanlæggeren Gehl er der bestemte 
kriterier og principper, der bør opnås før, at en bypark er livlig eller livable.  
For at designe et beboeligt byrum skal visse kriterier og principper opnås, ifølge Gehl. 
Byrummet skal tilbyde beskyttelse, komfort og herlighedsværdier. Derudover er der fem 
væsentlige bymæssige principper, som er vigtige forudsætninger, når der arbejdes med 
menneskelig dimension; at indsamle, at integrere, at invitere, at åbne og at øge. 
Principperne sikrer, ifølge Gehl, at mennesker og begivenheder er samlet, og dermed sikres

kvaliteten af byrummet ved at invitere folk til at tilbringe mere tid i byrummet. Desuden er 
der fysiske elementer, som enten formidler eller forhindrer "se og høre" kontakter, som 
Gehl mener er de mest almindelige former for kontakter. Disse elementerne er: ingen 
vægge, kort afstand, lav hastighed, én plan og orientering ansigt til ansigt. Studiet af 
Superkilen viser, at den røde og sorte zone er mere eller mindre i overensstemmelse med

Derudover viser Superkilen sig at være en populær lokal bypark, som stort set bliver brugt 
af alle sociale og kulturelle grupper i området. Dog indikerer resultaterne, at kun få grupper 
formår at være fysisk aktive i Superkilen. Studiet indikerer at faciliteterne i Superkilen er 
alt for multifunktionelle, og at de derfor kun appellerer til de personer, som har evnen til at 
være kreative i deres fysisk udfoldelse. Den gruppe som i virkeligheden formår at anvende 
Superkilens faciliteter til fysisk aktivitet er en homogen gruppe, som består af unge danske 
mænd. Tendensen er, at gruppen som bruger Superkilen aktivt, er ressourcestærke personer, 
som ikke lader sig forvirre af de multifunktionelle faciliteter. Derimod anvender de fleste 
brugere parken til rekreations formål, hvor både gåture og afslapning er de to mest 
foretrukne aktiviteter. Selvom gåture er en form for fysisk aktivitet, er det ikke tydeligt om 
Københavns kommune er tilfreds med denne form for aktivitet eller om de ønskede en mere 
intens form for aktivitet. Dette havde studiet ikke mulighed for at afklare, da et møde med 
Københavns kommune ikke var muligt grundet deres presset tid. Alt i alt tyder studiet på, 
at de multifunktionelle faciliteter kun appellerer til en bestemt gruppe af brugerne, og virker 
forvirrende for en stor del af brugerne. Det gælder især de socialudsatte unge, som grundet 
deres baggrund ikke formår at være aktive i deres fysisk udfoldelse, men foretrækker 
traditionelle og klare faciliteter. Studiet kommer frem til endnu en konklusion, som er, at 
nærheden er en væsentlig faktor, når man ønsker at opfordre en bestemt gruppe til at bruge 
byparken. Ifølge studiet bor de fleste brugere enten på Nørrebro eller indenfor 1 km fra 
Superkilen. Selvom nærheden til byparken er en væsentlig faktor for at tiltrække flest 
mulige brugere, er det ikke ensbetydende med, at brugerne ønsker at være fysisk aktive i 
deres lokale bypark. Det gælder kvinder – og især kvinder med anden etnisk herkomst end 
dansk, som ikke foretrækker at være synlige for deres omkringskreds, mens de dyrker 
motion. Studiet tyder på, at nogle af kvinderne, som ikke foretrækker at være fysisk aktive 
i deres lokale bypark, ikke har noget imod at dyrke motion et andet sted, som ikke er ligeså 
åbent og ”afslørende”.  
Som følge heraf, forslår studiet at byparker, som ønsker at få de lokale til at benytte parken 
fysisk aktivt, bør inddrage dem i designfasen, og undersøge hvad de i virkeligheden ønsker, 
at deres lokale bypark skal indeholde. Derudover er det væsentligt, at parken også har 
traditionelle og tydelige faciliteter, der ikke giver mulighed for forvirring af brugerne.  
Til sidst er det vigtigt at sætte fast, at det kan fremgå urimeligt at tilskynde alle grupper af 
brugerne til at være fysisk aktive i lokale byparker grundet kulturelle og sociale årsager.

1 Introduction 
Healthy, happy and productive society is a goal and a wish of every municipality. When 
the majority of world population lives in cities, urban design is becoming more and more 
crucial in enabling healthy lifestyle and shaping good habits (United Nations 2014).  
 
“The definition of health care is broadening to include our environment, rather than just 
clinical care and medicine”(Nelson 2016) 
 
An urban design can either increase or decrease the citizen’s physical activity level. This is 
where an urban planner, architect and engineer can create and contribute to an inviting 
design of the urban environment and public space, to encourage the citizens to use the urban 
facilities in desired way. There is a discussion about whether it is the public space, which 
creates the activity or whether it is the people who are already active, who make use of 
urban facilities. Investigations have shown that effective urban planning and organization 
of the urban facilities have a great importance for the number of users (Harvard n.d.). Urban 
planning that focuses on making the daily life more active leads to making the healthy 
choice, an easy choice. Protection from wind and bad weather, safety issues, good location 
of seating places and in general, an optimal public space management, are contributing to 
a better use of the urban spaces. In this way, the urban environment plays a major role in 
the level of activity among citizens and therefore may be an important factor to support 
public health in cities. 
 
1.1 The frame of the study 
This study focuses on urban parks and their ability to improve the public health in cities, 
with Superkilen park as a main case study. The study is a Post Occupancy Evaluation of 
Superkilen and does investigate its impact on the user’s physical activity.                         
Superkilen is a public urban park located in Nørrebro district in Copenhagen, that consist 
of three zones; Green zone, black zone and red zone. It is a significant public park that have 
managed to get a large and positive international attention. It recently received an important 
design award, Aga Khan Award for Architecture in Abu Dhabi (Njor Maise 2016a). In 
addition, Superkilen was nominated for the Mies van der Rohe prize in 2012 (DAC 2014) 
and in 2013 Superkilen was listed as one among the world’s 21 best projects  by AIA- The 
American Institute of Architect (Realdania 2016).  
This study examines the use of Superkilen, distinguishing between ethnic Danes and 
citizens with different ethnic background, and the lower - higher level of education. The 
distinction between Danes and people with different ethnic background and between the 
educational levels is based on the Copenhagen municipalities’ statistics. The Copenhagen 
Municipality’s public health report in 2014 shows a major difference in life expectancy 
among the different districts in Copenhagen. The report distinguishes between 
‘disadvantaged districts’ and the rest of the districts in Copenhagen, whereby the statistics 
shows a lower life expectancy in the ‘disadvantaged district’ than in the rest of Copenhagen.  
“The exposed areas are characterized by a number of factors such as low income, short 
education, no employment, non-western background, small homes, etc.” (Diderichsen et 
al. 2014).  
To meet the Copenhagen Municipality statistics and to have the same basis, this study 
examines Superkilen and the use of it on the same basis as Copenhagen Municipality, 
taking into account the ethnicity and educational level as determining factor.

1.2 The background of the study 
The reason why health issues and physical activity is a burning issue among urban planners, 
is linked with the observation that the obesity is a growing public health problem all around 
the world, and Denmark is not an exception. According to the Danish Board of health, in 
Denmark 47% of the adult population are overweight and 13% are obese, whereas the 
hardest hit are the younger age groups and people with low education or income. Only 29% 
of adults Danes are moderately or severely physically active at least 4 hours a week. In 
addition, muscles pain is the most widespread symptom among Copenhagen residents, 
additionally more than the half of the population suffer from pain in the back or lower back 
and just as many suffer from pain in the neck and shoulders (51%) (Sundhedsstyrelsen 
2015).  
Why are these facts vital for this report and how can this problem be seen from a civil 
engineering point of view?  
Many studies have shown that physical activity prevents overweight and obesity, thus 
prevent all the diseases that they both lead to. In addition, the studies have proven that 
physical activity is directly related to musculoskeletal diseases and increase the risk of 
depression, cancer, cardiovascular, heart disease and type-2 diabetes (Diderichsen et al. 
2014). It is therefore essential to increase the level of activity in daily life in order to live a 
long and healthy life.  
The urban parks can be experienced as a respite from the hectic atmosphere associated with 
modern cities and can bring nature and recreational opportunities closer to home. In 
addition, urban parks can have a major role in attracting international attention to a specific 
city. The quality of life in the city is usually associated with having opportunity to use urban 
parks as well as being provided with the natural resources as river or a forest.  
Many studies proving linkage between urban life and health problems. Urban parks are 
therefore a necessity in the modern world and especially in busy cities like Copenhagen 
(Razak et al. 2016).  
A recent study proves that an optimal urban planning, the one which includes urban parks, 
have a direct effect on increasing the activity level (Hill 2016). This recent study will be 
explained further in section 4.1 (The relation between urban planning and physical 
activity). Regarding POE studies, there have only been made few POE studies on urban 
parks and one of them is a recent Turkish study, that examine the user’s satisfaction with 
certain parks in Turkey (Özkan et al. 2015). The Turkish study will be described in section 
4.3.1 (Existing studies that deal with urban parks and physical activity). 
 
In view of all studies that have been done already, this study differs by performing a POE 
of an urban park, with main focus on Physical activity. The study examines how the users 
use Superkilen and whether Superkilen has managed to promote the activity of local 
residents of Nørrebro and whether it appeals to all social groups in Nørrebro.

1.3 The study focus and research questions 
As the study investigate whether Superkilen appeals to all social groups in Nørrebro, the 
study’s target group is broad and covers all potentials users of the park; children, adults, 
and old. In addition, the research examines the difference in use among Danes and people 
with other ethnic background. It is interesting to examine whether the park is used by all 
local residents across the ethnicity or only appeals to a specific audience. 
The project will contribute to broader knowledge about the impact of Superkilen on local 
residents’ physical activity level. The project aims to clarify why and how local residents 
use Superkilen for physical expression and then formulate a series of recommendations, for 
Copenhagen Municipality and other stakeholders. The recommendations are both 
suggestions to improvement of Superkilen and to future urban development projects, with 
a goal to promote physical activity. Thus, the study is interesting for urban planner and 
designer who work with urban parks but also for municipalities and other organizations, 
which operate and maintain them.  
 
The study’s main research question is: 
How is Superkilen used by local residents and do the park promote local residents’ 
physical activity?  
 
To be able to answer the main research question, sub-questions are developed as support: 
 What characterizes Superkilen project, particularly with regard to support for 
physical activity-across age and ethnical background  
 Who is using the park and what characterize the use? 
 Is Superkilen used by those who already are aware and engaged in physical activity 
or is the park used by new groups? 
 Does Superkilen works as intended by Copenhagen municipality?  
 What can others who will realize similar projects learn from Superkilen project? 
 
The Copenhagen Municipality’s intention with Superkilen is described in section 5.4 
(Purpose and vision).  
 
1.4 Limitation 
The study focuses on investigating the approach to achieve a successful urban park, which 
is attractive for the local residents and encourage them to use its facilities. Many studies 
have already explained the importance of physical activity and the linkage to the urban 
environment. It is therefore not the aim of this project to prove it again, but to investigate 
whether Superkilen successfully encourage all kind of local citizens to be physically active.  
Copenhagen Municipality had several goals during creation of Superkilen, for instance to 
promote the integration of the area’s local residents and to reduce the crime level in the 
area. This thesis does not include those factors during the study of the park, however, the 
differences in use of the park facilities between Danes and users from other ethnic 
background- in physical activity framework- is a part of the project, and can therefore 
indicate if the Municipality goals has been achieved.

2 Methodology 
This chapter explain the philosophy that guide the research practice and thus gives a basis 
to the tools and techniques that are used in the research methods. 
The POE concept will be described following by an explanation of how the different steps 
in a POE are employed in this study. 
 
2.1 Post occupancy evaluation of urban parks 
This section presents the POE concept based on a POE guide developed by the Higher 
Education Funding Council for England (HEFCE) (Blyth et al. 2006). In addition, the 
section contains a brief reflection of using POE in urban parks. 
A POE method is used to evaluate a particular design or construction from the user’s 
perspective “POE is a way of providing feedback throughout a building’s lifecycle from 
initial concept through to occupation. The information from feedback can be used for 
informing future projects, whether it is on the process of delivery or technical performance 
of the building” (Blyth et al. 2006). This is one of many definitions of POE as there is not 
one standard definition of the term, though the definitions are almost identical, where the 
main focus is on the users. POE as an evaluation tool, allows to evaluate how well the 
requirements for a building have been met. It is remarkable that most of the definitions are 
related to buildings and not urban spaces. It is therefore important- in this particular study- 
to adapt the POE methods for the purpose of investigating urban parks instead of a building. 
Just as there are different variations of POE definitions, there are also variations of POE 
methods and guidelines. Even if this study is based on a POE guide that deals with 
buildings, the guide is still useful because it has a high degree of flexibility ”It allows either 
to develop your own approach using a range of existing evaluation techniques or use an 
established method” (Blyth et al. 2006). To adapt the POE guide to Superkilen study, own 
approach using a range of existing evaluation techniques, is used. In addition, the POE 
study has been supported by supplementary literature, which focuses on urban parks. This 
is described further on in section 3.4 (The study’s methodological process). 
 
There are three different benefits of making a POE; a short term-, a medium term- and 
a longer term benefit. Depending on these benefits the POE serves several purposes. The 
short term benefits consist of inform the decision making of possible problems and finding 
solutions to them. In addition, the space use of the building or urban space can be improved 
through feedback from users. The medium term benefits involve possibility of finding new 
uses for the examined construction and gives possibility to organizational change and 
growth. In addition, long term improvements in building performance and in design quality 
does serves the longer term benefits. As well as it gives a strategic review.  
Further on, there is three stages of the review process; operational-, project- and strategic 
review. The operational review is normally carried out 3-6 months after occupation and 
aims to investigate whether there are problems that should be resolved immediately. The 
project review is carried out 12-18 month after occupancy during after thing have calmed 
right and thus have the possibility to investigate the buildings performance and evaluate if 
the building does meet the long term needs. The last review process is the strategic review 
that carried out 3-5 years after occupation and have the possibility to evaluate whether the 
building still meet the organizational need, that may have changed. 
The POE investigational levels can either be quick surface review, investigative review 
or a deeper diagnostic review. The difference between the three investigational levels is 
the degree of thoroughness during the analysis. While the quick surface review only gives 
==PAGE:13==
 
12 
 
a snapshot of the project through few interviews the deeper diagnostic review is much more 
detailed analysis through a deeper occupant responses. 
The POE can focus on three main areas; process, functional performance or technical 
performance. The process performance examines the delivering of the project and o how 
the building is managed. The functional performance looks at the organizational goals and 
examine how well the users need are supported. The last main focus area is technical 
performance and looks at the physical systems performance (Blyth et al. 2006)  
 
According to HEFCE, the above-mentioned POE concepts are all targeted buildings but 
are still used in this urban parks study. Even though the urban parks are open organizations, 
that usually are accessible around the clock, they are still facilities and can be treated as 
buildings, in the context of a POE study. A recent Turkish POE study of urban parks (Özkan 
et al. 2015)  examines four urban parks through a questionnaire that investigate the user’s 
evaluation of the technical, functional and aesthetic performance of the facilities. The POE 
method is directly based on a usual POE for buildings and adapted to a park by changing 
the questionnaire to fit the parks facilities instead of the buildings facilities. The Turkish 
POE will be explained further on in section 4.3.1 (Existing studies that deal with urban 
parks and physical activity) and the similarities and differences with this study, will be 
highlighted in section 7 (discussion).  However, it must be clarified that the Turkish POE 
was discovered late in the process and therefore it was not possible to follow their steps in 
the POE of Superkilen, though it may be reasonable to do, as the studies are very similar.  
 
The POE can be made with different approach that all depends on each study. Section 3 
(Method) does explain the used approach in this study case and the background for using 
those specific methods.

3.5 Strengths and weakness of the methods 
There are both advantages and disadvantages of quantitative and qualitative research 
methods. Therefore, there is a clear benefit to combine both approaches as complementary 
to each other. 
Regarding the quantitative method, it has the ability to give an understanding of individual 
phenomena. In this study, the quantitative research allows to focus on a particular park and 
explore the use of it by including the users in the study through a survey. Thus, the strength 
of the quantitative method is that it enabled to  create an overview of large amount of 
information, and therefore draw general conclusions (Forskningsprocessen 2010).  
However, there are different aspects of collecting answers through the survey, electronic 
and manually collected surveys. Even the electronic survey enables to collect many 
questions in a short time, still it can be difficult to get a representative sample. While a 
distribution of survey manually in the park among the users is much more focused, it 
requires a longer time and is very dependent on the weather. 
All in all, the quantitative method allows a clear statistical presentation of the results that 
can be used to make a comparison with other studies, but does not allow to get deeper 
insight in the results. 
On the other hand, the qualitative method supports the quantitative method very well, as it 
can provide a deeper understanding of the data. The qualitative method is much more 
flexible and has a high adaptation ability, as the observation or interview can be edited 
depending on the study findings. However, the biggest weakness of the this kind of research 
is the presentation of results, which cannot be presented statically and therefore allows 
different interpretations (Research n.d.).

4.1 The relation between urban planning and physical activity 
“A healthy city is not one that achieved a particular health status. Rather, it is a city that 
is conscious of health and striving to improve it. What is required is a commitment to health 
and a process and structure to achieve it. A healthy, active city is one that is continually 
creating and improving opportunities in the built and social environments and expanding 
community resources to enable all its citizens to be physically active in day-to-day life” 
(Edwards, Peggy; Tsouros 2008).  
 
This above definition of a healthy city is consistent with a recent study that proves the 
connection between urban planning and local citizen's physical activity (Hill 2016). The 
study “Physical activity in relation to urban environments in 14 cities worldwide: a cross-
sectional study” was published in The Lancet in April 2016 and documented how physical 
activity is directly linked to the urban environment. The study was conducted on nearly 7. 
000 adults aged 18-66 years in 14 cities in ten countries on five continents:

4.2 Urban parks- yesterday and today  
The use of the urban spaces has changed extremely during the history and according to 
Gehl, the explanation is depending on the three main outdoor activities. 
There are three main outdoor activities that a city can be used for: necessary activities, 
optional activities and social activities (Gehl 2007). These three activities are affected by 
the urban design in various degrees. The necessary activities are less affected by the urban 
design; thus, it uses the urban facilities as a mean to achieve the everyday activities; go to 
work, go to school, wait for a bus, go shopping etc. While the optional activities are most 
affected and related to the physical environment in the city, which either encourage you to 
be outside and make use of the facilities- take a walk, sit on a bench, play etc.- or discourage 
you from a spontaneous use of the city, and pushes you to hurry up and only complete the 
necessary activities. The social activities- also called resultants activities- are consequences 
of the necessary and optional activities and are more or less random activities. The social 
activities are when you met other in the urban space and contact with them, either by 
greeting them or by talking to them. The social activities get supported when setting up 
better physical conditions to the necessary and optional activities (Gehl 2007).  
 
 
Figure 4-The relation between urban activities and quality of the physical environment according to Gehl 
Jan Gehl illustrates the relationship between the urban activities and the quality of the 
physical environment in figure 4. The higher quality of the physical environment, the higher 
activity level, where the optional activity is the more affected activity. In addition, all of 
==PAGE:29==
 
28 
 
the three activities are closely related and influence each other, thus a city or urban space 
must meet the needs of each activity. However, the necessary activities are infrequent in 
urban parks and usually only use parks as a passage.  
The amount of optional and necessary activities in the urban space has changed 
significantly alongside historical development. Only100 years ago, the life and perception 
of the city was completely different than it is today. The city life was characterized by many 
necessary activities related to the industrial life, which the city was dominated by. Figure 
5 shows Jan Gehl’s depiction of activity change in parallel with the urban development. 
 
 
Figure 5-The change in the urban activities according to Gehl 
Around 1960 there is a significant change of the city use.  The necessary activities are 
reduced to a minimum, while the optional activities boom as well as the active optional 
activities. The difference between passive and active optional activities is that passive 
activities mainly consist of sitting at café’s and parks, while active activities include motion 
activities (Eskelund 2013).  
The city has changed from being a city where only everyday activities took place to a city 
where leisure time is spent. “The contrast is stark: from citizen being healthy on the parks 
benches to today's parkour that use the bench as a tool for a variety of creation movements” 
(Realdania 2006). The cities are in movement and the streets should therefore be designed 
to accommodate the arising physical activities from residents. According to Eskelund 
(Pernille 2012), it is not enough anymore to assign urban spaces to different groups of 
activities, where one is for runner, the other for relaxing and the third for shopping etc. 
Eskelund recommend to flow the city together to accommodate the new trends that the city 
is affected by. Eskelund describes the new trend as a fluid city, where there is not the same 
framework as in the past, but things are more fluid and people can afford to use the facilities 
on their own way. Facilities must therefore be adaptable to meet the trend and the need. 
Eskelund points out how not many years back, there was a general perception that the grass 
is not allowed to be walked on, and you were referred to special sports facilities to be 
physically active on. The city was divided in different spaces for different uses and 
==PAGE:30==
 
29 
 
everything had its function “in the urban park there was the pathway to stroll on, the 
benches to sit on and the green lawns to enjoy from a distance” (Eskelund 2013). During 
1960’s and 1970’s, a cultural change happened in the society. The perception of a tough 
functional division of the city was being challenged and people sought a softer and mixed 
functional city. The criticism of the rough frameworks for physical activity and the ‘boring’ 
sports centers grew and an article written back in 1982, criticized the sport centers by taking 
up the issue “why the hell are sports halls so square, standardized and boring” (Eskelund 
2013). The hippie culture was an important reason of the public perception of the city, thus 
the citizens became more experience oriented and the green areas became utilized in new 
ways. 
Today, it becomes an ideal that cities must be active and livable, whereby the large and 
modern cities tend to arrange marathons for its citizens as a sign of modernity and health 
awareness. People use different reasons for being physically active in urban spaces, where 
health factor is the most common reason why a person chooses to be active. Good 
experiences and social trends is often also mentioned among the reasons for a more active 
lifestyle. 
But what is the factual reason that urban residents have become more aware of health 
issues and it somehow has become fashionable to do sports in the urban spaces? 
Many explanations for why the residents of the city have become more health-conscious 
are recently related to the work environments in cities. The explanation is that workplaces 
became more sedentary and thus people seeks physical activity as an alternative. Another 
explanation is based on the need to break the boring approach to do sport “it should not just 
be a matter of sport clenched severity or medical clinical sense that characterize the 
picture” (Eskelund 2013).  In 1980 the effort of physical activity and sport has been 
perceived as boring and people seek new and creative ways to be encouraged. People 
sought therefore urban space, which could bring playful and creative approach to physical 
activity. Eskelund describe this new trend in his book ‘Midt i en surfertid’ (Eskelund 2013) 
and compares the urban facilities with the ocean waves. More specifically, he considers the 
physically active people in the urban space as persons that allow themselves to use benches, 
paths, facades, threes and other urban facilities, to be ‘surfers’ in the urban space.  
However, Eskelund has a critical perception of the growing health trend in cities. He 
expresses the trend in a social perspective, where he sees it as a ‘recreational expression of 
creativity’ that help to increase the gap between the different social groups in the 
city.  According to Eskelund, the need for being recognized is connected with that ‘we 
relate ourselves to the economy through our creativity’. Thus, Eskelund argues that the vast 
majority of athletes in the urban spaces are those, who are highly educated and well paid 
“it is well-educated with good incomes that are most physically active. The creative class 
is also the recreational class. The losers in contrast, are typically less well educated and 
they manage not to put themselves in motion in the same way. The losers are those who 
continue to choose wrong and does not move from the good intentions sofa-landscape in 
front of the TV and out of the city’s surging active life” (Pernille 2012). 
 
The background and history of the use of urban parks leads us to the newer urban design 
theories and principles, that is actual when working with urban parks today.

4.3 A good urban park with regard to support for physical activity 
The following sections will look into the urban design theories which are essential to take 
into account, when conducting an urban park in general and in terms of promotion of 
physical activity. The theories are based on studies from experts in the field, who defined 
what should to be considered when working with urban spaces. 
The section will mainly explore following subjects: cultural-related use of parks, livable

4.3.1 Existing studies that deal with urban parks and physical activity 
How can urban parks support and promote physical activity? 
This section presents studies, which focused on examination of what is needed in an urban 
space to promote physical activity. 
A recent Turkish study made in January 2015 aimed to determine the factors that increase 
user satisfactions with certain urban open spaces and thus also increase their space 
usage  (Özkan et al. 2015). As support to this purpose, a POE was made to determine how 
well the design of urban open spaces meet users’ expectations and to investigate the reason 
for dissatisfaction to be determined during occupancy stage. The Turkish study is important 
to bring to the light, because it has the same research objectives as this research, and 
therefore can be used as point of reference even if it was carried out in another country. 
The method of the Turkish POE study will be described to be able to reflect on the 
similarities and differences between the two POE (the POE method used in this study as 
well as the Turkish POE method). 
In the referred study, a coastal area in Trabzon city was chosen for the study area. The aim 
of the study was to investigate why urban spaces, that are constructed to meet the user's’ 
needs, are not used at the desired level. 
The used methods were built on determination of physical conditions and on a survey using 
5 point Likert attitude scale. The idea was to compare user’s requests with physical findings 
for four different coastal urban spaces in Trabzon.  In each of those four urban spaces, 70 
participants were asked to evaluate the technical, functional and aesthetic performance by 
a 5 point Likert attitude scale. The surveys were conducted on weekdays and weekends. 
The evaluation was very specific as the participants were asked to evaluate the equipment, 
the covering, the safety issues, the cleanness of the parks, the maintenance and the green 
areas.  
The study concluded that it is possible to design urban spaces which can to a larger extent 
meet users’ expectations and encourage them to use the space in a larger degree, by 
considering five following criteria during the designing phase: 
 
1-   Involve the users by constructing activity spaces that the users wish for 
2-   Remove spaces that have been constructed independently of design project 
because it negatively affects users 
3-   All spaces should be given function. Spaces, which does not have specific 
function should be optimized for activities by planning equipment in those spaces. 
4-   End-of-life structures should be removed 
6- Spaces that were specified in design phase but were not constructed or spaces 
whose functions was changed should be made compatible for activity. 
 
Hence, the study offers five options for how an already exist urban park or an urban park 
still in the design phase can promote the users’ use and encourage them to be more active.  
The Turkish study has the same objectives as this study, they both look at urban parks and 
examines their use. Both studies examine the parks in order to come up with suggestions 
==PAGE:32==
 
31 
 
on how to encourage people to use the parks to a greater extent. However, the Turkish study 
investigates the parks at a more detailed level and examines the technical, functional and 
aesthetic performance from the user evaluation. In contrast, the methodology used in the 
study of Superkilen is at a more general level, as it does not look at each facility in the park. 
The method used to investigate Superkilen, is not only based on the questionnaire but also 
on own observation and interviews with experts in the field. Additionally, the study does 
focus on the user’s activity and the way they use Superkilen. 
Even though the purpose of both POE studies is similar, the approaches are a little different. 
The Turkish study focuses on if the facilities in the parks lives up to the expectation and 
satisfaction level of the users while the study of Superkilen focuses on the user’s activities 
in the park. Even if the POE method in the Turkish study is much more detailed than the 
POE of Superkilen, it is not necessary to evaluate all the facilities based on the user’s 
assessment in Superkilen, as the study focuses on the activities. The findings of the Turkish 
study are set in conjunction with the findings in the study of Superkilen, in section 7-
discussion. 
 
Another study is referred in a paper by Karla A. Henderson from North Carolina State 
University, USA. The paper provides a summary of the research, within urban outdoor 
recreation venues, including parks and its effect on public health (Henderson 2006). In the 
paper, Henderson only deals with nature based parks (as the green zone in Superkilen-see 
figure 14). 
The paper points out the proximity aspect as the main factor that encourage people to use 
urban parks and be physical active “Nevertheless, without the proximity of urban parks the 
opportunities for physical activity likely would be greatly limited in most cities” (Henderson 
2006). The paper refers to earlier studies that shows an important relation between the urban 
parks location and the number of users. Henderson refers to (Crawford et al. 2008), whose 
studies showed that access to attractive large public spaces was associated with higher 
intensity of walking. In addition, the studies showed that people who used public open 
spaces, as urban parks, were three times more likely than others, to achieve recommended 
levels of activity. The paper also brings about another research, which proves that access 
to parks is related to more activity of children and youth. (Sallis et al. 2000) does 
indicates that access to parks or parks located in walking distance, promotes the activity 
levels for children and youth. 
Safety is also pointed out as an important characteristic of urban parks. Various studies 
show that people who are least active are also more concerned about safety issues (Donner 
et al. 2009). This is in relevance with Gehls 12 criteria (figure 6) where one of the three 
categories consist of protection and safety issues. 
In addition, the paper refers to studies that found a link between nature or green areas and 
physical activity. (Sullivan 2004) proves that trees and grass located certainly in urban 
parks, promote greater use of urban spaces and therefore support the physical activity. 
Physical characteristics of urban parks does also have a huge effect on the usage of the 
park, according to Henderson’s paper. Facilities allow people to use them in certain ways. 
For example, in the same way as a bicycle path encourage more people to use bicycle in 
specific area, a walking route does encourage people to walk more. 
 
Even though these studies provide a set of guidelines for how to design urban parks that 
support physical activity among citizens, still it might be challenging task. Many studies 
suggest namely that it is not enough to have general guidelines when building an urban 
park, and that the use depends on the cultural background of the park users.

A Turkish study from 2011 have shown cultural differences in using urban parks (Ozguner 
2011). The study aims to explore Turkish residents’ use of urban parks in a cultural context. 
The study tried to understand the difference in use between Turkish people and western 
people, when it comes to urban parks.  
The study points out different studies that also investigate the residents’ use of urban parks 
in a cultural context, but that were made in other countries. For instance, an old study in 
the United State showed that white-Americans favors a more natural and less managed 
urban spaces, whereas African-Americans and Latin-American likes more managed urban 
spaces that has structured facilities such as ball fields (Kaplan & Frey Talbot 1988). 
Another similar study was made in UK and point out that Indian people in UK highly prefer 
to visit parks with many family members or in groups in contrast to British people that 
prefer to visit parks alone for recreation. 
That’s why the Turkish study wants to examine how Turkish residents prefer to use the 
urban parks and compare the results with similar western studies. The method used in the 
study consist of a questionnaire survey that was carried out in two Turkish parks in Ispata 
city. The questionnaire used mainly open-ended questions to identify the reasons of their 
answers.  The survey was carried out twice a week in April, June, August, September and 
October when the parks were intensively used. The interesting findings from this study was 
the huge difference in use between Turkish residents and Western residents. Picnic and 
barbecues were the main uses of an urban park among Turkish people, wherein Turkish 
people preferred to visit parks in big groups- mainly their families- to have lunch or dinner 
together. The studies that were made in western countries and were point out in this study 
as well, showed that the western people mainly use the parks for walking, dog walking, 
sport activities and exercises. Thus, while western use parks for active recreation Turkish 
use them for passive recreation. It is also in accordance with results found by (Gobster 
2002), that was made in Lincoln Park in Chicago and showed that the minority groups 
preferred the passive activities, such as picnicking and socializing, wherein Whites 
preferred to be physically active. Thus, the Turkish study is consistent with other similar 
studies that point out that the biggest different in park use between Western and Non-
Western people is the active/passive use and the way they prefer to visit a park, wherein 
Western prefer individuals visit of parks, Non-Western prefer groups visits. The new 
finding in the Turkish study was related to the safe issue, wherein citizens in Western 
countries concern for safety issues and point it as a main reason for either to use a park or 
not, the Turkish citizens didn’t concern for the safety in urban parks. The study explain that 
the reason is not that the Turkish people don’t find safety as a main issue, but because 
Turkish find urban parks safe for themselves and their children and thus don’t think about 
it as a problem. The study point the large use pattern of parks in Turkey as a main reason 
for why they feel safe. Another reason that the study point out is the presence of many 
families in Turkish park that may give a feeling of a safety. 
The study also discusses the importance of making parks that support the users’ needs and 
requirements, to be sure that the parks will be used regularly. For instance, the study shows 
that Turkish people expects to find supply of drinking water and shaded areas in urban 
parks. The reason for the supply of drinking water is that Turkish use the parks mainly for 
picnic and barbecue and need clean water to wash their hands and fruits but also to make 
tea and coffee. 
In addition to the cultural differences, there are also studies showing that parks and green 
areas are most beneficial for resource-rich people when it comes to physical activity (Wolch 
et al. 2014). This is also in accordance with Copenhagen Municipality’s public health report

in Copenhagen, than in other districts. Also, the center for sport and architecture in

4.3.2 Livable urban spaces 
Urban planner designates several parameters that contribute to a livable city, many of which 
can be scaled to urban parks. 
Livable city parameters, which can be also used to create livable urban parks and encourage 
people to use them, will be clarified below. 
The human dimension and the meeting between people is a high priority when it comes to 
design of livable cities and urban places. In the book ‘byer for mennesker’ (Gehl 2010), 
Gehl points that there is an interaction between the city and the citizens. Consequently, he 
notices that the city shapes us, and we also shape the city. According to Gehl, liveable city 
does stimulates kindness, courtesy and social opportunities and is directly related to the 
presence of people in the urban space. We get inspired by other people's activity and 
presence “the presence of other people is in itself a signal that there is worth to be…kids 
are watching from the window the other kids are out playing and they rush off to be with 
them” (Gehl 2010). It's people, who either make urban spaces livable or unlivable, but this 
does not happen by itself. The role of careful urban planning is to make urban space 
appealing to the people and give them the incentives and the ability to use it. One of the 
most important parameters that can create livable urban space is the number of users and 
the time they spend in the certain space. According to Gehl, the number of users in a 
particular urban space is the quantity and the time they spend is the quality. The bigger 
number of people that only race through the spaces does not create a livable city in the same 
way as if a small number of people move slower and makes several stays in the space. Thus, 
the number and time are important planning parameters for the strengthening of livable 
urban space. 
Gehl describes walking and cycling as important factors because they create slow traffic. 
As urban planner, it is essential to adapt the urban space in a way that it provides the 
opportunity to walk with the opportunity of stay and experience and not only to enable 
quick walking traffic. According to Gehl, it is almost always easier and more effective to 
increase the quality, thus the desire to spend time in a certain space, than the quantity or 
the number of people, it creates better urban quality. 
Physical measures, as soft edges are in addition also pointed as main factor for creating 
livable cities. The urban edges can contribute to provide clarity, comfort and security. Gehl 
==PAGE:35==
 
34 
 
defines good edges as the edges that give a full view of all events across the room and do 
not allow the possibility of unpleasant surprises. Soft edges are related to the time that the 
users spend and either contributes to a greater use of the urban space or conversely. In the 
book “Byer for mennesker” (Gehl 2010), Gehl encourages urban planners to create inviting 
and rich in details edge zones in residential areas. Open front yards and front areas and in 
general semi-private zones in residential areas, are pointed out among solutions to create a 
livable urban space. This finds also application in other urban spaces. Edges that marks 
different areas without being hard and repulsive, can contribute to a more inviting urban 
space, thus livable urban space. 
In connection with livable cities, the certain density is often pointed as a factor that 
contributes to livable atmosphere in the city. Gehl points the density as important as well, 
but he clarifies that it is not a matter of course “There are many examples of places with a 
high building density and poor urban space, that does not work as intended. New districts 
often have close and loud constructions still there are too many, too big and too poor urban 
spaces that does not inspire people to come out and use the space” (Gehl 2010).  Gehl 
argues that reasonable density with good urban quality contributes more to a livable city 
than higher densities. Especially high density of residential areas with tall houses is very 
critical for a livable city, according to Gehl. Although this phenomenon applies to cities in 
general, it can still be considered in relation with urban parks. 
Based on Gehl's concept of density in cities it is conceivable that a reasonable distance 
between the facilities in a park as well as an optimal location will also encourage people to 
use them and appear more inviting to use.

5.4 Purpose and vision  
Superkilen is, as mentioned, a subproject of a major urban renewal project of Mimersgade 
neighborhood. The overall project aims to improve Mimersgade neighborhood in 
Nørrebro- both regarding physical features and social integration. The overall project, 
which Superkilen is part of, aims to create a positive development of the area to make it 
more attractive to live, work and visit (Kommune 2003). 
In this context Superkilen conceived as an urban park which will give the area a special 
character and show the district's diversity. "Superkilen must be a beacon for 
Nørrebro…Superkilen must reflect the neighborhood's diversity of people with different 
cultural, social and age backgrounds" (Kommune 2003). 
According to the competition program, the Copenhagen Municipality has several purposes 
of establishing Superkilen, which can be divided into four points: 
 
1- To create a multi-functional relaxing and activity area for recreation purposes to 
local residents in Nørrebro. This objective is intended as a means of serving a 
greater purpose: to gather the neighborhood's diversity of residents, thus promote 
integration through recreation. 
2- To create opportunities for play and movement activities for all ages and 
encourage local citizens to use them. 
3- To be an attraction for the entire Copenhagen. 
4- To create a physical relationship between Nørrebrogade and Tagensvej. 
 
” The proposed solution must have a high architectural level, which can help to transform 
the space to a landmark for neighborhood- a creative and diverse urban space for the 
enjoyment of local residents across age, gender, religion and culture and as an attraction 
for the whole city as well” (Kommune 2003). 
 
The second point of Copenhagen Municipality’s purpose with Superkilen, is the most 
relevant for this study. 
 
5.5 Citizen involvement  
One of the main things that Superkilen project has been admired for- both nationally and 
internationally-is the citizen involvement and the great influence that local citizens had on 
the finished project. Thus, a description of the citizen involvement process, based on the 
Copenhagen Municipality competition plan and the project description of Superkilen, is 
explained in this section.  
In the Copenhagen Municipality’s competition plan it is explained that the Municipality 
had citizen involvement as a theme and that the citizens had contributed to the competition 
program (Kommune 2003). The Municipality therefore requires that the proposed solution 
must provide a proposal, which includes citizen involvement in the future process. The 
==PAGE:45==
 
44 
 
project description explains the method of cooperation with citizens in practice. The 57-
different national background that the local resident of Mimersgade neighborhood 
represents was a starting point for the citizen involvement process. The entire cooperation 
between the consultant team and the local citizens consist of picking 57 different objects 
from 57 different countries, which further on will be placed in Superkilen. This is done 
through a series of meetings with representatives of the local area as well as workshops 
with citizens. In addition, it was possible to submit individual proposals on Superkilen 
website. The selection of objects was done by the consultant team in dialogue with 
representatives from the local area. The selection was based on the object's function, 
history, robustness, operation and maintenance, economy and aesthetic. 
Additionally, five groups travelled to five different countries- Palestine, Thailand, Texas 
and Jamaica- to acquire five certain objects that have been selected in citizen’s workshops. 
These objects were later on installed in the park.  
 
5.6 Layout of Superkilen 
The 57 objects are divided into three different zones, three parts of Superkilen. The park 
extends, as mentioned before, from Nørrebrogade to Tagensvej and consists of the red, the 
black and the green zone. The different zones and colors are integrated to form one 
connected park but with three different proposals. 
 
5.6.1 The red zone 
The red zone consists of a red zone and cycle path, that physically connect Nørrebrogade 
with Mimersgade (see figure 12). The zone is 13235 m2 and is an urban extension to the 
Nørrebrohall, that is facing Superkilen through the red zone. As the Nørrebrohall offers 
sport and cultural activities, the red zone also offers the same.

The black zone or Mimers Plads is the middle part of Superkilen that physically connects

facilities, mixed basketball court and skateboard area and swings. In addition, sitting and

6.6 Interview 
Below is a summary of interviews with two experts, picked for the study. Both experts are 
interested in urban planning and know Superkilen project. The interviews had been 
transcribed and then summarized, basing on their most important points. 
 
6.6.1 Interview with Klaus Eskelund 
Klaus Eskelund is educated in physical education and social studies and he works as a high 
school teacher. Besides, he also makes sports consultancy projects for municipalities. 
Eskelund is an author of the book 'Midt i en surfertid', which is about the modern city we 
live in and the new trends, that are associated with body culture in the modern society. The 
book tries to give an idea of why the body culture changes in line with community and city 
development and also provides an insight into the modern society, that according Eskelund, 
has as ideal that we all must be “city surfers” and constantly be active. Eskelund has also 
written articles and participates in interviews that criticize Superkilen and other urban 
planning projects, for being narrow versioned and only directed towards, what he calls 'the 
creative class'. The creative class is, according to Eskelund, an American concept, a term, 
describing those people, who produce new knowledge and are very normative for how the 
urban development is. According to Eskelund, typically the well-educated and those with 
a strong economic and culture belong to the creative class. The book ‘Midt i en surfertid’ 
and his criticism of Superkilen, makes Eskelund an appropriate person to interview in 
connection with this study. 
 
Eskelunds main three key points that he emphasizes highly during the interview are:  
1. That a different citizen involvement may have result better physical activity 
facilities.  
2. The fact that Superkilen appeal to the creative class 
3. In connection with gentrification process, there may be an exclusion of previous 
life forms.  
 
During the interview, it appears clearly that Eskelund thinks that Superikilen has become a 
beautiful urban park. He can also understand that it has received as much international 
attention and several awards, regarding its design and the architecture. However, Eskelund 
does not believe that Superkilen appeals to physical activity, as the project aimed for. 
Eskelund believes that the main focus in Superkilen have been more on design and 
architecture than on functionality. Although the project phase included some citizen’s 
involvement meeting, still Eskelund does not think that the involvement was optimal for 
the final project. According to Eskelund, the meetings may have focused on the facility’s 
design instead of the core activity that the citizens need in an urban park "what do you know 
from your countries and what do you know from your and then we try to mix it all together 
so that there is something fun, but does it work? "(Klaus Eskelund).  
==PAGE:59==
 
58 
 
During the interview, Eskelund focused on the green zone as a specific example of how 
confusing the design can be. According to Eskelund, the basketball court in the green zone, 
which both is a basketball court and a skating area at the same time, seems very confusing 
and it is not clear, how to use it. These doubts go alongside the current trends in design 
culture that likes to be creative and play with forms, without necessarily incorporating 
functionality for users. According to Eskelund, if an urban park should increase the activity 
level among citizens, the park should be more activity-oriented. Specifically, he suggests 
to let the design grow out of the activity needs and not vice versa "So take it the other way 
around. I think. I do not think it bears the mark of this is the way it has been made in”. 
One thing that Eskelund finds characteristic of most new urban projects is that they all 
appeals to the creative class. Superkilen is no exception according Eskelund. Although he 
thinks that Superkilen is a very multicultural park and in some way, reflects the diversity 
in Nørrebro, still he criticizes it for being too multifunctional and thus only appeal to the 
creative users. The open facilities and the mix of many different facilities together can 
create confusion for many users, who may feel insecure about the use of the park, he 
believes. What Eskelund in fact criticizse is the new trend, which he believes exists among 
urban planners. According to him, the trend is about congest the city with sports facilities 
and then believe that all people will be plunged into physical activity, "It just do not" says 
Eskelund. The only group that may be encouraged by this trend is the creative class, 
Eskelund believes. The reason for this is that the creative class takes their own initiative to 
figure out how to use the space for physical activity. The other groups in society requires a 
form of organization and a sense of commitment before they get physically active, 
according to Eskelund. This opinion he builds on the many sport studies, which shows that 
those who do most sports and keeps themselves in shape and eat healthy, is typically the 
well-educated people with strong economic bases. Therefore, Eskelund does not only 
believe in building a fitness facilities but focus on how to get people use them.  
Additionally, Eskelund expresses concern about the gentrification processes that he thinks 
lead to exclusion of some groups. Even though Ekselund agrees that Superkilen creates 
good publicity for Nørrebro district and does attract new groups to the area, he raises 
concern about that Superkilen contributes to replacing those, who already lived there with 
new user groups. His concern is not only related to Superkilen, but generally to the 
gentrification processes around the city. He believes that replacing the residents with new 
resourceful groups will only push the problems to other areas instead of solving them.

6.6.2 Interview with Preben Astrup 
Preben Astrup is a sport consultant, who advises, associations, municipalities and ministries 
about socially disadvantaged people and the integration of them in sport activities.  
Preben works on Get2Sport project, which aims to support volunteer trainers in the socially 
disadvantaged neighborhoods, to ensure that children and young people in these areas are 
active in sports. Get2Sport is attached to the Danish sports federations DIF. In May 2016 
Astrup won Gerlev-Prize for his project, Get2Sport, and for his efforts to promote and 
support the integration through active participation in associations in disadvantaged 
neighborhoods around Denmark. The Gerlev-Prize is awarded to a work, which has helped 
developing sport quality and which has brought renewal in attitudes to sport cultural 
location and options(Olling Jakob 2016). Therefore, Astrup is a relevant key figure in the 
investigation of the case study, as Superkilen also is located in socially disadvantaged area 
(Mimersgade neighborhood). 
 
Astrup’s main three key points that he emphasises greatly during the interview are: 
1. That there are major cultural differences in the practice of sports  
2. That you are not always aware of the target audience when designing projects to 
promote physical activity in socially disadvantaged areas  
3. That there is a hysteria in Denmark about physical activity. 
 
Astrup states that the difference between the cult of sports is very large between boys and 
girls. He experienced great difficulty in engaging girls in sports projects and problem is 
bigger, when it comes to girls with different ethnic backgrounds. According to him, it is 
because of the cultural background that those girls are used to. His experience shows that 
it creates problems when girls with different ethnic backgrounds are physically active in 
public spaces. They prefer to be either indoors or in a secure environment far away from 
the field of view. Thus, Astrup does not believe that Superkilen appeal to girls with different 
ethnic background to come and be physically active there, still he thinks that the park is a 
good place for the girls for recreation. Astrup sees also problems in Superkilen, when it 
comes to disadvantaged young men. Astrup differentiates between organized and 
unorganized sports, concerning the resource-rich and resource-weak young. According to 
Astrup, Superkilen is a park that calls for unorganized sports, where people come and 
occupy spaces and facilities in their own ways. This is according to him a great challenge 
for the disadvantaged young, as they already come from a disorganized life and therefore 
are not strong enough to organize their sport activities on their own. It requires a very 
organized person with a very well-organized life to cultivate unorganized sports, according 
to Astrup. "I think that the organized sport has a lot to offer for children and young people 
and perhaps also older, which life is not incredibly organized. If you look at who cultivate 
the so-called unorganized sports, then you will see the vast majority are prosperous" 
Astrup second important point is that it is "a white man who dreams of rescuing a black 
man". He thinks it is strange that many projects do not take hold in what the target group 
of young disadvantaged people really want to take in use, but instead they are built on the 
designer's’ ideas about what is the best for the young. He mentions climbing lanes and 
skateboard tracks as failed solutions, when it comes to promote physical activity among 
young disadvantaged men. According to Astrup, an ordinary football field will in most 
cases be enough to engage these young people in physical activity "What is my wildest 
dream, then it must also be Hassan’s. They believe that Hassan must have the same dream 
as my son called Søren. When Hassan did not use the offer, so they think that there must be 
something wrong with Hassan. But it may well be that it is you that something is wrong 
with as architect and urban planner. They do not ask them".   
==PAGE:61==
 
60 
 
The third point that Astrup raised during the interview is, what he calls a hysteria about 
physical activity in Denmark. Astrup remember how sport in the 70s was associated with 
the formation, upbringing and drama. Today, sport and physical activity are associated with 
health and become a high priority in urban planning projects around the country. According 
to Astrup, it has changed the way you work with cities in order to focus on getting people 
to do something between blocks instead of just to sit and enjoy a meal together. According 
to Astrup, the biggest difference between Dane and persons with different ethnic 
backgrounds is that they prioritize things differently. He believes that Danish women are 
very concerned about what they eat and to keep their shape, rather than who they eat with 
"Meals is not just about weight but about community […] Maybe even spend an hour on 
eating in good fellowship and cut down on the run ". On the other hand, he believes that 
women with different backgrounds do not value good physical shape as high, but instead 
value the community and eating together, much more. 
 
Based on the above-mentioned points, Astrup message is that sport and physical activity 
are not always crucial for health and integration, but that the quality of what you do is more 
important. Additionally, Astrup encourages to employ more females in projects, which aim 
to engage young women with other ethnic background in sports, what he believes, will 
make them and their parents more confident. Likewise, Astrup believes that organized sport 
is the best option, when working with disadvantaged young people, whose daily life is 
already unorganized.

and are listed below.

Surprisingly, there are most facilities in the least used zone (green), while the facilities in 
the red and black zone are much less. It seems that the users are not attracted by the amount 
of facilities, but rather by the function of those facilities. This is inconsistent with the 
Turkish POE that is described in theory chapter, section 4.3.1. The Turkish POE (Özkan et 
al. 2015) claims that all spaces should be given functionality and that spaces, which were 
not made functional should be optimized for activities by planning equipment in those 
spaces. The study claims that this will increase user satisfaction and the space usage level. 
The difference in between Superkilen and the Turkish study raises the issue, whether the 
use of urban parks is depending on cultural aspects. While some groups in Denmark may 
prefer open spaces to express themselves creatively, the Turkish users may prefer clear 
function of facilities. This is actually, what another Turkish study showed. The study (also 
mentioned in theory chapter, section 4.3.1), illustrates cultural difference between Turkish 
people and Western people, in using urban parks (Ozguner 2011). 
If the number of facilities is not essential to the number of user, the popularity of the black 
and red zone may be described by other designing factors. 
One of them is the content of core facilities that are the main gathering point for the users. 
The observations indicate that the users tend to gather around certain facilities instead of 
being scattered. The empty red space in the red zone does not contain any physical facilities 
but is still a gathering point for a creative execution of sport (skating, rollerblading, etc.). 
In addition, the swings, which are mostly used as sitting facilities for both children and 
adults, are very popular among the users. All in all, there is a tendency that the red zone is 
popular among young people that do not seem to be bothered by noise. The black zone 
contains core facilities as well, as the slide is a collecting point for kids and their parents, 
and the fountain, which is used as sitting facility during the summer. This is in accordance 
with Gehls urban principles (see figure 7), which among others, focus on collecting events 
and to encourage people to spend more time in a certain urban space. The black zone 
attracts both small children and adults that seek relaxation. In addition, the red and black 
zones are both open spaces, which do support Gehls see and listen principles (see figure 8). 
In the red zone, most of facilities are gathered around the walk- and bike path, so that it is 
easy to use the facilities on one’s way. Regarding the black zone, most of the facilities are 
gathered around the water fountain, which is a kind of central point of the black zone. 
However, the see and listen principles are not supported in same way in the green zone. 
The green zone is extending over a long and narrow area that more or less reminds of a 
path rather than a park to spend time into. In addition, it contains relatively high hills, which 
do not support the factor of opening and inviting.  
Regarding Gehls’ 12 quality criteria (see figure 7) for a public space, the protection and 
safe factor are among the most important criterias in an open space. A paper by Karla A. 
Henderson from North Carolina State University (Henderson 2006) agrees with Gehl’s 
safety requirements by pointing safety as the most important factor in urban parks.  
==PAGE:64==
 
63 
 
This is, among others, ensured by the bicycle path, directing cyclists and setting the safety 
boundaries for the transient and other pedestrian users. Protection against traffic is also 
guaranteed by the clear boundaries an in Superkilen by the no entry for cars.  
The safe factor is also secured partly by the good lighting in all three zones. On the contrary 
safety factor lags regarding the coating in the red zone. The coating has been replaced 
several times, because of the complaints about slippery bike path during the rain (Lorenzen 
Benedicte 2015). 
 
7.1.2 2. finding- Proximity is significant 
One important characteristics of the frequent users of Superkilen is that the most of them 
live close to the park. The distance factor is the most important aspect to encourage people 
to use Superkilen. This arises from figure 17, which show that 91% of the frequent users 
of Superkilen do live in Nørrebro or within 1 km from Superkilen. In contrast, 56% of the 
infrequent users do live close to Superkilen. This is consistent with the paper by Karla A. 
Henderson from North Carolina State University, USA. Her paper is mentioned in theory 
(chapter, section 4.3.1) and mentions the proximity aspect as the main factor that encourage 
people to use urban parks. 
 
7.1.3 3. finding- Walking and relaxing are preferable  
The empirical study indicates that the users of Superkilen prefer to walk and relax during 
their visit.  This is independent of whether they are frequent or infrequent users. The users 
that prefer to cycle in Superkilen seems not to be many, according to the survey answers. 
However, the percentage of cycling may be much higher than indicated in figure 19. One 
explanation of this could be the factor that the manually distributed surveys did not cover 
the cyclists, because of the difficulty of stopping them during distributing the survey.  
It is unlikely that all cyclists through the park are deliberate users of it. It may well be that 
they only cycle passing by, because of the cycling infrastructure provided, and the park is 
not their destination. However, there may be a part of the cyclists, who deliberately choose 
to cycle in Superkilen and thus are conscious users of it. Nevertheless, the assessment of 
whether the cyclists are users or not is difficult and therefore it was not done during 
observation.    
 
7.1.4 4. finding- The multifunctional facilities confuse some user’s groups 
The study revealed that a group of the users of Superkilen do feel an improvement in their 
activity level due to Superkilen. 27% of the users that use Superkilen frequently, feel more 
physically active after establishment of the park. Only 34% of the frequent users can say 
with certainty that they do not feel any improvement in their physical activity in relation 
with Superkilen, while 78% of the infrequent users say that they do not feel any 
improvement. The frequent users are also those, who are more likely than the infrequent 
users to achieve the recommended level of activity. Although more than one third (27%) 
of the frequent users do feel an improvement, which is relatively a high number, still the 
observations in Superkilen shows that there is not a high diversity in the group of users that 
are active. There is some indication that the most active users are a group of young Danish 
men in their twenties. One important characteristics of the active group is that they express 
themselves physically in active ways, whereas the most exercised activity is skating. 
However, there is a lot of walking and cycling people, that may also be users of the park. 
This may be consistent with some of the critics related to Superkilen. Both interviewed 
sport consultant’s, Astrup and Eskelund, who criticize Superkilen of targeting one specific 
social group by consisting too many multifunctional facilities. According to Astrup it 
==PAGE:65==
 
64 
 
requires a lot of organizing to exercise unorganized sport, as the slaters do. The 
multifunctional facilities, open spaces and facilities that call for unorganized sports, may 
therefore exclude some groups from being physically active in a place as Superkilen.   
 
7.2 The intentions of Superkilen 
Below is a presentation of who use Superkilen and what characterize the use. There will 
also be discussed if the users are those who already are aware and engaged in physical 
activity or is the park enhancing the activity among new groups. 
The study shows that Superkilen is more or less used by all social and cultural groups in 
the local area. The only group, who is excluded is the older generation. This group does 
almost not exist in either the survey or the observations. One explanation can be related to 
the few number of elderly people in Nørrebro, as figure 5.1 in case chapter, show a variation 
of compisition of age, where it seems as young people is the mayority among Nørrebro 
residents, while the older are the minority. 
The use of Superkilen is very different depending on the group, which is using the park. 
What characterize the most active in Superkilen is their young age and the factor that they 
are Danish men. The study does not show if they are resource rich persons, but according 
to the interviewed experts, the persons, who can handle to do unorganized sports, are also 
those, who are resource rich persons. In addition, kids are also very physically active in 
Superkilen and that cover both Danish as kids from other cultural background. The 
tendency is that the other groups (adults, young women and young men from other cultural 
background) prefer to use the park passively. However, the observations show that the 
adults men, who brings their children to play, have a higher tendency to be physically active 
with their children than women, who also bring their children to play.  
Does Superkilen then works as intended by Copenhagen municipality?  
The Copenhagen Municipality intentions with Superkilen are listed as four points in section 
5.4. There is no doubt that Superkilen succeed to be an attraction for the entire Copenhagen 
and has attracted worldwide attention, due to its special design, which both reflects the 
local residents' different cultures and also is multifunctional. However, this 
multifunctionality of the facilities does not support the purpose of “creating opportunities 
for play and movement activities for all ages and encourage local citizens to use them” 
(Kommune 2003). Based on the results of the study, it is clear that the multifunctional 
facilities in Superkilen only attract few user groups. As mentioned before, the users group 
that manage to use the multifunctional facilities actively and really get a sweat, are also 
those who already are resource rich. Even though, many users group from different social 
and cultural background are represented in Superkilen, the study shows that only few users 
manage to be physically active by using the provided facilities. The vast majority of the 
users use the facilities for recreational purposes. However, the most popular activity among 
the users is walking, which of course is a physically active action. It is yet not entirely clear 
if Copenhagen Municipality did intended walking as one of their purposes or expected other 
physical activity levels among the users of Superkilen. This was among the issues that 
would be clarified during an interview with Copenhagen Municipality, if it took place. This 
is, however, not an obstacle to reach suggestions for further urban parks project that 
promote to improve the physical activity among the local residents.

7.3 Suggestions to take into consideration when designing urban parks 
To be able to consider what others, who will in the future realize similar projects, can learn 
from Superkilen, a number of suggestions are listed below. The suggestions are based on 
the results of the study and the literature study, and are divided into specific suggestions 
for Superkilen and general suggestions for similar projects that goal to promote physical 
activity among local residents.  
 
Below are the suggestions aimed at Copenhagen Municipality to improve Superkilen in 
terms of physical activity among local citizens: 
 
The multi-track in the green zone (see annex 5, figure 9) should be rebuilt to a 
traditional football track, for the benefit of local boys. The present track is a 
mixture of skater space and a basketball court and is according to the study 
rarely used.  
 
More covered areas, as it turned out that all use of the park stops during rain. 
 
Below are the suggestions aimed at similar urban parks projects that aim to improve the 
physical activity among the local residents: 
 
A serious involvement of local citizens, not only in design but more aimed at 
the use- what do they want to do in the local park? 
 
Clear and traditional facilities, that do not allow confusion and that everyone 
can manage to use. This can of course be supplemented with facilities for the 
creative users. 
 
Collection of the facilities to make them visible and accessible for the users (as 
in the red and black zone and not division of the facilities over a long distance 
as in the green zone) 
 
Accept, that some local parks are not able to encourage all social and cultural 
groups in physical activity. For instance, the tendency is that women and 
especially women from other ethnic background do not prefer to be physical 
active in their local parks, as they will be visible for their surroundings. 
However, the same women may be physical active in other parks that are far 
away and maybe covered with trees, where they are not as visible as in their 
local parks. Though, they may still use the local park for recreational purposes.  
 
7.4 Method reflection 
The study of Superkilen is based on empirical methods in form of questionnaire and 
observation and is supported by two interviews with two informants. The interviews 
indicate some tendency in the use of Superkilen and are supported by the questionnaire and 
observations. For instance, that the use in Superkilen depends on the background. However, 
two interviews are not enough to serve as a basis for theories and explanations. It required 
more interviews with experts in urban planning and sport before it can form a larger 
experience that can be used to create a clearer picture of the use. Discussion is also 
incomplete without an interview with Copenhagen Municipality, which would enable to 
contribute to the discussion by evaluating the results based on municipality’s own intention 
while constructing Superkilen. Nevertheless, the study uses materials available on the 
Internet, to assess how the project intentions fit the study results. 
With regards to the observation, a different approach may possibly also have a different 
effect on the results. The observation used in the study is only aimed at users of Superkilen, 
who spend time in the park, either actively or passively, but do not investigate pedestrians 
and cyclists. A greater focus on pedestrians and cyclists might have had big changes in 
examination of the user behavior of Superkilen. One way to perform it in practice, would 
==PAGE:67==
 
66 
 
be to use some observations on only counting pedestrians and cyclists and look at their 
gender and nationality. However, there are many different ways of observing and not all 
may be included in a study like this, because of limited resources and time. An ideal 
approach would be to involve more people, who would conduct the observation, one group 
could focus exclusively on those, who spend time in the park, while another group would 
focus on pedestrians and cyclists. This would allow to make comparison between both 
groups and could lead to different results on how users consume the park. 
As the study is based on a questionnaire as well, a change in the questions may also provide 
different results. For instance, the choice could have been made to divide respondents in 
women and men and compare their use and examine whether there are different tendency 
depending on gender. Though, the gender is included in the observations but could also be 
included in the questionnaire as well. This would provide a basis for comparison between 
observations and questionnaire. However, there are many different ways to analyze and 
classify the results, and a choice of one method will naturally lead to a rejection of other 
methods. 
 
What make this study special is that it develops its own method by combining an POE of a 
specific urban park with a literature study, instead of working with POE and literature study 
separately (see figure 3 in method section). The combination of those two methods, make 
it possible to build a conclusion and come up with suggestions to improve Superkilen and 
to take into consideration when designing other local urban park.

8 Conclusion  
The urban structure and the modern urban life are among the main factors that result in 
physical inactivity, the cause of many health problems for people living in the cities all 
over the world. Due to the alarming rate of physical inactivity among residents in cities, (in 
Denmark 47% of the adult population are overweight and 13% are obese) urban planners 
are using local urban parks as opportunities to promote the physical activity among local 
residents. This study goes a step further by performing a Post Occupancy Evaluation of 
Superkilen urban park in Nørrebro district in Copenhagen, alongside a literature study of 
promoting physical activity in urban parks. The POE study aims to clarify why and how 
local residents use Superkilen for physical expression and then supported by the literature 
study, formulate a series of recommendations, for Copenhagen Municipality and other 
stakeholders. 
The literature study indicates that livability is a main factor to encourage people to use a 
certain urban space. According to Gehl, there are some criteria and principles that should 
be achieved in order to make a designed urban space livable. The case study of Superkilen, 
which is based on 111 persons that answered a survey and 20 observations in the park, 
shows that the red and black zones are to some extent in consistent with the theoretical 
criteria and principles, and therefore these zones are more used, compared with the green 
zone, which does not fulfill the criteria so well.  
In addition, there are many studies that indicate cultural differences in the use of urban 
spaces. This suggest that designing urban parks is directly depending on the users and their 
background and that there is not one way to design a good urban park that encourage their 
use of the local residents. In addition, the study implies that the urban spaces are most 
beneficial for resource-rich people regarding the physical activity.  
 
The case specific conclusion that can be drawn from this study, indicates that Superkilen is 
mostly used by one group, regarding physical activity. However, it is a popular park used 
by all different groups in Nørrebro. The results of the study demonstrate that those who 
manage to use Superkilen actively and innovatively, are one homogeneous group of young 
Danish men. Based on this study and other similar studies, that also examine urban parks 
and physical activity, the reasons for which not all the users manage to be physically active 
are both related to cultural- and social aspects. The cultural aspects are basing on the study, 
the reason to the inactivity among women in urban spaces, and especially women with 
different ethnical background than Danish. Regarding the social aspects, the study indicates 
that only those who already have a strong social background and thus are resource-rich, are 
able to use multifunctional facilities and perform unorganized sport and therefore be 
physically active. The case study reveals that walking is the most popular activity among 
the users of Superkilen, and that the proximity is the most important aspect, which 
encourages people to visit Superkilen. However, the study suggests that a replacement of 
some of the multifunctional facilities in Superkilen, which only are rarely used, with other 
more traditional facilities, may encourage more users to be physical active.  
 
Finally, we may conclude the study with a suggestion that encouraging all various groups 
of people in physical activity in urban park is not always a reasonable goal. The same group 
of people that may be physically active in an urban park far away from their home, may 
not prefer to be physically active in their local park, due to cultural reasons. In some cases, 
it must be accepted that not all of the users will be physically active in local parks. 
Therefore, mapping the target user group and focusing on their needs and abilities, would 
be the most appropriate in designing urban parks, which aim to improve the physical 
activity among the users.

9 Recommendation for future research 
This study does not include the transient and cyclists in the observation of Superkilen, due 
to the inconvenience of both focusing on the main users and the transient/cyclists at the 
same time. However, including them in the study may leads to different results than this 
study. Therefore, a specific recommendation would be to perform the same study as this 
but with greater focus on the transient and cyclists.  
 
Regarding the conclusions, the study finds that there is not one right way to design an urban 
park that promote physical activity. In addition, it is close depending on who the users of 
the park are, as there are both cultural and social aspects in the way the users practice sport. 
One proposal of the study is therefore to involve the potential users of the park in the 
planning stage with main focus on the use and not only the design of the park. The study 
leads therefore to openings for further research on what the users in Denmark wants to be 
offered in their local urban parks and furthermore if there are any difference in the users 
need depending on the different areas in Denmark.  
 
Another interesting research, which is based on the suggestions of this study is to examine 
the female’s behavior in urban spaces regarding physical activity. A specific study could 
be to assess how the female users of Superkilen, who prefer to use the park for recreational 
purposes, is willing to be physical active in other parks, which is not necessarily close to 
their residence.
