Abstract
The Order bound is one of the best known lower bounds for the minimum distance of
an algebraic geometric code. It is a generalization of the Feng-Rao bound. Historically
the order bound was ﬁrst deﬁned for the dual of one-point algebraic geometry codes. A
generalization of the order bound for any algebraic geometry codes was given by Bee-
len [2]. Similar techniques used in the Feng-Rao and the order bound can be used to
ﬁnd a lower bound on the generalized Hamming weights of algebraic geometry codes.
In this thesis order bound for any algebraic geometry codes as given in [2] is discussed.
Then the lower bounds on the generalized Hamming weights of one point algebraic
geometry codes and generalized Hamming weights of two point Hermitian codes are
discussed. Several mistakes in the literature are corrected.

Chapter 1
Algebraic Geometry Codes
In this chapter we present the basic notations of algebraic geometry codes. In order to
do it we need to understand the relationship between algebraic function ﬁelds and alge-
braic curves. We begin with a berief introduction to the coding theory. For the reader
who wants to get more familiar with coding theory we refer to [22],[23] and [24]. Then
the basic concepts of algebraic function ﬁelds are presented in the second section. For
the reader who wants to go in the depth of the theory of algebraic function ﬁelds and
their relation to codes we refer to [10]. In Section 3 of this chapter afﬁne and projective
variety are discussed. For the reader who wants to read more about algebraic curves
we refer to [21] and [25]. Finally in the last section of this chapter algebraic geometry
codes are deﬁned. Since the main focus of this thesis is discussing order bound and
lower bounds on the generalized Hamming weights, all presentations in the ﬁrst chapter
are very compact.
At the end of the ﬁrst section the organization of this thesis is given.
1.1
Introduction
Imagine you are calling to your friend to say something very important to him for ex-
ample the date, time and place of an important meeting. Unfortunately the sound is
not clear, because of bad weather or some technical problems. Your friend asks you to
repeat the date, time and place of meeting. You repeat the message as many times as he

wants. Your friend repeats the received message for you and you conﬁrm it. Now he is
sure that he has received the message correctly. It is a very simple example of a commu-
nication system. In this example the sender and receiver in the communication system
are people-people. There is no restriction. The sender and receiver in a communication
system can be people-device or device-device. For example a CD or DVD is read by a
CD-player is an example of device-device sender and receiver. As a more important ex-
ample we can mention the messages transmitted by a satellite. These messages, which
usually are transmitted in form of binary codes are surely inﬂuenced by the noise. The
receiver of the binary codes, which might be another machine in the earth surely does
not get the exact sent messages by the satellite. In a communication system a sender
and a receiver try to transmit information trough a channel, where noise causes errors
in the transfer of informations. The tools we use today to detect and correct errors are
called error-correcting codes.
The theoretical framework for error-correcting codes was ﬁrst presented by Claude
Shannon in the article "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" in 1948. Error
correcting-codes or simply codes are designed for the reliable transmission of informa-
tion across noisy channels. The basic idea in construction of codes is adding redun-
dancy to the message. Repeating a message is one example of adding redundancy. In
order to mathematize the idea we ﬁrst choose a set of symbols or alphabet. We let q
be a power of a prime number and Fq be the ﬁnite ﬁeld of order q. We use these q
symbols as the set of alphabet. We assume that each message can be represented by
using k symbols from Fq, where k is a positive integer. Therefore each message can
be considered as an element of the vector space Fk

Now the message m has been encoded into the codeword c. We note that the sender
sends the codeword c not the message m itself. Due to the noise in the channel it is
not always true that the receiver has also received c. Hence we might assume that the
receiver received r. The process of translating r into c is called decoding. We deﬁne
e = r −c as the error vector. If e is known the decoding process is very easy, since
c = r −e. The problem is that e is unknown. The natural solution to ﬁnd c is to go
trough all codewords in C and ﬁnd one closest to r. It will be the best candidate. If there
are more than one candidate, then the process of decoding becomes more difﬁcult. The
difﬁculty of the decoding process depends on the code the sender chooses to encode
the messages.
When we send some messages and the receiver is waiting to get it, indeed we wish him
to receive the messages as correct as possible. If you have many codes to encode your
messages, indeed you will choose the good one. By a "good code" we mean a code,
which helps us to detect and correct more errors. In other words we choose a code, in
which the decoding process is more efﬁcient. Therefore we need a criteria in order to
ﬁnd out the error correcting ability of a code. This criteria is rate and minimum distance
of a code. Rate of an [n, k]-linear code is k

Large minimum distance increases our error correcting capability. Therefore we are
interested in codes with large minimum distance. However calculating the minimum
distance might be very expensive, as it might demand going through all codewords.
Hence ﬁnding bounds on the minimum distance of a code is one of the important prob-

d ≤n −k + 1.
Codes achieving the equality in the Singleton bound are called maximum distance sepa-
rable codes or MDS codes. This class of codes is important, since they have the greatest
error correcting and detecting capabilities.

Algebraic geometry codes can be considered as the evaluation of certain functions at
the distinct subset of points on an algebraic curve. In 1982 Tsfasman, Vladut and
Zink showed that inﬁnite families of algebraic geometry codes exist that meet the Var-
shamov–Gilbert bound. Since then the theory of algebraic geometric codes has been
deeply investigated. The theory of algebraic-geometry codes requires fundamental re-
sults of algebraic geometry and algebraic function ﬁelds. Each of these theories is a
very large and complicated area of mathematics. We continue in the following sections
to introduce the basic notation of algebraic function ﬁelds and algebraic geometry at a
level sufﬁcient to understand algebraic geometry codes.
The Order bound is one of the best known lower bound for the minimum distance of
an algebraic geometric code. It is a generalization of the Feng-Rao bound. The order
bound was ﬁrst deﬁned for the dual of one-point algebraic geometry codes. A general-
ization of the order bound for any algebraic geometry codes is given by Beelen [2]. In
Chapter 2 the order bound given by Beelen is discussed.
The concept of generalized Hamming weights of linear codes was ﬁrst introduced by
Wei. Considering minimum distance as a certain property for one dimensional sub
codes, generalized Hamming weights are a generalization of the concept of mini-
mum distance in higher dimensions. In Chapter 3.1 the basic properties of generalized
weights are ﬁrst discussed. In Chapter 3.2 a lower bound for the generalized Hamming
weight of one-point algebraic geometry codes is given. We end Chapter 3 by giving a
lower bound for the generalized Hamming weights of two-point Hermitian codes.
Finally in Chapter 4 we summarize the conclusion of this thesis.

1.2
Algebraic Function ﬁeld
The theory of algebraic function ﬁelds is a broad area of mathematics. Going deep
into this theory is out of the scope of this thesis. In this section we present the basic
notation and results of algebraic function ﬁelds at a level sufﬁcient to understand alge-
braic geometry codes. The concepts of valuations, places, divisors and the genus of a
function ﬁeld are presented. This section is mainly based on "Algebraic function ﬁelds
and codes" written by Henning Stichtenoth. For the proofs refer to [10].
1.2.1
Places
Deﬁnition 1.1. Assume that K is a subﬁeld of the ﬁeld F. Then F is said to be an
extension ﬁeld of K denoted by F/K. The degree of F/K denoted by [F : K] is the
dimension of F as a vector space over K. We say that the extension is ﬁnite if [F : K]
is ﬁnite and inﬁnite otherwise.
Deﬁnition 1.2. Assume that F/K is a ﬁeld extension. An element α ∈F is called
algebraic over K, if there is a non zero polynomial f(x) ∈K[x] such that f(α) = 0,
where K[x] is the polynomial ring over K. If α is not algebraic over K, then α is called
transcendental over K. The ﬁeld extension F/K is called an algebraic extension, if ev-
ery element of F is algebraic over K. The set ˜K = {x ∈F | x is algebraic over K}
is called the ﬁeld of constants of F/K. K is called algebraically closed in F, if ˜K = K.
Deﬁnition 1.3. Assume that F/K is a ﬁeld extension and let α1, α2, ..., αr belong to
K. Then the smallest subﬁeld of F containing both K and all elements α1, ..., αr is
denoted by K(α1, ..., αr).
Theorem 1.1. Assume that F/K be a ﬁeld extension and let α1, α2, ..., αr belong to K.
Then
(1) The extension K(α1, ..., αr)/K is ﬁnite if and only if α1, α2, ..., αr are all algebraic
over K.
(2) An element α ∈F is algebraic over K if and only if [K(α) : K] < ∞.

contradiction and so c ≤2g.
If now for all pairs adding op to c −1 exactly one of them is a gap and the other is a
non gap, then we have c = 2g.

The concept of generalized Hamming weights was ﬁrst introduced by Wei in 1991 [3].
They are useful in several ﬁelds. For example today we know that they are useful in
cryptography and trellis coding. Wei ﬁrst used this concept to characterize the cryptog-

In Chapter 3.1 deﬁnition and the basic properties of generalized Hamming weights are
given. In Chapter 3.2 a lower bound on the generalized Hamming weights of one-point
algebraic geometry codes are discussed. This section is mainly based on the work of
Petra Heijnen and Ruud Pellikaan [9]. In Chapter 3.3 we discuss a lower bound on the
general Hamming weights of two-point Hermitian codes. This section is based on the
unpublished work of P. Beelen, O. Geil, R. Matsumoto and D. Ruano [1].
3.1
Introduction
Assume that C is an [n, k] linear code over Fq. The Hamming weight of a codeword
c from C is deﬁned to be the number of non zero symbols of c. We can look to this
concept from another point of view. For instance we can consider c as a subset of C
and try to apply the concept of Hamming weight to any subset of C. In this section we

Deﬁnition 3.1. Assume that D is a subset of Fn

supp(D) = {i | ther exist c = (c1, ..., cn) ∈D such that ci ̸= 0}.

Deﬁnition 3.2. Assume that C is an [n, k] linear code over Fq. For any 1 ≤r ≤k the

dr(C) = min{ |supp(D)| | D is an r-dimensional linear subcode of C}.
The weight hierarchy of C is deﬁned to be {d(C) : 1 ≤r ≤k}.
It is not difﬁcult to see that the 1’th generalized Hamming weight of C equals to the
minimum distance of C. In order to see it, it might be a good idea to apply the concept

d(C) = min{|supp(x)| | x ∈C , x ̸= 0}.

all one dimensional sub codes of C are distinct. We let S be a one dimensional sub
code of C. So S can be described by a basis consists of one element say s. Any
other codeword t in S is a multiple of s and we have supp(t) = supp(s) = supp(S).
Therefore to ﬁnd d(C) we could look at all one dimensional sub codes of C and pick that
one op with minimum supp. But it is exactly the deﬁnition of d1(C) the 1’th generalized
Hamming weight of C Therefore we have
d(C) = min{ |supp(D)| | D is an 1 dimensional linear subcode of C} = d1(C).

Theorem 3.1. Assume that C is a [n, k] linear code with k > 0. Then we have
1 ≤d1(C) < d2(C) < ... < dk(C) ≤n.
Proof. Since k > 0, it is clear that C has at least one non zero codeword and then
d(C) = d1(C) > 0. It is also clear that dr−1(C) ≤dr(C) for any 1 ≤r ≤k, since
any sub code of dimension r −1 is a subset of a sub code of dimension r. So we
will show that strict inequalities hold. Assume that D is a sub code of C such that
|supp(D)| = dr(C) and rank(D) = r. Assume that i ∈supp(D). We deﬁne Di =

dr−1(C) ≤supp(Di) ≤supp(D) −1 = dr(C) −1.

Corollary 3.1. (Generalized Singleton Bound): Assume that C be a [n, k] linear code
with k > 0. Then we have

Proof. By previous theorem we have dr(C) < dr+1(C) < ... < dk(C) ≤n. It follows
that dr(C) + (k −r) ≤dk(C) ≤n. Hence
dr(C) ≤n −k + r.
The result of the Lemma is in agreement with the singleton bound as d1(C) ≤n −k + 1.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
In this thesis we have investigated the generalized Hamming weights of algebraic ge-
ometry codes. This concept is a generalization of the minimum distance for higher
dimensional subspaces of a code.
We have ﬁrst discussed the order bound for any algebraic geometry codes and we have
seen that this bound is at least as good as the Goppa bound. Then we have discussed
a lower bound on the generalized Hamming weights of one point algebraic geometry
codes. We have seen that the technical ideas used to ﬁnd the lower bound on the gen-
eralized Hamming weights of one point algebraic geometry codes are similar to those
used to get the order bound for any algebraic geometry code. All these ideas have orig-
inated from the the celebrated Feng–Rao bound. The Feng-Rao bound provides a lower
bound for the minimum distance of a large family of codes. We have seen that to get
the order bound for a code, using a ﬁltration of subcodes of the code is the key point.
Finally we have seen that the idea of using a ﬁltration of subcodes of the code can be
used successfully to get a lower bound on the generalized Hamming weights of two
point Hermitian codes.
We do think that the generalized Hamming weight of two point Hermitian codes will
be exactly what the bound predicts, but that we indeed do not know at the moment. We
could begin to check it by going through some concrete examples. If we had time, we
would ﬁx some concrete two-point Hermitian codes with the proper choice of parame-
ters and work out the lower bound on the generalized Hamming weights of these codes.
This task is very time-consuming and even needs to write a computer program.
80
==PAGE:82==
The order bound for the minimum distance of two-point Hermitian codes given in
Chapter 2.3 agrees with the actual minimum distance of two-point Hermitian codes
found by Homma and Kim [26]. Since ﬁnding the actual value of the minimum dis-
tance of algebraic geometry codes is not known in general, we might think that the order
bound for an arbitrary algebraic geometry code will be agree with the actual minimum
distance of the code, but there are examples where the order bound does not give the
correct minimum distance!(See at [2] at the end of section 3.) So more improvement of
the method are expected. One possible improvement of the method is explained in the
following paragraph.
One of the ideas we used to get the order bound is generalization of Weierstrass semi-
group from one point to two points. We think that it could be possible to attack the
problem by generalizing the Weierstrass semigroup for more than two points to get
even better results.
To get the order bound or bounds on the generalized Hamming weights, we have cho-
sen a sequences of divisors and then analysed the underlying Weierstrass semigroups.
We might think that another way to attack the problem is working directly on the prop-
erties of Weierstrass semigroups in hope of ﬁning some useful results.
