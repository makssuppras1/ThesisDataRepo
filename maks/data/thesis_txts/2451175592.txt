II
Behavioural design has gained large acceptance within the last ten years and is now widely 
used by governments, organisations, and private companies. There exists comprehensive work 
describing behaviour and principles which can be used for influencing behaviour. However, there 
exists little information on how to link the specific behavioural problem to the most effective 
principles for changing behaviour.
Therefore, this thesis has examined the connection between different types of behavioural design 
problems and behavioural design solutions, providing behavioural designers and researchers 
with useful information of what types of behavioural solutions are likely to successfully change 
behaviour for specific types of behavioural problems.
To examine the connection between problem and solution, we found inspiration in the inventive 
theory of TRIZ and compared successful and unsuccessful solutions to different behavioural 
problems in 218 behavioural design case studies. Thus, we first had to develop a behavioural 
design framework which could be used for comparing problems and solutions of the cases in a 
generic way. The problems and solutions of the behavioural design framework were developed 
by revising existing literature. The cases were described via the behavioral design framework 
and then used for examining how the success rate of different solution principles changed when 
the type of problem changed.
Our results indicate that there is a connection between the type of behavioural problem, and 
which solution is most likely to change behaviour. The results indicate that it is not necessarily 
the same solution principle that works in all situations, and that the solution principle(s) best 
suited to solve a behavioural design problem likely depends on the behavioural design problem 
and the context.
Our development of a behavioural framework has created a foundation for describing behavioural 
design in general terms, making it possible to compare different types of behavioural problems. 
Our results furthermore, emphasises the need for taking the characteristics of a behavioural 
problem into consideration when choosing and designing a behavioural intervention. Our results 
can help designers by providing guidelines for designing, founded in other designers work and 
experience via behavioural design cases.

NOMENCLATURE
Behavioral changes: A general term for using behavioral design for changing behavior. 
(Example: Get people to walk more steps per day, Get people to sort their trash at home).
Behavioral design: ”Behavioural design is the design of interventions made within the scope of 
a designer with the purpose of changing behaviour in a certain way.” as defined in section 
2.1.2.
Behavioral designer: See description of designer.
Behavioral design problems: Collective description of a problem which occurs due to the way 
people behave. These can be described by problem parameters.
(The) Behavioral Design Framework: A term for the collection of final problem parameters, 
final solution principles, and level of success, that behavioural design problems and 
solutions are characterised by and the cases are coded by.
Cases: The case studies found for this project that describes interventions made for solving 
behavioural design problems. Several cases can come from the same case source.
Case database: The collection of cases found for this project, which fulfilled our criteria defined 
in section 4.1.1. The case database consists of 218 cases.
Case source: One source often describes the testing of several interventions. Each of these is 
added as a case, though they come from the same source. 
Coding: The process of defining the behavioural design problems and solutions, level of success, 
and other general information of each case in the case database by using the Behavioural 
Design Framework.
Current behavior: How subjects are currently behaving. Current behaviour is the behaviour 
which the designer wants to change.
Descriptive (problem) parameters: The parameters found in the theories of behavior and 
behavior change, and described as influencing behavior. The descriptive parameters 
are possible to describe from the information provided in the cases, but not possible to 
operationalize.
Designer: Anyone working with behavioral design or likewise, but does not have to be either 
from a company or have a background within behavioral design or similar.
Filled in framework: The behavioral framework coded with the cases from the case database.
Intervention: A behavioural design solution for changing behavior which consists of one or 
more behavioral solution principles.

Example 1: Nomenclature exemplified
To illustrate the meaning of the terms described in the nomenclature, we here provide an 
example of a behavioural case, where many of the terms are used.
A Norwegian hotel chain experienced that they often had to throw out a lot of food because 
the hotel guest took more food from the buffet than they could actually eat. Thus they hired a 
behavioural designer from a behavioural design consultancy to try and change the behaviour of 
the hotel guests to reduce the food waste.
The task was to get the hotel guests (the subjects) to stop taking too much food (the current 
behaviour) on their plate and instead visit the buffet several times (the target behaviour) if the 
guests did not feel full after having finished their first plate of food. 
The behavioural designer examined the problem space: she observed the hotel guest’s behaviour 
around the buffet and interviewed several of them to understand why they behaved as they did. 
After having examined the problem space, she moved to the solution space: what could be 
done to change the behaviour of the subject. The designer brainstormed and came up with two 
different ideas for interventions that might work for changing behaviour:
1. 
Introduce smaller plates
2. 
Set up a sign in the buffet which urged people not to take so much food, but instead go 
to the buffet twice. The message read: “20% of our guest waste food because they have taken 
too much on their plate. We expect you not to be one of them.” and add a sign on the tables with 
instruction on how to get a new plate if you needed more food.
The first intervention uses the behavioural solution principle of changing the default for the 
plates, thereby forcing the subject to take less food at the time.
The second intervention uses three solution principles: norms (20%..), imposing expectations 
(we expect you..) on the subject, and provide guidance (how to get a new plate).
All of the above descriptions is an example of one case, testing two interventions using one and 
three solution principles, respectively.

CHAPTER 1 
INTRODUCTION
Behavioural design i.e. design that makes the user behave in an optimal way of their own "free" 
will, by utilizing their bounded rationality (Simon, 1990), has gained large acceptance within 
the last 10 years (Jeppesen, 2019), and is now widely used by governments, organisations, and 
private companies. Simultaneously the number of publications presenting suggestions of how to 
change behaviour by implement behavioural design interventions has become comprehensive 
with publications such as Lockton (2013), Tromp, Hekkert, & Verbeek (2011) and Dolan et al. 
(2012).
 
However, some people remain critical towards the extent to which behavioural design is applied 
today. Designer and researcher Kristina Niedderer for instance, argues that there is currently 
too little documentation of the effects on behaviour change, and thus we do not know why or 
how nudges and other behavioural designs influence behaviour (Niedderer, Clune, & Ludden, 
2018). Niedderer proclaims that behavioural designers have focused on developing selection 
strategies for interventions, rather than frameworks for evaluating how well the strategies work 
(Niedderer et al., 2018).
 
Assistant Professor of Economics at the University of Aarhus Mette Trier Damgaard is also critical 
when it comes to the extensive use of nudging. She claims that nudging is used indiscriminately, 
and often in situations where other solutions might be preferential (Jeppesen, 2019).
 
Even though the study of behaviour has existed for many years, the understanding of when a 
behavioural design intervention is likely to work successfully is limited. There is no certainty 
that a behavioural solution principle, which works in one situation, will also work in another. 
Thus, even if behavioural designers thoroughly examine a problem and its causes, they are 
still left to develop new behavioural interventions by potentially costly trial and error, as the 
certainty of the effect of a specific behavioural intervention is low. Thus, there is plenty of access 
to knowledge of what behaviour is, and how to influence it, but there is little information on how 
to link the behaviour of the subject (the person performing the unwanted behaviour) to the best 
intervention.

INTRODUCTION
For example, if behavioural designers come across the following behavioural design problem: 
How to make the employees (subjects) in an office turn off the light (the target behaviour) at 
the end of the working day? How should the designers solve this behavioural design problem? 
Should they develop an intervention by hanging signs in the office space telling the subjects that 
nine out of ten people remember to turn off the light? Alternatively, should they send an email 
at the end of the day reminding them to turn the light off? Or both? They only know that this 
type of solutions has previously worked for other behavioural designers, but they do not know 
whether they are likely to work for this specific problem before they have tried. Moreover, there 
may be completely different types of solutions for this kind of problems, which they may not 
have thought of.
 
Although there is a research gap within behavioural design about the coupling between problems 
and solutions, examples of such frameworks exist in other fields. The famous engineer and 
inventor Altschuller managed (with the help of others) to categorize and connect inventive 
mechanical problems to the solutions which are most likely to work. He did so by creating a 
framework with 39 generic problem parameters defining mechanical problems and 40 generic 
solution principles for solving them. This framework is now known as the contradiction matrix 
(Gadd, 2011). Altschuller connected the inventive problems to the solution principles by studying 
approximately 40.000 patents. The contradiction matrix is only one part of the inventive theory 
of TRIZ founded by Altschuller (Gadd, 2011). Using the contradiction matrix and other tools of 
TRIZ, the engineers of today can compare their technical design problems with problems of the 
past and find inspiration for a solution principle, which has previously worked (Gadd, 2011).
Is it possible to implement a similar approach within behavioural design where designers 
from the previous examples can compare their behavioural problems to problems that other 
behavioural designers have previously tried to solve? This is the question, that has laid the 
foundation for our curiosity, and which has led to the present thesis.
1.1 WHAT IS THE AIM OF THE PROJECT
We aim at examining the link between behavioural design problems and behavioural design 
solutions by comparing behavioural cases, which have both succeeded and failed. Thereby we 
aim at providing useful information of what types of behavioural solutions are likely to work for 
what types of behavioural problems to behavioural designers.
1.2 RESEARCH QUESTION
The overall aim of our project can be reduced to the following research question:
How are behavioural design problems and behavioural solution principles connected in a 
successful way?
==PAGE:17==

1.2.1 RESEARCH OBJECTIVES
To be able to answer the research question, we have formulated the following research objectives:
• 
Find out which parameters are important in defining and characterising behavioural design 
problems
• 
Find out which behavioural design principles for solving behavioural problems currently 
exists
• 
Find out how to determine success of behavioural design solutions
• 
Find as many behavioural cases as possible and classify them by applying the identified 
problem parameters, solution principles, and success rates.
• 
Analyse the correlation between the problem parameters, the solutions principles and the 
success/failure of the identified behavioural cases, to determine what solution principles 
are most likely to work for what types of problem parameters
• 
Give recommendations of which solution principles are most likely to work for what 
problems as characterised by the problem parameters.
• 
Give recommendations of which solution principles are most likely to work in combination, 
and which are most likely to work alone.
1.3 STRUCTURE OF THE REPORT
In the first chapter, we have introduced what we aim at doing through this project: building 
a behavioural design framework which can be used for answering the question: how are 
behavioural design problems and solutions connected successfully? 
To be able to create the framework, we first need to determine how we will frame the project, 
and what is to be included in the project. This is presented in the second chapter, by defining 
behavioural design, explaining the inventive theory of TRIZ and explaining what a behavioural 
design problem is. 
In chapter two, it is determined that to build the behavioural design framework, we need to 
examine how to describe behavioural problems and behavioural solutions. And for comparing 
these, we need behavioural cases and a way to determine whether the cases are successful. This 
is then done in chapter three by conducting a literature review where we examine what the 
literature defines as parameters for describing behaviour, principles for describing solutions 
and a way to determine the success of a behavioural intervention. 
Having done this, the only thing missing is the cases. The cases and how we have found them are 
introduced in chapter four. We furthermore distill the information from chapter three as some 
of the information found in the literature does not fit within our frame of behavioural design, 
or is not applicable for comparing cases. The distillation of information leads us to our final 
behavioural design framework. 
Before we code the cases, we check that there were agreement of how the different elements of 
the framework are defined. Having ensured this, the behavioural design framework is then used 
for coding the cases into a complete dataset of comparable problems, solutions, and success. An 
overview of the filled out behavioural design framework (the dataset) is presented in chapter 
five. In chapter six, we present the result to the initial question, of how problems and solutions 
are connected. In chapter seven, we discuss what the result shows and how the result can be 
used. Chapter eight concludes the whole project.

2.1 WHAT IS BEHAVIOURAL DESIGN
Since behavioural design is a relatively new field, there has not been established a general 
agreement of what the definition of behavioural design is. In this section, we discuss how 
literature defines behavioural design, and what our definition of behavioural design will be. This 
is done to provide the reader with a clear impression of what has been considered relevant for 
our project regarding behavioural design, and thus, whom our project is relevant for.
2.1.1 BEHAVIOURAL DESIGN IN LITERATURE
Behavioural design has roots in the field of behavioural economics and have been able to gather 
insights from multiple fields such as psychology, economy, and brain science to better understand 
behaviour (Datta & Mullainathan, 2014). Later, other branches of changing behaviour occurred 
such as nudging introduced by Thaler and Sunstein (2009) defined as: “any aspect of the choice 
architecture that alters people’s behaviour in a predictable way without forbidding any options 
or significantly changing their economic incentives” (Thaler & Sunstein, 2009, p. 6). This is also 
explained as subtle pushes towards a better choice (Thaler & Sunstein, 2009), which means that 
nudges are meant to be discrete and almost unnoticeable. This is supported by (Cash, Hartlev, 
& Durazo, 2016) defining behavioural design as “Designing for antecedent behaviour change

Another broader definition which does not include only implicit interventions is found in 
‘Design with Intent’ as Lockton defines this branch of behavioural design as ”design intended 
to influence or result in certain user behaviour” (Lockton, 2013, p. 3).
2.1.2 OUR DEFINITION OF BEHAVIOURAL DESIGN
From the literature discussed in the previous section, we extracted the premises and key 
elements of behavioural design that fitted our understanding of the term the most. We believe 
that behavioural design does not need to be implicit, as making the user aware of the need for or 
intent of changing their behaviour (like reminders to walk via a fitness watch) can be a tool of 
intervening for changing behaviour. We have defined behavioural design in the following way:
“Behavioural design is the design of interventions made within the scope of a designer 
with the purpose of changing behaviour in a certain way.”
 
The phrase within the scope of a designer will now be further clarified. By this, we mean that 
all the type of behavioural changes a designer will be responsible for conducting is within this 
definition. To clarify:
The intervention needs to be a process, a system, or a product. This means that no politics, 
legislation, subsidies, and other societal structural changes are behavioural design.
• 
No architecture or urban planning designs, but only add-ons to the existing 
environmental context.
• 
Therapeutic solutions are not behavioural design as it is not in the scope of a designer. 
If an intervention is tested, which both includes behavioural design and therapeutic 
help, it is not possible to assess whether it is therapy or the behavioural design that are 
facilitating a change in behaviour.
 
To further clarify when an intervention is with the purpose of changing behaviour, the following 
bullets have been added:
• 
The work of changing behaviour should not be confused with improving usability or 
user experience. User experience is not actually changing the behaviour but rather the 
process of increasing the subject’s satisfaction.
• 
Neither do we believe that forcing a behaviour onto the user by making the target 
behaviour, the only possible option is behavioural design.
• 
The purpose of changing behaviour is not buying behaviour, i.e., increasing sales of a 
consumer product by designing products or services that make users buy a consumer 
product. However, one condition of buying behaviour is accepted as changing behaviour. 
This is if it is changing from buying something to buying another similar (but somehow 
better version), like an eco-washing machine instead of a regular one or organic food 
instead of conventional, etc. And then, the buying of one thing is instead of another that 
the user initially decided to buy.
 
This definition along with the clarification is what will be behavioural design, and what the 
following work is based on when referring to behavioural design.

2.2 TRIZ AND BEHAVIOURAL DESIGN 
In this section, we will give a general introduction to the theory of inventive problem solving, 
known as TRIZ (Gadd, 2011). This is done with the purpose of providing the user with an 
understanding of how TRIZ works how TRIZ was developed, and thus, how a similar approach 
can be adapted in behavioural design.
2.2.1 INTRODUCTION TO TRIZ
TRIZ (in Russian: Teoriya Resheniya Izobretatelskikh Zadatch) is a theory which was developed by 
the Russian engineer Genrikh Saulowitsch Altshuller with the purpose of helping engineers and 
other inventors solve difficult mechanical problems Moehrle, (2005). An inventor can be viewed 
as someone designing, in the case with TRIZ, mechanical solutions. The basic idea of TRIZ is to 
give the inventor easy access to relevant experience and knowledge of former inventors when 
he/she is trying to solve new problems (Moehrle, 2005). 
The inventor can do so, by following a four-step process shown in Figure F2-1 known as the 
prism of TRIZ. The four steps have been explained in the following way by (Moehrle, 2005):
1. 
Analyse the specific problem in detail to 
find the core of the problem
2. Match the specific problem to an abstract 
problem 
3. Find an abstract solution to the abstract 
problem
4. Use the abstract solution as inspiration 
for developing a specific solution to the 
initial specific problem.
A significant part of using the prism for 
solving problems is the contradiction matrix 
together with the 40 inventive principles. The contradiction matrix and the 40 inventive 
principles are tools which help the inventor conduct the above step 2 and 3 in the prism of TRIZ, 
See Figure F2-1.  
According to Alschuller technical problems are found everywhere, and “the foundation of these 
problems are always the removal of contradictions” (Altshuller, 2008, p. 128). By finding the 
contradiction, you can easily explain your problem in general terms via the contradiction matrix, 
and from here, get recommendations for the inventive solutions which are most likely to solve 
your problem. The easiest way to describe (and understand) this, is by an example:
Example 2: Problems and contradictions
If we want our car to be able to drive faster, we could give the car a bigger motor. But by adding 
a bigger motor, we are unintentionally making the car heavier, thus making it more difficult to 
move, and as a result, slower. This is the contradiction in making the problem difficult to solve.
If we then try to describe this contradiction in a generic way we might say, that we want an 
object to move faster, but to do so, we are unintentionally making it heavier. So, what we want 
to achieve is to make the object faster, without adding to the weight. Now we have arrived at the 
abstraction level at which we can use the contradiction matrix.

same result.
Or if we were to use principle 13; the Other Way Around one might think about moving weight 
from the car, instead of adding a better motor to make it drive faster.
The 48 features of the contradiction matrix along with the 40 inventive principles, was developed 
by going through 40.000 patents and categorizing both the problem and the solutions (Gadd, 
2011). The solutions were furthermore categorized by which of the contradictions they have 
successfully helped to solve, meaning that you only get suggestions for solutions which have 
worked in similar problems.

2.2.2 TRIZ IN BEHAVIOURAL DESIGN
We believe that a similar process to the prism of TRIZ can be implemented in behavioural 
design, by having the behavioural designer follow the same four steps as shown in Figure F2-3: 
the prism of TRIZ. We do not believe that it is possible to describe behavioural design problems 
by contradictions. This is due to the fact that we believe that behavioural design problems are 
always built on solving the same basic contradiction: how to change the behaviour of the subject 
without forcing them. Thus, we need to find another way of describing behavioural problems in 
a generic way. 
The process of developing both a framework of comparing generic problems and thereby 
recommended a generic solution and building up a database of previously conducted successful 
instances (like the 40.000 patents) can inspire our work of creating a similar version of the TRIZ 
contradiction matrix but within behavioural design.
To develop a similar process, where a designer can follow the prism of TRIZ in a behavioural 
design context, we will have to:
1. 
Figure out how to describe specific behavioural problems in general terms
2. 
Figure out how to describe specific behavioural solutions in general terms
3. 
Figure out, how 1. and 2. are connected by collecting and comparing existing examples of 
successful behavioural design through a case study.
With these steps defined, we will investigate how to frame the behavioural problems.
2.3 WHAT IS A DESIGN PROBLEM
The purpose of this section is to define what a design problem is by looking at literature, and 
hereby figure out how to describe a generic behavioural design problem.
As already mentioned, behavioural design is a relatively new field of study, and thus, there is 
no literature describing what constitutes a behavioural design problem. Instead, we will look 
at literature within design, to use the existing definitions of design problems in a behavioural 
context. This has been done by looking into the definitions of design problems in two prominent 
and highly relevant theories.
The first one is Nigel Cross, which provides tools for designing successful products in his 
book Engineering Design Methods (Cross, 2000). Cross conducts an analysis of several design 
problems and concludes the following: ”[...] design problems have in common is that they set a 
goal, some constraints, within which the goal must be achieved and some criteria by which a successful 
solution might be recognized.” (Cross, 2000, p. 13). 
The goal is the target that the designer wants to achieve by fulfilling the criteria for a successful 
solution. The problem must be solved within the constraints of the context in which the problem 
occurs. Cross then primarily focuses on the goal of the change and within what constraints the 
possibilities of that change are.

The second theory describing a design problem is Dorst (2015), which propose a frame consisting 
of nine elements which can explain the problem, and how to arrive at a solution (Dorst, 2015). 
The first four elements in the frame explaining the problem are:
- Archaeology: Understand why the problem has occurred, and what has formerly been done to 
try and solve it
- Paradox: Find out why the problem is difficult to solve - what paradox makes it a problem 
- Context: Who are the stakeholders
- Field: What happens around the problem, and during the problem, what is the situation.
Archaeology can be described as the past, where the context and field relate to the current. The 
paradox can be described as the essence of the problem and can be used to derive where the

Various theories incorporate different aspects of emotions in their theory of what determines 
behaviour as can be seen in Table T3-2. Though emotions are in all theories as an internal

Theory of planned behaviour, theory of reasoned action, and theory of interpersonal behaviour 
includes emotions as what the subject feels towards the behaviour in question - what is their 
attitude (Ajzen, 1991; Fishbein & Ajzen, 2010) or their affect (Darnton, 2008) towards the 
behaviour. All three theories claim that how you perceive a behaviour can be decisive for whether 
you will act out the behaviour.
On the other hand, Kruglanski et al. (2015) criticises attitude, as he claims it will not influence 
behaviour, as he instead claims that it is ‘want’ or ‘desire’ that drives behaviour and that you 
can want something without having a positive attitude towards it (e.g. wanting to flee a country 
because of war, without liking it (Kruglanski et al., 2015)).
In a more general understanding of the influence of emotions, we have the theory of self-
efficacy (Bandura, 1978) which states that the general emotional state of mind of the subject 
influences the willingness to behave. 
Thus, as we see in Table T3-2 emotions is mentioned in different theories, though the 
understanding of what way emotions influences behaviour is understood differently across the 
theories.

3.1.3 MOTIVATION
Along with physical context and capability, motivation is the most commonly mentioned 
parameter of behaviour in the theories which have been reviewed. As we can see in Table T3-4, 
the term used to describe motivation is also quite similar throughout the theories. Motivation is 
broadly captured by the COM-B theory, distinguish between two different types of motivation: 
reflective (self-reflective intent) and automatic motivation (wants and needs) (Michie, S Atkins, 
L West, 2014).
The reflective motivational factor – intent – is the same term as used by theory of reasoned action, 
theory of planned behaviour and theory of interpersonal behaviour to describe motivation. For 
theory of reasoned action, theory of planned behaviour and theory of interpersonal behaviour 
they further divide intent into three sub-parameters: attitude, perceived norms and behavioural 
control (Ajzen, 1991; Fishbein & Ajzen, 2010), and attitude, affect and social factors (Darnton, 
2008), while COM-B does not divide intent any further.
The other part of COM-B’s definition of motivation includes automatic motivational factor 
which has similarities with the driving forces of activity theory and goal theory, as (Esnaashari 
et al., 2015) defines that motivation is the energetic component of activity and arises when a 
connection is made between a need and an object. (Kruglanski et al., 2015) state that wants and 
attainability is what drives behaviour but not as motivation but rather a goal. 
In this way (Michie, S Atkins, L West, 2014) combine several other theories’ understanding of 
motivation – and goals - into one overall motivational parameter.
(Fogg, 2009a) has created his own take on motivation and instead says that motivation is due to 
three different parameters: Social acceptance/rejection, Pleasure/pain, and hope/fear.
A somewhat different approach to motivation is the transtheoretical model. According to Velicer, 
Prochaska, Fava (1998), the subject builds up motivation to change behaviour, by first creating 
an intent to do the behaviour and later acting. Thus, motivation is seen more like stepwise 
progress towards performing a behaviour, than something that is either there or not.

3.1.4 FAMILIARITY
The familiarity of a behaviour is not a central theme throughout the theories as it is only 
mentioned in three theories (see Table T3-5), but we have included it in this review as it does 
seem to be of importance when describing behavioural problems, as (Simon, 1990) says; “When 
intelligence explores unfamiliar domains, it falls back on “weak methods”, which are in depended 
on domain knowledge. People satisfice – look for good-enough solutions – instead of hopelessly 
searching for the best.” (Simon, 1990, p. 17), meaning, that people will react differently when 
presented with the unfamiliar.
Miltenberger too has a whole chapter on how to teach unfamiliar behaviour to an individual 
(Miltenberger, 2014) and in the behavioural grid, there is differentiated between familiar and 
unfamiliar behaviour (Fogg, 2009b).
3.1.5 CAPABILITY
The parameter of capability relates to whether you can perform a behaviour. This is quite an 
important parameter based on the times it is mentioned by the various theories (Table T3-6).
Most theories primarily talk about capabilities as a subject’s cognitive ability to process and 
understand a behaviour which includes (Theory of reasoned action (Fishbein & Ajzen, 2010), 
Theory of planned behaviour (Ajzen, 1991), theory of bounded rationality (Simon, 1990), theory 
of self-efficacy (Bandura, 1978)). Though a few include other factors as well, such as physical 
capabilities and resources (Fogg’s behavioural model (Fogg, 2009a)
 and COM-B model (Michie, S Atkins, L West, 2014). Chatterton et Wilson come across capability 
as one way of characterising psychology as a part of what influences behaviour (Chatterton & 
Wilson, 2014).
Finally, the system theory has divided the action into two modes of thinking that are present 
when acting or behaving; reasoning and intuition (Kahneman, 2003). The first being deliberate 
and effortful, while the latter is done without effort and often comes spontaneously to mind.

3.1.8 PHYSICAL CONTEXT
Physical context is related to the environment in which the behaviour takes place; the theories 
mentioned in table PC agree that the environment has a role to play in shaping behaviour: ”to 
explain the behaviour of a system of bounded rationality we must describe the systems processes and 
also the environments to which it is adapting.” (Simon, 1990, p. 17).
But like Simon’s theory of bounded rationality, most theories merely state that the physical 
context plays a role, without defining in what way it influences or define behaviour (Theory of 
reasoned action (Fishbein & Ajzen, 2010) Elements of behaviour (Fishbein & Ajzen, 2010) and 
theory of interpersonal behaviour (Darnton, 2008)).
COM-B has a more practical approach to the facilitating conditions as Michie, S Atkins, L West 
(2014) includes the physical context as a question of whether the physical context is making the 
behaviour possible to perform or not. 
Other theories such as the 4D behavioural framework aim at giving a more nuanced way of 
analysing the context by presenting technological, infrastructure/environmental and social/
institution as influencers of behaviour (Chatterton & Wilson, 2014). Lockton describes an 
element in the context as being the user’s interaction with a system (Lockton et al., 2010).
The theory has mixed categories related to the physical context, and categories related to the 
subject, making it somewhat unstructured.
A more structured approach which also includes categories is found within activity theory. Here, 
the analyses of the physical context are divided into the tools used for performing the activity, 
and the object which is transformed or influenced through the activity (Bedny & Karwowski, 
2004; Esnaashari et al., 2015).

3.1.10 
TIME
The term time is covering a diverse selection of definitions in the various theories, as can be 
seen in Table T3-11, which all have in common, that they are all factoring in time in one form 
or another as a parameter for determining behaviour. Elements of behaviour work with time in 
intervals, which defines when behaviour is happening, or at least when you are observing the 
behaviour happening (Fishbein & Ajzen, 2010). Triandis’ theory of interpersonal behaviour has 
the frequency of the behaviour included (Darnton, 2008) to determine whether the behaviour 
is a habit. 
Another way of defining time is found in the 4D behavioural framework (Chatterton & Wilson, 
2014) and the behavioural grid (Fogg, 2009b). The 4D behavioural framework describes the 
durability as a parameter which characterizes a behaviour (Chatterton & Wilson, 2014) based 
on the interval in which it happens; one-off, repeated, dependent, enduring, and norm-setting 
(Chatterton & Wilson, 2014).
The behavioural grid instead categorises the target behaviour by seven categories (Fogg, 
2009b) which are quite like those of Chatterton & Wilson; one time, one time leading to 
ongoing obligations, a period of time, predictable schedule, on cue, at will, always. These seven 
categories later became three categories in the behavioural wizard (Fogg & Hreha, 2010); one 
time, duration, and permanent change. Both theories have strengths and weaknesses in being 
used for characterizing behaviour.

was only 5 kg) (Bedny & Harris, 2005; Esnaashari et al., 2015).

he defines consequences as the environmental events that follow a behaviour (for example 
being punished or getting praise), though opposite to activity theory, consequences are actual 
events which can be objectively observed and described, wherein activity theory the result can 
be spiritual as well. All theories mentioning outcome, have been shown in Table T3-13.

3.2 PRINCIPLES FOR BEHAVIOUR CHANGE
The purpose of this section is to review theories which all present a collection of solution 
principles with the purpose of getting an understanding of what principles currently exists 
for changing behaviour. By principles is meant a method or way of influencing and changing 
behaviour. An example of solution principles which are proven to influence behaviour could be 
norms, reminders, salience, etc.
We will not mention which principles were found in the eight theories as the total number of 
principles across all theories are 289. But all the principles which were included at the end of 
the process can be viewed in appendix A3-1 Instead we will shortly describe the purpose for 
which each of the theories was developed. Below is given an overview of the selected literature 
containing solution principles, and by what purpose they have been written.
To start with a high-level description of solution principle Michie et al. (2014)’s Behaviour 
Change Wheel (BCW) is meant to be a practical tool relevant for policymakers and other shapers 
of the society (Michie et al., 2014). The BCW is based on 19 theories made practical by high-level 
descriptions of intervention options, including applied examples. 
Another approach also introducing not only behavioural design principles but a thorough guide 
on how to detect and accommodate the behavioural change is Behavioural Mapping (Datta & 
Mullainathan, 2014). The theory describes the process of developing behavioural design for 
societal change, pointing out the importance for defining the bottleneck which is blocking for 
the desired behaviour and hereby use the appropriate behavioural design principles to overcome 
those.
Being used by policymakers as well, MINDSPACE (Dolan et al., 2012) provides nine overall 
strategies on how to develop behavioural design solutions based on a synthesis of research 
within the field (Dolan et al., 2012). MINDSPACE is a mnemonic name which refers to the nine 
strategies for influencing behaviour: Message, Incentives, Norms, Defaults, Salience, Priming, 
Affect, Commitment, and Ego. These strategies consist of a mix of concrete design principles, 
tips when designing for subjects, and general advice, all of which mostly focuses on changing 
the context in which the undesired behaviour occurs. 
Moving from societal principles to product-related and practical solution principles, we have 
Design with Intent (DwI) created by Dan Lockton (2013) and described in his Ph.D. Design with 
Intent describes a set of inspiration cards with concrete suggestions for principles to be used, 
and examples are illustrated with pictures (Lockton, 2013). Another theory focusing on product-
related solution principles, but with a specific focus on the social responsibility of the designer 
is Tromp et al.’s Design for Socially Responsible Behaviour (Tromp et al., 2011) though Tromp 
does interject that the list is not comprehensive, but the presented principles should be viewed 
as examples of design solutions for behaviour change.
Design for Sustainable Behaviour Strategies (Bhamra, Lilley, & Tang, 2011) is targeting designers 
with sustainable use in mind. Seven strategies for sustainable use of products are presented 
and ranges on a scale of power in decision making from subject to product. For a broader use 
than sustainability-related design strategies, the prefix eco- are removed from all principles in 
appendix A3-1.
In the theory of Persuasive System Design (PSD), the focus is still within the designers work 
but delimited by design of computerized software or information systems (Oinas-Kukkonen 
& Harjumaa, 2009). PSD describes 28 design principles of persuasive system content and 
functionality using examples of software requirements and implementations to ease the 
understanding.

3.3 SUCCESS AND QUALITY OF EVIDENCE
This section will examine how success and levels of success have been defined in the literature 
to determine when there is a successful link between the behavioural problem and solutions. 
Therefore, this section will investigate the definition of success in theory and how it is 
practically used to determine how well an intervention is functioning. Furthermore, the quality 
of evidence will be discussed by assessing the different methods of conducting case studies and 
their reliability.
3.3.1 SUCCESS IN LITERATURE
We have from a theoretical point of view searched for definitions of success to be able to assess 
and determine the level of success in case studies of behavioural design. Looking through 
theoretical mentions of how to define whether a behavioural intervention is successful or not, it 
turns out, that success is very troublesome to define, and though it is easy to find mentioning of 
successful or unsuccessful behavioural interventions in most studies and theories, very few of 
the articles mentions how this is defined. However, we were able to detect two articles describing 
their way of categorizing success rates.
 
When Cash et al., (2016) assessed a behavioural design company for the success of their cases, 
they asked for both the successfulness for the behavioural design company regarding time, 
budget, etc. and for the feedback from the client (Cash et al., 2016). These were examined by given 
a score from 1-5, whereas a collective score of four was perceived as successful. This success 
criterion was provided by subjective feedback from employees on qualitative data. However, in 
the study from Hamari, Koivisto, & Pakkanen (2014) examining 95 case studies of persuasive 
technology, they categorized the case by qualitative, quantitative and mixed results and by 
results being positive, partially positive and negative or other (Hamari et al., 2014). A result is 
considered positive if all studied elements are measured positive, meaning that a change has 
been detected. If only some of the studied elements are measured positive, the result is partially 
positive, and if there was no or a negative effect on the studied elements, the result was defined 
as negative or other.
This concludes our literature review of success, which is limited due to the amount of literature 
defining success. These definitions and scales of success will be considered in the method 
chapter.

be too low level – e.g. Emotional Social Support and Practical Social Support from the Behavioural 
Design Wheel which was merged into one principle (Social Support). 
4. Clustering
After we had removed all the principles which did not live up to the requirements or were 
doubles, we were still left with 136 principles. As this is quite an overwhelming number of 
principles to be presented with for any designer, we decided to cluster principles with similar 
characteristics together. 
While creating the clusters, we assigned a heading and a definition for the cluster. For each 
definition, it was tested that the sub-principles within the category fitted only that definition 
and none of the others. The heading for each cluster was written imperative, inciting, and 
inspire the designer to use the principle, e.g., Use Triggers, or Provide Reward. 
In the end, we had 25 clusters, which were given a title and a definition and became our final 25 
solution principles. These 25 solution principles enable us to compare the effect of a solution 
principle across cases. The final list of solution principles and their definitions will be presented 
in section 4.3.2, page 45.

CURRENT BEHAVIOUR
The behaviour that is currently performed and aimed to change by the designer
SUBJECT
Community, Segment or Population
The subject describes whom the designer is trying to target by the behavioural intervention. 
The subject categories define what the individual subjects have in common as a group and 
can also indicate the size of the subject group.
 
COMMUNITY
If the subject is part of a common club, workplace or otherwise have a common affiliation 
or a common sense of belonging, they are said to be a community. This is likely to be a part 
of your identity. E.g., living in the same apartment complex or small town, being part of the 
same football club, working together, etc. It is indeed the relation to the community that 
defines the subject being in a community.
 
SEGMENT
If the subject group is being targeted based on what characteristics they have in common, 
but otherwise have no relation to each other, then the subject group is defined as a 
segment. E.g., people buying Nike shoes, smokers, flight passengers, etc.
 
POPULATION
If the target group is everybody/unspecified, it is defined as population. If the solution is 
tested with a specific type of target group but is meant for everybody, it is not population, 
as we only know that it works within the group in which it is tested.

The activity level describes the level of the behaviour that the designer aims to change. 
Activity is the highest level and often requires the most effort for the subjects to change. 
Operation is the lowest level and is only performed subconsciously. These parameter 
options should be seen as a hierarchy of activities, whereas activities consist of tasks, 
tasks consist of actions and actions consist of operations. Therefore, the activity level can 
be said to describe the ‘size’ of the Behaviour.
ACTIVITY
An activity is not something that is performed in a specific situation but can be done in 
many ways and often all the time. E.g., living healthy is an Activity. It can be done in many 
ways and in different situations, whereas the task of eating a healthy meal is one specific 
situation.
TASK
This is a concrete way of achieving something. A task can be looked at as an isolated 
incidence, which does not influence other tasks. A task being isolated means that it makes 
sense to perform only the task and not in relation to other behaviours. E.g., the task of 
eating a healthy meal, taking out the trash, watering plants, etc.
 
ACTION
Actions, on the other hand, do not have a purpose on its own, and it needs to be part of a 
sequence to make sense, E.g., choosing to print double-sided (an action) does not make 
sense unless you are planning on printing the document.

Frequency refers to how often the target behaviour is expected to take place.
ONE-OFF
If the target behaviour should occur once or rarely enough for consequences of the action 
not to make a difference. For example, disposing of a bicycle, writing your will.
 
RARELY
The behaviour rarely takes place, but often enough for you have a memory of how to 
perform it without feeling like you are doing it for the first time. If it is likely to happen at 
maximum every 2nd month, it can be said to happen rarely. For example, going on vacation, 
visiting someone at the hospital. 
 
REPEATED
If it happens often, but without being a habit or part of the daily routine, it can be said to 
happen repeatedly. E.g., Going to a restaurant, buying new clothes, paying the electricity bill, 
etc.
 
DAILY
If the behaviour happens daily or almost daily, e.g., from Monday to Friday or several times

4.3.2 FINAL SOLUTION PRINCIPLES
Below are the 25 final solution principles, including their definitions. The solution principles 
should be used for characterising the solutions used in the interventions to be able to compare 
solutions across cases. The solution principles have each been assigned a number which is 
consistent throughout the report. This should be a help for the reader to locate a specific 
principle easily.
In Figure F4-5 (page 45- 47), each of the solution principles and their definitions is listed. 
The solution principles along with their sub-principles (the original principles which were 
clustered together) are also displayed in appendix A4-3. Here, each sub-principle is defined.  In 
Figure F4-5 the remaining sub-principles from the theories explained in the literature review 
(section 3.2, page 25) is listed.
1. GAMIFY
The target Behaviour should be fun and challenging to perform, which will be the main reason 
for the subject to perform it. Gamify is reliant on our natural drive to compete against ourselves 
and others. A motivational factor can be comparison against oneself or others. 
2. PRACTICE BEHAVIOUR
Subjects try or practise the target Behaviour either physically or mentally to understand how 
to do a Behaviour, to ease the point of entry and overcome obstacles for performing the target 
Behaviour. This can boost their confidence in their ability to perform the Behaviour.
3. MAKE IT SALIENT
People respond to what grabs their attention by being noticeable and standing out in the 
context. Salience can be used when creating passive, static interventions that are not related 
to timing. 
4. CONSIDER MESSENGER
Who or what presents the incentives has a great impact on the subject’s perception of the 
intervention and how likely the subject is to perform the target Behaviour promoted by the 
messenger.
5. USE TRIGGERS
A trigger is used when someone needs to call a thought to mind at the right time. The subject 
already knows how to perform the Behaviour and what Behaviour the reminder is prompting 
the subject to do but needs to be reminded of it.
6. PRIME USERS
Use subconscious exposure to awake a basic instinct, e.g., desire or guilt to influence the target 
Behaviour of the subject.
7. PRESENT FEEDBACK
Provide the subject with information about how he is performing, during, or after his behaviour 
to encourage (continuous) performance of the target Behaviour.
 
==PAGE:60==
   |   46
METHOD
8. PRESENT POSSIBLE CONSEQUENCES
Presenting the subject with information about the possible outcome of their performance 
prior to the action. This can be used both in situations where target Behaviour is to perform 
or not perform the action.
9. PROVIDE REWARD
Use the opportunity for desired or positive reinforcement to be granted to the subject if he/
she performs the target Behaviour.
 
10. PROVIDE PUNISHMENT
Use the risk of undesired or negative outcome inflicted on the subject or their property if he/
she does not perform target Behaviour or performs an unwanted Behaviour.
11. TELL OR SHOW WHAT OTHERS DO
Make use of the fact that humans are gregarious animals and therefore have a natural drive 
to cooperate to achieve a common goal or to have a sense of community. They also want to fit 
in social groups, which means they are easily affected by what others do as they want to do 
the same.
 
12. ACCOMMODATE THE TARGET GROUP
Messages targeted towards a more specific subject group/subject makes it more persuasive as 
it will fit their attitude, interests, and stances better. 
13. REQUIRE ACTION
Make the target Behaviour necessary to perform, for the user to complete the task they 
originally wanted to perform.
14. SELECTIVELY PRESENT CHOICES 
When the subject is about to select something, create a sense of equal choice. However, one or 
more choices are presented more attractive to indulge target Behaviour.
 
15. SET STANDARDS
A deliberate standard choice (which supports the target Behaviour) have been made for the 
subject, making it more difficult for the subject to make an undesired choice.
16. ENHANCE COMMITMENT
How to make people stay committed to a goal that they want to achieve for them self by 
enhancing/enabling their self-control. Using motivational and encouraging comments/
messages can enhance commitment by making the subject wanting to achieve the goal even 
more or feel worse by dropping out. 
 
17. PRESENT INFORMATION FIGURATIVELY
Engage the subject by explaining information in a figurative way to make it more interesting 
and/or easy to understand. This could be done by using color-coding, metaphors, etc.
 
18. HELP CONSTRUCT SELF-IMAGE
People are likely to act accordingly to the expectations they have for themselves. By helping 
them create/restructure their mindset, they are more likely to change Behaviour.
==PAGE:61==
47
19. SET GOALS
Creating a goal that the user can strive towards, will make it more likely for them to change 
Behaviour, or behave in a desired consistent way.
 
20. IMPOSE EXPECTATIONS
People are likely to act according to the expectations that are stated for them. By imposing 
expectations or responsibilities, the subject is likely to fulfil them. By being told what 
expectations apply to you and/or what you are a part of, you are likely to act accordingly.
21. PROVIDE GUIDANCE
Make it easier for the subject to complete the task by having them follow a specific sequence 
or by giving them guidance on how to perform the Behaviour to avoid them making mistakes. 
This can be done by linking elements in the Behavioural design context that should be used 
together. 
22. MAKE IT SIMPLE
Make it easier for the subject to understand what/how the target Behaviour should be 
performed, by simplifying the content the user is exposed to.
23. VALIDATE ACTION
Make people aware of what they are about to do by giving them extra time to think so they are 
conscious about their actions and/or confirm that they have considered their action and still 
want to continue.
 
24. DIVERT ACTION
The subject is distracted from the current behaviour. This is done by having the current 
behaviour substituted by introducing an alternative.
25. EVOKE EMOTIONS
People are heavily influenced by their feelings and what they find attractive when making 
decisions. Use direct emotional cues for changing Behaviour or attitude towards Behaviour.

4.4.2 CODING ITERATIONS
To achieve high reliability of all cases being coded identically regardless of the coder, several 
code checks were performed. We did the coding check by having both coders (the authors) fill 
out the same cases (around 15 each time) individually and then compare if they were filled out 
similarly. The process of checking cases can be viewed in appendix A4-5.
By doing the case checks, we discovered that not all definitions for the solution parameters were 
clear, and thus, we added several separation rules, that describes how the solution principles 
differ from each other. These separation rules appear along with the definitions of the solution 
principles can be seen in appendix A4-3. 
We conducted four coding checks with approximately 15 new cases each time. For each of the 
case checks the inter-coder reliability was calculated by the value of Krippendorff’ alpha (Hayes, 
Andrew F. and Klaus 2007) (see Fact box 2), which determined the agreement level between 
the coders’ determination of filling in data (Freelon 2013). The alpha values can be viewed in 
appendix A4-5. Krippendorff’s alpha is one of the inter-coder reliability calculators commonly 
used. We calculated Krippendorff’s alpha using the online calculator ReCal OIR (Freelon 2013). 
One of the reasons for Krippendorff’s alphas wide acknowledgement is due to that data can be 
processed even with small sample sizes, multiple coders, or lack of data (Hayes, Andrew F. and 
Klaus 2007).
When we calculated the Krippendorff’s alpha for the solution principles, we held each solution 
principle up against each other. E.g., if a solution in a case consisted of 8 principles, each of the 
principles were tested against each other rather than check if we agreed overall to a case. 
We see below that the Krippendorff’s alpha values from the final coding check displayed in the 
Fact box 2: Krippendorff’s alpha
The alpha value ranges from 0 ≤ alpha ≤ 1, where an alpha value close to 1 represent a high level 
of agreement amongst the coders (Krippendor 2011).
In general, it is recommended to reach an agreement equal to an alpha value of 0,8, which is 
characterized as high reliability.
The minimum level of acceptable agreement is 0,6-0,67 (De Swert, 2012). If an agreement close 
to the minimum value is reached, it should be explained why the level of agreement is that low 
and why the coders choose to continue with this.

4.5.1 EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS
For analysing the data in the filled out behavioural design framework, we have used exploratory 
data analysis.
Exploratory data analysis (EDA) is commonly used for look into data sets and by finding 
correlations and gaining insights, then test newly formed hypotheses based on the discoveries 
from the data (Unwin, 2010). John W. Tukey, who was the first to describe EDA, described the 
methodology as being a detective exploring the data. Although he states, that the investigator 
should have a general goal in mind when looking at the data, he/she should be open-minded to 
other inputs (Unwin, 2010).  
The methodology of EDA is building on an overlapping pattern of analysis and data processing, 
continuously investigating new combinations and relations in the data. There is no specific way 
of conducting an EDA, although graphical representation seems to be frequently mentioned tool 
for discovering connections. There are not any specific requirements for the data to be used for 
an EDA. Therefore, we use EDA to investigate the linking between the problem parameters and 
solution principles from the case database.

4.5.2 SIMILARITY MEASURE
The method of similarity measure is useful for assessing how many times one element is 
occurring when another element is as well. When coding the cases, we saw that solution 
principles are often used together in one intervention. A similarity measure can reveal whether 
there are any patterns in which solution principles are used together. This will be measured 
using Jaccard-Needham vector similarity measure (Zhang and Srihari 2000; Gupta and Sardana 
2015), see Fact box 3.
The Jaccard Needham similarity measure is beneficial for us to use, as it only bases the 
calculations on incidents were one of the two elements investigated occur. Take, for example, 
the solution principles Gamify and Feedback. The similarity measure is only based on out of all the 
times Gamify occurs, how many times does Feedback occur, and vice versa. What is not included, 
is all the other combinations of the solution principles which would lower the similarity value 
heavily. Therefore, the Jaccard Needham similarity measure was chosen to find correlations 
between solutions principles.

5.1.4 CONCLUSION
After the behavioural design framework was highlighted, we looked at what the data in the 
behavioural design framework looked like. From this, we have learned that the data is a mixture 
of cases coming from both designers and researchers, and most of the cases are case based/
cohort control studies. We have furthermore seen that some types of behavioural design 
problem occur more often than others and that we, therefore, will know more about some types 
of behavioural design problems than others. 
Lastly, we have learned that there are no indications of how often a solution principle is used 
and how likely it is to succeed. Similarly, it does not seem that there is a difference in success 
rate when two, or three principles as compared to just using one principle in an intervention. 
That led us to the following key findings:
Key findings
1. 
No current connection between how often a principle is used and how successful it is.
2. 
Only a small connection between the success rate and the number of principles used.
Next step is to determine whether the problem parameters change the success rate and the 
frequency of solution principles and thus, if we can see whether some principles are better 
used in some situations than others. Furthermore, we will investigate whether the success rate 
changes if we look at the combination of each solution principle, e.g., whether Make It Salient 
gets a higher success rate when combined with Impose Expectation or Tell Or Show What Others 
Do or when it is used alone.

6.1 FOUNDATION OF COMPARISONS
In this section, we will present a way of ranking the solution principles and a threshold for what 
we believe should be the minimum criteria for making valid comparisons of solution principles.
6.1.1 RELATIVE SUCCESS RATE
To be able to compare solution principles in relation to problem parameter options and other 
solution principles, we saw the need for a relative success rate (RSR). This will enable us to 
compare solution principles to each other, determine which solution principles is best suited for 
the behavioural parameter, e.g: If the problem is of the Activity Level; Action is it then better to 
use the solution principle consequences or the solution principle rewards to change behaviour?
==PAGE:76==
   |   62
RESULTS
The RSR is calculated as the factor of how successful the solution principle has been, i.e., the 
percentage of success vs. failures, and how reliable that number is by multiplying how many 
successful instances there are of that case (see Fact box 4).
Ideally, we would only look at how successful a solution principle was within each parameter 
option regardless of the number of appearances of the solution principle. However, as we have a 
limited number of cases, and therefore limited knowledge of some of the solution principles, we 
believe that the most reliable way of ranking the solution principles is to factor in, the number 
of occurrences of each solution principles, e.g. the number of times use trigger have been used 
for solving a behavioural problem with daily as the frequency.
We will use the RSR for ranking the most suitable solution principle for each problem parameter 
option, and if a solution principle is found suitable in combination with others, the list of suited 
principles will be ranked by the RSR as well.
6.1.2 THRESHOLD
We have, besides the RSR value, made a threshold for when solution principles are included in 
the ranked lists. The purpose of this is to exclude principles, which we do not find relevant to 
include in a ranked list for comparing how problems and solutions are connected successfully.
The reason for excluding the solution principle in the ranked list for a behavioural parameter 
option could be due to the number of occurrences of a solution principle. For example, if we only 
have one occurrence of Use Trigger being used for a behavioural design problem with an Object, 
which is Non-Human, we are not able to conclude anything regarding whether Use Trigger works 
for Non-Human Objects. Another reason could be that the success rate of solution principles is 
too low within that problem parameter option. E.g., if Use Trigger has been used successfully five 
times but unsuccessfully ten times, there might be other principles which are better used for 
solving the behavioural design problem. 
Thus, we have set a threshold for when a principle can be included in a rank of successful 
solutions for a behavioural parameter based on the success rate and the number of successful 
occurrences.
A solution principle is included in the ranked list if there are at least four occurrences of the 
solution principle within a problem parameter option. This is the limit that we would be 
comfortable with recommending and using ourselves. 
The solution principle furthermore needs to have a success rate of at least 75 %. This value is 
chosen based on the average success rate of the principles being 75% as we would prefer only 
recommending usage of solution principles with a success rate higher than the current standard.

be different if we look at the solution principles individually. Perhaps some combinations of 
solution principles work better than others, and this is a link we will investigate in this section.

6.5 CONCLUSION AND KEY FINDINGS
The main key finding from the result section is that it seems there are tendencies showing that 
it does make a difference in what situations a solution principle is used, and it is possible to 
predict which solution principle is most likely to work based on knowledge about the problem. 
The key findings from each result section have been described below.
Key findings from chapter 5
1. 
Looking across the solution principles, we found that there is no correlation between how 
often a solution principle is being used and how successful it is.  
2. Neither is there any evidence that it is better to use two or three principles in the same 
intervention, than a single solution principle - at least when looking across all cases with a 
varying number of solution principles. This underlines our initial considerations that there 
currently might not be any evidence or guidelines of when to use one solution principle 
rather than another.
Key findings from problem parameters
3. There seems to be a correlation between which behavioural problem you are trying to solve, 
and which solution principles might work. The solution principles were not just evenly 
==PAGE:100==
   |   86

distributed amongst all parameters, and there was a visible difference in which solution 
principles were most recommendable for which problem parameter options. We can 
furthermore see that the success rate and the relative success rate of a solution principle 
change depending on what the problem they are trying to solve.
4. Some parameters seem to have a strong influence on the type of solution principle most 
frequently used, while others have less influence. This is visible in the problem parameter 
Frequency as many of the highest recommended solution principles are the same across 
parameter options.  
5. Some solution principles only seem to work in some situations, while others can be used 
quite generally. Some principles only seem to work for certain problem parameter options. 
These principles are Present Feedback, Accommodate The Target Group, Set Goals, Require 
Action, and Selectively Present Choices, while others seem to work regardless of the problem 
parameter option such as Enhance Commitment, Present Possible Consequences and Impose 
Expectations.
Key findings from combining solution principles
6. Some principles seem to be successful in most combinations, while other principles only 
work when being combined with certain solution principles. This can, for example, be 
seen for Gamify, which generally have a low success rate when in combination with other 
principles. However, if combined with Tell Or Show What Others Do and Present Information 
Figuratively the success becomes a lot higher.
All the above findings have been included in one final framework to be used by designers when 
designing new behavioural solutions. 
Through using this behavioural design framework, it is possible to find the most suitable 
solution principle for each problem parameter option, this solution fits into, and then investigate 
whether those principles should be combined with other principles as well.

The purpose of this chapter is to discuss the meaning of our results. We will do so by 
first discussing the six key findings and highlight this project’s contribution to research

7.1 KEY FINDINGS FROM RESULTS
The six key findings from the results will be discussed in the following with the purpose of 
discussing the reason and the rationale of our key findings.
One overall achievement is that it has been possible to indicate there is a connection between 
the behavioural design problem and when a solution principle should be used. We have done this 
by building a framework which can be used to characterise behavioural design problems and 
solutions by using problem parameters and solution principles. We furthermore found six key 
findings from the results, that will be discussed with respect to literature and the results from 
the previous chapter. 
Key finding 1 + 2: 
No overall correlation between usage, combinations or success of solution principles
This paragraph will discuss key finding one and two from the result section: 1. There is no 
correlation between how often a solution principle is being used and how successful it is. 
2. There is no evidence that it is better to combine solution principles than just using one alone.
The two key findings underline our assumption also derived in the result chapter, that the 
designer must always look at the specific problem parameter or solution principle when deciding 
on which solution principles to use, and whether the solution principle should be combined 
with other solution principles. It does not seem that there is any solution principle that always 
works or one that never works. The same is the case when combining solution principles. The 
==PAGE:102==
   |   88
DISCUSSION
choice of solution principle(s) must, therefore, always be considered according to the context 
of use and other elements affecting the behaviour performed. This underlines the statements of 
Niedderer, Clune, & Ludden (2018) and Trine Damgaard, which both states that there is too little 
knowledge regarding when and how behavioural nudges work (Jeppesen, 2019).
Key finding 3
There seems to be a correlation between problem and successful solution principle
This finding is partly an answer to our research question introduced in the introduction by first 
acknowledging that it seems that there is a connection between the behavioural problem and 
the chance of success of a solution principle(s). 
Throughout our research, we have not found any behavioural theories, which commented on 
or suggested how the behavioural problem space should be accommodated when developing 
solutions. Tromp, Hekkert, & Verbeek (2011)’s Design for Socially Responsible behaviour is 
one of the theories that slightly consider when to use what kind of solution. She presents 11 
strategies for influencing behaviour on a two-axis diagram ranging from hidden to apparent and 
weak to strong. Another framework which is closely related to what we are trying to do is Fogg’s 
behavioural wizard found online (Fogg, 2015), Here Fogg has created several choices related 
to the frequency and behaviour type for the target behaviour which defines the behavioural 
change. Based on these, the behavioural wizard has created guides on how to achieve this 
target behaviour. This is close to what we have tried doing, but Fogg only looks at the target 
behaviour, without considering neither context or current situation, making our work far more 
comprehensive when looking at the behavioural design problem.
These theories are the ones we found that are the closest to provide recommendations of when 
to use which solution principles. Thus, this seems to be the first contribution to defining the 
link between behavioural problems and specific solution principles within behavioural design, 
and although it is not in the detailed level of TRIZ contradiction matrix, it is still a significant 
contribution to designer’s further work, and a beginning for a behavioural TRIZ. 
We did expect a correlation between which solution principles to use for which problems when 
we started investigating the research question. However, since the results are only as good as 
the framework and the cases describing them, decisions in the development of the framework 
could limit the observable correlations between parameters and principles. Therefore, a result 
in itself is, that it has been possible to build a framework that can explain correlations between 
behavioural problems and solutions though there is still some work to be done to optimize the 
framework to make up for the limitations, which will be discussed in section 7.4.
Key finding 4: 
Some parameters seem more important than others for determining behaviour
A part of determining the most successful solution principles to a certain behavioural problem 
is to be able to describe the problem with the most indicative problem parameters. From the 
results, we saw that some problem parameters seemed more important for determining optimal 
solution principles than others. This was evaluated from which problem parameters have the 
greatest variety of most recommended solution principles across the parameter options.
The frequency parameter had many of the same solution principles across the options of One-
Off, Rarely, Repeated, and Daily. Even though it was not all the same, the variance was low, and 
therefore, it could be considered removed or replaced. But for other parameters such as Activity 
Level, Object, and Behaviour Type, there seemed to be a large variance across the highest ranked 
parameter options. The variance across parameter options are due to several factors: 
First of all, the scattering of principles depends on how many parameter options there are within 
==PAGE:103==

the problem parameter. 
Second, the distribution of interventions within each parameter option is affecting the division 
of solution principles.
Third, by making recommendations based on the relative success rate, we should also consider 
how many instances there are of successful cases. 
Fourth, some of the parameter options used for describing the parameters are very broad, 
and this might make some of the parameters insignificant. For example, the Subject can be 
described by the options Population, Segment or Community, but the category Segment does not 
say anything about what unites the group of people - only that they have something in common. 
Therefore, there might be parameters, which seems less significant but is just missing more 
precise parameter options.
When considering these four factors, it is hard to determine which problem parameters are 
the most effective ones to determine which solution principles to use. It could be that some 
significant parameters are still missing, or that the parameters could be operationalized with 
more indicative parameter options. Therefore, we cannot conclude anything determining the 
problem parameters effect on the recommended solution principles, but as a start indicate, that 
the tendency shows, that some problem parameters seem more influencing.
Key finding 5:
Some solution principles only seem to work in some situations, while others can be used 
quite generic.
First off, it is quite interesting that there are clear tendencies for when some principles work or 
not. There are examples of other behavioural articles presenting conditions for when a solution 
principle is likely to work such as Dolan et al. (2012), but Dolan does not give a more general 
overview of when the principles can be used, or compare behavioural solution principles to 
behavioural theory. When looking from a practical point of view, designers often have an idea 
of what behavioural parameter they are trying to influence (see for example Sheeran & Orbell 
(2000)) but this does not mean that the principles used necessary works for that very reason, 
and since there is no overall framework (until now) examining these connections it is difficult 
to say why some solution principle works in only specific situations, and why some works in all 
situations. 
That some solution principles work across most parameter options could potentially indicate 
that there are limitations in our framework, or it could be, that some principles are independent 
of the problem. The reason why some principles work in all situations could be:
I. We lack the parameters which determine when those solution principles will work or not
The reason why some solution principles seem to work across parameter options could be due to 
a lack of one or more parameters which are more significant for determining whether a solution 
principle can be used or not. As shown in the method section 4.2.1., it was not possible to include 
all parameters, and perhaps an important parameter has been excluded. But since all principles 
have at least one of the parameters in which they are only recommended for one parameter 
option it does not seem that another parameter, would give cause to any great changes. Possibly 
if all parameters were closer examined, to determine whether they truly are significant for the 
solution principles, we would see even more clear tendencies within the solution parameters.
II. The solution principles are too broad
It is possible that some solution principles are too broad, which is why they would end up 
being recommended across all parameters. However, even though solution principles were too 
broad, we would still expect the same success rates, and therefore it is more likely that it can be 
explained by some principles is not occurring in any of the ranked lists. I.e., the cases which have 
==PAGE:104==
   |   90

a decent number of occurrences but is not recommended, could be solution principles which are 
too broadly defined.
III. Some solution principles work for everything
It could be, that the solution principles that work independently of the option parameters work 
regardless of what problem is being solved. This is difficult to either confirm or dismiss, though 
it does seem, that the principles which are often recommended across parameter options are 
also the solution principles with a high general success rate which could confirm this theory.
IV. Lack of data
Though it is true, that the parameters which have the most data are also most frequently 
mentioned, it is not directly proportional. For example, Use Trigger which occurs the most in the 
cases is only recommended for more than one parameter option in two out of eight parameters 
(as Use Trigger is not recommended in any of the parameter options for Change Process). Tell Or 
Show What Others Do, on the other hand, have fewer occurrences in the cases than Use Trigger, 
but are recommended in more than one parameter option in six out of eight parameters. So, 
even though there are more data points for Use Trigger, Tell Or Show What Others Do are often 
more recommended, indicating it is at least not just a question of lacking data.
With four indications of why some solution principles seem to work across many different 
problem parameters, this could be the foundation of further research and practical experiments, 
to investigate the finding even further. 
Key finding 6:
The success rate of combining solution principle highly depends on how they are combined
As we saw in the results, some principles are mainly used in combinations with a few other 
principles, and only a single principle is standalone. We furthermore saw from looking at general 
tendencies of combining solution principles, that a solution principle that seems to work best 
alone could potentially be successfully combined anyhow.  
We do not have the data to show all combinations of solution principles, and thus, we are not 
able to say something about them. The total number of possible combinations of all solution 
principles are 600 (when only looking at two principles), and with our 218 interventions, we 
do not have enough data to comment on all combinations. But this high number of possible 
combinations is also why it is interesting that some combinations occur up to ten times across 
the interventions.
To our knowledge, there exists no current research regarding how solution principles within 
behavioural design can be combined and whether it is more successful in doing so. However, 
the effect of solution principles used together is often being tested. There are examples of 
companies which not only test several interventions to determine which is the most effective 
for the specific problem, but also test the amplifying effect from combining solution principles 
in one intervention (Hallsworth, List, Metcalfe, & Vlaev, 2017; OECD, 2017, p. 205). However, 
these are individual cases, and the knowledge gained from these is hard to apply to another 
context generally. Our work provides a comprehensive overview of which solution principles 
work together by examining a lot of these individually tested cases.

7.2 DISCUSSION OF METHODS
In the following the methods used for developing the framework and deriving the result will 
be discussed. The framework has a strong influence on how the results look, and therefore, 
we found it important to discuss the choices that have been in that development process and 
different approaches.
7.2.1 STRUCTURE OF FRAMEWORK
The framing the project as building on the prism of TRIZ introduced in the introductory chapter 
have had an impact, as we implemented a theoretical approach to the framework. Another 
option of creating a framework could be from talking to various design companies and getting 
their input on what shapes behaviour, to create a more practical framework. Or if we were not 
inspired by TRIZ and the prism of TRIZ, it might not have been a framework at all. Even though 
we were not able to describe the behavioural problems as contradictions like TRIZ, there are 
possibly many other ways of linking problems to solutions, that have not been considered.
7.2.2 PROBLEM PARAMETERS
In this thesis, we chose to focus on the parameters found in the literature. This means that we 
might have overlooked parameters which are important for the success of a solution principle 
simply because they have not been presented in the literature which we reviewed. Perhaps a 
parameter such as the media of intervention is important. It might make a big difference if a 
solution principle is presented via a text message, a product, or a person, and some principles 
might work better through on media than another. Similarly, other ‘practical’ parameters (e.g., 
the cost of a solution principle, or cultural differences) might have been overlooked, simply 
because they have not been presented in the theory we have come across. 
Thus, it would, in general, have been interesting to get an even better understanding of what 
parameters are significant for which solution principle should be recommended and which 
ones might be excluded or replaced as well as which parameters might have been overlooked. 
But as we conducted a comprehensive literature review, the same parameters which we have 
overlooked, have been overlooked by all previous theorists. And no matter how thorough a 
search, there would always be potential parameters which had been overlooked.
7.2.3 METHODS FOR DETERMINING SOLUTION PRINCIPLES
In our work of creating a collection of solution principle across theories, the immaturity of 
the theories caused the need for extensive removal of principles as many were out of scope or 
doublets/similarities. It was furthermore necessary to reduce the number of principles, as all 
including principles of all theories would have meant a total number of 136 solution principles. 
Another approach to creating the solution principles could have been to directly adapt an 
existing framework such as Design with Intent, the Behavioural Wheel on one of the other 
presented frameworks to avoid cutting out principles. However, none of the theories were found 
sufficient as each theory had been developed with a slightly different purpose than ours, and 
selecting one theory only, would, in our opinion, not suffice to cover the field of behavioural 
design. Therefore, we aimed at selecting all the principles that would fit within our definition of 
behavioural design, hence leaving the solution principles different in design expression.

7.2.4 ACQUISITION OF DATA
Another factor which might have influenced the validity of our work is the way of which cases 
were identified. In an ideal project, we would have been able to make a more thorough case search 
and locate a larger amount of cases, within different case characteristics to cover a widespread 
problem and solution space. The cases in this project were obtained from websites of behavioural 
design companies, articles, and former theses. This might have created unforeseen biases in the 
framework, as someone else already has selected cases on criteria not visible in our work. But 
like so many cases were available to us this way, and as it is furthermore quite difficult to find 
cases solely by searching directly for them, it was not beneficial for us within the available time 
frame to conduct a more thorough case search. We furthermore have a quite large number of 
different sources, where the cases have been found, thus reducing the biases (as compared to if 
the cases were only taken from 1 or 2 sources).
Even though we only implemented cases found via other sources we still have cases which we 
did not have the time to read and include in the framework due to time restriction, and thus, it 
was not beneficial for us to spend extra time searching for cases. In the future, we would like 
to expand our search for cases to include even more cases. In general, it would be great for the 
validity of the final framework if more cases were to be included in the framework to be able to 
make a more extensive analysis checking for significant results.
7.2.5 ANALYSIS OF DATA
Comparing by relative success rate
In order to be able to recommend solution principles most suited for a particular parameter 
option, the comparable relative success rate (RSR) has been calculated from the success rate 
multiplied by the number of successful cases. As we see from the RSR in some of the options, it is 
often the same solution principles that lie within the most recommended principles for use. This 
is due to that the number of cases within each solution principles varies from 3-41. Especially 
within the problem parameters with only 2-3 options, it is very likely that the solution principles 
represented in most cases will have the highest factor. This results in a less nuanced picture of 
the division, as it always seems like the designer should use the same principles. Multiplying 
the number of successful cases is causing a distortion in the diversity in the recommended 
solution principles, however, to prevent, that the solution principles which might not work 
are recommended because of the lack of data, multiplying by the number of successful cases 
is currently the best solution. But as more cases are added in the future, it should only be the 
success rate (or similar) which determines the ranking of a principle.  
Methods for exploring data
It was decided to use exploratory data analysis (EDA) for comparing and looking for tendencies 
in the data from the framework. Another approach could have been to conduct another type of 
data comparison analysis, e.g., testing for hypotheses. But since this field of research is relatively 
new, and we were not able to find similar work, we thought that we would jump to conclusions 
too fast by start formulating hypothesis that has not been founded in literature.
To be able to statistically determine which problem parameters had the highest impact on 
which solution principle to use, we could have used logistic regression. In this way, we could 
have statistically determined essential problem parameters in the framework and from there, 
developed the problem parameters further.
However, logistic regression requires a larger amount of data. We could come across this by 
clustering the solution principles into larger groups and possibly reduce some of the problem 
==PAGE:107==
93
parameters, but we could risk that we would lose the precision of the results. Therefore, the 
results from the logistic regression were not used, as our current dataset did not fit the type of 
statistical analysis.  
A Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA) was also considered in the analysis of data. QCA is able 
to compare cases across parameters, to discover tendencies which are common for an outcome 
(Rihoux & Ragin, 2009). So, instead of only looking at one parameter at the time, and see which 
principles are recommended, we can look at principles which work for combinations of problem 
parameters. This would explain in more detail which solution principles should be used for 
which problems, and if there were tendencies in the type of the problem. However, when looking 
at string combinations, hypotheses are needed to limit the data, one is looking at the time, to be 
able to derive any conclusions from the QCA. Therefore, the EDA has made a strong foundation 
for formulating hypotheses for the QCA, which have not been possible without. Performing a 
QCA would be a natural next step in the process of deriving links between behavioural problems 
and solution principles.
7.3 CONTRIBUTIONS AND IMPLICATIONS 
7.3.1 FOR PRACTISE
The aim of the project was to create an evidence-based framework for designers that link 
behavioural problems with the most suitable behavioural design solutions. We want to underline 
that the framework should not in any way replace the work of a behavioural designer or any of 
the processes one is going through with designing for behaviour. The framework should be a 
tool to support the development of solutions for behaviour change.
The framework is built on a case database with cases selected from our inclusion and exclusion 
criteria. Already this database of cases contributes to the design practice by functioning as a 
collection of qualitatively evaluated cases, i.e., successful cases for the designer to be inspired 
from.
The framework is built upon generally described behavioural problem parameters and 
behavioural solution principles - it is then up to the designer to describe the specific problem 
in the generalized terms and translate the suggested solution principles into the specific case 
that they are working on. The framework provides guidelines for designing, founded in other 
designers work and experience with behavioural design cases. In Example 7 we have given a 
concrete example of how we imagine our work to be used by designers in the future.
The work with the framework has further highlighted the importance for designers to understand 
the problem space thoroughly. E.g., the Change Process (Opposition, Not Aware, Intent) were often 
not described, which we can only encourage designers to be aware of. This presents valuable 
insights into the type of solution needed. Capability and opportunity for performing target

When designers investigate the problem space more deeply, they not only understand the 
mechanisms underlying the behaviour better, they also add valuable data to the framework to 
help and inspire other designers in the future.

7.3.2 CONTRIBUTIONS AND IMPLICATIONS FOR RESEARCH
With this thesis, we have contributed to research within the field of behavioural problems, 
behavioural design solutions, and how these interrelate. These, however, are not definitive, but 
points in the direction of using the theory more practically.  
This is seen from our operationalization of the problem parameters. We studied the literature 
within behavioural design and theories of behavioural change, and the parameters found 
suitable for the framework, were operationalized into 2-5 options for each, making it possible 
to describe the link to solution principles by analysing those. The work of operationalising the 
problem parameters can lay the foundation for further investigation into describing behavioural 
problems in general.

7.4 LIMITATIONS
In this section, we will discuss the limitations of our project, with the purpose of showing 
which considerations need to be considered when using our framework, and what parts of the 
framework might be further developed.
When starting the process of building the framework, we did not know if it was possible to find 
a connection between behaviour and solutions - we did not even know if it were possible to 
describe behaviour in a generic way. Both have been achieved, and we have shown that there 
might be a connection. There are limitations to our work, which the reader needs to take into 
consideration when using our behavioural framework. Some of the considerations and hence, 
limitations of the framework will be presented in the following.
Problem parameters limited by operationalizability
The included parameters were selected based on theory and by assessing whether it was 
possible to implement the parameters. Having to be able to operationalize the parameters, 
caused exclusion of many theoretically acknowledged parameters simply because they were not 
operationalizable. Perhaps we have excluded some of the most important parameters from the 
theory because it was not possible to observe or operationalize. It would be interesting to try 
and find ways to operationalize the parameters which were not possible to include at this stage, 
such as emotions.
Reliability limited by binary success rating
The choice using a binary scale to determine the success rate of the case has had some 
consequences. One issue of using a binary scale for success is that it is leaching out the differences 
in which solution principles creates the biggest change based on the problem at hand, or which 
ones works best together. Success rates are interesting, and looking at this gave interesting 
results, but we might have gotten more comprehensive correlations between data if we were 
able to look more nuanced at when something is successful.  
To make a list of requirements for separating cases into levels of successfulness, the elements 
of the list and how these should weight in the rating system were considered. Should the case be 
evaluated in percentage or percentage points increase? Should we consider whether the change 
was regarding short-term/immediate changes or long-term/lifestyle changes? Lifestyle 
changes might be harder to achieve a positive result on, and hence a lower success rate in long-
term changes should be equivalent to a higher success rate in a short-term change. 
A qualitative evaluation of a case was also considered. Right now, it is not included as a part of 
the evaluation, although we consider it as a valuable factor, that the subjects at least are not 
negatively affected by the solution. We were all along specific on that the qualitative evaluation 
==PAGE:110==
   |   96

could never stand alone, as people do not always behave as they say they will. This also implicates 
the investigation of the qualitative data, as just by asking subjects on what they think of the 
intervention, they might be biased by that in their answer. 
Case database limited by lack of data
The results presented in the previous chapter are based on the data from the cases we have 
available. Though having spent an extensive amount of time adding cases to the framework, we 
still have a quite limited dataset compared to the number of parameters and solution principles 
linked together. This means that even though one solution principle was not ranked high in 
one parameter option, it does not necessarily mean that it does not work successfully within 
that option, means that we do not have any data showing whether it works or not. It could be 
interesting to investigate why some of the problem parameter options were less frequently used 
and similar, why some of the solution principles have been used less than others. 
Even though our dataset is limited, we have set high selection criteria for the cases included, 
and therefore, we will argue that the dataset might be limited but robust. It only consists of 
cases with the quantitative evaluation of success, which has the consequence of cutting away at 
least half of the cases we have been looking through.
We further limited the number of recommendations made by setting the threshold of at least 
four cases and a minimum of 75% success rate. But again, we would rather recommend fewer 
principles that work, than more principles with a higher risk of failing. 
To our knowledge, there exists no work with the same number of cases and these high criteria to 
the evaluation of success. To our knowledge, there exists no work evaluating the same number 
of cases regardless of the success rate. So, even though we might benefit from a larger amount 
of data, it is still a highly robust dataset which has managed to evaluate far more cases, than any 
other case study within behavioural design. 
7.5 FUTURE WORK
In this section, we will highlight the future work needed for progressing with this project. 
When developing and working with the behavioural framework throughout this project, several 
thoughts came to mind in how the framework could be further developed. 
In the future, we would like to expand our investigation of the problem parameters. It would be 
interesting to examine which parameters were important to practitioners, as well as find out if 
any of the current parameters should be changed or removed.
 
The solution principles could be further examined, as we imagine it to be useful to compare the 
solution principles introduced by us from a theoretical point of view to the ones used in industry 
to see whether the ones defined in this project seem applicable to behavioural designers.
The success level should preferably be developed to a more nuanced scale to make more detailed 
recommendations on which solution principles to use. However, this requires a comprehensive 
study of evaluating what should be a part of determining the success level. If this is developed in 
the future, it would be a valuable parameter to include in the behavioural framework.
As an ongoing future task, we would like more cases to be added to the framework, as more data 
would mean higher security in which solution principles are likely to work.

DISCUSSION
==PAGE:113==
The aim of this thesis was to investigate how the successful connection between behavioural 
problems and the solutions can be described. We did this with the purpose of creating a 
framework that can show which behavioural solution principles are most likely to work for a 
specific behavioural design problem. This was done with the purpose of assisting behavioural 
designers and others working with changing behaviour in the process of developing behavioural 
interventions. 
To create the behavioural design framework and examine the link between problem and solution 
we had to find a way to describe a behavioural problem in a general way, describe a behavioural 
solution, and connect those two by collecting and comparing case studies of behavioural design.
The basis of the problem parameters and solution principles were found in the literature of 
behavioural design change and then adjusted so they could be analysed and compared across 
cases. Cases of testing and conducting experiments on behavioural changes functioned as the 
link between the problems and solutions, and the case studies found in literature and from 
behavioural design companies were coded into the framework. The defined problem parameters 
and solution principles were then used to describe the cases in the same structured way. We 
made an exploratory data analysis on the data from the framework and where then able to 
conclude on the connection between the problem parameters and solution principles.
The result of this process was a framework consisting of 9 problem parameters, 25 solution 
principles, and a binary success rate, which have been used for characterising 218 cases for 
behaviour change. The overall success rate from all cases in the database is 75%. 
We found that there currently does not seem to be a link between how many times a solution 
principle is used by behavioural designers, and how successful it is. Neither did we see a strong 
link between how many solution principles are used in one intervention and the success rate. 
This means that we cannot provide a general recommendation of which solution principles are 
always likely to work and how many solution principles to combine.
However, we found that the type of behavioural design problem a designer is trying to solve 
seems to influence which solution principle is most likely to work - some stronger than others.

==PAGE:114==

Furthermore, we found that some solution principle seems to work best in combination and 
others alone. 
Based on this, we have made a list of recommendations for which solution principles are most 
likely to work for changing behaviour for each of the problem parameter options. We have 
combined this list with recommendations for solution principles that are likely to work better 
when combined. 
Our thesis indicates that it could be possible to link behavioural problems and solutions, in 
a model like TRIZ, and that it is possible to use generic descriptions for characterizing real 
behavioural cases. Our results indicate that it is not necessarily the same solution principle that 
works in all situations, and that the solution principle(s) best suited to solve a behavioural design 
problem depends on the behavioural design problem and the context. Hence, we have answered 
our research question of how behavioural design problems and solutions are connected. 
Although the framework will need further expansion, and the problem parameters and solution 
principles further refinement, we have contributed to a deeper understanding of when and how 
behavioural solutions works, making it easier for behavioural designers to create successful 
interventions. We give access to knowledge across behavioural design fields for one designer to 
be able to utilize other designers' experiences on successful interventions.
