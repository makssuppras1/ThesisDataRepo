Abstract
In a world where security is receiving increased attention, the research areas of biometric
authentication systems are experiencing rapid growth. One of these areas is Automatic
Speaker Verification systems (ASV systems), which authenticate a person by recogniz-
ing their speech. However, such systems have a problem: They do not perform well
when enrolling a person with one language and validating using another language. This
thesis brings the research area forward by doing comparative testing on the pre-trained
x-vector models for SRE16 and VoxCeleb created by Snyder et al., using two new multi-
lingual datasets, SPARC and SWAN. Furthermore are two feature extraction methods, the
Glottal Flow Cepstral Coefficients and the Residual Phase Cepstral Coefficients, imple-
mented. The tests on the x-vector models include different combinations of channels and
languages. Both models showed that the language used in enrollment and validation had
a high impact on performance, and validating with a previously unseen language further
degraded system performance.

Introduction
Biometrics is the use of automated recognition of individuals based on their behavioural
and biological characteristics. It enables individuals to gain access to a system through
security with measurable and distinctive physiological as well as behavioral parameters.
The use of biometrics goes a long way back in history. An example would be the use
of fingerprint cataloging in 1881 by Juan Vucetich, collecting the fingerprints of criminals
in Argentina. There is a vast amount of biometric identifiers available, every one unique
to the owner of them. The physiological identifiers include characteristics such as fin-
gerprints, palm veins, palm print, hand geometry, iris recognition, retina, and DNA. The
behavioral identifiers concern themselves with the pattern of the behavior of an individual,
having characteristics such as gait, typing rhythm, and voice.
This thesis is about the voice characteristic. It is possible to split the voice characteristic
research into two subgroups. There is the speech recognition group, where the content
of the message is of interest, i.e., what someone is saying, and the speaker recognition
field, which is more interested in determining who is speaking. Both fields have seen
a tremendous increase in research since their beginnings and are used in a variety of
applications.
Speaker recognition can be further divided into speaker identification and speaker veri-
fication, as shown in Figure 1.1. The speaker identification task is to find the identity of
an unknown speaker. An example of speaker identification could be to find out who is
currently talking in a group conversation. Speaker verification is possible to use without
the speakers’ consent. Due to this, it is used heavily in the surveillance field. Speaker
identification is performed by taking a speech sample of an unknown speaker and score
it against a database containing different speakers. The speaker identification system
performs the scoring, outputting the speaker model with the highest likelihood of being
the speaker.
Speaker verification (also referred to as speaker authentication) is the task of verifying
the identity of the speaker. Its usage is primarily in security applications, where autho-
rization is required to gain access to something. As opposed to speaker identification, the
speaker has usually given the authorization to record his speech. The speaker verifica-
tion system, therefore, acts as a gatekeeper. If a speaker wants access to a system, the
speaker’s claim needs to be verified, i.e., that he is whom he claims to be. Verification of
this happens by taking the speaker model from the database corresponding to the identity
claim and validating a speech sample from him against the speaker model. The speaker
verification system calculates a resemblance score from the speakers’ speech sample
and the speaker model of the claimed identity. If the score is positive, the speaker verifi-
cation system estimates the claim to be valid, and if the score is negative, the speaker is
assumed to be an imposter. As opposed to the speaker identification system where the
output is an estimate for a speaker match for every model in the database, the speaker
verification system outputs a single score, a match, or non-match.
There are a few different ways that a speaker recognition system can calculate the score.
When validating a speaker against a database of speakers, the database is either an
open or closed set of speaker models [1]. With the closed set approach, the speaker
model chosen is the one with the closest resemblance to the input speaker. This approach
forces the system to make a decision and output a speaker model. With an open set, it

text-dependent system, in general, is a more reliable and accurate system. However, it
has the drawback of creating a language-dependency on the system. A text-independent

utterances. The text-independent system, therefore, has to be able to model the general
underlying properties of the speaker’s voice. In contrast to the text-independent system,
the text-dependent system has no language dependencies.
A challenge that is just as big as the language-dependency, if not bigger, is the challenge
that stems from inconsistencies present when using audio from multiple sources. In the
speaker recognition community, audio sources are referred to as channels. The audio
quality can differ heavily between channels. The audio quality difference can impact the
systems performance, and is known as the problem of channel mismatch. The channel
mismatch problem is most noticeable when audio used to develop the ASV system and
to test the system are different.
It would be ideal to have an ASV system with no language-dependency and no invariances
to channel differences. A vast amount of languages exists around the globe. Each with
multiple dialects, and thus a system that is dependent on a single language would not be
usable by many. A genuinely language-independent system has to be text-independent
because if the system is text-dependent, a language classifier needs to be available, as
previously mentioned. To create a system that is both text-independent and language-
invariant thorough testing has to be performed with multiple languages.
This thesis focuses on the language-dependency for ASV systems and how channel mis-
matches can impact an ASV system’s performance—primarily looking into text-independent
systems to obtain a truly language-independent system by testing with multiple languages.
However, before diving into this, background knowledge of how an ASV system work is

required. The background knowledge includes the theory, how to implement one, and
how to obtain standardized results. From a top-down approach, an ASV system consists
of three critical components; (1) the front-end, (2) the speaker modeling system, and (3)
the back-end.
The front-ends task is to do feature extraction of the input utterances and other types of
processing. There are several approaches to do this, and they are discussed in Chapter
2. The features are extracted from a raw audio signal and contain speaker characteristics
used to model the speaker. It is possible to process the signal further by using a Voice Ac-
tivity Detection (VAD) system, either to eliminate parts where the speaker is not speaking
or to reduce background noise.
The speaker modeling system takes the features for each speaker that was extracted by
the front-end. The speaker modeling system then creates a speaker model containing the
speaker features from the input features. Chapter 3 shows the different ways to model
speaker features and different ways to represent the speaker features.
The back-end does the scoring of the speaker features. In an ASV system, the similarity
score obtained from calculating the similarities between the speaker features extracted
from the input utterance and the speaker features of the claimed identity is used to verify
the claim. If the similarity score is above a certain threshold, the speaker is a match.
There exist different methods to calculate the similarity score. They are shown in Chapter
4.
It is also necessary to know how to calculate the performance of an ASV system. The
scoring follows a standardized approach, done by measuring the false acceptance rating
and the false rejection rating, i.e., how many imposters get accepted as the identity they
claim to be and how many actual speakers get rejected despite a valid identity claim. The
approach is explained more in-depth in Chapter 5.
Once performance scoring is known, we now know how the entire system works. It is time
to look at what others have done within the ASV research field to combat the language-
dependency on the ASV systems. Chapter 6 contains insight into this area, as well as
different approaches to minimize the channel mismatch issues. The end of the Chapter
contains the points of interest extracted from the related work. These points described
lay the base for the remaining part of the thesis.
Some parts of the ASV system requires training to be able to operate efficiently. The
training requires datasets containing an exceptional amount of recorded speech from a
large group of speakers. Some of these datasets contain up to several hundred hours
of speech. However, it is not only the size of the dataset that comes into play when effi-
ciently training an ASV system, the number of available languages also plays a significant
role. Chapter 7 looks into the content of several datasets that are publicly available. The
chapter also contains two new datasets not used in any prior works, the SPARC and the
SWAN datasets. These have been made available to be used for performance testing of
the final models used in this work.
In some of the related work, the models used have been made available for public use.
These come as pre-trained models. Pre-trained models are models that have already
been configured and are ready to be used from the get-go. They do not require training,
as the name implies, which saves a substantial amount of time. Instead of creating a new
model, using a pre-trained model with a sufficient performance score makes it possible to
test other challenges met in the creation of an ASV system. Chapter 8, therefore, contains
some of the available models. The chapter introduces them and contains a comparison

Feature extraction
As mentioned in the Introduction chapter, an ASV system consists of several different
modules. This chapter covers the first part of the system, the front-end. It contains various
ways of extracting features of interest from the utterance and further processing tools such
as the Voice Activation Detection (VAD) system. However, before going in-depth with the
different methods, looking into what makes an audio sample is of interest.
When a speaker utters an utterance, the utterance is in the shape of a sound wave. This
sound wave is a continuous-time signal. When we are capturing it with a microphone, we
are converting the sound wave to a sequence of samples, thus changing it to a discrete-
time signal. In the Speaker Recognition Community, the term sample has a dual meaning.
It can both represent the data point, as is the case in this context, or a analog or digital
representation of biometric characteristics [4]. A sample can either be a single value
or a set of values at a point in time and space. The wave is captured by a sampler,
which extracts these samples from a continuous signal. Figure 2.1 shows a signal and its
sample points. Once the signal has been sampled, it possible to reconstruct the signal
from the sample points. The term sample rate denotes the rate for sampling audio and is
measured in Hertz. Hertz is a unit measured in seconds, i.e. if a signal is sampled with
16 kHz (kilohertz), the signal has been sampled with 16,000 points per second. A signal
with a duration of 2 seconds sampled at 16 kHz would then yield 32,000 individual data
points. A higher sample rate means more sampling points per second. The original audio
sampled is referred to as the sample data.
After an utterance is processed, it is possible to either feed it directly to the speaker mod-
eling module or process it further. Processing it further has the opportunity of identifying
the components of the signal that contains speaker characteristics. These components
can make it easier for us to identify the speaker. An example of such is to filter out parts of
the audio that contains no speech. Finding the parts of the audio that contains no speech
is possible by using a VAD system. By using a VAD system it is possible to reveal con-
tent such as linguistic content. However, it can also show what parts of the signal carry
unnecessary information such as background noise. If such parts exist, it is possible to
reduce the noise, or even discard them.

2.2
Linear Predictive Coding
Linear Predictive Coding (LPC) is a feature extraction method that can be seen as a
standalone method but also works as a pre-step to other feature extraction methods.
These are methods such as LPCC, GLFCC, and RPCC. For more information regarding
these feature extraction methods, see Section 2.3, 2.5 and 2.6, respectively. The LPC
is an approach that performs well in clean environments but faces challenges in noisy
environments [14]. What makes the LPC approach interesting is its way of extracting the
spectra and pitch formants. The formants are the spectral shaping that results from an
acoustic resonance of the vocal tract in a human. The LPC is also known as the temporal
approach, invented to equivalent the resonant structure of the vocal tract that developed
the original sound.
The computation of the LPC consists of four steps. The steps in order are pre-emphasis,
framing, windowing, and lastly, computing the LPC. The steps can are seen in Figure 2.4.
The pre-emphasis goal is to make the signal less prone to precision effect by applying a
filter to the signal. Pre-emphasis also spectral flattens the signal, making it possible to
characterize the audio spectrum and quantify the signal’s pitches.
The next step is to split the signal into frames. Each frame consists of M samples of about
20 to 40 seconds duration. There is a standard overlap of 10 ms between two adjacent
frames to ensure stationary values between frames. The third step performs windowing
on the obtained frames. The purpose of this step is to minimize the edge effect. The

Glottal Flow Cepstrum Coefficients
The Glottal Flow Cepstrum Coefficients (GLFCC), introduced in 2013 [19], is a feature for
speaker identification that captures speaker-specific characteristics from speakers’ vocal
excitation patterns. The GLFCC captures the glottal flow from the airflow arising from
the trachea and passing through the vocal folds. Vocal folds are speaker-specific, where
for some individuals, the vocal folds never close entirely while, in other cases, the vocal
folds close entirely and rapidly. The glottal opening can vary from speakers, where some
speakers have a glottal opening that is approximately equal in width along the length
of the glottis. In contrast, other speakers can have an opening that more resembles a
triangular-shaped opening that occurs according to the structure of their vocal folds. Due

2.5.1
IAIF algorithm
The IAIF-method is based on a priori knowledge about the overall shape of the transfer
function of the vocal tract (Figure 2.8(b)) [20]. When it comes to vowels, they are of an
all-pass nature with some high energy regions. If the tilting effect of the glottal source (the
curve of Figure 2.8(a)) can be removed from the speech spectrum, then it is possible to
estimate the local tract with a fair accuracy using LPC (Section 2.2).
The flow diagram of the IAIF algorithm is shown in figure 2.9. The IAIF-algorithm calcu-
lates an estimate of the glottal contribution and the vocal tract transfer function with an
iterative structure that repeats twice. At the start of the first iteration, the effect of the
glottal source to the speech spectrum can be modeled by computing an LPC-analysis of
the first order to the speech signal. Referencing to Figure 2.8, this implies the curve in
2.8(a) can be estimated by computation of a crude envelope for curve 2.8(d). This pro-
duces the estimated glottal contribution. It is then removed by applying a higher-order
LPC-analysis to the signal where the source’s effect is removed. The first estimation of
the glottal excitation can then be obtained by removing the tract and lip radiation’s effects
from the original speech signal by inverse filtering.
The glottal wave estimate that is the result of the first iteration is then used in the second
iteration to compute a more accurate model of the glottal contribution. The effect of the
glottal excitation on the speech spectrum is estimated during the second iteration and
uses an LPC-analysis with an order that usually equals two or four. A note to remember
on the first iteration of the estimation of the glottal contribution is that the computation
should not be using an LPC-order higher than one. After removing the estimated glottal
contribution, the model is formed again for the vocal tract using the higher-order LPC-
analysis. The final results can then be obtained by inverse filtering the effects of the vocal
tract and lip radiation from the original speech signal. [20]

Hidden Markow Model
Hidden Markow Models (HMM) is a speaker modeling method that has been used exten-
sively in the Speaker Recognition community [24, 25, 26]. HMMs are a type of Markov
Models (see Appendix B) where the states are hidden from view, meaning they are not
observable from an outside viewpoint. The HMM is not controllable from outside forces
either. The HMM does not produce any output when it is in a state, but rather it is the
transition between states that produces an output. The HMMs hidden states possesses
the Markov Property (see Appendix C), where the probability of entering a new state is
only dependent on the current state, and none of the previous states.
The reason that HMMs are great for Speaker Recognition is their tendency to stay in a
particular state and then suddenly to jump into a new state, remaining in that current state
for some time. This trait is beneficial, since it is expected that the speaker’s characteristics
do not change suddenly. Figure 3.2 shows the possible transitions of a HMM element.
There are three transitions, a self transition, a null transition and a forward transition. The
self transition transitions back to the same state. Null transitions produces no output for

The Universal Background Model (UBM) is, in essence, a very large GMM trained to

smaller than the previous one until reaching a bottleneck, and this makes it possible to

the layer, it is possible to use the filters multiple times at different points on the input. It

• Convolution layer - Applies a convolution operation in the form of a matrix multipli-

Recurrent Neural Network
A Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) is a type of neural network that makes use of se-
quential information. Compared to a traditional neural network where it is assumed that
all inputs and outputs are independent of each other, the RNN uses the previous output
as input for the next round of outputs. This allows the RNN to have a ”memory” that can
capture information about previous calculations. RNNs have seen much success in the
speech recognition community [53], language modeling [54], and text processing [55].

Speaker Feature Scoring
The previous chapters have looked into different techniques of preprocessing and how to
set up a speaker model to capture different speaker characteristics. The remaining part of
the ASV system is the back-end, i.e., the method of scoring different speaker features. An
example of feature scoring with the i-vector was shown in Section 3.5, where the scoring
was performed by calculating the cosine distance score on i-vector pairs. This chapter
will cover three different scoring methods, starting with the oldest technique, the Principal
Component Analysis (PCA), then moving on to the Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA),

The JFA speaker feature modeling method is not taken into account in this chapter, as

4.1
Principal Component Analysis
The Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a technique that can do dimensionality reduc-
tion and the scoring of speaker features. When used to score speakers, the PCA analyzes
the interrelationship between a large group of variables and does so by splitting them into
smaller amounts of variables, referred to as principal components. Splitting them into
smaller components is a feat where the loss of information is minimal [59]. PCA is a
machine learning technique that can recognize patterns and correlations among speaker
features. When the PCA finds a strong enough correlation between different variables,
it reduces the dimensions. The PCA then keeps dimensions that contain the most cru-
cial information, while discarding dimensions containing redundant information—thereby
retaining the information deemed essential, while simultaneously simplifying the speaker
features.
Generally, the PCA dimensionality reduction consists of five steps
1. Standardize the data
2. Compute the covariance matrix
3. Calculate eigenvectors and the eigenvalues
4. Compute the Principal Components
5. Reduce dimensions of the dataset
Since the data used in recent Speaker Recognition approaches usually use the i-vector
or the x-vector feature modeling techniques, the data has already been standardized.

Measuring ASV model performance
Biometric Performance Evaluation of biometric systems is possible by calculating the
Equal Error Rate (EER). It is an ISO/IEC standard biometric metrics [63], where the EER
is the error rate at which the False Match Rate (FMR) and False Non-Match Rate (FNMR)
are equal. To correctly evaluate a biometric system’s accuracy, an extensive amount of
bona fide and impostor attempts on the system are performed. The similarity scores are
used for measurement and thus saved. There are several concepts in the evaluation of
Biometric systems.
False Match Rate (FMR) is the proportion of impostor attempts that are falsely declared
to match a template of another object. False Non-Match Rate (FNMR) is the proportion
of bona fide attempts that are falsely declared not to match a template of the same object
Failure-To-Acquire Rate (FTA) is the proportion of attempts for which the system fails to
produce a sample of sufficient quality. False Accept Rate (FAR) and False Reject Rate
(FRR) are similar to the FMR and FNMR, but they differ in that the definition distinguishes
between attempts and transactions. A transaction can consist of a sequence of attempts,
and depending on the configuration of the system, the individual attempts can affect the
transaction differently. The FAR and FRR also take the FTA into account. If a transaction
consists of only one attempt, the FAR and FRR can be calculated with this
FAR = FMR ∗(1 −FTA)
FRR = FTA + FNMR ∗(1 −FTA)
Finally, it is possible to obtain the performance by calculating the Equal Error Rate (EER).
This is done by calculating the point where the proportion of False Matches is equal to
False Non-Matches, e.g.
FNMR = FMR
(5.1)
It is possible to plot the performance evaluation result in two ways, either by plotting a De-
tection Error Trade-off (DET) graph or plotting a Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC)
graph. The DET graph plots the FRR along the Y-axis, and the FAR along the X-axis,
i.e., false-negative vs. false-positive rate. Since the Y-axis shows the number of match
errors, the curve that is closest to the bottom, or X-axis, corresponds to the best biometric
performance. The ROC graph plots the true positive (1 - FRR) vs. the false positive rate
(FAR). The best biometric performance will be near the top of the plot.

Related work
The previous chapters looked into the fundamentals of ASV systems. It is also of in-
terest to look at related work to properly understand the challenges associated with ASV
systems. This Chapter contains others’ approaches to language and channel mismatches
and their findings. It consists of three sections, a section targeting the language-dependency,
a section dealing with the challenge of channel mismatch, and a summarizing of the key
challenges and points of interest.
6.1
Language-dependency
Language dependency has been the main point of interest in several works. In Kleynhans
et al. [64] using MFCC as front-end feature input, language-specific UBMs where trained
to normalize speakers’ scores. They found that using language-specific UBMs yielded a
better result than both language-independent UBMs and UBMs trained on relevant lan-
guage families. However, while language-specific UBMs yields better results, but also
drastically limits the possible uses of the ASV system. In Bhattacharjee et al. [65], they
used a GMM-UBM setup with MFCC features to test the performance degradation that
occurred when phonetics of the enrolment and the test language were drastically different.
They tested with Hindi, English, and Arunachal Pradesh, a language local to North-East
India. Their research showed that due to a resemblance between Hindi and the local
languages, the performance of the system was much better when a combination of those
two languages where used in enrolment and test. With the local language as enrolment
and Hindi as test language, they obtained an EER of 6.82%, while the EER was 11.36%
with English as test language.
A GMM-UBM setup was also used in [66] with English and Cantonese speakers. Different
experiments were carried out. In the first, the model training was done with English and
tested with the English speaker verification set and the Cantonese speaker verification set.
It showed an apparent performance degradation when there was a language mismatch
as opposed to when testing was done with the same language. The second experiment
was performed with the Cantonese training set, and verification done on both the English
and Cantonese set, respectively. Finally, the third experiment contained the use of a
pooled training set. In this experiment, the system were additionally trained with a subset
of both languages. The third experiment yielded a robust model that was less sensitive to
language changes in the verification step. In [67], Misra et al. proposed a novel phoneme
histogram normalization technique to match phonetic spaces of two different languages.
With a GMM-UBM setup, it was possible to increase the system’s performance when the
phonetic profiles of two different languages were made similar to each other.
Snyder et al. has shown that neural networks might be the next big contender to the field.
They have created an x-vector approach that has seen multiple iterations. First introduced
in [58] and improved with data augmentation in [7], the original version uses two PLDAs
trained from the extracted x-vectors. Both use the training data for the x-vector training,
but the second PLDA also trains on data that resembles the test data. The second PLDA is
thereby adapted to the test data. It used a standard i-vector approach for comparison and
showed a performance that outperformed the i-vector, yielding an EER of 11.9% where the
i-vector scored an EER of 13.6%. Data augmentation showed even better performance
for the x-vector. Data augmentation also showed improvement in the performance of the
i-vector. However, with the i-vector, the improvement was not substantial.

6.2
Channel mismatch
Channel mismatch encompasses a larger area of mismatches and can be quite com-
plicated. The mismatch can arise from any combination and are not necessarily limited
to mismatch of the recording device, network capacity, quality, noise conditions, illness-
related conditions, stress-related conditions and transitions between different media, to
name a few [69]. There exist various approaches to try to combat channel mismatch.
An approach is to use normalization to either transform the data [70], or to transform the
parameters of the used model.
The case of channel-mismatch exists in the speaker verification task and in any area that
uses speech. In [71], Ming et al. took an approach where they trained on data that had
added noise, to create a more robust system, thereby showing that data augmentation
is a viable approach to combat this problem. With data augmentation, an utterance can
be augmented with not only noise, but also with music, speech, and reverberation. This
approach was taken in [7].
Pelecanos et al. proposed in [72] to add a discriminative criterion to GMMs to reduce
handset mismatch. By performing a direct mapping between features from one channel
type to an undistorted target channel. The overall performance improved, but with map-
ping functions optimized using a full matrix representation, they degraded the performance
of the baseline system they tested.
In [37], the speaker model was a hybrid between a GMM and a Support Vector Machine
(SVM). Li et al. showed that the GMM-SVM was better to adapt to noisy conditions than
standalone GMM and SVM. The SVM performs well on small-scale data and is a robust
method, while the GMM performance is excellent on the speaker verification task.
Another approach is to add a VAD system to the front-end of the ASV system [9, 58],
attempting to filter background noise from the frames where there is speech, and remove
the frames where there is no speech. Adding a VAD system adds complexity to the setup,
but improves performance.
6.3
Focus points
From the language-dependency section, it is clear that the biggest challenge is the dispar-
ity between the languages used in enrolment and in test. The performance degradation
changes drastically depending on whether the languages are from the same language
family or not. Further of interest is the use of neural networks to model speaker features.
Since some of the models have shown adequate performance, it would make sense to
use one of these available models and build on top of it to enhance its performance.
The channel mismatch section shows that it is of interest to look into how data augmen-
tation of the dataset can improve the system’s robustness. From related work performing
data augmentation, it was clear to see a performance improvement, when the training
dataset had added noise.
The focus points should, therefore, be to see what impact the language discrepancy and
channel mismatches have when testing. It could be with the following steps

7.7
Data Augmentation
Data augmentation is a method to increase the size of a dataset. When a dataset is
deemed too small, this method is a possible approach. It is done by augmenting the
samples from the dataset. By using data augmentation, it is possible to increase the
potential dataset several times. In the case where the dataset consists of audio samples,
it is possible to augment it with other audio files, adding noise components such as noise,
music, or reverberation to the original audio file.
There are different ways to do this. In [7], it was done by adding random noise, music,
reverberation, or other speech onto the original audio file. Another way of augmenting the
audio can also be to slow or speed up the audio [91].
There exist publicly available datasets useable for the task of audio augmentation. The
Room Impulse Response and Noise (RIR) dataset and the MUSAN dataset, are two ex-
amples of such.
7.7.1
RIR
The Room Impulse Response and Noise (RIR) dataset, introduced in [92], contains room
impulse responses and isotropic noises from the Real World Computing Partnership sound
scene database. It is a dataset that combines several other datasets such as the RWCP
sound scene database and the Aachen impulse response database (AIR), to name a few
[93].
7.7.2
MUSAN
The MUSAN dataset contains music, speech, and noises [94]. It consists of approximately
109 hours of audio, that is publicly available. The 109 hours are split into 60 hours of
speech, around 42 hours of music and noise as the remainder. The speech part consists
of reading speech and speech from hearings, committees, and debates. The music is
from Jamendo, a free music archive and consists of western art music and popular genres.
Lastly, the noise part consists of noises such as fax machine noises, dial-tones, and rain,
to name a few.

Pre-trained models
The previous Chapter looked into different available datasets, and datasets used to train
some of the models outlined in this Chapter. This Chapter contains the different pre-
trained models that are available for public use. A pre-trained model is a model where
the weights and hyper-parameters have previously been calibrated by training. The cali-
brated weihts are then extracted into a set of parameter files. From this, it is then possible
to calibrate a replica of the network with these parameter files so that it has the same
performance as the initially trained model but without the need to train it. Using a pre-
trained approach dramatically saves time and makes it possible to deploy models where
the datasets required to train it are not available.
While this opens up for a whole new catalog of available models, using a pre-trained
model without validating its performance, can be a disadvantage. Without validating the
model ourselves, there is no guarantee that the models’ performance is as good as their
creators claim. A restriction imposed when searching for available pre-trained models is
then, that it has to use a publicly available dataset that are either open-source or available
for this thesis. Having such a restriction unfortunately significantly limits the list of models
available.
Luckily, the Kaldi toolkit for speech recognition [3] is available. The Kaldi toolkit features a
great catalog of pre-trained models, categorized by the datasets they use for verification.
These pre-trained models are created following a predefined Kaldi recipe. They contain
pre-trained models on the 2016 NIST SRE Test Set (see Section 7.1.10) (SRE16 hence-
forth) as well as a model trained on both of the VoxCeleb datasets (see Section 7.6). Each
dataset has two pre-trained models that follow the Kaldi recipe, where the first example
variant is a GMM-UBM model that uses i-vectors and a PLDA backend to score, while
the second is a DNN that uses X-vectors and a PLDA backend. The DNN originates from
[7], while the GMM-UBM is a generic model. The GMM-UBM is referred to as the i-vector
model, while the DNN is referred to as the x-vector model. An added note, however, is
that the SRE16 models contain two PLDA backends. One is trained on the same training
data as the i-vectors, while the other PLDA trained on an additional dataset that resem-
bles the dataset, used for enrolment and testing. The dataset used to adapt one of the
PLDAs is the SRE16 major, contained as a subset of the SRE16 dataset.
The i-vector and the x-vector have been trained on both the VoxCeleb datasets and the
SRE16 dataset, leading to a total of four different models. An i-vector VoxCeleb variant,
an i-vector SRE16 variant, an x-vector VoxCeleb model, and an x-vector SRE16 model.
The VoxCeleb models were trained on VoxCeleb1 and VoxCeleb2, which only contains
English utterances, while the SRE16 models were trained on datasets containing multiple
languages.
Since the underlying architecture of the i-vector models is almost identical, they could be
assumed to contain the same approach down to the individual steps. However, the i-
vector model used for the SRE16 dataset contains an additional data augmentation step.
Due to this, the four models have been split into four subsections to avoid confusion.
At the end of the chapter, a comparison of the models is made, and a set of them chosen
for testing of the new datasets, the SPARC dataset (Section 7.4) and the SWAN dataset
(Section 7.5).

8.1
i-vector VoxCeleb
The i-vector VoxCeleb model [95] is based on a prior example for the SRE10 dataset
[96]. It consists of a UBM using 2048 components, an i-vector extractor, and a PLDA
backend. It uses the VoxCeleb1 and VoxCeleb2 dataset, where the entire VoxCeleb2
and the majority of the VoxCeleb1 is used for training purposes. The training dataset has
a size of 7,323 speakers and 1,276,888 utterances.
The model uses MFCCs as its input, where the utterances were sampled with a sampling
frequency of 16 kHz, a frame-length of 25 samples, and everything below 20 Hz and
7600 Hz are filtered out. 30 Mel bins are used for the Mel filterbank, and 24 MFCCs are
calculated. Furthermore, the model uses a VAD to compute each dataset’s energy, to find
non-speech frames, and remove them.
The model training and scoring consist of eight stages. An overview is first given, and
then each stage is dived into afterward. The stages are
1. Prepare VoxCeleb datasets
2. Feature extraction of datasets
3. Train UBM
4. Train i-vector extractor
5. Extract i-vectors
6. Train PLDA backend
7. Do PLDA scoring
8. Compute performance of system
Preparation of VoxCeleb datasets
The preparation of the VoxCeleb1 and VoxCeleb2 datasets involves the creation of several
pointer files. For each dataset, a wav.scp file is created, which contains a list of all the
individual wav files available from the two datasets, listing the full path to the files. An
utterance to speaker file denoted utt2spk is created as well, linking utterances to their
respective speakers. Furthermore is a trial file created for the VoxCeleb 1 dataset, since
part of the dataset is used to test the system’s performance. The trial file contains a set of
trials with previously unseen speakers. Each speaker then has a set of tests associated
with it. The trials are split into two categories
1. Trials where the speaker is tested with an utterance of same class, e.g. a target.
2. Trials where the speaker is tested with an utterance of a different class, e.g. a non-
target.
The VoxCeleb1 training part and the entire VoxCeleb2 are then combined together, such
that they are merged into two datasets, a dataset for training and a dataset for testing.
Feature extraction of datasets
After the datasets have been appropriately split into a training and testing dataset, the
datasets are processed, and MFCCs calculated from them. This is done with a window
length of 25 samples and transformed using a Mel-spaced filterbank containing 30 bins.
24 of the 30 MFCCs computed are kept.
The MFCCs are processed with a VAD system, it applies voice activity detection on the
frames, to check for empty features and unvoiced utterances.

Training the UBM
The training of the UBM consist of two parts, first a diagonal UBM is trained. It applies a
cepstral mean and variance normalization sliding window, and uses the VAD computations
calculated in the previous stage. The diagonal UBM is first initialized with a means to
random data points and is then trained by doing several iterations of the Expectation-
Maximization algorithm.
When the diagonal UBM trained adequately, it is possible to train the full-covariance UBM,
used at a later stage. The full-covariance UBM is trained using the diagonal UBM, as it
speeds up the process. It uses the EM algorithm to train and is trained over 4 iterations.
Training the i-vector extractor
In the fourth stage, the i-vector extractor is trained, since the dataset is of a substantial
size, containing over 1 million utterances, the training of the i-vector takes a very long
time to do. Furthermore, the dataset consists of many short utterances that are harmful
to the training of the i-vector extractor. Thus, to reduce the training time of the i-vector
extractor and improve its performance, it only trains on the 100.000 longest utterances.
The i-vector extractor uses the full-covariance UBM to train.
Extract i-vectors
Once the i-vector extractor training is over, the next step is to extract the i-vectors from
the training set and the test set. When the i-vectors are extracted, the speaker-level i-
vectors are length-normalized, while the utterance-level ones are not. When the i-vectors
are normalized, it possible to use the PLDA backend since the data is now normalized.
Train PLDA model
It is now time to train the PLDA model, but before that, the mean vector is computed from
the i-vectors to center them. LDA is then used to reduce dimensionality on the i-vectors,
extracting the 200 most essential dimensions of the i-vectors.
It is then possible to train the PLDA using the centered i-vectors. It uses the speaker to
utterances pointer file generated earlier to compute the within-class and between-class
variances.
PLDA scoring
Step six is then to do the PLDA scoring, done with the VoxCeleb trials, scoring each trial
in the trial file, outputting a log-likelihood score of each trial. If the PLDA believes that
the speaker and the utterance are a match, the outputted log-likelihood score is positive.
Otherwise, it is negative. The PLDA does not know whether the input speaker i-vector
and utterance i-vector is a target or non-target; it only computes a score.
Compute performance of system
Once all the trials are done, the system is validated by computing the EER of the system.
The scores of each trial from the VoxCeleb trial is taken and split into their target and
non-target categories. The EER is then calculated by taking the FNMR and the FMR and
finding the point where the proportion of false matches is equal to false non-matches.
The i-vector system has a score EER of 5.329% on the combined VoxCeleb1 and Vox-
Celeb2 datasets.

In this step, a cepstral mean and variance normalization sliding window is used on the

In this step, the training data is split into archives. A speaker can repeat in the same

the statistical pooling layer is set, setting it to pool over a length of 10,000 frames, which
corresponds to 100 seconds. The minimum number of frames that it is allowed to compute

This step handles the training of the x-vector model. It calls the Kaldi DNN function with

estimates obtained with the trails file. They are split into target and non-targets, and the

9.5
RPCC
The RPCC feature extraction method is implemented in Python. It was not possible to
find any previous implementation of the method. The implementation uses SciPy [100]
and LibROSA [101] for Signal Analysis and Signal processing. The RPCC calculation is
done in nine steps
1. Calculate the residual signal r(n) = s(n) −ˆs(n)
2. Calculate the Hilbert transform rh(n)
3. Calculate the analytical signal ra(n) = r(n) + jrh(n)
4. Calculate the residual phase RP = Re(ra(n))
|ra(n)|
80

==PAGE:89==
5. Perform FFT on the signal
6. Calculate the magnitude spectrum of the residual phase
7. Apply Mel filterbanks to transformed signal
8. Take log of signal
9. Calculate the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) of the log signal
The utterance is loaded with a sample rate of 16 kHz. The utterance is around 10 seconds
long, giving more than 120k individual sample points. Graphs of the transformations have
been omitted, as the values range from negative to positive between individual sample
points.
The first step is to create the residual signal. This is done by using LibROSAs lpc package,
outputting ˆs(n). It is then subtracted from the original signal to get the residual signal.
The second step is done with the help of the SciPy package. It has a Hilbert Transform
available at disposal. rh(n) is calculated with this.
The third step is done by adding the residual signal, and the Hilbert transformed signal
together. The fourth step is then to compute the residual phase. This is done by taking
the real part of the analytical signal and dividing it with the analytical signal’s magnitude.
Step five and six can be done in one step by using LibROSAs Short Time Fourier Trans-
form (STFT) on the residual phase, giving a window length, a hop length, and the number
of FFT components used. The window length, hop length and FFT components are set
to
lwin = sampling rate
4
lhop = sampling rate
10
FFT = 4096
(9.2)
The magnitude spectrum is created from the result of the STFT. The signal can now be
split into Mel bins. This is done by using LibROSAs Mel filter function to create 30 Mel
bins, where the lower frequency bound is set to 20, and the higher frequency bound is set
to 3700 so that it resembles the MFCC configurations used for the x-vector model. After
the Mel filter has been created, it is applied to the magnitude spectrum signal.
The log is then applied to the Mel warped signal in step eight, and finally, the log signal is
transformed with the DCT to obtain the final RPCCs.
The algorithm for calculating the RPCCs has been created in two variants, one where it
takes in a Kaldi feature stream and outputs the RPCCs in the shape of a compressed
filetype that is used in Kaldi. The other variant is a Numpy array structure that is saved
into a file directory structure that resembles the original dataset file structure. The reason
this is done will be explained in Section 9.7 and Section 9.8.

9.7
Testing RPCC X-vector model
Two approaches were made to try fitting the x-vector model to use RPCCs instead of
MFCCs as its feature extraction method. Because the feature input method is changed,
the model needs to be trained again. The testing of the RPCCs is done with the VoxCeleb
model. The reason being that not all the datasets for the SRE16 model is available, but
both of the VoxCeleb datasets and the datasets for data augmentation are.
9.7.1
Implementing RPCCs into Kaldi recipe
The first approach involves adding the RPCC computation to the Kaldi recipe, so there was
no need to recreate the MFCC computation step. The MFCCs in the recipe is calculated
by a bash script called make_mfcc.sh. It calls the Kaldi backend compute-mfcc-feats
function and computes the MFCCs through this. To increase performance, Kaldi uses a
queue system, so it is possible to split the computation of MFFCs up into several jobs
that can be run in parallel. Each job performs two functions, the first is to calculate the
MFCCs, and then the resulting MFCCs are piped into a copy-feats function that copies
the features to their designated location while simultaneously logging the process to a log
file.
The compute-mfcc-feats function was substituted with the RPCC computation function,
so that the RPCC function was given a set of files it had to compute the RPCCs for, and
once these where computed they were piped into the copy-feats function.
By trial and error, it was found that some of the libraries used to develop the RPCC function
were not able to be run in parallel, resulting in errors during computation. So another
approach was taken.

9.7.2
Computing RPCCs before testing
The second approach was to pre-process all of the audio files before using them for test-

The benefits to this approach are two-fold: (1) all of the pre-processing is done before
testing the system, making it possible to cache them, and (2) the computed feature files
are much smaller in size. This approach, however, has a drawback, because the data
augmentation also has to be done outside of the testing stages. Due to time constraints,
data augmentation was omitted, as it was thought that a dataset size of over a million
utterances should be sufficient to train the model.
This approach, however, was halted as well. The script for the RPCC processing cannot
run on a GPU, which severely slows down the process. After running for three full days,
and not being close to done, another approach was taken. This included using a smaller
dataset, and an attempt to create a simpler model to validate the RPCCs.
9.8
LSTM
To validate the RPCCs, an LSTM model was created. The purpose of this smaller model
is to lower the training time to validate the RPCC method faster. The model is made in
Python using the PyTorch toolkit [103]. It draws inspiration from [104], and consists of five
layers. The layer structure is seen in Figure 9.1, it consists of 2 LSTM layers, a dropout
layer, a linear layer, and a log-softmax layer. The model is trained on the VoxCeleb1
dataset only. The reason only VoxCeleb1 was used was due to time constraints.
A dataloader class was created to fit the VoxCeleb1 dataset. The dataloaders’ purpose is
to shuffle the data and make the dataset iterable. Two dataloaders are used when using
the model, one for training and one for validation.
During the model’s training, it was found out that the VoxCeleb1 dataset was heavily
biased towards a small group of speakers. As the RPPC computation script does not
concatenate the speakers’ utterances into one single audio file, the lengths of the different
utterances could not be taken into account, only the amount of utterances available per
speaker. Insight into the dataset found the speaker with the lowest amount of utterances
had 45 utterances, while some speakers had over 1000. This meant that the system
would be biased towards speakers with a higher amount of available utterances.
It was required to normalize the dataset. A subset of samples would be taken from each
speaker and used, discarding the rest so that each speaker would have the same amount

83
==PAGE:92==
of utterances available. To find this number of utterances that would allow using as many
speakers as possible, but still keep enough utterances to train on, a score was calculated.
The score was used to find the best speaker size to utterance size ratio. By iterating
through sample size in ascending order, it was possible to see that using a sample size
higher than 45 utterances per speaker would discard nine speakers. Using 55 utterances
per speaker would discard 117 out of the 1211 speakers, so the best ratio had to be found.
Before normalizing the dataset this way, every utterance shorter than 5 seconds was
discarded. This was done to normalize the utterance lengths so that every speaker’s
utterance is precisely 5 seconds long. By doing so, the dataset is dramatically reduced.
However, this also removes a bias where an utterance could be 5 seconds long, while
another would have a duration of 2 minutes.
Then the number of utterances per speaker was normalized. This was done by finding a
point where every speaker could be kept, with as many utterances as possible. The point
was found to be 45, because higher than that, speakers were beginning to get discarded.
However, discarding utterances like this also meant that 94,147 samples were not used,
equaling to 63.34% of the dataset. However, it was preferred to keep as many speakers
as possible, so with the utterance length normalization and a fixed amount of utterances
per speaker, 54,495 utterances are left, where each speaker has 45 utterances.
The training of the LSTM was done with a batch size of 50 and trained over 50 epochs.
The training is split into two parts, a training part where the weights are calibrated and
an evaluation part where data not seen in the training phase is tested. At each phase,
the LSTMs’ hidden state is initialized to random values. A batch of speakers is then
loaded into the model. The model outputs the estimates of which of the speakers spoke
each utterance. The loss is calculated from the estimates and the actual targets, and the
weights of the model are calibrated accordingly.
In the evaluation phase, the test dataloader is used. This is to test the models’ capability
of performing on previously unseen data. If the training error goes down, but the validation
error goes up, it is a sign of the model overfitting the trained data. This is the case for
the current version of the model. Figure 9.2 shows the training and validation loss, where
the model has trained on 50 epochs. During the evaluation phase, the weights are not
calibrated. After each epoch, the training and validation loss is saved.
The model has been tested on the trial file of the VoxCeleb1 dataset. An utterance and a
speaker is given to the model, which in turn outputs a score. The score is then saved for
performance verification later.

10.3.1
Potential sources of error
After the testing was done, it was found out that the trial file could be biased towards
non-targets. The trial files for both the SPARC and SWAN datasets have been created in
the same fashion. Each speaker had their own utterances set as targets, while all other
available utterances were set as non-targets. The result is a situation where the majority
of the trials are non-target trials. That could lead the model to guess that it is an imposter
as default because previous sightings have shown that there is a higher likelihood of being
right this way.
Of interest is also the VoxCelebs overall poor performance on the SPARC dataset. The
performance obtained definitely hints at an error residing somewhere, considering the
worst performance is with English utterances. English should be among its best perfor-
mances, since that was the only language available during training. The reason behind
this could be because the enrolment is with session 2, which contain noise. However, the
VoxCeleb model contains a VAD that should be able to filter out some of this noise.
10.4
LSTM
The LSTM was created to test GLFCC and RPCC feature extraction methods since time
constraints did not allow to fit the feature methods to the x-vector model properly. How-
ever, the focus was first to get the model to work before implementing both of the feature
extraction methods thoroughly. As such, only the RPCC method was tested. The trained
model’s result was briefly shown in the implementation Chapter, but is shown again in
Figure 10.5 for easier reference. From the training loss and validation loss, it can be seen
that the model is overfitting. It is overfitting on the training data and is therefore unable to
work on previously unseen data accurately.
Overfitting can be due to several reasons. If the network has more parameters than the
number of observations available, the model remembers the observations without learning
anything. This could be a potential reason as to why the model is overfitting.
Since the model is overfitting on the training data, it is not possible to correctly validate
the GLFCC feature extraction method. Another potential source of error is the largely
reduced dataset used. An LSTM model requires a big dataset to train correctly, and during
the implementation, a majority of the dataset was discarded to normalize the number of
utterances for each speaker.

Conclusion
This thesis aims to introduce ASV systems and show how a language-dependency and
channel mismatch can impact their performance. The introduction gives insight into all
the different parts of the ASV system consisting of the front-end, speaker modeling sys-
tem, and back-end. The theory behind the front-end explains feature extraction methods
through different approaches available to extract speaker characteristics from an utter-
ance. There are different approaches to perform speaker modeling. This thesis describes
some of the common ways to do it. Finally, the last part of the introduction shows how to
perform speaker feature scoring in various ways.
Besides giving an introduction to ASV systems, this thesis also looks into several pre-
trained models. How they are structured, the datasets used for training, and how to use
them. A description of the datasets used for training is covered as well. Two brand new
datasets, the SPARC and SWAN, are introduced in this thesis and used for testing of the
subset of the pre-trained models. The results obtained further prove that language mis-
match has a high impact on systems performance. With English and Hindi in enrolment,
the SRE16 model obtained an EER of 5.73%, while English used in both scored 1.76%.
An article containing these results was created and published.
The tests of both of the x-vector models also show that using multiple languages during
training has a strong influence on the performance. The SRE16 achieved overall better
results than the VoxCeleb model. The reason behind, is probably due to the SRE16s train-
ing dataset containing multiple languages, as opposed to the VoxCelebs training dataset
that only contains English. Despite scoring better than the VoxCeleb model, the SRE16
models’ performance still varies greatly depending on the amount of introduced noise.
The performance variation is especially noticeable when using different channels. A case
of channel mismatch affecting the performance is seen with the SPARC dataset, where
English in both enrolment and test scored 4.21% with the OOD PLDA when iPhone 10
was the only channel used. On the other hand, enrolling and testing with iPhone10 and
Samsung S7 scored 11.17% showing a severe performance degradation.
Two feature extraction methods, the GLFFC and RPCC proposed by Wang et al. in [19],
has been implemented in Matlab and Python in this thesis. The RPCC is used as input fea-
tures for a small LSTM to test its potential performance for ASV systems. Unfortunately,
due to the LSTMs overfitting on the training set, it is impossible to verify its performance.
The future points of interest are a validation of the GLFCC and RPCC feature extraction
methods and improvements to the LSTM. If the feature extraction methods perform better
than MFCCs, it is of interest to see how well-performing the SRE16 and VoxCeleb models
can become, using the GLFCC and RPCC instead. The LSTM model can be drastically
improved. The first step is to remove the overfitting on training data, and then improve
the layer structure to see how well it can perform.

A
Anatomy of Speech
The speech processing for humans has been fine-tuned through evolution, making it pos-
sible to identify people based on their speech alone with high precision. Understand-
ing the auditory system and how it can recognize voices even before understanding the
speech content makes it possible to understand better and create an automatic speaker
recognition system.
A.1
The Vocal System
The vocal system is composed of several components that make it possible to produce
sound. Figure A.1 shows a sagittal section of the nose, mouth, pharynx, and larynx.
They make up the upper portion and the most significant part of the speech production.
The other part used for audio production is the lungs, but these do not have a direct role
besides providing the pressure difference to produce speech.
A.1.1
Trachea
The trachea, also known as the windpipe, connects the lungs to the throat. The Larynx is
positioned on the top of the trachea [24]. Figure A.2 and A.3 shows a sagittal section of
the upper part of the trachea.
A.1.2
Larynx
The larynx, also referred to as the voice box and the vocal folds (also frequently called
the vocal cords), are part of the larynx [105]. Figure A.2 and A.3 shows a cross-section
of the larynx, while Figure A.4 shows the entrance to the larynx, as viewed from the top.
Vocal folds
The vocal cords consist of twin infoldings of mucous membrane. These infoldings are
called the vocal fold and the ventricular fold, which are stretched horizontally across the
larynx. The vocal folds vibrate and can restrict the airflow coming from the lungs. Figure
A.5 shows the vocal folds.
The area above the vocal folds is called the glottis and is the starting point of control of
articulation. The glottis is the area between the larynx and the pharynx. When the muscles
of the vocal folds are tense, the more closed the opening of the vocal folds becomes.
When they are fully open, there is the least amount of resistance [24].
The resonant frequency of the vocal folds determines the voice pitch of a person.
A.1.3
Pharynx
The pharynx is the throat and starts at the end of the larynx. It is divided into three tunnels;
The laryngopharynx (located just above the larynx), the oropharynx (going into the mouth),
and the nasopharynx (going into the nose).
When the air has passed the vocal folds, exited the larynx, and entered the pharynx, the
articulation process begins. Often also referred to as the vocal tract. The pharynx consists
of different sections and depending on the amount of air going through the vocal cords
and the amount of tension in them, different sections will be used to produce a different
kind of sound [105].

A.2.1
Ear
The ear is divisible into three parts: the external ear, the middle ear or tympanic cavity,
and the internal ear or labyrinth [106].
The external ear consists of the auricula or pinna, and the external acoustic meatus. The
auriculas purpose is to collect the vibrations of the air that contains sound. The external
acoustic meatus then leads the air inward from the bottom of the auricula to the tympanic
cavity.
The tympanic cavity is an irregular, laterally compressed space within the temporal bone.
It contains several movable bones comprised of a chain which connects its lateral to its
medial wall. The purpose of the chain is to convey the vibrations communicated to the tym-
panic membrane across the cavity to the internal ear. The chain consists of the malleus
(hammer), incus (anvil), and stapes (stirrup). The mucous membrane of the tympanic
cavity is continuous with that of the pharynx, through the auditory tube.
The internal ear is an essential part of the organ of hearing. The internal ear, also referred
to as the labyrinth because of its complex shape, is connected to the biggest part of
the auditory nerve. It consists of two parts: the osseous labyrinth and the membranous
labyrinth.
The osseous labyrinth consists of three parts: the vestibule, semicircular canals, and the
cochlea. These are cavities hollowed out of the bone’s substance, and contains a clear
fluid, in which the membranous labyrinth resides. The semicircular canals are comprised
of the superior, posterior and lateral, and are all placed behind the vestibule. The canals
propagate the waves to the cochlea. The cochlea resembles a snail shell that receives
sound in the form of the vibration of the waves. Inside the cochlea spiral, thousand small
fine hairs reside, also called cilia, when there is vibration in the fluid, the cilia are con-
nected to the auditory nerve and transmit the motion signal they experience when feeling
vibrations.

Markov Models
A Markov Model is a stochastic state-space model that involves random transitions be-
tween states where the probability of entering a new state only depends on the current
state, and not any of the previous states [107]. The model possesses the Markov property
(see Appendix C), which means that it is a memoryless model.
The Markov models are categorized into four different groups of models depending on the
autonomy of the system and depending on the system information that can be observed
at each state. The groups can be seen in Table B.1.
Fully Observable
Partially Observable
Autonomous
Markov Chain
Hidden Markov Model
Controlled
Markov Decision Process
Partially Observable Markov Decision Process
Table B.1: Different categories of Markov Models
The simplest of the four models is the Markov Chain. It is both autonomous and fully
observable. It is not possible to modify the Markov Chain by actions of an outside agent,
as the controlled processes and all information are available at any state from the model.
If the model is still fully autonomous, but it is only partially observable, it is known as
a Hidden Markov Model. In a Hidden Markov Model, there exist underlying states that
are not directly observable, but can influence the observations done by the model. The
states are connected with probability transitions. The latent states in the Hidden Markov
Model possess the Markov Property, which leads to the observation states not required
to possess it.
If outside agents can control the system, then it is considered a Reinforcement Learning
model. If it is fully observable and controlled, then it is of the model type Markov Decision
Process. The last model type is when a system is partially observable and controlled by
outside agents. These model types are known as Partially Observable Markov Decision
Processes.
