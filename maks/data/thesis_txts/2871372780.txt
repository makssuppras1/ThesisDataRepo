Abstract
The Surface Water and Ocean Topography missions offers a unique opportunity to moni­
tor the inland water bodies with greater accuracy than the previous satellite missions. This
thesis has as a main focus to apply SWOT data to Danish lakes to check the reliability of
the data and the way that they perform with lakes taht have varying sizes and characteris­
tics. The investigation was based on WSE observations. A combination of satellite­filtered
WSE, gauge data, and meteorological parameters was used to evaluate SWOT’s accu­
racy. The filtering technique was based on two parameters: setting thresholds to variables
and applying statistical approaches in order to remove outliers and observation with bad
quality. Important parameters were added such as the rainfall data and evaporation to
see if each lake has a rise or a decrease in the WSE. All the results demonstrate that
SWOT is capable to capture the lake level variation with high correlation in comparison
to in­situ measurements. Overall, the findings highlight the potential of SWOT data for
reliable inland water monitoring and emphasize its importance for hydrological studies.

Introduction
Surface water bodies are natural or human­made features that provide essential water
resources to people and the environment. They play a major role in maintaining ecolog­
ical balance and supporting ecosystems. Any changes in terrestrial water storage can
significantly impact the hydrological cycle, disrupting the delicate balance that sustains
both human and natural systems. The planet tends to warp up due to climate change and
therefore the availability of the water is becoming more demanding. In order to reduce this
water resources should be created or even expanded. Regions like Africa are particularly
vulnerable, where the limitations in infrastructure and resources make it difficult to pro­
vide adequate water, especially clean and safe drinking water. Rising global temperatures
and extreme weather events can affect the shape of water bodies. As these challenges
grow, infrastructures should be made in order to ensure the long term availability of water
everywhere.
Recent advancements in monitoring technologies have transformed the way that water
bodies are being studied. Modern remote sensing and satellite missions gave the oppor­
tunity to capture and study the capacity of water bodies globally. These tools give detailed
information on the surface water dynamic changes, which helped in studying trends and
fluctuations. By combining observational data with satellite information, our understand­
ing of water resource systems is being increased.
Monitoring water bodies is crucial for measuring water levels, understanding storage pat­
terns, and analyzing their dynamics. Processes like evaporation can affect the water level.
Natural phenomena like droughts and floods can also change immediately the state of a
water body. Satellites can effectively measure changes in water volume giving valuable
insights into status of this water body. When disruptions to water bodies occur their im­
pacts on the surrounding ecosystem can be significant. By using satellite data this issue
can be prevent from further damage, which can be vital for both ecosystems and human
life.
Monitoring water bodies is necessary due to the limitations of in situ measurements. With
the satellite data, researchers gain a broaden view of the area of study. Although, in situ
measurements provide a good quality of data they can be very localized and a satellite
can capture water bodies that they might have not being measured before. Satellite data
allows researchers to observe lakes, rivers, and areas where water bodies form physi­
cal boundaries. Satellites use altimetry to measure height and optical or multi­spectral
imaging to estimate width, though all measurements include some uncertainties.
The SWOT mission represents a significant milestone in remote sensing and it was launched
at 2022. It is equipped with a Ka­band Radar Interferometer which provides high­resolution
measurements of water surface height and slope. The mission is designed to monitor all
types of lakes, with a focus on those larger than 250 m². SWOT offers global coverage
and addresses limitations of previous satellite missions. In particular, it reduces errors
and improving data accuracy.

2.1
The Surface Water and Ocean Topography Mission
The Surface Water and Ocean Topography (SWOT) mission has as a main purpose to
capture valuable data and insights on ocean dynamics and terrestrial surface water sys­
tems, including rivers, lakes and wetlands (Rodriguez et al. 2018).
The SWOT mission development was a collaboration of the Canadian Space Agency
(CSA), the United Kingdom Space Agency (UKSA), NASA and the French space agency
(CNES). It it was launched on December 16,2022 (Nair et al. 2021). The satellite experi­
enced a six month fast­sampling phase calibration for 6 months where the satellite orbit
the Earth in one day repeat cycle monitoring the same areas daily (Bergeron et al. 2020).
It orbited in a altitude lower that 857km.
Figure 2.1: SWOT Mission Timeline (NASA 2018).
This phase was focused on calibrating the satellite instruments and validating the accu­
racy of the data. The SWOT data was compared with in situ data in order the necessary
corrections to be applied for the calibration. After calibration the satellite enters the sci­
ence phase where it transitioned to a 21­day repeat orbit (Larnier et al. 2018). This orbit
performs a near global coverage and allow the SWOT mission to capture the water sur­
face elevation.
SWOT is a result of advanced technical design to succeed ambitious goals. It’s unique
configuration allows it to succeed near global coverage with accurate measurements. The
matrix below outlines all the core characteristics of the mission and it includes detailed
information about each parameter.

2.1.1
Instruments and Operation
KaRIn SWOT mission is equipped with a Ka­band Radar Interferometer (KaRIn), which
operates in the Ka­band frequency range with nadir incidence angles ranging from 0.6°
to 3.9°. The satellite has two antennas placed at either end of a 10 m baseline, which
together create a two dimensional swath of water surface heights. These swaths are
approximately 50 km wide and they are located on either side of the satellite, separated
by a 20 km nadir gap. To capture data within this gap, a nadir altimeter is used to provide
measurements for calibration purposes.
Figure 2.2: SWOT Coverage and Nadir Gap(Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and T. Pavelsky
2016) .

The operation works like this: one antenna transmits an electromagnetic signal toward the
closest swath, and the second antenna receives the back­scattered signal. The phase
difference between the transmitted and received signals is then analyzed to derive water
surface elevation. Each point on the Earth’s surface is observed from two different posi­
tions, allowing for precise measurements of water heights (Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and
T. Pavelsky 2016)(Rodriguez et al. 2018). The KaRIn operates in two resolution mode,
the high and the low resolution. The high resolution mode increases the precision of the
data with are in a larger area while the low resolution mode reduces the the amount of
data that needs to be send back to Earth. This means that it can select the amount of
data that it can receive.
Poseidon3C Altimeter is a dual band altimeter that works parallel with KaRIn, because
it fills the nadir gap which is not being observed by it. In particular, it operates with Ku­
band at 13.575 GHz and with C­band at 5.3 GHz, achieving by this high precision in
different conditions. Ku is the band that it is being used more and C band is for rough
weather conditions. Poseidon3C Altimeter is important for the calibration process (World
Meteorological Organization (WMO) 2024).
Advanced Microwave Radiometer or well known as (AMR) has as a main purpose to
minimize the effect of atmospheric water vapor (NOAA 2025).
2.1.2
Coverage Capabilities
The SWOT mission provides better coverage compared to previous nadir altimeters. It
achieves near complete coverage for latitudes between 78° S and 78° N. Higher latitudes
expect to gain more frequent observations with 10 or more revisits above 70° N/S every
21­day cycle. In contrast, most areas are sampled only once or twice per cycle, with some
regions in the equator experiencing even lesser.
Despite SWOT’s near complete coverage, gaps do exist. These are mainly due to the
orbit inter­track distance and the nadir gap which is most seen around the 25°S – 25°N
band. The largest gaps exist between 10° S and 10° N where undetected area reaches to
almost 7%. In some of the cases, this percentage can be higher. Considering that SWOT
operates with these constraints, it achieves a 90% coverage of all land and ocean areas
within its orbit inclination. This is integral, as it guarantees useful data for the majority of
places across the globe ((Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and T. Pavelsky 2016)).

2.1.3
Accuracy
Although SWOT data may not achieve the precision and accuracy of in­situ measure­
ments, it offers global observations of land hydrology, providing valuable insights into
previously unmonitored or sparsely studied regions. SWOT mission can measure ocean
surface topography with great accuracy and high resolution. The output of their observa­
tion is a two dimensional product.
With the above characteristics the SWOT mission achieves a the following goals:
Observed Areas:
• Water bodies with an area of at least 250 m2 can be observed, with the possibility
of detecting areas as small as 100 m2.
• Rivers with widths greater that 100 m can be observed, with the capability to detect
widths as narrow as 50 m.
Height Accuracy:
• Water surface height accuracy can be less than 10 cm when averaging over a 1 km2
region.
• Accuracy can be less than 25 cm when averaging over regions between 250 m2
and 1 km2.
Slope Accuracy:
• River slope measurement accuracy can be less than 1.7 cm/km when averaging
over a 1 km2 region.
Estimation Error:
• Water extent can be measured with an estimation error of less than 15% for evalu­
ated water bodies and river reaches.
These specifications ensure that SWOT provides the accuracy and spatial resolution re­
quired to investigate inland water bodies. This enables to track hydrological processes,
seasonal variations, and water level dynamics (Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and T. Pavel­
sky 2016).
2.1.4
Errors and limitations
Despite its capabilities SWOT mission has certain limitations that affect data accuracy
and coverage. These challenges are a results of atmospheric interference, limitations of
the instruments, the satellite’s orbit and challenges in processing the data can all affect
the quality and accuracy of the results.
Atmospheric Effects
SWOT’s data accuracy in being effected by the atmospheric interference and delay radar
signals. This id due to the water vapor, temperature, and pressure, as well as ionospheric
and tropospheric delays caused by vertical or horizontal variations in the atmosphere
(Boncori 2024).
Instrument and Roll Errors
Radar technology is important for distinguishing water from land, particularly in areas with
dense vegetation. However, even slight shifts in a satellite’s orientation such as roll errors
can distort water surface elevation measurements. Roll error refers to the rotation of the
satellite around its front­to­back axis, similar to a tilting motion.

These errors are especially problematic when monitoring narrow water bodies, where
small deviations in alignment can introduce significant inaccuracies. To minimize these
effects it is important a calibration to be applied and on going correction to ensure that the
data remain still precise and readable (Dibarboure et al. 2018).
Decorrelation
In some cases the radar signal loses coherence and this phenomenon is called decor­
relation. Decorrelation can reduces data quality and affects elevation measurements. It
may occur in both smooth and rough water surfaces. Numerous factors contribute to this
issue which are: strong winds which creates surface disturbances, low back scatter from
calm water, geometric distortions, horizontal displacement, temporal variations, thermal
noise and processing errors (Boncori 2024).
Nadir Gap
One of the limitations of SWOT’s KaRIn instrument is the 20 km nadir gap which is an
area directly beneath the satellite where no data are being collected. Although, a nadir
altimeter is being used but it is placed for the calibration. So areas directly under the
satellite’s path still experience lack of data (NASA 2018).
Temporal Sampling Limitations
SWOT mission has a 21­day repeat cycle and it struggles to capture short­term hydro­
logical events. This makes it less accurate for monitoring rapid changes in water levels
which can be caused from storm surges and flash floods. In these cases data from other
satellites with more frequent overpasses are combined with those of SWOT in order to
see these changes that have occurred (Biancamaria, Lettenmaier, and T. Pavelsky 2016).
Coverage Gaps
SWOT generally offers an extensive global coverage but they are some gaps that remain.
The 20 km nadir gap between swaths caused a lack of data in the area beneath the
satellite and they are also some other areas that they have not being fully observed and
they are with latitude 25°S–25°N and 10°S–10°N (NASA 2018).
Data Processing
SWOT has SAR . SAR is a interferometric aperture radar which collets the data with highly
complex technique and tremendous precision. It is required, in order the measurement to
maintenance their high accuracy, to be applauded calibration and corrections during the
mission to avoid errors.
Ka­band Frequency Sensitivity
The Ka­band frequency provides high resolution data, but it has also a limitation which is
the atmospheric attenuation especially in areas with high humidity levels. This humidity
can weaken the radar signal and minimize the measurement accuracy. This phenomenon
may occur in tropical and humid environments (Rodriguez et al. 2018).

2.2.1
Radar Altimetry
Radar altimetry has been a key technique for providing valuable information about the
solid Earth and ocean dynamics(Rodriguez et al. 2018). Its primary purpose is to monitor
important geophysical phenomena, such as Earth’s gravity field and ocean isodynamic.
By the time passing radar altimetry has became the main tool for monitoring sea surface.
Satellite Altimetry Measurement Principle
To accurately determine the water surface height h, it is necessary to know both the satel­
lite altitude relative to a reference ellipsoid H and the distance r between the satellite and

the surface. The relationship is defined as follows:
h = H −r

Here:

Interferometric Wide­swath
Interferometric Wide swath is major development in remote sensing because it gave the
ability to map the elevations of the water’s surface in two dimensions over large regions.
It has a larger coverage in comparison with the classic nadir altimetry.
Interferometric Wide Swath is a side looking aperture radar that cover the area at the right.
The side looking geometry it allows a accurate cross­track resolution and eliminates the
ambiguities between measurements on each side of the radar footprint. Each antenna
works autonomously and receives echoes independently (Boncori 2024).

2.2.3
Error sources
Wide­swath altimetry uses SAR interferometry to get high­resolution measurements of
water surface height. Although, they are numerous error souses which can affect the
accuracy of the measurements roll errors, phase errors and range errors are some of the
main causes of the interferometric error budget. To obtain the required level of precision,
these mistakes must be minimized.
Roll Errors
Roll Error are a quite important error which occur due to error in the estimation of the
satellite roll angle. This wrong estimation leads to a rotation of the entire swath and they
increase linearly with the cross­track distance and they affect greatly the wide­swath mea­
surement (Rodriguez et al. 2018).

Systematic Phase Errors
Unmatched differences in the two channels signal path lengths can result in systematic
phase problems, for example temperature delays in radar components can effect the mea­

can be used. The negative thing is that the phase difference drift over time and it needs
calibration on a regular basis. Another reason for systematic errors is the interaction
of the interferometer antennas with the baseline. This produces interferometric phase
screen which need to be adjusted with interferometric cross­over calibration(Rodriguez
et al. 2018).

nature of the radar systems, where the transmitted signal interacts with multiple scatters
withing the resolution cell. The intensity and the phase change from pixel to pixel due to
the random distribution of scatters, by increasing the strength of the signal the speckle will
not be eliminated. If the baseline grows then the variation will become more noticeable.
It can only be reduced with multi­looking techniques and high resolution systems.

3.1.1
SWOT Version C Dataset
The SWOT mission is designed to monitor surface water and ocean topography and to
provide high resolution elevation data.
Version C data is the most recent corrected version of the SWOT dataset with updated and
more accurate processing algorithms with calibrated phase data. They have expanded
the variables of the data with better resolution. They have increased the accuracy and
calibration and they have created a user guide for guideness. These data have being
available from January 25,2024 with repeat cycle 10 of science phase.
Generally SWOT Version C Dataset have many types of products. The two main ones
that could be used are the Pixel Cloud and the Raster Image Data. The Pixel Cloud Data
consists of individual pixels where each point represent a specific detection data rather
the Raster Image data are a structured grid format, where every pixel represent a point
of water or no water across a continuous area. For this study the main focus is on the
Raster Image data.
SWOT_L2_HR_Raster_2.0
One of the most advanced datasets are those for SWOT Level 2 High­Resolution Raster
Image Data Product C. The were created based on observation with the Ka­band Radar
Interferometer, and this product offers precise measurements for the water surface ele­
vation worldwide.
They were captured with a UTM projection, the SWOT_L2_HR_Raster_2.0 dataset is ac­
cessible in geographically with resolutions of 100 and 250 meters. The data is available
to users in netCDF­4 format and they are available at NASA’s Physical Oceanography
Distributed Active Archive Center (PO.DAAC). The dataset has 120 km swath width and
nearly global coverage, which spans 90°N to 90°S and ­180° to 180° longitude, make it ap­
propriate for tracking a variety of hydrological phenomena, including seasonal variations
in surface water features and water surface fluctuations. The dataset’s format makes it
highly suitable for a wide range of scientific applications. The 100m Resolution dataset
collects the details more accurate, whereas the 250m Resolution dataset is better suited
for large­scale analysis.

To understand better the water lake dynamics, Weather Data from DMI was added to
examine how the atmospheric conditions might affect the water surface elevation. The
data was downloaded through a script from KU which gave access to these high resolution
weather data. The resolution of the data that was selected was based on the grid. There

role in capturing meteorological patterns and which could affect the lake. The preferable
grid, was the one with step 10km x 10km, in order to have better resolution for the area
of study.
DMI has set weather stations across Denmark to capture meteorological data but some
stations may have incomplete datasets. In such cases, data from available stations in the
broader area are combined with the wanted one and with interpolation the occurred gaps
are now filled.

it was used the hourly data, which later became daily. The available parameters in the
dataset were the air temperature, precipitation, wind speed, relative humidity, radiation
and atmospheric pressure. The air temperature shows the warmth and the coldness of
the air while precipitation show the amount of water that falls from the atmosphere due to
the snow and the rain. Wind speed shows the movement of the air and humidity indicates
the amount of moisture that exists in the air, which can indicate evaporation. All these
parameters can show seasonal water level fluctuations.

Methods
This section presents the exploratory analysis, the applied filtering approach, the corre­
lation results and examine which lake water levels are influenced by rainfall and evap­
oration. In the exploratory analysis and in the filtering approach it will be analyzed and
evaluated the quality of SWOT data in comparison with in­situ data in order to find the
filtering that fits best the gauge data. Each individual value and variable will be examined
in order to select the finest way to achieve more accurate results.
Additionally, it will be described the correlation that it will be achieved in order to find which
lakes SWOT satellite can detect better. Finally, it is given the methodology for detecting
the increase and the decrease of lake water level.

4.2.3
Filtering Based on the water level
Global Median per Lake
In order avoid extreme values mess with the results, it was calculated the global me­
dian WSE for each lake to keep things consistent. Using the median made more sense
because it is not sensitive to outliers.
Remove values ±1 meter
Then, all the WSE values that were more than 1 meter above or below the global median
were removed for each lake. These points are just noise and errors and by removing
them the data became more reliable.
Apply 3σ filter
A 3σ filter was applied for each date. That means that they were removed all the WSE
values that were more than three standard deviations away from the daily median. This
helped clean up weird spikes on specific days and made the time series more stable.
Apply median and Mean
The final steps of this process, was to calculate both mean and median WSE of each
day and compare them with the WSE colored by each variable. To have valid results
everything was compared with in situ water level measurements. This comparison helps
identify any differences between the satellite and ground­based data, making it possible
to detect potential errors or biases in the dataset.

4.3
Validation and Correlation
To validate the filtered SWOT data a comparison was performed with the in­situ measure­
ments. This step was included to assess how well the satellite observations corresponded
to ground­based measurements.
Each SWOT observation was matched to the corresponding gauge station using a lookup
table that linked the in situ gauge IDs to the SWOT Lake IDs. After combing the two
datasets, the Pearson correlation coefficient was calculated for each lake in order to ad­
dress the relationship between the derived WSE values and the in­situ measurements.
This step ensured that the SWOT observations were reliable before continuing with the
rest of the analysis.
4.4
Water Level Decrease
In order to go a little bit deeper, meteorological data from DMI were added for investigation
to see how the rain can cause changes in water surface elevation.
The methodology behind it is the the data were collected for specific grid cell of which
covers each lake. These data that were downloaded has hourly resolution while they
were resampled to daily averages in order to be combined with the SWOT data and the
in­situ data.
For this analysis all the meteorological variables were included, such as radiation, wind
speed, temperature, humidity and rainfall data. Each of these variables was plotted along­
side SWOT and in­situ data to visually their potential influence on WSE variations.
The correlation between rainfall and WSE was examined by computing lagged correla­
tions for time lags ranging from 0 to 15 days. Seasonal correlations were also calculated
to explore how the relationships varied between winter, spring, summer and autumn.
For each SWOT pass, the accumulated rainfall between that pass and the previous one
was also calculated. This accumulated rainfall was compared with SWOT WSE through
scatter plots and time series to understand its direct impact on water level fluctuations.
As described in Section 2.3, all the equations used to compute the net water balance for
a lake are presented. With these equation it was calculated the evaporation and the lake
water level rise which in important for monitoring and predicting lake dynamics.
The daily water balance captures how precipitation and evaporation vary over time. By
summing the individual daily changes in lake level, the cumulative lake level rise can be
determined.

33 Danish lakes have RMSE values between 0.13–0.21 m with a correlation coefficients
greater of 0.90m. These results aligns closely with the one delivered from other studies in
other regions. In particular, in a a recent research at Lake Léman in Switzerland SWOT
shows the same range of RMSE values from 0.13–0.21 m with slight lower correlation
from 0.79 to 0.80 (Martinez, Dubois, and Brunet 2025). This difference, which is close to
–10 cm is due to outliers.
Another study, whish was applauded to Yangtze River in China showed that L2_HR_PIXC
product has RMSE values lower of 0.10 m, showing a slightly better precisionb(Zhang et
al. 2025. Although this study achieved better RMSE values, it was placed in a different
terrain where the hydrological setting differ significantly.
A broaden analysis was developer for six lakes by using L2_HR_LakeSP product with av­
erage RMSE of 0.22 m and a moderate correlation of 0.68 ( T. M. Pavelsky et al. 2023).
Both results are lower of the outcome of this current study. The more accurate filtering
in Danish lakes it gave more accurate results. In another study, in Australia they used
SWOT L2_HR_Raster product which showed WRMSE values around 0.10 m across var­
ious lakes showing higher accuracy across different water bodies (Thomas et al. 2024).
Generally, the multi­filtering that was applied in this study combined variable thresholding
like ±1 m WSE bounds and 3 σ outlier removal gave more reliable data and kept SWOT’s
consistency. This study showed that the reliability of the SWOT data to monitor the in­
land lake water levels but it also showed that with a accurate pre­processing, SWOT can
produce high­quality WSE time series.
6.4
Future Research and Improvement
They are numerous ways that this thesis could be expanded, due to to the nature of the
SWOT data, that are new and they are still unexplored. The following section address
so of the potential improvements or different scopes that may occur in order to see how
SWOT satellite monitors lakes from different perspective.
Pixel Cloud
For this type of thesis, the data that they were used was the
Raster product. To expand this process, it could be a good
idea to also apply the Pixel Cloud product to these lakes in
order to see the differences. The Pixel Cloud consists of in­
dividual points or pixels, while the Raster represents a struc­
tured, gridded format. In the Pixel Cloud, we typically work with
scattered data, and spatial interpolation is needed to create a
continuous map.
Dark Fraction
Dark fraction is a variable which could be further investigated
more in order to improve filtering. Some individual values, al­
though they have a high dark fraction, can also be beneficial for
the dataset. It would be better in some cases the dark fraction
to be re­examined.
Validation in larger
lakes
This study was applied only in Denmark. A good idea it would
be to be apply the same processes globally in order to evaluate
how lager lakes lakes respond to SWOT data. Of course it
would be expected to react better but it would be interesting to
be applied.

6.5
Conclusion
In this case study the the filtering techniques and the validation with the in situ data showed
promising results for the SWOT data. Still this mission it is its early stages and in some
cases some limitation might exist but the results and the resolution that it has indicate how
superior it is from the previous ones. The 21 days repeat time, the spatial coverage and
the spatial resolution are the main reasons why this mission have more advantages that
the previous ones.
Q1: How accurately can the SWOT capture WSE in lakes in Danish lakes and what
kind of filtering proved most reliable?
A comparison between the SWOT data with the in situ data showed that the SWOT data
in many cases combine well with the observed measurements of the lakes. At SWOT
a accurate filtering was applied in order to prepare the data for scientific analysis. This
filtering which involved thresholds in dark fraction threshold, WSE quality and a 3 σ outlier
removal in order to derive later the median value of each day. It is showed that for the 250

Q2: What kind of lakes can SWOT detect accurately?
SWOT can detect better larger lakes. In the larger lakes their is a finer resolution and
more data coverage that in the medium and the small one. The interesting thing is, that
although we would have expected the medium lakes to have lower resolution some of
them have better than those of the large lakes. In the medium size lakes, the size of the

lakes present the biggest challenges. While they may occasionally show good results,
the number of observations is often very limited in the derived water surface elevation.
Q3: How does meteorological data effect the lakes?
Meteorological data are responsible for the changes in the water surface elevation. All the
meteorological data were combined in order to extract the net water balance and to see
if the water level is increasing or decreasing. Rainfall, temperature, humidity, wind speed
and radiation were the needed variables for this analysis. The outcome showed that all
the lakes are experiencing a decrease and this is because the evaporation is stronger
than the precipitation.

Dark Fraction could re­examined. It also would be nice to see again the increase and
the decrease of the WSE and add other hydro­meteorological variables or use a different
model.
