Abstract
There is a need to develop a standardized and aﬀordable indoor environment benchmarking
tool in Denmark. In other countries, such tools have seen considerable market penetration
and are oﬀered on commercial terms, often by university departments.
The aim of this master thesis was to characterize the indoor environment and occupants
well-being and health in the Lyngby city-hall and Newsec Lyngby headquarters oﬃces.
In this process would a general protocol and a benchmarking tool be developed. The
characterization was archived through measurements of air temperature, relative air
humidity, CO2 concentration, illuminance, and sound pressure level.
The measurements in the study buildings tended to be high. The temperatures were outside
the acceptable range of 20-26◦C in approximately 66% of the time in the Lyngby city-hall
oﬃces and in approximately 96% of the time in the Newsec oﬃces. The relative air humidity
in Lyngby city hall were very dry with measurements within the interval of 24%-34%, while
the relative air humidity in the Newsec oﬃces fulﬁlled the recommendations. The measured
CO2 concentrations in the buildings were below the recommended maximum. Illuminance
measured in most Lyngby city-hall oﬃces was below 500lux in approximately 82% of the
occupied time. The illuminance measured in most of the Newsec oﬃces were below 500
lux in approximately 90% of the occupied time. The sound pressure level measurements
never exceeded 85 dB. Lyngby city-hall had medians within the interval of 36dB-46dB,
while the measurements from the Newsec oﬃces showed medians within the interval of
17dB-36dB. 58% of the occupants who responded to the Newsec questionnaire experienced
too noisy oﬃces every day for the past 4 weeks, which did not match with the low sound
pressure level measured and pointed to the measurements were unreliable.
The questionnaires showed a frequently discomfort in the perceived temperature, air
movement, and noise level in the study buildings. More than 90% of the occupants from
the Newsec oﬃces who responded to the questionnaire experienced multiple days with too
high temperatures, too little air movement, and too noisy oﬃces.
A benchmarking tool were developed and based on the result from the questionnaires and
measurements with a weight distribution of 50%/50%. The ﬁnal benchmarking grade for
Lyngby city-hall were 61%, high indoor environment quality. Newsec Lyngby headquarters
had a ﬁnal grade 56% which was within the interval of medium indoor environment quality.

Resume
Der er behov for at udvikle en standardiseret og overkommelig indendørsmiljø
vurderingsværktøj i Danmark. I andre lande har sådanne værktøjer oplevet en betydelig
markedsindtrængning og tilbydes kommercielt, ofte af universitetsafdelinger. Målet med
dette speciale var at karakterisere indendørsmiljøet og sundheden i Lyngby Rådhus
og Newsec Lyngby hovedkvarterskontor.
I denne proces ville der blive udviklet en
generel protokol og et vurderingsværktøj.
Karakteriseringen blev opnået igennem
målinger af lufttemperatur, relativ luftfuktighed, CO2-koncentration, belysningsstyrke og
lydtrykniveau.
Målingerne i studiebygningerne var tilbøjelige til at være høje. Temperaturerne var uden for
det acceptable område på 20-26◦i ca. 66% af tiden i Lyngby råhus og i ca. 96% af tiden på
Newsec kontorerne. Den relative luftfugtighed i Lyngby rådhus var meget tør med målinger
inden for intervallet 24% -34%, mens den relative luftfugtighed i Newsec-kontorer opfyldte
anbefalingerne. De målte CO2-koncentrationer i bygningerne var under det anbefalede
maksimum. illuminancen målt i de ﬂeste kontorer i Lyngby rådhus var under 500lux i
ca. 82% af brugstiden tid. Illuminansen målt i de ﬂeste Newsec-kontorer var ca. 90% af
den brugstid. Lydtryksniveau målt i bygningerne oversteg aldrig 85 dB. Lyngby rådhus
havde medianer inden for intervallet 36dB-46dB, mens målingerne fra Newsec-kontorer
viste medianer inden for intervallet 17dB-36dB. 58% af de brugerne, der besvarede Newsec
spørgeskemaet havde oplevet støjende kontorer hver dag i de sidste 4 uger, hvilket ikke var
i overensstemmelse med de lave lydtryksniveau målt, hvilket pegede på at målingerne var
upålidelige.
Spørgeskemaerne viste ofte ubehag i den oplevede temperatur, luftbevægelse og støjniveau
i studiebygningerne. Mere end 90% af brugerne fra Newsec-kontorerne, der besvarede
spørgeskemaet, havde oplevet ﬂere dage med for høj temperatur, for lidt luftbevægelse og
for støjende kontorer.
Et vurderingsværktøj blev udviklet som var baseret på resultaterne fra spørgeskemaerne og
målingenerne med en vægtfordeling på 50% / 50%. Den endelige karakter for Lyngby rådhus
var 61%, svarende til høj kvalitet af indendørsmiljøet. Newsec Lyngby hovedkvarteret blev
vurderet til en karakter på 56%, som ligger inden for intervallet for middel kvalitet af
indendørsmiljøet.

Introduction 1
1.1
Background
In Denmark and the rest of western world humans use as much as 90% of their lives indoors,
where a large part of this time is used in a workplace environment [40]. The wellbeing of
occupants is aﬀected by several indoor factors, e.g. thermal, lighting, moisture, mould,
acoustic, radiation, chemical compounds, particulates. Studies has shown that exposure to
these can cause both long-term and short-term eﬀects[39].
During the past 40+ years, surveys have shown that the indoor environment in buildings
aﬀect their occupants and that substandard indoor environment may impair well-being,
health and productivity, which ultimately may lead to considerable economic losses for
companies and, due to the scale, also for society. The surveys are often made in open
space oﬃces, as it is typically employees in these rooms who have problems with indoor
climate parameters such as temperature, air quality and noise.
Poor indoor environment can be caused by poor air quality or overheating in the oﬃce
space. Although several studies have been made, a speciﬁc cause for the employees’ health
symptoms, which often occurs at the workplace, has still not been found. This is due to a
fact that the measurements are made in an open oﬃce space with multiple occupants with
diﬀerent perceptions of the indoor climate [24]. Previous studies have shown that poor
indoor environment in oﬃces has an impact on employees’ work and concentration ability.
In some cases, it may even result in employees taking long-term sick leave [25].
Several study has been conducted about these problems throughout the years, which aim
to characterize or optimize the indoor environment. The understanding of the importance
of the indoor environment has lead to indoor environment benchmarking tools, such as The
NABERS Indoor Environment rating tool[26] and Totalværdi Indeklima from Innobyg[20].
There is a need to develop a standardized and aﬀordable indoor environment benchmarking
tool in Denmark. As such a tool can arise awareness to the importance of the quality of
the indoor environment in oﬃce buildings. Furthermore, a standardized and aﬀordable

The NABERS Indoor Environment rating tool is developed by the State of New South
Wales and Oﬃce of Environment and Heritage[26][16]. It is a guide/tool for facility,
building or oﬃce managers and tenants who are responsible for commercial oﬃce spaces
and wish to understand more about managing the indoor environments they control[26]
[16]. The NABERS IE rating is calculated from the overall scores awarded for the building
for air quality, acoustic comfort and thermal services and oﬃce layout benchmarked against
market performance. The tool aims to create three NABERS IE ratings. One that rates
the whole building, one for the base building service and one for the tenants. These ratings
are aligned with the responsibilities of each group, as each group have the control over
diﬀerent things. Following ﬁve parameters were used to rate the buildings:
• Thermal services; Measured by the air temperature, radiant temperature, relative
humidity and air speed provided to the space[26].
• Acoustic comfort; Measured by the level of noise disturbance in the oﬃce space
for tenancy ratings and the building’s ability to minimise external noise for base
building ratings[26].
• Indoor air quality; Measured by the eﬀectiveness of ventilation provided and levels
of pollutants (carbon monoxide, particulate matter (PM10), formaldehyde and Total
Volatile Organic Compounds (TVOCs))[26].
• Lighting comfort; Measured by the level of overhead light (horizontal illuminance)
provided at the occupant’s desk level to provide an indication of the potential for
eye strain, and therefore the occupant’s ability to work eﬀectively[26].
• Oﬃce layout; Measured by the occupant satisfaction survey for tenancy and whole
building ratings to assess the eﬀectiveness of the spatial arrangements of walls,
partitions, furniture and equipment in relation to ﬁxed elements like windows and
heating, ventilation and air conditioning[26].
The NABERS IE tool uses two measurement approaches. An occupant survey and a
collection of on-site data. These two approaches are used to determine the quality of IE
parameters as presented in ﬁgure 2.1. The weighting of each IE parameter is diﬀerent to
each rating types; Whole building, base building, and tenants.

2.3.1
Indoor air quality
The quality of the indoor air depends on the content of oxygen, moisture, odorants and
contaminants from humans, furniture, building materials and processes. Air quality is
important for achieving a comfortable and productive indoor climate. Poor air quality can
lead to symptoms such as eye, nose and throat irritation, headache, fatigue and dizziness,
but it can also lead to more severe and prolonged illness [5] [35].
The production of carbon dioxide (CO2) by occupants is proportional to the level of
8

Technical University of Denmark
activity [35]. CO2 is a gas that is found in the outdoor air at a concentration between
350-500 ppm, of which CO2 occurs in human exhaust air at a concentration of about 4%
[28]. Several studies have shown that high indoor concentrations of CO2 can aﬀect the
well-being and productivity of the occupants, which makes the CO2 level interesting in
most indoor climate studies. CO2 is a good measurable indicator of the air content of
bio-eﬄuents and of the pollution from persons in relation to the size of the ventilation [28].
Relative humidity is deﬁned as the ratio of the amount of water vapor in the air at a given
temperature to the maximum amount of water vapor that may be in the air at the same
temperature[14]. Therefore, the relative humidity depends on the temperature. The higher
the air temperature, the lower the relative humidity will be, at the same vapor content.
The relative humidity is measured in percent from 0-100% [4] [37]. Too high humidity will
provide breeding ground for house dust and molds, and if these grow, people in space may
suﬀer from symptoms such as e.g. headaches and irritated mucous membranes [39].
In this thesis is the indoor air quality characterized by the CO2 concentration and the
relative air humidity.
2.3.2

The thermal indoor climate includes thermal parameters such as air temperature, ambient
surface temperatures, the operative temperature, temperature variations of the air layers,
draught, the relative humidity [39]. The thermal indoor climate will be perceived as
comfortable if there is no thermal discomfort. There must therefore not be draught as
well as too low or too high air temperatures. In this thesis is the thermal environment
characterized by air temperature and relative air humidity.

2.3.3
Acoustics
The indoor acoustic climate has a great impacted on the well-being of occupants[30]. Poor
acoustic conditions can aﬀect concentration, sleep and stress levels [30]. Indoor acoustics
denotes the distribution of sound in indoor spaces. The most important concepts are
absorption, reverberation and speech intelligibility. Absorption is the ability of building
materials to extract sound from a space. It is closely related to the reverberation time,
which describes how long a sound "hangs" in the room after the noise source has stopped.
Reverberation time and absorption are also related to speech intelligibility, a measure of
how well desired sound appears clearly. The shorter the time the sound hangs in the room,
the clearer the desired sound or speech appears. It is important that the reverberation
time in the individual room is adapted to the application, as it otherwise causes impaired
speech intelligibility and higher noise levels [12][11].
The sound in an indoor environment can be characterized by the Sound Pressure Level or
SPL. The lowest sound pressure that can be heard by the human ear is called the hearing
limit and the highest that can be sustained is known as the pain limit. The pain limit is
one million times greater than the hearing limit. To avoid generating numbers in such a
large range, a logarithmic scale expressed in decibels (dB) is used. Here the lowest sound
pressure level is 0 dB (the hearing limit), while the pain limit value is approx. 120 dB.

2.3.4
Lighting
Lighting in oﬃce spaces is commonly a combination of daylight and artiﬁcial lighting.
Windows are the source to daylight in the indoor environment, which results in most
workplaces being placed next to windows. When the daylight does not reach the desired
lighting level artiﬁcial lighting is applied. The lighting conditions in an indoor environment
are characterized by several parameters with illuminance as the most commonly known,
because it is easily measurable. The lighting level is measured in illuminance (lux). While
it is important to get daylight inside the building it is crucial to avoid too high illuminance
level, which can result in glare and other side eﬀects. Insuﬃcient lighting in the indoor
environment can aﬀect the health of the occupant who can experience symptoms as fatigue
and headache[3].

Protocol 3
Throughout the project have a protocol been constructed. The purpose of the protocol is
to act as a general instruction for conducting the necessary building investigations leading
to the benchmarking of the indoor environment. The protocol consist 3 parts; the general
protocol which describes the steps of the building investigation, the checklist preparation,
and the questionnaire preparation. The checklists were used to characterize buildings and
study areas, while the questionnaire was used to evaluate how the occupants perceived the
indoor environment.
3.1
The Protocol
The protocol sets the structure of the building investigations and make it possible to
replicate the procedure. The protocol describes the process during the investigation week
and how to summarize the results. The protocol has been divided into 5 sections:
• Initial building visit
• Selection of study area(s) and monitoring locations
• Building characterization and monitoring
• Administration of the occupant questionnaire
• Sample and data processing
The time-frame for each building investigation is one week. Throughout this week the will
building be characterized and monitored, and the occupants will answer a questionnaire on
the perceived indoor environment and their health. The activities carried out during
building visits and their time schedule are presented in table 3.1.
This protocol is
constructed to have all building visits conducted during a week.In studies with where time
is not a crucial factor the activities can be stretched out over several weeks. The initial
building visits can be conducted the week before the measurements starts, and the deadline
of the questionnaire can be delayed to reach a higher response percentages. Note, if the
questionnaire deadline is delayed, the outdoor conditions can changes and inﬂuence the

3.3
Selection of Study Area(s) and Monitoring Locations
Monitoring will be done in a limit number of study areas, which all must be covered by
the questionnaire. The chosen study areas should be able to represent the most occupied
work areas in the building, as the measurements will be used to benchmark the indoor
environment in the building as an whole. If the the most occupied area are not taking
into account will the ﬁnal benchmarking of the building not be accurate. The preferred
characteristics for a study area are presented in list below. However, these can be ignored
if the building spaces does not reach the preferred criteria at the expense of the accuracy
of the ﬁnal benchmarking.
• The area must be occupied in the work hours during the study.
• Study area must not be located next to special areas (e.g., Printers and kitchen)
The selection of monitoring location within the study spaces will be done during the ﬁrst
day of the study week when the study areas has been selected. The monitoring locations
will be used for continuous measurements of the following indoor climate parameters:
• Temperature
• Relative humidity
• CO2-concentration
• Acoustics
• Light

placed at any ﬁtting location using the following general guidelines for indoor monitoring

Discussion 7
In this chapter the main ﬁndings and associated sources of errors are discussed. The
discussion is divided into smaller section according to the diﬀerent topics to make it
manageable.
7.1
Communication
Through out the project many companies was contacted and asked too participate in the
study. The process of ﬁnding a study building took all most two months. The building
contact persons who responded to the invitation to the project were in general interested.
They were aware of the common problems with could be found the indoor environment.
Multiple companies showed interests in the project, but were not able to contribute as a
cause of company policies. This process took longer and demanded more resources then
predicted. Because this part of the project had problems, the two ﬁnal contact persons
was asked about the communication and invitation to the project. The conclusion on the
response is that a more detailed invitation was needed. How many people will visit the
building, how mush equipment in gonna be placed, for how long are the equipment gonna
measure the indoor climate, and what is the companies role in the study besides providing
a building. These was some of the comments and wishes to the invitation.
Two diﬀerent companies and building was used in the study, with diﬀerent contact persons.
Both cases aimed to use the constructed protocol, but adaptation to the circumstances was
needed. The communication with the Lyngby city-hall was primarily done by visiting the
building and diﬀerent building responsible. Checklist was answered during building visits
in collaboration with the building service team. This process gave a good understanding
of the building but it reacquired multiple visits to the building. The process was handled
diﬀerent with Newsec Lyngby headquarters as the project was reaching the end. A bigger
part of the communication was done by email, such as the building checklists. The building
checklists was send to the contact person, who collected the needed data and returned the
checklists with the needed information. Each procedure had pros and cons, but as the

7.2
Outdoor Conditions
It was important to note the big diﬀerence between the outdoor conditions for the study
buildings. Lyngby city-hall was placed in the middle of Kongens Lyngby next to the
train/bus station.
The density of cars and other noise sources in the surrounding
environment was signiﬁcant higher in this area.
Cars, busses, and trains were all
contributing to worse outdoor conditions for noise and air quality. This was not a problem
for Newsec Lyngby headquarters as the building was located in a green area surrounded
by trees and nature.
The outdoor weather conditions changed during the measurement period. When the
measurements were conducted at Lyngby city-hall did no rain occur and the temperatures
were signiﬁcant lower. It was raining during the ﬁrst visit at the Newsec oﬃces, where
the measurements were conducted. Further more did the temperatures increased, and the
night temperature were approximately 5◦C higher.
7.3
Results
The measurements were collected over two weeks during the summer.
Because the
measurements only were conducted during this time period, the result are not representative
for an entire year. As the outdoor conditions changes throughout the year, the results
from the measurements and questionnaire would most likely change as well. The results
were heavily inﬂuenced by the current situation as the results were collect over a short
period. The sound measurements would be aﬀected a great deal if construction work was
ongoing during the sound measurement.
The measurements of illuminance in the two buildings were not conducted under the exact
same conditions. The layout the oﬃces in the two buildings were a contributing factor for
the placement of the measurement locations. All workstation in the Newsec oﬃces were
placed with a window facade on the side and a cabinet behind. This made it possible to

Further Studies
As the thesis is part of a larger project which, aims to build up a comprehensive database

Additional studies could be conducted to test modiﬁed protocols for projects with longer

Conclusion 8
Two buildings were chosen to participate in the project, Lyngby city-hall and Newsec
Lyngby headquarters. A generalized protocol was developed and used to measure ﬁve indoor
environment parameters and to conduct a questionnaire, which were used to characterize
the buildings, occupants and indoor environment.
The temperature in the buildings exceeded the recommended temperature interval. The
temperature in Lyngby city-hall exceeded 26◦C in approximately 66% of the time, in the
most critical oﬃces. The temperature in Newsec Lyngby headquarters exceeded 26◦C in
approximately 96% of the time, in the most crtical locations.
The relative air humidity in the buildings was for the most within the recommended range.
Lyngby city hall had very dry indoor with relative air humidity within the interval of
24%-34%. Newsec oﬃces in the interval of 37%-59%. The measured CO2 concentrations
in the buildings were below the recommended maximum.
The illuminance measured in Lyngby city-hall was below 500 lux in approximately 55%
of the occupied time in one of the oﬃces while the remaining locations measured a too
low illuminance in approximately 82% of the occupied time. The illuminance measured in
most of the Newsec oﬃces were too low in approximately 90% of the occupied time.
The SPL in buildings never exceeded 85 dB. The SPL measurements in Lyngby city-hall
had medians within the interval of 36dB-46dB, while the measurements from the Newsec
oﬃces showed medians within the interval of 17dB-36dB.
The questionnaires showed a frequently discomfort in the perceived temperature, air
movement, and noise level in the study buildings. More than 90% of the occupants from
the Newsec oﬃces who responded to the questionnaire experienced multiple days with
too high temperatures, too little air movement, and too noisy oﬃces. Of the occupants
responding from Lyngby city-hall, more than 50% sometimes had symptoms in the form of
fatigue, tiredness, dry and irritated eyes, headaches, and diﬃculty concentrating. Most
of the occupants in the Newsec oﬃces who responded to the questionnaire experienced
tiredness or fatigue, and diﬃculty in concentrating.

56% which was within the interval of medium indoor environment quality.
