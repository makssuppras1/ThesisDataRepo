Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to analyse the processes of coastal proﬁle evolution by build-
ing an eﬀective numerical model in Matlab®. The focus is put on cross shore sediment
transport, analysing the change in morphology that a sandy beach undergoes.
In particular, the numerical model simulating the coastal proﬁle evolution consists of
four main components: the breaking model, the undertow model, the sediment trans-
port model, and the morphology model. The three main components are interconnected,
meaning that a change in one formulation directly aﬀects the result of the other formula-
tions.
The model is initially calibrated for surface elevation and breaking position against the
Hybrid experiment, described by van der Zanden et al. (2019) [26], and the experiment
carried out by Scott et al. (2005) [21]. The Hybrid case comprised the ﬁnal part of the
SINBAD experiment [11], carried out in the “Canal d’Investigació i Experimentació Marí-
tima” (CIEM) in Barcelona for a mobile bed subject to regular waves. The experiment
was carried out under bichromatic wave conditions. The experiment described by Scott
et al. (2005) was carried out for both regular and irregular wave conditions in the Oregon
State University’s O. H. Hinsdale Wave Research Laboratory, with the bathymetry mod-
elled in scale with the average geometry observed on 11 October 1994 of the DUCK94
ﬁeld experiment [21].
The model is then calibrated for the undertow velocity proﬁle for the Hybrid experiment
[26]. The aim is building an undertow model comprised of a linear and an hyperbolic
tangent formulation of the eddy viscosity, connected by a weighting function, that works
well for a wide range of cases.
In order to check the validity of the numerical model, two experimental cases of the SANDS
project are considered for the morphology test. The comparison is carried out for the
iii
==PAGE:9==
medium scale CIEM ﬂume in Barcelona and large scale GWK ﬂume in Hanover [6]. The
two cases comprise both the erosive and accretive conditions, with the erosive condition
being characterised by stronger wave conditions that lead to an oﬀshore movement of the
sand bar (eroding beach), while the accretive condition sees an onshore movement of the
sand bar (accreting beach). The model is tested on both conditions. The calibration
enabled to choose for a sediment transport ﬁnal formulation and for a value for its free
parameters.
After the model validation, two nourishment schemes are tested on a wave climate se-
quence of alternating accretive and erosive conditions. The results are then compared
and discussed.

1.0.1
Overview of the Coastal Processes
Numerical and experimental studies of the coastal morphology evolution and its transport
processes have signiﬁcantly advanced the understanding of this topic over the last decades
[28]. The complexity in its study lies in the interlinked processes that deﬁne the cross-shore
beach behaviour, as waves, currents, sediment transport and bathymetry strongly depend
on one another. In fact, when the bathymetry changes as a consequence of a change
in the sediment ﬂux gradients, wave and current parameters undergo a modiﬁcation too
[18]. Due to this reason, the coastal processes are still not fully deﬁned and understood
due to the interaction of many interlinked aspects such as wave forcing, set-up gradients,
turbulent eddy viscosity, bottom roughness, and wind [17]. This thesis work will focus on
short-period waves (T < 30 s), generated in nature by the action of wind [23].
The coastal processes start in deep waters, where waves are generated by wind forces
over a distance called fetch. When winds blow over a certain time tdur and fetch distance
X, waves develop and propagate towards the coast. This typically happens when wind
velocity exceeds a critical value of about 1 m/s near the water surface [23]. If the wind
blows for a suﬃcient amount of time and it is not limited by the fetch distance, the fully
developed sea condition is reached. Deep waters are deﬁned by the relation kh ≥π, where
h is the water depth and k is the wave number, deﬁned as k = 2π/L, where L is the wave
length. Thus, the wave number k oﬀers a spatial quantiﬁcation of a given wave. While
propagating towards shallow waters (where approximately kh ≤π/10), waves undergo a
process called shoaling, which is characterised by a decrease in the wave celerity c and
wave length L and an increase in wave height H. This process is deﬁned by neglecting

energy losses that will occur in the boundary layer, assuming that the energy ﬂux is
conserved up to the point of incipient breaking [22].
As waves propagate from the oﬀshore deep water region onto beaches, their approximately
sinusoidal linear shape transforms into increasingly non-linear orbital motion, due to the
ampliﬁcation of the higher harmonics [19]. The particle orbital motion beneath the waves
changes from a circular path in deep water, to a more elliptic shape as higher interaction
with the bottom occurs moving towards shallower waters (see Figure 1.1). The particle
velocity vector follows the direction of wave propagation under the wave crest, while the
opposite occurs under the wave trough [23]. The particle velocity, u, is usually much
smaller than the wave celerity, c, with the exception of the breaking waves.
One measure of increasing non-linearity as waves propagate in shallow waters is skewness.
This asymmetry about the horizontal axis consists of shorter, higher crests and longer,
shallower troughs [19].
Another measure of wave non-linearity is velocity asymmetry. As waves propagate further
onshore, the asymmetry becomes predominant about the vertical axis. Waves tend to
increasingly pitch forward, showing a steep front face and a more gradual rear face [19].
Both skewness and velocity asymmetry generally comprise the onshore sediment transport
Figure 1.1: Wave propagation towards shallow waters [24]
component [19]. The region in which waves assume a velocity asymmetric shape is called
the surf zone and it is where several complex morphodynamic wave-beach interactions
occur. The surf zone is the shallow water area where waves break. Here, energy conversion
from ordered wave energy to turbulence and heat takes place [7]. The velocity asymmetry
increases considerably due to the process of shoaling, with increasing wave heights and
decreasing wave lengths and celerity. Due to the wave steepness becoming larger as the
depth becomes shallower, a point of instability is reached, which is manifested in the
breaking process. In fact, the process of shoaling, if purely mathematically analysed,

1.0.2
Changes in Shoreline position and Coastal Erosion
The shoreline retreat poses a great deal of problems to coastal communities, as it implies
a loss of beach and a setback of the coastline [14]. Changes in shoreline positions are
mainly due to three mechanisms [14]:
1. Changes in shape of the coastal proﬁle after storm surges and storm waves. This is
known as acute erosion.
2. Changes in volume of the coastal proﬁle due to a gradient in the littoral drift. This
is known as chronic erosion.
4
==PAGE:26==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
3. A setback of the shoreline position due to sea level rise. The higher mean sea level
result in the beach proﬁle readapting to a new equilibrium shape.
These three processes are often combined in real life. A big distinction shall be made
between a change in volume and a change in shape. The former is caused by a deﬁcit
between the supply and loss of sand in a coastal cell along the shoreline and characterised
by the so-called “long-shore transport”. It goes by the term chronic erosion and it is
generally seen in the erosion of the seabed and the coastal erosion [14]. Instead, the latter
is caused by extreme events, such as storm surges and strong waves, which result in an
oﬀ-shore transport of sediment. This leads to an oﬀ-shore breaker bar and characterised
by so called “cross-shore transport”. It goes by the term acute erosion. This process is
reversible, as accretive conditions generally occur when the weather stabilises again after
these extreme events, leading to an on-shore migration of the deposited sediments and a
new steepening of the beach [14].
In addition, it should be stated that the acute erosion poses less threats to the vulnerability
of the beach, than the erosion caused by a loss of beach volume.
In fact, when the
acute erosion occurs, the cross-shore beach proﬁle ﬂattens out, leading to an oﬀ-shore
migration of the breaking point and subsequently to more wave energy being dissipated
before reaching the coast [14]. This makes the beach more resistant to further erosion
and easily rebuildable by onshore transport during mild wave climates. However, very
extreme events can transport the sediments so far oﬀ-shore that the process is irreversible,
or very slow to return to its original shape at the very least [14].
Erosion is a very common phenomenon occurring in many coastal areas in the world. At
present, sources from the Danish Coastal Authorities (DCA) show that portions of the
west coast of Denmark would likely recede up to 2 to 8 meters per year, if no coastal
protection measurements would be applied to the area. Human causes to coastal erosion
mainly fall in the category of chronic erosion, as structures built on rivers, ports, and
coastlines may block the natural supply of sediments to the beach. In addition to the
usual human causes, climate change has started to pose threats that will have to be
addressed in many coastal areas worldwide. In particular, sea level rise will accelerate the
erosion of already retreating shorelines. In addition, the increasing probability of extreme
meteorological events caused by climate change will lead to higher storm surges, more
violent wave climates and possible changes in wave directions as well [14]. These issues
will have to be addressed with countermeasures in order to limit the coastal damages.

1.0.3
Coast and Shore Protection
In order to address the erosion problem, multiple countermeasures are used in the engi-
neering practise. They are subdivided into coast protection and shore protection.
The term coast protection refers to measures used against coastline retreat, hence pro-
tecting urban settlements and infrastructure [14]. The most common types are seawalls
and revetments.
Shore protection refers to measures aiming to combat the shoreline retreat and has the
nourishment strategy as the most common example [14].
Mixed coastal and shore protection measures instead are structures used to directly protect
the coast, while at the same time maintaining the nourishment volume in place [14]. The
most common types include groynes and detached breakwaters.
A possible alternative classiﬁcation consists of the distinction between hard and soft mea-
sures. Hard protections involve the presence of structures, such as groynes and detached
breakwaters. Soft protections instead do not involve hard structures and include sand
nourishment as the most common engineering solution.
The study and design of hard structures is not part of this thesis work, and will not hence
be discussed further. Instead, the focus will be put on sand nourishment, as the ﬁnal step
of this work will consist in the modelling of a cross-shore morphology after a nourishment
scenario was put in place.
Sand nourishment consists in the placement of large amounts of sand on speciﬁc areas
of the beach proﬁle in order to reduce the speed and the impact of erosion. It does not
have an absolute permanent impact on the environment like groynes do, which can trap
the long-shore sediment transport of sand, causing a sediment deﬁcit downstream. In
addition, hard structures could prevent water exchange in the bays that are formed be-
tween subsequent structures, decreasing the water quality of sea areas used for recreational
purposes.
Sand nourishment strategies consist in applying a volume of sand on the beach, hence
altering the sediment budget over a certain stretch of the shore.
However, since the
cause of erosion is not stopped, new volumes of sand must be periodically added to the
beach. This means that sand nourishment does not represent a permanent solution for
the shoreline erosion problem, but instead requires a long-term maintenance eﬀort [14].

Model Description
This chapter provides a description of the numerical model built in Matlab ®. It collects
the theory used, together with the description of new formulations implemented in the
code. In addition, it includes the parametrization of some coeﬃcients, added to the code
after a calibration made on experimental results.
Contributing to the overall model and project aims, this chapter will discuss and incorpo-
rate several criteria within the numerical model. These include deep water linear theory,
a breaking model, an energy dissipation model, an undertow model, a sediment trans-
port model (considering both phenomena pre-wave breaking and in the surf zone), and a
morphology section.

2.6
The Undertow Model
The undertow model is developed in the surf zone, which extends from the incipient wave
breaking until the limit of drawdown [22]. The swash zone, deﬁned in Section 2.5 is hence
not considered in the undertow model.
The surf zone is subdivided into an “outer” region and an “inner” region. This concept
was ﬁrst introduced by Svendsen et al. (1978). He deﬁned the outer region as a transition
region, where a rapid transformation of breaking wave proﬁles takes place [16]. The inner
region instead sees the presence of stable bore-like waves that propagate onshore following
the dynamics of wave breaking, before entering the swash zone [16]. Due to the process
of wave breaking, the turbulence that is produced in this zone leads to a signiﬁcant rate
of sediment transport and change in morphology. A graphic representation of subdivision
of the surf zone is given in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Nearshore region subdivision into shoaling region, outer surf zone, inner surf
zone and swash zone [8].

2.9
The Sediment Transport Model
The sediment transport mechanisms are often subdivided between a bed load and a sus-
pended load model in literature. These two processes (and hence their distinction) are
still lacking a common deﬁnition in literature. Roughly speaking, the bed load is the part
of the total load that is in an almost continuous contact with the bed during the grains’
movement. Hence, the main driving mechanism to determine the bed load is found in the
eﬀective bed shear stress acting on the grains’ surface [7]. The suspended load, instead,
is the part of the total load that is put into suspension by the ﬂuid turbulence and is
moving without the constant contact with the bed [7].
A general way of diﬀerentiating between the bed load and the suspended load is made by
introducing the concept of reference level b. The reference level is deﬁned as the distance
from the bed above which the sediment transport is deﬁned as suspended load.
The
value the reference level takes is not commonly deﬁned in literature. In the experimental
work of SINBAD, carried out in the CIEM wave ﬂume at the Universitat Politècnica de
Catalunya in Barcelona, the reference level was assumed constant and equal to b = 0.005
m [27]. In the work of van Rijn (2007 a,b), the reference level is a roughness-dependent
elevation, taken as a function of the current and wave-related bed roughness [29] [30]. In
the present work, the bed level is assumed as a linear function of the grain size d and is
taken as b = 75d. It must be pointed out that the model simpliﬁes the reality by assuming
all grains to have a diameter equal to the medium sand diameter d50 (constant uniform
granulometry distribution).

Chapter 4
Nourishment Scenarios
In this Chapter, two nourishment scenarios are analysed. Validating the model on the
morphology data from the SANDS experiment in Chapter 3 showed that the model per-
forms well in the short term, but fails to reach an equilibrium in the long term. This
model fault is very common in coastal modelling, and shall not be seen as an absolute
failure of the model. Instead, the model proved to be capable of moving sand onshore in
the modelled laboratory accretive cases, while many models fail in this prediction. It was
hence chosen to model the nourishment scenarios for a relative short duration, abandon-
ing the idea of modelling the coastal processes for a time scale of months, and focusing on
a time scale of days. In addition, the model has not been tested against complex coastal
processes, such as the presence of a berm and the sediment transport happening on its
lee side. The simplicity of the present model may fail to capture these peculiarities.
It is for this reason that the results presented in this chapter shall be taken with critical
thinking, focusing more on the comparison between the nourishment scenarios rather than
an absolute validation of the performance of the single strategies.
It was chosen to model two nourishment strategies, starting from the end stage of the
accretive test in Hanover. The two nourishment schemes are presented in Figure 4.1.
The two nourishment strategies were taken from Figure 1.4, with the ﬁrst one being
categorised as foreshore (beach) nourishment and the second as shoreface nourishment.
The two curves are kept geometrically simple and are, of course, a simpliﬁcation of reality.
The two nourishments were modelled so to have the same volume, with the foreshore
nourishment having V = 14.76 m3/m, and the shoreface nourishment having V = 14.75
m3/m. This way, the results on the coastal morphology can be directly compared.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
A simple numerical model developed in Matlab®was calibrated and tested against mea-
surement data from diﬀerent laboratory experiments. Good calibration results were found
for the surface elevation and breaking model, with the computed surface elevation follow-
ing precisely enough the measurement points, showing a good validity of the breaking
formulation and energy dissipation model used.
The undertow velocity proﬁle that resulted from the calibration work, showed a good
agreement with almost all the experimental values from the Hybrid experiment, with
the exception of the region immediately following the wave breaking position. In the
ﬁrst part of the surf zone, in fact, the computed undertow velocity was overestimated.
The results, calibrated by tuning the free parameters in the eddy viscosity and weighting
function formulations, overall proved the validity of the formulations used. Nonetheless,
improvements could possibly be made to the ﬁrst eddy viscosity formulation to better
match the velocity proﬁle immediately after the breaking point.
The sediment transport and morphology validation showed a good prediction of the
breaker bar position and height in the erosive condition cases of both SANDS experi-
ments. Despite a slight delay in the oﬀshore movement of the breaker bar’s position,
compared to the experimental data, the model proved to be able to correctly predict the
oﬀshore bar migration for the analysed laboratory experiment duration. The validity of
the total load formulation, before the breaking point, and of the bed load formulation, in
the surf zone, was particularly proven in the accretive case. The model was in fact able to
predict the onshore movement of sand and the development of the bar, starting from the
end of the erosive experimental case. However, the model showed an inability in reaching
an equilibrium in the long run, with the modelled breaker bar never stopping its oﬀshore
62
==PAGE:84==
CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION
movement. While the model’s validity was hence proven for time scales of hours/days
(modelling a time frame of days with a computational time of a few minutes), this cannot
be said for longer time frames.
It is for this reason that the two nourishment schemes were chosen to be run for a relative
short time, abandoning the idea of running the morphology for a time scale of months.
The two end stage proﬁles were then compared and analysed for their stability and their
recreational purposes. Future investigative works could improve the model so to reach a
morphology equilibrium and test it against more complex coastal processes. As a result,
longer time frames and other nourishment strategies could be examined.
