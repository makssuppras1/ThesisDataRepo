Abstract
This study investigates, through IDA ICE simulations and diﬀerent equations, the major electricity and
cost diﬀerences of greenhouses and growth chambers for growing medicinal cannabis. An entire buildings
indoor climate, energy usage and costs are complex and therefore some details are vague, and a lot of
assumptions are made, this however makes it possible to create a comparison of the buildings which is
accurate based on those assumptions.
Because the study is entirely reliant on simulations and equations, an almost complete walkthrough of the
method has been made with regards to the vegetative phase of the growth. This walkthrough also goes into
small investigations in regards to diﬀerent control strategies and tries to ﬁx some of the inherent assumptions
in an IDA ICE simulation that might have a large eﬀect in a greenhouse.
There are also other small investigations in terms of diﬀerent control strategies, to observe what diﬀerent
strategies meant for the building as a whole, and its costs. One of the investigations show that it is not
worth utilizing ventilation for cooling purposes in a zone that is supplied with CO2, another investigation
tried to explore shading controls to limit the daylight that the zone received when the plant has a risk of
getting too much light.
Cannabis growth is assumed to undergo three main stages of growth, starting at the stickling phase
which then moves onto the vegetative phase and ends in the ﬂowering phase. Results showed that there were
both diﬀerences between the building and the stages, indicating that the strengths and weaknesses of either
building type made them viable options. The greenhouse showed a trend of requiring less energy and thus
a lower operational costs of about 15.6% compared to the growth chamber. The growth chamber showed a
much more stable indoor climate, which gives more security and control over the product.

og pris forskelle af væksthuse og lukket væksthaller til groning af medicinsk cannabis. En hel bygnings
indeklima, electricitets forbrug og priser er et meget complex studie, og derfor er der nogen detaljer som ikke
er fuldstændig deﬁneret, og der er taget nogen nødvendige antagelser, som tilgengæld gør det muligt at lave
en fuld sammenligning mellem bygningerne baseret p˚a de taget antagelser.
Fordi studiet er lavet med simuleringer og ligninger er der lavet en gennemgang af metoden brugt til
at undersøge bygningerne for den vegetative fase af groningen. Denne gennemgang laver ogs˚a sm˚a un-
dersøgelser ang˚aende forskellige kontrol strategier og prøver at løse nogen af de underliggende antagelser
som simuleringsprogrammet IDA ICE tager, som har en relativ stor eﬀekt i væksthuse.
Der er ogs˚a andre sm˚a undersøgelser i forhold til forskellige kontrol strategier, som undersøger hvad de
forskellige strategier betyder for bygningen som en helhed. En af undersøgelserne viser at the ikke er eﬀektivt
at udnytte ventilation til at køle en zone som der bliver tilføjet en forhøjet mængde af CO2 til. En anden
af undersøgelserne undersøger kontrol af skyggelægning i perioder hvor planten har en risiko for at f˚a for
meget lys.
Cannabis er antaget at have tre gronings faser som starter i stickling fasen, og g˚ar derefter til den

den lukket væksthal. Væksthallen viste sig tilgengæld at have et meget mere stabilt indeklima, som ville
resultere i mere sikkerhed og control over produktet.

Introduction
The cannabis industry has grown signiﬁcantly in recent years all over the world, including in Denmark. The
main cause for this growth is the recent legalization of medicinal cannabis and the wider use of THC and
CBD products in the medicinal industry.
Cannabis in itself is a plant that grows exceedingly well in many climates and is often termed a weed
in some cultures. The speciﬁc demands that are set on medicinal products including medicinal cannabis
results in the need to grow it in a controlled climate that can consistently provide uniform concentrations of
chemical by-products in the plant. Because of the demands regarding medicinal cannabis, it is necessary to
grow it indoors, the most common industry standards for this is either a greenhouse or a growth chamber.
Greenhouses often have the advantage of utilizing sunlight to help plants undergo photosynthesis, whereas
the light would otherwise have to be supplied through lamps. Greenhouses also usually have eﬃcient cooling
by opening the windows in the roof, which is necessary because of the high solar irradiance and thus heat
entering the greenhouse. The usage of these roof windows is however severely limited and eﬀectively removed
when having to follow the requirements and demands for growing medicinal cannabis.
Closed growth chambers do not allow sunlight access and therefore has to spend extra energy on lighting
to compensate. Cooling also is not much simpler than for greenhouses as there are no windows and therefore
no possibility of natural venting. A closed growth chamber will however be much better insulated, and the
micro control on the climate would be much more precise and better ﬁt for the medicinal industry which is
very demanding on product speciﬁcations.
Objective
This project will go into details of a greenhouse and a closed growth chamber, simulate their energy usages to
get certain indoor climate values well ﬁt for growing cannabis, and most importantly, the diﬀerences between
the buildings will be speciﬁcally investigated and deﬁned around the context of medicinal cannabis.
The investigated indoor climate parameters will be light as PPFD (Photosynthetic Photon ﬂux density),
CO2, temperatures and humidity. Therefore, there are also a lot of important values not considered in this
project, such as water and nutrients provided to the plants. A speciﬁc evaluation of total operational costs
is not done, and instead a speciﬁc estimation of the cost of the considered parameters.
Literature in general around a complete evaluation of cannabis growth based on a complete set of indoor
variables is very sparse, therefore, changes in the climate are observed, but are not reﬂected upon in terms
of the potential diﬀerence in product it might resolve in.
In the following chapters, ﬁrst a literature study is made to deﬁne the projects speciﬁcs and to better
get an idea of how the growth of cannabis is simpliﬁed in this project. Following that is theory necessary
to compliment the simulation to get a precise result, after which a method chapter describes the zones, how
they are simulated, and how they are evaluated.
After that the results that can be obtained from executing the method are made, the optimization of
the diﬀerent building types are made within reasonable extents and the strengths and weaknesses of each
type and direct comparisons are made to deﬁne which type of building makes the most sense for growing
cannabis.
Finally, a discussion and a conclusion will summarize the results and go into further work possibilities to
better go into depth with some of the more uncertain aspects of the obtained results.

Literature study
What is cannabis
Cannabis (also known as Marijuana) is a hemp plant deﬁned distinctly from other types of hemp such as
industrial hemp by its concentration of THC above 0.2%. There are 3 common varieties of cannabis known
as Sativa, Indica and Ruderalis, and most types of cannabis used in industry are a mix of these varieties
[[1]]. Cannabis is a plant mainly grown for the cannabinoids they contain, most commonly THC and CBD.
Since the growth rate of the plant, and the THC and CBD concentration depends on the strain being
grown, and how it is grown, the actual product is dependent on these factors, which means it is diﬃcult to
estimate an optimal product without extensive research and optimization within the speciﬁc ﬁeld of strain
optimization.
Cannabis has 3 main growth stages, assuming cloning is used, which is the most employed and reliable
method for getting a consistent product. The 3 main stages are explained in the following sections [[2]].
Propagation/Stickling phase
The stickling phase is the stage where cuttings are taken from a mother-plant and planted. This means the
plant starts out as a small cutting with no roots. Without roots, the plant is very vulnerable for a period of
time until it has properly set roots. During the stickling phase it is important to have a high air humidity
and suﬃcient but limited light to allow the stickling to set new roots and grow into its own plant. The
period where a plant is in the stickling phase is slightly dependant on the strain but is often commonly 2
to 3 weeks. In this project 18 days are assumed to be suﬃcient. The plant needs a photoperiod of 18 hours
day and 6 hours night during this stage.
Figure 1: Cannabis in stickling phase, picture by Brett Levin [[3]]
Vegetative phase
Once the plant has taken roots properly, it is moved to a larger pot, and the vegetative phase begins. In
the vegetative phase, most of the growth of the plants vegetation will take place, and it will grow a lot. The
5
==PAGE:8==
vegetative phase can last between 3-16 weeks depending on the desired size of the plant when it ﬂowers. There
is very little literature for the optimal vegetative phase duration to optimize THC and CBD production, and
once again it is very dependent on the strain. Most growers choose the shortest possible vegetative phase
to optimize the amount of harvests rather than the size of the harvest. Short growth periods with many
harvests per year also reduce the risk of diseases setting on plants.
To keep the cannabis plant in the vegetative phase, it is necessary to have a photoperiod of 18 hours day,
and 6 hours night. It is also in this stage that mother plants are kept in the vegetative phase, and cuttings
are taken from them.
For a cannabis plant in the vegetative phase, humidity and temperatures can both be fairly high to
optimize the potential photosynthesis that the plant can undergo.
Figure 2: Cannabis in the vegetative phase
Flowering phase
Once the cannabis plant in the vegetative stage goes to a photoperiod of 12 hours per day, it will start to
ﬂower, ﬂowering can most commonly last between 3-12 weeks, very dependent on the strain, most cannabis
sativa plants need closer between 8-11 weeks of ﬂowering.
During the ﬂowering phase, ﬂowers will set,
on which most of the THC and CBD will be concentrated. The humidity should be lowered signiﬁcantly
compared to other growth stages, and the temperature should also be a bit lower, as the natural habitat for
the ﬂowering cannabis plant would be fall, as opposed the the vegetative phase, which has naturally been
summer.

What is medicinal cannabis
Medicinal cannabis is the term for cannabis grown under certain circumstances to follow certain medicinal
regulations, most of which are explained in this section.
Medicinal cannabis has been a growing industry because of the recent legalisation and medical uses that
it has. In Denmark, a 4-year trial was initiated on January 1st, 2018, where Lægemiddelstyrelsen hands out
permission to grow cannabis and make cannabis ﬂowers. On July 1st, 2018, this trial was expanded to also
include a separate permission to turn the ﬂower into the medicinal product [[5]]. As of the 12th of February
2020, 41 diﬀerent companies had been granted permission to grow medicinal cannabis. [[4]].
Because medicinal cannabis is grown for medicinal purposes, it is necessary to follow GMP (Good medical
practice) and GACP (Good agricultural and collection practices) regulations, as well as general medicinal
regulation in Denmark, which adds a lot of extra demands for how the plant is grown and produced.
These regulations makes an eﬃcient greenhouse production more complicated, while it is much easier to
follow regulations and demands in a closed growth chamber with complete control of the indoor climate,
independent of the exterior climate.
An example of this is that it is not allowed to spray medicinal cannabis with pesticides, which immediately
puts a huge risk on utilizing the venting capabilities in a greenhouse, as that would put the plants at risk of
infections and sickness. Even if one were to open the windows and take the risk of diﬀerent plant diseases
entering, fairly dense insect nets are required on all windows which would lower venting capabilities and
increase costs. [[6]]
Cannabis has many diﬀerent cannabinoids, but the 2 main cannabinoids that are understood and used
are THC (Tetrahydrocannabinol) and CBD (Cannabidiol). Where THC is the cannabinoid mostly known
for the psychoactive eﬀect, and CBD has applications as a painkiller.
Medicinal applications for medicinal cannabis includes but are not limited to:
 (THC) Increases appetite and reduces nausea in people undergoing chemotherapy, or people with
HIV/AIDS
 (CBD) Painkiller against chronic pain, muscle spasms and arthritis
Medicinal cannabis has many other medicinal uses as well, some of which can be read on Lægemiddel-
styrelsens website. [[7]] [[8]]

Theoretical optimal growth of Cannabis Sativa L - Temperature, Light intensity,
Humidity and CO2
According to research [[12]], the THC/CBD potency does not increase with the amount of light provided to
the plant, however the plant produces more plant mass, which has a similar concentration of THC and CBD.
Therefore, the concentration does not change, but the mass does, resulting in an increase in THC and CBD
based on an increase in lighting. In total the THC and CBD production is assumed to be directly linked to
the mass of the plant.
While the amount of light only aﬀects the plant mass, the frequency of the light used can have an eﬀect
on the THC and CBD densities [[13]]. Light in this project is deﬁned mostly by its PPFD values, therefore,
the small variety that diﬀerent light frequencies might have are ignored.
The optimal growth of cannabis can thus be deﬁned by ﬁnding a balance between operating costs (for
artiﬁcial light, ventilation etc.), time and plant growth to get the optimal setup.
Since both time and plant growth depend on the strain of cannabis being grown, getting optimal operating
costs based on deﬁned optimal climate would be an alternative way to do this optimization.
A study in 2008 [[14]] made several tests on Cannabis Sativa L during its ﬂowering stage and its growth
based on diﬀerent variables, and found that the highest rate of photosynthesis could be achieved for Cannabis
under the conditions of 1500 µmol
m2·s light, and 25-30. Additionally, it tested the plants reaction to elevated
CO2 concentrations between 250-750 ppm. The test showed a signiﬁcant increase in net photosynthesis and
much better results in water use eﬃciency. The study discusses a previous study that claimed an increase
in net photosynthesis of about 30% with an increase in CO2 [[15]], while the study itself shows an increase
of up to 50%.
A similar study was done later in 2011 [[16]], which showed a 44% increase in photosynthetic rate when
CO2 went from 390 ppm to 700 ppm.
Some hobby sites for growing cannabis claims the potential to increase yields by 30% when going all the
way up to 1500ppm CO2 [[17]]. This number is not further backed up and thus it can be assumed to be
experience or industry practice rules of thumb.
Because of a lack of reliable studies at CO2 concentrations above 750 ppm, it is assumed that the
photosynthetic rate of cannabis somewhat looks the same as it would for tomatoes where the enhanced
growth from CO2 supplementation stops at somewhere around 800-1000 ppm. See CO2 response curves for
tomatoes in e.g. [[18]]
While these numbers are from studies on optimal growth parameters for cannabis, it is important to note
that not all variables are iteratively changed in the studies. Based on simple plant biology, it is known that a
higher CO2 level can also change the optimal light, humidity and temperature, as they are all dependant on
each other in the plants process of photosynthesis and respiration. It is likely that an optimal combination
of temperature, CO2, humidity and light is slightly diﬀerent from the stated values, and dependant on the
strain of cannabis.

ﬁnd is what is necessary to combine the results from an IDA ICE simulation with relevent equations and
deﬁnitions. With the theory, it should be possible to deﬁne the energy usage, prices and climate variables
based on the output of an IDA ICE simulation.

IDA ICE does not simulate a plant very well, and in this project it has been chosen that the plant and
its impact on the climate will be calculated after the simulation, by using the IDA ICE results.
The plant is speciﬁcally assumed to have an inﬂuence on humidity and CO2. Therefore it is necessary
to, after the simulation, make balance equations for those parameters. To ﬁnd those balances, diﬀerent
parameters are needed as well, and a compilation of the necessary deﬁnitions are included in the following
theory.
PPFD at plant canopy
PPFD [[28]] (Photosynthetic photon ﬂux density) is a unit type that describes photoreactive lighting and is
deﬁned as µmol
m2·s and is the number of photons useful for photosynthesis that hits an area per m2 per second.
PPFD is a spot parameter and can be used to describe the speciﬁc useful lighting that hits a plant canopy.
PPFD can be translated from daylight and artiﬁcial lighting, however it is relative to the frequency of light.
Daylight usually has a varying wavelength distribution over the year, dependant on the angle of the sun,
however this slight change is neglected in this project.

Cannabis and leaf area index
Cannabis, and plants in general, are aﬀected by the indoor climate they grow in.
Temperature, light,
humidity, CO2, water, nutrients, trimming, training, and more are all variables which determine the plants
growth and ﬁnal yield.
For this project, the variables investigated are Temperature, light, humidity, and CO2. Other variables,
less dependent on the building or the ventilation are assumed to be at an ideal level. The eﬀects a plant
has on itself and the zone it grows in, which are taken into consideration are humidity through transpiration
and CO2 through photosynthesis.
A plant absorbs heat through photosynthesis as it is an endothermic reaction, however the impact of this
and other smaller indoor climate eﬀects (such as the release of small amounts of CO2 by plants at night) are
negligible and not taken into consideration.
A lot of a plants eﬀects on an environment can be estimated based on the Leaf Area Index (LAI), which
is a measure of the amount of leaf surface area that a plant possesses compared to the area it occupies.
The leaf area index is therefore relative to the leaves of a plant, and is aﬀected by overall growth, how
long the plant grows, if it is pruned, and more. Some studies to predict the LAI of plants have been done
for e.g. Cucumbers and Tomatoes [[30]]. However, there are not many extensive papers on LAI curves over
time for cannabis and therefore some assumptions are made for the LAI instead.
The simpliﬁed assumption of the LAI of the cannabis plant is that it goes from a LAI as a stickling of
0.1 to 0.5, where it then moves to the vegetative phase, and grows from a LAI of 0.5 to 2, and then in the
ﬂowering phase, the LAI goes from 2 to 3. Because the leaf area index is relative to the area and therefore
to the plant density in a zone, it also depends very much on the growers strategy.

transpiration from the plant and the speciﬁc amount of CO2 that it uses up over time. The LAI is used in

Method explanation

be covered in one project. The optimization this project seeks to ﬁnd is whether it is more optimal to grow
cannabis in a greenhouse or a growth chamber based on the requirements for cannabis to be used in the
medicine industry.
Both greenhouses and growth chambers are within standard practices for growing medicinal cannabis
and speciﬁc investigation into their strengths and weaknesses would be beneﬁcial knowledge. In this section,
there is an explanation of how this comparison between the two diﬀerent buildings to grow cannabis in is

The total energy usage is gained through the usage of IDA ICE and post processing climate data. First

Shading theory and integration into IDA ICE
Shading in a greenhouse has many purposes, some of which are necessary to not only optimize the climate,
but also to regulate and control the growth stages of the plant. Research done by Ludvig Svensson (Svensson
University) presented by Paul Arkesteijn [[23]], showed that light levels as low as PAR=0.1 µmol
s·m2 meant that
plants recognized it as a day cycle, which meant forcing the 12 hour day and 12 hour night cycle necessary for
cannabis in the ﬂowering stage was diﬃcult without blackout shading during periods where natural daylight
was present longer than 12 hours a day.
IDA ICE simulates light with a simple sky view factor, as opposed to a ray-tracing method which would
accurately ﬁnd concentrations of light and operational temperature based on a grid. The sky view factor
only looks at the window, and how much of the sky that window can actually see, and determines light
entering the zone based on that. Therefore, in IDA ICE, operative temperatures are averaged over a zone
and local hot-spots are not simulated. Because of this simpliﬁcation, identifying a maximum operational
temperature with direct sunlight is not possible, and it is assumed that direct sunlight would require more
precise spot cooling on some of the plants as their surface temperatures would become higher than the air
temperature.
To deal with these high operative temperatures, a diﬀusing shading is often used which evenly spreads
the light over the entire zone. Because the eﬀect of this shading can not be accurately simulated in IDA ICE
with the current single zone setup, it is assumed that it is necessary for the plants environment. Research
[[24]] and [[25]] shows that diﬀusing natural daylight often has a positive eﬀect on plant growth.
Shading is also used at night to insulate the building by keeping in lighting, radiation, and heat. Opti-
mally, a double layered shading system would be used, where a diﬀusing shading that blocks a low amount
of light of around 20%, and diﬀuses the remaining light completely, would be combined with a completely
reﬂecting/blackout shading. The combination allows the zone to micro control with the diﬀuse shading and
macro control with the reﬂective shading.
Examples of two interior shadings for these purposes have been found from the company Ludvig Svens-
son [26] [27].The diﬀusing transparent shading Harmony 2047 FR shades about 20% of direct light. The
reﬂective/blackout shading Obscura 10070 FR WB+BW shades up to 99.9% of direct light.
These two shadings claim to have an energy saving potential of 47% and 70% for heating and cooling
respectively when used optimally. Reaching these numbers is not explained further by the company on the
20
==PAGE:23==
products datasheet and are therefore not taken directly.
The usage of the diﬀuse shading would be to reduce overheating and turn direct light into diﬀuse light to
improve uniform growth and to prevent local high operational temperatures that could cause plant stress.
The blackout shading works to further limit daylight during critical periods and to shade at night, to provide
the zone with a better heat retention, artiﬁcial light utilization and reduce light pollution.
Figure 9: Picture of blackout shading from Svensson university presentation by Paul Arkesteijn [23]

simulation will be done where the impact of the diﬀusing shading is explored. in this way, the diﬀusing
shading will be applied post simulation as an assumption on its approximate eﬀect on the energy usage.

Investigations and optimizations
In buildings, there are many methods available to control diﬀerent systems, and the level of detail opti-
mizations can go into are almost limitless. Optimizing is therefore a relative term for buildings, as it is
theoretically a never ending term. In this section, a few control strategies are explored based with the de-
ﬁned theory and method. The purpose of these investigations is to understand some of the possible control
strategies, how they aﬀect the building, and how they could potentially be further explored.
Reﬂective shading investigation in the vegetative phase
The blackout shading is used for night periods. Some simulations are also run to see the eﬀect that the
blackout shading would have if it shaded during the day for the vegetative phase.
The purpose of the shading in the following comparisons will be to reduce the energy usage as much as
possible, while also reducing peak demandsl. There are requirements for the minimum light needed in the
vegetative phase, which means shading during a sunny day, while resulting in reducing the necessary cooling,
will also greatly increase the necessary energy requirement for artiﬁcial lighting as the blocked sunlight would
be replaced with artiﬁcial light, which also needs to be cooled.

the maximum lighting that the plants should receive, such as the stickling phase.
Necessary stickling shading
The stickling phase is the phase where the plants are at their most vulnerable, therefore the temperature
setpoints are set to be lower than they are for the vegetative phase, to prevent stress which is much more
lethal in that phase.
Additionally, according to [[38]], there is a maximum amount of light that the cuttings should receive
where simply diﬀusing the light, which was the solution in the vegetative phase, is not enough. Propagation
can be split into 3 phases, which respectively concern the early stickling with no roots, early rooting, and
deﬁned rooting. For the earliest stage with no rooting, a PPFD above 200 µmol
m2s , can result in the desiccation

systems are many times higher for the greenhouse. A greenhouse will for most of the year not require much
heating, however those few times during the night cycle in winter when the lights are turned oﬀand the
external temperature reaches below -12 , a large amount of heating is required for short periods of time.
The actual heating and cooling capacity in the zone could be lower if the setpoints were tweaked or if
certain control strategies were changed, however this could risk the indoor climate, a risk that the growth
chamber does not share.

Discussion
With the growth of the medicinal cannabis industry in Denmark, many companies have started to throw
their money into getting an early lead on the production of cannabis to get the early advantage inherent in
new markets. For this reason, many companies have diﬀerent strategies as one speciﬁc optimized strategy has
not become industry standard, and many of the competing companies do not want to share their discoveries
or experience with others.
Optimizing an industry, or even just optimizing parts of an industry, is something that happens through
a combination of research, development and experience, and not something possible with one investigation or
one project. The purpose of this project was therefore to get a broader and somewhat simpliﬁed perspective
and numbers on the two most popular competeting strategies for growing medicinal cannabis, either in
a greenhouse which has a more volatile climate because of the outside factors inﬂuencing it, or a growth
chamber, which is almost entirely independent of external factors, and therefore much easier to control.
This project has been very broad, and optimal growth variables are not yet very well documented for
cannabis. A lot of assumptions were made to simplify certain aspects of the buildings, the properties of the
cannabis plant and the zone controls. These assumptions helped to make a complete investigation of the
indoor climate values of temperature, humidity and light, while also getting a price to keep the climate at
the deﬁned setpoints.
These assumptions, while making this investigation more feasible within the time frame, do create an
uncertainty around the results, which could put into question the actual comparison, and therefore the results
should be considered very critically in the case that some of the assumptions turn out to stray from reality.
A lot of smaller investigations were also carried out to investigate the eﬃciency of some normal systems
used in high tech greenhouses or growth chambers.
These investigations concern shading, lighting and
ventilation. These smaller investigations were not fully explored in the many possible control schemes they
could have had, some of which might have led to diﬀerent results. Examples that could be investigated
more could be utilizing ventilation cooling at night when the CO2 supply is turned oﬀ, and a better tool to
simulate the Par perfect concept for greenhouse shading [[39]].
The ﬁnal results both utilize ideal heaters and ideal coolers, and CO2 is supplied through liquid CO2,
which is a fairly clean way of supplying CO2. Some alternative methods which have cross dependencies of
these indoor climate values could also be investigated, perhaps together with a more deﬁned cannabis plant.
This could for example be looking into reusing the water from dehumidifcation systems and condensation.
Another example could be burning natural gas, which gives CO2 while also providing humidity and heat to
the zone.
Another example could be utilizing desiccant dryer systems such as an AGAM system, which not only
removes humidity, but also provides the zone with heating. An AGAM system was not considered because
the control systems were chosen based on the vegetative phase, which needed very little heating, however in
the stickling / propagation phase, there is a demand for heating, though in the results gained in this project,
the demand for dehumidifying were relatively small in that phase.
The ﬁnal results gained in this investigation are related to the cost of operation of the speciﬁc growing
zones based on similar indoor climate values. If this investigation was combined with an in-depth optimized
cannabis strain and properly documented mass diﬀerences in growth based on the indoor climate values,
then it would be possible to create an accurate price estimation in DKK/kg of product (THC or CBD).
Some numbers for this exist based on actual operations and experience but these lack the ability to estimate
the possible savings of changing something in a zone.
There are simple methods utilized in the industry which are also not considered, such as the price
43
==PAGE:46==
diﬀerence versus product output gained if vertical growing was considered, which has plants growing in
diﬀerent setups that potentially better utilizes a 3 dimensional space.
It is important to also consider the simulations location, as the comparison is relative to the external
climate. Some of the strengths of the greenhouse which relied on the external climate would be diﬀerent
based on the location. In warmer climates, it would be more diﬃcult to utilize the free cooling through the
facade, and condensation would most likely not occur as often. On the other hand, in warmer climates, there
is usually also more sunlight, which can remove the need for lighting. Therefore the precise comparison and
price diﬀerence between the buildings will change based on the climate.
To summarize, simulation tools are a good way to calculate and predict costs. If every variable about
growing something, e.g. cannabis was included in a simulation, together with methods for keeping a good
indoor climate in a greenhouse, then it would become increasingly simpler for the industry to accurately
plan and control their growth and optimize them. Like most of the building industry, many things can be
found out in post, and changed/ﬁxed with experience, but it would be a lot cheaper if major decisions and
control schemes could be made and planned before the building was built.

==PAGE:47==
Conclusion
By simulating a greenhouse and a growth chamber, their diﬀerences in terms of energy usage and indoor
climate have been discovered based on the deﬁned setpoints, guidelines and demands for growing cannabis.
Through this investigation, it was determined that Greenhouses have a lower electricity usage over a yearly
period varying depending on growth phase, with the biggest diﬀerence between the two zones was the
vegetative zone, where the greenhouse used 36.5%. And a total energy usage diﬀerence over all the phases
of 16.6%
While the greenhouse has a lower energy usage, the total capacity for both heating and cooling systems
had to be a lot larger, as the greenhouses climate is a lot more inﬂuenced by outside factors which results
in peak energy demands being larger, which would require a higher installation cost for the systems, which
could potentially result in the savings from a greenhouse not being as prevalent.
In terms of risks that the diﬀerent zones face, it is assumed that both zones with the deﬁned controls
have satisfactory levels of tightness if built properly. It is however also fair to assume that a greenhouse
will have a larger risk of leaks which could bring in fungal spores or result in zone mixing, especially with
time as it is not uncommon for greenhouses to have a generally less consistent shell tightness and potential
humidity problems in certain situations such as e.g. condensation forming on the internal glass while there
is high wind pressure on the glass, which can cause vibrations that can lead to water droplets landing on
the plants.

From a pure costs standpoint, a greenhouse would be better for growing cannabis, and if it was determined
acceptable that temperatures and humidities very infrequently go above or below the setpoints, then the

Growth chambers, while more expensive on a yearly basis, have a much higher possibility of having a
consistent and succesful growth, for example, even if it was an extremely hot/cold day outside not seen in the
last 10 years, the plants in the growth chamber would not be threatened/put at risk. It is also much more
likely that every single plant in the growth chamber will have the exact same growth conditions, and therefore
the exact same product amount in them. If a certain method of growing cannabis with high amounts of
CBD/THC was found, that required a very speciﬁc indoor climate, then this could be an advantage.
Both methods have their strengths and weaknesses, and their own inherent risks and rewards.
It is
therefore not possible to say for certain that one method is better than the other without a deﬁned set of
priorities.
