Abstract 
A new design for separation processes in order to reduce the energy requirement of these processes has 
been introduced in this work. The new design is based on processes that separate compounds that form 
azeotrope or/and have close boiling points through extractive distillation with the use of solvent. The 
solvent swap method is developed in order to select and verify the appropriate solvent for the solvent 
swap unit. The methodology is based on the solvent selection method of (Gani, et al., 2006) which has been 
extended, in this work, according to the additional criteria solvent should reach. The method takes into 
account the solvent swap unit and the solvent recovery unit, which, in this work, are liquid-liquid extraction 
and evaporator, respectively. Firstly, the method is analyzed conceptually and secondly case studies with 
rigorous simulations are performed.

1 Introduction 
One of the most used and most important unit operations for separation in chemical and refining industries 
is by distillation (Feitosa de Figueirêdo, Pontual Guedes, Manzi Monteiro de Araújo, Gonzaga Sales 
Vasconcelos, & Pereira Brito, 2011). In a chemical plant the energy requirements for the distillations and 
their support facilities can be more than the half of the total operating costs (Julka, Chiplunkar, & O'Young, 
Selecting Entrainers for Azeotropic Distillation, 2009). Environmental awareness and the need for less 
energy-intensive processes have led to the development of alternative and new processes which are less 
energy demanding and in sequence more environmentally friendly.  
1.1 Separation by distillation  
As it is already mentioned, separation by distillation is the most common operation. This operation is based 
on phase creation (as qualitatively seen in Figure 1.1.1), followed by phase separation (Seader, Henley, & 
Roper, 2011). Therefore, a vapor phase, which is not soluble with the feed, is created based on energy 
transfer and species of the mixture are transferred between vapor and liquid phases. The driving force for 
separations through distillation is the relative volatility (Equation 1.1).

concentrated in the least volatile compound is obtained. Therefore, the distillate product consists mainly of 
the most volatile compound and the bottom product of the least volatile compound is obtained. The 
greater the deviation of the relative volatility from unity is, the easier the separation is.

1.2 Azeotrope 
Azeotrope is a mixture of two or more compounds whose the composition of vapor and liquid phase is the 
same at a given pressure and temperature (Luyben W. L., 2013). When the mixture reaches the azeotropic 
composition (as seen in Figure 1.2.1) the relative volatility is 𝛼𝑖,𝑗= 1  and the mixture cannot be further 
separated. Therefore, one product of the distillation is pure compound and the other product is the 
azeotropic composition. Depending on the feed composition the top and bottom product might be 
different, for example if the feed composition is on the left side of the azeotropic composition, then the 
least volatile compound is the pure compound obtained from the distillation as product. Otherwise, the 
most volatile compound is obtained as a pure product. In both case, the other product of distillation is the 
azeotropic composition. 
 
Figure 1.2.1. Qualitative VLE phase diagram of a binary mixture when azeotrope is formed. 
Feature of azeotrope is that is boiled at different temperature from the boiling points of pure compounds 
being contained in the mixture. Azeotropes can be classified with respect to their boiling points and with 
respect to their miscibility in the liquid phase. 
1.2.1 Classification of azeotropic mixtures 
1.2.1.1 Boiling point criteria 
Minimum-boiling azeotrope or positive azeotrope 
The azeotrope whose boiling point is lower than the one of the pure components is called minimum-boiling 
azeotrope or positive azeotrope (Figure 1.2.2 a). 
Maximum-boiling azeotrope or negative azeotrope 
The azeotrope whose boiling point is higher than the one of pure components is called maximum-boiling 
point or negative azeotrope (Figure 1.2.2 b).

azeotrope (at 1 atm) that acetone and chloroform form is shown. This azeotrope is called negative azeotrope. 
 
1.2.1.2 Miscibility in the liquid phase 
Homogeneous azeotrope 
When, at a given pressure, at equilibrium temperature the liquid phase of the azeotrope consists of one 
phase, the azeotrope is called homogeneous (Figure 1.2.3 a). 
Heterogeneous azeotrope 
When, at a given pressure, at equilibrium temperature the liquid phase of the azeotrope consists of two 
immiscible phases, the azeotrope is called heterogeneous (Figure 1.2.3 b).

As it is mentioned previously, azeotropic mixtures or mixtures with close boiling points cannot be separated 
by fractional distillation as their average relative volatility is almost 1. Therefore, it is checked whether by 
changing the pressure, the azeotropic composition changes and whether the average relative volatility of 
compounds with close boiling points changes (whether it becomes >>1 or <<1). In case that, the mixture is 
not pressure sensitive (pressure does not alter the azeotropic composition and the average relative 
volatility) other ways of separation are used. These separations can be based on addition of a third 
compound which is called solvent (extractive distillation or azeotropic distillation) or on the use of barriers 
(membrane separation) or on the use of solid agent (adsorption). More detailed description about these 
processes follow.  
1.2.2 Separation techniques for azeotropic mixtures and mixtures with close boiling point 
1.2.2.1  Pressure-based separation 
Pressure-swing distillation  
Pressure-swing distillation is the system of two ordinary distillations operating in series at different 
pressures (Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011). When a binary azeotrope disappears or its composition alters at 
least by 5 mol% by changing the pressure (as seen in Figure 1.2.4), pressure-swing distillation is proposed to 
be used. In Figure 1.2.4, T-x-y curves of a minimum-boiling azeotrope in different pressures are depicted. 
Compounds B1 and B2 form azeotrope which is pressure sensitive, the composition of azeotrope changes, 
when the pressure changes. As it is seen in Figure 1.2.4, and according to T-x-y curves, in the first 
distillation operating at P1, azeotrope which has the lowest boiling point is obtained as top-product and 
pure B1 is collected as bottoms product. The azeotrope goes to the second distillation operating at pressure 
P2 > P1. In this pressure, the feed is on the left-side of azeotrope and pure B2 can be collected. Therefore, B2 
whose boiling point is higher than the one of azeotrope is obtained as bottoms product and the azeotrope 
is obtained from the top and is recycled to the first column.

1.2.2.2  Solvent Based Separations 
In case that the azeotrope is not pressure sensitive, then an organic solvent might be used. The role of the 
solvent is to alter the relative volatility of the compounds in order to break the azeotrope and the 
separation to be feasible. 
Organic Solvents 
In general, solvents are widely used in the chemical industry in many applications, such as to promote 
reactions, either as a reaction medium or as a carrier to deliver the chemical product to the required 
quantity. They are also used in cleaning and washing steps of the reaction stage, as well as for the 
extraction, separation and purification of chemical compounds (Green Chemistry and Hazardous Organic 
Solvents. Green Solvents, Replacement and Alternative Techniques; Gani, et al., 2006). 
The need for cleaner and more sustainable technologies has led to new solvents development such as ionic 
liquids (Gani, et al., 2006), which (because of their properties whose analysis follows) decrease the risk of 
air pollution (Kulajanpeng, Suriyapraphadilok, & Gani, 2016).  
Ionic Solvents 
Ionic liquids are salt liquids at room temperature. They are consisted of an organic cation, an alkyl chain 
length of the cation and an organic or inorganic anion (Abumandour, Mutelet, & Alonso, 2016). Their 
properties make them to be considered as good solvents.  The fact that they have low melting point (below 
100 oC) and negligible vapor pressure and heat of vaporization (Kulajanpeng, Suriyapraphadilok, & Gani, 
2016), ther ability to “break” azeotropes and their easy of recycling, make them to be considered 
environmentally friendly processes solvents. Their possibility of loss to the atmosphere is reducing (Pereiro, 
Araújo, Esperança, Marrucho, & Rebelo, 2012) and the energy requirement is low (because of separation 
easiness) (Kulajanpeng, Suriyapraphadilok, & Gani, 2016). The fact that they are thermally and chemically 
stable (they begin to decompose around 400 oC, (Huddleston, Visser, Reichert, Willauer, Broker, & Rogers, 
2001) and non-flammable as well as their ability to be dissolved to a broad range of compounds (Rodriguez,

anions and cations, physical, chemical and biological properties of ionic liquids can be “tuned” according to 
the needed characteristics for a specific application (Kulajanpeng, Suriyapraphadilok, & Gani, 2016), 
(Rodriguez, 2016).

Azeotropic distillation  
In azeotropic distillation a third entrainer is used in order to form a new, minimum-boiling azeotrope which 
is heterogeneous (Kiss & Suszwalak, 2012). In most of the cases the entrainers are partially miscible with at 
least one compound of the mixture. In each stage of distillation, two liquid and one vapor are in 
equilibrium. The new minimum-boiling azeotrope has the lowest boiling point and is collected on the top of 
distillation column. Then it goes to a decanter where it splits in two phases. The one phase consisting 
mainly of entrainer is recycled to azeotropic distillation and the other is sent to a second distillation column 
to obtain the left compound of the initial mixture (see Figure 1.2.7).  For instance, azeotropic distillation is 
used for the IPA dehydration. Three columns are used to obtain IPA and water (Van Hoof, Van den Abeele, 
Buekenhoudt, Dotremont, & Leysen, 2004). In the first column, which is a fractional column, water is 
obtained as bottoms product and the top-product consists of a stream which is close to the azeotropic 
composition. This stream goes to an azeotropic distillation where entrainer is added to break the 
azeotrope. The entrainer is the benzene and IPA is obtained as bottoms product and IPA, water and 
benzene as overhead product. The latter product goes to the decanter where it splits in two phases. The 
benzene-rich phase is recycled to azeotropic distillation and the water-rich phase goes for further 
separation to the third distillation. In this distillation, water is collected as bottoms product and the 
distillate product containing benzene, water and IPA are recycled to the azeotropic distillation.

Liquid-liquid extraction 
Liquid-liquid extraction can also be used to separate compounds that form azeotrope or have close boiling 
points (Cabasso, 1983). The mixture not being able to be separated by conventional distillation comes into 
contact with another compound, which is added to the liquid-liquid extraction unit. The new compound is 
immiscible with the one of the mixture compound, and hence two phases are created, and miscible with 
the left-compound of the mixture. Therefore, the added-compound extracts the compound of the mixture 
that is miscible with and both of them are further led to a unit (such as conventional distillation) where 
they are separated (Cabasso, 1983). 
1.2.2.3  Separation by barriers 
Membrane separation  
Membranes are also used to separate compounds that form azeotrope or have close boiling points. More 
specifically pervaporation, which is a combination of membrane and evaporation, is used  ( Garcia 
Villaluenga & Tabe-Mohammadi, 2000). Driving force of pervaporation is the different rates of sorption and 
diffusion of the feed components through the membrane and not the relative volatility of the components, 
which are desired to be separated, like in distillation separation. Therefore, the separation is not influenced 
by the vapor-liquid equilibrium of the compounds. Components of liquid stream permeate through the 
membrane and then they are evaporated. The rate of permeation through the membrane (flux) and the 
affinity of the membrane towards one compound of the feed, as well as the ease of diffusion of the 
permeating molecules through the membrane (selectivity) are determined the separation of azeotropic or 
close boiling components of a mixture  ( Garcia Villaluenga & Tabe-Mohammadi, 2000). Pervaporation is 
used when the composition of the compound that is desired to separate is low related to the other 
compound of the mixture. 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
26
Liquid adsorption  
Another way of separating compounds that have close boiling points or they form azeotrope is by 
adsorption. A porous solid agent that does not change phase or composition and is immiscible with the 
liquid feed is used to adsorb one of the two compounds of the mixture in its exterior and interior surfaces 
until equilibrium is achieved. The adsorbent compound is called solute when it is in the liquid and 
adsorbate during the adsorption and the left compound of the mixture is called solvent or carrier and is not 
absorbed. The adsorbate is then desorbed and the solid adsorbing agent is regenerated to be used again 
(Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011). However, the desorption step needs high temperature and/or low 
pressure and consequently high equipment cost (Kiss & Suszwalak, 2012).

1.3 Motivation 
1.3.1 Method and Design 
In this work, azeotropic compounds and compounds that have close boiling points which are separated 
through extractive distillation are studied. The extractive distillation systems are usually high energy 
intensive especially for the solvent recovery column. The need for less energy consumption processes and 
consequently for more environmentally friendly processes leads to the proposal of alternative new 
processes design which are less energy-demanding. In this MSc a new design to reduce energy required for 
such separations is proposed. The design has been inspired from the solvent swap processes that have 
been widely applied in pharmaceutical processes.   
1.3.2 Solvent swap process 
The solvent swap process according to pharmaceutical processes is the process used to remove solvent S1 
(original solvent) that is used in an earlier processing step and replace it with another solvent S2 (swap 
solvent) that is better for the next processing step (Papadakis, Tula, & Gani, 2016). Adjusting the definition 
for chemical processes and for separations through extractive distillation, solvent swap process is the 
removal and at the same time replacement of the solvent (S1) that is used in extractive distillation with a 
new one (S2) resulting less energy consumption in solvent recovery unit.  
In this work, the solvent swap process takes place in a separation task based on immiscibility difference. 
More specifically, a liquid-liquid extraction is considered as solvent swap unit, where the original solvent is 
collected as a raffinate and swap solvent together with the compound (main product) coming from the 
extractive column  are obtained as extract. Prerequisite for this is the solubility of the solute in the swap 
solvent to be greater than the solubility of solute in the original solvent and the original solvent to be 
immiscible with the swap solvent. The recovery of swap solvent carries out in an evaporator, where the 
swap solvent is recycled to the liquid-liquid extraction unit and the left mixture-compound is collected as 
product. 
For a successful solvent swap process by extraction, solvents should reach certain criteria (as seen in Table 
1.1), such as miscibility criteria and boiling point difference between the original solvent and the swap 
solvent. In order for solvent swap selection to be considered suitable for the process, swap solvent should 
ensure high average relative volatility between the solute and the swap solvent, no azeotrope formation 
between solute and swap solvent and preferably no azeotrope formation between swap solvent and 
original solvent.  
A design for a solvent swap process is proposed. In this process, an evaporator is used as solvent recovery 
unit in order to recover and recycle the swap solvent to the liquid-liquid extraction unit and collect the 
product (the product is the compound which comes from the bottom of the extractive distillation). In order 
for the evaporator to be used as solvent recovery unit, the relative volatility of swap solvent and solute 
should have great deviation from unity, for instance 𝛼𝑠𝑜𝑙𝑢𝑡𝑒𝑠𝑤𝑎𝑝 𝑠𝑜𝑙𝑣𝑒𝑛𝑡

1.3.2.1 Evaporators 
Evaporator is used in this case to transfer the most volatile compound of the mixture from the liquid phase 
to gas by heat transfer (Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011). It is desired the whole quantity of solute to be 
vaporized and the swap solvent to stay in the liquid phase. Some of the most widely used continuous-flow 
evaporators are described. 
Horizontal-tube evaporator 
Horizontal-tube evaporator (Figure 1.3.1) consists of a horizontal cylindrical vessel which has a horizontal 
bundle of tubes in the lower section (Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011). Steam circulates in these tubes and is 
condensed while the solution being outside of the tubes is boiled and consequently concentrated. The 
bubbles leaving the evaporator are the desired product and they are collected. Moreover, due to the 
bubbles’ movement, agitation in the solution is carried out.  
 
Figure 1.3.1. Horizontal-tube evaporator is depicted (Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011) 
 
Short-vertical-tube evaporator 
In short-vertical-tube evaporator (Figure 1.3.2 the tube bundle is vertical. In this evaporator the solution is 
inside the tubes and the steam is condensed outside of the tubes. The fact that the solution boils inside the 
tube causes the solution to circulate and provide higher agitation and higher heat-transfer coefficients 
(Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011).  
 
Figure 1.3.2. Short-vertical-tube evaporator is depicted (Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011) 
 
Long-vertical-tube evaporator 
The difference of long-vertical-tube evaporator (Figure 1.3.3) from short-vertical-tube evaporator is that 
the tubes are longer providing a separate vapor-liquid disengagement chamber (Seader, Henley, & Roper, 
2011).  Therefore, higher liquid velocities can be obtained and consequently higher heat-transfer 
coefficient. 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
29
 
Figure 1.3.3. Long-vertical-tube evaporator is shown (Seader, Henley, & Roper, 2011) 
 
Thin-film evaporator 
The solution is inside of the heated tube until the compound with the lowest boiling point starts to 
evaporate. The bubbles go to the condenser which is located outside of the evaporator (but as close as 
possible) and are liquefied. The liquefied bubbles are collected as product. 
Short-path evaporator  
Short-path evaporator is a separation process used instead of various processes of the chemical, 
pharmaceutical and food industry (Sales-Cruz A. M., 2006). It consists of a cylindrical body which is 
surrounded by a cylinder. The inside cylindrical body is the evaporator and the outside cylinder is the 
condenser (as seen in Figure 1.3.4). The distance between evaporator and condenser is short (shorter than 
the mean free path of the evaporating molecule, (Sales-Cruz A. M., 2006) and also the residence time of the 
distilled liquid is short.  The latter can be satisfied by inserting the liquid with the form of a thin film. In the 
space between evaporator and condenser (distillation gap) the pressure and the temperature applied are 
low.   
 
Figure 1.3.4. Illustration of short-path evaporator (Sales-Cruz, 2006) (Sales-Cruz A. M., 2006)

1.4 Objective 
The objective of this MSc thesis is to propose alternative for process which is called solvent swap process 
that “result” less energy demanding than the initial process. The new design is based on processes, where 
compounds that from azeotrope or have close boiling points are separated by extractive distillation with 
solvent use. Usually, the more volatile compound is collected from extractive distillation. Then a solvent 
recovery column is used to recover the solvent and recycle it back to the extractive column and the solute 
is collected as overhead product. The new design considers of a swap solvent to remove and replace the 
original solvent in a liquid-liquid extraction unit and to recover the swap solvent in an evaporator. The 
original solvent is recycled to extractive distillation and the new mixture of the solute and swap solvent is 
sent to solvent recovery unit. In this case, the solvent recovery unit is an evaporator, where the solute is 
vaporized and the swap solvent is recovered and is sent it back to liquid-liquid extraction. Therefore the 
new design consists of three units, an extractive distillation, a liquid-liquid extraction and an evaporator. It 
is desired the energy requirement for solvent recovery unit in swap solvent to be less than in the base case. 
For this reason, the selection of the swap solvent considers to be critical. 
Therefore, a method for the application is developed. Characteristics that swap solvent should have in 
order to be considered a good solvent, with respect to solvent swap processes, are analyzed in the four 
steps solvent swap method. The solvent selection is based on computer-aided methods and tools and 
experimental data. After a feasible swap solvent has been found, conceptual analysis is required rigorous 
verification that the energy requirement is less than the energy needed in the base case. Finally, the 
applicability of the method is highlighted through case studies of azeotropic mixtures and mixtures of 
compounds that have close boiling points. Solvent swap method is applied and swap solvents are found.

The solvent swap selection methodology is based on the solvent selection general procedure proposed by 
(Gani, et al., 2006) and, in this work, it has been extended taking into account the specific requirements for 
solvent swap problems in chemical processes. The methodology is a step-by-step algorithm to identify the 
suitable solvents for the solvent swap unit and evaluate the selection through performance criteria and 
simulations. The methodology is depicted in Figure 2.1.1.

2.1.1 Step 1: Problem definition 
In step 1, the problem and the objectives of the specific problem are defined. Characteristics of the process 
which is desired to be studied are given. Solvent used in the process is defined and the need of removing 
and replacing the original solvent with another one is explained. In chemical industries an extractive 
distillation is usually used for the separation of two compounds (A and B) that have close boiling points or 
form an azeotrope. For this separation a solvent (S1, original solvent) is required to change the relative 
average volatility between the compounds to make the desired separation feasible. As it is seen in Figure 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
33
2.2, one of the compounds (B) is obtained as a top product of the extractive distillation and the other 
compound (A) goes with the original solvent and is obtained as the bottom product which is led to a second 
column. In the second column, which is called solvent recovery column, the original solvent is recovered 
and recycled back to extractive distillation column and the compound is obtained as the top product. 
However, the original solvent might not be efficient for solvent recovery column with respect to the energy 
consumption as high amount of energy might be needed to separate the solute (A) from the solvent. 
Therefore the objective of this problem is to select another solvent to swap the original solvent in a solvent 
swap operation where the swap solvent extracts the solute (A) from the original solvent by liquid-liquid 
extraction. The solute (A) is then removed by evaporation.

and A (solute) and solvent (original solvent) go to a solvent recovery column where solvent is recovered and recycled back to 
extractive distillation and A is collected as top product. 
 
2.1.1.1 Solvent swap unit  
In solvent swap operation original solvent is removed and at the same time replaced by swap solvent. In 
this work, this task is decided to take place in a liquid-liquid extraction unit. Thus, after the extractive 
distillation, the original solvent and the solute-A of the feed go to liquid-liquid extraction unit. Swap solvent 
is also added in the unit. Purpose of this unit is for the solute A to be transferred from the original solvent 
to the swap solvent. This means that the solubility of the solute A in swap solvent should be higher than the 
solubility of solute A in the original solvent and also that swap solvent should be immiscible with the 
original solvent so that two phases to be created. Also, boiling point of swap solvent should be as high as 
possible so the solute A can be removed by evaporation. 
In Figure 2.3, the flowsheet of the solvent swap process is depicted when liquid-liquid extraction unit is 
used as solvent swap unit. The bottoms product of the extractive distillation goes to liquid-liquid extraction 
unit where the swap solvent is added. The solute A is extracted to the swap solvent and the original solvent 
which is almost pure is recycled to the extractive distillation. The solute A and the swap solvent are led to 
the solvent recovery unit in order to collect the solute A and recycle the swap solvent in the liquid-liquid 
extraction.

In the Base Case (Figure 2.2), solvent recovery distillation is used as a solvent recovery unit in order to

The swap solvent is selected such that the swap solvent recovery (or solute-A removal) requires a low 
energy operation. This is feasible when the swap solvent, like an ionic liquid, is liquid at the temperature-
pressure of operation and does not easily vaporize. Therefore, the solute-A can be removed by non-
conventional means such as short-path evaporation, simple evaporation or distillation under vacuum.

2.1.2 Step 2. Feasible swap solvent candidates 
The objective of this step is to find all potential solvent candidates that can swap the original solvent in the 
liquid-liquid extraction unit.  
Solvent swap unit: Liquid-liquid extraction  
As it has been already mentioned, in liquid-liquid extraction, original solvent and swap solvent should split 
in two phases. It is also desired swap solvent and solute-A to be miscible. The solute-A should be more 
soluble in the swap solvent than in the original solvent.  
The LLE ternary diagram of the original solvent, solute and swap solvent which is desired to be obtained, is 
the one depicted in Figure 5. In case of the ternary LLE plot, the original solvent and solute-A are miscible 
(y-axis), swap solvent and solute-A are miscible (z-axis) and original solvent and swap solvent are 
immiscible (x-axis). The raffinate consists only of solute-A and original solvent and the extract, on the other 
side, contains solute-A and swap solvent. For liquid-liquid extraction unit to be feasible to operate, it is 
desired the composition of the three compounds in the liquid-liquid extraction unit to be inside the two-
phase boundary, so that two phases are created. In addition, it is also desired for the boundary layer to be 
as close as possible to the z-axis and y-axis, so that the ternary LLE plot approaches the ideal ternary LLE 
plot. In this case, the raffinate can be recycled to extractive distillation.

original solvent and solute-A can be obtained from the separation unit and is recycled to extractive 
distillation. The second product obtaining from this separation unit is the swap solvent which is recycled to

purification units are required and the energy needed for solvent swap case might be higher than the 
energy needed for the base case. The energy requirement is dependent and determined on the process 
conditions of both cases, the mixture characteristics and the selected swap solvent. Therefore, the swap

2.1.3.1 Evaporator Unit 
As the unit used to separate the swap solvent and the solute is based on heat transfer the principles for this 
unit are similar to the ones of separation through fractional distillation. Therefore, in order for separation 
through evaporator to be feasible, swap solvent should not form azeotrope with solute-A and have much 
higher boiling point than solute-A so that pure solute-A can be obtained as vapor product. However, this is 
ensured by Step 2 where swap solvent that has boiling point higher than the one of original solvent is 
desired to be found. Therefore, taking into account that the original solvent used in extractive distillation is 
the heaviest compound with respect to boiling point (which means that its boiling point is higher than the 
one of solute), boiling of swap solvent is higher than solute-A, B and the original solvent. Moreover, 
attention should be paid in the average relative volatility. The higher the average relative volatility of solute

solute-A (Figure 4) and consequently being feasible to use one evaporator as solvent recovery unit. Finally, 
it is important for the swap solvent to be environmentally friendly, so swap solvents should belong to 
specific category of compounds.

4 Case studies 
In this chapter the applicability of the developed method is desired to be highlighted through case studies

4.1 Case study 1.  Azeotropic mixture

acetone and 12.5% of methanol (Luyben W. , 2008). Thus, these compounds cannot be separated by 
conventional distillation. In this work, the separation of these compounds by extractive distillation with the 
use of solvent is studied. According to (Luyben W. , 2008), water can be used as to perform extractive 
distillation in order to separate acetone from methanol. As seen in Figure 1, acetone is collected as a top-

which are led further to a solvent recovery column. In this second column methanol is separated from the 
solvent and collected at the top as well, as it is more volatile than water. The water is then recycled from

4.1.4 Step 4. Validation  
The objective of this step is to validate the preselection of 1,2,3-MBenzene as a swap solvent (which is 
obtained by the analysis in Step 3). Energy requirements when solvent swap method is applied are 
estimated and compared with the energy needed in the Base Case (Table 1).  
After the extractive distillation, where acetone is separated from methanol, water and methanol go to 
liquid-liquid extraction column where 1,2,3-MBenzene is added. The objective of the liquid-liquid extraction 
column is to obtain an extract which consists of methanol and 1,2,3-MBenzene and a raffinate which 
contains water and small amounts of methanol, which is in accordance with Figure 4. It is desired that the 
extract is rich in methanol and the raffinate is depleted in it and also that the raffinate composes at least of 
96% of water. As the raffinate contains only water and methanol, it can be recycled to extractive distillation 
as solvent stream.  
The extract is led to the solvent recovery unit, where methanol is obtained as distillate-product and 1,2,3-
MBenzene is recycled to the liquid-liquid extraction column. The desired purity of methanol-product is the 
same as in the base case, 99.5%.  
To estimate the composition of extract and raffinate, as well as to find the molar flow of 1,2,3-MBenzene 
required to obtain the desired raffinate and extract composition, tie lines of ternary LLE diagram are 
constructed (Figure 4). The solubility of methanol in 1,2,3-MBenzene is higher than the one of methanol in 
water. However this is not verified through UNIFAC thermodynamic model and further experimental 
verification is required.

4.2  Case Study 2. Mixture of close boiling points compounds 
Heptane-Toluene Separation using n-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) as solvent 
Aromatics and nonaromatic compounds cannot be separated by fractional distillation because of the close 
boiling points of the compounds and the fact that several combinations of these compounds have the 
potential to form azeotrope (Steltenpohl, Chlebovec, & Graczová, 2005). Toluene and heptane belong to 
the category of compounds with close boiling points. The boiling points of toluene and heptane at 1 atm 
are 383.9 K and 371.6 K (ICAS database) respectively. It can be seen from the VLE diagram depicted in 
Figure 1 that the separation through fractional distillation is not feasible and in case that it is feasible too 
much energy is required to obtain pure products. 
 
Figure 4.2.1. VLE diagram of toluene and heptane which have close boiling points at 1 atm, when ICAS and UNIFAC as 
thermodynamic model are used. It can be concluded that separation of these compounds through fractional distillation to 
obtain pure compounds is not feasible. 
In this case, the separation of these compounds using extractive distillation and solvent according to 
(Steltenpohl, Chlebovec, & Graczová, 2005) is studied. N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) is used as solvent to 
carry out extractive distillation so that toluene and heptane are separated. As Figure 4.2.2 shows, heptane 
is collected as distillate product of extractive distillation and NMP with toluene flow downward the 
extractive distillation and are obtained as bottoms product. They go for further separation to solvent 
recovery column where toluene is collected as the overhead product and NMP is recycled to extractive 
distillation column.

4.2.1 Step 1. Problem Definition  
The objective of this step is to find an organic solvent (swap solvent) that swaps the NMP (which is the 
original solvent) in a liquid-liquid extraction unit. The swap solvent should extract toluene (solute) from 
NMP and at the same time be immiscible with NMP, so that two phases are created. Besides, the swap 
solvent should have better performance in solvent recovery unit of Solvent Swap Case (Figure 4.2.3) than 
the performance of NMP in solvent recovery column of the Base Case (Figure 4.2.2).  The better the 
performance of the swap solvent in the solvent recovery unit, the less is the needed energy requirement. 
As depicted in Figure 4.2.3, in Solvent Swap Process heptane is obtained as a top-product of extractive 
distillation. NMP and toluene, which are the bottoms product of the extractive distillation, go to the liquid-
liquid extraction unit where the swap solvent is added. In liquid-liquid extraction unit, toluene is extracted 
from NMP into the swap solvent, as toluene is more soluble in swap solvent than in the NMP and NMP is 
obtained as raffinate which is recycled to extractive distillation. The mixture of swap solvent and toluene, 
which is the extract of liquid-liquid extraction unit, is sent for separation to solvent recovery unit, where 
toluene is obtained as distillate product and the swap solvent is recycled to liquid-liquid extraction unit.

4.2.2 Step 2. Feasible swap solvents candidates 
The objective of this step is to find all the potential solvent candidates that can remove and at the same 
time replace NMP, which is the original solvent, in a liquid-liquid extraction unit. Therefore in Step 2, swap 
solvents that reach characteristics according to liquid-liquid extraction are selected. 
It is essential for the NMP and the swap solvent to be immiscible. Therefore in LLE unit two split-phases 
between the NMP and the swap solvent should be created. It is also necessary that the swap solvent will 
extract solute, so the toluene (solute) should be miscible with the swap solvent. To predict whether NMP 
and swap solvent will split in two phases or not, their solubility parameters can be checked. In this case, a 
swap solvent which has solubility parameter close to the solubility parameter of toluene and at the same 
time the difference between swap solvent solubility parameter and NMP solubility parameter is high is 
searched. However, as it has already been explained, the solubility parameter is not the only criteria that 
determine whether two compounds are miscible. Therefore, through LLE plot verification or through 
experimental data, the miscibility of the examined compounds can be checked. In addition, the swap 
solvent should have a higher boiling point than the one of NMP so that liquid phase in LLE can be ensured

4.3 Case Study 3. Azeotropic mixture

used as separation unit and ethylene glycol as solvent. Ethanol is obtained as top-product of the extractive 
distillation and water with ethylene glycol as bottom-product of extractive distillation, as seen in Figure 1. 
Bottom-product is sent to solvent recovery column where water is separated from ethylene glycol. Water is

4.3.1 Step 1. Problem Definition 
In this case study the objective of the problem is defined. Ethanol and water form azeotrope and ethylene 
glycol is used as solvent, as shown in Figure 4.3.1. Therefore, the objective of the problem is to select an 
organic solvent (swap solvent) that removes and replaces ethylene glycol (original solvent) in a liquid-liquid 
extraction unit. The first requirement that swap solvent should reach is that the ethylene glycol and the 
swap solvent should be split in two phases and the latter should extract the water (solute) from ethylene 
glycol. The second requirement is that the swap solvent should perform better than the original solvent in 
the solvent recovery unit from energy point of view.  
An illustration of the solvent swap process is shown in Figure 4.3.2. Ethanol is collected as the overhead 
product of the extractive distillation. The mixture of the ethylene glycol and water which is the bottoms 
product of the extractive distillation is sent to liquid-liquid extraction unit where the swap solvent is added. 
Ethylene glycol is immiscible with the swap solvent and water is more soluble in the swap solvent than in 
the original solvent. Therefore, a raffinate consisting of ethylene glycol and traces of water is obtained and 
is recycled to extractive distillation whereas the extract consisting of the swap solvent and the water goes 
to the solvent recovery unit where water is collected as distillate product and the swap solvent is recycled 
to liquid-liquid extraction unit.

solvent in which the water solubility is higher than the water solubility in ethylene glycol. As it has

solubility parameters. However this is not always the case as there are compounds whose solubility

parameter and at the same time there is a large deviation between solubility parameters of swap solvent 
and ethylene glycol. However, it is via ternary LLE diagram or solubility experimental data that determines 
whether the studied compounds are miscible. Another consideration for the swap solvent is its melting 
point, which should be lower than 280 K, in order to ensure that no solid formation will occur in the

5 Conclusions 
In this work the design of a new process, solvent swap process, is proposed. The new design is to replace 
the solvent recovery system in extractive distillation processes with a new design which will be less energy 
consuming. In the proposed design the solvent recovery system consists of a liquid-liquid extraction unit 
and an evaporator. In the liquid-liquid extraction unit swap solvent is added and remove and at the same 
time replace the original one. The solute is extracted in the swap solvent and they are sent to the 
evaporator where swap solvent is recovered and recycled to liquid-liquid extraction unit and solute is 
collected. As, it can be seen the proposed process is based on the solvent selection. Therefore, the 
efficiency of this process depends on the swap solvent selection and thus, a solvent swap method is needed 
to be developed. This method is based on computer-aided methods and tools as well as on experimental 
data. Rigorous simulations are required to estimate the energy demands when the desired swap solvent 
has been selected. The energy requirements are compared with the energy demand of the base case 
process.   
Conceptually analyzing processes for azeotropic mixture separations as well as for separations of close 
boiling components, it can be seen that by finding the desired for each process swap solvent the energy 
requirement can reduced from 50% to 90%. The energy reduction depends on the mixture that is desired to 
be separated and the operating conditions of the base case process. 
Three case studies are studied. The two of them deal with the mixture of A and B that form azeotrope. The 
first case deals with the separation of acetone and methanol when water is used as original solvent. It is 
found that 1,2,3-trimethylbenzene can be used as swap solvent. In this case, different ways of solvent 
recovery unit, in order to recover and recycle the swap solvent and collect the methanol (solute), are 
approached. The simple evaporator operating at 1 atm is not able to give methanol at the same purity as in 
the base case and besides the methanol quantity produced is much less than the one of the base case. The 
second way of solvent recovery unit that is approached is by vacuum evaporators, where the purity of 
methanol obtained is 99% and the energy requirement is...% than the energy required in the base case. 
However, the methanol product flow rate is 100kmol/hr less than the methanol production in the base 
case. Finally, a short-path evaporator as solvent recovery unit is used. High purity of methanol is produced 
and the energy reduction comparing with the base case is 74%. The other examined case of azeotropic 
mixture separation is ethanol-water separation when ethylene glycol is used as solvent where organic 
solvent cannot be found by the method, but more than one ionic liquids are found, following the method. 
However, experimental verification is required to in order to assure that the ionic liquids selected can be 
used. In case that the selection is verified, then by using the ionic liquids, 100% pure water can be obtain 
and the energy reduction comparing to base case will 70% 
The third case study deals with heptane-toluene which have close boiling points and NMP is used as 
solvent. Organic swap solvent cannot be found by following the method but the ionic liquid 1-butyl-4-
methylpyridinium tetrafluoroborate can be used as swap solvent. The toluene production is the same as in 
the base case, the purity is 100% and the energy savings can be up to 90% (it depends on the operating 
conditions of the base case).

6 Future work and discussion 
The solvent swap process is a new design and in this work the solvent swap method is developed. More 
binary systems that form azeotrope or have compounds with close boiling points should be studied. In 
order for the right swap solvents to be found, experimental data among the swap solvent and the binary 
mixture should be available. In this way, more accurate results will be obtained. 
In case that experimental data are not available and predictive models are used, the experimental 
verification is considered to be necessary. 
It is also desired the thermodynamic models, such as UNIFAC, UNIQUAC, NRTL, to describe better the 
liquid-liquid behavior of the compounds. Thus, a better estimation for UNIFAC parameters and also 
experiments to fit the UNIQUAC and NRTL models are required. 
 
As it is mentioned in this work, the economic evaluation is the one that determines whether the solvent 
swap process is better or not than the base case. Therefore, after selecting the   swap solvent and verifying 
through rigorous simulations, economic evaluation is required. 
Solvent swap method can be used in gas separation where extractive distillation and solvent recovery 
column are used, as in case of ethane and CO2 separation. The columns in gas separation operate at high 
pressures and thus high amount of energy are needed. In case that a swap solvent can be used, the energy 
of the whole process will be reduced. The swap solvent will be used to extract the compound being 
consisted in the bottom product of the extractive column. The extraction will carry out in a liquid-liquid 
extraction unit at lower than the initial pressure. The separation of compound and swap solvent will be also 
performed at lower pressure. In this way, the energy required for the process might be reduced. However, 
analysis of this case is required.
