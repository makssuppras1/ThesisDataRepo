Abstract
This thesis leverages neural networks, time-adaptive quantile regression and
stochastic differential equations to improve day-ahead forecasts for wind- and
solar power production using a model-free approach. We developed a three-step
corrective procedure to effectively error-correct forecasts and forecast-ensembles.
Neural networks are used to find the optimal basis function for the time-adaptive
quantile regression algorithm. The number of steps you apply in the procedure
will depend on your application. In almost all the areas and type of energy com-
binations presented in the results, improvements are observed. The improve-
ments are measured by the CRPS, Variogram and Quantile scores. We show
that in almost all cases, our methodology improves the forecasts. Case studies
showed potential profitability of improved forecasts, though limited sample size
affected statistical significance. The set of new methodologies for error correc-
tion of energy forecasts, contributes to reliable renewable energy systems in the
future. We will publish the developed Python package for use by forecasters
and practitioners by end of year.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The world is witnessing a growing urgency to address the issue of climate change.
Governments, corporations, countries and individuals alike are setting ambitious tar-
gets to achieve carbon neutrality by 2030 or 2050 (Ministry of Foreign Affairs of
Denmark, 2024).
This global effort, often referred to as the green transition, de-
mands the collective participation of all stakeholders in creating a sustainable future.
Among the various sectors that can contribute to this endeavor, energy trading and
renewable energy forecasting stands out as critical players, wielding the potential to
drive significant change.
Concurrently, artificial intelligence is rapidly evolving, but still many language models
remain closed-source and inaccessible, despite promises of transparency and open
access (OpenAI, 2024). This (lack of) transparency presents a significant challenge
for researchers and practitioners seeking to improve and refine these models. Similarly,
many proprietary mathematical models are not shared with the public eye either. To
address this issue, the concept known as model-free error corrections, has emerged as
a promising approach. By developing techniques to correct models without requiring
intimate knowledge of their internal architecture, we can make progress in enhancing
their performance and reliability for a wide range of forecasts; frankly almost all
forecasts that are based on predictions from a basis could gain from this approach.
Accurate forecasts for solar and wind power are crucial for maintaining the stability
and reliability of the power grid. As renewable energy sources are variable and de-
pendent on weather conditions, precise predictions help grid operators balance supply
and demand, preventing blackouts and ensure a steady flow of electricity as well as
a grid frequency that stays within the desired range. Correcting renewable energy
forecasts enhances economic eﬀiciency by reducing the need for expensive backup
power sources. By accurately predicting renewable energy output, grid operators can
optimize the use of existing resources, minimizing costs and emissions associated with
running additional fossil fuel-based (backup) power plants, see e.g. (Kandpal et al.
(2024), McKinsey (2020), International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) (2020)).
This aligns with global efforts to combat climate change and supports the goals set
in international agreements such as the Paris Agreement (Kolkowska, 2023).

1.0.1
The Danish Energy Market
We will take offset in Nord Pool as one of the leading power markets/exchanges of
Europe, especially covering the Nordics and therefore Denmark.
There are three
main ways you can participate in the energy markets: Day-ahead, intra-day and the
balancing market. As the name suggest the day-ahead market accepts bid and offers
from market participant up until 12:00 CET on day d −1 for delivery on day d. The
price is found as an optimization problem, where social welfare is maximized, see
(Nord Pool Group, 2023a).
The intraday market is a continuous market that provide a mix of 15-, 30- and 60
minute blocks in 16 different countries throughout Europe via the Nord Pool exchange.
Here bids are matched with offers in real time and the price is not available to us in
a simple to access dataset, see (Nord Pool Group, 2023b).
The balancing market ensures balance and stability on the grid. In Denmark, En-
erginet makes sure there is always balance, and therefore your balancing order will
always be filled. If you have a huge surplus sell order that needs balancing, you are
to expect a bad price for your energy. We will be using the balancing market in our
case-studies in Section 5, since these prices, albeit volatile, is available to us. The
volatility is asymmetric, meaning that the price can go very high, but rarely becomes
significantly negative. The law of supply and demand applies, almost as good as in
theory, in the balancing market. If you forecast a big surplus of energy - compared
to consensus - one day in advance, then you would expect the balancing price to be
lower than the day-ahead price and vice versa, see (Nord Pool Group, 2023c).

The Danish TSO, Energinet, has recently introduced the P90 requirement, see (Gade

services, such as batteries, or in the case of Ireland, high-speed spinning flywheels,

reserve bids in the market. The key condition is that the reserve bid must be fully
available at least 90% of the time. Energinet typically reviews the past three months
to assess how often a reserve provider has experienced a shortfall. If a provider fails
to meet the promised amount more than 10% of the time, they are no longer eligible
to participate in the P90 service (Lunde et al., 2024).
This requirement directly
motivates the use of quantiles, specifically the 90%-quantile, as it highlights the need
for accurate day-ahead quantile forecasts of energy production.

1.1.1
A Historical Prelude
The median is a simple concept and can often be thought of as the middle, when
ordering sticks by length or sorting people by height, the median is the ”data-point”
in the middle. The idea of the median is age old, however estimation of the median in
a more mathematical context, was proposed circa 1757-1760 by Ruđer Josip Bošković,
a Jesuit Catholic priest from Dubrovnik (Stigler, 1984). Bošković was studying how
to find the equator on a rotating planet from three observations, developing on some
of Newton’s work.
His work precedes some of Legendre’s work on Least Squares
presented in 1805. Later, Laplace starting building upon the work of Bošković, and
developed his ”methode de situation”, which basically laid out the groundwork for
Bayesian statistics and probability as we know it today. Following this, Francis Edge-
worth introduced his ”plural median - a geometric approach to median regression”.
Standing on the shoulders of Bošković, Laplace and Edgeworth, Roger Koenker and
Gilbert Bassett, Jr. famously published and popularized Regression Quantiles, which
presented Quantile regression as we know it today, (Koenker & Bassett Jr., 1978).

1.1.2
Similar Work and Contributions
Here, we will discuss previous work on quantile regression and neural networks, fol-
lowed by a description of this thesis’ contributions.
In H. A. Nielsen, Nielsen, et al. (2006) they try to go from wind forecasts to wind en-
sembles and then to quantile forecast. Their approach include statistical modelling,
since they argue that they can not go directly from the wind power ensembles to
quantile forecasts. In H. A. Nielsen, Madsen, and Torben (2006) they use quantile
regression to try and extend their forecast.
Lauret et al. (2017) uses quantile re-
gression models to model solar power intra-day. They show that using Numerical
Weather Predictions (NWP) improves their quantile regression models. However, in
this thesis we will restrict ourselves. The forecasts we assume given to us, of course
rely on NWP, but when we correct these forecasts we do so in a completely weather-
and model-free environment, which is solely data-driven.
This goes to show that quantile regression and wind- and solar forecast goes hand in
hand and probabilistic forecasts make sense in this setting.
We will now turn our attention to an interesting aspect of this thesis, which, although
not directly applicable, has garnered attention in several papers. Specifically, this in-
volves using neural networks for quantile regression. Note, that our application is not
the same, but the motivation for the multidimensional quantile loss functions stems
from here. Cannon (2011) implements quantile regression with neural networks in R,
and in Bremnes (2020) the quantile functions are specified by linear combinations of
Bernstein basis polynomials, while their coeﬀicients are assumed to be related to en-
semble forecasts by means of a highly adaptable neural network. This article provided
good inspiration for this thesis. Instead of Bernstein basis polynomials Alitasb and
Salau (2024) is a good example of using Radial basis functions instead. In Fakhoury
et al. (2022) they have developed an Expressive Spline-based Neural network that rely
on the combined ideas of the very recently published Kolmogorov Arnold Networks,
ensembles of probabilistic trees, and multivariate B-spline representations.
We can conclude that using neural networks with quantile regression also makes sense.
To do a time adaptive quantile regression model we will employ the simplex method
for quantile regression described in J. Møller et al. (2006). We note that a similar
approach is also found in numerous other articles with different notation, see e.g.
(Van de Geer, 2003, Alhamzawi et al., 2011, Hubert et al., 2004).
In the development of Probabilistic Forecasts of Wind Power Generation, J. K. Møller
et al. (2016) uses stochastic differential equations to model the residuals. This is a
neat trick developed at the Dynamical Systems department at DTU, to the best of the

These ensembles are made available at 12:00 CET at day d−1, covering the 24 hours

The actual observations are available from Energinet through their data provider
Energi Data Service (Energidataservice, 2024). The actual observation are available

Table 2.1 summaries the statistics for the data. We will here comment on the aspects
we find interesting. The data consists of 18718 hourly data points corresponding to
approximately 2.14 years worth of data. One would not expect huge differences across
the two regions DK1 and DK2, but still there are installation differences; for example
DK1 has a much higher max output for Onshore Wind Power than DK2.
DK1’s Onshore wind power production is the biggest production amongst the six
power productions.
Kurtosis is used as a measure for how heavy the tails are in a probability distribution,
where distributions with a heavy tail has high kurtosis. For comparison the standard
normal distribution has a kurtosis of 3. No extreme kurtosis values are to be found
in the dataset with DK1 Solar power being the biggest kurtosis at 4.23.
In Figure 2.2 we present the raw data plotted versus time.
The solar power has
daily and yearly seasonality, while the wind power does not; see Figure 2.5.
In
general the observations are highly autocorrelated. The solar power shows negative

3.1
Quantile regression
The original paper on quantile regression is (Koenker & Bassett Jr., 1978) with an up-
dated book published by the same author in 2005, (Koenker, 2005), and a modernized
version from 2018, (Giles et al., 2018).
We can no longer, as is the case in linear regression, write the closed analytic form
for quantile regression due to the absolute loss function. This is the reason that we
need linear programming to find the optimum for quantile regression.
In the previous sections we found that we are interested in both the actual mean pre-
diction, but also the quantiles. We start off by explaining vanilla quantile regression.

There is no universally accepted method for evaluating quantiles, so we will present
several measures. The main issue with these measures is the lack of statistical tests
to determine if the new model is ”good enough.” This means we cannot easily assess
how close we are to the true model (J. K. Møller, 2006).
We will here assume that our data has been split into a training and test set, see
Section 3.7.3for more information on how this have been split. We will apply these
scores to the test set e.g. out of sample data, that the models have not seen before.
The reason is, that usually the scores will be better in the training set, which the
parameters are fitted to, and thus one would need to take into account the number
of model parameters.

fraction should be (at least) close to the quantile value i.e. for the 70th percentile,
approximately 70% of the data should be below this quantile.

We will base our explanation of the inner workings of an LSTM on Figure 3.5. The
basic idea behind an LSTM network is that we have some sequence of variables in

other literature, called the cell state. The hidden state, or the short-term memory, of
the LSTM is what runs in the bottom of each LSTM module. Each module works for
each time step. Now we will dive into the each gate in the module. The short-term
memory concatenated with a user-specified input serves as the input to the Input
Gate.
We send this through a sigmoid-activation function and multiply with the
previous cell state, or the long-term memory. Since the activation function here is
a Sigmoid activation function this input gate determines how much of cell state we
pass on through this module. This trick of sending the concatenated input through a
sigmoid activation function, is used multiple times in an LSTM module. Basically, it

actually improves performance and is advantageous to get out of local minima.

• Minimal hyper-parameter tuning: The default parameters often work well,

• Computationally eﬀicient: Adam has low memory requirements and is com-

In Algorithm 3 we have outlined how our multidimensional loss function works. Even
though we do not model the gap between the quantiles directly and optimize the loss
function such that the gap is greater than zero, we still do never get crossings in our
corrected ensembles. The reason is that our τ-function picks out the correct ensemble
from the sorted ensembles and the actual observation. Thus the FFNN has incentive
to model non crossing quantiles to every time step, since this would minimize the loss.
By doing it this way we teach the network how the ensembles should be corrected,
and only inputting the actual observations into the loss function makes sure that we
do not use look-ahead bias or the observations when we are updating our ensembles
for the test set.
There is quite some power to the subtlety of this loss function. Normally when doing
quantile regression with neural networks you model each quantile individually. Then
you could sort all the quantiles after to alleviate the problem of crossings, however,
that solution is not smooth, since you could experience jump in your quantiles. This
is not usually done in the literature to the best of the author’s knowledge. Our loss
function targets the specified quantile, and not just randomly sorts ”some” produced
quantiles. Further more, the for-loop presented in Algorithm 3 can easily be par-
allelized in TensorFlow speeding up the calculation significantly. This speeding up
may be needed, since summing the loss for all quantiles at once, means we usually
needs more epochs to get the same accuracy, compared to if we trained each quantile
separately.
This was how we did it with the FFNN. We are also correcting ensembles with the
LSTM network presented earlier. We are using the exact same approach. The only

Discussion
In the beginning, this thesis were investigating the well known Vector Error Correc-
tion Models (VECM) on data from Germany and The Netherlands, following ideas
from (Johansen, 1985). The forecasts in this data was of such bad quality, that no
meaningful models could be build, and thus we abandoned the idea of using data from
Germany and the Netherlands. The data we used for Germany and The Netherlands
came from the ENTSO-E’s transparency platform (ENTSO-E, 2024).
Therefore, we will here only discuss results from Danish data from Energinet and the
forecast provider for the ensembles here.
We will repeat an important point here.
In general, when handling each time step separately, as is common in electricity mar-
kets, quantile forecasts or single-horizon probabilistic forecasts of power production
are adequate. For scenarios involving asymmetric cost functions, reliable estimates
of the single-horizon conditional density are necessary, making the use of CRPS po-
tentially optimal. However, these forecasts lack information about the temporal evo-
lution of future power production.
Therefore, if factors such as storage capacity,
start-up time, or costs are considered, more sophisticated forecasting and evaluation
methods are required. In such instances, evaluating forecasts using the Variogram
score becomes crucial (Sørensen et al., 2023). In this thesis we will take a pragmatic
approach to this and evaluate both CRPS and Variogram score with great impor-
tance, since the application of this thesis may be single-horizon forecasts or it may be
that we want to include factors such as storage capacity etc and therefore need the
Variogram score as well.

5.2
Validation of Ensembles by Trading Strategy
Participants will secure a position in the balancing market, as Energinet ensures
overall market equilibrium.
Typically, producers are not inclined to speculate on
the price differentials between the day-ahead and balancing markets. The primary
objective is risk minimization, given the potential for extreme price fluctuations in
both directions within the balancing market. In Denmark, this risk is asymmetrical;
prices can escalate significantly but rarely turn substantially negative, see Figure
A.12.
5.2.1
Case study 1: Own Wind Power Plant - Fixed Risk
Setting the scene.
Imagine you are the proud owner of a wind power plant, that produces all of DK1
onshore wind power.
You have 50 ensemble forecast for the day ahead, and the
median is what you consider most likely to happen. Due to regulatory instances/risk
regulations/over- or under-promises etc. you must trade on the 33%-quantile. That
means that according to your forecast you are selling less power every day, then what
you actual think is going to happen, so that you are sure you can deliver on your
promises.
We do not consider the intraday market here, since that is continuous
trading and that would require us to consider VWAP prices, and this data is not
easily accessed.
In the day-ahead market you one day predict that the 33%-quantile = 100MWh. The
price you can sell to in the day-ahead market is 15€/MWh. We assume the balancing
price is lower than the day-ahead market, this is true approximately 64% of the time.
Even though, as previously mentioned the primary objective is risk minimization we
will make the assumption here that the price in the balancing market is 10€/MWh
Now, two things can happen.
1. You are right, and you collect the 1500€, no sweat.
2. You are not right.
2a. Your wind farm produced less than the 33%-quantile, so you must pay in the
balancing market, to be able to fulfill on your promise of the 100MWh. Turns out
your windfarm produced only 75MWh, so you need to buy 25MWh in the balancing
market. This is less expensive at 10€/MW. You only made 15 · 75 – 25 · 10 = 875€.
2b. Your windfarm produced more than the 33%-quantile, say 125MWh, so you must
sell the additional 25MWh in the balancing market at 10€/MWh. Instead of the
possible 125 · 15 = 1875€, you only made 100 · 15 + 25 · 10 = 1750€.

closer to one we see higher volatility in the trading profits (wider histograms) for
the produced profit. This makes sense giving the asymmetric risk in the balancing
market, as well as higher fluctuations on the upper quantiles. If you, for example, have
predicted a high production on the 90% quantile, but you do not deliver, you have
to buy a lot back in the balancing market, and thereby reducing profits or causing
losses.
Note that the trading period is far from significant in this case study. The results are
included primarily for transparency and should be taken with a grain of salt.

5.3.1
Going from Offline to Online Implementation
The whole idea of this 3-step corrective procedure is to let the data update the model
itself i.e. data-driven. This is a key-point! The vision for the framework developed
in this thesis is that it should run in an online implementation and all parameter are
continuously adapting to the current environment. Therefore if we sometimes have
spoken of data-driven neural network it should be seen in the context of the online
implementation, where we in this section will introduce actual data-driven neural
network types.
Neural networks weights.
With the neural networks we currently are using they need re-training now and again.
We will here just mention that in the previous four years there have been a huge
development in letting the weights be ”liquid” i.e. able to change over time, and thus
named Liquid Neural Networks (LNN). Instead of describing the system dynamics
with non-linearities (as weights and activation functions), they instead ”construct
networks of linear first-order dynamical systems modulated via nonlinear interlinked
gates”, from the original paper, (Hasani et al., 2020). The outputs of these LNNs
is computed by numerical differential equation solvers on a running basis, always
ensuring the optimal ”weights” for the given environment.

5.3.2
Ideas for Improvement
In the beginning of 2022 the successor to N-BEATS was presented: Neural Hierar-
chical interpolation for Time Series (N-HiTS). N-BEATS was famous, since it was
the first deep learning model that finally successed in beating traditional statistics
frameworks for time series forecasting. N-BEATS stands for Neural Basis Expan-
sion Analysis for Interpretable Time Series, and the original paper is (Oreshkin et
al., 2019). The Neural Basis Expansion part of N-BEATS is what really make this
suitable for this thesis. N-HiTS improved accuracy and lowered computational costs
compared to N-BEATS, see the original paper (Challu et al., 2022). N-HITS does
this by using hierarchical reconciliation across different time frequencies, so that it
faster can learn long and short term dependencies of the time series.
Incorporating these models in a ”liquid” framework, seem like the ultimate setup that
this thesis could benefit from.
Considering the huge volatile shift in energy prices through 2022, see Figure 5.5. This
leads us to believe that were something like that, albeit unlikely, to happen again, we
would like the model to detect regime changes. We could incorporate Hidden Markov
Models or e.g. non-linear SETAR models.
Since our TAQR algorithm excels in predicting the actuals values, a big thing that
would be very interesting to investigate is a point-forecast-driven logistic SDE as
introduced in (Sørensen et al., 2023). Hopefully this could improve the scores further.
A natural way to go from here, given many models that forecasts the same thing,
would be to consider ensemble machine learning models, like random forest models.
A training measure could be our own weighted score e.g. S = 0.2·CSS+0.4·CRPS+
0.4 · Variogram.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The primary objective of this thesis was to leverage time-adaptive, data-driven neural
networks and quantile regression to improve day-ahead forecasts for wind and solar
power production using a model-free approach. Additionally, we aimed to assess the
profitability of energy-trading strategies under specific assumptions.
We developed a three-step corrective procedure using both Feed Forward and Long
Short-Term Memory neural networks to data-drivenly update and find optimal basis
function from our forecasted energy production ensembles. When considering aspects
that relied on proper estimation of the correlation structure, we improved the ensem-
bles’ Variogram score by fitting an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process to the residuals and
simulating from it to obtain our SDE-corrected ensembles.
For predicting the actual value of energy production, we deployed our Python ver-
sion of a Time Adaptive Quantile Regression algorithm, which provided significantly
better results than, for instance, the median from the original ensembles. The re-
sults demonstrate the effectiveness of the NN-SDE-TAQR combination in enhancing
forecast accuracy for renewable energy resources.
In the two case studies, we evaluated the implications of updating our ensembles under
different scenarios. While theoretically improved quantiles and predictions sometimes
translated to profitable trading, the limited sample size prevented the results from
being statistically significant. This limitation arose from the nature of time series
data and the extensive data requirements for training neural networks.
The methodology employed in this research, particularly the use of Python for data-
driven basis corrections and SDE simulations, proved to be robust and reproducible.
Challenges such as handling singularity in the basis function matrix, X, and collinear-
ity in ensembles were encountered and addressed through specific workarounds.
This thesis contributes to the renewable energy forecasting field by introducing a set of
new methodologies that combines neural networks, stochastic differential equations,
and time-adaptive quantile regression. This synergistic method sets creates better
forecasts as measured by CRPS, Variogram and Quantile score.

A.2.4
Illustration of Simplex
We aim to solve the following linear programming problem using the Simplex algo-
rithm. The steps up to the final solution can be seen in Figure A.1. We provided the
algorithm with the starting point (x1, x2) = (0, −2). It is a feasible starting point,
but it not on the edge of the vertices in the constraint polyhedron, which has its
acceptable area to the left of the red, orange, green and blue constraint.
Maximize
3x1 + 4x2
subject to
2x1 + x2 ≤8,
x1 + 3x2 ≤12,
x1 −x2 ≤3,
−x1 + 2x2 ≤6,
x1, x2 ≥0.
To solve this problem, we convert it to a standard form by adding slack variables
s1, s2, s3, s4:
2x1 + x2 + s1 = 8,
x1 + 3x2 + s2 = 12,
x1 −x2 + s3 = 3,
−x1 + 2x2 + s4 = 6,
x1, x2, s1, s2, s3, s4 ≥0.
The objective function becomes:
Maximize
3x1 + 4x2 + 0s1 + 0s2 + 0s3 + 0s4.
