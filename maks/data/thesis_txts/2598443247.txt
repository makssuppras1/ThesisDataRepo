Introduction
When it comes to the reproduction of audio signals, the loudspeaker transducer is usually the
most non-linear element of the signal chain. Therefore, a huge eﬀort has been made by many
companies and researchers in order linearize the most non-linear components of a loudspeaker
transducer unit. The most non-linear components would be the suspension and the force factor,
each depending on the displacement of the transducers membrane, as well as the inductance,
depending on the displacement of the membrane and the current. The eﬀective radiation area
of the transducer plays another important role. It changes,in most common speakers, with the
displacement. The optimization process for a loudspeaker transducer is often only done for the
transducer unit itself, while another important component is the enclosure.
Back in the days, in order to achieve the desired behaviour when designing a loudspeaker, it was
necessary to align the properties of the enclosure perfectly to the characteristics of the trans-
ducer. Nowadays, some problems that occur due to misalignment of transducer and enclosure,
like a distorted frequency response, can be coped with DSP-units which are getting cheaper
and more powerful. With the rise of bluetooth and other wireless speakers, the listening be-
haviour of the typical music consumer has changed. The demand for very small speakers came
up with that. The alignment of very small enclosures to a transducer can lead to problems.
Besides other eﬀects a too small enclosure will lead to a poor bass response. However, what
did not change in the behaviour of the typical music consumer is the demand for a good bass
response. The use of too small enclosures is suspected to introduce unwanted distortion, or the
ampliﬁcation of the transducer distortion that is already there. The air inside the enclosure
acts as a spring when being compressed or expanded. However, this behaviour is not linear,
as a higher force is needed to compress the air than to expand it. When using an enclosure
that is well aligned to the transducer, this eﬀect might stay in fairly linear regions and show no
eﬀect. When exaggerating the mismatch of the transducer and the enclosure, the non-linearity
of this eﬀect might have a bigger impact. In addition, the compression and expansion of the
air volume in the enclosure is directly connected to the eﬀective area of the transducer and
the displacement. As stated before, the eﬀective area changes for most loudspeakers with dis-
placement. This might amplify the eﬀect of the non-linear compression and expansion process.
The eﬀect described earlier is suspected to directly act as an additional distortion to the signal.
As a secondary process further distortion is suspected to occur when trying to equalize the
loudspeaker to a desired frequency response. If the lower bass response induced by the too
small enclosure is equalized to a frequency response, similar to a loudspeaker with the same
transducer unit and a good enclosure alignment, then more current is needed to come to the
same displacement. As described before, certain non-linearities of the transducer unit depend
on the current. Therefore, a higher current would result in more distortion. Due to the higher
displacement that results from the higher current, the non-linear compression and expansion
eﬀect could be driven into even more severe regions of non-linearity.
In this thesis it will be shown how the enclosure aﬀects the produced distortion, harmonic as
well as intermodulation. Both simulations and measurements have been used to analyze what
impact which component has, and if it is suitable to just use a DSP-unit to counteract the
negative inﬂuence of a too small enclosure.

Statement on special circumstances
This thesis was written during the CoVid-19-crisis. The main practical part was done during
the lockdown-phase in the beginning of the crisis, which made it hard to ﬁnd a time and place to
carry out measurements. As DTU’s laboratories were closed during this time, the measurements
had to be done externally. This was done once and could not be repeated. Over the course
of the thesis, the evaluation of the measurements and certain simulation showed that certain
parameters which were chosen for the measurements were not optimal to show the phenomena
that are discussed in this thesis. It was still attempted to use all obtained results to the best,
to achieve a good overview of the topic.
Theory
In this part, the theory of the phenomena discussed in this thesis will be shown. Common dis-
tortion measures that were used to characterize the driver measurements and their models will
be shown. The main non-linearities in a loudspeaker driver will be explained. It will be shown
how a loudspeaker driver generally interacts with a closed enclosure, and the non-linear behav-
ior of the closed enclosure and its interaction with certain diver parameter will be explained.
In some parts of the theory section it was necessary to use concrete loudspeaker driver data to
illustrate certain behaviors. Later in this thesis, in the method section, three drivers that were
used for the measurements and for the simulations will be introduced. The parameters of the
normal unmodiﬁed driver (further explanations later) were used in the examples were driver
data was needed.
Distortion Measures
To characterize the performance of a loudspeaker driver or a complete system, it is important to
look at the produced distortions. Non-linear systems produce additional spectral components
that don’t necessarily have to be present in the original stimulus. These components can be
perceived as distortion or a general loss of clearness in the signal. The two types of distortion
that will be subject of this thesis are the harmonic distortion and the intermodulation distortion.

It can be seen that symmetrical non-linearities
mostly produce odd order harmonics, both for the harmonic distortion and the intermodulation
distortion. Asymmetrical non-linearities produce mostly even order harmonics. The examples
shown in Figure 2 are rather extreme cases, as the upper one is very symmetric and the lower
one is very asymmetric. It could also be the case that a non-linearity has a very symmetric

non-linearities can show certain symmetry in some areas. In cases like that, the distortion
pattern will contain both even and odd order harmonics. The diﬀerent distortion pattern can
be helpful to identify causes for non-linearities.

In the signal chain of a sound reproduction system, the loudspeaker driver is usually the most
non-linear component. Depending on the type of non-linearity, usually additional spectral com-

ponents are generated as a result of distortion. A typical loudspeaker driver has three main
sources of distortion. Those distortions are evoked by the force factor BL, by the compliance
Cms or the stiﬀness Kms respectively, which is the inverse of the compliance, as well as the

Force factor BL
Figure 3: Force factor Bl(x) of an overhang and equal-length coil-gap conﬁguration [7, p.9]
The force factor denotes the coupling between the electrical and the mechanical domain. It is
deﬁned by the cross product between the magnetic ﬂux density B and the length of the voice
coil l. The voice coil l is the part of the voice coil that is in the magnetic ﬁeld in the air gap of
the magnetic system of the loudspeaker driver (as seen in Figure 64). The force factor varies
depending on the displacement. In Figure 3 the course of the displacement dependent force
factor for an overhang and an equal-length coil-gap conﬁguration is shown. A loudspeaker has
an overhang conﬁguration, when the height of the voice coil is higher than the height of the air
gap of the magnetic system (as seen in Figure 64). For the equal-length coil-gap conﬁguration
the height of the voice coil is equal to the height of the air gap. Figure 64 shows the diﬀerence
between those systems. With the equal-length coil-gap conﬁguration, the voice coil has less coil
windings in the magnetic ﬂux when it is displaced. Therefore the voice coil l is smaller so that
the cross product Bl also drops oﬀ. With an overhang conﬁguration, the same amount of coil

can be kept fairly linear in that range. The non-linear force factor can produce high distortion

force factor is not frequency dependent. Similar to that, low frequency components can produce

Suspension Cms/Kms
Figure 4: Upper: Non linear compliance of a loudspeaker driver depending on the displacement;
Lower: restoring force of the suspension. Symmetric (blue), Asymmetric (red). [1, p.6]
The suspension of a driver behaves like a spring with a non linear spring constant. The com-
pliance Cms is the inverse of the stiﬀness Kms. The force deﬂection curve in the lower pane
of Figure 4 describes the behaviour of that spring by F = Kms(x)x = x/Cms(x). It can be
seen in the upper pane of Figure 4 that the compliance gets lower with higher displacement,
e.g suspension gets stiﬀer the higher the displacement. The compliance is fairly linear for small
displacements. The inﬂuence of the compliance is frequency dependent and looses its impact
on the distortions above the resonance frequency of the driver. Therefore the intermodulation
distortion is low.

Where µ is the permeability of the surrounding material, N the number of coil windings, A
the area of the voice coil wire and l the length of the voice coil wire. The varying parameter
here is the permeability of the surrounding material. With positive displacement, the voice coil
moves out of the magnetic system and is more and more surrounded by air, which has a low
permeability. With negative displacement, the voice coil is, to a high degree, surrounded by
iron, which has a much larger permeability as air. As the permeability is a factor in Equation 3,
the inductance increases with increasing permeability. Therefore the inductance in Figure 5
is higher for negative displacements. Figure 5 also shows that the usage of shorting rings can
linearize the inductance. Shorting rings are usually made of copper or aluminium, as the per-
meability of those materials is much closer to the permeability of air, than the permeability of
iron to the permeability of air.
Similarly to the displacement, the inductance also depends on the current in the voice coil.
The relationship between the magnetic ﬁeld strength H and the ﬂux density B is given by
Equation 4.
B = µH
(4)
This relationship is not linear.
Depending on the material, a magnetic saturation will be
reached when increasing the magnetic ﬁeld strength (Figure 65). When no current is ﬂowing
through the voice coil, the magnetic ﬂux is given by ﬁeld strength of the magnetic system and
the momentary surrounding permeability, by Equation 4. When a positive current is ﬂowing
through the voice coil, the voice coil will induce a magnetic ﬁeld that adds up to the magnetic
ﬁeld of the magnetic system. This will result into a higher saturation of the magnetic circuit,
resulting into a reduced permeability (Figure 65). With a negative current the opposite eﬀect
will occur. This eﬀect results in an inductance behaviour, as seen in Figure 6, where a negative
current results in a larger inductance and a positive current in a lower inductance. Similarly
to the displacement, shorting rings can reduce the eﬀect. The magnetic ﬁeld that is induced
by the voice coil will induce a current into the shorting rings. This current will induce another
magnetic ﬁeld, that counters the magnetic ﬁeld of the magnetic system and the voice coil. This
reduces the total ﬂux and the inductance of the coil.

The interaction of the loudspeaker transducer and the enclosure
The type, design and dimension of an enclosure has a high impact on the performance of the
whole loudspeaker. In this case only the interaction of a driver with a closed box will be shown,
as all further simulations, measurements and other investigations are based on a closed box
system.
A complete explanation and discussion on how to model a loudspeaker driver will not be part
of this thesis. A detailed discussion of this subject is available in the literature [9]. Only those
parts relevant to this topic will be highlighted.
Adding a closed box to a driver means primarily adding a compliance, as the air in the enclosure
will act similar to a mechanical spring.

In this section, all components for the evaluation of the enclosure non-linearities will be shown.

Driver builds
To separate the diﬀerent eﬀects that might occur in the diﬀerent measurement setups, three
diﬀerent drivers have been made. Those drivers where based on the PTT6.5W04 driver made
by Puriﬁ. Puriﬁascribes this driver the following attributes:
• Low Force Factor Modulation
– Prevents voice coil current from modulating Force Factor (Bl). The PURIFI motor
completely avoids this classical Achilles’ heel of long stroke drivers. This translates
into low intermodulation (IMD): clean, undistorted midrange even in the presence
of a heavy bass.
– Equates to low impedance modulation, meaning that drive current is not distorted
by cone motion.
• Very Constant Force Factor over Excursion
– Prevents voice coil position from modulating the force factor. This is the classical
cause of “burbling” i.e. amplitude modulation of the midrange by large low-frequency
cone excursions.
• Low Surround Radiation Distortion
– The surround contributes to sound output. Conventional surrounds produce distor-
tion as they deform. PURIFI’s Neutral Surround geometry avoids this mechanism
without constraining motion. This reduces harmonic distortion as well as intermod-
ulation distortion.
• Low Magnetic Hysteresis Distortion
– Hysteresis means that magnetic domains in iron retain traces of previous magneti-
zation/demagnetization cycles which cause distortion when magnetized or demagne-
tized again. This distortion masquerades as benign harmonic distortion when tested
with a sine wave but takes on a crackling or noisy character with more complex
signals.
– Removing hysteresis distortion translates into a very “ﬂuid” presentation with ex-
cellent front/back separation in the stereo image and a perfectly black background
between instruments.
• Cleaner than large-cone short-stroke drivers

– The combination of the above characteristics results in a compact long-stroke driver
that delivers complex sound with a clarity and lack of eﬀort that was previously
the exclusive province of large-area short-stroke drivers. Additionally, thanks to the

Measurement setup
To show the inﬂuences of the diﬀerences between the three drivers, they have been measured.
Those measurements were acoustic measurements done in an anechoic chamber. The anechoic
chamber was very small with 5.5m x 4.4m x 3.4m(l,w,h). Data on the acoustic behavior of the
room was not available. With the small dimensions of the room, room modes could be expected
in the range of interest. Acoustic measurements were still preferred over laser measurements, as
with a laser measurement certain eﬀects of the non-linear eﬀective area could not be captured.
The measurements were done using the Red box (Røde box) made by PuriﬁAudio. The Red
box is a measurement hardware that combines a usb audio interface with an integrated low
noise ampliﬁer for the DUT output and inputs for the measurement of microphone and laser
outputs, as well as the measurement of the DUT current. The Red box is completely operated
by a Matlab GUI and is usually used by PuriﬁAudio for loudspeaker driver development and
product end-test. The Red box Matlab software had several pre-build measurement scripts, like
measurement of frequency response, impedance measurements or two tone measurements, that
could be started through the GUI. All measurements were done with a measuring microphone
in 40cm distance, as the Red box Matlab software had a built in compensation to calculate the
response at 1m distance, from the input measured at 40cm distance. Some pre-build scripts
had to be adjusted for the sake of this projects. For each driver and each enclosure volume
4 diﬀerent measurements were done. Those 4 measurements combined were a measurement
run. Between each measurement run the enclosure was opened and a certain amount of blocks,
and/or rings (as described in the previous section) were added. For a good trade-oﬀbetween
resolution and time needed for the measurement, it was chosen to reduce the volume 0.3 liter
after each measurement run by adding 3 blocks and/or rings. This resulted into 14 measurement
runs per driver. Note: Only 38 blocks and rings ﬁtted into the enclosure, therefore in the last
step only 2 blocks were added. The used measurements were the following:
Raw measurement
The standard measurement script, to measure frequency responses of the fundamental and
harmonics, impedance and current responses, was used for the raw measurement. This mea-
surement utilizes an exponential sweep signal as a stimulus. The script had a functionality to
normalize diﬀerent measurements. This was done by specifying a sound pressure level value
and a frequency range. The script would then iterate over multiple measurements, until the
average sound pressure level within the speciﬁed frequency range would match the speciﬁed

sound pressure level. The chosen sound pressure level was 90dB between 200Hz and 300Hz, as
initial test had shown, that the drivers were quite similar in that range regardless of the volume
in enclosure.
Target measurement
The target measurement was meant to increase comparability between the drivers under normal
conditions. The idea was to drive the normal driver with a stimulus that had a constant voltage
(as done in the raw measurement for the normal driver), and manipulate the stimuli for the
driver with the bad suspension and the driver with the bad motor, so that the frequency
response is the same as the raw frequency response of the normal driver in that measurement
run. If the frequency response is the same, then the displacement is also roughly the same,
therefore all non-linear components, that depend on the displacement, in the individual drivers
are driven the same. This measurement was only done for the driver with the bad suspension

Conclusion
In this thesis the inﬂuence of a closed enclosure with small volumes on the distortion behaviour
was investigated. To achieve this three diﬀerent drivers were made, one driver with an opti-
mized motor system and an optimized suspension, that would not produce Sd-modulation, one
driver with an optimized motor system, but non-optimized suspension, and one driver with an
optimized suspension, but not optimized motor system. It was investigated what happens to
the distortions in a raw case, and in a case were the impact of the too small enclosure was
counteracted, by raising the low frequency content.
One huge problem was that the used simulation did not entirely match the real measurements.
The exact same behaviour of the driver could not be predicted through the simulations, there-
fore it could not completely be trusted. However, the simulations qualitatively showed the same
behaviour as the real measurements therefore they could still be use to analyze the behaviour of
the system. Also the used measurement parameter were problematic, as levels were chosen too
high, so that certain simulations crashed when driven with the same parameter. And even the
two-tone experiment had to be aborted for two of the drivers as the voltage levels were too high.
As it was hardly possible to redo the measurements, the simulations had to be adjusted so that
they could work. This resulted into a worse inter-comparability between the measurements and
the simulations.
After several simulations and measurements it is safe to say that the size of the enclosure has
a clear impact on the harmonic distortion of the whole system. The impact of the enclosure
is strongly displacement dependent and therefore strongly dependent on the right speaker/en-
closure alignment, as small enclosures lead to peaks in the displacement. When a target curve
is used to cope for the bad bass response of a too small enclosure, high THD will occur when
frequencies around half of the system resonance frequency are excited, as the second harmonic
is the dominant contribution to the THD. The eﬀect of Sd-modulation, that is produced by
certain suspensions ampliﬁed this eﬀect. It can even be said that for very small enclosures and
thus high Qtc values the THD is dominated by the distortion that is produced by the enclosure,
especially when Sd-modulation is involved.
The contribution of the enclosure to the intermodulation distortion is rather small. Similar to
the THD it is dependent on displacement and is the highest, at f = fc. Even high changes in the
system quality factor have a rather small impact on the intermodulation distortion produced
by the enclosure. This is due to the low impact in higher frequency regions. The intermodula-
tion distortion is dominated by driver non-linearities. When the driver has a suspension with
Sd-modulation, the intermodulation distortion produced by the enclosure can have a higher
impact on the total intermodulation distortion.
Finally, based in the previous investigations it can be concluded that an acoustical alignment
is still important. It is easily possible to cope for the loss of low frequency content of a too
small enclosure with a DSP. Still it is not advisable to drive the system at and below the
resonance frequency of the system. Counteracting the loss of low frequencies requires a higher
current. Therefore the use of a driver with a very well designed motor system and without
Sd-modulation is not as prone to the formation of intermodulation distortion. The THD that
60 of 61

August 24, 2020
has its maximum at f = fc/2 can not be avoided, as it is dominated by the air-spring, when
very small enclosures are used. In this case the system should not be driven in this frequency
areas.
