Abstract 
The interest in timber construction, is increasing around the world, especially in medium-and high-
rise buildings, but there are some challenges due to the basic properties of wood. Because wood is a 
combustible material, it contributes to fires, which will expose and threaten the lives of buildings 
occupants in a different way than in non-combustible structures. Therefore, a better knowledge and 
understanding of fire behaviour inside these buildings is necessary.  
In this context, this thesis studies three different room sizes constructed of wood and concrete, 
respectively, using numerical modelling in the Fire Dynamics Simulator. A mesh of wood cribs was 
placed inside the compartment to investigate fire spread in the different compartment configurations. 
All compartments were designed to have the smallest opening factor possible, in order to be 
representative of buildings with multi-glass-layered windows that would not necessarily break in the 
event of a fire.  
The results showed that the fire inside the compartments does not travel directly from the initial fire 
to the surrounding wood cribs. Instead, in the wooden compartment, the fire spread from the initial 
fire on the floor to the nearby wall and then to the ceiling, where it spread until the wood cribs on the 
floor were ignited, through heat radiation. While in the concrete compartments, the fire source burned 
until the end and did not spread further, because the compartment material is non-combustible, and 
thus the temperature did not become high enough for ignition in the large compartments studied. 
The models included thermocouples and 3D slices to study the temperature development and 
distribution. The results showed that the temperature is very high under the ceiling, and the 
temperature is getting lower closer to the floor. The temperature inside the wood compartments 
increased to 1000 °C during the fire duration. The temperature rose and fell very quickly due to the 
size of the compartment, while the temperature rise was very small and almost uniform inside the 
concrete compartments during the entire fire duration of 30 minutes. 
Keywords: Travelling fire, large open wood compartment, Fire spread, temperature development, 
heat release rate (HRR) and numerical simulations

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
As 3D model technology progresses, the architecture of building’s evolves, becoming more unique 
while also getting more complex. In particular, modern, large, multiple-story buildings such as 
commercial buildings etc. These kinds of buildings often consist of tall atriums, and large open 
compartments. The atrium and open compartment increase the building's ventilation, lighting 
conditions, togetherness inside the building etc. Other than that, high-rise buildings with open 
compartments, atriums and skyscrapers are very popular and are seen as a symbol of modern 
society[1]. Therefore, we are building more and more high-rise buildings in the world, mostly in the 
form of steel, composite, and concrete, which emit more CO2, and this does not fit into our modern 
society. CO2 emissions must be reduced, and the materials must be sustainable to protect our 
environment and society [2]. Most of the building industry in the world uses a combination of 
concrete and steel materials because the combination of these two materials increases the strength 
and rigidity of concrete structures [1] 
To achieve a more realistic fire safety design in high-rise buildings, which consist of atriums and/or 
large open compartments, engineers have done some experimental and numerical simulations to 
assess the buildings' life safety. The results indicated that the fire may start to burn locally and then 
the flame will spread to other areas. This type of fire was also observed based on fires in real life that 
have happened over the past two decades, such as the World Trade Center Towers [3]. Another 
statistical survey of an open compartment of a modern building “Informatics Forum” which is located 
at the university of Edinburgh, revealed that traditional fire safety design methods were only 
applicable to 8% of the total volume of the building (other areas being out of range due to Eurocode 
limitations, such as opening factor (> 0.2), compartment height (4m), size of the compartment (> 
500m2). [4] 
These findings highlight the need for a more accurate definition of fire situations that considers the 
dramatically diverse spatial layouts that have become more popular in modern architecture. For the 
sake of fire safety engineering design, there is currently a lot of interest in approaches that represent 
more realistic fire scenarios. 
==PAGE:8==

To build a unique and architecturally complex building in the future, while the building is also 
sustainable, the best solution could be wood. Wood is one of the world’s oldest building materials 
and most sustainable. At present, some countries have already built some of the tallest buildings by 
using wood as the main structural material, and this has been a big success[5]. 
The reason to use wood as a building material is that wood has a lot of qualities that have not been 
paid attention to before. Some of the qualities are that the timber material will shorten the construction 
process, because the material makes it possible to prefabricate elements outside the construction site, 
and wood is also lighter than any other building material and therefore requires a smaller 
foundation[5]. 
But as wood is a combustible material, it often contributes to unwanted fires, which result in numerous 
injuries and fatalities [6]. Therefore, the use of wood was limited due to its flammability and the 
spread of fire characteristics in the past, especially for high-rise buildings, because most of them 
would be burned. Fire usually behaves differently inside a large open compartment that is built up 
with wood construction, because the wood's natural property is combustible and often contributes to 
fires. Until now, some experiments have been conducted inside a small wood compartment to study 
traveling fires[7], but not inside a large open compartment with fully exposed wood to provide some 
fundamental knowledge and understanding of traveling fires. However, a series of experiments are in 
process inside a large open concrete compartment with CLT ceiling to study travel fire[8]. The 
temperature conditions and design of wood elements are based on the standard temperature-time 
curve (DS/EN 1991-1-2 [9] and ISO 834 [[10]]), which just fulfils the requirements of the authorities. 
As a result, there is a great need for a series of experiments and studies of travelling fire and 
temperature conditions inside large open wood compartments to collect more acceptable data that can 
be reasonable in fire safety engineering communities to better protect wood buildings in the future 
[11].

1.2 The Scope of this Project 
The goal of this project is to incorporate the efforts that have already been made in the field of 
travelling fires inside large open wood and concrete compartments or atriums, as well as to conduct 
additional numerical studies in the field to better protect high-rise timber buildings from travelling 
fires. In this context, the focus of this master project will be to apply a numerical tool (FDS) to 
investigate the fire spread inside large open wood and concrete compartments. Other than that, the 
maximum fire temperature inside the compartment, which will be achieved in the flashover phase, 
will also be investigated to indicate the development of the temperature T (t) in the compartments 
and the effect of the temperature on the spread of fire to other areas to clarify the reason behind it. 
The scope of this study is to show how important it is to include a different way of fire investigation, 
particularly by a numerical tool (FDS) simulation. The fire safety of a building is based on the 
traditionally accepted methods, which are not good enough for high-rise complex buildings that are 
made of timber. The methods are also time-consuming, and they sometimes require an experimental 
test, which will be much more expensive.  Each full-scale test for a normal operation typically costs 
a lot of money, and full-scale tests are often required to fully evaluate the performance of a proposed 
fire safety system. Even a series of tests may be inconclusive in some situations. A testing series does 
not guarantee that the scientific proof required to approve a code modification or validate an existing 
code will be produced. Furthermore, the testing involves weeks of planning to put up complex 
equipment, a long period of data gathering, and time to analyse the results. 
In two ways, the availability of FDS has the potential to decrease the costs of full-scale fire testing. 
Firstly, simulation studies can be used to replace some experiments. Secondly, room configuration, 
amount of flammable items, heat, and smoke movement can be simulated in a short time. FDS can 
also be used to select the most critical characteristics for full-scale testing, allowing full-scale tests to 
be employed more effectively. As a result, the FDS is an important tool in fire research, both for 
studying circumstances that are too costly to test extensively experimentally and for assisting in the 
design of data gathering operations that are precisely targeted to provide as much information as 
possible with a limited budget.

2.1 Ignition 
When investigating fire spread, it is important to understand how ignition will happen. Ignition 
happens when three important factors fuel, oxygen, and heat, are all available at the same time. When 
they are available, the ignition happens and there will be a fire. The fire process is illustrated in the 
fire triangle (Figure 1 chemical chain reaction). 
 
 
 
 
Ignition, fire spread, and burning rate are all factors that influence fire behaviour. These three fire-
related items can be considered independently, but they share something in common that causes them 
to influence fire behaviour together. Let’s start with the ignition system. Ignition can occur in two 
ways: controlled ignition or autoignition. Controlled ignition will be achieved either through a small 
flame, a spark, etc., and autoignition occurs when a chemical reaction takes place inside the fuel, 
which will generate enough energy to heat it. In this way, more energy is created because some of the 
energy was already lost in the environment due to conduction and radiation. It is necessary that the 
fuel reach a certain energy level to become more gasified; otherwise, the autoignition will not happen. 
When the fuel is gasified and a given temperature is available, the ignition happens.

Here is detailed description of the control ignition and autoignition of materials. Ignition of materials 
in our environment, including wood products, can happen in two ways. Firstly, the materials will be 
heated (self-heating) and will burn, and secondly, the materials will be exposed to fire directly and 
burn as wood often does. As wood is a combustible material, it will also burn when the materials 
reach a given temperature. This can occur as a reaction between oxygen and hot gases produced by 
the wood when it reaches a temperature of 300 °C[24]. This process is called pyrolysis of cellulose, 
which is an endothermic process of pyrolysis that develops gas. The gas consists of carbon monoxide, 
carbon dioxide, hydrogen, and hydrocarbons. When the gases are released outside, the exothermic 
reaction will occur, meaning that the gases and oxygen will meet each other, causing the wood to 
ignite. There are many kinds of wood, like hardwood, wood-oak, plywood, etc. Wood-oak will burn 
slowly and have a longer burn time, while softwood will burn easily, faster, and with a shorter burn 
time, but wet wood is difficult to ignite. [25]

2.2 Flame Spread on Solid Surfaces 
A lot of unique and modern materials are available and useful in offices, apartments, and other areas 
in our modern society, which has impacted our lives in a positive direction[26], but this has decreased 
the fire safety of building engineering, because most of the materials are combustible, and this will 
increase the risk that the building burns very quickly, because the fire will spread much faster through 
these materials. 
After the ignition of a material, the fire will spread in a variety of ways that are described in detail 
below, and it is critical to address this in this master's thesis because the focus of this research is on 
fire spreads and travelling fires in large open compartments and/or atriums. The fire can spread in 
the following ways[27] : 
• The fire can spread when a burning object comes into direct contact with other objects,  
• Via radiation when the fire is strong enough and can emit sufficient heat to ignite combustible 
materials that are not in direct contact with the burning objects. 
• Via conduction when the heat is transferred through materials that can ignite other materials 
that are in contact.  
• Via convection when air is collected above a fire at a higher level, such as below the ceiling. 
When this happens, a large amount of hot gas and smoke will gather, and this will cause the 
room temperature to rise to the point where the autoignition of objects might happen. 
• Via flashover, when the flames spread out of the room through an opening and the fire will 
spread to the neighbouring sections, etc.  
• The last thing that can affect fire spread is wind. The wind has a big effect too. That can help 
the flame to spread other areas much quickly. 
The fire spread on the solid surfaces is determined by the relative burning of the construction materials 
along the exposed surfaces. The critical heat rate of flame is normally calculated by measuring the 
distance travelled and noting the time it takes for the flame to move forward. The velocity of flame 
spread on a thin or thick objects will be determined by the following equation 2.3 and 2.4.

2.2.1 Downward Flame Spread  
When a fire is ignited, the fire will normally burn in the wind's direction, but some factors can cause 
the fire to spread in any direction. The downward fire will typically happen when the surface

2.3 Fire Development inside a Compartment 
Fire development inside a compartment is described by the four stages: incipient, growth, fully 
developed, and decay phase, which are also illustrated below in figure 7. The amount of fire depends 
on the fuel and the ventilation.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
• The growth or preflashover is the phase when the fire is localized around its source and the 
temperature in the compartments is still relatively low.   
• The steady phase, or fully developed, is the phase when all combustible materials are involved 
in fire and flames appear to fill the entire compartment and escape through the ventilation 
openings. The maximum energy release rate and room temperature are also identified in this 
phase. The fire is fuel controlled in the growth phase, where a sufficient amount of air is 
available, and when the flashover occurs, there is not enough air in this stage to burn all the 
materials, thus becoming a ventilation-controlled fire. 
• The decay phase is the phase when the fire begins to die as the fuel is consumed. Because in 
this phase, it is important to have detailed knowledge about the materials that are used in the 
building. Because the maximum temperature that has been achieved in this phase will fall, but 
the maximum element temperature has a meaning about how much the elements are damaged 
due to fire, i.e., the temperature will move to the centreline of a material, and there is a huge 
risk of damage to it. 
These phases describe the fires in the compartments. The size of the fire is defined in terms of the 
rate of hate release (kW) as a function of time. The cold air will accumulate in the lower part of the

2.4 FDS 
The Fire Dynamic Simulator is a computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software that was created by 
NIST and VTT to solve fire-driven fluid flow problems and describe how it spreads. This numerical 
tool can solve the fire behaviour in complex buildings with a focus on smoke and heat transmission 
from fires. The software was created to solve numerically variants of the Navier-Stokes equations 
appropriate for low-speed, thermally driven flow with an emphasis on smoke and heat transport from 
fires. The Smoke View is a visualization program used to display the output of an FDS 
simulation[28]. 
The first version of the FDS was published to the public in 2000, and the current version that will be 
applied in this project is 6.7.16. The FDS was created to solve the real fire challenges in fire protection 
engineering. 
FDS/PyroSim a full file modelling system that allows you to model, perform, and evaluate fire 
simulations quickly and efficiently. PyroSim can model smoke movement, heat transfer, and flame 
propagation using the physics of FDS to determine tenability and available safe evacuation time. 
Burning and other fire situations can also be reconstructed using PyroSim. In its default setting, the 
tool has the following important parameters [29]: 
 
• Modelling of compartments geometry 
• Creation of uniform, non-uniform, and multiple mesh. 
• Fire growth and fire spread 
• Heat and combustion transportation inside the compartment.  
• Heat transmission between gas and solid surfaces via radiant and convective heat 
transfer 
• Surface temperature, room temperature, smoke view and fire velocity and fire 
direction 
• Pyrolysis

2.5 Definition of Travelling Fire 
Many researchers or fire engineers, such as Jamie Stern-Gottfried, [31] have already studied traveling 
fires in large open compartments, atriums, and multi-storey buildings in order to provide a better 
understanding and knowledge of fire travelling fire. According to these researchers, the compartments 
should be divided into two primary fields: near and far fields[16]. These zones are related to the fire. 
The fire travels within the compartment from near field to far field when there are enough hot gases 
to ignite. The heat is most strong in the close field region, which describes the area where the fire is 
burning, and the structural materials are directly exposed to flames and get the most extreme heating. 
On the other hand, the far field, which is further away from the fire source, where heat is generated 
by the smoke layer. He investigated the idea of travelling fires several times, and he provided some 
articles about the idea [31], [32]. The literature review shows that, with the help of experiments and 
numerical simulations, they confirmed that the temperature distribution is not uniform in a large 
compartment during a fire, which contrasts with the standard requirements compared to the large 
compartments. [13]. The figure 8 illustrates travelling fire and near and far field.

3.4.2 Import Material Data 
In these simulations, two different materials will be 
used to define the geometry of the model: wood and 
concrete. PyroSim includes a database file that contains 
data and data references. The material will be imported 
by selecting the material from the library to the current 
model. When this is done, then it is possible to define 
the physical properties of the solid surface. The thermal 
response of a solid surface can be calculated using the 
material parameters. The surface is used as a layered 
surface, and the heat transfer through the surface uses 
the boundary condition of air at ambient temperature. A 
brief description of the pyrolysis reaction, combustion, 
thermocouple, and 3D slice is given below. 
In these simulations in FDS/PyroSim, the fire will be defined by pyrolysis and combustion and by 
thermocouples and 3D slice the room temperature and direction will be measured: 
• Pyrolysis: The pyrolysis is a process in which a solid surface, like wood in this case, generates 
fuel vapor when the wood is heated and reaches a given temperature, releasing combustible 
gas. The pyrolysis temperature of wood ranges between 250 and 300 °C. For this to happen, 
the gas phase reaction must be defined, and when the wood reaches 250 to 300 °C, it releases 
carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, hydrogen, and hydrocarbons. When the gases are released 
outside and come into contact with oxygen, the wood will ignite. This pyrolysis process in 
PyroSim is defined by a single homogeneous material which is heated and gasified completely 
when the HRR is specified, the PyroSim uses the heat of combustion defined by the reaction 
to calculate the fuel vapor mass release rate that will result in the gas release. In these 
simulations, the pyrolysis temperature is considered to be 300 °C [24]. 
• Heat of combustion: This is defined by the reaction of fuel vapor with oxygen. When the 
chemical reaction between gases happens, Fuel vapor results from pyrolysis, and oxygen is 
provided by air. The combustion process mix of fuel and oxygen will control the reaction rate.  
The heat of combustion for the wood walls are applied to be 18 MJ/kg [35] and for the wood 
crib 16.7 MJ/kg[37].

4.1.6 Sub-Conclusion 
The heat release rate, travelling fire, and temperature differences between the wood and concrete 
compartment are compared. The results of the fire development inside the wood compartment showed 
that the fire development agrees with the concept of travelling fires, which was the main purpose of 
this master paper. The fire spread from the burner area to the nearby wall and then to the ceiling. 
When the nearby wall and ceiling were ignited, then the fire expanded on these surfaces further, until 
the fire and high temperature ignited the wood cribs and then travelled step by step from the wood 
cribs to the wood cribs until the whole compartment was burned. This traveling fire is illustrated with 
some 3D visualizations that showed that the fire develops step by step until it becomes a fully 
developed state of full compartment involvement. Other than that, the 3D slice shows a clear increase 
in temperature as the burning region passes. It is clearly seen that the temperature developed 
uniformly in the compartment before the flashover, but non-uniformly after the flashover, in the decay 
phase, because the compartment size is very large. The size, oxygen, and amount of combustible 
material impact that the temperature would not be uniform during the flashover and after. But this 
temperature scenario does not apply to the concrete compartment, where the temperature almost 
developed uniformly inside the concrete compartment during the whole fire, except for the burner 
area, where the temperature increased for a while, but did not spread further. It could be possible that 
the fire could spread further in the concrete compartment if the simulation had been running for a 
long time to produce enough heat to ignite the wood cribs. 
From the results of the fire inside the wood compartment, it is observed that a high amount of oxygen 
is available inside the compartment, which leads to a quick higher fire and temperature, but shorter 
duration. This is due to the fact that a larger size, where enough oxygen is available, leads to a higher 
temperature but a shorter duration, which is good for the load-bearing structural, which does not 
become more damaged and thus the fire is assessed to be fuel-controlled.  
Furthermore, a calculation of compartment temperature and fire duration is prepared by using the 
Danish fire method. Comparing the calculation and simulation results. The simulation results are 
acceptable. The calculation results can be seen in Appendix F. 
Moreover, a calculation of the flame height is implemented to determine why the flame can’t spread 
inside the concrete compartment. As the results showed, the flame height is calculated to be 0.52 m. 
That was not enough to ignite the wood cribs, and thus the fire didn’t spread further. The calculation 
results can be seen in Appendix G.

release rate increased as well, which most likely happened a mistake during the simulation set up. 
Another error is that the heat release rate results are higher than the heat release rate of the fire source, 
which is 500 kW and used in these simulations. The temperature was also analysed inside the concrete 
compartments, and the results showed that the temperature was very low compared to the wood 
compartments, where the temperature inside the wood compartments was around 1000 °C, while it 
was only 120 °C. The temperature was almost uniform inside the concrete compartments during the 
whole fire duration. 
Finally, if there was used a higher heat release rate fire source and if the simulation was run for a 
longer time, it could be possible that the surrounded wood cribs would be ignited after a while and 
then the fire would travel further and further. It is absolutely a good idea to be investigated in the 
future.

4.4 Uncertainties in the Models and Experiments 
In this paper, the FDS/PyroSim numerical tool was tested by conducting two experiments. One of the 
experiments, which is a wood crib and consists of 104 pieces, was done by NIST, and the other one 
is a small timber compartment, which was done in Sweden, and the burner fuel consists of wood crib 
and furniture’s. The heat release rate of these experiments is tested by the FDS/PyroSim to reproduce 
it as it was experienced in the experiments. The results of the heat release rates are illustrated and 
discussed, which can be seen in Appendix C and D. 
There are different kinds of uncertainties in these experiments that must be considered. Some of the 
uncertainties can be from the assumptions that were made in these two simulations, because there 
was a lack of information from the experiments. This information is related to thermal material 
properties, the amount of burner material and using of different materials as a fire source, especially 
in mass timber compartment, which is done in Sweden. Lack of information causes that the heat 
release rate which is experienced in the experiment to be reproduced with a deviation, and this can 
underestimate the real results that have been conducted in this master project’s investigation. The 
overall uncertainties are a combination of the lack of information related to the experiments and 
assumptions that are conducted for the model set up.  
The next challenge is the fire condition. In most experimental research, the fire is ignited in such a 
way that it is difficult to do it in the FDS/PyroSim. It’s difficult to define the fire source as it has been 
in the experiment and burned. One of the examples is the experiment that was done in Sweden. In 
this experiment, the author burned a bin full of crinkle paper, weighing around 650 grams, with one 
liter of liquid. This is also a huge challenge because this is not easy to define, and you need to progress 
it in a way that is roughly the same as the experiment. This challenge can impact whether the fire 
starts very quickly compared to the experiment or slowly, which will also cause that the flashover 
and heat release rate to not be the same as the experiment. 
Regarding the real simulations, which were conducted in this paper to study travelling fire, the 
development of temperature and heat release rate inside the large open wood and concrete 
compartments. It is important to mention that the factors that are used to set up the models are based 
on the literature review and the two experiments' available input data. So, this can increase the 
importance of the final results, which is considered as the greatest agreement for this review and will 
increase the value and creditability of this dissertation.

Conclusion 
This paper presents three compartment fire tests with different sizes, where each size is simulated 
both with wood and concrete material, which is modelled with a mesh of wood cribs to measure the 
heat release rate, travelling fires, and temperature development in large open compartments. This 
paper was performed to report these results and consider the effects of combustible (wood) and non-
combustible (concrete). In addition, these results are also compared between the wood compartments 
of different sizes and simultaneously between the concrete compartments of different sizes to study 
whether the compartment size has an effect on the fire and temperature development. The temperature 
was measured by some thermocouples that were evenly distributed throughout the compartments. 
The most significant events that were observed and reached by these numerical studies show that the 
fire inside the wood compartments spread from the burner area to the near walls and then to the 
ceiling, once the ceiling was ignited. The fire spread via the ceiling to the other end of the 
compartment, at the same time the fire spread across the wood crib on the floor and further throughout 
the whole compartment, where flashover and the duration of a fully developed fire were also 
experienced, which was very short because the compartments were very large and a sufficient amount 
of fresh air already existed, which is the reason. The decay phase continued indefinitely until 0:30 h 
after the flashover, and the temperature that was reached at this stage was significantly lower than 
300 °C. The fire speed was fast, which can impact occupant evacuation options and firefighter 
response scenarios. These experiences can increase our understanding of the speed and duration of 
fire to provide a robust solution in the future. However, these significant events do not count for the 
concrete compartments because the fire force and temperature were not high enough to ignite the 
wood cribs, and the fire could not expand in the concrete compartment. 
The comparison of temperature results between the wood and concrete compartments shows that the 
temperature was very high inside the wood compartments compared to the concrete compartments. 
The temperature developed uniformly inside the concrete compartments during the fire duration, 
while the temperature developed uniformly during the preflashover and non-uniformly under the fully 
development phase and decay phase inside the wood compartments.  
==PAGE:66==
61 
 
The temperature falls down after the flashover inside the wood compartments, while the temperature 
increased until the end inside the concrete compartments, but generally the temperature was very low 
inside the concrete compartment. 
The next event that has been studied is the heat release rate of the different models that were 
implemented using both wood and concrete materials. This event is only experienced inside the wood 
compartments, where this energy is released by the burning of wood structures and wood cribs, while 
in the concrete compartments, the wood cribs aren’t ignited, thus causing this event to not be 
experienced. Moreover, the results showed that as the compartment size was increased, the heat 
release rate also increased. 
However, the method that was used in this master paper produced reasonable outcomes. They agreed 
qualitatively with the validation models' output data and literature, but more research is needed to 
study the concept of travelling fire.

5.1 Suggestions to Further Work 
There is clearly future work to be done on exploring the capability of the FDS/PyroSim approach. 
The model validations represented in this master project have addressed some of the most important 
problems that must be considered in the future, and this will help the researchers in further work to 
gather and provide some realistic results. Moreover, a combination of wood and concrete 
compartment test is also conducted which has given some good ideas to be implemented in the future. 
This section will provide some suggestions regarding these challenges that were faced in this master 
for future interest. 
The first possibility is that if there is an opportunity to do a simple experiment inside a wood 
compartment to investigate the heat release rate of the fire process, where you already know the 
material's thermal properties, amount, and thickness, the fire source and fire ignition are also 
important to be high-prioritised because when setting up in FDS/PyroSim, it is important to use the 
correct method, otherwise the results will not be sufficient. This simple experiment will be a huge 
advantage when it is time to model in FDS/PyroSim because you will know the process and, 
obviously, you will not face all the challenges of reproducing the heat release rate as it was 
experienced in the experiment. In this way, it will be possible to avoid the challenges that were faced 
in this paper. It is important to consider this because the determination of pyrolysis and the definition 
of the ignition process are very important and must be defined in PyroSim as the experiments. A 
simple model will help to obtain realistic results with the use of PyroSim with the minimum number 
of errors. 
The second possibility, which has been left for the future due to lack of time, was to examine the 
effect of the more opening, size etc. The quantity of windows and doors is a key factor in fire 
behaviour. This has an impact on not just the spread of fire due to the amount of oxygen entering the 
compartment fire through the opening, but also the amount and velocity of hot gases. All simulations 
are studied and simulated with a minimum size opening in this paper. This shows that the small 
opening factor has a big influence on the HRR curve, because the fire after the flashover will only 
move around the opening and will not become down. And this issue can be because of PyroSim, since 
PyroSim has some difficulties due to this, which can be a good opportunity to investigate in the future 
by comparing the results between a compartment with minimum and maximum opening factor.  
The third possibility is the use of a combination of wood and concrete in a large open compartment. 
A model with a combination of these two materials is simulated to create an overview of how fire 
==PAGE:68==
63 
 
will travel and develop in this condition. As it is seen in the below figures, which is a comparison of 
a complete wood compartment and a combination of wood and concrete, the heat release rate results 
show that the fire will travel from the burning area to the far fields by burning the wood walls and 
ceiling, and then ignite the wood cribs by high temperature and direct radiation from the ceiling. But 
the total heat release rate inside the compartments, which is a combination of wood and concrete, is 
lower than the complete wood compartment, which is very exciting to consider in a high-rise building 
or large open compartment. This is a great opportunity to design with a combination of wood and 
concrete due to the excellent and individual effect they have on protecting the most important exposed 
areas from fire. It is possible to do it with gypsum or concrete layers to avoid future threats. This 
research needs some independence and deeper analysis of particular new proposals and methods to 
achieve some good results.

C Validation Simulation Model 
In order to use FDS for fire simulations in this master project, the supervisor demands a validation 
simulation to ensure that the FDS software used in this master project is feasible and setup process is 
correct. The validation is done according to the experiment, which was done by NIST. The 
information is available in the FDS validation guide [38] and the NIST Technical Note 2102 [39]. 
The test is a full-scale fire test with a wood crib. The wood crib is made up of 100 pieces with 10 
layers and 10 pieces in each layer. The dimensions are 56 cm by 56 cm with a cross-section of 3.8 
cm. The material properties, etc., are considered according to the validation guide and literature 
review. The duration time is 60 min. In the experiment they ignited the wood crib with 0.75 kg crinkle 
paper with uniformly spread at the base and ignited at the two points. The purpose of this validation 
is to compare the heat release rate between the simulation and the experiment, and this will increase 
the creditability of the tool and processes that will be provided in this master project.

C.2 The Validation Results 
Figure 29 compares the measured and predicted heat release rates between simulations and the 
experiment. The simulations are all performed using thin wood as a fire source. The grid resolution 
is considered to be 8 cm, 4 cm, and 2 cm for the crib, as NIST used. As it is seen in the below figure, 
the simulation with a 4 cm cell size has reached a roughly similar heat release rate as the experiment. 
The experiment reached its highest heat release rate of 396 kW at 23 minutes, while the simulation 
with 4 cm reached 328 kW at 18 minutes. The growth and decay phases are also the same as in the 
experiment. But the simulations with the 8 cm are over the predicted heat release rate. The reason is 
that the mesh size is bigger than the wood crib size and this will cause that the wood crib will not 
burn as it must, and this is the most important value to considered because cell size has a huge impact 
to the results. In the experiment, NIST burns crinkle paper, which makes it impossible to do in 
PyroSim. The other reason is that the experiment is done in the research laboratory, where the 
conditions are different in PyroSim than in the laboratory. The last reason, which is the most important 
parameter to consider, is the pyrolysis process. The wood pyrolysis process is very complex. The 
wood will create char-lay, which prevents the heat from burning further, but sometimes the char-lay 
will break and the heat will be transferred inside the wood, which will burn more and more.

simulate the heat release rate that is experienced in the experiments. This was tested by performing 
two validation models (wood crib and timber compartment) where a sufficient setup is determined

assumptions which are related to the literate review.
