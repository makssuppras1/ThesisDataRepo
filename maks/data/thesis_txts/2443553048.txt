This master thesis is prepared at the Department of Civil Engineering at the Technical

The aim of the master thesis is to investigate how the indoor climate in bedrooms
affect our sleep quality and next-day performance. Associate Professor, Pawel Wargocki

Abstract
More than 20 years of one’s life is spent in the bedroom when sleeping. Sleep qual-
ity is essential for our health, well-being and next-day performance. However, there is
very little information on how bedroom air quality affects the quality of sleep. In recent
years, many low-cost sleep monitors and actigraphs have made their way into the market.
They allow carrying out reasonably simple measurements of sleep quality at a large scale.
This project attempts to shed some light on these issues by comparing the performance
of a few selected sleep monitors and by measuring the effects of bedroom air quality
on sleep quality. To meet the latter objective, 30 persons were recruited to sleep in
their bedrooms for two weeks under two different conditions: with the door to the bed-
room opened and closed. Only people who sleep alone were selected. Bedrooms were
not ventilated by mechanical systems and windows stayed closed during measurements.
Recruited persons slept with the wearable monitor which registered their sleep qual-
ity from Monday to Friday, i.e., four nights on each week. Every morning they rated
their sleep quality, air quality in the bedroom as well as made logical thinking tasks
for 3 minute that examined their cognitive performance. For that purpose, they used a
specially developed app on a smartphone. Temperature, relative humidity, and carbon
dioxide concentration were continuously logged. The measurement showed that there
was a clear difference in the CO2 level between sleeping with the bedroom door open
and closed, respectively. Temperature and relative humidity did not differ significantly.
Measured bedroom conditions were compared with objectively and subjectively mea-
sured sleep quality. It was found that the objectively measured data corresponded well
with the subjective cognitive test and the next-day questionnaire responses regarding
the indoor air quality. It was shown that the subjects next-day performance improved
when sleeping with the bedroom door open.
To meet the former objective, several low-cost monitors were purchased. It was found
that the tested monitors had different focus and functions. In case of this master thesis,
the Fitbit Alta HR and the Sleepace Sleep Dot seemed to be the appropriate choice for
tracking sleep hence they both provide the requested information.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The current study supplements to the limited knowledge concerning the relation between
indoor climate and the quality of sleep. We examined how sleep quality and the next
day performance are affected when sleeping with the internal bedroom door closed or
open. We expect that the intervention, sleeping with the bedroom door open, improves
our sleep quality and next day performance in contrary to sleeping with the bedroom
door closed.
The paper ”Thermal Environment IAQ and Sleep,” (P. Wargocki et al., 2018) asks
an extremely relevant question - “Why do we know so little about thermal and IAQ and
effects on sleep?” - the simple answer is due to limitations in the technology. With
the new technology, it is possible to monitor sleep with wrist-worn unobtrusive sensors
from home in own bedrooms and not in sleep labs. These unobtrusive measurements
allow identifying the different sleep stages. With that said the research can move out
of the laboratory and into the bedrooms - which is what this master thesis is focusing on.
Current field measurements indicate that the levels of CO2 in bedrooms occupied by
two people sleeping, easily exceed 2000 ppm, (Canha et al., 2017). Consequently, the
limited findings on the subject of sleep quality and bedroom IAQ have been the primary
motivation to do the research presented in this master thesis.
1.1
Why is This Relevant?
A typical winter day in Copenhagen has outdoor temperatures around 0-5 °C (DMI,
2018). Ventilating a Copenhagen apartment during winter can thus easily turn into a
chilly experience. Many Copenhagen apartments are typically built in the early 20th
century hence these apartments are naturally ventilated by window opening. Opening a
window over a longer time is consequently not always a solution during winter. However,
the absence of ventilation can cause high levels of CO2 in bedrooms, (Bekö et al., 2010)
and poor indoor air quality and therefore lead to poor sleep quality, (Strøm-Tejsen et
al., 2016).
It has been shown that sleeping with an open window improves the air quality in the
bedroom and thereby the sleep quality by increasing the air change rate. A Dutch study
has investigated the impact of window/door opening-mediated bedroom ventilation and

Background
2.1
Sleep Physiology
2.1.1
Why We Sleep
Human beings approximately spend one-third of their lives sleeping. Sleep is universal
for all forms of life, and its absence can cause physiological consequences. Until recently,
there were no concrete answers regarding ’why we sleep’ compared to other basic drives
in life (eating, drinking and reproducing). It was not fully understood why we suffer
from these health consequences when not sleeping. The book Why we sleep, (Walker,
M., 2017) summarizes the key aspects of why sleep is so essential.
In the last decade, an explosion of scientific discoveries has been made on the field.
There is not only one reason behind why we sleep but numerous. To understand the
importance of sleep it is necessary to understand, that sleep contains different sleep
stages - all equally important. The two main types of sleep is REM and NREM.
Dream sleep (REM) is important. When dreaming, we relieve painful memories and
creates a space where the past and the present is melded together. This proceeds to
inspire creativity.
During other parts of our sleep (NREM) the brain restores memories. Sleep enhances
our ability to make logical decisions, memorize and learn. A good night of sleep improves
these abilities. Sleep also helps us to mend our emotions and refine our immune system.
Our metabolism and appetite are regulated when we sleep furthermore sleep slows the
effects of aging and increase longevity, (Walker, M., 2017).
2.1.2
Course of Sleep
Sleep is essential for our mental and psychological health and changes throughout our life.
The course of our sleep differs accordingly to our age. A general trend indicates that the
efficiency of sleep declines with age. In the infant years, sleep is very important. From
the first years of life to adulthood the changes of how sleep is initiated and maintained
is distinct, (Colten and Altevogt, 2016). Infants sleep approximately 16 hours a day.
Later in life sleep is still important but the amount of sleep is decreased. Adults needs
between 7-9 hours of sleep. Table 2.1 shows the optimal course of sleep for young adults
age 18-25 years and adults age 26-64 years, (Hirshkowitz, M, 2016).

2.1.3
Sleep Efficiency
Sleep efficiency is the ratio of total time spent asleep during the night and the total
amount of time spent in bed.
It can also be described as how successfully sleep is
initiated and maintained, (Brandon Peters, 2018).
2.1.4
Sleep Latency
The expression sleep onset latency (SOL), refers to the time it takes to fall asleep. The
length of time going from full wake-fullness to sleep (normally from wakefulness to light
sleep), (Walker, 2017).
2.1.5
REM and NREM
Every night humans cycle through different stages of sleep. Sleep is characterized by
two major types of sleep, rapid eye movements (REM) and none-rapid eye movement
(NREM). The two stages of sleep each have unique characteristics. The cycling through
the two main stages of sleep is still not fully understood. However, with the absent of
one of the sleep stages and/or irregular cycling through the sleep stages, it can lead to
sleep disorders, (Walker, 2017).
REM sleep also referred as dream sleep is defined by the presence of mixed-frequency
and low-voltage brain wave activity, muscle atonia, and rapid eye movements. Through-
out an individual’s sleep, the periods with REM sleep prolongs throughout the night.
Initially, the REM period only accounts for 1-5 minutes but as the night progresses the
REM period progresses, see figure 2.1, (Walker, 2017).

NREM is divided into four stages, and each of the stages is representing a continuum of
relative depth, see figure 2.1. The four NREM stages are associated with physiological
activity and brain activity. Sleep stage 1 serves as the initial sleep stage. Sleep stage 2
requires more intense stimuli than when going from awake to sleep stage 1. Sleep stage
3 and 4 also called slow wave sleep (SWS) occurs mostly during the first part of the
night, (Colten and Altevogt, 2016).
A complete cycle of sleep passes through REM and NREM. Typically each cycle takes
an average of 90 to 110 minutes. Within these cycles, each stage lasts between 5 to 15
minutes. From figure 2.1 it is seen that the sleep stages progress cyclically from stage 1
through stage 3 and 4 to REM and then to begin again with stage 1. During a typical
night, we cycle through 4 full stages of sleep and then wakes up in the 5th cycle of
sleep. The first cycles of sleep contain long periods of deep sleep and a relatively short
periods with REM. As the night progresses the periods with deep sleep decreases and
the periods with REM sleep lengthens, (Walker, 2017).

Circadian Rhythm
The circadian rhythm also referred to as the biological 24-hour clock controls humans
sleep/wake cycle and is essential in everyday life determining for physiological and psy-

chological behavior. The circadian rhythm contributes to modulate food consumption
and physical activity along with regulating the body temperature, heart rate, muscle
tone and hormone secretion throughout the day. The rhythm is present for all living

However, the cycle is regulated by sunlight and body temperature, (Colten and Altevogt,

2.2
How To Measure Sleep
2.2.1
Actigraphy
Actigraphy is a method used to monitor human rest/activity cycle including wake/sleep
cycles by wearing a small wrist-worn device (typically a fitness watch). According to
the article Actigraphy (Singh, 2018) the devices monitors activity by collecting data
generated from movements. The movements detected by the device are assigned to pre-
determined epoch intervals by translating the data to digital counts. Most of the devices
used to track sleep patterns utilize this method.
Actigraphy is widely used by athletes both professionals and amateurs to track move-
ment. However, actigraphy is also commonly used in the research field and has become
a significant tool in sleep research. In recent years the actigraphy has become readily
available, more reliable and consistent making sleep research more widespread. There
are several commercial actigraphy devices in a wide range of prices.
The individual devices have different criteria for making sleep/wake estimations, and
the algorithm behind them varies.
Some of the devices use a multi-parametric test,
polysomnography (PSG) together with actigraphy and perform regression and/or dis-
criminant analysis on the paired data. Other devices use an algorithm supplying several
sensitive settings enabling the proportion of wake/sleep the algorithm should predict,
(Singh, 2018).
The term sensitivity refers to the actigraphy’s ability to detect sleep (the ability to
detect the absence of movement). Generally, the reported sensitivity is very high, 90%
or more. However, the specificity ranges much lower (24 % - 65%). Specificity refers to
the ability to detect wakefulness. The specificity has a natural upper limit hence not all
awakenings necessarily are accompanied by movements. This is where the single channel
of actigraphy differs widely in the assessment of sleep/wake from the multi-parametric
test, (Singh, 2018).
In the evaluation of different actigraphy devices, the ability to detect movements (sensi-
tivity) and the ability to detect wakefulness (specificity) are the key information and are

what determines a good actigraphy device. The actigraphy becomes less accurate as the
sleep becomes more fragmented. In general, actigraphy works best when estimating the
total sleep time. If the actigraphy is too sensitive, the device tends to overestimate the
time asleep thus the ability to detect wakefulness is the most important, (Singh, 2018).
The main strength of actigraphy is the ability to monitor sleep/wake patterns over
an extended continuously period from home. However, it is also the main weakness of
actigraphy. Laboratory studies are easier to oversee, and technical problems are typical
resolved faster. With the home recordings, the risk of losing data due to technical diffi-
culties or non-compliance (failure to wear the device) is higher. Nevertheless, actigraphy
is at present the most cost-effective method of monitoring sleep objectively.
2.3
Indoor Air Quality
2.3.1
Bedroom Air Quality
Air quality in bedrooms is strongly affected by occupants. Bedrooms are typically not
influenced by major contamination sources like in kitchens, bathrooms or utility rooms.
Bedrooms generally are denser than other living spaces in a dwelling and are where the
occupants spend a least 7-9 hours every night. This means that the indoor air quality is
negatively influenced by the occupants if there is no ventilation. Due to the many hours
spent in the bedroom and the size, the resulting build-up of CO2, heat, and humidity
can be massive.
Poor air quality in bedrooms can cause sleep disruption and reduced breathing dur-
ing sleep. Exposure to decreased oxygen and elevated carbon dioxide levels during the
night can lead to sleep disruptions, (Caddick and Gregory, 2018).
2.3.2
Bioeffluents
Humans emit bioeffluents, i.e., gases and particles generated due to metabolic processes.
An adult emits during exhalation approx. 4 % CO2, the limited value for a workplace is
0.5 %. A person at rest emits approx. 15 l of CO2 per hour and up to 45 l of CO2 per
hour when active. However, significant changes in the indoor air are necessary before the
human body notices a decreased content of oxygen in the air, (Johansson, J. et al., 2011).
The amount of bioeffluents released, including sweat, bacteria, skin degradation prod-
ucts, and digestion of the oral cavity, stomach, and intestines are also a great source
of pollution. The number of bioeffluents emitted varies and depends on hygiene and
activity. Bioeffluents are not toxic.
The most used method for determining the amount of bioeffluents is by measuring the

2.3.3
Appropriate Air Quality in Dwellings
According to the Danish Building Regulation (BR18) paragraph 443, there must always
be an air change rate of at least 0,30 l/s per m² heated floor area, both in a living
space as well as in the dwelling in total. This also applies when using demand-controlled
ventilation.
The 0,30 l/s per m² corresponds to standard room height approximate to an air change of
0.5/h. The purpose of this is to ensure the necessary air change. Both requirements are
minimum requirements that both must be met, (Bygningsreglementet, 2018). Besides
the air volumes can be increased to specific quantities in kitchen, bathrooms, utility
rooms and rooms in the basement.

CHAPTER 3
State Of The Art
A literature study was surveyed and researched to gain a wider perspective on the sub-
ject. Measuring sleep quality at home (in actual bedrooms) with low-cost sleep trackers
while comparing it with the indoor air quality is a relatively unexplored field. The fol-
lowing section “State Of The Art” presents a literature survey on the topic, listing and
summarizing the most relevant articles. Each of the articles is presented with a brief
description, results, and comments of relevance.
The results of this literature study suggest in general, a strong possibility for the re-
lationship between bedroom air quality and sleep quality.
1) Window/door opening- mediated bedroom ventilation and its impact on
sleep quality of healthy, young adults
A. K. Mishra, A. M. van Ruitenbeek, M. G. L. C. Loomans, H. S. M. Kort, 2017
Description: In this article, the effect on sleep quality was investigated by sleeping with
an open bedroom door or window versus sleeping with closed bedroom door or window.
The quality of sleep was measured subjectively by answering questions concerning the
quality of sleep and objectively by measuring the sleep quality with actigraphy trackers.
The purpose of the experiment was to determine whether sleeping with window/door
opened improved the quality of sleep and whether it improved the air quality in the
bedroom. The number of participants in the study, n=18.
Result: The objectively measured sleep quality (based on actigraphy readings) and the
perceived air quality (based on a questionnaire) were improved when the level of CO2
was decreased. The average CO2 level was 717 ppm for open conditions and 1150 ppm
for closed conditions.
Furthermore, the result showed significance across the analyzed sleep parameters; for
sleep latency, sleep length and number of awakenings.
Comments: Due to the limited size of the experiment it was not clear whether opening
an internal door or a window in bedroom was the preferred solution. 7 out of the 18
subjects slept with an open bedroom door contrary to sleeping with an open window,
from this it was not clear which of the two interventions that produced the effect.

2) The effects of bedroom air quality on sleep and next-day performance P.
Strøm-Tejsen, D. Zukowska, P. Wargocki, D. P. Wyon, 2016.
Description: In 2016 the bedroom air quality and its effect on sleep and next-day per-
formance were examined in two field-interventions experiments. The experiment was
carried out in single-occupancy student dormitories. An actigraphy was used to measure
sleep, and an indoor climate logger was used to measure the CO2 level, temperature, and
relative humidity. In the first intervention, the subjects were exposed to two-bedroom
ventilation conditions; the bedroom ventilation was manipulated by sleeping with an
open or a closed bedroom window. In the second intervention, the ventilation was oper-
ated by a fan in the air intake, whenever the CO2 level exceeded 900 ppm the fan was
enabled. During both interventions, the subjects had to answer a questionnaire, regard-
ing their perceived sleep, and to take two logic tests to test their cognitive abilities in
the morning after waking up.
Result: The resulting average CO2 level from having closed and open windows were
2585 ppm and 660 ppm, respectively. The resulting average CO2 levels from the dis-
abled and enabled fan in the air intake were 2395 ppm and 835 ppm, respectively. The
perceived air quality and the objectively measured sleep quality were improved when
the level of CO2 was decreased. The next day-performance increased likewise when the
CO2 level was reduced.
Comments: The sleep quality and next-day performance concerning the bedroom air
quality was improved due to improved ventilation. However, the intervention with a
fan in the air-take creates some noise which in the long term could become a problem.
Likewise, the bedroom temperature decreases in both interventions and accordingly a
problem could arise in continuation of reduced temperatures in the bedroom.
3) Effect of natural ventilation on indoor air quality and thermal comfort
in dormitory during winter
Zhangping Lei, Chuanping Liu, Li Wang, NaLi, 2017.
Description: The study investigated the effect of natural ventilation on perceived sleep
quality in dormitories, by differentiating the windows opening widths. This study was
executed in multiple-student dormitories during winter in Beijing. The windows in the
dorms were either closed completely or open to a certain width during the night. During
the experimental nights temperature, humidity, and CO2 were measured. In the morn-
ing the subjects had to answer a questionnaire to rate their mental state level and their
thermal sensation. To avoid biased answers, the subjects were unaware of the opening
width of the window.
Results: The study indicated that the quality of the indoor air increased when the
opening width of the window was increased. However, the level of thermal comfort de-
creased. It was shown in this study, with this specific location, season, dimension and

number of people in a dorm, that an appropriate opening width approximately is 0.055
m2 to ensure thermal comfort.
Comments: The findings of the study are only applicable in the location where the
study was completed. The study showed that the mental state level gradually increased
when the opening area of the window increased. The findings were a trade-off between
the opening width of the window, the thermal comfort and the level of the mental state.
4) Analysis of the influence of ventilation rate on sleep pattern
Laverge, Jelle; Janssens, Arnold, 2012.
Description: The field study conducted in 2012 had 22 subjects all living in dorms,
participating in the experiment.
The subjects were asked to sleep with an open or
closed window while the quality of sleep and the quality of the air in the room were
measured. The subjects responded to questionnaires concerning their perceived sleep
quality the next morning.
Result: The result from this field experiment indicated that opening the window had
a minimal effect on the sleep pattern and the sleep efficiency tended to decrease with
open windows.
Comments: Unlike the study of Strøm-Tejsen et al., the interventions showed no ef-
fects on sleep quality. The data from the actigraphy did not show significance, and the
subjective data only showed a minimal impact on the sleep pattern. This small effect
could thus, have been biased since the remaining data did not show direction toward a
better sleep under the conditions. The expectation of sleeping with open windows could
have been the source of the bias.
5) The influence of the indoor environment on sleep quality.
Urlaub, S., Grun, G., Foldbjerg, P. and Sedl- Bauer, K, 2015
Description: The article is an extensive literature review including 24 scientific stud-
ies on how the indoor environment influence sleep quality.
Result: The effect of ventilation on sleep quality is nearly unknown. The paper found
two studies addressing ventilation and CO2, and both of the studies did not report any
significant effects on sleep quality. The ambient temperature was documented to affect
sleep quality. Temperature above thermal neutrality was shown to reduce deep sleep,
REM-sleep and the total length of sleep.
Comments: The article was brought in Healthy Buildings Europe, 2015 and the au-
thors are concluding that current studies form a rather incomprehensive basis and much
more research is needed in this field of indoor air quality and sleep quality.

been if the experiment was completed in private homes.

Hypotheses
The purpose of the experiment was to investigate whether bedroom indoor air qual-
ity effects sleep quality and next-day performance; this was examined in two field-
interventions. The two field interventions included sleeping with an open and closed
bedroom door. The study mainly focus on the air quality in the bedroom. It was inves-
tigated whether the CO2 build up in the bedroom when sleeping with a closed bedroom
door influenced the sleep quality.
Four hypotheses were therefore examined, see figure 4.1:
Hypotheses 0: There is no difference between sleeping with an open/closed door in pro-
portion of obtaining a better sleep quality.
Hypotheses 1: Sleeping with the bedroom door open result in better air quality in the
bedroom.
Hypotheses 2: A better sleep quality will be achieved because of the good air quality in
the bedroom which is a result from sleeping with an open door.
Hypotheses 3: The next day performance improves because a good sleep quality is a
result from sleeping with an open door.

5.2.1
Outline of Experiment
To participate in the experiment the subjects, both males and females, had to be in
the age group from 18-30 years. The subjects were asked to live in Copenhagen and
were able to sleep alone in the entire measuring period. The subjects were also asked
to comply with some requirements, such as not drinking alcohol and coffee at certain
times and keep a regular sleeping routine. The recruited people were fellow students
and friends.
The initial plan was to conduct the experiment over a period of three weeks, but this
was too big of a demand for the participants and therefore two weeks were a better
compromise for both parts.
The field experiment was conducted in private Copenhagen apartments. The subjects
were asked to wear a Fitbit wristwatch when going to bed, which were used to determine
the subjects time of sleep and to record their sleep quality during the night (different
stages of sleep). A logger was placed at their bedside table measuring the CO2 level,
temperature, and relative humidity in the room.
The experiment was divided into two campaigns - campaign one and two.
In total
thirty subjects agreed to participate in the experiment. In the first campaign thirteen
subjects participated, and in the second campaign seventeen subjects participated.
Each campaign where running in a period of two weeks, however, the measurements were
only conducted from Monday to Friday in both weeks (2x4 days). The first campaign
occurred from the 3rd–15th of September and the second campaign from the 24th-5th
of October, see figure 5.2. The figure shows the progress of the experiment. In the first

5.4.3
Accuracy of Climate Loggers
To ensure that the climate loggers used in the experiment measured approximately the
same, they were gathered in a temperature and CO2 controlled room to evaluate. In the
specification from the manufacturer the older climate loggers have a total accuracy at
room temperature +/ −75 ppm at 600 ppm and 1000 ppm. The newer climate loggers
have a total accuracy of +/ −50 ppm.
In the evaluation two of the climate loggers showed a tendency of measuring above
and below the average of the remaining loggers, see figure 5.8. The black and the black-
dotted line depicts the upper and lower limit of accuracy in the interval +/ −50 ppm
from the total average (without outliers). The measured average was 430 ppm, and the
majority of the data lies within the accuracy of 430 ppm +/ −50 ppm.
The data from the logger above the upper limit had to be adjusted/calibrated. The
average of the outlier was 543 ppm, well above 480 ppm (being the upper limit of accu-
racy). To make the data-set consistent with the remaining climate loggers the averaged
difference (430ppm/543ppm = 0.79) was multiplied on the data-set to ensure the same
frame of reference. The same method was applied to the outlier just below the lower
limit of accuracy.

To evaluate and assess the measuring data, different tools and statistical methods were
applied.
Excel was the key tool throughout the entire course of data processing. Excel was used
to store and sort data. Later the spreadsheet tool was used in the pre-processing phase
and lastly it was used in the statistical processing together with other more advanced
analytical tools. Following tools/programs were used throughout the data processing.
• Excel
• JMP, Statistical Software from SAS
• The Wilcoxon Signed-Ranks Test Calculator (Stangroom, 2018)
7.1
Normality Test
To test for normality the Anderson-Darling test was used. The test works as an add-in
to JMP and determines whether a data-set comes from a certain probability distribu-
tion, in the current case the normal distribution.
The test is a modification of the
Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) test. The corresponding p-value indicates whether the data
sample is normally distributed. If the p-value is less than 0,05, then data is not normally
distributed, (McNeese, 2011).
7.2
Statistical Test
To evaluate whether interventions caused differences in sleep quality or not three different
types of statistical tests have been utilized. Depending on measuring scale, whether
the data were normally or not normally distributed and whether they were related,
or independent, different statistical tests were applied to test whether differences were
statistically significant. Following statistical tests were used:
• Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed ranks test
• T-test for matched pairs
• X2

Two-tailed Test vs One-tailed Test
The region of rejection can be determined in a one or two-tailed test.
A one-tailed
test is referred to as a directional test whereas a two-tailed test is referred to as a non-
directional test. The two tests differ in location but not in size of the region of rejection.
The region of rejection is for a one-tailed test entirely in one end. The region of rejection

in a two-tailed test is however located at both end of the distribution sample, see figure

Using a one-tailed test is only justified when having a specific prediction about the
direction of the difference. The hypotheses tested with a one-tailed test in the follow-
ing analysis were based on scientific evidence. The one-tailed hypothesis test refers to
finding the probability (P-value) under one tail whereas the two-tailed hypothesis test
refers to finding the probability under two tails. The two-tailed test is appropriate when
sufficient literature was not present to predict the direction of the difference.
Data were analyzed accordingly to a 5 % significant level.
If the P-value was less
than the significance level (P<0,05), then the hypothesis was statistically significant.
Contrary if the P-value was larger than the significance level (P>0,05), then the null
hypothesis was rejected.

Effects of length of sleep
In section 9.2 it was shown that the subjects time in deep sleep systematically decreased
with increasing CO2 levels (around 10-14 min). Less deep sleep causes more amyloid,
which is a toxin protein that accumulates in the brain killing surrounding cells and the
risk of developing Alzheimer’s will significantly rise. During deep sleep, such deposits are
effectively cleaned from the brain, (Walker, 2017). Also, in deep sleep, memories that
have been put in the hippocampus (short-term memories formed during the previous
day) are redistributed to the neocortex, where they will be long-term memories. (Tuck,
2017). Declarative memories (the ability to store and recall facts) appears to be given a
boost during deep sleep, (Sleep score, 2018).
The subjects time in REM sleep decreased when CO2 increased by about 10-15 min
already when CO2 concentrations were at 1000ppm-1200 ppm. REM sleep is essential
to recover episodes experienced during the day, both normal, stressful and traumatic
experiences.
Dreaming offer psychological resolution when waking up each morning,
(Ohayon et al., 2017).
The shortest time measured in light sleep was at low (500 ppm-1000 ppm) and high
(1700 ppm-3000 ppm) CO2 levels. Light sleep is where your metabolism regulates itself
and where the body process emotions and memories, (Walker, 2017).

Indoor parameters
The field-intervention, sleeping with the bedroom door open/closed showed that sleep-
ing with bedroom door open improved some conditions. Valid for all subjects was an
increased level of CO2 when sleeping with the bedroom door closed. However, the ac-
tual bedroom concentration of CO2 varied a lot among the subjects. Some bedrooms
generally had higher CO2 levels than others. In some cases, the level of CO2 for one
participant with closed door was equal to the CO2 level of another participant with
open door.
These variations were however anticipated.
The study was based on a
field-experiment and was not conducted under controlled conditions like in a laboratory.
Furthermore, no active ventilation of bedrooms was implemented. The outcome will
therefore always be more varied.
The variations can be a result of different circumstances. The size of bedrooms can
be one of the main reasons why variations in CO2 levels were observed. The CO2 ac-
cumulation is greater in a small bedroom compared to a larger bedroom because the
volume of air is smaller. Other parameters like gender, weight and height can also con-
tribute to elevated CO2 differences. The exhaled CO2 depends on the body size of the
subject. A person weighing less would accordingly emit less CO2, (see section 2.3.2).
In present study, the focus has been on how the subjects responded to the conditions
when sleeping with open or closed bedroom door. It could be equally interesting to ex-
amine, how the subjects respond to elevated levels of CO2 regardless of whether sleeping
with bedroom door open or closed. Some analyses presented in section 9.2 looked at
this aspect.
Differences in bedroom temperature were observed in the current study. Subjects sleep-
ing with closed door experienced increased temperatures compared to subjects sleeping
with the bedroom door open. However, the median difference was minimal, in campaign
one the temperature difference was 0,3 °C and in campaign two 0,9 °C. It cannot be
rejected that the temperature differences could influence the sleep. Mishra et. Al (2017)
found that within the recorded temperatures in the bedrooms that warmer conditions
led to longer time spent in deep sleep. Previous studies, Pan et Al., 2012 and Lan et
Al., 2016 showed the same tendency.
Regarding relative humidity, a small difference was noticed between the two bedroom
conditions. The relative humidity was slightly lower with the bedroom door open. The
relative humidity was not below 40 % in either condition, so it is unlikely that the in-
crement from 56,2 % to 60,8 % in campaign 1 and increment from 56,2 % to 60,8 % in
campaign 2 disturbed the outcome of the results. Since no studies are looking at the
effect of RH on sleep quality, the effect hereof cannot fully be rejected and should be
investigated in the future.

The effect of sleeping in a “new environment”
The subjects were exposed to a “new environment” when they had to sleep with the
bedroom door open (or closed). Most of the subjects are living in shared apartments
(but not sharing bedrooms) with +1 roommates, and 2/3 of the subjects usually sleep
with the bedroom door closed. The subjects were therefore suddenly sleeping in a sort
of ”foreign” environment. One half of the human brain will not sleep as deeply as the
other, when sleeping in a ”foreign” environment, like in a hotel or a laboratory according
to Walker (2017).
If you compare the electrical depth of the deep NREM slow brainwaves on one half
of someone’s head relative to the other when they are sleeping at home, they are about
the same. But if you bring that person into a […] unfamiliar sleep environment – one
half of the brain sleep a little lighter than the other, as if it is standing guard […] due to
the potentially less safe context […]. (Walker, 2017, page. 66).
To ensure similar brainwaves on both halves of the brain and thereby eliminate this
source of error, it is suggested that the experimental period could be extended. In this
way, the subjects are given more time to get used to the ‘new’ environment and thereby
it can be examined better whether there was a strong influence of this bias on the present
result.
The more nights the individual sleeps in the new location; the more similar the sleep
is in each half of the brain. (Walker, 2018, page. 66).
Subjects
The size of the test group was a balance between recruiting as many people as pos-
sible for statistical power and available resources and time. The number of subjects
that participated in the study contributed to a robust research. However, the study
would have had more statistical power with more subjects. The studies mentioned in
the literature review, chapter 3, used around the same number or fewer subjects than
this current study and succeeded to achieve significance in their research studies.
Length of experiment
An extended period of measuring would have benefited the experiment in two ways;
it would have strengthened the results and tendencies, and the subjects would have had
more time to get used to the new environment when sleeping with the bedroom door
open. However, it was not possible to recruit subjects to be involved in a three-week-long
experiment (or longer) it was too big of an effort for the subjects. Future studies could
look at this more closely.

Baddely’s Grammatical Reasoning Test
The test results from Baddeley’s Grammatical Reasoning Test showed significant im-
provement of performance when sleeping with the bedroom door open.
It was decided to let the individual subjects decide the order of when to sleep with
open and closed bedroom door. This was done for the convenience of the subjects and
to keep the subjects blinded to the evaluation later on. Furthermore, the learning (im-
proved performance independent of exposure conditions) would appear regardless of the
order of conditions. It would be best if half of the group slept with doors open in the
first week and half with bedroom doors closed. However, it turned out that two-thirds
of subjects slept with the bedroom door closed in the first week hence the design was
unbalanced and biased towards rejecting the null hypothesis especially due to learning.
The choice of using the cognitive test ‘Baddeley’s Grammatical Reasoning’ was based
on experience from a previous study, (Strøm-Tjesen et al., 2016) and because it was a
short 3-minute test, making it convenient for the subjects during a busy morning before
going to work/school. The test was structured so that the order of the questions was
random every day. To avoid the influence of ’know-how,’ it could be considered to use
a test that generates different types of tasks every day. This would give a reason to
believe, that the learning ability would not be such a dominating factor.
Pollution sources
Indoor air contains several pollution sources; including pollution coming from outdoors
and pollution from indoor sources like cooking and fumes from cleaning and building
materials, (Stokel-Walker, 2018). When sleeping with the bedroom door, open pollu-
tants from the entire apartment and bedroom were mixed, while with door closed there
were primarily pollutants from bedroom.
Sleeping with the door open could affect the results positively or negatively – positively
by reducing the levels of pollutants in the bedroom and negatively by transporting pollu-
tants from other rooms in the apartment. In future studies, the effect hereof needs to be
investigated carefully especially when there is strong penetration of pollutants between
different rooms.
Equipment
The equipment used in the field experiment was mostly stable. However, the tablets
explicitly bought to the purpose caused problems. The subjects had a hard time con-
necting the tablets with their home network making it almost impossible to use. To
solve the problem, we asked the subjects to use their smartphones which luckily made
it even easier for the subjects as they were more familiar with their own devices.

CHAPTER 12
Conclusion
The purpose of the present field-experiment was to investigate how bedroom air quality
affects the sleep quality, the next-day performance, and perception of indoor air quality.
Bedroom air quality was changed by sleeping one week with open bedroom door and
one week with closed bedroom door.
The experiment included questionnaires, a cognitive test of concentration ability, phys-
iological measurements with actigraphy and measurements of the indoor air quality.
Thirty subjects participating in the experiment slept in their bedrooms in one week
with bedroom door open and another with bedroom door closed. The usable data from
21 subjects were used to perform analyses.
The conclusions from this study can be formulated as in the following:
• There were substantial differences in CO2 concentrations between the two con-
ditions in the direction of increased CO2 concentration when sleeping with the
bedroom door closed. The median CO2 difference between open/closed conditions
was approximately 700 ppm. Parameters such as temperature and relative humid-
ity showed a minimal difference between the conditions.
• Objectively measured sleep quality showed that the sleep efficiency and awakenings
during night were unaffected of the two interventions (open/closed bedroom door).
REM sleep and deep sleep were affected in expected direction; the subjects tended
to sleep shorter time in these stages when sleeping with the bedroom door closed.
Total time asleep and time in light sleep showed inconclusive results.
• The results from Baddeley’s Grammatical Reasoning Test showed that the per-
formance of the cognitive test measuring concentration improved in the morning
when sleeping with the bedroom door open.
• The scores from the Gronning Sleep Quality Scale did not show differences between
conditions, neither when it was related with CO2 levels. Subjectively assessed sleep
quality did not change.
• The subjectively reported sleep onset and awakenings during night did not show a
difference between conditions when sleeping with the bedroom door open/closed.
These parameters were not related to CO2 levels.
• The subjective evaluations regarding the freshness of the air showed significance
toward the expected direction. Subjects perceived air quality to be fresher in the
morning after waking up in bedrooms with door open.
==PAGE:92==
84
12 Conclusion
• The subjective evaluations regarding the light level in the bedroom showed signif-
icance. The subjects perceived the bedroom to be darker with the bedroom door
closed.
• The subjective evaluation regarding temperature and noise level did not show
significance.
Subjects did not perceive the temperature or the noise levels as
changed in the morning after waking up in bedrooms with the door open.
• The results of the objective measured sleep quality show accordance with the good
sleep quality markers, proposed by the National Sleep Foundation. Minutes awake,
time in light sleep, time in deep sleep and sleep efficiency are within the benchmark.
However, REM sleep is slightly lower than the benchmark.
Sleeping with the bedroom door open resulted in better air quality - both the objectively
measured CO2 concentration decreased and the subjectively reported freshness of the
air improved supporting hypothesis 1 put forward in chapter 4.
There is a tendency in the results suggesting that the next day performance improved
when the air quality improved as indicated by the reduced CO2 levels. However, it was
not possible to support the hypothesis put forward in chapter 4 that next-day perfor-
mance would improve because sleep quality improves with the bedroom door open. The
plausible explanation were differences in absolute levels of CO2 (air quality levels) in
bedrooms of different subjects.
==PAGE:93==
CHAPTER 13
Future Studies
In future studies, more subjects participating in the experiment would be advised to
achieve more statistical power.
Also, more repetitions during the experiment would
strengthen the results and be useful to confirm that the result will be maintained. In
the present study the participants were healthy young adults in the age group 20-30
years. In furture studies, it could be interesting to examine other age groups including
children and older adults to see whether the age of the participants influences the results.
The choice of using the actimeter Fitbit Alta HR was based on availability together
with an investigation of different actigraphy devices, see section 6, Case Study. The
reliability of Fitbit is somewhat unexplored, and further studies of the device could be
interesting.
Additionally, it would be interesting to investigate whether CO2 alone or other pol-
lutants are responsible for the effects observed during the field experiment.
The reports from Groningen Sleep quality scale did not show any differences when sleep-
ing with the bedroom door open. In future studies, it could be advised to use a more
in-depth scale for assessing sleep quality. Or whether such subjective assessments can be
used at all to measure sleep quality. Matching the responses against PSG (polysomnog-
raphy) measurements could be interesting.
Lastly, in a similar field experiment, it would be advised if half of the participants
slept with the bedroom door open in the first week and the other half with the bedroom
door closed to avoid an unbalanced design of the experiment.
