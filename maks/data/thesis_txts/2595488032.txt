Abstract
This project establishes a simple coupled-mode theory model to describe the transmission responses
of cavities demonstrating sub-wavelength confinement of light. A perturbational approach is used to
include nonlinear effects that are enhanced due to the small mode volumes.
The nonlinear effects
are the Kerr effect, two-photon absorption, free-carrier absorption and index changes by free-carriers
and temperature changes. The model is explored in four different situations that portray experimen-
tal characterization methods. The situations involve continuous-wave light input, modulated signals,
frequency-swept pulsed transmission and analysis of single-pulse dynamics. The situations are explored
with different powers or mode volumes to predict the nonlinear responses that can be observed in ex-
periments. These characterization methods are investigated to provide insight for future transmission
experiments that can be used to characterize the enhanced nonlinear responses and indirectly extract
the size of the extreme spatial confinement of the electric field. Finally the scaling of the nonlinear ef-
fects are investigated and materials are proposed, which promote the Kerr effect for future fast nonlinear
switching applications.

Introduction
Photonic nanocavities are small cavities, which can trap light inside to create high energy densities.
The strong electric field in the cavity can amplify the light-matter interaction by a factor PF, called the
Purcell factor, as seen in Eq. 1.1 [1].

The Purcell factor describes the enhancement of the spontaneous emission rate in a cavity. Q is the
quality factor of the cavity describing how well the optical energy is stored and V is the effective mode
volume of the cavity. The parameters Q and V are measures of respectively the temporal and spatial
confinement of the photonic cavity, which can be controlled in the design of the structures.
Up until now the dominant pursuit in research has been to maximize the quality factor of photonic

The goal of this project is to investigate the implications of the extremely small mode volumes on the

signals through the nanocavities is investigated by coupled-mode theory (CMT). CMT provides a simple
framework that can connect the optical modes of different systems by imposing energy conservation
and time-reversibility. The linear CMT can then be expanded to cover more complex phenomena by
including nonlinear effects as perturbations in the model, which either modifies the eigenfrequency of
the cavity or introduces losses. In chapter 2 and 3 the theoretical framework is set up and the CMT
model is developed. Three nonlinear effects are considered in the model: The Kerr effect, the effect of
temperature changes and the effect of carriers. The temperature and carrier dynamics are modelled as
rate equations where the main source is two photon absorption loss.

CHAPTER 3
Coupled Mode Theory
The coupled-mode theory (CMT) is a well-established framework that is set up to accurately model
complex photonic devices by a set of simple ordinary differential equations (ODEs).
The coupled
mode equations links the interaction of incoming and outgoing propagating waveguide modes with the
leaky cavity modes. The CMT equations can be derived straightforwardly from the requirement of
the energy conservation, time reversibility and the existence of resonance mode(s). The derivations in
this chapter of the linear CMT equations follows the book by Joannopoulos [16]. Another more general
approach of deriving the CMT equations can be found in [17] in a framework of the quasi-normal modes
(QNM) of the cavity. The QNM approach leads to the same common linear CMT equations for a single
cavity system but it pertains the phase changes in the cavity, which are important for systems with
multiple coupled cavities. For this work a single cavity coupled to waveguides is considered and thus
the simple pragmatic approach to deriving the linear CMT equations is presented. After establishing
the framework of the coupling of propagating waveguide modes and a single cavity resonance mode,
the nonlinear effects are added as perturbations to expand the CMT model to capture the rich physical
interactions in nanocavities.

the other effects are proportional to N so the expected frequency shift of the cavity mode is positive.

3.8
Verification
Before analysing the EDC nanocavities it is vital to check that the model can reproduce experimental
results in literature.
The expanded CMT model with all the nonlinear terms provides a complex
framework with many terms to incorporate. For the verification of the CMT model the article Switching
characteristics of an InP photonic crystal nanocavity [18] is used. The experiment differs in the sense
that the material is InP, which is a direct semiconductor and thus has faster carrier dynamics and that
the experiment uses a two pulse characterization. The first pulse provides the energy to the nanocavity
and then a signal pulse probes the cavity with a certain delay from the pumping. The advantage of
such an experiment is that the temporal dynamics of the cavity is probed but the drawback is that it
requires a complicated optical setup for lock-in of the probe. This experimental article proves useful for
verification as the parameters and the CMT definitions are identical except differences in the experiment
and the use of three carrier lifetime constants.
In Fig. 3.5(a) the experimental and simulated result from [18] is shown to the left. The x-axis is
the time delay between the pump and probe pulses. The CMT equations are solved for the time before
and after the pulses with the delay being iterated. The result is shown in Fig. 3.5(b). The computed
transmission is seen to agree well with the work from [18].
The accurate reproduction is achieved by proper selection of the temporal spacing in the CMT
computations. The dynamics of the pulse transmission works in the ps range, which is therefore the
necessary time spacing. When the ODE’s are computed in MATLAB, the ODE solvers use the Runge-
Kutta method, where the time spacing is decided to achieve the desired resolution. A function tolerance
can be set to impose high solution accuracy. However, this method is implicit with little control on the
actual time spacing. Instead the MATLAB script is written with a loop on the ODE solver to solve for
the small time [ti, ti + ∆t] and then the final computation solution is used as the initial condition. This
allows for explicit time spacing selection when solving the mode energy dynamics in the cavity. A time
spacing of ∆t ∼0.01∆tFWHM is found suitable for modeling the pulse dynamics in this verification test
and in the pulse transmission investigations shown in chapter 6 and 7.

energy squared. The generation of carriers is also depending on the two photon generation, hence U 2.
This means that the resonance frequency shifts from the carrier dynamics and temperature changes
scales faster than the Kerr effect for increasing energies.

CHAPTER 7
Single pulse characterization
method
The results in chapter 6 showed unique characteristics of the transmission responses of the different
nonlinearities by sweeping the wavelength of the pulses. This characterization method makes use of the
overall transmission increase of the pulse when the center pulse frequency matches the detuned cavity
frequency. Another approach would be to investigate the pulse dynamics of individual pulses with a
fixed frequency. This characterization method is employed in this chapter.
7.1
Pulse peaks
The nonlinear change in cavity resonance frequency ∆ω(t) does not only affect the transmission of
the pulses but also the shape due to phase changes in the cavity. Instead of varying pulses’ center
frequency/wavelength and measuring energy transmission, a single pulse spectrum can be analyzed to
detect the pulse shape changes. In Fig. 7.1 the output power signal and phases changes as a function
of time are shown for an incoming 100 fJ pulse of width τFWHM =10 ps with center frequency matching
the original cavity resonance frequency. Four situations are considered; (a) The Kerr effect exclusively,
(b) the carrier effect, (c) the temperature changes and (d) with all nonlinear effects included. In the
first situation (a) it is seen that the phase change from the Kerr effect is small so the pulse shape is
undisturbed by the Kerr nonlinearity. The only change from the incoming pulse shape is that the pulse
is widened in time due to the cavity response delay, γ, and the peak is delayed by 2 ps. The analytical
shape of the delayed Gaussian pulse response, which depends on both ∆tFWHM and γ, can be found
in [33]. In the carrier and temperature cases, (b) and (c), the nonlinear effects are significant enough
to cause a pulse shaping. The phase is seen to reach 1
2π in magnitude and show a damped oscillation
in both cases. The origin of the damped oscilations is the fast nonlinear shift of frequency away from
the original cavity frequency and thus the input field frequency.
The evolution of the carrier and
temperature changes is seen in Fig. 7.3. It is seen that the decay times of the carriers and temperature
are slow compared to the pulse width. This creates the sudden ”on” shift of the nonlinear frequency
change that can create a transient response where the phase overshoots if the nonlinear effect is strong
enough.
The oscillatory phase response can also be observed with the Kerr effect for larger pulse powers but
it is found that it requires a short pulse duration compared to the cavity response time. The extra
condition arises because the Kerr effect depends directly on the cavity mode field and thus follows the
field unless the change happens faster than the response time of the cavity. 1
The output pulse and phase, when all nonlinear effects are included, is shown in Fig. 7.1(d). It is
seen that the pulse shape is similar to the carrier effect’s pulse response, which reveals that the carrier
effect is dominant. The phase however shows a longer oscillation period due to the temperature and
carrier effect counteracting each other.
The spectra of the output pulses are retrieved by Fourier transformation and shown in Fig. 7.2.
Fig. 7.2(a) shows a pulse spectrum with decreased FWHM compared to the input pulse signal. This
is due to the delayed response in the cavity as the Kerr effect is too weak to cause spectral changes.

The boundary conditions for the problem are perfect electric conductor (PEC), perfect magnetic
conductor (PMC) and scattering boundary condition (SBC). The PEC and PMC is used for the mirror
symmetries for the 1/8 cut of the cavity whereas the purpose of the SBC is to allow light to escape
through the air-box interfaces as if there was no finite end to environment. The PEC boundary condition
is set up as: n × E = 0, which means that the electric field cannot be parallel to the face with PEC
BC and must be polarized into the interface. The PMC is written in a similar way for the H field:
n × H = 0. PMC is placed at the XY (z=0 nm) plane and on the YZ (x=0 nm) plane shown in Fig.
8.1(a) while PEC is placed at the XZ (y=0 nm) plane. The light is transversal with the H field pointing
in-plane and out of the cavity while the E field is only in the plane and is pointing in the y direction
at the PEC interface. This is the mode that has the highest temporal confinement, i.e highest Q. The
use of the mirror symmetries by PEC and PMC only finds a fraction of all the eigenmodes as the other
eigenmodes are found with different configurations of the imposed symmetries/anti-symmetries of the
fields but as the point of the investigation is only to find the highest confined mode, this configuration
is sufficient.
Going back to the SBC, the purpose of that is avoid reflections of outgoing waves from the cavity
caused by the finite length of the computational domain. In the stationary1 eigenmode search it is
written as: n × (∇× E) −i · n × (E × n) = 0. It is a first order approximation of the Sommerfeld
radiation condition 2 [35, 36] that ensures that outgoing waves can exist while incoming waves can only
satisfy the BC if the electric field amplitudes are zero. Although this boundary condition can extinguish
reflected waves, the numerical scheme is not perfect [36]. To accommodate the imperfection, the air-box
size is increased until the solution becomes invariant of the size. This is the first convergence test that
is seen in Fig. 8.2(a). The quality factor is used as the figure of merit for convergence. It is seen to
follow a damped harmonic oscillation. The convergence result is used to decide the suitable air-box
sidelength. The air-box added sidelength is chosen to be 2.5 µm with the estimated absolute relative
error of 1 % estimated from the converging Q value.

Gap investigation
The model of the EDC nanocavity is then tested for variations of the cavity. Recent articles in literature
states that the extremely small mode volumes from the bow-tie structure is highly dependent on the gap
between triangles [10, 37, 38]. As these dimensions at the two tips are among the smallest feature sizes

diameter and especially on the surrounding pattern that affects the exposure by back-scattered electrons
increasing the exposure sizes. The state-of-the-art resolution on EBL is ≤10 nm. In this investigation
the gap is changed from 40 nm to 1 nm to show the trend of the mode volumes. Simultaneously as
the gap is changed the radius of curvature is decreased at the apex of the air structure to mimic the
fabrication precision. The smallest feature sizes presented in this investigation are unattainable with
present technology but provide the trend of enhanced confinement.

The investigation of the current EDC nanocavity design has made it apparent that the nonlinear effects
from the carrier and temperature changes are greater than the Kerr effect for the EDC nanocavities. This
follows by the increased nonlinear absorption (TPA and FCA) when the mode volume is decreased. The
dominant temperature and carrier effects create unique characteristics in the transmission measurements
as seen in chapter 6 that can be useful for characterization of the mode volumes by the complex interplay

CHAPTER 10
Discussion and Outlook
Four different characterization methods have been presented in the chapters 4-7 that show different
ways to observe nonlinearities. Each method has the potential to be used for characterization of the
mode volumes by analyzing measured nonlinear transmission responses and comparing them with CMT
results. They do however have different requirements for the characterization, which are to be discussed.
The first method is to use a CW input signal and either vary the power or optical frequency. This
method is the simplest to prepare for a measurement as it only requires a tunable CW laser source that
is coupled to the cavity and then detection by a power meter. This makes the CW characterization
suitable as the first measurement to see if nonlinearities are present in the response. An important
thing to bear in mind is the presence of optical bistability. As seen in chapter 4 the optical bistability
can occur with nonlinear effects when either the power or frequency is swept. It is observed by sweeping
the parameter both directions - from low to high and back. Although the CW response has only been
explored with the Kerr effect in this project, it is known from literature that optical bistability can also
occur other nonlinear effects like the temperature effect [30].
The second method involves an intensity modulation of CW light. The transmission of a modulated
signal is seen in chapter 5 to include higher order harmonics. The higher order harmonics were only found
from the Kerr effect as the slower nonlinear effects obscures the harmonics. It is thus only interesting
for cavities made in materials that suppress the two-photon absorption. This characterization method
might be used for characterizing AOS devices due to the sensitivity of the measurement. A modulation
experiment can be made by a CW laser source and a GHz optical intensity modulator to create the
input signal and then a high speed photo detector for measuring the output power trace.
The last characterization methods involve pulse signals instead of CW light. The merits of doing
pulse experiments are two-fold. First is the consideration of the coupling efficiency to the EDC cavities.
It might require large power input signals to obtain reasonable mode energies in the cavities. A pulse
signal is convenient because the energy can be compressed into short time-span leading to a large peak
power. Secondly pulses can be shaped to time widths that is comparable to the nonlinear dynamics.
The pulse width can be broadened externally by use of dispersive waveguides. The pulse energy can be
controlled by an optical attenuator. The transmission results in chapter 6 showed a situation where the
pulse center frequency are swept. This requires a tunable pulsed laser source. One of the advantages
of this characterization method is the simplicity that detection can be done with a simple power meter
by sweeping the input frequency in a similar fashion as with the CW characterization method. But the
method differs from the CW characterization in the sense that the initial state is well defined with the
zero cavity mode field before the pulse enters. Thus, no bistability is observable for pulses.
The last method is the spectral analysis of a pulses with fixed frequency. This method requires
one less adjustable parameter but needs an optical spectrometer to capture the pulse dynamics. It
was seen that the different nonlinear effects make unique changes to the pulse shape.
Comparing
the transmission spectra of pulses in EDC cavities and in the larger PhC cavities it is seen that the
responses are distinguishable from another even when higher powers are used to create nonlinear shifts
in the same order of magnitude. This result points towards that the mode volumes can be uniquely
determined by comparing transmission measurements with the expected shapes from the CMT model.
The information is available without knowing the coupled power as the different nonlinear effects scale
different with the energy and can be tracked by the making a sweep of the input pulse energies.
Regardless of the characterization method chosen for an experiment, the parameters used for the
CMT equations has to be determined for analysis and validation. The parameters can be classified in
3 groups: The invariant parameters, the fabrication dependent parameters and the fitting parameters.

The simulations presented in this report have assumed that the light is guided in the plane of the

ment is more favorable compared to the reflection of the top surface. A transmission measurement is
advantageous because it measures small signal peaks on a zero background instead of dip in a large
reflected power signal. Additionally, the coupling efficiency is expected to be larger with dedicated in-
plane waveguides. The current EDC cavity design does not yet feature waveguide coupling as apparent
in Fig. 1.1 so transmission measurements of the EDC cavities are not yet possible. But this is the next
design step for the topology optimization, which is currently being explored.
Once the magnitude of mode volumes in the EDC cavities has been established experimentally, the
next steps are to investigate the applications of the extreme confinement. There are many promising
possibilities with enhanced light-matter interaction both for active devices and passive components.
One prospect could be low-threshold lasers that can be combined with the nonlinear switching fea-
ture to transmit ultra-low energy pulses. Another example could be all-optical switching networks for
manipulation of pulses or fast computation purposes. The speed of nonlinear nanophotonic devices is
determined by two things - The nonlinear effects decay times and cavity response time. The tempera-
ture change and free-carriers are seen in chapter 7 to decay with decay times of 43 ps and 500 ps. This
puts a limit to the duty cycle of signals being transmitted through the cavity. The second limitation is
the response time of the cavity. A high quality factor leads to slow responses and decreases the range
of nonlinear frequency changes as was seen in Fig. 5.4. An ideal material for fast optical switching
networks is one that has a strong Kerr effect so that the speed is not limited by carrier dynamics. As
discussed in chapter 9 this can be accomplished by silicon-rich silicon nitride for devices made for CMOS
platforms, while for III-V plaforms with integration with active devices a material candidate could be
the ternary compound of AlxGa1−xAs.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
Four different characterization methods have been analysed using the nonlinear CMT model to inves-
tigate the nonlinear effects in EDC cavities. The choice of the characterization method will depend on
the available equipment and which method performs better in terms of extracting the mode volumes as
the different methods have different requirements. The first case investigates the transmission of con-
tinuous waves in small cavities with the Kerr nonlinearity only. It is seen that the Kerr effect detunes
the cavity resonance frequency, which can be measured by either sweeping the frequency or the power
of an input signal with the laser frequency below the cavity resonance frequency. This can be used to
identify the Kerr mode volume if the coupled power is known. For continuous waves the phenomenon
of optical bistability occurs, which has two stable transmission solutions when the Kerr effect is strong.
This illustrates the necessity of performing the sweep in both directions. The lineshift induced by the
Kerr nonlinearity is mapped for different Kerr mode volumes, quality factors and input powers to show
the scaling of the Kerr effect and illustrate the required power in the input waveguide.
Second case is investigating how the Kerr nonlinear effect can be measured by a power-modulated
CW input signal.
It is found that the Kerr nonlinearity introduces a wide range of higher order
harmonics in the transmitted power output. The range of detectable higher order harmonics in the
spectral measurement depends on the quality factor of the cavity as higher quality factors leads to
slower response times and thus a smaller range of attainable frequency changes. A power sweep similar
to the CW characterization method can be done, which detects a peak in the higher order harmonics
when the power matches the requirement for shifting the resonance frequency to the selected input
frequency. This can be used to characterize the Kerr mode volume when the modulation amplitude
is small. But the higher order harmonics are significantly reduced when the carrier and temperature
effects are present, which makes this method impractical for silicon nanocavities.
The third case investigates transmission of pulses in EDC cavities with the parameters of the current
EDC cavity design. The transmission of pulses have been tested for the 3 different nonlinear effects. It
is found that the carrier and temperature effects are stronger than the Kerr effect with the expected
parameters of the current cavity design. The transmission results including all nonlinear effects are seen
to have peaks on both sides of the original resonance frequency, which are identifiers of the nonlinear
effects. The results show significant nonlinear effects for 10 ps pulses when the coupled energy is 1 pJ
or larger.
The fourth case investigates the nonlinear effects that can be observed directly from the shape of
a single transmitted pulse. The spectral shape of the transmitted pulses show clear changes from the
input pulse. These spectral changes can be used to identify the nonlinear mode volumes. A comparison
is made of EDC cavities with different spatial confinement and a larger PhC cavity, which reveals that
the spectral changes in a small cavity can not be replicated by using a larger energy in a PhC cavity.
This reveals that the spectral changes can uniquely characterize the spatial confinement due to the
different scaling of the nonlinear effects.
The scaling of the nonlinear effects with respect to power in the current EDC nanocavity design
shows that the nonlinear changes from the carrier and temperature effects far exceeds the changes from
the Kerr effect from the present EDC cavity design. The nonlinear frequency changes are analysed with
respect to the mode volumes VFCA and VKerr. It is found that VFCA decreases more than VKerr when
the spatial confinement is increased. This reveals that although all the nonlinear effects are enhanced
by the increased spatial confinement, the carrier and temperature effects become more significant. This
is not a problem for characterisation but a strong Kerr effect is preferred for applications like all-optical
switching to obtain fast responses. A literature search of material candidates has been done to identify

materials that have a large nonlinear index while simultaneously have a low nonlinear absorption. The
promising material for all-optical switching in a CMOS platform is Si7N3 due to the bandgap energy

In III-V materials it is predicted that a ternary alloy can be created from AlAs and GaAs that has a

E.1
Pulse shaping by Kerr nonlinear effect
Oscillatory phase behaviour was seen for the temperature and carrier nonlinear effects in Fig. 7.1 but
not for the Kerr effect which was too weak. To investigate the presence of phase oscillations by the
Kerr effect a set of cavity and pulse parameters are varied and shown in Fig. E.1. The Kerr constant is
increased in magnitude and the pulse energy is also large to provide a strong nonlinear response. The
values are noted in Fig. E.1. The figure shows a set of 3 cavity parameters for 2 different pulse widths
- 10 ps and 1 ps. The quality factor is varied as this also determines the speed of which the cavity field
responds. A time constant can be defined for the cavity delay by τc := 1
γ = Q
ω0 which can be compared
to the pulse τFWHM. Mikkel Heuck has in his PhD thesis [54] investigated the oscillatory behaviour
of transmitted pulse responses with the Kerr effect and found that these exists in the regime of short
pulses 1 but not for long pulses, where the response can be seen as a quasi-steady-state regime. This is
evident in the results in Fig. E.1. The phase oscillations appear from the sudden nonlinear frequency
shift that moves the cavity frequency away from the input frequency. When the cavity response time
(quantified by τc) is long compared to the pulse duration the frequency shift overshoots and creates
the oscillations in the phase. A mechanical analogy is the damped harmonic oscillator which has an
oscillatory response if the system is under-damped. An interesting application of the pulse shaping by
the Kerr nonlinearity is that the pulse can exit the cavity faster than the expected delay from the cavity
which can be useful for switching networks.

E.3
Cavity response for different free carrier times
The carrier dynamics is affected by how fast the carriers can recombine which is quantified by the
effective carrier lifetime τfc. This parameter is affected by the actual cavity design as the presence of
surfaces can increase the rate of carrier recombination. While the details of the structural dependence
has been neglected in this analysis the air gaps in the structure are expected to decrease the lifetime
τfc and probably require more time constants to express as discussed in section 3.5. In this section the
lifetime influence on the pulse transmission response are shown. In Fig. 8.1 the response of the EDC
cavity investigated in chapter 6 is shown for input pulses with 10 ps FWHM and 200 fJ energy. The
carrier lifetime τfc is varied and the transmitted pulse shape is investigated. The result is shown in Fig.
E.4. It is seen that for long lifetimes the carrier dynamics is invariant of τfc since the power variation
is happening on a much shorter scale. A change of the transmitted pulse only appears then τfc is on
a comparable timescale with the pulse width, which is when τfc < 10∆tFWHM. This observation has
been consistent when the pulse width ∆tFWHM is changed. The carrier effect is decreased when τfc
is decreased to a point where the transmitted pulse shape is governed only by the cavity delay as the
other nonlinearities (Kerr and Temperature changes from TPA) are insignificant with the small input
energy. This is seen in Fig. E.4 for this particular investigation when τfc <100 fs.
The expected lifetime for the silicon cavity would be between 10 ns and 100 ps which would be
unaffected by the details of the surface recombination of carriers for this choice of pulse duration.
