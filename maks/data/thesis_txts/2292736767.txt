Abstract
Water stress is an increasing concern due to climate change and population pressure.
Potable water is a timeless issue in many African countries and with increased water
pollution and growing birth rates, there is a high need for integrated water resources
management. This study sets up a hydrologic-hydrodynamic forecasting model of the
Upper Zambezi in southern Africa, which may serve as the foundation for constructing
a more advanced operational river discharge forecasting model.
The project is part of the TIGER-NET initiative launched by ESA to exploit the
advantages of Earth Observation technology and promote the management of water
resources in Africa. Under TIGER-NET constraints all software must be open-source
and free. The model is based on the Soil and Water Assessment Tool, and is a coupled
model with a rainfall-runoﬀcomponent and a routing component. The main forcing
of the model is precipitation which is implemented via data from NOAA-GFS. Pre-
vious studies had shown that NOAA-GFS greatly overestimates precipitation on the
African continent, and a short section in this study covers the issue of scaling the
precipitation product correctly. An external routing scheme was implemented in order
to allow for data assimilation and loss through ﬂoodplains. The calibration was per-
formed against a time series of in situ discharge measured at Katima Mulilo from 2005
to 2011. Validation was done against the same station for 2012 to 2014. Validation
was also performed against a more scarce data set from Lukulu. For Katima Mulilo
calibration and validation showed satisfactorily results. NSE was respectively 0.79 and
0.59. The WBE showed that the model tended to underestimate discharge. Validation
at Lukulu provided poor results and showed overestimation of discharge, recognising
the inﬂuence of the nearby Barotse ﬂoodplain.
In evaluation of the model uncertainties two diﬀerent AR 1 models are tested. Both
models were implemented in real time forecasts issued at Katima Mulilo for lead times
from 0 to 7 days. The operational probabilistic forecasts are evaluated on their NSE,
RMSE, coverage, and CRPS and their performance is evaluated against climatology
and persistence benchmarks. The evaluation only covered a 100 day span in the low
ﬂow regime, hence persistence proved much better than the two forecasting experi-
ments. Further development of the model is recommended, with emphasis on a better
representation of model error and evaluation of a longer forecasting period.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Water stress is an increasing concern due to changes in the global climate and dynamic
populations trends. Every year millions of people fall victim to ﬂoods and drought all
over the world. As the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) predicts
more frequent extreme weather events in the future, the need for managing water
resources increases. Since many large river networks have several riparian countries
the need for Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM) calls upon intergov-
ernmental cooperation in order to maximise the resultant economic and social welfare
without compromising the sustainability of vital ecosystems[5].
Countries in the tropical and subtropical areas are especially at high risks. The num-
ber of ﬂood deaths in Africa has risen from fewer than 2000 people in 1950-1969 to
more than 15,000 people between the years 1990-2009[6]. Based on climate changes
predicted in the Fourth Assessment Report from the IPCC, Shongwe et al. [7] forecast
an increase in the severity of dry extremes in the western parts of southern Africa due
to a decrease in mean precipitation during austral summer months. Hence, more severe
droughts in the southwest of southern Africa are to be expected, meanwhile countries
farther north as Zambia, Malawi, and northern Mozambique will experience enhanced
precipitation.
SWAT is the acronym for Soil and Water Assessment Tool, which is a river basin- or
watershed-scale model. It is useful in predicting impacts of weather, soils, land use
and land management on water supplies and pollution as well as soil erosion, fertility
and crop production[8]. The SWAT model is public domain and can provide useful,
real-world assessments.
The aim of this project is to design a hydrologic-hydrodynamic forecasting model via

SWAT for the upper Zambezi River. The Zambezi River ﬂows through southern Africa
and the river basin is shared by eight countries, amongst others Zambia, Malawi, and
Mozambique. The model is intended to act as a real-time forecasting model for the
discharge of the upper river network. The model will be calibrated to observation data
from a hydrometric station in Katima Mulilo and validated to the same station as well
as against a hydrometric station in Lukulu, which lies upstream from Katima Mulilo.
By assimilating observation data and including precipitation forecasts with lead times
up to seven days, the model will be able to predict discharge and thereby contribute
to improved water resources management in the area.
Any advancement in IWRM may improve living conditions for millions of people in the
riparian countries in times of drought and/or ﬂoods. The model is intended to be part
of the TIGER initiative, which was launched in 2002 by the European Space Agency
(ESA) to endorse the use of Earth Observation data for water resources management
in Africa.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter describes TIGER-NET and the software environment used in this project.
It also provides the reader an insight to the geography and the economic state of the
basin, and ﬁnally a brief depiction of previous studies undertaken in the Zambezi basin
is given.
2.1
Software Environment
In 2002, responding to the urgent need for action in Africa, the European Space Agency
launched TIGER initiative - Looking after water in Africa, to promote the use of Earth
Observation (EO) for improved Integrated Water Resources Management (IWRM) in
Africa. The overall objective of TIGER is to assist African countries in water man-
agement by exploiting the advantages of EO technology[9]. TIGER-NET is a major
component of TIGER and it aims to support satellite-based assessment and moni-
toring of water resources through development of an open-source Water Observation
and Information System (WOIS) along with capacity building and training of African
water authorities and technical centres. Product Group 09 (PG09), which as a subcat-
egory of TIGER-NET, deals with large scale, hydrological models of river catchments
in Africa, simulating river discharge and thereby contributing to the development of
a Water Observation and Information System. Within PG09 two diﬀerent workﬂows
have been designed: The oﬄine Model Development Workﬂow (MDWF) for replicating
river discharge for historical periods and the Operational Simulation and Forecasting
Workﬂow (OSFWF) for modelling real-time discharge. The main applications of these
include operational hydrological forecasting of ﬂood and drought hazards as well as
long-term planning and scenario analysis[10].

According to the technical speciﬁcations of PG09, WOIS has to meet some speciﬁc
user criteria. Most importantly the software and all the required input data must be
open-source and public domain. This has the potential to diminish the accuracy of
the results as the input data may be of poorer spatial and perhaps temporal quality
than state of the art EO data.
It is worth noting, however, that for the manage-
ment of ecological releases even discharge forecasts with moderate accuracy can be
beneﬁcial[11]. Another important criterion is that the system must be applicable for
data-scarce areas. Many large areas in Africa do not have gauge stations and in-situ
data are limited in both temporal and spatial dimensions. Furthermore, even though
data may exist, some governments may be opposed to cooperating in transboundary
data sharing. Hence, large areas of Africa are considered data-scarce in hydrological
matters, but the system must be applicable in any case.
The Soil and Water Assessment Tool (SWAT) has been chosen as the modelling setup.
SWAT is often used for modelling river basins or watersheds. The basics of SWAT and
its governing equations will be covered in section 4.2.
WOIS is operable from a Geographic Information System (GIS) and under the con-
straints of free, open-source software, Quantum GIS (QGIS), Geospatial Data Abstrac-
tion Library (GDAL) and MapWindow GIS were chosen as the GIS interfaces. First,
the plugin MWSWAT is used for setting up the model in MapWindow. Raster geospa-
tial data formats are read by GDAL, and the model is calibrated via the open-source
Parameter ESTimation program (PEST) in the QGIS interface. Various aspects of
PEST are customised by using the programming language Python. Installation and
source code are available for download from the TIGER-NET webpage (www.tiger-
net.org).
2.2
Study Area
2.2.1
Geography
The Zambezi River is the fourth largest river basin on the African continent and lies
in the semi-arid zone of southern Africa between 10◦S and 20◦S. The river stretches
from Angola and north-western Zambia, through Mozambique where it enters into the
Indian Ocean. The river basin is shared by eight countries over its course of 2600 km.
The drainage basin of 1.37 Mio km2 is split into diﬀerent portions between the eight
countries: Zambia (40.7%), Angola (18.3%), Zimbabwe (15.9%), Mozambique (11.4%),

Malawi (7.7%), Botswana (2.8%), Tanzania (2.0%), and Namibia (1.2%)[12]. The Up-
per Zambezi is here deﬁned from the headwaters of the river to Katima Mulilo in
Namibia, a smaller town approximately 200 km upstream from Victoria Falls in Liv-
ingstone, Zambia. The Upper Zambezi is fed by various tributaries, most notably the

Most of the Zambezi Basin is high plateau land with elevations of the plateau varying
between 800 m and 1450 m above mean sea level[14]. The upper Zambezi watershed is
generally characterised by gentle slopes creating large ﬂoodplains and swamps along
the course of the river. Floodplains are areas of low lying land that are subject to in-
undation by lateral overﬂow water from rivers or lakes with which they are associated.
The largest ﬂoodplain, the Barotse Plains, starts just west of Lukulu and spreads along
the river for about 200 km[11]. Along most of its length the plain is over 30 km wide,
reaching 50 km at the widest. The main body of the plain covers about 7,700 km2[12],
but the maximum ﬂooded area can cover more than 10,000 km2[15]. The main eﬀect of
the ﬂoodplains is the dampening and delay in discharge and the loss of water through

Areas of the Upper Zambezi are dominated by underlying materials of sandy lithologies.

2.2.2
Precipitation
Rainfall is strongly seasonal in the Upper Zambezi and occurs almost exclusively be-
tween October and April.
The total amount of rainfall in the basin is on average
around 950 mm yr−1. There is, however, a strong geographical gradient from north to
south. In the north extensive areas receive over 1400 mm on a yearly average, whilst
areas in the south receive less than 700 mm[14] (see Figure 2.2). Furthermore, rainfall
variability from year to year is considerable, making rainfall reliability for agricultural
and livestock production another over-riding issue. Total basin runoﬀcan vary be-

2.4
Previous Studies
Numerous studies have been carried out on the Zambezi River basin in the past few
decades, and many diﬀerent hydrological models have been designed for the basin,
varying both spatially and temporally. In 1991 Vorosmarty and Moore [12] coupled
a water balance model with a transport model operating at an 0.5◦spatial scale and
a monthly time step. The result was a systematic and substantial overestimation of
the mean annual runoﬀ. A lumped rainfall-runoﬀmodel was calibrated by Beck and
Bernauer [17] for long-term mean annual ﬂow over the period 1900-2002 using monthly
precipitation data. The results were satisfying with Pearson correlations coeﬃcients
varying from 0.6 to 0.98, but no validation was performed[18].
More recently, Meier et al. [11] designed a forecasting framework for the prediction of
discharges applied to three diﬀerent subbasins of the Zambezi River. The key param-
eter for the model was soil moisture obtained from remote sensing data. The model
yielded good results, but no validation was performed as all six years of data from
July 1995 to January 2002 were used for calibration. In 2013 Michailovsky and Bauer-
Gottwein [19] developed a rainfall-runoﬀmodel for the Western Zambezi using SWAT
and remote sensing data only. Model ﬁt was improved through data assimilation but
poor results were obtained during the validation period due to large errors in the pre-
cipitation estimates and uncertainties in the ﬂoodplain sub-model.
In 2014 Liechti et al. [20] adapted and applied a version of the SWAT model to the full
Zambezi basin at daily time steps taking special consideration to ﬂoodplain behaviour.
The model was compared to the original SWAT setup via the Nash-Sutcliﬀe coeﬃcient
and hydrograph plots and showed improvement when comparing simulated volume
ratios from both models to observed data. A sensitivity analysis was performed and
results showed that stream ﬂow parameters were more sensitive than baseﬂow param-
eters. However, no data assimilation was included.

3.1
Digital Elevation Model
An elevation model must be included as terrain slope plays a major role in runoﬀ. The
elevation data set is also used for automatic watershed and river network delineation.
The global DEM Altimeter Corrected Elevations 2 (ACE2) is one of the most accurate
global DEMs[10]. ACE2 was created by merging data from the NASA Shuttle Radar
Topography Mission with altimetry data sets[21]. In ACE2 the globe has been divided
into tiles of 15◦× 15◦, where the name of a tile refers to the southwest corner of the
tile. Building on the work of Jensen and Lovring [22] tiles covering the entire Zambezi
River from 0◦N to 30◦S and 0◦E to 30◦E were reused from their project. The tiles
were then mosaicked into one raster and cut to only cover the Upper Zambezi. Due to
the size of the basin the resolution of the DEM was changed from 500 m to 1000 m to
reduce computing time and avoid exceeding the recommended limit of 2 mio. cells in
a catchment[22].
The DEM can be downloaded from http://tethys.eaprs.cse.dmu.ac.uk/ACE2/.

has a spatial resolution of 1.0◦until 02.02.2006, and 0.5◦hereafter. NOAA-GFS can
be downloaded from http://nomads.ncep.noaa.gov/.

Chapter 4 covers the relevant theory behind SWAT and provides a short description

4.1
Hydrological and Hydrodynamic Modelling
In this project the model is split into a hydrological rainfall-runoﬀcomponent and a
hydrodynamic routing component. A hydrologic model is a simpliﬁed representation
of a part of the hydrologic cycle. Hydrologic models are primarily used for prediction
and for understanding hydrological processes. They are essential for water resources
management. Figure 4.1 shows the general sequence of processes used to model the
hydrologic cycle.
Whilst the hydrological component calculates the conversion of rainfall into runoﬀ,
and produces the surface runoﬀhydrograph, the hydrodynamic routing component
can predict the changes in shape of a hydrograph as water moves through a channel.
Routing can for instance be used to examine whether a burst of rain travels as a deluge
or it trickles out. A ﬂood event may cause a large bulge in a hydrograph upstream, but
cause less of a stir downstream, depending on factors such as the shape of the channel
and ﬂow resistance.

4.2
Soil and Water Assessment Tool
SWAT is physically based, meaning that it is based on parameters which are measur-
able in the ﬁeld and the model represents real-world physical processes. It is semi-
distributed implying that it is permitted to change in space as the focus area can be
split into a desired amount of subbasins. A beneﬁt of the semi-distributed model is
that it has a more physically-based structure in comparison with the lumped model,
in which the system has been spatially averaged and therefore may be regarded as a
single point in space. In contrast the fully distributed model considers hydrological
processes taking place at various points, an is therefore very expensive computationally
as it deﬁnes model variables as functions of space.
In this project the model is set at river basin scale and run in daily time steps. A
one-way coupling between the hydrological component and the routing component is
implemented, so that once runoﬀhas entered the river channel, the water cannot move
back into the land phase. In TIGER-NET focus is solely on the quantity of stream
ﬂow, hence investigations of pollution, soil fertility and/or other processes regarding
the quality of the stream ﬂow is outside the scope of this project. In the following
sections the most important processes of SWAT in regards to TIGER-NET will be
explained.
For a more extensive description of SWAT, readers are referred to the
SWAT documentation[3].

4.2.2

Evapotranspiration includes all processes by which liquid and solid water on the Earth’s
surface becomes atmospheric water vapour. It includes evaporation (from rivers, lakes,
bare soil etc.), transpiration, which is speciﬁcally from within vegetation, and sublim-
ination which stems from ice and snow surfaces.
In SWAT evaporation (Ea) is calculated from the potential evapotranspiration (PET).
PET (E0) is a theoretical value. It is the evaporation that would occur from a well
vegetated surface when moisture supply is not limiting. Actual evaporation drops be-
low its potential level as the soil dries out[24]. There are various methods of estimating
PET, and in SWAT three alternatives are available. The one chosen for this project is
the Hargreaves method as it only requires air temperature as input:

compensation from deeper soil layers can be controlled by two coeﬃcients: EPCO
and ESCO. ESCO is called the soil evaporation compensation coeﬃcient and it mod-

ESCO is the more the model is allowed to extract water from deeper layers to meet

4.2.4
Inﬁltration
Inﬁltration refers to the entry of water into a soil proﬁle from the soil surface. As water
percolates, the soil becomes increasingly wet, causing the rate of inﬁltration to decrease
with time until it reaches a steady value. The ﬁnal rate of inﬁltration is equivalent to
the saturated hydraulic conductivity of the soil. The amount of water entering the soil
is calculated as the diﬀerence between the daily rainfall and the daily surface runoﬀ.

of storage formed by the volume of the constant cross-section along the reach length.
In other words, the wedge storage is proportional to the diﬀerence between inﬂow and
outﬂow (qin −qout), whilst the prism storage is proportional to the discharge in the
given reach (qout). Using K in seconds as the time of travel of the ﬂoodwave through
the channel, the total stored volume is the sum of the volume of the prism and the
volume in the wedge:
Vstored = K · qout + K · X · (qin −qout)
(4.14)
where X is a weighting factor in the range 0 ≤X ≤0.5. The value of X depends
on the shape of the modelled wedge storage. When X = 0 there is no wedge storage.
In natural streams, X is typically around 0.2, however, the accuracy of X is not of
great importance, because the results of the Muskingum are relatively insensitive to
X[24]. Equation 4.14 can be rearranged to give the storage function for the Muskingum

method:
Vstored = K · (X · qin + (1 −X) · qout)
(4.15)

to

In the original SWAT setup it is assumed that the river has trapezoidal channel dimen-
sions with a ﬂat channel bed and ﬂat ﬂoodplain (see Figure 4.4). A modiﬁed, more
natural version of this setup was included to a SWAT version from 2009 where the
ﬂoodplain has a slight slope, as does the riverbed. The lowest point of the river is in
this version located at the centre of the reach 1 m below the bottom of the river edges
(see Figure 4.4).

ﬁed, more natural setup of channel and ﬂoodplain as used by SWAT2009.
The modiﬁed version has been used in this project. When the river channel overﬂows
the excess water will spread across the ﬂoodplain. On the other hand, in drier periods
where the river receives no contributions from the groundwater, it is possible for water
to be lost via transmission through the sides and bottom of the channel. Transmission

losses are aﬀected by the dimensions of the river channel and the eﬀective hydraulic
conductivity of the bed, CH K in SWAT. In SWAT there is both a CH K1 represent-

stored in the bank. From bank storage water may move into an adjacent unsaturated

deﬁned for the last HRU in the subbasin.
This coeﬃcient is known in SWAT as

4.3
Floodplain Behaviour and Data Assimilation
As stated in above in Subsection 4.2.10 water can be lost from the channel either to
the deep aquifer or bank storage. The area of Upper Zambezi is as mentioned char-
acterised by many ﬂoodplains. The Barotse Floodplain which lies south of Lukulu is
one the greatest wetlands in Africa. Liechti et al. [20] set up a revised SWAT model
of the Zambezi River basin taking into account ﬂoodplain behaviour via a modiﬁed
reservoir approach. By separating the outﬂow of the reservoir simulating a ﬂoodplain,
into main channel ﬂow and ﬂow over the ﬂoodplain, the model proved more accurate
when evaluated in terms of the Nash-Sutcliﬀe coeﬃcient and hydrograph plots. It is
expected that the Barotse Plain will play a signiﬁcant role when modelling the Upper
Zambezi and especially in high ﬂow season from November to April water may en-
ter the plain from the channel and never return. SWAT has a ﬂoodplain component
which is very complex and therefore requires much computational power. In return
the results may not be very satisfactorily as ﬂoodplains are so complicated to model.
For this reason an external routing script created by Bauer-Gottwein et al. [25] was
implemented allowing for loss through ﬂoodplains. It is done in a very simple way, by
just taking a percentage of the water completely out of the system. For each subbasin
there is a loss parameter LOSS which may be calibrated or ﬁxed at a certain value.
As the Barotse plain mainly lies in subbasin 4, this is the loss parameter which most
likely will have the biggest eﬀect on the system.

order to record the progression of the objective function. Instead of using observation

were included and replaced the observations. For NSE the ideal value to obtain is 1 and
for WBE the ideal value is 0. This formulation ensured a reasonable trade-oﬀbetween
ﬁtting the observed hydrographs (optimising NSE) and matching the observed water
balance of the catchment (optimising WBE).
Finding the global minimum of the objective function in a non-linear model such as
SWAT poses some problems when using PEST, as it uses a local search algorithm and
therefore may get ”stuck” in a local minimum instead of searching the whole multidi-
mensional space for the global minimum. In an updated version of PEST, the global
optimiser SCEUA P is available and interchangeable with PEST. SCEUA P is like
PEST in all ways, except that it ﬁxes the problem of getting stuck in a local minimum
by use of the Shuﬄed Complex Evolution algorithm (SCE).
4.6.1
Shuﬄed Complex Evolution algorithm
The Shuﬄed Complex Evolution algorithm is a global optimisation strategy developed
by Duan et al. [29]. The design was motived by the highly parameterised, non-linear
rainfall-runoﬀmodels and the cohering issues of ﬁnding the optimal parameter set of
a model. The algorithm is based on the idea of using information from previous runs
to improve new model runs.
In the ﬁrst step a population of points m is sampled randomly from the feasible space.
The space has as many dimensions n as it has parameters to calibrate and the points
are generated within the deﬁned interval for each parameter. The objective function
is computed at each point and the parameter sets are then ranked and stored in an
DTU Environment
Page 32 of 110
==PAGE:47==
Chapter 4. Methods
Maria Facchin Asmussen
array from smallest to largest function value. The array is then divided into several
communities or complexes p, each containing 2n + 1 points. Each complex is made to
evolve based on a statistical ”reproduction” process that uses the simplex geometric
shape to direct the search towards an improved direction (this is called the Competitive
Complex Evolution and can be read about in [29]). The complexes are then shuﬄed
and points are reassigned to other complexes to ensure information sharing. As the
search progresses, the entire population will converge towards the neighbourhood of
the global minimum and the processes is repeated until a criterion of convergence has
been reached.[29].

such as the number of complexes and maximum number of model runs. By adjusting

5.4
Model Error
Uncertainty is an inherent element of hydrological models and can arise from various
sources: Errors in input data, a too simple model setup, use of non-optimal parame-
ters, errors in measurements, conversion errors etc. Distinguishing between the errors
in a discharge model like this is impossible based on the available information, and
simpliﬁcations and assumptions must be made in the representation of the model error.
As with most time series data, it is expected that the residuals of the model are cor-
related over time. If the model overestimates ﬂow one day, then it will most likely
also overestimate ﬂow on the following day. When errors are correlated, statistical test
of the signiﬁcance of the parameters and the conﬁdence intervals for predicted values
will be incorrect. This may be solved by augmenting the model with an autoregressive
model for the random error.
By considering the makeup of the hydrological part of the model vs. the hydraulic
part, it is expected that the hydrological part will be the biggest error contributor.
While the routing parameters like the Muskingum parameters and Manning’s friction
factors are uncertain, runoﬀuncertainty can be expected to be much larger due to
the uncertainty of the precipitation product, as well as parameterisation error in the
model. As no direct measurement of runoﬀis available, it is assumed that the mag-
nitude and autocorrelation of the relative runoﬀerror are the same as magnitude and
autocorrelation of the relative model residuals from observation data from Katima
Mulilo:
wi = Qsim,i −Qobs,i

the noise term contribute to the output. As the meteorological inputs are correlated, it
is assumed that the runoﬀerror is spatially correlated between the various subbasins.

The major source of error in the observation data is the rating curve, which is used
to transform measurements of river levels into discharge. Rating curves are very un-
reliable as they continuously change due to erosion and deposition in the channel, so
they must be updated frequently.
As no information is given on the measurement
procedure, it is assumed that the error on the in situ measurements is uncorrelated
in time and proportional to the discharge. The relative error is assumed to be 10%,
which is a typical value for in situ discharge derived from rating curves[25].

This chapter covers the evaluation of the model as a probabilistic forecasting tool. Sta-

6.1
Forecasting Data
In order to evaluate the model as a probabilistic forecasting tool, new NOAA-GFS
precipitation ﬁles had to be downloaded. The forecasting period was decided to run
from 22.09.2014 to 31.12.2014. As mentioned NOAA-GFS release forecasts every three
hours for lead times up to seven days. Precipitation forecasts issued at midnight every
day in the forecasting period were downloaded with six hour intervals from zero hours
ahead up to 168 hours ahead (seven days). Similar to the data pre-processing when
setting up the model (Section 4.4), four forecasts were summed into total precipitation
in one day. The area covered by the precipitation extended from 10◦S, 15◦E to 20◦S,
30◦E. As air temperature plays a less important role in the model and air temperature
does not vary as much as precipitation on a daily basis, only 1-day ahead forecasts were
used for temperature forcing, so that the forecasting experiments are solely inﬂuenced
by changes in predicted precipitation.
The various forecast horizons of zero to seven days use diﬀerent precipitation forcings,
so that the forecasts only use data available at the simulated issue date. A precipitation
ﬁle was made for each forecasting horizon, resulting in a total of eight precipitation
ﬁles each covering all 15 subbasins. Each ﬁle covered the whole forecasting period, but
when the forecasting was done, the assimilation script was made to only read until the

WOIS experiment respectively during forecasting. The method of calculating NSE in
the Forecasting Script was compared to the method used in the Routing Script, and
the two methods are (as expected) equal. Perhaps the large diﬀerences are due to the
temporal trend, which was not completely removed, by the AR1 model.

Chapter 7
Discussion
This chapter discusses methods of improvements. Improvements can be made in the
setup of the model, in the calibration process, in the error model analysis, as well as
in the forecasting process.
7.1
Model Setup and Calibration
Initially the basin was split into two by the slope band of 1%.
Preferably a slope
band should divide the area into equal halves, but the area was here divided as 65%
low slope to 35% high slope. In order to improve the model one might test a smaller
slope band, so the slope division becomes more equal. More slope bands could also
be implemented.
Lowering the land use, soil type and slope percentage thresholds
would increase the accuracy, but also the complexity of the model. The threshold size
of 30,000 km2 could be lowered for a denser stream network, but as with the other
thresholds this would cost more computational power.
A key issue of the model is that it does not take ﬂoodplain behaviour properly into
account. SWAT does allow for a wetland out-take but it is highly complex and would
increase computer time drastically. Therefore the external routing scheme implemented
simulated ﬂoodplains in a much more simple manner, by merely incorporating a LOSS
parameter, that takes a calibrated percentage out of the system. For this model only
subbasin 4 was allowed any loss, but a more complex model may choose to take water
out of subbasins 2 and 3 as well, as the Barotse Floodplain also covered parts of these
areas.
The results of the model calibration showed a good ﬁt between simulations and obser-
vations from Katima Mulilo. NSE was at 0.79 indicating a very good model, whilst
59
==PAGE:74==
Chapter 7. Discussion
Maria Facchin Asmussen
the WBE was quite small at -4.9 m3s−1.
The validation results for Katima Mulilo
were not as good, perhaps aﬀected by the model’s strong dependence on precipitation.
Especially in the northern areas, where precipitation is much heavier than in southern
areas, were the model simulations strongly dependent on precipitation. Unfortunately
the precipitation product available greatly overestimated precipitation in the study
area, which became very noticeable in the rain season from November to April every
year. A more accurate precipitation product would most likely improve the model ﬁt
when the model simulations are so highly dependent on rain.
The simulation of baseﬂow was an issue in all calibration runs.
The groundwater
threshold for baseﬂow to occur (GWQMN) was ﬁxed at 100 mm. This value was cho-
sen based on results made by Bauer-Gottwein et al. [25] on the Kavango River which
has a relatively low discharge. Kittel [26] investigated the Volta River which has a
higher discharge than Kavango, but lower than Zambezi, and for Volta GWQMN was
ﬁxed at 200 mm. Seeing as there is much more water running through the Zambezi,
perhaps the threshold should have been at 200 mm or even higher. This way the river
would take longer to dry out, so the water would remain in the shallow aquifer for a
longer time.
In the calibration process there was a problem with a few of the calibrated parameter
values which seemed to stick to the bounds. Widening the allowed intervals of said
parameters may improve the model. It may, however, also have the opposite eﬀect,
in that a parameter may never reach its optimum value if initiated too far from its
optimum. The intervals of the parameters in question could be either widened, limited
or transferred in further investigations. Finally, in situ observations from other hydro-
metric stations would be beneﬁcial for a more spatially correct model.
When making any changes to the model, computational power must be taken into ac-
count. The ﬁrst calibration run, which included 14 parameters, took about 30 hours,
whilst the ﬁnal calibration with 48 parameters took up to seven days to complete,
showing the importance of keeping any unnecessary parameters out of the calibration
or at least ﬁxed.
7.2
Error Model
The runoﬀforcing was considered the dominant source of error, and the magnitude
and autocorrelation of the error was quantiﬁed based on comparing simulations to
DTU Environment
Page 60 of 110
==PAGE:75==

observations. Assuming that runoﬀis the only error is a highly simpliﬁed view, as
the ﬂoodplains as mentioned will also contribute errors to the hydrodynamic routing
scheme. Transmission is an increasing issue when the study area covers a very large
region, where water travels a long way from the source to the outlet. The methodol-
ogy was based on the error model incorporated by Bauer-Gottwein et al. [25] on the
Kavango, which covers a much smaller area, therefore routing errors in the Kavango
scheme will have been less signiﬁcant, than what they may be for the Zambezi River.
Any information recorded on the methods of data gathering can improve and elaborate
the error model analysis.
The analysis of the residuals showed a highly temporal correlation. For this project
the autocorrelation of the errors was assumed to be described by an AR1 model. An
investigation of the AR1 model residuals showed that the residuals fell within the 5%
signiﬁcance level, suggesting that an AR1 model was appropriate for describing the
autocorrelation the errors.
The residuals of the AR1 model, however, still showed
some seasonal correlation, so perhaps a higher order autoregressive model should be
tested for a better description of the error autocorrelation. The temporal correlation
was evident in both the AR1 model set up by WOIS, as well as the AR1 model where
the autocorrelation of the error was quantiﬁed by the error between simulations and
observations.
In order to improve error modelling it may also be beneﬁcial to investigate the diﬀer-
ent components of SWAT. SWAT may be divided into surface runoﬀ, interﬂow, and
baseﬂow, and for each component an individual error could be incorporated for higher
model error accuracy.
7.3
Operational Hydrological Forecasting
Assimilation of in situ data was shown to greatly improve the model performance com-
pared to an open-loop run, but based on coverage not all residuals fell within the 95%
conﬁdence interval. Comparison of various forecasting experiments showed that the
choice of the autoregressive model, plays a role on the outcome of the performance
of the forecasting system. A higher observation error showed better statistical perfor-
mance, but less sharpness as the conﬁdence intervals widens.
It was expected that the persistence forecasts would be most accurate for 0-day ahead
to 2- or 3-days ahead. Climatology would be most reliable for long-term forecasting, but

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to construct a hydrologic-hydrodynamic operational fore-
casting model of the Upper Zambezi area using open-source software and free data.
The system is speciﬁcally adapted to the conditions prevailing in many African basins
where data is scarce due to budget constraints, intergovernmental disputes, or other
issues. Setting up a hydrologic-hydrodynamic model is a very complex task as the
system is highly parameterised. The modelling approach in this study was based on
coupling a separate routing model to a rainfall-runoﬀmodel.
The rainfall-runoﬀmodel was based on the Soil and Water Assessment Tool and proved
to be highly dependent on precipitation. Based on previous studies the precipitation
forcing was scaled accordingly to comparison results between NOAA-GFS data and
FEWS-RFE data. The model showed good results in the calibration period and satis-
factorily results for the validation period at the same hydrometric station in Katima
Mulilo.
There were issues concerning a lack of baseﬂow as well as a few parame-
ters which seemed to stick to their calibration bounds. Validation results for Lukulu
upstream of Katima Mulilo were quite poor, but could most likely be improved by
including ﬂoodplain behaviour in the model. As NOAA-GFS data were only available
from February 2005 onwards, the model cannot be extended further back in time, but
as the time series grows with real-time data an updated calibration should be run and
validated.
An AR1 error model was set up in order to eliminate correlated errors in the forecasting
system. Two diﬀerent AR1 models were tested. Both models had a time correlation
parameter close to 1, so the main diﬀerence was in the model error, which was 8.61%
in the baseline experiment and 9.31% in the WOIS experiment. In both models less
than 5% of the residuals were outside the conﬁdence 95% conﬁdence interval, but in
63

both models a temporal correlation was still evident after application of the AR1 model.
Both autoregressive models were applied to the hydrologic-hydrodynamic model and
tested as forecasting systems. Both setups were evaluated against climatology and
persistence benchmarks. The WOIS experiment performed better than the baseline
experiment most likely because the error component was slightly larger. Better per-
formance statistics was, however, traded for less sharpness of the forecasts. Evaluated
against benchmarks the model failed, as the forecasting period was too short and only
covered the low ﬂow regime. The assimilated forecasts were better than climatology
only when forecasting 0-day ahead, and for WOIS also 1-day ahead. By extending the
forecasting period of the persistence forecasts, their performance statistics changed
drastically, and whilst NSE was still close to 1, persistence forecasts were generally
worse than the two forecasting experiments.
Before implementing the model in water resources management, it is recommended to
elaborate the calibration scheme and speciﬁcally include a more complex component
of ﬂoodplain behaviour. The Upper Zambezi is highly aﬀected by the great extend
of the Barotse ﬂoodplain, making discharge especially in the high ﬂow season harder
to predict. Furthermore, higher order autoregressive models should be evaluated in
an attempt to eliminate temporal correlation completely. Finally, a proper evalua-
tion of the forecasting system would require an extension of the evaluated period up
to at least one year, so that both high ﬂow and low ﬂow regimes are taken into account.

Assimilation approach via the
Kalman Filter
Kalman ﬁltering is an algorithm that uses a series of measurements observed over time,
and produces estimates of unknown variables that tend to be more precise that those
based on a single measurement alone. It is widely used in data assimilation techniques
in which forecasted states are updated as new measurements are acquired, taking into
account any relative uncertainties[34].
At each discrete time increment, a linear operator is applied to the state to generate
the new state, with some noise mixed in, and optionally some information from the
controls on the system if they are known. This is know as the a priori state estimate
as it does not include any observation information from the current time step.
