Abstract
Superconducting qubits are a leading platform for scalable quantum
computing, where the implementation of fast, high-fidelity two-qubit
gates remains a central challenge. This thesis investigates the Sudden
Net-Zero (SNZ) gate, an entangling two-qubit gate scheme based on
baseband flux pulses, aiming to establish a theoretical foundation
of the gate and its experimental considerations within the SQuID
laboratory setup.
The analysis combines analytical modeling of the transmon Hamil-
tonian with numerical simulations via a master equation solver to ex-
plore qubit–qubit coupling, focusing on interaction between computa-
tional and non-computational states, for realizing a fast CZ/CPHASE
gate. The scheme intentionally exploits intermediate leakage to non-
computational states, operating at the quantum speed limit of gate
operation.
Parameter sweeps characterize gate performance as a function of
flux amplitude, idle time, and pulse duration. A key result identifies
a critical detuning to coupling threshold, ∆/J2 ≈1.603, governing
viable gate operation. Hardware constraints, such as a 1 GS/s sam-
pling rate, are addressed by fine-tuning the flux amplitude using a
parameter B, maintaining gate speed. Simulations also show that flux
pulses shorter than the optimal duration lead to irreversible leakage,
emphasizing that pulse lengths should always be rounded up.
Concludingly, the findings provide crucial tools for a framework to
build a feasible experimental implementation of the SNZ gate, which
contributes to the efforts towards scalable quantum computation.

1 Introduction
I
n our modern world the demand to solve complex problems is
increasing. Technological advancements are rapidly evolving, and
due to Moore’s law the number of transistors in integrated circuits is
doubled every two years. Making chips smaller, approaching ∼10
nm dimensionality, the laws of classical physics are no longer obeyed
at these scales. It is therefore imperative to understand how quantum
physics can be exploited in technology [1, 2].
In the 1980’s Feynman proposed that a quantum computer based
on quantum logic would be suitable for modeling quantum systems
– which sparked the field of quantum computing (QC): A branch
of computing which is utilizing quantum effects, and in doing so,
outperforming modern supercomputers at given tasks, such as prime-
factor factorization, unstructured search, simulating quantum systems,
chemical reactions, optimization problems, cryptography, machine
learning, AI, and many more [2]. In 2019, Google claimed to have
solved a problem in 200 seconds that would have taken 10,000 years
to solve on a classical computer [3].
However, the current bottleneck across all physical implementa-
tions of quantum computing is noise and decoherence, gate fidelity, error
rates, and scalability. The fragile nature of quantum states compared
to classical states serves as a major challenge in the field of quantum
computing, where it is desired to be able to perform exact gate op-
erations. In state-of-the-art quantum computing, there are several
promising physical QC platforms that have ingenious ways to work
around these bottlenecks, e.g. superconducting qubits, trapped ions,
topological qubits, spin qubits, and photonic qubits.
Superconducting qubits is a leading candidate in this matter, with
promising technological advancements in qubit-number, quantum
error correction to mitigate quantum errors and implementation. It is
therefore a promising platform for quantum computing.
To achieve universal quantum computing a universal gate set is
necessary. A universal gate set is any set of quantum gates that can
approximate any gate to a desired precision ϵ using a sequence of
gates from the gate set. The Solovay-Kitaev theorem indicates that
certain finite sets of gates can be used to realize universal quantum
computing, and is one of the most significant results in the field of
quantum computing [4, 5].
The Controlled-Z (CZ) two-qubit gate is one choice for a two-qubit
gate to be used in a universal gate set. One example of a such gate set

2.2.2 Open quantum systems and decoherence
In physical implementations, quantum systems interact with their
environment – and this interaction remains one of the greatest chal-
lenges of modern quantum technology. In this setting, the Schödinger
equation method is not sufficient because states might not be pure
anymore. Therefore, a framework to describe how quantum states
interact with their surroundings – what happens when dissipation to
the environment occurs – is needed.

2.4 Introduction to superconducting qubits
We use superconducting materials in quantum computing because the
current and voltage state of a normal metal wire are not suitable for
quantum computing because information is lost in internal scattering
processes. To get rid of scattering, we use a superconductor to encode
quantum information, and we get: Superconducting Qubits. In
this section I will introduce the phenomenon superconductivity, then
introduce the classical framework to understand the dynamics of elec-
trical circuits, and then finally quantizing the variables, going into the
quantum regime.

2.5 The transmon qubit: An anharmonic oscillator
The transmon qubit was first described in 2007 as a special type of
qubit specifically insensitive to charge noise [15]. The name transmon
is an abbreviation of transmission line shunted plasma oscillation qubit,
meaning that it was introduced as a type of superconducting qubit
that utilizes a transmission line resonator in parallel with a Josephson
junction. This Josephson junction is what solves the equidistance
problem and introduces the anharmonicity, as introduced in the
previous section.
The transmon is essentially an LC-circuit where the linear inductor
with inductive energy EL has been replaced with a Josephson junction
with inductive energy EJ, which will be derived in this section. The
transmon is a further development of the so-called Cooper pair box,
but with a significantly increased ratio of Josephson energy and
charging energy EJ/EC. It is this ratio that sets the transmon apart
from a Cooper pair-box and a phase qubit. A transmon qubit will
typically have an EJ/EC ∼several 10′s to 100′s [15].
Two other quantities are crucial in operations of a transmon; the
anharmonicity and the charge dispersion. The charge dispersion
describes the variation of the position of the energy levels as a func-
tion of the charge offset ng and thereby determines the transmon’s
sensitivity to charge noise. In general, we want the transmon to be as
insensitive to charge noise as possible to ensure stability throughout
operation.

As EJ/EC increases, the potential becomes deeper and flatter
around its minima, leading to more tightly bound, harmonic-like
states with smaller anharmonicity. While this improves charge noise
insensitivity, it also reduces the energy difference between adjacent

to design without leakage into higher states. Conversely, a lower
EJ/EC ratio increases anharmonicity but worsens charge dispersion,
introducing more dephasing.
This tradeoff is central to transmon design: an optimal working
point balances gate speed, fidelity, and coherence by choosing an
appropriate EJ/EC, often around 50–100. The shape and spacing
of wavefunctions shown in the plots visually highlight this com-
promise—wider wells (high EJ/EC) support more bound states and
narrower level spacings, whereas shallower wells (low EJ/EC) have
fewer levels but stronger nonlinearity.

Now that we have covered X and Y gates, the last gate to achieve full
control over the qubit state in the Bloch sphere (seen in Fig 2.2) is the

updating the phase ϕ in the control electronics, effectively rotating
the qubit’s state around the Z-axis by an angle θ:

3.3 Readout in the dispersive regime
As mentioned in the introduction to this chapter, readout is a crucial
part of doing experiments with qubits – no matter the platform. In
this work, readout is used to check that the qubits were initialized
in the desired state (e.g. |11⟩), to quantify leakage, phase, etc. – all
parameters which are relevant for QC experiments and a two-qubit
CZ gate in particular, but more about this in Chapts. 5 and 6.
In a dispersive measurement a qubit is coupled off resonantly with
a linear resonator, see Fig. 3.3. Photons in the resonator will acquire
a phase shift which depends on the resonator’s frequency, and the
resonator’s frequency will depend on the qubit’s quantum state. The
readout procedure is to probe the resonator and measure the scattered
microwave signal which allows for qubit state distinction [7, 8].

one has to perform an ensemble of identical experiments to recover
an optimal measurement fidelity.

When designing such a two-qubit gate in a physical qubit platform,

the gate. It has to be easily implemented, tunable, stable, have a high
fidelity – and it has to correspond to an actual physical interaction,
which will govern the Hamiltonian.
There are two main classes of two-qubit entangling gates employ-
ing different coupling mechanisms:
Fixed coupling: This typically involves either capacitive or induc-
tive coupling elements that create a constant interaction between
qubits. The primary advantage of fixed coupling is its simplicity
and ease of implementation. However, because the coupling is
always active, it cannot be dynamically adjusted or turned off
during operation. This can lead to unwanted interactions between
neighboring qubits.
Tunable coupling: In this approach, the interaction strength
between qubits can be dynamically controlled. This allows the
effective coupling strength to be increased during gate operations
and reduced to near-zero when not in use. This selective activation
helps to reduce crosstalk and improve scalability in multi-qubit
systems.

The start point is the zero flux point (sweet spot) of the |11⟩state.

branch.

quantum states |11⟩and |02⟩.

5.3.1 Advantages and challenges of the SNZ scheme
Here I summarize and elaborate on the advantages of the SNZ pulse:
• Operating at the speed limit: The population inversion time is
determined by tlim. This tlim is the time it takes for a unipolar
pulse to do a π-pulse, exchanging the two-qubit population from
|11⟩→|02⟩and back again. The reason why we reach the speed
limit is because the pulse is sudden and therefore not adiabatic any-
more, thus introducing maximal leakage out of the computational
subspace amid the pulse.
• A greatly simplified tuneup: This means that the pulse is step-like
and flat. The previous pulses (e.g. those in Fig. 5.2) had to follow
an optimized so-called Martinis-Geller scheme with carefully opti-
mized trajectories to minimize leakage [28]. Now, with the SNZ
pulse, the gate is adiabatic on purpose, meaning that there is no
demand for optimizing gate trajectory.
• First-order flux insensitivity: Like the CNZ pulse scheme, the
SNZ scheme uses the flux symmetry of the transmon Hamiltonian,
which makes the conditional phase and single-qubit phases of the
pulsed qubit insensitive to low-frequency noise [4].
• Repeatability: Minimizing the history dependence: Regarding
charge-up of capacitors in the circuit it is significantly desired to
use a bipolar flux-method, to cancel out any charge-buildup on the
capacitors. This also justifies using the Lindblad master equation
(assuming Markovian dynamics) to solve the system.

5.4.7 The fine-tuning parameter B
Now that we have uncovered the fundamental limit of when the
CPHASE gate can be implemented in the given sequence, we might
raise the question: How easy is it to hit the right SNZ amplitude, idle time,
and pulse length in a laboratory?
In a laboratory setting, the flux pulse will be generated by some
ultra-fast quantum control hardware called an OPX (more about the
experimental aspects in Sect. 6). The OPX component sets the width
of the time bins of the flux pulse, i.e. a resolution of how precise in
time pulses can be resolved. In the SQuID lab, this time resolution
is 1/sr = 1 ns, where sr = 1 GHz is the sample rate. If the pulse
shape changes faster than this, the OPX cannot keep up. In order to
fine-tune the conditional phase, it is therefore much easier to control
the amplitude of the flux bias, i.e. the voltage, than controlling the
sampling rate of the flux pulse generator – we cannot change this.
To work around this limitation we tweak the SNZ pulse by adding
one flux point with a lower amplitude than A, see Fig. 5.17. Another

5.4.8 Gate speed and sample rate limitations
Now that we have looked at how the gate performance changes with
varying A, tϕ, and B it is insightful to also analyze tp. Until now
we have covered that the tϕ idle time greatly impacts the conditional
phase and leakage – and so this will be the parameter that sets the
conditional phase. For this reason, one chooses a fixed tp and sticks
with this throughout all experiments. While tϕ ≪tp and for this
reason the accuracy of tϕ is the bottleneck, it is still important to
choose tp correctly.
==PAGE:60==
CHAPTER 5. NUMERICAL SIMULATIONS OF THE SUDDEN NET-ZERO GATE
55
It turns out to be especially important, given that the SNZ pulse
has the inherent feature that it already operates on the speed limit.
This means that if we try to employ a tp pulse shorter than the
measured tlim population inversion time, then it will not be possible
to make a perfect-fidelity CPHASE gate because of leakage losses.
With other words: If we try to make the gate faster, we are against the
fundamental speed-limit of how long a population inversion takes,
and we therefore cannot do the CZ gate without having leakage. In
Figs. 5.21 and 5.22 I show that: If tp is 10 % too short there will always
be leakage which is not possible to correct for.
From this numerical simulation it can clearly be concluded
that one should always round up the tp pulse time, instead of
rounding down, since minimal leakage is otherwise unattain-
able.

6.1.1 Frequency conversion and IQ-mixing
In the experimental implementation of a qubit technology one has to
conform to the experimental limitations and work around technical
limitations. Instead of directly generating GHz signals (qubit frequen-
cies are typically in the 4-8 GHz range) we generate low-frequency
IQ baseband envelopes in the MHz regime (in the OPX), and use an
analog mixer (inside the octave) to mix these MHz signals with a local
oscillator (LO) signal – this is called frequency upconversion (and
downconversion when going the other way).
In Fig. 6.1 it is seen that there are two signals for the drive, feed,
and readout lines, respectively. As indicated in the figure, these are I
and a Q signals which the OPX quantum controller feeds to the octave.
In the following I will explain the working principle of IQ-mixing in
upconversion.
1. The OPX generates baseband I(t) and Q(t) low-frequency signals
(intermediate frequency ωIF) and sends them to the octave through
the two respective channels seen for each output signal in Fig. 6.2,
marked by I and Q.
2. The octave receives these two IF signals I(t) and Q(t) – which
define the amplitude, phase, and shape of the desired microwave
pulse. These signals are typically in the 10’s to 100’s of MHz range.
3. The octave produces a stable high-frequency microwave tone called

6.5 Walkthrough of methods for benchmarking the
implementation of the SNZ CZ-gate
As a final step in the strategy for implementing the SNZ gate it is
useful to discuss metrics to quantify the performance of the gate. To
do this, one can employ a combination of experimental benchmarking
techniques and theoretical characterization tools. These allows to
quantify the gate’s fidelity, isolate specific error channels, and compare
performance against standard two-qubit operations.
One of the most scalable and robust techniques for gate fidelity es-
timation is interleaved randomized benchmarking (IRB). IRB involves run-
ning standard randomized benchmarking (RB) sequences while inter-
leaving the gate under test—in this case, the SNZ CZ gate—between
each Clifford operation. By comparing the decay curves of the refer-
ence and interleaved sequences, one can extract the average fidelity of
the implemented gate independent of state preparation and measure-
ment errors. IRB treats the gate as a black-box unitary and is especially
useful for comparing the SNZ CZ gate’s performance directly against
native or hardware-defined CZ gates [28].
Finally, one can also performfull quantum process tomography (QPT).
This technique allows to reconstruct the full process matrix (χ-matrix
or Pauli transfer matrix) of the implemented gate. By preparing a
complete set of input states and performing quantum state tomogra-
phy on the corresponding outputs, one can recover the full quantum
operation [31, 37] and use this to more precisely estimate gate fidelity
and compare our implementation to other platforms.

7.1 Discussion
In this project, I have presented a theoretical framework to describe
the behavior of the Sudden Net-Zero (SNZ) gate, in theory and in
experiment. The findings mark a step forward in developing fast,
high-fidelity two-qubit gates for superconducting quantum processors
in the SQuID laboratory.
To describe the SNZ gate behavior, a fundamental understanding
of the transmon qubit system was formulated, using the Lagrangian
of an LC-circuit, adding the Josephson junction, and deriving the
Hamiltonian of a transmon qubit. The interaction physics of coupling
two gates was derived, and the collective behavior of the energies of
two qubits was plotted, showing the flux branches. The flux branches
revealed that one can utilize couplings between computational (|11⟩)
and non-computational (|02⟩) states using avoided crossings between
these spectra. The avoided crossings emerge due to couplings between
energy states, which turn out to be mathematically described by a
constant coupling, J2.
To simulate the interaction between states |11⟩and |02⟩(t), a two-
level system with a flux-variable detuning ∆02 and a constant coupling
J21 was therefore implemented. The results of this model reveal

Appendix. A.3) changing the detuning ∆and coupling J2 values
showed that the detuning ∆affects the vertical periodicity, and
regarding the horizontal periodicity, a more comprehensive model
was set up to understand the behavior.
In the analytical model employing matrix algebra, it was found
that the parameter that determines the viability (phase freedom)
of the SNZ gate is ∆/J2. A characteristic width of the window
detuning ∆= 1.603 was found. What physically happens beyond
this limit is that the population inversion matrices are rotating
not around the longitudinal {|11⟩, |02⟩}-axis anymore, but rather
a skewed axis, where the state never reaches the |02⟩state fully,
and therefore is never able to gain a phase (since this phase really
comes from being far away from the |11⟩state energetically).
One of the biggest challenges in implementing the SNZ pulse
will be the OPX sample rate limitations: Getting the pulse to
operate sudden enough – essentially for the gate to live up to
its name. To address this problem, the fine-tuning parameter B
was implemented. By sweeping the fine-tuning parameter B, I
demonstrated effective control of conditional phase and leakage.
The intuition behind this behavior is that the conditional phase is
sensitive to small phase changes while idling for time tϕ due to
the large detuning ∆02 that is present in the idling period. This is
evident in the conditional phase and leakage plots in Figs. 5.7 and
5.8 where both parameters go through several periodic cycles in
only 2 ns. This reveals that changes in the phase of |11⟩happen
on the order of sub-nanoseconds. But given the sample rate of the
OPX, simply tuning the amplitude effectively changes the time that
the pulse is on, by adding a very small window in time where the
pulse is effectively halfway on.
An interesting feature of the SNZ pulse is that because it is operat-
ing at the speed limit, the bipolar pulse length tp/2 should always
be rounded up, given that it is only possible to recover from a too
long pulse than a too short pulse to achieve minimal leakage, see
Fig. 5.22. Here, it was shown that a 10 % too large tp showed full
recovery of the minimal leakage, while a 10 % too short pulse did
not recover the minimal leakage.
Simulating an open quantum system-version of the same two-
qutrit model with realistic relaxation and dephasing values of T1
and T2 from Ref. [28] (while keeping the remaining parameters
at optimal values) it was seen that this imposes a 0.6 % state
leakage from the |11⟩state. An interesting result is that the leakage
rate L1 decreases when adding relaxation and dephasing to the
model, which is counter-intuitive. However, this makes sense, since
relaxation yields a channel for the |02⟩state to relax to the |01⟩
state, for example. This is not an indicator for a higher fidelity, since
==PAGE:73==

68
population still leaks out of the two-qutrit subspace, thus making
the CPHASE gate unsuccessful. Therefore, one should do a more
accurate measure of the gate fidelity in these cases, e.g. calculating
the entanglement fidelity – which will circumvent pitfalls like this.
The conditional phase and leakage parameters have been chosen,
however one could also have chosen other parameters, e.g. the
entanglement fidelity, to quantify theoretical gate performance. In
a two-qutrit model with no dissipation, in areas where A = 1, it is
possible to achieve a fidelity of F = 1, given that the simulations
are ideal two-level simulations. In a real-world setting, the fidelity
will always be lower than 1. One way to map the fidelity more
quantitatively is via the entanglement fidelity briefly discussed
earlier, which is a proposal for future work.
Finally, it can be concluded that the numerical simulations display
(a) the fast nature of the gate, (b) the configurability of the conditional
phase by tuning A, tϕ, and B, and (c) the experimental considera-
tions show a step-by-step approach to implementing the SNZ gate
by preliminary calibration experiments, and proposes a method to
measure the conditional phase and leakage, while also briefly pre-
senting three different ways to benchmark the gate performance, via
e.g. interleaved randomized benchmarking or full quantum process
tomography.
7.2 Conclusion
This thesis has developed a bottom-up theoretical framework for ana-
lyzing the Sudden Net-Zero (SNZ) gate in superconducting qubits.
Starting from the quantization of the LC circuit and the inclusion of
the Josephson junction as a nonlinear inductor, we derived the trans-
mon Hamiltonian and incorporated coupling mechanisms relevant
to gate engineering. Building on this foundation, a two-qutrit model
and matrix-based description of the SNZ flux pulse were constructed
to capture the essential dynamics of the gate.
By combining analytical modeling with numerical master-equation
simulations, we have demonstrated that the SNZ gate offers a fast and
tunable approach to implementing high-fidelity two-qubit CPHASE
gates. The {A, tϕ}-landscapes revealed a strong correlation between
conditional phase and leakage rate L1 out of the computational sub-
space. A key result is the identification of a critical detuning threshold
∆≈1.603J2, beyond which complete population transfer and phase
accumulation are no longer achievable.
Additionally, we found that the large ∆02 detuning present during
idling makes the SNZ gate highly sensitive to sub-nanosecond pulse
timing errors. To address practical hardware constraints, such as the
OPX’s 1 GS/s sampling rate, we introduced a fine-tuning parameter B
to adjust the flux amplitude, effectively designing a means to fine-tune
the conditional phase and leakage, while maintaining gate speed.
==PAGE:74==
CHAPTER 7. DISCUSSION AND OUTLOOK
69
The results also showed that the length of the bipolar fluxes t2/2 in
an experimental setting is preferred to be slightly longer, rather than
slightly shorter than the theoretical optimum, as slightly too short
pulses resulted in irrecoverable leakage, while slightly longer pulses
maintain high fidelity by tuning parameters accordingly.
Overall, the SNZ gate presents a viable approach to high-speed
entangling two-qubit gates, with strong potential for scalability —
provided that decoherence, leakage, and experimental distortions are
managed and optimized. These findings offer both a theoretical and
practical foundation for future experimental realization within the
SQuID laboratory and beyond.
7.3 Outlook
Looking at state-of-the-art quantum computing, we can ask: How
high do we need our fidelities to be to implement a successful two-
qubit gate? Current efforts are posted both in reducing errors and
leakage, but also in correcting errors that have already happened:
This is called quantum error correction (QEC). QEC can suppress
errors if physical gate and measurement error rates are below a certain
threshold. Surface codes tolerate error rates up to approximately 1 %
per gate operation [38]. Current T1 and T2−echo times in transmon
qubits allows for high gate fidelities over 99.9 % for the SNZ gate, as
shown in Ref. [4]. The SNZ scheme is therefore a promising scheme
for scalable quantum computing, given that errors are below the
threshold and therefore can be corrected.
However, several improvements and developments at this point is
possible:
By implementing the full Hamiltonian in the numerical simulations,
essentially the full transmon Hamiltonian with terms H1, H2, and
the interaction term Hint, one will be able to simulate crosstalk,
couplings, etc. between qubits. The challenge in this is that the
complexity of simulations quickly increases, as one has to manually
keep track of which energy branch corresponds to which state
(hybridization changes the eigen-energies). Obtaining a detailed
understanding of the flux spectra is crucial for the implementation
of SNZ, since it relies on the fact that we do not cross other avoided
crossings on our way to and from the |11⟩↔|02⟩-avoided crossing.
One of the greatest challenge of the SNZ pulse is not the quantum
physics – it’s the classical control hardware. The current 1 ns-level
timing granularity makes it hard to land exactly on the narrow
leakage and conditional phase condition ridge where the gate is
both accurate, fast and low-leakage. This thesis has presented ways
to work around this by adding more parameters.
Designing an automated version of the SNZ gate in the qubit
control software Pelagic where one essentially chooses two qubits,
and based on the saved information from calibration experiments
==PAGE:75==
CHAPTER 7. DISCUSSION AND OUTLOOK
70
the program will know which SNZ parameters to implement –
thereby implementing the SNZ gate in one click.
Once the SNZ scheme has been successfully setup, one can employ
and analyse other pulse shapes, such as the one in Appendix A.1.
Referring to the results of the state fidelity in Sect. 5.4.4, a better
metric of the fidelity as more interactions are included in the model,
is the entanglement fidelity, because the state-dependency of the
fidelity is eliminated.
Evaluating the experimental implementation of the SNZ gate, the
sudden behavior of the pulse will always bring along with it a
physical realization, and we can pose the question: How well does
the AWG inside the OPX produce this SNZ flux-pulse, and are
transients a big problem? A way to work around these transients is
to observe the actual SNZ pulse behavior and design a predistorted
signal, to counteract transients.
