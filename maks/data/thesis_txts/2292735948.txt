Abstract
One of the challenges in the development of Perovskite Solar Cells(PSSC) is
the instability of the photoabsorbing perovskite(PS) layer. A popular choice
for the perovskite layer are mixed halide perovskites(PS). PS being an ionic
crystal, dissolve easily in water and is therefore sensitive to humidity. Pro-
tecting the PS ﬁlm by coverage with an inorganic ﬁlm would be a means to
improve their stability. NiO is a p-type conductor and as such could func-
tion as an anodic protection layer. PS ﬁlms are unstable at high temperatures
⪆250oC. Deposition of inorganic ﬁlms should therefore occur at suﬃciently
low temperatures.
In this study it was attempted to characterize low temperature sputtered NiO
ﬁlms. The NiO ﬁlms electronic properties would be investigated by Elec-
trochemical Impedance Spectroscopy(EIS), Ultraviolet Photoelectron Spec-
troscopy(UPS) and Ultraviolet Visible Spectroscopy(UVVis). This would lead
to a prediction of whether low temperature NiO ﬁlms match the electronic
structure of a PS layer, to function as hole extraction layer in a PSSC. The
ﬁlms which were characterized as polycrystalline NiO ﬁlms would result in
a valence band position close to match that of a mixed halide perovskite. An
attempt was made to sputter deposit NiO ﬁlms on a planar PS layer and a meso-
porous PS layer. It was possible to deposit NiO on the mesoporous PS layer
without signiﬁcant degradation of the layer. Deposition on a planar PS layer
would lead to some alterations of the optical and crystalline properties of the
layer. The NiO ﬁlms were not able to protect the perovskite when in contact
with water. An investigation of the stability of NiO ﬁlms in alkaline solution
would reveal that the ﬁlms were stable, but the presence of pinholes would lead
to a degradation of the ﬁlm. It was therefore concluded that NiO is a likely
candidate for protecting a perovskite from humidity as well as functioning as
hole extraction layer in a PSSC device.

Introduction
The era of covering the worlds energy demand by fossil fuels is coming to an
end. At least several phenomena indicate this trend. Fossil fuel consumption
is not a reversible energy resource. Once combustion of oil has occured it
will take thousands of years for plants and oceans to absorb the CO2 produced
during combustion, and again turn it into an energy dense resource in the
earths crust. The destructive impact to our environment with increased CO2
concentrations in our atmosphere leading to global warming eﬀects as well as
acidifying the oceans are phenomenae which seriously alters the living condi-
tions for many species. Furthermore the rising energy prices through the past
decade as well as the decreased output of oil in Europe, would indicate fossil
fuels as being a limited resource.[41]
Meeting the worlds energy demand by some alternative energy resource is
a challenge. A continuous transition to a more sustainable energy production
is more promising in case of the energy demand for stationary applications.
Such as heating houses and supplying electricity. Conversion of solar energy
to electricity by solar cells, is one example of a more sustainable electricity pro-
duction. The market for solar cells is gaining terrain in less developed coun-
tries, especially in areas which are not reached by the electricity grid, solar
cell technology can be competitive.[34] The solar cell market is largely repre-
sented by silicon based technology. One way to increase the market for solar
cells is if they can be produced at a lower price. Perovskite Solar Cells(PSSCs)
have emerged from research within Dye Sensitized Solar Cells (DSSC) , where
the perovskite was used as a light absorbing dye on the mesoporous electron
conducting TiO2 layer.[30]. As thin ﬁlm PSSC devices can be fabricated by
simple solution processing techniques, they are scalable and can oﬀer low pro-
duction costs. They have shown to be eﬃcient but yet unstable, as solar cells.
Reaching a non-stabilized eﬃciency of 20.1% within few years, close to that
of multicrystalline silicon (20.8%).[1]
The research in PSSC devices have shown impressive eﬃciencies, which
is of hard use if they are not stable. A popular choice for the Perovskite pho-
toabsorber are methylammonium(MA) lead halide(X) salts (MAPbX3). These
being ionic crystals, dissolve easily in water.[30] One of the challenges in sta-
bilizing the PSSC devices is to protect the Perovskite from humidity. For
a relative humidity of 50%, the half-life of the absorbance at 600nm is just
26 days for MAPbI3[13].

An even more challenging area of sustainable energy production, is within
energy storage for mobile applications. The main problem being the low en-
ergy density of stored energy based on sustainable energy production. As well
as the loss of eﬃciency when converting one type of energy to another. Huge
expensive batteries are required if a car should store enough electricity to cover
the same distance as fossil fuel based cars. Furthermore the amount of materials
required for battery production to replace the fuel based cars in the world are
insuﬃcient.[42] Photoelectrochemical fuel production is a technology which
could oﬀer a means of directly converting solar energy to fuel.
In the case of hydrogen production for fuel. A photoelectrochemical(PEC)
cell device capable of driving both an oxidation of water(1,23V vs RHE) as
well as reduction of 2H+ −→H2(0V vs RHE) requires a signiﬁcant photo-
voltage. To achieve a suﬃciently large photovoltage at high eﬃciency, one
has to harvest both low and high energy photons with minimal loss of photon
energy. To ensure a suﬃciently high photovoltage, considering overpotential
losses etc., the band gap energy required should be signiﬁcantly higher than
the photovoltage needed. By matching the conduction band of one semicon-
ductor to the valence band of another, a tandem device can indeed utilize both
low and high energy photons to obtain a large photovoltage. Due to several
limiting factors, such as the photocurrent at each semiconductor being equal,
the optimum ideal eﬃciency can be found for semiconductor materials with
bandgap energy at 1,7eV and 1,1eV respectively[7]. Silicon has shown to be
a good candidate for low energy photons with a bandgap energy of 1,12eV.
The high bandgap material requires a bandgap energy of 1,7eV. A good can-
didate for the high bandgpap material has yet to be identiﬁed. In a tandem
water splitting device silicon has shown to both being able to perform as a
p-type oxygen evolution electrode as well as n-type hydrogen evolution elec-
trode. Protective ﬁlms such as TiO2[35] on cathode and NiO[23] on anode
have been utilized. This gives ﬂexibility as to which side of the device the high
bandgap material should be applied. If NiO is capable of both protecting the
perovskite as well as having good hole conducting properties, perovskite could
be the candidate for the high bandgap material. The bandgap of a Perovskite
photoabsorber (MAPbX3) can be tuned to have an energy from approx. 1.5-
2.2[27] by altering the Bromide to Iodide ratio. NiO having a high bandgap
and therefore transparent, allows it to be applied to the front side of a device
without signiﬁcant loss of intensity of light irradiated from the sun.
Perovskite stability is limited to a certain temperature. NiO as protective

layer is therefore investigated at low temperature processing conditions. This
leads to the following thesis statement
What are the characteristics of low temperature(<250oC) processed NiO ﬁlms? Do
they oﬀer an opportunity to both act as eﬃcient hole transport layer as well as a water
proof layer towards a mixed halide perovskite?
Method
The NiO is mainly deposited by reactive magnetron sputtering, but attempts
to evaporate and spin coat NiO have also been made. Evaporation would be
done in a high vacuum chamber by means of an e-beam.
A mixed halide perovskite layer is spin coated using a recipe from Zhao et
al[46]. Grazing incidence X-ray diﬀraction(GIXRD) and UV-Vis are utilised
for characterizing the crystallinity and bandgap of NiO. The oxidation state
of NiO is characterized by X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy(XPS). The data
obtained will be compared to literature values. It will further be investigated
how diﬀerent processing parameters aﬀect the electronic structure of NiO. The
Electronic structure of the NiO will be determined by UV-Vis spectroscopy,
UPS and Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy(EIS). The electronic struc-
ture of the PS will be estimated from UV-Vis spectroscopy and reference val-
ues. From the determined electronic structures, the energy level alignment of
the NiO and mixed halide perovskite will be estimated.
Alterations of the PS will be investigated after NiO deposition by XRD
and UV-Vis. The NiO ﬁlm deposited on the PS will be tested for its ability
to protect the PS from water. The quality of the NiO ﬁlm is determined by a
KOH etch of the ﬁlm deposited on a silicon substrate. Scanning Electron Mi-
croscopy(SEM) images of the surface after etching are taken to reveal pinholes
as the silicon substrate is assumed to be reached through pinholes. The same
method has been used previously to reveal pinholes on a TiO2 ﬁlm.[4]

Background
1.1
Perovskite Photoabsorber
MAPbI3 deteriorates quickly in humid conditions[13]. Substituting 20% io-
dide with bromide in MAPbI3 has shown to improve stability against humidity
relative to when no bromide is present[27]. This does not mean the bromide
containing perovskite would be stable in water. The improved stability would
also be at cost of a slight drop in eﬃciency[27].
When considering deposition of NiO on perovskite it is good to know the
temperature stability of the perovskite. A mixed halide perovskite was prepared
similar to the procedure performed by Zhao et al[46]. They showed that a
1:1:1 mixture of PbI2:MACl:MABr, had the highest eﬃciency as well as the
perovskite ﬁlm being the most compact over other ratios of the composition.
MACl was estimated to act as a sort of glue during deposition, but basically
being evaporated from the crystal structure during annealing thereby resulting
in a more compact ﬁlm, leading to the formation of a MAPbI2Br perovskite.
Thermogravimetric analysis would show a loss of mass initiated at 125oC with
approximately 76% mass remaining when reaching 300oC in 30min. The lost
mass would be due to chloride and bromide sublimation. Further showing that
at 125oC the loss of mass would only be due to chloride sublimation. At which
temperature Bromide might sublime is less obvious as the MACl have an onset
of 185oC and MAI 234oC.[14]
The MABr onset is estimated to be approx 50oC higher than that of MACl
from the graphic results of Zhao et al[46]. They suggest that the sublimation of
the halide atoms at low temperatures, would be due to a diﬀerent mechanism
than the sublimation of the MAX organic precursors.
This suggests that even though mass loss occurs of the perovskite ﬁlm at low
temperatures this might just be due to chloride evaporation and not necessarily
a representation of the thermal stability of the perovskite ﬁlm not containing
chloride.

The stable forms of nickel can be determined from pourbaix diagrams. The
basis of the pourbaix diagram is a measure of the equilibium phases of Ni-
species in aqueous solutions at varying pH and potential values. The pourbaix
diagrams in Figure 2, are calculated at 25oC and 200oC. The diagrams show
that β-Ni(OH)2 is the more stable form at temperatures T≤200oC in the range
of weak alkaline to strongly alkaline solutions at the stability range of water.
β refers to the unhydrated Ni(OH)2. At T≥200oC the bunsenite(NiO) is the
more stable form. In highly alkaline solution the oxidation of the alpha and
beta phase leads to oxyhydroxides (NiOOH) where the stochiometry is not
well deﬁned.[5]
Some models propose that it is not simply one phase of NiO which is stable
at a speciﬁc pH and potential. Suggestions have been made that an inner layer
of NiO and an outer layer of the hydroxide would be the stable form.[5] The
relative magnitude of the inner layer depending on potential of electrode. An
Figure 3: Nickel phase passivation range[32]
older pourbaix diagram indeed suggests a passivating oxide or hydroxide layer
in alkaline solutions, at the oxygen evolution potential, as seen in Figure 3. In
practice the kinetics towards equilibrium are slow, the dissolution rate of NiO
at pH = 1 and 60oC being 1 monolayer a month.[5]
Regardless of which equilibrium NiO/Ni-hydroxide composition to be ex-
pected, the slow kinetics of phase transformation and dissolution rate leads to
an expectation of NiO as being suﬃciently stable to protect a perovskite layer
from humidity. The energy level alignment will depend on the electronic
structure of the Nickel phases, and so knowledge of the equilibrium structure
is important.
Electronic properties
In theory NiO should correspond to a conductor, due to its unﬁlled 3d-band.
In practice stoichiometric NiO is an insulator with a conductivity of σ25oC
= 10−14(Ωcm)−1.[33] The conductivity can be improved signiﬁcantly by the
12

presence of Ni3+, due to Ni deﬁciencies, thereby turning NiO into a semi-
conductor. Polycrystalline NiO would also lead to higher conductivity due to
grain boundary behaviour.[24] Higher crytallinity of NiO ﬁlms, might there-
fore not necessarily be an advantage.

EHeI = 21.2eV , EHeII = 40, 8eV . The kinetic energy at the sample is given as
EkinS = hν −Eb −φS
(29)
where φS is the sample work function and Eb, the binding energy required
to lift an electron from its bound state to the fermi level. The detector fermi
level is initially equal to that of sample as both are grounded, as such the kinetic
energy at the detector (EkinD) would be given by.
EkinD = hν −Eb −φD ⇔Eb = hν −φD −EkinD
(30)
42
==PAGE:44==
where φD is the work function of the detector.
In case φD < φS It is noted that the maximum binding energy to be mea-
sured at detector is limited by the value of φS. On the other hand if φD > φS,
the maximum binding energy would be limited by φD and information would
be lost on position of φS. To avoid this situation, the fermi level of detector is
shifted by a speciﬁc potential Uapp, so equation 30 becomes
EkinD = Uapp + hν −Eb −φD
(31)
Calibration: Uapp is generated from batteries, the value depends on the temper-
ature of room, so one should do a calibration at every measurement, to obtain
comparable data.
As the sample is grounded the sample fermi level is given by EfS = 0. Gold
being a metal has no band gap, it is therefore assumed, that the valence band
of gold is positioned at its fermi level Ev = EfS).
Now the maximum kinetic energy to be detected is from electrons released
from the sample valence band. In case of gold, being released from the fermi
level. Equation 31 then reduces to
EkinDmax = hν + (Uapp −φD) ⇔(Uapp −φD) = EkinDmax −hν
(32)
In the XPS and UPS data treated in the results section the kinetic energy (Ekin)
has been corrected for (Uapp −φD) obtained from a calibration.
Ekin = EkinD −(Uapp −φD) ⇔Ekin = hν −Eb
(33)
Gold calibrations were not made prior to each measurement, so variations due
to temperature are not accounted for.

Quality and Stability of NiO ﬁlm
Some of the sputtered NiO ﬁlms from Table 7 were tested in terms of their
protective abilities. As a simple water drop test in section 8.4, would not be
able to explain why a NiO ﬁlm would not be water impermeable. Scanning

holes would be hard to identify by SEM, in case these were very small. Silicon
etches anisotropically in KOH, the etched structures are easy to identify as
they are pyramid shaped. NiO was therefore deposited on a silicon substrate.
In case pinholes were present, the KOH solution would be able to etch the

was then etched in a KOH solution. Assuming that NiO deposited on a silicon
substrate to a certain degree could represent the NiO deposited on a perovskite
substrate.

9
Uncertainty
The perovskite precursor solution would age after a while, producing lighter
ﬁlms with time. Only two perovskite solutions were made during the time span
of the project, so consistency in the fabricated perovskite ﬁlms can not be ex-
pected. As most fabrication methods were conducted in air, except sputtering,
particles could settle on the ﬁlms, not only those occassionally being observed
with the naked eye, but as well those not visible. Cleaning procedures were
expected to aﬀect outcome of results, as these were not fully optimized during
the time span of the project.
10
Discussion
Low temperature deposited polycrystalline ﬁlms of NiO were possible to fab-
ricate by sputter deposition as well as evaporation. The varying deposition
parameters would lead to similar values of the crystal size being 22nm, which
is a slightly larger grain size than those obtained by sputtering using a NiO
target in a previous study[12]. Regardless of any uncertainties in the measure-
ments, it is worth noting that the work function determined by Mott Schottky
plots is that of the bulk electrode, whereas that of UPS measurements corre-
sponds to the NiO surface. XRD measurements did not reveal any hydroxide
species, even after a 40nm NiO sample was exposed to air for 5 months. XPS
measurements conﬁrmed the surface to be NiO as well. Suggesting that the
fabricated NiO is rather stable in air. The electronic structure of NiO obtained
by UPS diﬀered signiﬁcantly from the results obtained by EIS Mott Schottky
plots. Signiﬁcant uncertainties were present for both the EIS measurements as
well as UPS, not to mention the ﬁlms are measured in signiﬁcantly diﬀerent
environments. nevertheless it was possible to conﬁrm that NiO is a p-type ma-
terial. The energy level alignment of NiO to perovskite showed a valence band
position in the range of the perovskite valence band. The values from Mott
Schottky estimated at -4.35eV and from UPS -6.01eV. the experimental pro-
cedures should be optimized though, to rule out signiﬁcant errors. A Reference
study show that the work function of NiO changes when it becomes partly
hydroxilated. The work functions of partly hydroxilated NiO(NiO/Ni(OH)2)
ﬁlms were determined at 4.8eV[45] and 5.5eV[19]. While that of pure NiO
would be at 6.43eV[19]. The latter would indicate that the valence band po-
sition of NiO is too low to function as hole extraction layer. While that of
the hydroxide might align better. Oxygen deﬃciencies could aﬀect the work
function as well. Leading to a bunch of parameters which can manipulate the
workfunction of the p-type semiconductor.
64
==PAGE:66==
The NiO ﬁlms would seem stable in alkaline solution. Where degradation
could be attributed pinholes and other nonuniform contributions to the ﬁlm.
These irregularities could not bee seen on as deposited ﬁlms, it was only when
immersed in a 4M KOH aqueous solution, that the irregularities would be re-
vealed. In some cases it was possible to deposit NiO polycrystalline ﬁlms on
perovskite with an Al2O3 layer without signiﬁcant degradation as estimated
by UVVis absorption and XRD measurements. Vaccum annealing the NiO
deposited ﬁlms in various conditions from 150oC - 300oC would in some cases
leave the perovskite ﬁlms slightly aﬀected and in other cases totally damaged
with no perovskite crystal peak identiﬁed. It must be noted though that to-
tal damage was observed for an aged perovskite ﬁlm annealed for 1.5 hrs and
a perovskite ﬁlm annealed at 300oC. These observations are consistent with
previous thermogravimetric studies, where the mass loss of ﬁlms would occur
somewhere in this temperature range[46].
11
Conclusion
Low temperature deposited NiO ﬁlms were possible to produce by sputter de-
position on perovskite ﬁlms. The NiO ﬁlms were not water impermeable due
to pinholes in the ﬁlm. The complete electronic structure of the low tempera-
ture processed NiO was not possible to determine exactly, due to uncertainties,
nevertheless it could be determined by UPS and Mott Schottky measurements,
that it is most likely a hole conductor with the fermi level close to the valence
band. It has a bandgap similar to that of crystalline NiO. An energy level
alignment of the NiO to a mixed halide perovskite, would show a reasonable
position in the valence band position of the NiO towards that of the perovskite,
and as such it is a promising candidate for being a protective, transparent and
hole extracting layer on a mixed halide perovskite.
12
Perspective
Optimizing processing conditions and avoiding irregularities, could lead to
producing pinhole free NiO ﬁlms, which could oﬀer the necessary protection
against humidity. Determining the electronic structure of low temperature
sputtered or evaporated NiO requires optimization of Mott Schottky model,
as well as excluding the conductive Sip substrate as being probed during mea-
surements.
