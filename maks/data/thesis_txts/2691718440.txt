Abstract
Fuel injection is an important part of engine development as it has a large im-
pact on the combustion process and thereby the overall efficiency of the engine.
Modeling fuel injection accurately using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) is
computationally expensive as the injection is very turbulent and the interaction
between the liquid fuel and ambient gas plays an important role.
A study of the primary atomization of the Engine Combustion Network (ECN)
spray D injector was carried out using the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian Transition
model, which is a feature added in verison 2020.3 of the commercial CFD software
STAR-CCM+. The model allows detached Volume of Fluid (VOF) blobs to be
transitioned to Lagrangian particles which reduces the mesh requirements and the
computational cost.
Using the ECN standard cold conditions, measured spray
quantities were compared with experimental data and different numerical models
used on a similar case. Mesh, turbulence model and transition length were chosen
as model parameters.
Results of the model validation showed poor correspondence with the compared
data, but that the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian Transition model was able to locate
VOF blobs and transition them to Lagrangian particles successfully.
Adaptive
Mesh Refinement (AMR) with the Free-Surface Mesh Refinement (FSMR) crite-
ria was successfully implemented to track and refine the cells along the interface
between the liquid fuel and ambient gas. The K-Epsilon, K-omega and LES tur-
bulence models were compared and it was noted that the number of transitioned
particles reduced by more than half when LES was used. This was deemed partly
due to the mesh which was not sufficiently refined rather than numerical inac-
curacies in the turbulence models. Increasing the transition length resulted in a
large increase in transition due to more VOF blobs falling within the specified size
requirement. The particle size distribution was off when compared to the data de-
spite increasing the transition length. This was deemed due to the poorly resolved
VOF phase which results in transition based on poorly resolved physics.
The overall conclusion was, that in order to accurately model primary atomization
of fuel spray, even with the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian Transition model which
reduces the required mesh refinement level, the mesh must be able to properly
resolve the VOF phase down to at least the size of the specified transition length.
The impact of changing the turbulence model and transition length can then be
determined.

Introduction
Since its invention over 100 years ago, the Internal Combustion Engine (ICE) has
been a vital and widely used method of converting the dense chemical energy in
liquid fuels to mechanical energy used for transportation and generation of elec-
tricity. While the basic principles have stayed the same throughout this period,
the development of the ICE has led to large improvements in reliability and effi-
ciency. Due to the fast advancement of technology, engineers now look into every
small detail of the engine development to optimize the process.
One area that has seen large improvements due to new software capable of doing
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), is fuel injection and combustion. These
processes are often characterized by high velocities and being highly turbulent,
which makes it difficult to observe the mechanisms that govern their behavior.
CFD simulations has made it possible to approximate the behavior of these pro-
cesses by using numerical modeling of flows and reactions.
Simcenter STAR-CCM+ is a commercial Computational Fluid Dynamics based
simulation software developed by Siemens Digital Industries Software [1]. It is
continuously updated to include the latest features in numerical modeling. The
release of version 2020.3 in October 2020 saw the addition of several new features.
One of these is the Volume of Fluid (VOF) to Lagrangian (LMP) resolved transi-
tion. This feature allows droplet formation in a VOF simulation to be tracked as
Lagrangian particles. This reduces the overall computational expense compared
to fully resolving the flow using VOF [2, p. 50].
There are several possible uses for this modeling technique, one is the primary
atomization of a fuel injection. Primary atomization is the initial removal of liq-
uid mass from the surface that happens when the fuel is injected at high pressure
into the combustion chamber. By using the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian transition
model, the small liquid ligaments that would require a very dense mesh to re-
solve in the VOF phase can be transitioned to Lagrangian particles, reducing the
computational cost.
1.1
Aim of study
In this study the capabilities of the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian transition model
will be investigated by using it to model primary atomization of a fuel injection
spray. Different parameters in the numerical model will be varied to determine
their effect on the simulation. The results will be compared with experimental data
and data acquired from literature where different numerical models have been used
on a similar case.

2.1
Physics of liquid spray breakup
Understanding the physics behind the breakup of a liquid jet to a fully atomized
spray is a vital component in optimizing the combustion process. It is equally
important in the implementation of spray modeling in CFD to understand the
properties that govern the behavior of the atomization process.
Atomization process usually consists of the initial removal of liquid mass from
the surface to form large liquid drops and the subsequent breakup of these drops
into tiny droplets.
These phenomena are, respectively, known as primary and
secondary atomization [3]. A series of dimensionless numbers are used to describe
fluid properties that are important in the atomization process.
The Weber number describes the ratio between the inertial forces and the surface
tension force acting on a liquid structure.

When a liquid jet emerges in a still atmosphere, it is subjected to forces that
cause instabilities in its surface. Eventually these instabilities are the cause of the
breakup of the jet surface into droplets. One of the phenomena that can instigate
breakup is Plateau-Rayleigh instability [4].

This internal pressure variation causes a growth of the displacement amplitude
which eventually initiates droplet formation. The droplets form when the pinched
areas rupture and the bulged areas transform into spherical droplets.
Another known type of instability between two fluid flows is the Kelvin–Helmholtz
instability. Consider two parallel streams of inviscid, incompressible and immis-
cible fluids positioned one above the other and assume that the upper and lower
streams are traveling at different velocities. The horizontal boundary represents a
sharp difference in velocity of the fluids. It is a shearing layer and can be modelled
as a vortex sheet. Any perturbation in the vortex sheet will, as with the Plateau-
Rayleigh instability, cause pressure differences along the interface which increases
the amplitude of the oscillation. The sheet will eventually start being carried by
the fluid streams which causes the interface to roll over in the vorticity direction
of the mixing layer illustrated in Fig. 2.2 [7].

When it comes to the specific breakup behavior of liquid fuel sprays in diesel
engine conditions, general theory is not as abundant. In real engine operation, the
diesel fuel injection velocity is high and the understanding of surface instability
and liquid atomization is still limited in the literature [8].
To better understand each phenomena that impacts the primary atomization pro-

cess, they can be divided into two categories: nozzle effects and gas-liquid interface
interactions. In Shinjo and Umemura (2010, 2011, 2011) several extremely high-
resolution grid simulations have been analysed to try and identify the gas-liquid
interface interactions that cause the primary atomization. The total number of
grid points used in their study ranges from 400M to 6.0B, showcasing the immense
computational requirements if primary atomization is to be fully resolved [8][9][10].
Figure 2.3: High resolution simulation of primary atomization from Shinjo and

One of their conclusions based on the viscous analysis and the numerical results
show that the dominant mechanism could be the Tollmien–Schlichting (TS) in-
stability, which is similar to the wall boundary layer flows. Other factors are the
effects of jet head wake and the strong interaction between the jet head and the
core surface. The jet head is the initial fuel that enters the chamber during an
injection and creates an umbrella-like shape. From the jet head, ligament breakup
occurs and disturbances are fed into the nearby gas which amplifies the atomiza-
tion of the jet head. Ligaments are also produced from the disturbed liquid core
surface. It is recognized that these conclusions are based on analysis which neglect
the effect of nozzle turbulence.
2.2
Governing equations
In CFD analysis, a range of physical phenomena are modeled using mathematical
models that are derived from fundamental laws that express conservation princi-
ples. By analyzing the flow on a macroscopic scale the discrete structure of matter
can be neglected and materials can be modeled as a continua.
The laws that govern the physical behavior of fluids and solids are the conservation
of mass, linear momentum, angular momentum, and energy [2, p. 7655]. There are
two different approaches to expressing the conservation laws. In the Lagrangian
approach each parcel is monitored as it moves through the flow domain. In the
Eulerian approach the fluid properties are observed at different points in space.
The conservation laws are expressed as equations and discretized to make them
usable in numerical simulations.

Surface tension
Surface tension is a force acting on the interface separating two fluids. In the VOF
method, an interface is not given by a sharp surface but instead by cells with a
volume fraction value between 0 to 1. The surface force is therefore modeled as a
volumetric force using the Continuum Surface Force (CSF) approach. This model
uses the smooth field of the phase volume fraction αi to calculate a vector normal
to the interface.
High-Resolution Interface Capturing
It is important to maintain a sharp interface between the two immiscible phases.
The High-Resolution Interface Capturing (HRIC) scheme is designed to mimic the
convective transport of immiscible fluid components, resulting in a scheme that is
suited for tracking sharp interfaces.
The HRIC scheme that is used in VOF simulations requires that the CFL number
is kept below 0.5 in order to preserve a sharp interface between the fluid phases.
If the Courant number is between 0.5 and 1, a controlled blending of HRIC and
First Order Upwind (FOU) schemes are used to add stability and robustness. The
blending is introduced in the case when a large time variation of the free surface
shape is present, and the time-step is too large to resolve details of it.
If the
Courant number exceeds 1 the FOU scheme is used. These values can be changed
but are the default values used in STAR-CCM+ [2, p. 8137].
2.3.2
Lagrangian multiphase (LMP)
The Lagrangian multiphase flow model is designed to simulate and track the flow
path of dispersed particles in a continuous phase. It utilizes a framework based
on the Lagrangian-Eulerian approach where the conservation equations of mass,

momentum, and energy for the dispersed phase are written for each individual
particle in Lagrangian form. The Eulerian form is used for the governing equations
of the continuous phase and they are modified to take into account the presence
of the dispersed phase.
The dispersed particles are defined as material particles whose mass and volume
are governed by the Lagrangian conservation laws of mass, momentum, and energy.
The change in momentum is balanced by surface and body forces that act on the
particle. The equation of conservation of (linear) momentum for a material particle

Two-way coupling
The dispersed phase and the continuous phase must be able to interact. This is
done via either one-way coupling or two-way coupling. The coupling refers to the
way that momentum, heat, and mass are exchanged between the phases. Using
one-way coupling means that only the continuous phase influences the dispersed
phase, whereas two-way coupling allows effects of the dispersed phase such as
displacement, inter-phase momentum, mass and heat transfer to influence the
continuous phase.
The displacement of the continuous phase by the dispersed phase is accounted for
through the volume fraction. The volume fraction of a Lagrangian phase is the
fraction of the local cell volume which that phase occupies.
8
==PAGE:21==
The Lagrangian dispersed phase equations, when integrated over a cell, yield the
changes in the momentum, mass, and energy of each particle between its entry and
exit. The sum of these changes for all particles crossing the volume provides the net
momentum, mass, and energy that is exchanged with the continuous phase. These
net exchanges enter the continuous phase equations as source terms [2, p. 8232].
2.4
Resolved VOF-Lagrangian Transition
Resolved VOF-Lagrangian Transition is a phase interaction model working be-
tween a VOF phase and a Lagrangian phase. It is a hybrid multiphase approach
that detects VOF droplets, also called VOF blobs, and transitions them into La-
grangian droplets based on a specified blob size criteria. The idea is to provide
accurate simulations by using the accuracy of VOF in areas of importance and the
less computationally expensive LMP model to track droplets.
When a VOF blob of the user-specified size is detected, all of the VOF phase that is
contained in the cell, or cluster of cells, is converted to a single Lagrangian droplet.
If the VOF volume fraction before the transition is close to 1, the Lagrangian
droplet has approximately the same size and velocity as the parent VOF blob cell,
as shown in Fig. 2.5 [2, p. 5192].
Figure 2.5: Transition of VOF blob to Lagrangian droplet
The Resolved VOF-Lagrangian Transition model uses the same mechanism that
the Blob Detection model uses to identify isolated VOF droplets within the VOF
field, using a fixed VOF threshold Value of 0.05. The blob detection model is used
to identify grid-resolved liquid droplets or gas bubbles in the VOF phase. Cells in
which the volume fraction of a certain phase is equal to or greater than a specified
value are identified as VOF blobs. A VOF blob can consist of many cells. It is
not required to activate the Blob Detection model in the VOF phase to use the
transition model.
2.5
Turbulence modeling
In the event of a flow with a high Reynolds number (Re>4000), as described in
section 2.1, the flow will be considered turbulent. Turbulent flow is characterized
9
==PAGE:22==
by disorderly and rapid fluctuations of swirling regions of fluid, called eddies.
The swirling eddies transport mass, momentum and energy much more rapidly
than molecular diffusion. As a result of this chaotic behavior, turbulent flow is
associated with much higher values of friction, heat transfer and mass transfer
coefficients [13, p. 362].
In CFD analysis, if the turbulence is to be resolved without the use of a turbu-
lence model, the grid must be able to resolve all the turbulent length scales using
the Navier-Stokes equations. This method is known as Direct Numerical Simula-
tion (DNS) and requires a large computational effort, especially as the Reynolds
number increases. It is therefore rarely feasible and turbulence models need to be
implemented to evaluate the effect of turbulence in the flow.
2.5.1

2.5.4
LES
An alternative to RANS turbulence modeling, is the use of the Large Eddy
Simulation (LES) turbulence model. In this model, the large scales of the tur-
bulence are resolved in the flow domain and the small-scale motions are modeled.
This means that the potential error caused by assumptions in the turbulence mod-
els are reduced. LES, while less computationally expensive than DNS, requires a
higher mesh resolution, especially in highly turbulent flows, compared to RANS
models in order to resolve the large scale turbulence. Subgrid models are applied
to approximate impact of the subgrid structures on the flow field. LES simulations
in this study adopt the Dynamic Smagorinsky subgrid scale model.
2.5.5
Wall treatment
Walls are a source of vorticity, it is therefore important to accurately predict the
flow across the wall boundary layer. In a laminar boundary layer the streamwise
velocity changes uniformly away from the wall. In a turbulent boundary layer the
streamwise velocity is characterized by unsteady swirling flows inside the boundary
layer as shown in Fig. 2.7.

Segregated Flow Solver
The Segregated Flow Solver is required when using VOF multi-phase modeling in
STAR-CCM+. It solves the conservation equations of mass and momentum in
a sequential manner. A predictor-corrector approach is applied to provide a link
between the momentum and continuity equations. The segregated solver has its
roots in constant-density flows. Although it can handle mildly compressible flows
it is not suitable for shock-capturing [2, p. 7733].

Literature review
A literature review of the paper: ”Experimental and Computational Investigation
of Subcritical Near-Nozzle Spray Structure and Primary Atomization in the Engine
Combustion Network Spray D” by M. Battistoni et al. [19] is included, as it reviews
four different numerical models as well as an experiment conducted under similar
conditions as is intended in this project. Throughout the report the paper will be
referenced as ”Battistoni 2018”.
The simulations and experiment were conducted as a collaborative study for the
Engine Combustion Network (ECN) leading up to the 5th workshop of the ECN
in Detroit, Michigan. Due to the application of x-ray diagnostics performed by
Argonne National Laboratory, a quantification of distributions of mass, phase
interfacial area and droplet size in the near-nozzle region was made possible. Based
on this data, several modeling frameworks were assessed in their ability to capture
and explain experimentally observed spray details from 0.1 to 14 mm from the
nozzle exit.
The modeling frameworks range from computationally efficient ”engineering-level”
simulations to high-fidelity simulations capable of providing detailed insight into
the experimental trends. The simulated conditions used were the standard cold
condition for the ECN spray D injector as shown in Table 3.1.
The spray D
injector is a single-orifice diesel injector with a nominal diameter of 180 μm and
a geometric K-factor of 1.5. It is designed to not experience cavitation under the
given conditions. Specifically injector nozzle #209133 was used. The fuel used
was n-dodecane (C12H26).

Primary and secondary spray breakups were modeled with the Kelvin-Helmholtz-

Eulerian Σ-Y
The second model is an Eulerian homogeneous unsteady multiphase model. It is
similar to the one developed by Vallet et al. [20]. The three main differences are
the following: the pressure equation corrected by García-Oliver et al. [21] is used
instead of the equation of state of Vallet et al. The new model is compressible, so
the energy equation is needed and liquid density and compressibility are computed
through correlations to experimental data. The liquid spreading into the ambient
gas is modeled with a turbulent diffusion flux given by Fick’s law. The standard
K-Epsilon model is used.
Since a RANS approach is used for the turbulence,
and the Spray D geometry is almost axisymmetric, a 2D axisymmetric domain is
selected in order to save computational time.
The computational mesh includes information for a portion of the injector, based
on nominal geometry specifications, as well as the discharge chamber. For the
discharge chamber, dimensions of 12 mm × 6 mm are selected to model a free fuel
jet where wall effects are insignificant within the time scales of interest. After per-
forming a grid sensitivity analysis, the mesh cell count is 54,423, with a minimum
cell size of 1 μm located at the orifice outlet and a maximum cell size of 450 μm.
VOF-LES
The third method is an Eulerian method, which enforces and maintains a sharp
interface between the two phases to model the fuel injection and spray evolution.
With this approach, liquid and gas phases are regarded as immiscible. The for-
mulation is based on the VOF methodology. In addition to mass and momentum,
the VOF solution method directly transports by convection the volume fraction of
one of the two phases, without any diffusion or source term. This constraint allows
the method to maintain sharp phase interfaces. In this work, the energy equation
is not solved and only two incompressible fluids, fuel and air, are considered. The
effect of surface tension is included in the momentum equation with the continuum
surface force model.
The phase interface is reconstructed with a piecewise linear interface calculation
(PLIC) method. Fluid densities and viscosities are constant in the pure phases,
while across the interface a linear average based on the local volume fraction is
adopted. Surface tension is also assumed to be constant. To account for non-
resolved subgrid-scale (sgs) fluctuations in the pure phases, the dynamic structure
LES turbulence model is included. Wall functions are adopted, as the very high
Reynolds number rules out any possibility of directly resolving the boundary layer
on the orifice walls.
A minimum grid resolution of 2.5 μm is obtained through the application of AMR
16
==PAGE:29==
based on volume fraction and velocity gradients, which extends far downstream.
This approach allows the number of cells to be limited to 60 million while captur-
ing the phase interface with sufficient accuracy. The timestep is controlled by a
convective CFL of 0.2.
CLSVOF
In the fourth and final method, the surface that separates the liquid fuel from
the gas is resolved with a time-accurate front-capturing technique. The numerical
methodology employed in this work assumes that the liquid surface is always well
defined and that it separates two immiscible fluids. The CLSVOF code is a mul-
tiphase compressible solver for the Navier-Stokes equations, which are solved with
the mass-, momentum-, and energy-conserving advection algorithm described by
Jemison et al. [22]. Because no model is imposed to represent turbulence in the
CLSVOF framework, fine grid resolution is employed to directly resolve the fluid
motion, akin to Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS).
Dynamic AMR is used to concentrate the computational resources at interfaces.
The computational mesh is a box of length 1.44 cm in the axial direction and 0.36
× 0.36 cm in the transverse direction with a minimum grid resolution of 1.7 μm.

4.2.2
Geometry modifications
As shown in Fig. 4.1, the geometry of Spray D as imported directly from the STL
file provided by ECN, includes a chamber which the nozzle is attached to. The back
of the chamber will be used as a pressure outlet to allow the flow to exit the domain
without causing upstream interference. In a multiphase flow simulation, the Mach
number determines whether the ambient pressure is used as a boundary condition
(cell-wise). This can cause the simulation to become unstable if the velocity at the
pressure outlet is trans-sonic [2, p. 2993]. To circumvent this potential issue, the
chamber is elongated to ensure that the flow will have decelerated and be sub-sonic
once it reaches the outlet. The updated geometry is shown in Fig. 4.2 along with
the boundary conditions.

Boundary conditions
The flow in the nozzle is driven by the pressure difference between the ambient
conditions in the chamber and the injection pressure from the inlet. Pressure outlet
boundaries are used as both the inlet and outlets. The walls of the chamber are also

pressure creating an ”infinitely” large chamber where the walls of the chamber do
not affect the spray.

4.5
Meshing strategy
In section 2.3.1 it was described how the VOF method requires the mesh to be able
to resolve the position and shape of the interface between the phases. In order to
resolve the details of the atomization, a fine mesh is required, resulting in a large
cell count and thereby significant computational costs.
A method used to achieve high resolution results while limiting the total number of
cells, is Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR). AMR refines or coarsens cells dynam-
ically based on criteria and will automatically interpolate the solutions quantities
to the adapted mesh [2, p. 3134].
Two mesh approaches will be used. One using AMR with the Free Surface Mesh
Refinement (FSMR) criteria, described in section 4.5.2, and another with basic
volume controls to refine the mesh in areas of importance. This is to determine if
the FSMR causes a loss of information when using the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian
transition model.
Common for both approaches are the following mesh models used to generate the
mesh:
• Surface remesh model
• Polyhedral cells
• Prism layer cells

==PAGE:39==
The prism layer cells are there to ensure that the viscous boundary layer can be
resolved properly with use of wall functions as described in section 2.5.5. Fig. 4.4
shows an example of a cross-section of the mesh in the nozzle. The polyhedral
cells are in the center surrounded by the prism layers. The size of the polyhedral
cells looks uneven due to the plane section, which is used to visualise the mesh,
cuts through them at an arbitrary position in the nozzle.

4.5.1
Basic mesh refinement
In the basic mesh approach, volume controls are used to refine the mesh. From
the inlet to the orifice of the nozzle, a volume control is added that dictates the
base size in that region. In the spray region a cone-shaped volume is added to
refine the mesh as the liquid enters the chamber. A fairly coarse mesh is used in
the remaining part of the domain to keep the cell count as low as possible. A mesh
with a minimum cell size of 15 μm is shown in Fig. 4.5.
27
==PAGE:40==
4.5.2
Free Surface Mesh Refinement
FSMR is an AMR criteria designed for VOF multiphase simulations. This method
refines or coarsens cells in the volume mesh to improve the resolution of the inter-
face between phases. A cell is refined to as many child cells as its vertices. The
algorithm attempts to identify cells downstream of the current interface position
and mark them for refinement. This is to avoid refining cells that already contain
the sharp interface which could disrupt it [2, p. 8139].
Because FSMR only seeks to refine at the interface, the cells within the liquid core
are not refined to the same degree, which could result in a less resolved atomization.
It is therefore generally important that the base mesh before the AMR refines is
not too coarse as accuracy can be lost. Volumetric refinement is therefore still
used in this setup, though the cell size will remain larger than in the basic mesh
refinement without AMR. Fig. 4.6 shows a mesh where the AMR has tracked the
liquid fuel as it enters the chamber.
Figure 4.6: AMR tracks the interface as the spray develops
The prism layers are not refined by the AMR as wall functions are used to resolve
the boundary layer and there is no need to refine the mesh along the wall further.
Three controls are used to adjust the FSMR:
• Minimum cell size - Cells will generally not be refined to less than this value
• Refinement level - the number of times a cell can be refined
• Update frequency - The frequency at which the AMR is triggered - Default
is every 5 timesteps

Discretization scheme
4.8.1
Time
For time discretization, the implicit unsteady scheme was used. The timestep was
dynamically adjusted using adaptive time-stepping based on Free Surface CFL
Condition to satisfy the requirements of the HRIC scheme used by the VOF model.
The 1st-order implicit unsteady scheme is used as the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian
Transition model is not compatible with the second order time discretization in
the Implicit Unsteady solver
4.8.2
Convective term
As described in section 2.3.1, the HRIC scheme is used as the convention term
discretization scheme for the VOF model to maintain a sharp interface between
the liquid and gas phase. A minimum timestep is implemented to prevent a few
bad cells with high CFL values from slowing the simulation down unnecessarily.
The momentum and Realizable K-Epsilon model uses a 2nd-order Upwind dis-
cretization scheme for the convective term.

6
Results
The results of modeling primary atomization of ECN spray D, using the Resolved
VOF-Lagrangian Transition model, are divided into several sections based on the
modeling parameters mentioned in section 5.5. The first is mesh resolution. Using
the VOF method requires the mesh to be able to capture the interface deformation.
Primary atomization is a process involving high Weber and Reynolds numbers and
capturing the interface in detail will therefore require high mesh detail as will be
shown.
The second parameter is the turbulence model. It was decided to use the Realizable
K-epsilon turbulence model as the default based on its known capabilities. For
comparison, and to ensure that the turbulence is not poorly captured due to this
choice, simulations are run using the K-Epsilon but also K-Omega SST and LES
turbulence models on otherwise similar setups.
The transition length dictates the required size of a VOF blob before it is transi-
tioned to a Lagrangian particle. By increasing this, more particles will be created,
changing the SMD profile and possibly the VOF phase.

with the advantage of a reduced cell count. The focus is on the VOF phase and
whether detached structures are located due to the effects of primary atomization.
The detached structures are candidates for transitioning to Lagrangian particles
when their size is less than the specified transition length.

parameters are the same as with the K-Epsilon and K-Omega. A potential reason
for this is that LES requires a higher mesh resolution, especially in the near-wall
region, to resolve the turbulent structures properly. Thus using a mesh that is
appropriate for RANS turbulence models may be unsuited for LES resulting in
poorly resolved turbulence and therefore a lack of transition.

In Fig. 6.17 and 6.18, the velocity fields using each turbulence model are illustrated
as vector fields using Line Integral Convolution (LIC). Both fields are shown af-
ter the pressure ramp is completed where the velocity field has stabilized. It is
seen that there is a noticeable difference in the spread, where in the K-Omega
simulation, the momentum from the liquid is transferring to the gas phase. The
gas phase is ”dragged” along and the boundary layer increases in thickness further
downstream. The velocity of the liquid core is decelerating as a result.
When using LES, the disturbance in the gas phase caused by the liquid is re-
duced significantly. The high velocity core persists further into the domain as the
momentum is not exchanged with the surroundings at the same rate as when K-
Omega, (or K-Epsilon which shows similar behavior as K-Omega), is used. Instead
vortices are observed in the surrounding gas near the liquid spray. These vortices,
while observable, contain little energy due to their low velocity.
These differences are a result of several factors.
The first is the fact that the
numerical framework of each turbulence model is different and will therefore yield
different results. Though in optimum conditions, these differences should be minor.
Secondly, as previously discussed, it appears that LES resolves the turbulence
poorly in these conditions, likely due to the mesh requirements not being met. This
resulted in a significantly lower number of transitioned Lagrangian particles, and
because two-way coupling allows momentum to be transferred both ways between
the Eulerian and Lagrangian phase, this could explain the spread in the velocity
field when using K-Omega and K-Epsilon rather than LES in this setup. The
Lagrangian particles separate from the liquid core into the surrounding gas, thus
transferring momentum to the gas phase, amplifying the spread in velocity.

6.3
Transition length
The amount of transition from VOF to Lagrangian particles rely on several pa-
rameters such as the mesh, transition length, and as shown in section 6.2, the
turbulence model. In this section, the impact of changing the transition length
will be analysed. Based on the setup using AMR with a minimum cell size of
15 μm, simulations with transition lengths of 15 μm and 25 μm are analysed and
compared to the default simulation which uses a transition length of 2.5 μm.
To better understand the process that leads to the transition, the surface of the
VOF phase is monitored as instabilities occur. Fig. 6.19 shows a closeup of protru-
sions in the VOF surface and subsequent Lagrangian particles that have separated
from the liquid core. In this case the detached liquid blobs instantly transition
due to them being smaller than the defined transition length. It should be noted
that the size of the Lagrangian particles illustrated in the scene does not represent
their actual size.

7
Conclusion
In this study, the capabilities of the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian Transition model
have been investigated. The model was introduced in a recent version of STAR-
CCM+ as an addition to its multiphase modeling options for cases such as fuel
injection. It was therefore used to model primary atomization of fuel injection
using the ECN spray D injector.
Initial results showed that the model was implemented successfully and Lagrangian
particles were transitioned from the VOF phase. The CFL value was monitored to
ensure it remained in the sub 0.5 region in order for the HRIC scheme to maintain
a sharp interface. The mass flow was compared and validated with experimental
measurements. While transition was observed, comparison of spray quantities with
data from Battistoni 2018 showed an underprediction of spread of the liquid mass
as well as the SMD of the transitioned particles. This came down to primarily the
poorly resolved VOF phase due to a mesh that was too coarse.
AMR was implemented with the FSMR criteria which tracks and refines the inter-
face between the liquid and gas. It was able to successfully track the development
of the jet head in the initial stage of the spray event. The minimal cell size of
15 μm used in the AMR did not allow for a detailed simulation of the formation
of ligaments from the liquid core. Furthermore the mesh at the transition from
nozzle to spray zone was refined significantly more than the user-defined minimum
cell size should allow. This zone generated a lot of Lagrangian particles whose size
distribution was far off the data in Battistoni 2018. The SMD distribution along
with the trajectory of many of the particles led to the conclusion that they were
once again the product of a poorly resolved VOF phase.
Base meshes without AMR were also analysed. It was found that reducing the
cell size from 20 to 5 μm significantly improved the sharpness of the interface and
the overall development of the spray. Due to the initial computational resources
available, the fine mesh was not able to capture the features of the spray further
downstream as the cell count would have exceeded what was feasible.
The Realizable K-Epsilon turbulence model was compared to the K-Omega SST
and LES models. Results showed that the K-Epsilon and K-Omega SST mod-
els performed very similarly which was deemed due to their similar numerical
framework. LES provided a slightly different flow behavior but no noticeable im-
provements when it came to comparing PMD and TIM values with Battistoni
2018. Another noticeable difference when using the LES turbulence model was
the amount of transition to Lagrangian particles occurring. Compared to the two
RANS models, less than half as many particles were present in the simulation,
indicating a lack of disturbances being applied on the surface of the VOF phase.
The transition length was increased, increasing the total number of VOF blobs
that become candidates for transitioning to Lagrangian particles. Increasing the
transition length from 2.5 μm to 15 μm saw a major increase in transition, but with
57
==PAGE:70==
large parts of it happening far downstream where the mesh was able to resolve
the slow breakup of the jet. The SMD profiles deviated from the compared data
especially within the first 6 mm of the spray. Increasing the transition length from
15 μm to 25 μm provided similar results, showing that a poorly resolved VOF
phase cannot simply be negated by increasing the transition length.
Based on previous simulation results, it was clear that the mesh required to resolve
the VOF phase of the atomization would require more computational resources.
A setup using 28M cells was run using high performance computing resources
from PRACE. The results were a significant increase in ligament formation and
subsequently the transition to droplets as the ligaments broke up. The simulation
was not run to completion but the preliminary results strongly indicated that the
better resolved VOF phase led to Lagrangian particles with a more accurate size
distribution.
To accurately model primary atomization using the Resolved VOF-Lagrangian
transition model, the VOF phase needs to be properly resolved down to the size
at which the transition happens. AMR with the FSMR criteria can be used to
achieve this mesh resolution while managing the total cell count. After the tran-
sition, breakup models and coefficients can be adjusted to better correspond with
experimental data.
7.1
Future work
Throughout this study, the mesh requirements have played a big role in achiev-
ing accurate results due to the available computational resources. A new mesh
convergence study should be conducted where it is possible to achieve a grid inde-
pendent solution. Based on that, a new analysis of the model parameters should
be conducted to ensure their effects are found without the mesh being a factor.
The reduction in computational time when increasing the transition length is in-
teresting when measured against the loss in accuracy and should be investigated.
