  Abstract
 
Future climate goals in EU and in Denmark require reduction in the greenhouse 
gas emissions by the end of 2050. One option to reduce energy consumption and 
greenhouse gas emissions is to retrofit older buildings to be more energy efficient. 
Hillerødsholmskolen has been a case building to study how different retrofitting 
options influence on the indoor environment and the energy consumption.  
 
Multiple different retrofitting options to improve indoor climate and energy con-
sumption were used. A Pearson’s correlation coefficient was calculated to see 
the most influential parameters regarding indoor environment and energy con-
sumption. Also, a box plot was used to see the range of each option to determi-
nate the optimal solution.  
 
Windows properties had strong correlations regarding all the indicators and the 
most optimal solution regarding overheating and daylight was external solar film. 
Regarding energy consumption double low-e solar control window was found to 
be the optimal solution. The external solar shading was found to have a strong 
influence on the south but no influence on the east. The most optimal solution 
regarding external solar shading was found to be the horizontal slats.  
 
Based on the results, solar shading does not increase the energy consumption 
and therefore more focus should be put to protecting the spaces against unnec-
essary solar gains. More simulations with more options should be made, as 
based on the results, the optimal window is a combination of the properties of the 
external film and the double low-e solar control window.  
 
As a conclusion Pearson’s correlation coefficient was found to be useful as the 
correlation corresponds to the optimal solutions. The optimal solutions were fo-
cusing on the correlations that were “strong” or “very strong”. However, ClimateS-
tudio is the most useful in the conceptual design stage as the energy simulation 
is relatively simplified. Therefore, the results should be validated in a more ad-
vanced software like IDA ICE or IES.

 Introduction
 
Denmark has a goal to reduce the greenhouse gasses by 70% before the year 
2030 compared to the year 1990. Full carbon neutrality is planned to have 
achieved before the 2050 [1], [2]. Buildings have a significant influence on that as 
it has been estimated that they are responsible for around 40% of the energy 
consumption in the EU. Also, the fact that around 75% to 90% of the current 
building stock will be still standing in the 2050, it is crucial that the existing build-
ings are retrofitted to achieve the goals. [3], [4] Based on the report by EU, the 
primary energy consumption is typically decreased by 17% in non-residential 
buildings when undergone an energy renovation [4]. Energy efficient buildings 
are an important mean to achieve the goals, but it will be a challenge as the heat 
consumption has been increasing from 2013 to 2018, even though there was a 
steady reduction in the energy consumption from the mid-1980s until the 2013. 
The reason is unclear, but it is believed that changes in the user behaviour have 
impacted as people have been used to higher indoor temperatures. [1] Although 
after the recent increase in energy prices, more Danes are interested in conserv-
ing energy than before [5].  
 
Currently there are 17473 buildings for education and research purposes in Den-
mark and from those around 60% have been built before the 1980s [6]. Based on 
the estimates in the Long-Term Renovation Strategy around 20% of those build-
ings haven’t undergone any energy related renovation and only a light energy 
renovation has been done for 55% to 60% of the buildings [1]. More retrofitting 
should be done in the EU to achieve the Paris agreement as the current rate is 
insufficient. One of the best ways to reduce the greenhouse gasses is to avoid 
new constructions and prioritise the retrofitting. [2]  
 
Buildings should be well-functioning, healthy and safe spaces for the users. The 
indoor environment has to comply with several requirements that are set in the 
standards. [7] It is important to focus on the indoor environment as in the EU, 
people spend around 90% of their time indoors [8]. Regarding schools, indoor 
==PAGE:9==
 
environment also affects on the academic performance of the student so, by im-
proving the quality of the indoor environment, better space for learning is created. 
[9] In the future more challenges might be caused by the climate change as more 
extreme climates become more common. Guidance and pilot projects on how to 
retrofit school buildings regarding the future climate have been done but the focus 
has mainly been on the performance of the building during winter and less weight 
has been given to the overheating. [10] 
 1.1.
 Problem statement
 
In this study the focus is to improve the thermal comfort in the old school building 
in Hillerød Figure 1. The Hillerødsholmskolen is built in 1950s and it is naturally 
ventilated. Currently old school buildings have a risk for overheating especially 
during summer as there is little to none solar shading. This study holistically as-
sesses different parameters that are related to the indoor environment and en-
ergy consumption. The parameters are selected to be passive as the energy con-
sumption of the building should not increase after the retrofitting.

The objective of this study is to characterise the parameters that have great influ-
ence on the thermal comfort and energy consumption and to see how solar shad-
ings contribute to the indoor environment quality. Also, based on the results the 
optimal solution for the building can be identified.  1 Introduction

 2.1.
 Indoor environment
 
It is estimated that people spent 90% of their time indoors so, the indoor environ-
ment quality is more important than ever. [8] Indoor environment includes thermal 
comfort, visual comfort, air quality, and acoustics. The last two subjects are not 
relevant for this study as it focuses on how solar shading affects on thermal com-
fort, daylight and energy consumption. Improving indoor environment quality can 
be beneficial for the owner of the building and for the user. Indoor environment 
can be improved by passive or active means, and they both have pros and cons 
as seen in the Table 1. 
 
Passive means are architectural and constructive means and therefore they need 
to be designed by the architects and the engineers in co-operation. It should also 
be noted that the passive means are often dependant on the meteorological con-
ditions and therefore the indoor environment is not stable. Active means are usu-
ally easier to execute than passive means as they are more flexible, and less co-
operation is needed as a specialized engineer can design the active means. They 
also offer relatively steady indoor environment but that comes with a cost. Com-
pared to the passive means they are often expensive; require energy, and they 
have high maintenance cost as they might break down. [11]

 2.1.1
 Thermal comfort
 
High or low temperatures, thermal bridges, cold surfaces, and draft can cause 
discomfort for the occupant. Thermal comfort is an important indicator of the in-
door environment as good thermal environment improves the learning perfor-
mance [9]. Thermal comfort depends on the activity level, clothing insulation, air 
temperature, radiant temperature, air velocity and air humidity [12]. The sensation 
of thermal comfort is controlled mainly by operative temperature which is deter-
mined by the air velocity, air temperature and radiant temperature. [13] 
 
Fully controlled static indoor environment has been mainly used to create optimal 
conditions for occupants. Although it is an expensive and complex solution to 
generalize thermal comfort so, the focus has been moving more to a dynamic 
indoor environment. It means more fluctuation in temperature, in humidity and in 
ventilation as natural ventilation is preferred. Dynamic indoor environment is usu-
ally created by using passive means that are controlled by the occupants. [12] 
 
The thermal comfort is assessed based on the standard DS/EN 16798-1 [14]. 
The operative temperature in the classroom should be between 20°C to 26°C. 
Hours with a temperature under and over the threshold are calculated as discom-
fort hours. 
 2.1.2
 Visual comfort
 
Indoor spaces have target illuminance levels for the daylight provision based on 
the use of the space. The space has sufficient daylight if the target illuminance is 
achieved over 50% of the space during the half of the daylight hours. For the 
classroom the target illuminance level is 500 lux, and the minimum target illumi-
nance level is 300 lux according to DS/EN 17037:2018 [15].

 2.4.1
 Windows
 
Openings in the façade are important as they allow daylight into the internal 
space. Daylight in internal spaces is important as it has an influence on learning 
and energy [18], [19]. The openings in the façade might be a weak link in the wall 
in the older buildings as the insulation capacity of the windows is not as good as 
the rest of the wall. However, with the new low-e windows, the windows might 
become net positive regarding energy as they let in solar gains. [19] In general, 
the Uw-value for the windows is calculated as a function of the thermal transmit-
tance of the components and their geometrical characteristics [20]. In this study 
the Uw-value shown in the tables is the whole windows (Uw) thermal transmittance 
and not only the glazing.   
 
Solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC) measures how much solar energy transmits 
through the window on a scale of 0 to 1. One means that the maximum amount 
of solar heating energy transmits through the window. Zero is the opposite as 
then a minimum amount of solar heating energy transmits through the window. 
[21] 
 
“Fraction of the incident solar radiation that is totally transmitted 
through the glass” [22]. 
 
Important factor in the sufficient daylight is the visible light transmission as the 
higher the value the more sunlight passes through the window. To achieve the 
target illuminance level in the space, having a window with high Tvis value will 
help to reach that target illuminance level. The Tvis is also measured in a scale 
of 1 to 0, where the one means a maximum amount of transmittance and the zero  
means the opposite.

As a mean to lower the risk for overheating, solar film has been applied to the 
glass to improve the properties of the original windows. With the solar film the 
windows SHGC is lower which means less solar gains. It’s recommended to apply 
the solar film externally and as seen in the Table 8 the SHGC is lower when the 
film has been installed on the exterior side than on the interior side of the window. 
In case the film must be applied internally, extra care should be used as the ab-
sorption of solar radiation becomes greater and thus increases the thermal stress 
of the glass, which therefore increases the risk of breakage. Applying a solar film 
is a complex matter and it should be thought through as to what the optimal so-
lution for the building is. [23] 
 
In this study the solar film has been applied to both sides of the window to see if 
there is a difference regarding overheating, daylight, and energy consumption. It 
should be noted that not all solar films are compatible with external and internal 
installation. In this study 3M Prestige 70 was used internally and 3M Prestige 
External 70 was used externally. The films were chosen as the transmittance of 
the visible light is high and the solar heat gain coefficient is lower. By using the 2 Methodolog

During winter the solar gains are beneficial as it is counted as a “free energy”. 
Solar gains can heat up the room and might lower the energy consumption of the 
building by lowering the need for heating. On the other hand, the solar gains might 
heat up the space during summer and affect the thermal comfort of the occupant 
in a way that lowers their ability to learn or study. [13] 
 
Improving thermal comfort and energy efficiency of the building, new windows 
can be a great option. As this study focuses on improving the thermal comfort by 
limiting the overheating, new windows are selected to have a low SHGC. Both 2- 
and 3-pane windows have been selected as the properties of the windows are 
slightly different. The selected 2-pane window is Solarban 90 which is a solar 
control low-e glass. The outer glass is a solar control glass, and the internal glass 
is clear. The 3-pane window is also a Solarban 90, but the two outer glazes are 
solar control glazes and only the inner one is clear. Both windows have a low-e 
coating to improve the thermal properties. 
 
==PAGE:19==
 
“Low-E coatings have been developed to minimize the amount of ul-
traviolet and infrared light that can pass through glass without com-
promising the amount of visible light that is transmitted.” [24] 
 
During winter the low-e coating reflects the interior heat back to the inside. Radi-
ant heat loss through the glass can be thus minimized by using the low-e coating. 
The use of low-e coating improves the insulation properties of the window as seen 
in the Table 9. [24] 
 
The third new window that was chosen was a window with MicroShading. It’s a 
product that has been developed in Denmark. It is a micro-structured film that has 
been placed between a 2- or 3-pane glazing, and it provides solar shading while 
maintaining a view out as seen in the Figure 4. The windows with MicroShade 
have also a low-e coating to improve the insulation properties of the windows. 
MicroShade was chosen as it is a passive solar shading method, and it is said 
that it raises the indoor environment quality significantly compared to other solar 
shading solutions [25]. The MicroShade windows offer better solar shading during 
summer as seen in appendix A4, where the effective SHGC for the months of 
June, July and August is lower than the value for the whole year. The reason for 
this is that the SHGC depends on the incident angle of the sun. To obtain the 
SHGC of the MicroShade windows, they were constructed in the SimShade by 
MicroShade software [26]. More detailed values for the MicroShade windows can 
be seen in the appendix A4. As the MicroShade product is a new window and 
solar shading solution at the same time, no other solar shading methods were 
used at the same time.

 2.4.2
 Type of solar shading
 
It is important to design and choose the solar shading that takes account all the 
different aspects of the building. With a proper solar shading you can limit the 
unwanted heating effect from the sunlight. The ability to shield the space from the 
sunlight is important as it keeps the internal temperatures lower. [13] By limiting 
the solar gains you also limit the available daylight and thus the solar shading 
should be designed carefully. As said previously the solar gains might be benefi-
cial during winter when they might lower the need for the heating.  
==PAGE:21==
 
As the study focuses on passive and more affordable means to improve indoor 
environment the chosen solar shading is fixed. Three options have been selected 
and modelled: vertical slats, horizontal slats, and an awning. The geometry of the 
respective solar shading has been modelled in Rhino/Grasshopper environment 
and can be seen in the Figure 5, Figure 6 and Figure 7. Blendex 240 was a 
refence product for the vertical and horizontal slats. The geometry is modelled 
based on the measurements found in the technical report [29]. Fixed vertical slats 
are the most suitable for the east and west facing facades as they do not limit the 
available daylight excessively. On the other hand, the fixed horizontal slats are 
the most suitable for the facades that are facing towards South as they limit the 
solar gains extensively. [13] The values used to calculate the Pearson’s correla-

There are also problems that need to be taken account when selecting the slats. 
The reflectance of the slats is important as it effects on multiple areas. By select-
ing reflective slats, the risk for glare is increased so, dark, and non-reflective slats 
are usually preferred. Using non-reflective slats affect more on daylight than using 
reflective slats and during sunny summer days the non-reflective slats absorb the 
solar gains and create a heated volume close the wall. This causes a situation 
where opening a window doesn’t cool the space. [13] 
 
The third type of the solar shading was an awning. It was first modelled as an 
overhang as all the solar shading types were fixed. But as it was modelled in an 
angle the geometry is more like an awning. Both overhang and awning work in a 
similar way. They both offer the best protection from the sun when the sun is in a 2 Methodolog
==PAGE:22==
  
vertical plane perpendicular to the window. Usually, the end of the awning is much 
lower than the overhang and thus can protect the space from the sun more during 
winter. With an awning made from translucent material you can increase the dif-
fused light in the room, but in cases where the colour reproduction is important 
like in classrooms it is unwanted. [13] 
 
In this study both vertical and horizontal slats are used in the east and south 
oriented classrooms to see the correlation, even though it might not be neces-
sary. The spacing of the slats was 240 mm and the angel of the slats was 0° for 
both the vertical and the horizontal case. The awning was modelled with a width 
of 1000 mm and on an angle of 15°.

 2.4.5
 Weather
 
Future climate has been estimated to be warmer than today and that effects on 
how we design the buildings. Also, the moisture content in the air is expected to 
rise. In the future, due to the higher outdoor temperatures and higher moisture 
content the installations are going to be larger and consume more energy, in or-
der to comply with the indoor climate requirements. Together with the higher tem-
peratures and higher moisture content there is an increased risk of condensation 
that must be taken account of when designing new buildings or retrofitting old 
ones, especially if designing mechanical ventilation or cooling. [32] 
 
IPCC has estimated six different scenarios in their Climate Change 2007 Synthe-
sis report [33]. The scenario used in this study is AB1 and that corresponds to a 
2.8°C temperature increase by the end of this century. AB1 is a scenario where 
the economic growth is rapid worldwide and the population peak can be found in 
the mid-century. After the mid-century the Global GHG emissions are going to 
decrease. New and more efficient technologies are invented that are driving the 
energy sources more towards non-fossil. In the AB1 scenario the energy sources 
are balanced with all the energy sources. [34]

indoor environment (Table 16). The first weather file is the one that is used in

 2.5.2
 Pearsonproduct-momentcorrelation
 
Evaluating how the parameters affect to the indicators Pearson product-moment 
correlation was used. In short, the Pearson product-moment correlation is called 
Pearson’s correlation coefficient and it is the most used mean to measure how 2 Methodolog
==PAGE:32==
  
strong the relationship is between the two variables. The Pearson’s correlation 
coefficient shows if there is a linear regression between the two sets of data.[37]–
[39] 
 
In the Figure 9 is an example of Pearson’s correlation coefficient that shows what 
the correlation graphically means. It is not the slope of the line but how well the 
line for the data points can be fit. [39]  
“In simple terms, it answers the question, can I draw a line graph to 
represent the data?” [38]  
 
 Figure 9Examples 
 
The Pearson’s correlation coefficient measures how strong the relationship is be-
tween the two data sets. The coefficient can be either positive or negative and 
the strength depends on the value. The Pearson’s correlation coefficient is a 
value between -1 to 1, where the 0 indicates that the variables do not have any 
relationship. Positive association as when the value of the Pearson’s correlation 
coefficient is greater than 0, means that when the value of one variable increases, 
so does the value of the other variable. If the Pearson’s correlation coefficient is 
less than 0, it’s called negative association. In this case when the value of one 
variable increases, the value of the other variable decreases. [37]–[39] 
 
As an example, considering two variables “number of people” and “CO2 concen-
tration in the room”. It would be a strong positive correlation as more people 
causes more CO2 and therefore the CO2 concentration in the room is higher. 
 
==PAGE:33==

 Discussion
 4.1.
 Method
 
In this study the sample file was created manually so, better results could have 
been obtained by using a different method to create the sample file. For example, 
creating the sample file by a Latin hypercube method, less simulations could have 
been run to have the same results. With this approach more parameters or op-
tions could have been used and more data could have been collected. Especially 
when using the Pearson’s correlation coefficient not all possible scenarios need 
to be simulated as seen in the thesis [40]. Although by simulating all the possible 
scenarios, the most optimal scenario can be seen from the results. Regarding the 
Pearson’s correlation coefficient, it should be noted that the parameters should 
be independent. In this study the parameters used regarding the windows are not 
independent as all the properties affect each other. Lowering the Uw-value of the 
window by adding a new glass pane or by adding the low-e coating, the solar 
heat gain coefficient and the visible light transmittance are also lowered. 
 
Only a few different types of solar shadings were selected to keep the potential 
scenarios within feasible limits. Furthermore, only fixed external solar shadings 
were simulated as the focus of this study was passive and more affordable solu-
tions. In this study, only one size, one spacing and one angle were simulated. 
Regarding fixed external solar shading, more scenarios should be simulated as 
the geometry of the shading has a significant influence on the results. Also, the 
difference of fixed and controllable solar shading should be studied. Even though 
the fixed solar shadings are more affordable than the controllable solar shadings, 
the indoor environment might improve more by using the controllable solar shad-
ings. In this study no venetian blinds or curtains have been modelled, to see the 
correlation between the indicators and the parameters. In reality the daylight lev-
els in the classrooms would be lower as venetian blinds or curtains would have 
been used, so that the students are able to see the computer screens or to pre-
vent glare.  4 Discussion
==PAGE:54==
  
It is known that the natural ventilation is complicated to model as it might involve 
a lot of human behaviour. In reality occupants have manual control over windows 
and can open or close them when needed based on temperature, humidity, or air 
quality. It was a challenge to model the natural ventilation for this case. Usually, 
the ventilation is controlled based on the CO2 concentration but in this study the 
windows are controlled based on the indoor temperature as there wasn’t any con-
trols or set points for the CO2 concentration in the ClimateStudio.  
 
Even though this study did not focus on the natural ventilation, the CO2 concen-
tration in the classroom was evaluated as a mean of examining if the modelled 
natural ventilation was performing as planned (Appendix A8). It was seen that the 
CO2 concentration during summer was close to the outdoor CO2 concentration 
levels and that suggests that the windows are open almost all the time. But during 
winter the CO2 concentration increased to over 2000 ppm which is over the 
threshold that has been set in the standards. But as seen in the studies [41], [42] 
the CO2 concentration is not unusually high for a naturally ventilated classroom. 
Even though the CO2 concentration and the ACH are in a similar level as in the 
study, the natural ventilation should have been modelled based on the assump-
tion that the space is ventilated by the standards as the use of windows is un-
known. This way the model would have been a base case with enough ventilation. 
To achieve the wanted level of natural ventilation the infiltration should have been 
increased to a minimum of 2.5 h-1 instead of 0.5 h-1 that was used. It should be 
noted that by increasing the air change rate the energy consumption would in-
crease especially during winter and therefore the results for the primary energy 
consumption might be too low in this study.  
 
The hour is a discomfort hour if the temperature is under 20°C or over 26°C. The 
set point for heating has been set to 18°C during night and 22°C from start of the 
usage. Most of the low temperatures appear in the early hours of the morning. 
Even though the model has been modelled with an unlimited heating capacity the 
set point for the air temperature is not met, which is probably caused by the 
method the results are calculated. The temperatures like 19.8°C and 19.9°C are 
calculated to low temperatures as the limit has been set to 20°C. However, the 
==PAGE:55==
 
difference between the temperatures of 19.8°C and 20°C is so small that most of 
the people cannot tell the difference. To eliminate that small error in the low tem-
peratures, the limit for under 20°C should have been set to around 19.7°C as 
currently too many hours have been calculated. The energy consumption needed 
for the heating is lower as the temperature is set to be lower during night. Alt-
hough in reality the heating system might not be able to provide the needed heat-
ing capacity and therefore the heating should have been modelled to start earlier. 
By increasing the air temperature before the occupation hours, the primary en-
ergy consumption would have increased. It should also be noted that the heating 
temperature is air temperature and the calculated discomfort hours are operative 
temperature. This might have some effect on results.  
 
The real number of hours when the school is under use is 1800 as there is 200 
school days in Denmark. The energy model uses either 2346 or 8760 hours, de-
pending on the indicator. The hours were stated in the standard DS/EN 16798-
1:2019. Furthermore, the daylight hours are the 4380 hours with the most diffuse 
horizontal illuminance in the weather file. The reason, why the real number and 
the numbers used in the model differ is that the model does not have any breaks 
or holidays during the year. Also, it should be studied how much daylight is during 
summer break and if that has affects on the daylight levels for the whole year. 
Better and more accurate results could be obtained by splitting the year to smaller 
sections. By splitting the year to smaller sections, more accurate modelling could 
be used regarding for example solar shading and natural ventilation. Also, some 
of the thresholds are different regarding the time of the year, as during summer 
higher temperatures are more acceptable than during winter.  
 4.2.
 Results
 
Results are as good as the model and as mentioned the real use of the building 
might be significantly different than what has been modelled so, the results should 
be interpreted accordingly. The most interesting result was the correlation be-
tween the heating energy consumption and the solar heat gain coefficient as 
more solar gains should have been resulted a lower heating energy consumption. 4 Discussion
==PAGE:56==
  
Same result can be seen from the correlation between the heating and the type 
of solar shading. By using an external solar shading, the heating consumption 
would decrease even though it should have been increasing as less solar gains 
can heat up the space. It seems that by decreasing the solar gains, the heating 
consumption does not increase in terms of naturally ventilated buildings. This 
would be interesting as it would open more possibilities to protect the spaces from 
solar gains and help to improve the indoor environment without increasing the 
heating consumption. However, it should be noted that that the natural ventilation 
was modelled based on the indoor air temperature and not based on the CO2 
concentration which is usually used. Therefore, the results should be validated 
by using another simulation software by example IDA ICE or IES to conclude 
anything.  
 
It is a complex and difficult task to design a space with a good indoor climate and 
with enough daylight as more daylight usually corresponds to a more solar gain. 
The optimal solution is usually a compromise between the thermal comfort, the 
daylight and the energy consumption, as too extreme solutions for one are often 
bad for the other. In terms of windows the external solar film was found to be the 
optimal solution for reducing overheating in both orientations in this study. It was 
also found to be the optimal solution regarding daylight however, for energy con-
sumption, double low-e solar control window was found to be the optimal solution. 
Based on these findings the optimal solution could be the combination of the re-
spective options. New double or triple-pane window with a low Uw-value and sim-
ilar SHGC and Tvis values as the window with external solar film, would probably 
be the optimal combination regarding windows. However, by choosing a window 
with too low SHGC and Tvis, the risk for the space not to comply regarding day-
light is significant. It can be seen in the results that the selected triple low-e solar 
control window had too low SHGC and Tvis to comply with the daylight. 
 
In terms of the external solar shading the orientation of the classroom had signif-
icant impact as no external shading should be used in the east oriented class-
room. However, in the south oriented classroom the horizontal slats were found 
==PAGE:57==
 
to be optimal solution as they reduced overheating and energy consumption sig-
nificantly. It was also found that by using external solar shading no significant 
increase was seen in the energy consumption even though it reduces the solar 
gains in the space. Based on the results, regarding daylight no external solar 
shadings should be used neither of the classrooms. Compromise could be made 
by using a controllable external solar shading. As this study focuses on passive 
and affordable means no controllable options were selected, but by having a con-
trollable solar shading, the amount of daylight could be determined automatically 
to ensure the optimal indoor environment, although the solar shading should also 
be controllable by the occupant. [11] Furthermore, it should be noted that only a 
few options of external solar shading were modelled and more should be simu-
lated, especially if using fixed solar shadings as they affect all year round.  
 
The weather scenario had some influence on the results although the optimal 
solutions were the same for all different weather scenarios. The most notable 
change was found regarding the energy consumption as warmer outdoor temper-
ature corresponds to a lower need for heating in the building. In terms of retrofit-
ting the building, by using the optimal solutions regarding today’s weather the 
solution would also be great for the next decades.  4 Discussion
==PAGE:58==
  5
 Conclusion
 
In this study different parameters have been holistically assessed regarding in-
door environment and energy usage in terms of retrofitting an educational build-
ing. Designing a space with a high-quality indoor environment and a low energy 
consumption is a hard and complex task. It was found that the optimal solution is 
a compromise between the parameters as too extreme solutions in one, effect 
too drastically to the others. 
 
The Pearson’s correlation coefficient was found to be a great way to study, where 
the focus should be when retrofitting. It was seen that the optimal solutions were 
focusing on the parameters that had strong or very strong correlation between 
the parameter and the indicator. Although convergence was not achieved in all 
the parameters and more simulations should be done, it was seen that relatively 
steady state was reached. 
 
Optimal solutions were focusing on the windows as the external solar film was 
found to the most influential option in both classrooms. It was also seen that the 
double low-e solar control window was the most influential option regarding en-
ergy consumption and thus it would seem that focusing on the windows would 
benefit the building the most. In terms of external solar shading, it should be used 
only in the South oriented classroom and the optimal option was found to be hor-
izontal slats. It should be noted that the optimal solution is based on the selected 
types of external solar shading and more simulations should be done with more 
different options to validate the optimal solution. It was also found that the optimal 
solutions were similar regarding the used weather scenario so, if the retrofitting 
is done today the solution will work greatly the next decades as well. 
 
As the ClimateStudio suits best for an early-stage design, more advanced thermal 
simulations should be done next if moving forward. IDA ICE or IES should be 
used to validate the results and confirm the optimal solutions.
