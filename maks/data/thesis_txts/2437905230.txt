Abstract 
 
Reducing the crop water footprint is an urgent task for modern societies. This requires 
using water more efficiently by increasing the amount of carbon assimilated by crops 
per unit of water. Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV) carrying hyperspectral and thermal 
cameras provide spatially explicit tools to better quantify crop water use at the farm 
scale. Nonetheless, current methods for estimating crop water use could benefit from a 
better parameterization of water stress factors, currently based on meteorological 
variables or standard vegetation indices. Therefore, UAV cameras can help quantify 
better stress responses. But this requires a careful identification of spectral wavelengths 
which are most sensitive to such changes beyond classic indices such as NDVI. This 
project provided indicators of crop water stress and physiology variables from remote 
sensing cameras that can be carried by UAV. 
 
The experiment was conducted in a growth chamber with controlled environment 
located at DTU Risø Campus. We chose maize and soybean as they are widely 
cultivated across the world and they present contrasting water use strategies (isohydric 
and anisohydric). We grew them under typical conditions of Southern Europe and part 
of South America (25℃/60% RH). We applied 3 water level treatments: high (field 
capacity, FC), medium (70% FC) and low (40% FC), with 6 replicates per treatment. 
We measured (a) canopy reflectance 2m above the plants; (b) canopy 
evapotranspiration; (c) leaf ecophysiology (photosynthesis, transpiration, stomatal 
conductance and leaf temperature). Five vegetation indices of NDVI, OSAVI, PSNDc, 
PRI, WI were calculated and statistical analysis of linear regression was performed. As 
we controlled for environmental and illumination conditions in the growth chamber, 
any changes in evapotranspiration, plant physiology and remote sensing variables could 
be linked to plant water stress.   
 
It was found that the two crops of soybean (isohydric) and maize (anisohydric) had 
quite different behavior in response to water deficit situation. This was reflected by the 
plant physiology variables as well as spectral indices and leaf temperature increasing 
in the case of soybean but not of maize. Further analyzing the correlation between 
physiology variables and VIs indicated that in soybean the PRI was most sensitive to 
water stress due to the reduction of stomatal conductance, potentially affecting 
photosynthesis, with drought. Contrary to expected correlations of physiological 
==PAGE:4==
 
 
variables were not significant with leaf temperature for soybean but they were for maize 
although significant differences were found between water treatments in soybean. For 
maize, an index related with canopy water content, WI was best for detecting water 
stress, which is consistent with maize’s anisohydric behavior where stomata are open 
for lower soil moisture levels which might produce decreases in leaf water potential. 
These results highlight the need of using different indices than the classic NDVI to track 
plant water status, and of incorporating the specific hydraulic strategies of crops when 
designing a monitoring strategy to track water stress. 
 
Finally, recommendations for future experiments was suggested to get better results. As 
several error sources exists due to lack of experience for this type of experiment.

1. Introduction 
 
The world population is most likely to continue growing during the rest of 21st century 
(Cohen, 2003; Gerland et al, 2014). The United Nations’ newest version of world 
population projection using data until 2017 indicates that the world population would 
increase further to 9.7 billion in 2050 and 11.2 billion by 2100 (UN, 2018). As 
agriculture provides basic living needs for human societies, population growth coupled 
with consuming growth requires food increase (Godfray et al, 2010; Keating et al, 2014). 
Currently, our food system puts much pressure on natural resources (Tilman et al, 2001), 
especially on water (Falkenmark et al, 1992). The first half of 21st century is probably 
to be the final period of rapid agricultural expansion. And the food demand of a 
wealthier and 50% larger world population will be a major driver of global climate 
change (Tilman et al, 2001). Thus, reducing environment pressure and using resources 
more efficiently in food provision becomes an urgent task for modern societies.  
 
Agricultural extensification and intensification are two ways for food increase. 
Extensification is to transform natural landscape into agricultural land, while 
intensification is to increase crop yield on existing agricultural lands by manners of 
fertilization, irrigation, pesticide application, and crop varieties improvement (Matson 
et al, 1997; Tilman et al, 2001; Galford et al, 2010; Tilman et al, 2011). According to 
current trends of global food production, richer nations are more likely to gain greater 
agricultural intensification, while poorer nations are more likely to gain extensification 
(Tilman et al, 2011). Land use and habitat conversion are actually a zero-sum game. 
More lands converting to cropland means less land remaining in the forms of grasslands, 
forests and other natural land habitats. As a result, agricultural extensification is 
considered as a dominant quantified cause of global habitat destruction and a 
conservative estimate of local ecosystem deteriorations (Tilman et al, 2001). Moreover, 
environmental externalities such as losses in biodiversity, ecosystem services or 
eutrophication are generated because more local resource use for cropland expansion 
(Galloway et al 2007). Intensification also has negative impacts to the environment. It 
changes the biotic interactions and patterns of resource availability in ecosystems, 
causing severe environmental consequences from local, regional, even to global extents 
(Matson et al, 1997). However, a sustainable food supply remains to be an intractable 
challenge for the whole world due to environmental impacts from agriculture along 
==PAGE:10==

with dietary changes (Keating et al, 2014). Sustainability in food provision context can 
be described as meeting current production goals without compromising the ability of 
future to produce food in the aspects of resource degradation or depletion (Matson et 
al, 1997). This definition appeals the current food systems to use resources more 
efficiently.  
 
Water footprint (WF) is one of the environmental indicators to monitor water use by 
human appropriation of whole food life cycle. (Galli et al, 2011; Lathuillière et al, 2014). 
WF measures the total volume of water used to produce or consume each of the goods 
and services, usually in unit of m3 (or Liter) per activity or product (Mekonnen & 
Hoekstra, 2011; Hoekstra, 2012). Currently, the agricultural sector accounts for about 
85% of global blue water consumption which includes groundwater and surface 
(Mekkonen &Hoistra, 2011). According to Mekonnen & Hoekstra’s (2014) study, the 
current global water consumption (based on reference period of 1996 -2005) in crop 
production will be saved by 39% if the green–blue WF of crop production around the 
world can be reduced to the level of 25th percentile of current global production. 
Reducing the water footprint in food provision becomes an urgent task for modern 
societies (Khan et al., 2009). This requires using water more efficiently by increasing 
the amount of carbon assimilated by crops per unit of water (Mekonnen & Hoekstra, 
2014). Cutting down WF per unit of production, in other words enhancing water 
agricultural productivity, will alleviate the burden on the global freshwater resources, 
which is quite finite. 
 
Obtaining crop physiological variables can be achieved by manual measurement with 
physiology instruments. Manual measurement requires numerous samplings, entailing 
intrusive equipment and adept skills (Trought & Bramley, 2011). Therefore, finding a 
faster, simper, cheaper and non-destructive alternative is vital to achieve high efficiency 
in both time and cost aspects (Hall et al., 2002). The first step is to provide spatially 
explicit tools to better quantify crop water use at the farmscale in order to be able to 
adjust management practices. Multispectral and hyperspectral imaging in optical or 
solar range (400 – 2500 nm) has been convinced to have high relationship with 
vegetation dynamics (Cunliffe et al., 2016). In visible spectral bands (400 – 700 nm), 
plant exhibits a particular spectral reflectance signature when absorbing visible light 
(Ustin et al., 2009). In near infrared spectral bands (780 – 2500 nm), plant reflects a 
significant amount of energy because of the inter structure of its leaves (Campbell & 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
Wynne, 2011). Thus, Visible (VIS)-to-shortwave infrared (SWIR) imaging allows 
detecting minor modifications in leaf bio-chemistry. Physiological status of plants can 
be assessed quickly in a non-intrusive way with the help of these spectrometers (Cifre 
et al., 2005). 
 
Acquiring remote sensing imaging data containing vegetation dynamics information is 
crucial to evaluate water use in crop production. This can be attained by remoting 
sensing from Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAV) carrying hyperspectral and thermal 
cameras (Colomina et al., 2014). Satellite is a common tool for obtaining remote 
sensing images (Campbell & Wynne, 2011; Courault et al., 2005). As the fast 
development of UAV related technology, UAV-based aerial remote sensing becomes a 
popular and economical method which replaces satellite more and more frequently in 
some fileds (Colomina et al., 2014). Generally, the remote sensing functions of UAV 
are determined by the sensors it carries. The sensors could have diverse types including 
thermal camera, multispectral cameras, hyperspectral camera, photometric elevation 
mapping sensor and Light detection and ranging sensors (LiDAR) (Berni et al., 2009; 
Burkart et al., 2015; Shahbazi et al., 2015). Equipped with various types of payloads 
with different sensors, UAV can be used applied to acquiring remote sensing data in 
diverse areas such as agriculture, forestry and environment (Nex & Remondino, 2014). 
According to Colomina’s (et al., 2014) research, the global unmanned aerial systems 
market revenue is worth 5400.0 M€ as of 2013 and expected to grow up to 6350.0 M€ 
by 2018.  
 
In crop monitoring, UAV has some advantages compared to other remote sensing 
acquisition systems (satellites, aircrafts, goniometers and based towers): low cost, 
flexible operation times, can be operated under clear sky or cloudy conditions and high 
temporal & spatial resolution (<1m) (Berni et al., 2009; Laliberte et al., 2011; Matese 
et al., 2015; Rango et al., 2009). Nonetheless, current methods for estimating crop water 
use and photosynthesis could benefit from a better parameterization of water stress 
factors which are currently based on meteorological variables or standard vegetation 
indices (Campbell & Wynne, 2011). Vegetation indices (VIs) attempt to estimate plant’s 
biophysical properties based on digital brightness values (Campbell & Wynne, 2011; 
Huete et al., 2002). Some vegetation indices are able to quantify green biomass, monitor 
plant stress status & canopy water content, and estimate properties such as green 
chlorophyll and carotenoids (Ceccato et al., 2001; Martinez & Ramos, 2015; Rapaport 
==PAGE:12==
4 
 
et al. 2015).  
 
As a response to water stress, vegetation changes its temperature and reflectance in 
several wavelengths (Campbell & Norman, 2012). The changes can be reflected by 
some vegetation indices. Rapaport (et al. 2015) studied leaf physiology and 
hyperspectral imaging changes of grapevine responding to different water status, 
including water deficit situation. The result shows that grapevine plant displays evident 
spectral response at VIS – SWIR regions by using several normalized vegetation 
indices, such as water balance indices (WABIs). 
 
Therefore, UAV with hyperspectral and thermal cameras can help quantify better plant 
stress responses. But this requires a careful identification of spectral wavelengths which 
are most sensitive to changes in water availability beyond classic indices such as 
Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI). Thus, the project’s aim is to provide 
indicators of crop water stress, photosynthesis and transpiration from remote sensing 
cameras that can be carried by UAV. We will try to find empirical relations by using 
statistical analysis of information from optical and thermal sensors and leaf 
measurements of photosynthesis, transpiration, conductance & temperature for two 
crops that present different hydraulic traits to regulate stomatal opening, which are 
isohydric and anisohydric respectively. Considering this, we clarify 3 objectives for the 
experiment: 
(1) Assess differences between the two crops of isohydric and anisohydric in the 
regulation of stomatal conductance and other physiological variables as well as 
changes in remote sensing variables; 
 
(2) Find spectral indices which are more sensitive changes to in water stress due to 
changes in stomatal conductance, transpiration, photosynthesis or in remote sensing 
variables; 
 
Finally, (1) and (2) may help find multivariate statistical method for detecting water 
stress in the two crops.

2.1. Experiment site  
The growth chamber experiment is conducted at Risø Environmental Risk Assessment 
Facility (RERAF) in Risø Campus of Denmark Technical University.  
2.1.1. RERAF 
RERAF is a new generation of phytotrons. It is a unique plant growth facility 
constituted by a row of physically and electronically separated environmental chambers 
with independent top and root compartments. Ecophysiological experiments can be 
conducted under fully controlled conditions. Climate conditions from subarctic to 
tropical can be well simulated. Water consumption, CO2 assimilation and nutrients 
taken up can be controlled and monitored continuously or over short time intervals, 
making it a good tool for simulation of natural environmental conditions as well as 
validation of predictive models. The detailed distribution of RERAF is shown in the

2.1.2. Growth chamber 
The dimensions of the chamber are 6m * 4m * 3.1m. It is designed to accommodate 
12m2 ecosystems or bench area. Independent climate control and ventilation system are 
installed to control the level of light, temperature and humidity. Artificial light merely 
or that mixed with sunlight can be provided at daytime and enough artificial “daylight” 
can be created even at night. Shutter and curtain arrangement controlled by computer 
systems makes it possible to switch between sunlight and artificial light. Temperature 
can be adjusted between 6℃ and 40℃. Humidity is control by interchange between the 
cooling and heating radiators as well as a water vapor generating system. Moreover, the 
systems also have the ability to simulate the diurnal and annual cycle of all main plaint 
growth parameters in a near-natural manner as well as provide extra special gases. The 
controlled experiment will be set in one growth chamber. 
 
2.2. Isohydric and anisohydric behavior  
Plants have the ability to control water loss by their tissues (Buckley, 2005). Different 
physiological characteristics of plants determine the degree of control over leaf water 
potential. Generally, plants can be categorized as isohydric and anisohydric due to the 
difference in leaf water potential control (Maseda & Fernández, 2006), which are two 
significant characteristics influencing the observed physiological responses under water 
stress status (Jones, 2009).  
2.2.1. Isohydric behavior 
Isohydric behavior is to conduct tight and constant control of leaf water potential using 
root-to-shoot hydraulic signalling and chemical interactions, consequently managing 
water loss through stomata. Therefore, a constant midday leaf water potential can be 
achieved and transpiration is limited, by reducing stomatal conductance in water-
abundant or drought conditions (Jones, 2009; Sade et al, 2012). 
 
Isohydric plants can achieve water potential homeostasis, which means they have the 
ability to adjust water loss within some extent in order to prevent inner water deficit 
(Buckley, 2005). The control of stomata is accomplished quickly through chemical-
based signals if leave water potential decreases to a threshold level. Finally, any further 
drops in water potential is avoided (Jones, 2009). The soil water status doesn’t impact 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
the water potential of their leaves, which keeps almost stable at certain level during the 
day as well as during mild water stress situation (Jones & Tardieu, 1998). 
2.2.2. Anisohydric behavior 
Anisohydric behavior is that plants have loose (but not absent) stomatal control over 
leaf water potential, causing which to be more variable. Therefore, the stomata are open 
and the photosynthetic rates are high for a longer period even with lowering leaf water 
potential (Jones, 2009; Sade et al, 2012). 
 
When compared to isohydric plants, the anisohydric plants do regulate leave water 
potential but at a declined rate. Different to isohydric plants, the leave water potential 
of anisohydric plants will decrease when soil water potential decreases. When it 
decreases to a threshold level, the stomata will start to control water loss (Jones, 2006). 
This is because anisohydric plants lacks of an interaction between chemical, hydraulic 
and even morphological signals, or their receptors are insensitive (Jones & Tardieu, 
1998).  
 
Anisohydric plants have weak response to water potential fluctuations with changing 
evapotranspirative demand in mild water stress situation, remaining photosynthetic 
capacity until severe stress occurs. By this time the leave water potential may have 
already declined to a significant level that is enough to damage their physiological 
processes. Consequently, the plants will cease many growth process and show visible 
symptoms of water deficits, such as wilting (Jones, 2009).  
 
2.3. Experiment setup  
We are interested in seeing the impacts of different water level to tropical crops during 
their growth. Soybean and maize are two major crops grown in America. As for 
physiological characteristics, soybean and maize belong to two opposite water use 
strategies, which are isohydric and anisohydric respectively. Accordingly, they were 
chosen as experimental objects and the growing cycles of two vegetation species were 
simulated. 
 
The experiment placed 36 pots comprising of 2 types of plants with 3 different water

2.6. Water supply  
2.6.1. Germination period  
At the beginning, 9 seeds were planted in each pot for both soybean and maize in order 
to make sure of the survival rate. During the germination period, the pots were left on 
the trays in the middle of the chamber. The seeds were watered every 3 days. Before 
irrigation, the pot weight was measured to record the water loss amount. Every 
irrigation time the water was given up to 2.5kg to each pot in order to keep the same 
water level. The pot weight was also measured to record actual added water amount.  
 
When plants have leaves that are huge enough for the measurement, they are thinned to 
2 plants per pot and given different level of water supply.  
2.6.2. Different water supply setup after germination  
The aim of different water supply is to impose water stress on the plants from decent 
water supply to mild water stress and even severe water stress (water deficit). Different 
water stress could have impacts on leaf water potential and stomatal conductance of 
plants. Finally, the impacts are reflected in hyperspectral data of special wavelength as 
well as thermal data. The well-watered condition is defined as 100% WHC (water 
holding capacity). For this type of soil, 4 kg soil was added in the pot and 2.5 kg of 
water was given to reach the 100% WHC. The experiment changed water supply for 
the crops from potential transpiration conditions to wilting point. 3 types of water

4.1. Plant physiology variables 
Plant physiology describes plants’ internal activities such as chemical and physical 
processes associated with life occurring inside the plants (Taiz & Zeiger, 2002). 
Different physiology variables are studied as indexes reflecting plant activities from 
leaf to canopy level. In this experiment, evapotranspiration (ET) is the physiology 
variable exhibiting plant water use situation at canopy level. And photosynthesis, 
transpiration, stomatal conductance & leaf temperature are 4 physiology variables 
showing plant status at leaf level.  
4.1.1. Evapotranspiration  
Evapotranspiration (ET) is the sum of Evaporation and Transpiration. The former term 
describes the amount of water taken up by the atmosphere from wet surfaces. While the 
latter term describes the water loss from plants to the atmosphere through their stomata. 
Therefore, ET is a collective term describing plant water use situation including all the 
processes of water transformation from nearby surface or leaf inside to the atmosphere 
(Campbell & Norman, 2012). 
 
Evapotranspiration (ET) results are at canopy level. In each measurement, initial weight 
and weight after watering of each pot were measured. Thus, ET can be calculated as,

Figure 5.5 (1) displays the result of WUE for soybean. Except day 23, the driest group 
(SW1 - 40%WHC) always has the highest WUE among the 3 different water groups. 
Although in some measurement days the WUE of the driest group is very close to the 
middle water group (SW2 - 70%WHC). As for middle water group and the wettest 
group (SW3 - 100%WHC), the former group is distinctly higher than latter group in 
some measurement days with respect to the WUE value. In other days, their values are 
almost the same or former group is a bit lower than the latter group, which can still be 
treated as the same. Therefore, this result suggests that, water deficit situation has 
evident impacts on the soybean. It will increase the water use efficiency of soybean, 
which means that the relative decrease in photosynthesis is less than the relative 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
decrease in transpiration. 
 
Furthermore, from former analysis we know that the driest group has the lowest 
photosynthesis rate. The WUE result suggests that the driest group has the highest WUE. 
Considering the definition of WUE, we can backstep to speculate the transpiration as, 
 
𝑇𝑟𝑎𝑛𝑠𝑝𝑖𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 ↓ = 𝑃ℎ𝑜𝑡𝑜𝑠𝑦𝑛𝑡ℎ𝑒𝑠𝑖𝑠↓
𝑊𝑈𝐸 ↑
 
 
It suggests that isohydric plant with lower soil water supply will have lower 
transpiration, which matches the theory well. Usually, stomata keep open when leaf 
conducts photosynthesis in order to absorb CO2. Transpiration will also be conducted 
in the meantime. As soybean is an isohydric plant, it has strong control on the stomata 
when meeting low soil water potential. Deficient water supply will cause soybean to 
close the stomata in order to reduce the transpiration rate and prevent water loss inside 
the plants. In order to keep growing, the plants will increase the WUE to prevent sharp 
decrease on photosynthesis rate. 
 
Maize 
 
Figure 5.5 (2) illustrates the result of WUE for maize. Different from soybean, for all 
measurement days, all the 3 water treatments have almost the same WUE values. This 
result suggests that different water supplies have minimum impact on WUE of maize, 
which also matches the theory. Contrasting with soybean, maize is an anisohydric plant. 
It has loose control on the stomata when meeting low soil water potential. Deficient 
water supply does not cause it to close the stomata noteworthily. Thus, the plant could 
keep the photosynthesis rate and transpiration. The water use efficiency keeps constant 
as a result.  
 
In summary, isohydric (soybean) and anisohydric (maize) plants have different behave 
on stomatal control as shown by the t-test on conductance when meeting with water 
deficit situation. Thus, the WUE results of soybean and maize show opposite 
conclusions. For isohydric (soybean) vegetation, dry treatment plants start with lower 
photosynthesis than the well irrigated (100% WHC), but later on the plants adjust and 
the differences decrease. The conductance is always lower and it is reflected in the 
transpiration rate. So, the plants must become more efficient in the use of water 
==PAGE:48==

(Photosynthesis/Transpiration) over time. But for anisohydric (maize) vegetation they 
do not have this pattern.

Soybean

superpose but only have small divergences between 510 nm and 620nm. This result 
suggests that different water supplies will have slight impacts on the reflectance of 
green light. Also, during experiment process it is noticed that the group with less water 
supply has greener leaves during the measurement, especially the bottom leaves.  
 
Furthermore, the 3 reflectance curves have distinct divergences at bands > 740 nm, 
where the driest group (SW1 - 40% WHC) has the highest reflectance and the wettest 
group (SW3 - 100% WHC) has the lowest. It suggests that less water supply will 
increase the reflectance of incoming radiation for the soybean plants at near infrared 
area. The reason may be that soybean plants (isohydric) with less water supply have 
lower transpiration rate. Cooling down the leaf temperature by transpiration is limited. 
As a result, the soybean plants will emit more longwave radiance to cool down, whose 
bands are at near infrared area.  
 
This trend keeps unchanged at the first 4 measurements, but the 3 reflect curves almost 
superpose at last 4 measurements starting at day 36. This may be because the 
measurement time interval is shorted from 3 days to 2 days at day 34. Comparing with 
the last 4 measurements, during the first 4 measurements plants have more time to adapt 
their water situation and shows on their reflectance behavior.

On May 2nd we measured reflectance both under outdoor environment and in the 
chamber for one pot of soybean and maize each. The outdoor measurement was taken 
in a sunny day with adequate incoming radiation. Pictures were taken with 
hyperspectral camera and plants were also measured with ASD. The aim is to see if the 
camera is noisy and if the light indoor makes artifacts on the reflectance. However, the 
result cannot be compared from hyperspectral pictures because the labels of plants were 
not recorded during the measurement. Instead, ASD result were used for analysis. 
Moreover, the maize plant was seriously influenced by the grass in the background. 
Therefore, the following charts only show soybean result from ASD.

6.2. discussion of correlation results  
In summary, soybean and maize have different results about correlations of VI & 
physiology variables. Most probably, it is because soybean (isohydric) and maize 
(anisohydric) belong to two opposite plants that have contrasting response to water 
deficit situation. Another important factor should be considered is that, different with 
soybean, maize was measured at an early stage. They keep growing very fast and 
correspondingly LAI increase rapidly, which is reflected by the increase of NDVI. As 
maize is an anisohydric plant, it has slight sensitivity to changes on low soil water 
potential (and water content). ET keeps increasing as the plants grow up. But for 
soybean, all the plants are already very big during the measurement. Therefore, it is 
hard to know if the settled water supplies are reasonable for them as it is noticeable that 
soybean plants almost use up all the given water in all the measurements.  
 
6.3. Discussion of potential sources of error  
It is worth to notice that the time interval between watering plants and measuring is not 
the same for each pot. The time deviation is generated from two causes. For one thing, 
all the plants were always watered at first on each measurement day. After that devices 
were set up to prepare for the measurement. Sometimes problems happened and the 
measurement was postponed as a result. This contributed to the time difference of 
around 30 minutes to 1 hour. For another, leaf took time to become stable in the 
physiology measurement, usually 2 minutes per leaf. For the experiment, 2 leaves were 
measured per pot, which means 12 leaves for one water group. In this case, the time 
interval between watering and measuring of the 6th pot was around 40 minutes 
compared with the 1st pot. Considering the time that plants change their behavior after 
giving them water, the time difference of the measurement may have non-negligible 
impacts on plant behavior and is an important cause of standard deviations. 
 
Another thing should be noticed is that, there were 2 plants in each pot and in the 
physiology measurement only 2 leaves were measure per pot. Sometimes the 2 chosen 
leaves were from different plants. Even if we measure 2 leaves in one plant, two nearby 
leaves in the same plant sometimes behaved differently. And for maize, the leaves were 
long and thin. It is found that sometimes two close parts in one maize leaf showed huge 
difference during the physiology measurement. This situation was reflected in the raw

Moreover, the error sources from instruments for collecting hyperspectral data are also 
important to be considered. For example, the wavelength period at bands > 900 nm is

In the vegetation indices analysis, the index of WI is calculated by two bands of 902

7. Conclusion  
 
This project conducted a growth experiment for soybean (isohydric) and maize 
(anisohydric) with opposite hydraulic traits to regulate stomatal opening in a proper, 
stable, and manual controlled environment. Different water supply from water deficit 
to well irrigated were applied to both crops. Finally, plant physiological (photosynthesis, 
conductance, transpiration and leaf temperature) and hyperspectral data over time were 
collected and analyzed in order to assess the differences between the two crops. 
Reflectance data of VIS – SWIR area were extracted and 5 spectral indices (NDVI, 
OSAVI, PSNDc, PRI, WI) were calculated. Furthermore, linear correlation analysis of 
physiology variables and vegetation indices were carried out in order to find appropriate 
vegetation indices which are more sensitive to changes in water stress due to different 
reaction in stomatal conductance and other physiological variables as well as changes 
in remote sensing variables. As the energy levels in the RERAF were constant during 
the whole experiment, we can assume that decreases in ET levels with respect to the 
maximum potential ET are reflecting plant water stress. 
 
To sum up, PRI is more sensitive to changes for soybean in water stress due to different 
reaction in stomatal conductance. Contrary to expected correlations of physiological 
variables were not significant with leaf temperature for soybean but they were for maize 
although significant differences were found between water treatments in soybean. For 
maize, an index related with canopy water content, WI was best for detecting water 
stress, which is consistent with maize’s anisohydric behavior where stomata are open 
for lower soil moisture levels which might produce decreases in leaf water potential. 
These results highlight the need of using different indices than the classic NDVI to track 
plant water status, and of incorporating the specific hydraulic strategies of crops when 
designing a monitoring strategy to track water stress. 
 
This information could be useful in the field with UAV (Unmanned Aerial Vehicles) to 
track water stress. For soybean, reductions in ET due to closing stomata can be tracked 
by PRI that will be related with the adjustments in photosynthesis due to the lower 
conductance. For maize, the stomatal conductance did not change significantly, which 
should have an impact on the leaf water potential and on the canopy water content. 
Therefore, indices related with changes in canopy water content such as the WI seem 
==PAGE:71==
63 
 
to be more sensitive in this case. The role of temperature as a stress indicator is not so 
clear as maize was correlated with it but not soybean despite. 
 
Talking about recommendations for future experiments to further study the behaviors 
of the two crops with opposite hydraulic traits, the suggestions are as followed. Firstly, 
dry treatments with even less water supply could be applied. As we noticed during the 
experiment, the 40% WHC was still too high for the extreme low water situation. 
Secondly, soybean plants could be measurement in an earlier stage in the future 
experiments. In our experiment, the soybean plants were thinned and applied different 
water levels when they were already very big, which was because we were lack of 
planting knowledge and experience. Finally, considering the error source of 
measurement time intervals, conducting measurements with better time control every 
time after irrigating maybe get better results.
