Abstract
This study addresses global concerns about the role of women leadership in the energy industry,
specifically examining its implications for economic and social development. The overarching
hypothesis suggests a correlation between increased gender diversity in leadership and improved
sustainability outcomes in the sector.
However, a gap persists in understanding the specific mechanisms linking women’s leadership
to sustainability within the energy industry. The primary research question guiding this study
is: How can the gender gap in leadership roles within the energy sector be effectively closed?
To comprehensively investigate, a methodology incorporating empirical analysis, interviews, and
desk studies is employed. This research aims to disentangle various sub-research questions:
• What is the current landscape of leadership roles held by women in the Nordic energy
sector?
• What policies currently exist that promote gender diversity and the role of women leaders
within the energy industry in the Nordic countries?
• How can the existing gender gap in the energy industry be reduced, especially in the Nordic
context, to address gender disparities in leadership positions?
This thesis contributes to the field by providing a comprehensive understanding of the link
between gender diversity and sustainable outcomes in the energy industry, with a specific focus
on the impact of women’s leadership. The results seek to uncover how women in leadership roles
positively impact sustainable energy initiatives, considering the industry’s academic background
and gender representation in STEM, while examining the progression from STEM education to
women’s leadership roles in the industry and mainly in the energy industry, initiating with a
broad exploration and gradually narrowing the focus to Nordic countries.
The study also emphasizes the importance of laws and policies in shaping gender diversity,
considering their impact on achieving Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and facilitating
women’s access to energy-related professions.

Introduction
Energy plays a pivotal role in driving economic growth, improving quality of life, and ensuring
environmental sustainability. In recent years, the energy sector sector has experienced signif-
icant transformations, driven by growing energy demand, technological advances, and urgent
environmental concerns. This has underscored the urgency of transitioning to renewable energy
alternatives, with initiatives like the Paris Agreement [1] and Sustainable Development Goals
[2] emphasizing the need for cleaner, more equitable, and environmentally sustainable energy
systems, along with affordable, reliable, and non-polluting energy consumption. Specifically,
SDG 7 aims to ensure access to affordable, reliable, sustainable, and modern energy for all.
Moreover, several other SDGs are directly or indirectly linked to energy, including SDG 11 (sus-
tainable cities and communities), SDG 13 (climate action), and SDG 17 (partnerships for the
goals). "These goals seek to endenergy poverty, foster sustainable urban development, and ad-
dress climate change through global cooperation. Despite appearing distinct, these goals share a
common solution: the use of renewable energy instead of fossil fuels. By adopting renewable en-
ergy, we can make substantial progress toward achieving these interconnected global objectives."
Moreover gender diversity in the energy sector is crucial, being a fundamental principle sup-
porting the quest for a sustainable energy future. Recognizing the essential link between gender
diversity and renewable energy transitions, the European Union (EU) has articulated gender
equality as a core value in its Gender Equality Strategy 20-25 [3], as well as in the Sustainable
Development Goals, specifically number 5 (Achieve gender equality and empower all women and
girls) [4]. These strategies not only emphasize the productivity gains associated with gender
equality but also underline the imperative of inclusive and diverse leadership to foster innova-
tion and competitiveness within the EU.
Despite concerted efforts to promote gender equality in the European Union, progress to-
wards inclusive and sustainable energy transitions remains hindered [5]. While EU directives
such as the Directive 2006/54/EC [6] and Directive 2022/2381 [7] aim to address systemic dispar-
ities as it will be further develop in this thesis, obstacles persist in several sectors. For example,
new technologies, including artificial intelligence algorithms, often reflect and perpetuate soci-
etal biases. One example is the gendered translations generated by platforms such as Google
Translator (see Figure 1).
These biases not only highlight the broader challenges of gender
inequality, also highlight specific manifestations within industries that can be extrapolated to
other sectors such as the energy industry, since the roles/adjectives translated make it seem like
women are suited for specific jobs/roles and not others, as men are. This occurs since algorithms
behind platforms like Google Translator are developed by individuals who may unconsciously
place societal biases, leading to outputs that reinforce stereotypes, such as representing men
more frequently as leaders than women.

Female leaders with a technical background, who can hold positions such as CEOs or de-
partment managers and actively make decisions within their companies, are under-represented
in the energy industry. The under-representation of women leaders in the energy industry re-
flects the systemic barriers to progress towards gender parity and inclusion. According to a 2020
report, women held only 12% of senior management roles in the energy sector [8]. Recognizing
these challenges is crucial to developing targeted interventions to address gender disparities and
promote greater diversity and equality within the energy sector and beyond.
To effectively address these challenges, it is crucial to examine the foundational factors con-
tributing to the gender gap in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics)
fields. STEM encompasses disciplines essential for innovation and problem-solving across vari-
ous industries, including the energy sector. As it will be deeper analyse during the thesis, many
roles within the energy industry require a strong foundation in STEM disciplines such as en-
ergy engineering, industrial engineering, environmental engineering, electrical engineering, and
mechanical engineering. Despite advancements in education, women continue to be underrepre-
sented in these STEM job positions, comprising only 35% of the STEM workforce in 2021 [9] (see
Figure 2). This persistent gender gap underscores the prevalence of systemic barriers inhibiting
women’s participation in STEM fields, including cultural biases and social norms. Furthermore,
the underrepresentation of women in STEM disciplines has significant implications for their
presence in leadership roles within industries like energy, where a STEM background is often a
prerequisite for promotion.

Moreover, gender diversity on the boards of energy companies has been shown to generate

only for equality and justice, but also to have a wider pool of talent and to promote cultural
and social change, in order to modify stereotypes and ingrained norms about gender roles.

2.1
Women in Sustainability
This section explores the evolving landscape of women’s roles in advancing sustainable practices
within the energy industry, highlighting their contributions, skills, leadership styles, and the
challenges they face in this sector.
2.1.1
Women’s Contributions to Sustainability: Attributes, Leadership, and Or-
ganizational Impact
To understand the relationship between women and sustainability, different research studies
have tried to uncover the capacities and skills that are critical to driving the energy transi-
tion and achieving sustainable outcomes. These studies show the multifaceted contributions
that women can offer to the sustainability landscape, highlighting the importance of their in-
volvement in shaping environmentally friendly practices and fostering social responsibility. For
instance, studies claim that women enrich environmental consciousness and social responsibility
within organizations [14]. This is due to their greater empathy, nurturing roles and a stronger
ethic of care. Researches agree that this attributes are translating to collaboration and stake-
holder agreement in the workplace environment, foster innovative and enterprising approaches
to sustainability challenges [15]. In other words, since women are often more inclined to collab-
orate and prioritize environmental care, this approach leads to more effective and sustainable
outcomes.
Furthermore, gender diversity is essential not only within the workforce of firms but also
in their corporate board since studies consistently demonstrate that achieving a balanced gen-
der representation in leadership enhances company performance and drives economic growth,
contributing to improved governance and public trust. The reason is that by considering more
points of view, overall company performance improves [12]. In addition, having a diverse group
of people fosters more ethical behavior and greater transparency within the company, which
externally translates into a better reputation and increased public trust [16]. In the energy field
context, researches also suggest a significant positive association between gender diversity on
boards and sustainability performance, indicating that a minimum board threshold of 30 per-
cent women is required to have a substantial effect on sustainability performance [12].Having
this minimum diversity ratio allows more individuals to identify with board members and to
interact with them more easily [11]. In order to achieve this, studies agree that one of the first
steps is to increase the participation of women in technical education [17] [18], which will be

Moreover, women exhibit characteristics closely aligned with sustainability principles, often
displaying a stronger commitment to environmental issues compared to men [14]. According to

the trajectory of sustainable development, facilitating a more resilient and equitable future.
2.1.2
Types of Leadership
In order to understand the different types of leadership relevant to sustainability, several the-
ories have emerged, shedding light on how board gender diversity can influence organizational
environmental performance.
The first leadership type to analyse is the Stakeholder Theory, which suggests that or-
ganizations should consider the interests of all stakeholders, including employees, customers,
communities, and the environment, rather than focusing solely on maximizing shareholder value
[24] [25] . Following this theory, scientific papers suggest that gender diversity helps promote
organizational environmental performance, perhaps because women have also seen themselves as
minorities in corporate boards. This means that the experience of knowing what it feels like to
be a minority enables them to better understand how to consider the interests of all stakeholders
and help others feel heard [21] [26] .
The second leadership type is the Agency Theory, which explains that there may be a
principal-agent problem in organizations, where managers (agents) may pursue their own inter-

ests at the expense of shareholders’ interests (principals) [27]. This underscores the importance
of female leaders, as women have been shown to exhibit lower tolerance for unethical behaviors
[28] [29]. Furthermore, women frequently contribute a fresh perspective to difficult situations,
which can rectify informational biases in the design of strategies and problem solutions [30] [31]
and also increasing women’s presence in decision-making roles could result in better financial
performance for firms, as discussed earlier in Section 2.1.1 [26].
Moving forward, the next leadership type can be explained according to the Resource De-
pendence Theory. This one focuses on the dependence of organizations on external resources,
such as capital, labour, and raw materials. Resource Dependency Theory highlights women’s
possession of specialized skills in sustainability and corporate governance, suggesting their po-
tential to drive innovative solutions to environmental challenges [26, 32, 33].
Last type is the Upper Echelons Theory, which posits that the demographics and character-
istics of top management, including the board of directors, influence organizational outcomes
[34]. This theory suggests that women’s greater concern for environmental risks and issues may
shape organizational outcomes, leading to more innovative and adaptive decision-making pro-
cesses [26, 34].
Examining these leadership styles illustrates how gender dynamics intersect with sustainabil-
ity initiatives. Although women face more obstacles in assuming leadership roles, as it will be
develop in further sections of this study, their leadership styles tend to prioritise collaboration
and cooperation, which is closely aligned with sustainability principles [22]. Their tendency
to demonstrate care and attention in general extends to environmental concerns, resulting in
greater awareness of sustainable practices and environmental protection. Women tend to exhibit
community characteristics, emphasising concern for the well-being of others and relationship-
building, which can foster inclusive decision-making processes that lead to sustainability since
women often demonstrate traits such as empathy, collaboration, and a focus on building relation-
ships[22]. These community behaviours can translate into leadership approaches that prioritise
environmental and social considerations, promoting more sustainable outcomes within compa-
nies [14].
The literature on gender diversity on boards highlights the significant correlation between
female representation and environmental performance, which can potentially benefit the energy
sector as it transitions to a more sustainable industry [26]. As discussed in this section, female
executives are more inclined to make ethical decisions and demonstrate heightened awareness
of environmental issues. This trend is evidenced by the positive relationship observed between
the proportion of women on boards of directors and companies’ adoption of green practices,
commitment to environmental sustainability, and environmental responsibility [11, 26, 35, 36].
In addition, female leaders’ hostility to risk, as demonstrated in the Upper Echelons Theory [26,
34], and inclination to empathy contribute to more ethical and sustainable business practices,

2.1.3
Barriers and Challenges to Women’s Leadership in Sustainability
Despite the positive outcomes about women leading to sustainability, the specific literature link-
ing female representation on corporate boards to environmental performance remains relatively
limited. These challenges emerge from systemic biases, cultural norms, and organizational struc-
tures, limiting women’s representation, opportunities, and leadership styles. [16] [17]
Studies across Europe reveal alarming statistics, with a significant portion of energy com-
panies lacking female representation on their boards or management teams, topic that will be
further develop in the following Section 2.2. The European Institute for Gender Equality un-
derscores this disparity, reporting that a mere 17% of women hold high-level positions in the
energy sector, highlighting a stark contrast to the 83% of men in similar roles [17].
Limited opportunities for career advancement further hold back women’s progress in the
energy industry. Persistent perceptions of the sector as a male-dominated domain, coupled with
enduring gender stereotypes, create barriers that restrict women’s access to leadership roles [17].
Also, perceptions about leadership, which are frequently connected to masculinity, exacerbate
prejudices against female leaders. For instance, attributes like assertiveness, confidence, and the
capacity to delegate are frequently associated with men, which devalues and makes women less
desirable in effective leadership roles [39]. This bias is especially pronounced in environments
where traditional gender norms prevail, where successful women are often perceived as competent
but distant, while those who conform to traditional roles are seen as caring but incompetent [40].
Moreover, organisational structures and cultural norms further compound the challenges for
women in sustainability leadership. Hierarchical systems often continue to maintain traditional
gender roles, limiting women’s access to leadership positions. Research indicates that organi-
sational hierarchies influence the extent of women’s participation and recognition in leadership
positions, exacerbating traditional gender norms in the workplace [41]. In addition, cultural
factors, such as the prevalence of machismo in certain regions, contribute to gender stereotypes
and hold up women’s advancement in leadership role [42].
The intersection of work and family responsibilities has emerged as a prominent and often
criticized challenge in recent years. This issue affects women not only within the energy industry
but also across various work environments. Different researches note that women, on average,
spend more time on domestic and caregiving duties, impacting their career progression [42] [43].
The decision to have children can also result in a motherhood wage penalty, wherein women
face reduced earning potential compared to men [44]. These challenges are compounded by
workplace cultures that fail to accommodate the needs of working mothers, perpetuating gender
disparities in leadership positions [43].

2.2
Energy Sector
Having outlined women’s contributions to sustainability, leadership styles, and the barriers they
face, we now delve deeper into the energy sector. This section explores gender representation
within the sector, using a case study at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) to examine
advancements in gender diversity as well as persistent gaps hindering equitable representation.
2.2.1
Gender Dynamics in the Energy Industry
The energy sector, as discussed earlier in this paper, is undergoing a significant transition.
However, it continues to face a persistent issue: the low representation of women, especially in
leadership positions. Despite this disparity, emerging trends within the renewable energy sector
present a contrasting landscape. Renewable energy sources are fostering greater gender diversity
compared to traditional energy sectors such as fossil fuels. This shift not only addresses equity
concerns but also underscores the sector’s potential for innovation and sustainable development.
When searching for information on gender diversity in the energy sector, multiple sources
consistently highlight the field’s significant gender imbalance. The International Energy Agency
(IEA) states, "Energy is one of the least gender diverse sectors of the global economy today,
with women making up only 16% of the energy sector’s employees despite representing nearly
40% of global employment" [5]. Furthermore, women account for only about 11% of start-up
founders in this sector, much lower than other industries. According to the Organisation for
Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), "On average, there are 76% fewer women
than men working in the energy sector," emphasizing the stark disparity [45] . The Energy Al-
liance also notes that, "Despite the renewable energy sector holding vast potential for women’s
participation, evidence reveals an ongoing under-representation of women across the renewable
energy value chain" [46].
The energy industry has started to address these issues through initiatives like "Equal by
30" [47], which has garnered support from the G7 and over 135 energy companies.
For in-

Gender gap in decision making
Another big gender gap in the energy industry is the lack of female representatives in leader-
ship positions is another indicator of a persistent challenge, already mentioned along this study.
Research consistently points out that women are notably underrepresented on corporate boards
and in senior management positions within energy companies [17]. For instance, a study con-
ducted by the European Institute for Gender Equality (EIGE) reveals a prevalent absence of
women in decision-making bodies across a majority of energy firms, with only a minority having
female representation at key levels of management [17]. This disparity is mirrored in the public
sector, where women occupy fewer high-level positions compared to men. Specifically, these
positions include executive roles such as Chief Executive Officers (CEOs), members of executive
boards, and other senior management positions crucial for shaping strategic directions and poli-
cies within energy organizations.
Furthermore, recent research emphasizes the detrimental effects of the gender gap on the
energy sector’s potential for innovation and economic growth [51]. Despite the recognition that
gender diversity enhances creativity and innovation, as well as all the benefits mentioned in Sec-
tion 2.1.1, the industry has paid limited attention to promoting diversity within its workforce
[52]. The past two decades of research have shown that including gender concerns in energy
sector decision-making can lead to improved development outcomes [51]. However, the limited
research on gender disparities in energy or environmental policymaking underscores the need
for further studies to better understand the impact of gender on energy policies and develop
effective strategies for promoting gender equality in the energy sector [52].
Also, empirical studies highlight a notable gender gap in decision-making roles within energy
companies, even in countries recognized as leaders in energy transformation, such as Sweden
[53]. Using a directional distance function (DDF) to assess technical inefficiencies, the study
identified persistent disparities across key indicators including employment, wages, senior man-
agement, and innovation. While countries with better gender balance have reduced wage gaps,
negative disparities persist in other areas, underscoring ongoing challenges. The research under-
scores the importance of targeted policies to enhance gender diversity and equality in the energy
sector, emphasizing inclusive strategies to bolster women’s participation and improve working
conditions. Addressing these disparities is critical not only for advancing gender equality but
also for maximizing the energy sector’s potential to drive sustainable development [54]
Moreover, the under-representation of women in entrepreneurship within the energy sector is
another significant gender gap. While women entrepreneurs comprise a significant percentage of
entrepreneurs overall, they remain underrepresented in the energy industry [55]. This suggests
that barriers to entry and gender bias may exist within the energy entrepreneurial landscape,
limiting women’s participation and contribution to the sector’s innovation and growth.

Legal Framework
This section offers an in-depth examination of the legal framework surrounding gender diver-
sity both in the European Union (subsection 3.1) and specific policies in the Nordic countries
(subsection 3.2). By analysing relevant laws and directives, it aims to understand the formal
mechanisms that support gender equality, and which ones can be adapted for the leadership
positions.
3.1
European Union Policies
The European Union has long been committed to the promotion of gender equality, recog-
nising it as a fundamental principle and a core value. The path towards gender equality in
Europe through legislative measures began in the early 1970s and has evolved significantly over
the decades. The EU legal framework for gender equality is laid out in Directive 2006/54/EC
[6], Directive 2010/41/EU [84], Directive 2014/95/EU [85], Directive 2022/2381 [85] and Di-
rective2023/970 (Article 9 and 10) [86]. In addition to these directives, the Gender Equality
Strategy 2020-2025 [87] and Goal 5 from SDG [88] are included in the framework due to their
significant role in shaping the policies and objectives related to gender equality, even though
they are not directives. These directives and strategies provide a strategic and comprehensive
approach to address gender discrimination, to promote equal opportunities and to ensure fair
treatment of all persons, irrespective of sex, in various fields, including employment.
Table 7 in Appendix Section 8 collects all the directives and strategies mentioned, as well
as, their goals and enforcement, if specified. It can be seen in Table 7 that nowadays, most
of the legislation is work-related, emphasizing how gender diversity can be promoted in the
labour market and specifically in the higher positions. For instance, it can be found Directive
2010/41/EU [84], which although it does not establish concrete targets or quotas, stresses the
importance of diversity in the professional environment. In similar terms, Directive 2010/41/EU
[84], arose in 2010, which guarantees that both men and women have equal opportunities in self-
employment activities. Directive 2010/41/EU [84] prohibits discrimination based on gender in
access to and supply of goods and services related to self-employment. It is a crucial step in
recognising and supporting the role of women entrepreneurs and self-employed females, thereby
promoting economic independence and gender equality in all forms of employment.
The directives also provide a legal framework for reporting non-financial and diversity-related
information. In 2014, Directive 2014/95/EU [85] was introduced, mandating large companies
and groups to report on their non-financial and diversity-related information. This directive
(2014/95/EU [85]) ensures that companies are transparent about their gender diversity status
and actively monitor and assess their progress. Together with the Gender Equality Strategy
2020-2025 [87] and the SDG 5 [88], these frameworks provide a robust foundation for legislative
efforts, fostering a more inclusive and equitable European society.

Finally, in 2022 was made the Directive 2022/2381 [7] which is particularly relevant to the
thesis topic since it includes specific and clear targets to increase female representation in lead-

the necessity for specific directives aimed at ensuring greater inclusion of women at the highest
levels of corporate and organizational governance.

Finland has a robust legal framework to promote gender equality, with the cornerstone being the

equality. Additionally, it requires public administration bodies, committees, advisory boards,
and other equivalent entities to have both women and men as members, with a minimum 40%
representation for each gender. This quota system is particularly significant for promoting gen-
der diversity in leadership positions, ensuring balanced representation at decision-making levels.

versity legal framework. Act 55/2001 [103], regulates employment relationships and protects
employees’ rights, providing clarity on contract terms and conditions.

Method
To gain in-depth insights into the experiences, perceptions, and challenges faced by female leaders
in the energy sector, we adopted a qualitative research approach. This involved conducting semi-
structured interviews with 13 female participants who currently occupy leadership positions in
energy companies across the five Nordic countries: Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway, and
Sweden.
4.1
Selection of the Participants
Participants were meticulously chosen based on their extensive career trajectories and involve-
ment in the energy sector. The selection process aimed to encompass a diverse array of female
leaders, representing various sub-sectors and energy sources within the industry, covering micro,
small, medium and large enterprises (see Table 13 in Appendix Section 8).
The decision to focus on female participants stems from a deliberate intent to delve into
the shaded experiences and perspectives unique to women in leadership roles. This approach
acknowledges the necessity of examining gender diversity through a lens that encapsulates the
lived experiences of women.
This thesis was conducted in collaboration with the Nordic Energy Equality Network (NEEN),
an organization established in 2017 to promote gender equality, diversity, and the empowerment
of women in the energy sector [117]. NEEN’s mission is to include diverse perspectives in the
sustainable energy transition, ensuring that women’s knowledge and experience are effectively
utilized. This collaboration was instrumental in providing access to a network of female leaders
across the Nordic countries, essential for the research.
With NEEN’s support, contacts from the five Nordic countries were facilitated. We reached
out to these potential participants from May 7th to June 7th. NEEN provided a total of 38
contacts, from which the following responses were received:
• Denmark: out of 13 contacts, 6 participants agreed to be interviewed.
• Iceland: out of 3 contacts, 1 participants agreed to be interviewed.
• Finland: out of 5 contacts, 2 participants agreed to be interviewed.
• Norway: out of 9 contacts, 2 participants agreed to be interviewed.
• Sweden: out of 8 contacts, 2 participants agreed to be interviewed.
Participants were contacted via email or LinkedIn, providing flexibility and accessibility in
the communication process. Many contacts did not respond, which accounts for the unaccepted
interview invitations. Additionally, one individual from Denmark expressed interest but had
limited availability, being only available in mid-July, which was outside the interview period.

4.3
Conduction of the Interviews
The data collection was conducted over a period of one month, from 18th May to 14th June.
During this time, semi-structured interviews were carried out, each lasting approximately 30
to 45 minutes. These interviews were designed to be flexible yet focused, allowing for in-depth
exploration of the topics while accommodating the unique perspectives and experiences of each
participant.
Each interview was conducted via Microsoft Teams, a platform chosen for its convenience
and reliability in facilitating remote conversations. With the interviewees’ consent, all sessions
were recorded to ensure we captured the dialogue accurately. The recording process was supple-
mented by the use of Otter, an application that generates transcripts of the conversations. This
dual approach—recording and transcription—ensured a detailed and precise documentation of
the interviews, which was important for later analysis.
The interviews allowed participants to share their insights freely while making sure that
key topics were covered. This method balanced getting specific information with allowing the
conversation to flow naturally, improving the quality of the data collected.

e. Feel patronised / Tasks given only due to gender
Following the challenges shown in Figure 7, it can be observed that 38% of the interviewed
women commented that they had felt patronized or even that they had received tasks only
because of their gender. This form of subtle discrimination manifests in various ways, often
undermining the professional capabilities and contributions of female leaders. Patronising can
be understood as the act of treating someone with an apparent kindness that betrays a feeling
of superiority [121]. It implies that the person being patronised is seen as less capable or knowl-
edgeable, leading to their opinions being undervalued or their skills being underestimated.
Tasks assigned based on gender stereotypes further exacerbate this issue. During the in-
terviews, the energy leaders often find themselves relegated to roles considered "suitable" for
their gender, such as administrative tasks or organizing team events, rather than technical as-
signments, as their own job positions are supposed to. This not only limits their professional
growth but also reinforces outdated gender norms within the workplace (q25 and q26 - Table 20).
Furthermore, during the interviews, leaders highlighted experiences of being assigned tasks
that felt gender-biased. For example, Leader 9 noted the prevalent expectation for women to
organize events or team-building activities, reinforcing stereotypes about nurturing roles rather
than leadership in technical areas (q27- Table 20). Similarly, Leader 7 described feeling patron-
ized in meetings, where male colleagues often assumed she lacked technical knowledge, high-
lighting broader biases in workplace interactions (q28 - Table 20).
These challenges not only limits their career development but also reinforces outdated stereo-
types, perpetuating a cycle of bias and inequality.
f. Need to constantly demonstrate skills
Closely associated with feeling patronized, female leaders in the Nordic energy sector highlighted
the constant need to prove their skills and capabilities. According to Figure 7, 31% of intervie-
wees reported experiencing this challenge. This challenge refers to the ongoing requirement for
women to consistently demonstrate their qualifications and abilities, often more than their male
counterparts, to gain recognition and respect in their professional roles.
Leaders emphasized the challenge of having their input undervalued in technical discussions,
particularly when compared to their male counterparts (q29 - Table 21). Leader 2 shared her
experience of needing to convince others of her competence as a chairman, a task she felt was
amplified due to her gender (q30 - Table 21). This sentiment was echoed by Leader 8, who

observed that women often need to exert more effort to gain similar levels of authority and

Leader 9 detailed a particularly challenging experience where she felt constantly undermined

g. Difficulty to be heard / h. Insecurity
In addition to the need to constantly demonstrate their skills, female leaders in the Nordic energy
industry face two other significant challenges: difficulty being heard and feelings of insecurity.
These challenges are interlinked, often stemming from the same underlying issues of gender bias.
Both challenges were highlighted by 23% of the leaders interviewed.
Women who need to continuously prove their value often find that their voices are not given
the same weight as their male counterparts’. For instance, the difficulty in being heard was
exemplified by Leader 5’s observation that male colleagues sometimes restate her ideas as their
own to gain acceptance from other men in the room, diminishing her contributions (q33 - Table

criticized based on perceptions of tone or assertiveness. She emphasized the double standard
where women are often penalized for behaviors that go unnoticed or even praised in men (q34
and q35 - Table 22).
This ongoing challenge to assert oneself and be heard can gradually undermine women’s con-
fidence, contributing to feelings of insecurity. Leader 9 described feeling undermined and always
having to validate her capabilities, which affected her self-assurance (q32 - Table 21). Leader 4
also expressed how these dynamics lead to self-doubt and questioning of her own abilities (q36

i. Retention of women in the sector
The second last challenge discussed in the interviews is the difficulty of retaining women in the

Despite advancements in gender equality, many women encounter ongoing obstacles that

j. Challenges with other women
The last challenge identified by the interviewees, though mentioned by only 15% of them, is the
difficulty of working with other women in the energy sector. This challenge includes experienc-

suggests that some women believe others should endure similar challenges, including instances

Leader 8 reflected on historical survival strategies among women in male-dominated fields.
She noted that in the past, many women in the energy industry adopted strategies to fit in and
succeed, often by aligning with male colleagues rather than supporting other women (q42 - Table
24). This approach did not encourage a culture of mutual support among women in the industry.
Such dynamics can worsen existing pressures and obstacles faced by women leaders. While
this challenge may not affect everyone, its impact on workplace dynamics and individual career

Policy Implications
This section discusses the policy implications of the study’s findings, including a summary of
key insights, relevance to current policies, recommendations for enhancing gender diversity in
leadership positions, and future research directions.
6.1
Summary of Key Findings
To address the issue of closing the gender gap in the energy industry, especially in leadership
roles, the role of women leaders in this context has been extensively explored. It has been ob-
served that, although there are different leadership styles among women and in comparison to
men, many studies highlight an inclination towards environmental and sustainable values, which
positively influences business performance, especially in the context of the energy sector.
In addition to this "green" approach, companies with more gender diversity in their manage-
ment teams have been found to show better results in innovation, problem solving and overall
organisational success. However, despite these positive findings on diverse leadership, the repre-
sentation of female leaders in the Nordic energy sector remains poor, which is partly attributed
to the low presence of females in the STEM fields crucial to this industry. For instance, the case
study at DTU highlighted a significant gender gap in graduates from key energy-related master’s
programs. In these fields, approximately 80% of graduates are male, while only around 20% (or
less) are female, with Environmental Engineering being the most gender-balanced exception.
In this work we intended to explain the lag through the prism of the legal framework, both
at European level and in the Nordic countries. The European Union has demonstrated a strong
commitment through various directives aimed at achieving gender parity and promoting gender
diversity. The Nordic countries, in particular, stand out for their progressive and pioneering ap-
proach, effectively implementing legislation to promote gender equality. It is note worthy that
this legal framework cover all spheres of society (education, domestic life, etc.), but also there
are specific directives to improve the gender equality situation in the workplace. For instance,
Directive 2022/2381 [7] directly promotes gender diversity on corporate boards. However, this
is the unique legislation targeting gender disparity in top corporate positions. Despite the ex-
tensive efforts of Europe and the Nordic countries to eliminate the gender gap, many of these
legislative measures lack practical details for implementation and set targets without precise
quotas.
Moving forward, practical research confirms that women are still significantly underrepre-
sented in high-responsibility roles within the Nordic energy industries. This study conducted
13 interviews to shed new light on how the legal framework affects women in the Nordic en-
ergy industry and to understand how mentalities evolve to obtain a deep understanding of the
challenges female leaders face or have faced related to their gender (Figure 7). These challenges
include feeling like a minority in a predominantly male environment, encountering biases, and
Page 52 of 104
==PAGE:55==
Energy Economics and Modelling
DTU Management
dealing with job offers in the energy sector that predominantly target men. Interviewees, par-
ticularly those with longer careers in the industry, often feel patronized or constantly need to
prove their knowledge in ways that are not expected of their male counterparts. Notably, these
challenges are widely shared across companies, regardless of their size or country of origin, and
do not seem to be polarized.
This work has identified several strategies to address these difficulties and promote gender
equality in the Nordic energy sector (Figure 8). These include promoting role models and men-
tors for women, both young girls and women already working in the company. This can be
achieved through mentoring programs within energy companies or by bringing female leaders
to schools and high schools to give talks and create a positive, contagious impact. Ensuring
gender diversity in recruitment processes from the beginning of careers in the energy sector is
also crucial. This can involve changing the language in job advertisements and seeking candi-
dates outside the company to increase diversity. Other effective strategies include the creation of
networks and the promotion of gender diversity in STEM education, which is key to cultivating
a pool of qualified female candidates for the energy industry. A controversial but potentially
effective measure is the introduction of legislation that proactively promotes gender equality.
Finally, improving diversity in the workplace, not only in terms of gender, can contribute to fos-
tering a more inclusive work environment and better results by having a wide range of opinions
from different backgrounds, which would also be conducive to more sustainable action.
Overall, gender diversity directives in the legal framework are a good start to promoting
diversity on corporate boards. However, there are still challenges that need to be tackled, as
outlined in the strategies proposed. Enhancing gender diversity in energy leadership positions
not only brings inclusivity to the industry but also fosters sustainable outcomes.

6.3
Recommendations and Strategies for Enhancing Gender Diversity in
Leadership Positions
To foster a more inclusive and equitable energy sector, it is crucial to implement targeted recom-
mendations and strategies that enhance gender diversity in leadership positions. The following
measures aim to break down existing barriers and promote the advancement of women in the
industry. These strategies have been developed based on suggestions from interviews, but pri-
marily reflect own contributions as part of the master’s thesis research.
(a) Increasing the Visibility of Women Leaders
• Interactive talks in schools/ high schools: Organize talks in schools and high schools
showcase examples of female leaders in the energy industry to inspire the younger
generation.
• Excursions and visits: Arrange visits to offices and industries, as well as organize
school trips to energy plants, to introduce students to the sector from an early age.
• Greater visibility in panels and conferences: Ensure the presence of women in panels
and conferences so they feel represented and supported in the sector.
• Women’s networks: Promote the creation of networks and support groups for women
in the energy industry where they can share experiences, challenges, and successes.
• Algorithm adjustment in social media: Modify algorithms on social media platforms
popular among teenagers to prioritize content featuring female leaders in STEM fields.
By ensuring that young users encounter these role models regularly, we aim to spark
their interest and inspire them to consider careers in STEM.
(b) Stakeholder Involvement
• Collaboration with gender equality departments: Increase collaboration with gender
equality departments in governments and energy ministries, and not only rely on
general gender equality direcitves or/and acts.
• Collaborative opportunities: Public-private partnerships between governments, uni-
versities, and energy companies aim to develop targeted educational programs. These
include internships, practical courses in collaboration with industry, and mentorship
programs to engage female students in leadership roles, fostering their career advance-
ment in the energy sector.
• Discussion forums: Organize forums and roundtables to discuss the challenges and
strategies for improving gender diversity in the energy sector, in particular in the
leadershop positions, involving all relevant stakeholders in various settings, whether
within companies or universities.
(c) Work-Life Balance and Flexibility
• Remote work options: Offer remote work options to facilitate balancing personal and
professional life without having to sacrifice one for the other.
Page 57 of 104
==PAGE:60==
Energy Economics and Modelling
DTU Management
• Flexible working arrangements: Implement flexible working hours to facilitate a better
integration of family and work life.
• Job sharing in senior positions: Allow two or more individuals to share responsibil-
ities at high levels, such as in the C-suite, ensuring a balanced workload and equal
contributions.
• Additional benefits: Provide benefits such as on-site childcare or subsidies for child-
care services.
• Parental equality policies: Promote policies that encourage both parents to share
childcare responsibilities, such as implementing equal parental leave. These policies
aim to ensure that neither parent has to compromise their career advancement due
to childcare duties, fostering a more balanced approach to parental responsibilities
within the workforce.
(d) Anti-Bias Training
• Continuous training: Conduct workshops on gender equality and unconscious bias
training for all levels of the company.
• Commitment from top management: Ensure that top management is committed
and actively participates in these training sessions, rather than making it a sole
responsibility of the human resources department.
• Impact evaluations: Assess the impact of the training through surveys and employee
feedback to continuously adjust and improve the programs.
(e) Legislation
• Specific laws: Advocate for specific laws in the energy sector that incentives the hiring
and promotion of women in leadership positions.
• Mandatory reporting: Establish mandatory public reporting of gender statistics in
leadership roles for all companies in the energy sector.
(f) Legislation
• Specific laws: Advocate for specific laws in the energy sector that incentives the hiring

• Mandatory reporting: Establish mandatory public reporting of gender statistics in
leadership roles for all companies in the energy sector.
• Directives for visibility: Implement directives requiring companies to ensure a min-
imum representation of both genders in panels and conferences featuring industry
leaders.
• Educational outreach: Introduce directives mandating schools to host STEM female
leaders regularly to inspire young women to pursue leadership roles in the energy
sector.

(g) Improvement of National Statistics
• Employee tenure statistics: Governments should provide comprehensive statistics on
employee tenure in companies to better understand and address the retention of

• Career progression timelines: National statistics should include data on the time it

In order to advance the understanding and practices related to gender diversity and leadership
within the energy sector, particularly in the Nordic countries, several key areas for future re-
search are proposed.
Firstly, addressing the current limitations in statistical data is crucial. Presently, there exists
a significant lack of specific statistics concerning gender diversity within the energy industry, es-
pecially when data is not disaggregated by key sectors such as renewable energy and fossil fuels.
Enhancing data collection efforts, potentially through collaborations between national govern-
ments and European bodies, could provide more robust insights. This could involve comparing
the career trajectories of male and female energy students as they transition into leadership roles,
and analyzing the timeframes required for energy sector employees to advance into leadership
positions, categorized by gender. Such comparative analyses would not only fill existing data
gaps but also lay the foundation for evidence-based policy interventions aimed at promoting

gender equality in leadership roles within the energy sector.
Secondly, conducting multi-university studies across various academic institutions offering
energy-related programs would be beneficial. These studies could explore and compare gender
disparities in leadership positions, identifying systemic barriers that hinder female representation
and advancement in the energy sector. By uncovering these barriers and understanding their
underlying causes, such research initiatives could inform targeted interventions and strategies
aimed at fostering inclusive leadership environments within the industry.
Moving forward, expanding the candidate pool for research purposes is imperative. This
expansion should encompass individuals from diverse generational backgrounds and varying
lengths of experience in the energy sector. Such diversity would offer deeper insights into the
experiences and perceptions related to gender diversity and leadership, facilitating an assessment
of evolving trends over time. Additionally, incorporating male perspectives in interviews would
provide a comprehensive understanding of gender dynamics in leadership roles. Moreover, en-
suring diversity across nationality, ethnicity, age, and background among research participants
would enrich the breadth of perspectives considered in future studies.
Lastly, longitudinal studies are crucial for research, as they involve the continuous obser-
vation of variables over an extended period to track changes and developments. They enable
ongoing monitoring of changes in gender representation and leadership roles across various sec-
tors of the energy industry over time. By identifying evolving trends and effective practices
that support sustainable diversity initiatives, longitudinal studies significantly contribute to en-
hancing organizational strategies and policies aimed at promoting gender diversity in leadership.
Overall, these proposed future research directions aim to address current gaps in knowledge,
inform policy development, and contribute to the advancement of gender diversity and leadership
practices within the energy sector.

Conclusions
" This thesis studied the gender gap in leadership roles within the energy sector, with a particular
focus on the Nordics. Through a comprehensive examination of empirical data, interviews, and
desk studies, this research aimed to show the share of females in this sector and the lack of data
missing to unravel the complexities surrounding female leadership in Nordic energy companies
and contribute to understanding how these challenges can be effectively addressed.
Firstly, this study through literature review underscores the significant contributions women
make to sustainability due to their environmentally conscious mindset and diverse leadership
styles. Despite this potential, empirical data confirms a substantial gender gap persists within
the energy sector. Moreover, there remains a lack of historical and current data regarding the
representation of women in specific roles within the industry. This data deficiency not only hin-
ders accurate assessment of gender diversity but also impedes targeted efforts to address these
disparities effectively.
Furthermore, the European and Nordic legal frameworks on gender diversity lay essential
foundations for achieving gender equality across various sectors, including leadership positions.
However, while these frameworks have led to progress, they often lack specific quotas to improve
female representation in the energy sector specifically.
Interviews with current female leaders in the Nordic energy sector have illustrated the chal-
lenges to be promoted in this industry. Biases, traditionally male-dominated roles, and the dif-
ficulty of balancing work and family life continue to impede women’s advancement to leadership
positions. Female leaders also report feeling a minority in a predominantly male environment,
experiencing patronisation and being assigned tasks based solely on their gender. Addressing
these multifaceted challenges is imperative to enhance the retention and progression of women
in the energy sector.
These challenges are consistent with findings in existing literature and are reinforced by our
interview methodology. We also identified strategies to effectively narrow the gender gap in
the energy industry, particularly in leadership roles, such as visibility of female leaders in the
industry as role models. This approach allows future generations to see themselves represented
and encourages them to study STEM disciplines relevant to the energy sector.
In conclusion, while progress has been made in improving gender diversity within Nordic
energy leadership, there is still considerable work to be done. Advances in legal frameworks
and heightened corporate awareness are positive steps forward.
However, implementing the
strategies outlined in this study is crucial to achieving genuine gender equality and diversity in
energy leadership. Gender diversity not only fosters innovation and sustainable practices but
also reflects a commitment to equity and inclusivity in the broader energy industry. "
