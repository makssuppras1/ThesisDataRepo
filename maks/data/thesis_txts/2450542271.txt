Abstract
The performance and potential of an Ice-source heat pump system was investigated for
the provision of space heat to a household located in Denmark, in two sections.
In
the ﬁrst section, the supercooling phenomenon, which is a characteristic of the ISHP,
was investigated theoretically and with the help of a transient numerical model of a
plate heat exchanger. The temperature diﬀerence of 1.95 K obtained in the model was
observed to be higher than the diﬀerence of 0.75 K obtained across the actual heat
exchanger. This suggested that the heat exchanger model was undersized as compared
to the actual component. The instability of the supercooled ﬂow due to freezing in the
heat exchanger was observed to be deferred by increasing the mass ﬂow rate of water and
by decreasing the mass ﬂow of the glycol-water mixture. For the experiments, an annual
heating demand of 55 kWh/m2 was simulated to be supplied for, on two chosen days in
February of a TMY. The two days were characterised by sub-zero ambient temperatures
and high and low solar irradiation respectively.
The ISHP system was operated for
a duration of 8 hours using 3-hour averages of ambient conditions and heat demand
occurring in the entire day. Inﬂuence of additional sewage heat was also investigated
by considering a typical hot water draw-oﬀproﬁle in a European household. A control
strategy to ensure the smooth functioning and switching of components via a computer
program was developed and implemented in the experiments. A COP in the range of
3.2 to 4.7 was observed for the heat pump for storage tank temperatures ranging from 0
◦C to 20 ◦C respectively. Solar fractions of 68 % and 47 % were obtained on a clear and
cloudy day respectively. An ice fraction of 54 % was obtained for an ice slurry volume
of 88 litres, resulting in a mass of 44 kg of ice. The storage tank of such a system was
observed to be around 50 % smaller for a 50 % ice fraction, than an equivalent sensible
heat storage volume, due to the utilisation of latent heat of fusion of water.

1. Introduction
1.1. Background
The energy consumption across the globe has grown disproportionately over the past few
decades. The culmination of the industrial age has enabled consumers all over the world
to gain access to commodities which have become a part of their daily lifestyles. These
commodities have been proven to consume a large amount of energy and most of it, from
perishable resources. Up until the advent of regulations imposed by governments, these
commodities or technologies were developed with emphasis laid on the cost of provision
rather than the energy consumption involved in its life cycle [1].
One such commodity is heat, which is a necessity in countries exposed to ambient tem-
peratures colder than comfortable living temperatures in households and other live-able
spaces. Space heating has been known to be provided by sources such as natural gas,
fuel oil, coal, biomass and other fossil fuels which are well known to contribute largely
to the emission of CO2 in the atmosphere and currently, majorly dwarf the renewable
sources such as PV, solar thermal, wind and hydro. The percentage of these renewable
sources used for heating and cooling in the EU account for a meagre 18 % of the overall
primary energy consumption. Currently, the heating and cooling sector comprises 50 %
of the annual energy consumption in the EU. This is largely due to the ineﬃcient use
of the above-mentioned sources to provide the necessary heat or cold in spaces. The
heating systems used in the existing buildings in the EU comprise gas boilers, direct
electric heaters, oil boilers or coal boilers, a large portion of which are already older
than their technical lifetimes. Almost half of the boilers used in the buildings have an
eﬃciency lower than 60 % [2]. The EU plans to curb this ineﬃciency by implementing
the Heating and Cooling strategy which ensures energy security in the member countries
while satisfying the COP 21 directive to reduce GHG emissions in order to control the
rise of the global temperature to well below 2◦C above pre-industrial levels [3]. Through
this strategy, the EU plans to reduce the emissions and overall imports by making the
sector more energy eﬃcient.
In Denmark, due to the oil crisis in the latter half of the 20th century, central dis-
trict heating systems were expanded to provide heat to most households in the urban
areas [4], restricting the use of individual boilers to remote places not connected to the
DH network. The heat provided by these DH networks is known to be produced either
through combined heat and power plants powered by coal and biomass or through large
solar heating plants in regions with favourable sunlight hours. With the 2020 targets

for the EU in mind and as a result of the policies and strategies put in place, the power
mix in Denmark is expected to be constituted by 50 % wind power by 2020, leading to
a larger contribution of renewable power in the country. This works in favour of using a
technology such as the Heat Pump, which has been predicted to occupy a much larger
portion in the future energy scenario for Denmark [5]. Heat Pump can be understood
to be the reciprocal of a refrigeration system, wherein heat from a low temperature heat
source is absorbed and delivered to a high temperature heat sink, which is the space
to be heated. Some of the heat sources include air, geothermal heat, groundwater etc.
For locations with ambient temperatures below 0◦C in the winters, an Air Source Heat
Pump (ASHP) can face diﬃculties in operation due to the formation of frost over the
evaporator tubes interacting with the outside air, leading to a reduction in the perfor-
mance and often, a halt in operation. In the case of availability of large space, the pipes
required for the Ground Source Heat Pump can be accommodated by digging trenches
about a meter in depth. But, for domestic household applications, it is often required to
dig vertically rather than horizontally, needing bores of about 9 to 16 metres in depth
which can be expensive and cumbersome. In addition, the tubes need to contain an anti-
freezing mixture along with water in regions with ambient temperatures falling below
0◦C [6]. Thus, as a result of the lack of availability of heat sources during the winters,
alternative avenues with regard to the use of convenient anti-freezing mixtures and nat-
urally occurring and manufactured heat sources are looked at in ongoing research [7].
Although there may be a lack of naturally occurring heat sources in the winter, a large
amount of heat generated as a result of industrial or household operations is wasted to
the atmosphere. According to studies referenced by the EU, about 20 - 50 % of the
overall energy consumption by the industries is discharged as waste heat [8]. This heat
is typically low grade in nature of a temperature below 200◦C. It is found that about
66 % of the waste heat generated is low grade in nature. Although this low grade heat
generated in the industrial sector is at a much higher temperature than the expected
temperature ranges for space heating, similar low grade heat is discharged from house-
holds in the form of sewage water, which is expected to vary between 10 −20◦C. This
heat has found use in households in Switzerland, where heat pumps are used to further
convert this low grade heat into desired space heating temperatures [9].
Another study focusing on predicting optimal future energy scenarios for Denmark
emphasises the need for smart integration of heat pumps in the Danish energy system
to achieve pre-deﬁned environmental targets [10]. Like other studies referred previously,
it also ﬁnds heat pumps to play a vital role in the future due to the predicted expansion
of wind power in Denmark. Complimentary to the DH network, individual heat sources
are found to comprise almost 36 % of the residential heating demand. Along with ASHP
and GSHP, individual solar heating systems were slotted to increase their contribution
from about 20 % in 2020 to almost 36 % in 2050. This solution was observed to be
an inexpensive solution for the 2050 scenario. But the practicability of such systems
was also observed to be a function of the available area [10]. Apart from this, one of

the major problems encountered in using solar heating systems in domestic households
is the storage of heat for periods when there is a lack of availability of suﬃcient solar
radiation. This is partially eradicated by the installation of heat storage tanks. Heat
storage tanks used in solar heating systems are usually well insulated containers made of
stainless steel, of a volume enough to provide domestic hot water and heating to a typical
household. The size of the tanks varies with the number of occupants to be provided
with, with larger tanks installed for multi-family dwellings. Usually, the recommended
size of storage tanks in Europe is also based on the size of the solar collector installed, of
about 50 l/m2 of collector area. As per the Task 26 IEA report on solar combisystems,
21 tanks varying in size from about 300 to 3000 litres were compared and discussed [11].
Combistore systems satisfy both, the space heating and the domestic hot water demands
of the households. The heat storages are either speciﬁc heat, phase change or chemical
in nature. Speciﬁc heat storages utilise a change in the temperature to store heat. A
favourable sensible heat storage material possesses a high speciﬁc heat capacity along
with a high density. Water with a speciﬁc heat capacity of 4.18 kJ/kg-K and a density
of 1000 kg/m3, proves to be the most attractive heat storage material for sensible heat
storages. Latent heat storages or phase change storages, on the other hand, operate
at a constant temperature and utilise the solar radiation to cause a change in phase,
typically from a solid to liquid state by absorbing the latent heat of fusion. This energy
stored is much larger than the sensible heat stored in the material [12]. As an example,
the latent heat of fusion of water is equivalent to the energy required to cause almost
a 80◦C change in the temperature. But, since the melting point of water under normal
atmospheric conditions is 0◦C, it cannot be used to provide heat directly.
1.2. Scope
To eliminate the problem of scarcity of heat sources in the winter, the possibility of
storing heat in ice while utilising the latent heat of fusion of water, is explored in this
study. Along with heat from the solar collectors, a possibility of utilising waste heat from
sewage water released from a household will also be looked at as a possible heat source.
Experiments involving the use of a solar thermal collector simulator as a heat source for
an ice-source heat pump will be carried out. Evaluating the performance of the heat
pump will be of utmost interest in order to compare with existing technologies using
other heat sources. It will also be of major interest to investigate if this system poses
any advantages with regard to the energy eﬃciency in comparison with conventional
solar heating systems. Since the use of ice presents a possibility of reduction in storage
heat storage volumes, a comparison of required storage sizes with conventional water-
based systems will also be conducted. Since this study focuses on the application of
a heat pump for ice slurry, possibilities of the usage of other heat sources will also be
discussed.

heat storage is presented in this section, with observations and inferences from literature

2.1.1. Heat Pumps
Heat pumps, as mentioned brieﬂy in the previous chapter, is a host of technologies that
lift heat from a low temperature to a high temperature.
Such systems work on the
principle of the second law of thermodynamics as proposed by Clausius. This law states
”It is impossible for any system to operate in such a way that the sole result would be
an energy transfer by heat from a cooler to a hotter body.” [13].
Figure 2.1.: Heat Pump Cycle [14]

A heat pump operates on a vapor compression cycle, which is the typical principle
of operation of a refrigeration system. Vapor compression operated heat pumps were
ﬁrst sighted, only with high capacities, in early twentieth century in Los Angeles and in
Zurich. In the last few decades, heat pumps with lower capacities have been observed
all over Europe and the USA, with countries such as France, Sweden, Spain, Italy and
Finland leading the regions with the highest number of units [15]. A vapor compression
heat pump/refrigeration system comprises four essential components as shown in Fig.
2.1, viz. compressor, condenser, expansion valve and an evaporator. In the scenario of a
heat pump, the working ﬂuid known as the refrigerant rejects the required heat through
a heat exchanger known as the condenser to the concerned space at a high pressure. This
ﬂuid is then throttled or expanded in an expansion valve to a low pressure corresponding
to its vaporisation temperature. This throttled ﬂuid is then added with heat from a heat
source in a heat exchanger called as the evaporator. In order for the vaporised ﬂuid to
be cooled to a liquid, it needs to pressurised to a level corresponding to its condensing
temperature. This is done in the compressor. The compressor is the only component in
the cycle which consumes energy to provide work.
Performance of a Heat Pump
Heat Pumps are evaluated based on the ratio between the amount of heat transferred
from the condenser to the desired space, and the power consumed by the compressor
to provide the necessary work input. This work input is the work required to lift the
refrigerant from the evaporation pressure to the condensation pressure corresponding
to the evaporation and condensation temperatures respectively. The vapor compression
cycle using R134a as the refrigerant is shown in a Logp-h diagram in ﬁgure 2.2 for a
temperature diﬀerence of 5K between the evaporation temperature and the ambient, and
the condensation temperature and the demand side respectively. In a logP-h diagram,
enthalpy is measured along the X-axis in kJ/kg and pressure on a logarithmic scale along
the Y-axis measured in bar. The red curves on the graph represent isotherms or constant
temperature lines in ◦C.
The heat source temperature was assumed to be at 0◦C with the desired supply tem-
perature assumed to be 35◦C. The resulting evaporation and condensation temperatures
were −5◦C and 40◦C respectively, and are represented by the cycle in blue in ﬁgure 2.2.
The condenser heat output, ˙Qcond is equal to the latent heat of vaporisation released to
the demand side in addition to the sensible heat released to cool the refrigerant down to
the condensation temperature from the outlet temperature of the compressor. The heat

2.1.2. Solar Thermal Collectors
The solar energy incident on the earth’s surface can be utilised using collectors of various
types depending on the application. Solar thermal collectors are used to transfer the
heat incident, to either a thermal storage or the demand side domestic water or space
to heated. The irradiation incident on the earth’s surface is often measured in terms of
a horizontal surface. To correct this value for a sloped collector, the following variables
are often deﬁned for a particular collector [16].
Latitude [φ]
The angular location to the north or south of the equator is known as the latitude. The
value of the latitude lies between −90◦and 90◦, where positive values lie in the north
and negative in the south.
Declination [δ]
The angular position of the sun at solar noon with respect to the plane of the equator
is known as solar declination. The value always lies between −23.45◦and 23◦, with
locations in north having positive values.
Slope [β]
The angle between the surface of the collector in consideration and the horizontal is the
slope of the collector. The value lies between 0◦and 180◦. If the value is above 90◦, it
means that the collector is facing downwards.
Azimuth angle [γ]
The angle between the projection of a normal to the surface of the collector on a horizon-
tal plane and the local meridian is called the azimuth angle of the collector. A collector
facing south has an azimuth angle of 0◦, −90◦facing east and 90◦facing west.
Hour angle [ω]
The angular displacement of the sun to the east or west of the local meridian due to
the rotation of the earth on its axis is called the hour angle. All values before noon are
negative, while after noon, positive.
Angle of incidence [θ]
The angle between the beam radiation on a surface of a collector and its normal is called
the angle of incidence.

2.2.1. Supercooling
Supercooling or undercooling is the phenomemon of cooling a liquid below its freezing
point. This phenomenon is observed in most liquids and is certainly a characteristic of
water. Water, if cooled slowly, naturally continues to exist as a liquid well below its
freezing point, before it experiences nucleation driving its temperature up to 0◦C, even-
tually freezing. Although experiments investigating this phenomenon have been carried
out since the beginning of the 20th century, the occurrence of the same has been known
since well before [36] [37].
This phenomenon has importance in both, natural and industrial or human-centric
occurrences. In nature, the cause of hail or snow formation or the freezing of water
bodies such as rivers, seas and lakes can be attributed to supercooling of water. Knowl-

scroll compressor. Both, the evaporator and the condenser used in the ISHP are plate
heat exchangers. The ISHP also consists of an intermediate glycol loop which circulates
between the evaporator and the supercooling heat exchanger. The glycol in the inter-
mediate loop absorbs heat from the supercooled water in the heat exchanger and rejects
it to the refrigerant in the evaporator. The supercooling HX cools the incoming water
which then proceeds towards the ultrasound crystalliser. In the case that the water is
cooled to a temperature below 0◦C, the crystalliser induces nucleation in the ﬂow leading
to the formation of ice slurry which ﬂows to the storage tank. The storage tank, made
of stainless steel, is cylindrical in shape and contains 4 ports at the side which can be
used as inlet and outlet ports for the solar collector loop and inlet and outlet for the
direct heating loop to the demand side, respectively. A port at the bottom of the tank
allows for drainage. The outlet for the ﬂow of water into the supercooling HX is located
at the bottom of the tank which follows the ﬁlter, also present at the bottom. The water
ﬂowing into the supercooling HX is drawn from the bottom of the storage tank through
two ﬁlters. First ﬁlter at the bottom of the tank prevents the ﬂow of impurities or for-
eign particles alongwith the water and the second ﬁlter prevents the inﬁltration of ice
crystals into heat exchanger. The pump maintains the ﬂow rate of the water going into
the heat exchanger at about 3.005 m3/hr. This ﬂow rate helps maintain the stability of
the supercooled ﬂow and prevents freezing inside the heat exchanger.

The ISHP was regulated using a computer program built using the software Agilent Pro

designed for instrument control. VEE relies on ﬂowchart based modelling as opposed to

5. Discussion & Future Work
This study has been carried out in two sections, one investigating the supercooling HX
used in the heat pump and two, the performance of such a heat pump for the provision of
a space heating demand. Both analyses were carried out with certain assumptions and
considerations which can be expected to yield diﬀering results from the ones obtained.
The numerical model for the supercooling HX was carried out based on the assump-
tions of the geometry and without regarding the heat pump side of the glycol loop. This
consideration can prove to be a signiﬁcant factor in the supercooling degrees achieved
as there is an added constraint of the demand being satisﬁed along with achieving sat-
isfactory phase transition in the evaporator. This also allows for a better control on the
parameters such as temperature and mass ﬂows on the glycol side. Thus, an extension
to the present model integrating the heat pump cycle can be expected to provide more
accurate results. Another assumption concerning the HX, is the consideration of one
average temperature for each side along the entire length of the HX. In reality, this tem-
perature varies along the length and thus presents varying heat transfer characteristics
at each section. Such a discretized mdoe can be crucial in understanding the conditions
propagating nucleating areas within the HX, providing an opportunity to better control
freezing of the supercooled ﬂow.
Extending on the point of understanding stable conditions for the ﬂow of supercooled
liquids, speciﬁcally water, a clearer deﬁnition of equations determining the limits of op-
eration is a necessity. During the period of this study, other methods to determine the
freezing of a supercooled ﬂow were also reviewed, such as the study conducted by Arora
et al. This study only focuses on turbulent ﬂows having Reynolds numbers higher than
3000 and thus could not be applied to the ﬂow in question, as the ﬂow in question was
always laminar. Further, the method requires the deﬁnition of a nucleation temperature
of water which is an ambiguous value because of the random nature of crystallisation
observed in supercooled ﬂows. A study conducted by Gilpin suggests −5.1◦C as the
nucleation temperature for quiescent water in drainage pipes. But this can greatly diﬀer
from the nucleation temperatures observed in a ﬂow of supercooled water. The method
employed on the numerical model requires a maximum degree of supercooling to be input
for the check to be performed. This is subject to the assumptions made and a higher
degree of supercooling set would result in larger durations of stable operation. Thus,
although this exercise provides indicative results, but, in order to develop systems that
can facilitate such ﬂow more predictably, further work in deriving such equations can
help in the development of similar systems.

The Ice Source Heat Pump, as can be observed from the results, can certainly prove
to be an attractive proposition in the space heating domain, especially in countries with
cold climates. The lack of solar radiation has a signiﬁcantly lower eﬀect on the running
time of such a HP as compared to existing heat pump technologies. This availability
is further accentuated during days with ambient temperatures below 0◦C, where ASHP
are likely to momentarily stop to run the defrosting cycle. Such conditions demand the
highest amount of heat and any stoppage in its provision is uncalled for. That being said,
certain modiﬁcations to the current system can greatly enhance its performance and the
available hours. To begin with, the current system used a single speed compressor which
allows the system to only operate as an ON/OFF system. As can be seen from the
results in section 4.2, due to the frequent occurrence of part-loads, the use of a variable
speed/frequency compressor can greatly reduce the energy use at times when the storage
temperature is high enough, still satisfying the demand. The variable speed systems can
operate at lower capacities for lower heating loads by modulating the speed at which
the compressor operates. This results in a lower mass ﬂow of refrigerant entering the
condenser, allowing better utilisation of the heat transfer area of the heat exchanger.
This renders the heat exchanger hypothetically oversized for the part-load condition,
enabling lower temperature diﬀerences to be achieved between the heat exchanger and
the surroundings. This, in addition to the reduced compressor work, can improve the
COP. Further, the large energy consumed as a result of peak currents experienced during
start-ups, proves the ineﬃciency of the ON/OFF systems which require multiple start-
ups, unlike variable speed systems which operate for longer durations without switching
oﬀ. This also helps maintain the air circulation within the room unlike ON/OFF systems.
The level sensors used in the tank were standard plastic level switches which were seen
to ﬂuctuate often due to the unpredictable formation of ice around them. Use of sturdier
and stable switches would greatly reduce the risk of consecutive start and stops, in spite
of the control strategy being implemented to prevent the same. The height at which
these sensors are placed also plays a role in the operation time of such a system. The
aim was to optimise the running time and the restart time, yet allowing for a signiﬁcant
of amount of ice to be melted before restart.
The collector area used for this study was 8 m2. This could be expected to be the
higher limit observed for houses of such small areas. In addition to this, better segre-
gation of available data in terms of beam radiation and diﬀused radiation can expect
to further lower the solar energy output from the collectors. The process used in this
study assumes a global radiation whereas, an assumption of completely diﬀused for a
cloudy day and beam radiation for a clear day would yield lower availability of the heat
pump and possibly increase the portion of auxiliary input required to provide the deﬁcit
in demand. Thus, the use of such a HP with actual solar collectors would be interesting
to investigate in the future. The results highlight the need for auxiliary heating on a
cloudy day. But it is also necessary to choose the least energy consuming auxiliary unit
to support such a system. The heat pump can be seen to function smoothly throughout

The location of the dwelling plays an important role in the type of heat source avail-
able to be tapped. For highly dense cities and towns, the installation of solar collectors
is infeasible due to constraints in available space.
Thus, tapping solar heat in such
places becomes diﬃcult and an impractical solution. In such cases, the consumers are
more likely to go with air source heat pumps which have a lower footprint and are also
cheaper. Consumers further away from the city with more space available can resort to
using ground source heat pumps or solar assisted heat pumps with thermal storages. The
air source heat pumps can prove to be cheaper than the solar assisted or ground-source
heat pumps, but tend to operate at lower eﬃciencies. In such cases, when space is not
a constraint, an investigation on the payback times of these three technologies can help
in choosing the right option. Although, SAHP and GSHP have high capital costs, the
energy savings achieved over their lifetimes can oﬀset these costs. These energy savings
can be improved by utilising a phase-change thermal storage such as an ice storage. The
reduction in size of the storage can also help in reducing the cost of such heat pumps
making them more favourable. Often, the use of a variety of heat sources is more feasible
for heat pumps integrated in district heating networks due to the investment and the
scale of the setup. For such heat pumps, locations close to the coast are more suited to
using seawater as a heat source. Additionally, using the existing infrastructure of the
sewage system to use sewage heat as a heat source for heat pumps can also be beneﬁcial
in satisfying the heat demand of communities. Thus, integration of an ISHP for larger
capacities can improve the availability of heat pumps in the DH network and ensure a
security of supply in sub-zero ambient temperatures. An economic evaluation of ISHP
with regard to other systems would therefore be an interesting topic for future studies.
The nature of ice build-up in the tank is also subject to the design of the tank. The
ISHP was operated once with no modiﬁcations to the tank, and for the experiments,
with thin steel plates immersed in the water above the top level sensor for support.
The running time for the conﬁguration without any support while generating ice, was
65 mins. While, the addition of the support, delayed the termination of the system by
about 12 mins., running for a total of 77 mins. This suggests the need for a smart design
of the storage tank for such a system storing ice. This change in the running times
can be attributed to the large water content of the ice slurry. With the addition of the
supports, the all the ice from the slurry is kept at the top by the support while allow-
ing the water to ﬂow downwards. Such collection areas for the ice, without inﬂuencing
the ﬂow of heat inwards through the solar loop, can help prolong operation times. A
study focusing on minimizing the dead volume in the tank would be fruitful in further
improving the energy storage capacity of such a latent heat storage. Also, determining
the ice content in the tank more accurately can provide a better understanding of the
size of the tank that can be used.

6. Conclusion
The study presented in this thesis focussed on the potential and application of the ice-
source heat pump utilising solar heat in the space heating domain.
The stability of the supercooling heat exchanger was investigated by means of a nu-
merical model of a plate heat exchanger. By varying the controllable parameters such
as the mass ﬂow rate of the glycol-water mixture and water, the delay in reaching an
unstable region was checked. It was concluded from this analysis that decreasing the
mass ﬂow of the glycol mixture and similarly, increasing the mass ﬂow of water can
defer the instability occurring in a supercooled ﬂow. Upon comparison with the actual
system, a higher temperature diﬀerence of 1.95 K was noted across the modelled HX,
suggesting that the modelled HX was undersized as compared to the actual HX.
The COP of the heat pump was observed to vary from 3.2 to 4.7 for storage tank
temperatures varying from 0◦C to 20◦C respectively.
The minimum COP of 3.4 for
such a system with a single speed and low capacity compressor can only be expected
to improve with a variable speed compressor with a higher capacity. Thus, it can be
concluded that this performance forms the lower limit of such a heat pump. Also based
on the operation, it can be veriﬁed that the control strategy implemented for such a
system is appropriate and enables smooth functioning and switching of the components.
The addition of sewage heat cannot be considered to be feasible due to the low amount
of heat tapped in a day compared to the investment of setting up such a system. For
larger networks, however, it can be a favourable option.
An ice fraction of 54 % was obtained for a volume of 88 litres of ice slurry in the
tank. This resulted in a mass of 44 kg. of ice. The use of ice slurry is seen to reduce
the thermal storage size comprehensively. A 50 % ice fraction in the tank reduces the
sensible heat storage volume by nearly half. Thus, it can be inferred that optimising the
volume of ice accumulated in the tank can further increase the amount of energy stored
in such a tank. Further research in the optimal design of an ice storage tank with ice
collection areas and accurate methods in determining the ice content in a storage with
ice slurry are proposed.
