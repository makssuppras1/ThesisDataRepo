Thermodynamic modeling of the solubility of pharmaceuticals with the PC-SAFT EOS
Abstract
In this work a simpliﬁed solubility equation has been used to be able to model
the solubility of pharmaceuticals. The Pertubed Chain Statistical Associat-
ing Fluid Theory (PC-SAFT) Equation of State (EoS) has been applied to
calculate the activity coeﬃcient as a function of the temperature and the sol-
ubility. The pure component parameters were estimated by a minimization
function that would minimize the diﬀerence between the calculated solubility
and the experimental solubility, by altering the parameters. The experimental
solubility’s that were used in the parameter estimation were all from solubility
data in pure solvents. These parameters was when used to model the solu-
bility in both pure and mixed solvents. Additionally some systems required a
interaction parameter between the solvent and the drug to be able to model
it properly. In these cases a constant or a temperature dependent interaction
parameter would be estimated based on the pure solvent data.
The pharmaceutical drugs that have been investigated are: paracetamol, naproxen,
ibuprofen, azithromycin and theophylline. For all of these pharmaceuticals at
least one ternary mixture (2 solvents + drug) has been included. The solvents
that have been used are water, methanol, ethanol and acetone. Benzene and
toulune solubility data have also been used for the parameter estimation to
have a hydrophobic compound in the parameter estimation.
The PC-SAFT results have been compared to the results that can be achieved
with NRTL-SAC as it is implemented in AspenTech. For NRTL-SAC both the
pure component parameters as well as the melting temperature and the heat
of fusion are considered adjustable parameters. This was to give NRTL-SAC
a better chance of modeling the temperature dependency. As for the results
the deviations of the pure solvent systems were very similar for PC-SAFT
and NRTL-SAC. However of the mixed solvent there were several cases where
NRTL-SAC were much worse than PC-SAFT. This proves that PC-SAFT is
a good choice for modeling the solubility of pharmaceuticals, especially if it is
supposed to be dissolved in a mixed solvent during production.

1
Introduction
In this project the goal is to model the solubility of pharmaceutical with the
use of the Pertubed Chain-Statistical Associating Fluid Theory (PC-SAFT)
Equation of State (EoS). This will be done by using Matlab modules to be
able to do the calculations. The model have previously been successfully used
to achieve decent results for pharmaceuticals in [27] among others. The pa-
rameters used in the PC-SAFT model will be estimated and the performance
of the model will be evaluated. This will be done for selected pharmaceuticals
in a number of diﬀerent solvents and solvent mixtures. The goal of all this is
to ﬁnd optimal parameters that will be able to suﬃciently model the systems.
Being able to eﬀectively model the solubility of pharmaceuticals is of great
importance in process design of the production of the pharmaceutical. This
would especially be when a crystallization process is investigated, but it will
also be helpful for other processes to make sure that unwanted precipitation
does not happen. Having models with good parameters for multiple solvents
are also crucial if the modeling is to be used during the screening of solvents
when the process is developed. Using the model instead of having to solely
rely on experiments would signiﬁcantly cut down the cost of developing a new
process for producing a drug.
The results from using PC-SAFT will be compared to the results that can be
obtained with NRTL-SAC. The parameter estimation and calculations with the
NRTL-SAC was done using the commercial software from Aspen Technologies.
2
Literature discussion
In this section a discussion of what have been reported in the literature re-
garding modeling of the solubility of pharmaceuticals with PC-SAFT (or other
similar models). Several diﬀerent articles have been published regarding mod-
eling including [27, 25, 2], which all make use of the PC-SAFT model and
where Bouillot et al. [2] is also modeling with NRTL-SAC to compare the re-
sults of the models. For the most part the systems that was investigated in
this comparison showed that PC-SAFT produced better results. PC-SAFT
have also been compared to modeling done with the cubic equation of state,
Soave-Redlich-Kwong (SRK) and Peng-Robinson (PR) with both one and two
interaction parameters was tested by Abdallah et al. [1]. In this the results
showed clearly that a lower deviation from the experimental results was ob-
tained for almost all points when PC-SAFT was used to do the modeling.
Overall this indicates that PC-SAFT is a good model to use when modeling
the solubility of pharmaceuticals.

2.1
Other models
A number of other models have been used to model systems that include phar-
maceuticals. As mentioned the older EoS (PR and SRK) have been compared
to PC-SAFT by Abdallah et al. [1] and the results clearly showed that the
accuracy of PC-SAFT was better. Another model that is used is the NRTL-
SAC model which have been implemented in the Aspen Tech simulator and
modeling framework. As mentioned the systems will also be modeled with
NRTL-SAC in this work.
The NRTL-SAC model have also been used to correlate and predict the solu-
bility of four diﬀerent pharmaceutical drugs by Mota et al. [17]. They report
that the absolute average deviation was 68% for the correlation of the solu-
bility in organic solvents, and that the solubility prediction in water had a
deviation of 38%.
In [28] the nonrandom hydrogen bonding (NRHB) theory have been used to
model diﬀerent types of phase behavior including the solubility of several dif-
ferent pharmaceuticals in diﬀerent solvent. The model shows good results and
is capable of making decent predictions (no interaction parameter) in some
cases.
The COSMO-SAC model has also been used for modeling the solubility of
pharmaceuticals by Lee & Lin [14] who investigated 11 diﬀerent drug and the
percentage deviation is reported to be 91% overall with the best method that
was investigated. The article focuses on modeling the solubility in ionic liquids.
COSMO-SAC has also been used by Hsieh et al. [11] where a large amount
of diﬀerent drugs and solvents have been considered. The percentage error is
reported to be 70%. In [29] the two models NRHB and COSMO-RS have been
compared for how well they can predict the solubility and both show good
results for some compounds and worse for others.
The ePC-SAFT EoS has also been applied to pharmaceuticals by Cassens
et al. [4]. ePC-SAFT is similar to the PC-SAFT model, but it is also taking
into account an electrostatic contribution. The model is taking the ionization
of the pharmaceutical into account since the aim of the model is to be able
to consider the pH-dependency of the solubility of the pharmaceutical. The
results of the model seems to be able to accomplish this well for several diﬀerent
temperatures.
An attempt at using a group contribution method to model the solubility of
pharmaceuticals have been reported by Pelczarska et al. [21]. The model that
is used assumes that the activity coeﬃcient is equal to the activity coeﬃcient
at inﬁnite dilution. They were attempting to get a completely predictive group
contribution model, but for most of the systems the prediction was underes-
timating the solubility. However they were showing decent results when they
added an experimental value so the model became semi-predictive.

Another group that have been attempting to use group contribution methods
to be able to predict the solubility of pharmaceuticals is done by Nouar et al.
[19]. They have used a modiﬁed UNIFAC model where the structural groups
are deﬁned as in the KT-UNIFAC model and the interaction parameters are
considered temperature dependent like the Pharma-modiﬁed UNIFAC model.
They are able show it is possible to obtain decent predictive results for diﬀerent
pharmaceutical compounds.
Finally Ellegaard et al. [6] has been modeling the solubility of pharmaceuticals
by using a fundamental ﬂuid theory that arises from statistical mechanics.
Their model includes requires a model that can calculate the activity coeﬃcient
as well a parameter for each pharmaceutical-solvent pair. The models that
they used for the calculation of the activity coeﬃcient were Wilson, Margules
or UNIFAC. The binary parameter has either been found by regression of
mixed solvent VLE and solubility data or estimated by using pure solvent
solubility data. The results are generally better when the binary parameters
were estimated from mixed solvent data, but in a lot of cases the results from
pure solvent estimation showed decent results.
2.2
Diﬀerent parameter estimation methods for PC-SAFT
There is a number of diﬀerent methods that have been applied to estimate the
parameters PC-SAFT. This is because the usual way is to ﬁt the parameters is
by using data for liquid density and vapor pressures of the pure components.
However for the pharmaceuticals these types of data is generally not to be
found, due to the pharmaceuticals being solids at normal conditions. For some
compounds they might not even exist as a gas due to the compounds breaking
down. This has lead to the need to use another method for determining the
parameters in the model. In all the articles that have been found the way that
the parameters are estimated is based on the solubility in pure solvents.
The parameters of the solvents that are used in the ﬁtting of the parameters
of the pharmaceuticals is known. Since the number of pure component param-
eters is 5, it will generally require at least 5 data points to do the parameter
estimation. This does leave a large amount of choices in terms of which data
to use in the parameter estimation. To mention how this have been handled
previously, in [25] this was done by estimating the pure component parameters
from solubility data of one pure solvent. For the other solvents a interaction
parameter between the pharmaceutical and the solvent was found to make the
model better. The interaction parameter was assumed to be a linear function
of the temperature. In [27] another method was used. Here the parameters
of the pharmaceutical was estimated based on the solubility of three diﬀerent
solvents, namely water, a polar solvent and a hydrophobic solvent.
Furthermore the solubility in water was multiplied by a factor (larger factor
for lower solubility), and a interaction parameter was used between water and
the pharmaceutical. The factor that was used to multiply the solubility of

4.2
The PC-SAFT EoS1
The PC-SAFT EoS is a variation of the SAFT model, where SAFT stands
for Statistical Associating Fluid Theory. The SAFT models are based on the-
ory from statistical mechanics, and they are equations of state that explicitly
accounts for a number of forces including: attractive (dispersion), repulsive,
hydrogen bonding and chain eﬀects. A number of diﬀerent versions of the
SAFT models have been developed, and the diﬀerences is in the expressions of
the terms that describes the diﬀerent forces, where it is mostly the dispersion
forces that are accounted for diﬀerently.
In SAFT the forces are applied sequentially and for a lot of SAFT models (not
PC-SAFT) the order is: First the ﬂuid is assumed to be consisting of hard-
spheres. At this point attractive (dispersion) forces are added. Afterwards
chain sites are added to the hard-spheres and chains are created. Association
sites are when added and the chains will when interact to create complexes
due to these association sites.
For PC-SAFT the structure creating the molecule functions a bit diﬀerent.
In the previously described way the dispersion forces was added to the hard-
spheres before the chain sites were added and chains were created. This means
that the dispersion forces are only considered to be acting between the hard-
spheres. In PC-SAFT the chains are instead formed before the dispersion forces
are added and accounted for. This means that with PC-SAFT the dispersion
forces are considered between chains and the dispersion expression is therefore
altered compared to earlier SAFT models to account for this.
To account for the diﬀerent forces that contributes to the residual reduced
Helmholtz energy, the diﬀerent terms are added together:
ares = ahs + achain + adisp + aassoc

5.3.1
Constant interaction parameter
The estimation of the constant interaction parameter was considered to be
done in two diﬀerent ways. The ﬁrst method was similar to what was used
in the estimation of the other parameters by calculating the solubility and
comparing it to the experimental value using the same minimization function
that is shown in equation (45). The second method was instead attempting
to minimize the same expression that was in the solubility function, which
is shown in equation (44), where the mole fraction was set at each of the
experimental values.
Both methods can be setup to either use the solubility at a single temperature
or the solubility at a number of diﬀerent temperatures. In both cases the inter-
action parameter is constant, but when multiple temperatures are included the
constant interaction parameter might be better at representing the solubility
for the entire temperature range instead of just for a single temperature.
For the cases where both methods were tested the resulting interaction pa-
rameter was found to be very similar. It is however much faster to use the
second method and that is why the constant interaction parameters that are
reported in this work were found using the second method. The reasoning that
the second method has a faster computation time can be shown by looking at
the expression below (reprint of equation (44)):

5.3.2
Temperature dependent interaction parameter
The temperature dependent interaction parameter that is used in this work is
generally given by the expression:
kij(T) = c0 + c1 · T + c2/T
(46)
Where c0, c1 and c2 is ﬁtted constants. For several cases the interaction pa-
rameter appeared to be linearly dependent on the temperature and in those
cases c2 was set to 0 and the estimation so the expression becomes linear.
Due to the way that the Matlab framework for the PC-SAFT model the tem-
perature dependent interaction parameter is estimated diﬀerently from when
it is a constant. It was done by estimating the interaction parameter for each
temperature using the method for a constant interaction parameter that was
covered in the previous section. The interaction parameters is when plotted as
a function of the temperature and a regression was done to ﬁnd the constants
in equation (46). The regression was accomplished with the function fitnlm.

Conclusion
It has been shown that the simpliﬁed solubility equation can be used to ob-
tain good results when the activity coeﬃcient is calculated with the use of the
PC-SAFT EoS. It is also been shown that the PC-SAFT model can be bet-
ter than NRTL-SAC when the purpose is to estimate parameters from pure
solvent solubility and extend that to mixed solvents. This is evident from the
fact that NRTL-SAC only was better than PC-SAFT for one mixed solvent
system (water-methanol-paracetamol). All the other mixed solvent systems
were signiﬁcantly better when modeled with PC-SAFT.
A consideration of the impact of the solvent-solvent interaction parameter. It
was shown that for the overall deviation of the modeling results that including
the solvent-solvent interaction parameter would only sometimes yield better
results. However in general including it would be making the solubility be
predicted lower in the high solubility part, while it is better in the low solu-
bility parts. This phenomena is explain by the fact that it is very likely that
the interactions between the solvents are impacted by the fact that the much
larger pharmaceutical is present. The reasoning behind the solubility being
predicted worse in the high solubility regions is that the impact of the phar-
maceutical is greater then a lot of it is dissolved. When only a little bit of
pharmaceutical is dissolved it is not going to be as impactfull on the solvent-
solvent interaction. It was also shown that it is possible to get a better ﬁt if
the solvent-solvent interaction parameter is estimated from the binary solubil-
ity data. This was however only done as a prove of concept for the system
water-acetone-azithromycin. For that system it was possible to reduce the de-
viation from 61.06% to 16.59%. This was not done for all systems since the
solvent-solvent interaction parameter would when only be applicable for that
one system.
