Abstract
In cold regions, the presence of moisture combined with freezing temperatures leads to a phys-
ical weathering process in rocks known as frost weathering. Frost weathering is driven by the
volumetric expansion of ice and ice segregation (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008). This study fo-
cuses on the former and how it affects the stability of hard rock slopes in cold regions through
fracture propagation, frost wedging, and freeze thaw cycles.
The study of fracture propagation assesses this process in two different types of hard rock:
Iddefjord Granite and Fauske Marble. The tests are organized into the three following sub cate-
gories including 1. basic fracture propagation (Test A), 2. fracture propagation from intervaled
inﬁlling of water (Test B), and 3. fracture propagation with a free surface involved (Test C).
The results show that signiﬁcant ice buildup is required to induce fracture propagation from a
mechanically made tip (Test A). The interval inﬁlling method (Test B) did not see any fracture
propagation throughout the four cycles. Both the basic fracture propagation (Test A) and free
surface fracture propagation (Test C) tests exhibited full fracture propagation throughout the
samples. Additionally, these results showed that the number of cycles required to fully propa-
gate the fracture through the sample is dependent on both the rock type and the distance to the
free surface.
The effects of frost wedging are assessed through laboratory work, ﬁeld investigation, numerical
modelling, and analytical approach. A frost wedging test (Test D) was performed in the lab-
oratory on two marble samples with wedges created within those samples. The samples were
geometrically designed to represent a basic planar failure. One block was created to be a stable
wedge, and the other an unstable wedge. The results of the laboratory investigation show that
the effects of frost wedging on these samples are independent of the slope of the sliding plane.
The ﬁeld investigation was conducted near Soknedal, Norway, and assessed areas that could
potentially have reduced stability due to frost wedging that occurs during months with sub zero
temperatures. The analytical approach shows that the uplift from ice formation in joints leads to
small horizontal displacements over time. The results of the assessment of frost wedging show
that the presence of ice within the joints of a rock mass can lead to slow movements within the
rock mass, thereby decreasing the overall stability of the rock mass.
Freeze thaw cycles have been shown to cause damage in the form of physical weathering,
and hence, decrease the strength of high porosity rocks. The damage to low porosity rocks
is typically less extensive due to their lack of pore space (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008). An
analysis of the effects of six freeze thaw cycles is conduced on low porosity Iddefjord Granite
core. UCS tests are conducted on the core samples which have been subjected to the freeze thaw
cycles and on a control group of core that was never frozen. The results showed no difference
in strength from that of the control group.
This study shows that some of the effects of frost weathering, speciﬁcally fracture propagation
and frost wedging, lead to a decrease in the stability of hard rock slopes in cold regions.

Introduction
In January 2017 a rockfall from a road cut occurred along Fv889 Ørahaugen. The rockfall
broke the existing ring net that had been installed to prevent debris from falling on the road and
resulted in 500 m3 of rock being spilled across the highway as shown in FIGURE 1.1 (Ramboll,
2018). Upon investigation it was found that there was a presence of ice within the boreholes
which were initially drilled to act as drainage holes for the slope. The accumulation of ice within
the slope combined with excessive water pressure lead to the failure of this slope (Nordbrøden
and Stefanussen, 2018).
Figure 1.1: Aerial view of rock fall that occurred in Havøysund (Ramboll, 2018).
This highway acts as the transportation corridor from mainland Norway into the village of
Havøysund on the island of Havøya. The village is located in Finnmark, the northernmost
county in Norway. Events like this in remote, northern regions can lead to isolation of commu-
nities, thereby affecting the well being of the local population. For this reason it is important
that rockfalls, like the one in Havøysund, and slope instabilities caused by the accumulation of
ice within hard rock slopes in cold regions, be better understood.
This study is succeeded by the engineering geology and rock mechanics specialization project
completed on the topic of rock slope stability in cold regions for the Department of Geosciences
and Petroleum at NTNU. This project was completed in the fall semester of 2019 and focused

Cold Regions
The term cold regions has various deﬁnitions. Campbell and Claridge (1992) have deﬁned it as
“areas in which permafrost occurs”. Another deﬁnition of cold regions describes it as a “part
of the earth system characterized by the presence of snow and ice at least part of the year.”
(Shen, 2018). Andersland and Ladanyi (2004) approximates it to be located at the 40th parallel.
The boundaries of these deﬁnitions vary from the permafrost boundary to a loose boundary that
may experience intermittent freezing of ground. Due to the variation in deﬁnition by study, the
author has devised their own deﬁnition for the purpose of this study.
This paper herein deﬁnes cold regions as an area inclusive of the high Arctic and sub Arctic
regions that extends beyond the permafrost boundary into more temperate climates that expe-
rience seasonal ground freezing. This deﬁnition of cold regions includes perennially frozen
ground and seasonally frozen ground and excludes areas with intermittently frozen ground. A
spatial distribution of such across the northern hemisphere is given in FIGURE 1.2. This deﬁni-
tion is not exclusive of the the Arctic polar region but instead is inclusive of both northern and
southern polar regions.

2.1
Slope Stability
The fundamentals of slope stability are based on the concept of a block sliding on an inclined
plane. In FIGURE 2.1 it can be seen that the forces acting on the block are derived from the
gravitational force. When these forces are broken down into separate components, they repre-
sent a portion of the gravitational force that is holding the block in place, and the other portion
of the gravitational force that is pulling the block down the slope. In slope stability these forces
are known as the resisting force and the driving force, respectively. When the forces are at
equilibrium the block stays in place, but when the driving force exceeds the resisting force the
block will slide down the plane (Hoek and Bray, 1981).
In applying this basic physics concept to the study of slope stability, the gravitational force is
not the only force working on the block. The force of friction between the sliding plane and the
block adds to the resisting forces acting upon the block. The cohesion between the block and the
sliding plane is an additional force that adds to this resistance. Together, at a basic level, these
three forces dictate whether a slope will remain intact or fail (Hoek and Bray, 1981). Other
forces derived from the natural environment and our engineered environment also play a role in
slope stability and will be discussed further in the following section.

2.1.2
Failure Modes
Slope failure occurs when the driving force exceeds the resisting force. The determination of
how these forces act and interact varies based on the geometry of the failure. For this reason,
rock failures are sub divided based on the mode in which the failure occurred. The four main
failure modes of rock are planar failure, wedge failure, circular failure, and toppling failure
(Hoek and Bray, 1981). The conditions of the planar and wedge failures will be outlined in the
coming sections.

2.1.2.1
Planar
Planar failures are the most basic of the failure modes in rock mechanics, occurring when a rock
slides down a single sliding plane. The release plane needs to have an azimuth of inclination
near parallel to that of the inclination of the slope. The inclination of the release plane must
be less than the inclination of the slope so that the release plane can daylight onto the surface
of the slope. The friction angle of the rock must also be less than both the inclination of the
release plane and the inclination of the slope so that failure can occur, if these conditions are
not met, the block is held in the slope by the force of friction. The conditions for planar failure
are governed by the following relationship (Hoek and Bray, 1981),
Inclination of Slope Face ( f) > Dip of Plane ( p) > Friction Angle(φ)
The planar failure is not often seen in the ﬁeld, as geology typically creates more complex
geometries. An image of what the failure looks like in practice is given in FIGURE 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Illustration of a planar failure (Wyllie and Mah, 2004).
2.1.2.2
Wedge
Wedge failures have an increased degree of complexity in comparison to the planar failure.
Rather than sliding along one plane, in a wedge failure the block slides along the intersection
of two planes. There is a potential for wedge failure when the following conditions are satisﬁed
(Hoek and Bray, 1981),
Inclination of Slope Face ( f) > Dip of Line of Intersection ( i) > Friction Angle(φ)
In addition to this, the orientation of the joint structures in the rock mass must intersect to
create this wedge geometry. An image of what this (idealized) situation looks like in theory is

2.1.3
Fracture Mechanics
There are three different failure modes in which the fracturing of a material can occur. One of
which results in a tensile failure of the material and the other two a shear failure. The ﬁrst failure
mode is due to stresses acting in opposite directions, creating a tensile strain in the material that
leads to failure. The second failure mode is from stresses acting in opposite directions but
oriented into the material. This results in a shear strain that leads to failure. The third occurs
when the stresses are acting towards each other, thereby create a shear strain within the material
that leads to failure (Roylance, 2001).
Figure 2.4: Illustration of the three different failure modes, I, II, and III (Roylance, 2001).
2.2
Frozen Ground
Frozen ground is a phenomenon that occurs in cold regions of the world, and is classiﬁed as the
ground reaching a temperature below 0◦C. The spatial and temporal extents of frozen ground
break it down into four different sub-classiﬁcations. In regions where the ground freezes every
winter and thaws every summer, the ground is referred to as seasonally frozen ground. When
the ground stays frozen year round it is referred to as permafrost (Andersland and Ladanyi,
2004). Permafrost has two main components to it. A permanently frozen zone, and above it an
active layer that undergoes freezing and thawing with the seasonal variations. The distribution
of the permafrost in a given area is what dictates the later three classiﬁcations of frozen ground.
Sporadic permafrost indicates areas in which the ground is 0 −50% permafrost. When the
ground consists of 50 −90% permafrost it is called discontinuous permafrost. Areas which

2.2.1
Freezing Process
When water undergoes the phase change from liquid to solid it results in a 9% volumetric ex-
pansion, and a 3% linear expansion, at zero degrees Celsius. When the temperature is further
dropped to −22◦C the volumetric expansion increases to 13.5 % (Tharp, 1987). When saturated
rock or soil is exposed to sub-zero temperatures, the freezing of water to ice and its subsequent
expansion can lead to the accumulation of stresses that can damage the surrounding material
(Wang and Zhou, 2018; Davidson and Nye, 1985). The four main parameters that govern the
behavior of freezing are the intensity, rate, duration, and number of freeze thaw cycles (Mat-
suoka, 2001b; Andr´en, 2019). During rapid freezing of water in rock or soil research has shown
that water freezes in situ. When water is cooled slowly capillary action forces referred to as
cryosuction, form layers of ice, called ice lenses (Andersland and Ladanyi, 2004).

2.3.1
Ice Wedging
Ice wedging is a process that commonly occurs at the boundary of the active layer and the
permafrost table in areas of continuous permafrost. Ice wedges in frozen ground occur as a
result of the thermal contraction of the frozen soil, that occurs when temperatures drop. The
thermal contraction exerts a tensile stress on the material; when this stress exceeds the tensile
strength of the frozen soil a tension crack forms in the ground. This crack remains throughout
the winter, and when temperatures rise in the spring the subsequent melting in the active layer
is drawn into this crack and freezes. This process is repeated over and over during the freezing
and thawing seasons and results in the formation of a wedge of ice in the soil (Andersland and
Ladanyi, 2004). FIGURE 2.6 shows a schematic of this process.
Figure 2.6: Schematic of ice wedging process (Pidwirny, 2006).
2.3.2
Soliﬂuction
The process of soliﬂuction is a downslope movement of soil that occurs in areas of permafrost.
It occurs on low angled slopes, and progresses at a very slow rate. These movements occur
as a result of the thawing and freezing of these permafrost soils. When the soils thaw in the
warmer months, the mass is able to slowly creep down slope. Once frozen again in the colder
months the mass is frozen in place (Matsuoka, 2001c; Andersland and Ladanyi, 2004; Harris
et al., 2008).
Figure 2.7: Visual of the process of soliﬂuction (Pidwirny, 2006).

2.4 Freezing in Rock
2.4
Freezing in Rock
The physical damage to rock slopes caused by the phase change of water to ice is refereed to as
the geomorphic process of frost weathering (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008). This process pro-
motes the production of debris from rock slopes, ranging from ﬁnes to rock falls (Matsuoka,
2001a; Matsuoka and Murton, 2008). Frost weathering is driven by the processes of ice seg-
regation and volumetric expansion. These two processes result in the formation of ice lenses
and ice wedges in available space within the rock mass. This can be either pore space or spaces
left by discontinuities. The area in which the ice accumulates governs the type of weather-
ing that occurs (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008). Ice lenses form within pores and is a process
known as ice segregation (microgelevation). Ice wedges form within discontinuities and is a
process known as ice wedging (macrogelivation) (Matsuoka, 2001b). These two processes will
be explained in the following sections.
2.4.1
Ice Segregation (Microgelivation)
The weathering of soft rocks is referred to as microgelivation. Microgelivation is dominated by
a process called ice segregation. Ice segregation is the result of cryosuction pulling unfrozen
water towards the freezing front. When the unfrozen water meets the ice it freezes and leads to
an accumulation of ice. This accumulation of ice is refereed to as an ice lens (Matsuoka, 2001b;
Murton et al., 2006). The growth of these ice lenses has the ability to fracture the surrounding
rock. There is evidence that the formation of ice lenses in low porosity hard rocks can cause
damage, although it is less extensive (Duca et al., 2015). Soft rocks are most susceptible to this
process as they are more porous and weaker than harder rocks (Murton et al., 2006).
2.4.2
Ice Wedging (Macrogelivation)
Intact, hard rock is signiﬁcantly less frost susceptible. The weathering of hard rock, which is
less porous and stronger than soft rock, is referred to as macrogelivation. This process does not
target the intact rock but rather the rock mass as a whole (Matsuoka, 2001b). The presence of
joint planes and fractures within a rock mass make the rock mass susceptible to frost weathering
(Matsuoka and Murton, 2008). It is within these discontinuities where water accumulates and
freezes when temperatures drop below zero. The volumetric expansion upon freezing leads to
the widening of joints and opening of preexisting fractures, as ice accumulates in these areas of
the rock mass (Matsuoka, 2001b).
2.4.2.1
Spatial and Temporal Relationship
The scale, both spatially and temporally, at which the freezing occurs is also of importance. In
rock masses that undergo rapid freezing, water freezes in situ and causes volumetric expansion
within the joints. When water freezes slowly, ice freezes in layers and causes less damage to
the rock body. From ﬁeld studies conducted by Matsuoka (2001a) it has been shown that the
widening of joints within rock masses is greatest during the shoulder seasons. This is due to the
diurnal temperature variations that occur in the autumn and spring months. These quick freeze-
thaw cycles increase degradation and decrease stability of the rock mass (Matsuoka, 2001a).
11
==PAGE:30==
Chapter 2. Background Information
Matsuoka and Murton (2008) presents three different frequencies in which frost cycles can be
categorized.
1. High frequency cycles: Short term cycles, typically diurnal. Result in shallow freezing
that only reaches a depth of approximately 20 −30 cm. Weathering from this cycle
produces small fragments of rock up to pebble in size (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008).
2. Annual frost cycles: Seasonal freezing. Scale of freezing depth increases to several me-
ters. Can lead to boulder size rock falls in steep alpine regions (Matsuoka and Murton,
2008).
3. Episodic freeze thaw cycles: Long term cycles where thawing only occurs in abnormally
warm summers. Can lead to rock avalanches. Recent changes in climate that have lead to
warmer temperatures have increased the frequency of these cycles (Matsuoka and Murton,
2008).
These different cycles show that the temporal scale of freezing is proportional to the spacial
scale of freezing into the rock mass (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008; Matsuoka, 2001b).
2.4.2.2
Rock Mass Properties
The formation of ice in a rock mass leads to two important phenomena. Firstly, the formation
of ice increases the cohesion of the rock body. This results in a increased stability when temper-
atures remain below freezing. The strength and stiffness of the ice body itself are temperature
dependent and inversely related (Davies et al., 2001). While the independent strength prop-
erties of the ice and rock are important, the stability of an ice ﬁlled joint is controlled by the
shear strength of the ice-rock interface (Davies et al., 2000). When temperatures rise and the
ice begins to thaw, the warm ice provides a slip plane for rock to fail along, thereby decreasing
the shear strength of the rock body (Davies et al., 2001). Secondly, the formation and accumu-
lation of ice in a rock body results in an increased pressure being exerted on the surrounding
rock. This increased pressure exerted on the rock body leads to degradation of the bedrock via
fracture formation and growth, as well as joint widening (Matsuoka, 2001b). The density of the
joints within the rock mass also dictates the susceptibility to frost weathering, as more joints
provide more spaces in which ice can form (Matsuoka and Murton, 2008).
2.5
Climate Change Impacts
The changing climate attributed to the current global state raises some concerns for the stability
of slope in periglacial and glacial environments. Davies et al. (2001) performed shear box
testing on an ice ﬁlled joint between two concrete blocks with strength properties similar to that
of granite. Testing was performed at varying temperatures to determine its inﬂuence on slope
stability. This study found that the conditions for failure do not require the thawing of the entire
ice body, but failure can occur when only a part of the ice body has thawed. The study concluded
that, in slopes where ice controls the stability of the slope, the stability is compromised with
warming temperatures (Davies et al., 2001).

Test A - Fracture Propagation
The purpose of this test is to look at the differences in fracture propagation between the two
different rock types as well as with different lengths of the cut slot in the granite. These tests
are a closed system as they do no have access to additional water during the test. Fracture
propagation testing was conducted on three different samples, two of which were composed of
Iddefjord Granite and one being Fauske Marble. There were two samples with the 9 cm cut slot
shown in FIGURE 3.2, one of each rock type.
Figure 3.2: Sketch of specimen used for tests A.1 and A.2.
For the granite sample the surface conditions are excellent, the sample had very sharp edges and
very little damage. On the other hand, the marble sample had more signiﬁcant damages to its
corners, and the edges were not as sharp. This difference between the two otherwise identical
samples is likely due to a difference in rock properties, with the marble being weaker, making
it more susceptible to damages. However, none of these damages pose as an issue to effect the
results of the testing.
For the 6 cm cut slot there was only one sample, as shown in FIGURE 3.3. This sample is
composed of granite.

3.2.2
Test B - Interval Inﬁlling - Fracture Propagation
The purpose of this test is to determine how the behavior of fracture propagation changes when
the cut slot is ﬁlled in intervals rather than all at once. This test acts as a semi-open system
as the cut slot has additional water introduced to it twice, unlike the previous test. The next
specimen is designed the same as the previous, but the process of water inﬁlling to the sample
is different. This test will use intervaled inﬁlling to mimic the effects of an open system on the
fracture propagation process. This will consist of three different inﬁllings of water during each
test. Each inﬁlling will ﬁll one third of the fracture as can be seen in FIGURE 3.4.

3.2.3
Test C - Free Surface - Fracture Propagation
The next specimen is designed to asses the effect of ice driven fracture propagation on a free
surface and to see how fracture propagation differs when it occurs near a free surface. A sketch
of the sample with dimensions has been provided in FIGURE 3.5 This specimen was designed
to asses the fracture propagation behavior at different distances to the free surface. The fracture
propagation from both these artiﬁcial fractures will be assessed for rate and angle of fracture, as
it is expected that the propagation behavior will have the fracture curve towards the free surfaces
instead of fracturing down through the sample, as the conﬁned fractures in the previous samples
are expected to behave.

to the cut slot so it will likely cause issues with keeping the cut slot water tight at the beginning
of the test. On the back side of the sample there is a defect from the cutting process that has
created an uneven surface. Extra care needs to be taken to ensure this side of the sample is water
tight. This defect on the back side effects both cut slots.

3.2.5
Procedure
Each cycle of each test followed the steps presented in the following.
1. Boil water 2 hours prior to testing (This step was applied to all tests on and after February
10th).
2. Place thermistor in cold room and begin recording temperatures 15 minutes prior to esti-
mated test start time (This step was applied to all tests on and after February 5th).
3. Tape open ends of the cut slot on the front and back side with industrial grade ﬂoor lining
tape. Rub tape and ensure no air bubbles are present between tape and sample.
4. Use digital caliper to take measurements of height, thickness, width, and width of cut slot
of the sample.
5. Photograph front side of the cut slot, back side of the cut slot, and the top of the sample.
6. Place sample into cold room.
7. Fill meniscus with boiled water and add to cut slot of sample. Record volume of water
added to sample.
(a) For tests A and C ﬁll to top of cut slot.
(b) For test B ﬁll one third of the way, and repeat every 24 hours until sample is full.
8. Record time and leave sample in lab for 24 hours.
9. After 24 hours has passed, remove sample from lab.
10. Photograph front side of the cut slot, back side of the cut slot, and the top of the sample.
11. Take measurements of height, thickness, width, and width of cut slot of the sample using
a digital caliper.
During the testing period the procedure was revised and new additions were made based on
troubleshooting from previous tests. When new steps have been added to the procedure it is
stated within the steps the date at which the step was added. In the results section of this
chapter the dates of each test will be presented at the beginning of each cycle to clarify which
parts of the procedure were followed for that speciﬁc cycle.

==PAGE:36==

Tap water is collected after it has been transported through a piping system. The turbulent
movement from the transport of the water creates air bubbles within the water that comes out
of the tap. These air bubbles remain within the water and are then integrated into the ice struc-
ture upon freezing. These air bubbles within the ice act as impurities in the ice structure which
decreases the strength of the ice. This was realized during the testing process and so the proce-
dure was revised. For this reason, boiled water was chosen to be used after February 10th, as
the boiling process removes a signiﬁcant amount of the air bubbles from the water, forming a
stronger and purer ice. The dates that each cycle has been conducted on is therefore included in
the presentation of the results.
The thermistor used during testing took approximately 15 minutes to adjust to the temperature
within the lab. This was not understood until after the ﬁrst few tests worth of thermistor data
was analyzed. Therefore, after February 5th this new step was added into the procedure.
The 24 hour time period for freezing was chosen in accordance with the freezing period used
by Davies et al. (2000) to ensure the sample was completely frozen. The cold room used for
the testing was a shared space and the temperatures did ﬂuctuate depending on the other users
of the space, and this time period allowed for accommodation of this.

Chapter4
Ice Driven Displacement of Blocks
4.1
Introduction
A multifaceted approach was taken to determine the effects of ice driven displacement of blocks
on the stability of rock masses. In October of 2019 a geotechnical assessment of a slope along
route E6 near Soknedal, Norway, was performed. This was conducted to identify blocks within
the slope that could be susceptible to being displaced as a result of ice accumulation within
the joint aperture. This ﬁeld investigation is combined with numerical modelling, an analytical
approach, and a laboratory investigation. These four different approaches aim to answer the
question: can the formation of ice in a joint plane destabilize a stable wedge in a hard rock
slope.
4.2
Field Investigation
The ﬁeld work for this project was performed by Natalie Bennett and Dr. Charlie Li on October
7th, 2019. The ﬁeld work took place 3 km south of the community of Soknedal in the Trøndelag
county of Norway. The ﬁeld work campaign consisted of the mapping of 13 localities along a
1 km long road cut along highway E6. These localities were selected as places along the rock
cut where displacement was likely to occur due to ice build up during the winter. The overnight
temperature was below zero the night before and was approximately 3 ◦C during the ﬁeld work
activities (Norwegian Meteorological Institute, 2019). A map summarizing the locations with
an inset of the location of the ﬁeld site within Norway is shown in, FIGURE 4.1.
59
==PAGE:78==
Chapter 4. Ice Driven Displacement of Blocks
Figure 4.1: Map of ﬁeld work area including the locations of all 13 wedge locations and inset of location
within Norway (Google, 2019).
4.2.1
Background Information
The geological history of the Soknedal area originates in the Precambrian to Cambrian pe-
riod with the formation of the Gula Group. The Gula Group is composed of metavolcanic and
metasedimentary rocks. The group consists of two formations, the Undal Formation and the
Sings˚as Formation. The Undal Formation consists of grey and black chlorite-sericite phyllites.
The Sings˚as Formation varies from calcite bearing chlorite-quartz schists to calc-silicate biotite
schists and gneisses. This formation additionally contains inclusions of chert and amphibolite.
The Støren Group consists of volcanic rocks that originate from the Cambrian to Ordovician
periods. This group consists of the Illfjell Formation and the Elgsjo Formation. The Illfjell For-
mation is composed of chlorite-epidote-amphibole maﬁc pillow lavas and pillow breccias. The
Elgsjo Formation is composed of quart-sericite-chlorite phyllites with inclusions of cherts and
tufts. The ﬁnal unit of interest in this area is the M´elange, originating in the Silurian period, is

4.4
Analytical Approach
The process that leads to the displacement of blocks in rock masses from ice accumulation in
the joint can be compared to as similar to that of soliﬂuction. The process that causes a loss
of stability and creep in permafrost. When ice forms within the joints of a rock mass it causes
71
==PAGE:90==
Chapter 4. Ice Driven Displacement of Blocks
uplift of the overlying block within the rock mass. This causes blocks to be displaced in the
vertical direction. Unlike when this occurs due to water pressure, the ice that has formed within
the joint causes an increase in cohesion, holding the rock mass together. This in turn stabilizes,
rather than destabilizes the slope.
When the ice forms the wedge is lifted in the direction perpendicular to the slope of the sliding
plane. When temperatures rise, the ice melts and the the now displaced block drops back down
to the sliding surface moving only in the vertical direction as gravity is the only force acting
on it. This results in a displacement in the horizontal direction. This process has been visually
explained in FIGURE 4.19 and is inspired by the soliﬂuction process given in FIGURE 2.7 from
Pidwirny (2006).
(a) Freezing.
(b) Thawing.
(c) Displacement.
Figure 4.19: Visual description of analytical approach. Yellow triangle represents a block, and the
black triangle represents the sliding plane. The blue arrow indicates the direction in which the block is
moved from the freezing of water within the joint. The green arrow represents the vertically downward
displacement of the block when thawing occurs. The red arrow represents the horizontal displacement
that occurs from one cycle of this process.
Over the course of many cycles of freezing and thawing the stability of the slope slowly de-
creases as blocks move further outward from the face of the slope. This phenomena can occur
on even low slope joints, destabilizing blocks that could have been classiﬁed as stable. This
approach assumes that during the thawing process the slope does not destabilize it, and that the
formation of the ice is uniform within the joint.

the space, and this time period allowed for accommodation of this.

Chapter5
Freeze Thaw Cycles on Hard Rock
5.1
Introduction
To determine the effects of freeze thaw cycles on hard rock ﬁve core samples of Iddefjord
Granite were used. In order to determine this, two of the samples were used for freeze thaw
testing, while the other three samples were not tested, and used as a control group. Once the
freeze thaw tests were completed all ﬁve samples were subjected to UCS testing. The results of
which will be compared to determine how freeze thaw cycles effect the strength of hard rock.
The aim is to answer the third research question: how do freeze thaw cycles effect the strength
of hard rock?
5.2
Freeze Thaw Test
The purpose of the freeze thaw test is to subject the samples to multiple cycles of freezing and
thawing to represent the effect of this process in nature. This test is conducted to degrade the
core samples through this process.
5.2.1
Methods
The step by step procedure that is used for this test is explained in the following. Each cycle
followed the same protocol for the test. This test required the use of two thermistors to monitor
the temperature in both the lab and the cold room. The two thermistors used are both Elitech
USB Temperature Logger (RC-5) as per previous tests. For more information on these refer
back to SECTION 3.2.4.
1. Place one thermistor next to water bath and one in the cold room, and set both to begin
recording.
2. Fully saturate sample by leaving it in water bath for 24 hours.
3. Put sample into sealable plastic bag and remove as much air as possible from bag. Once
the air has been removed seal the bag.
4. Place sample into cold room for 24 hours.
91
==PAGE:110==
Chapter 5. Freeze Thaw Cycles on Hard Rock
5. Take sample out of cold room, remove from bag and place in water bath to thaw. Leave
here for 24 hours.
6. Repeat steps 3-5 six times.
The 24 hour time period for freezing was chosen in accordance with the freezing period used
in the previous tests which follows Davies et al. (2000) to ensure the sample was completely
frozen. The cold room used for the testing was a shared space and the temperatures did ﬂuctuate
depending on the other users of the space, and this time period allowed for accommodation of
this.

Chapter6
Discussion
This chapter will discuss the results this study. This includes the results of all laboratory tests. It
will outline the general limitations from the laboratory set up that apply to all of the testing along
with additional discussion of the test speciﬁc results and limitations. This will also include a
discussion of the numerical modelling and ﬁeld investigation.
6.1
General Laboratory Discussion
The two main limitations of the laboratory testing in general are the lack of temperature control
within the cold room, and the design of the samples. The design of the samples allows them
to act as a closed system, meaning they do not have access to additional moisture as occurs in
nature. The effects of these limitations are discussed in the following. The length of freezing
period and how it could be adapted for future testing will also be discussed in this section.
6.1.1
Temperature
While using the cold room in the Ice Lab, the temperature of the room was not within the
researcher’s control. The room is a shared space and the temperature was dependent on the
needs of other projects. This resulted in a high variance in the temperature data of some tests as
the temperature of the cold room was changed mid test. This poses an issue in the comparison
of temperature data between tests and how the change in temperature affects the ice growth.
While it is known from, Tharp (1987), that the expansion of ice increases to 13.5% when the
temperature decreases to −22◦C, the data is not complete enough, due to the inconsistency in
temperature data, to draw conclusions in regards to whether or not fracture propagation from
the expansion of ice is greater at lower temperatures in these speciﬁc tests.
Another issue with the temperature in the laboratory was discovered from the formation of
the ice within the cut slot. The temperature distribution within the cold room was not evenly
distributed. During testing, the samples were consistently placed with the front end facing
closest to the door and the back end closest the wall. When ice escaped from the free end of
the sample and on to the top it was always on the end nearest the door. This showed that the
heating of the room was uneven, which serves as another limitation of these tests.
99
==PAGE:118==
Chapter 6. Discussion
6.1.2
Free End
The free end at the top of the sample allowed for the ice to expand upwards, and sometimes
spill over and form across the top of the sample. This reduces the amount of ice pressure being
applied along the walls of the sample as the ice ”escapes” out the top. This process of the ice
escaping was observed during test observations of the ﬁrst cycle of Test C. The ice escapes
through the top during ice formation, once the fracturing of the ice within the cut slot is audible.
During this time, the ice fractured which resulted in unfrozen water shooting up through the
free end and freezing on the top of the sample. In future testing, this issue could be addressed
by covering the free end and conﬁning the ice to expanding within the area of the cut slot.
6.1.3
Freezing Period
Th freezing period was not a limitation of the tests, but rather could be reﬁned going forward to
improve the efﬁciency of further research. The freezing period chosen for the tests was 24 hours.
This was chosen based on past research by Davies et al. (2000). From the hourly observations
made during tests A.3, C.1, and C.2, it can be seen that only minor changes occurred after hour
4, and no signiﬁcant changes occurred after hour 6. From these observations that were made
it can be recommended that if these tests be performed again, in the same conditions regarding
temperature, volume of water, size of cut slot, and material surrounding the ice, that there could
be a reduction in testing time.
6.2
Test Speciﬁc Laboratory Discussion
This section will go through the results and limitations speciﬁc to each test conducted during
the laboratory investigation.
6.2.1
Test A
The results of test A show that the ice driven propagation of fracture in hard rock is dependent
on the rock type, as well as the distance to the bottom of the sample. The granite block took
three cycles to have the fracture fully propagate, where as the marble sample only took one
cycle, proving that it is dependent on rock type. Looking at the results of the 6 cm cut slot in
granite it was seen that no fracture propagation occurred in the ﬁrst two cycles of testing. When
the water was changed to boiled water the fracture then began to propagate. This indicates that
the ice formed from tap water was not strong enough to propagate the fracture from that cut
slot. This means that a stronger ice with less impurities like the boiled water or larger area in
the cut slot is necessary for this geometry.
One of the general limitations of the laboratory work done during this study was that the samples
acted as a closed system. The samples are considered closed systems as they did not have access
to an inﬂow of water, as would typically occur in nature. Due to this discrepancy the behavior
of fracture propagation, would vary from the behavior as seen in this testing. It is likely that the
inﬂow of water in nature would cause more signiﬁcant fracture propagation to a rock than was
seen in the laboratory samples.
100
==PAGE:119==
6.2 Test Speciﬁc Laboratory Discussion
6.2.2
Test B
It was expected that this test would be able to induce fracture propagation of the sample. It is
hypothesized that the fracture propagation did not occur due to a smaller volume of ice freezing
at each interval. The amount of ice is proportional to the amount of the volumetric expansion.
Additionally, when the ice froze it had room to expand upwards. This resulted in a smaller
amount of pressure being exerted from the ice onto the sample. When the second and third in-
tervals were added, the ice below had already formed. Volumetric expansion was only occurring
above the already frozen ice from the previous interval. Therefore the same effect with the ﬁrst
interval happened subsequently with the second and third intervals, and no fracture propagation
occurred.
This is supported by the evidence that none of the ice within the cut slot fractured during any of
these tests. In all other tests performed in this study, the ice within the cut slot showed evidence
of fracturing. The fracturing of the ice that occurred in the other tests is the result of the ice
expansion coming inwards from the rock wall. This pressure pushes the unfrozen water, that
lies in the center, up and out of the top of the cut slot, which leads to ice spilling outside the cut
slot onto the top of the sample. When this process occurs, cracking can be heard within the ice
and fractures can be seen forming within the ice.
This does not mean that fracture propagation would not occur if more cycles of testing were
to be conducted. It is believed that, during the fracture propagation tests, the ice pressure does
create some micro fractures within the rock in addition to the fracture. This is conﬁrmed by the
signiﬁcant increase in permeability of the granite samples after the ﬁrst cycles were conducted
in tests A.1 and A.3. For this reason it is believed that, after enough tests are performed on this
sample, that fracture propagation would eventually occur.
If this fracture propagation were to occur, it is thought that the rate of propagation would be
signiﬁcantly faster than that of the single inﬁll tests. Similar to the phenomena that creates ice
wedges in permafrost soil, as was discussed in SECTION 2.3.1. Once a fracture was present the
new water at each interval would be drawn into the fracture and cause greater expansion there.
This process would cause greater damage to the rock in a shorter period of time. Additionally,
using a softer rock such as marble for this test would have likely allowed for fracture propaga-
tion, as from the results of tests A.1 and A.2 showed that the rate of fracture propagation was
larger in the marble than the granite.
6.2.3
Test C
During this test it was expected that the fracture would propagate from the tip of the notch in
the cut slot. In both tests the fracture instead propagated from the corner of the cut slot closest
to the free surface. This is a result of the stress accumulating in this corner rather than the tip of
the notch. The stress should have accumulated at the tip of the cut slot rather than the corner,
and this is likely due to the notch of the cut slot being too short of a cut into the sample.
The fractures that occur within the ice provide insight into the stresses acting on the sample.
In this test, speciﬁcally C.1, the ice fractures were on an angle in line with that of the angle of

fracture propagation. In test C.2 it was less obvious but still evident that the angle of ice fracture
aligned with that of the angle of fracture propagation.
Test C.2 was not able to be completed to failure due to Covid-19 restrictions. From the behavior
of the fracture propagation from the previous tests on the granite samples from Test A and Test
C.1, it is expected that it would have taken one additional cycle for the fracture to completely
propagate through to the free surface.
6.2.4
Test D
The most signiﬁcant source of error in this test was the leakage that occurred before the ice
formed within the sample. Efforts were made to mitigate the leakage before tests began, and
also when tests began. As testing progressed this was managed better, but sometimes it still
went unnoticed until the test had been completed. This leakage reduced the amount of ice
pressure being applied to the wedge.
The tape that kept the sample watertight during this test also worked to resist the movement of
the wedge induced by the ice pressure. Determining the effects of this tape quantitatively was
outside the scope of the project, but should be noted as a limitation of the results produced from
this test. The various glues that were used during this test are also a signiﬁcant limitation. The
amount of cohesion that the glue provided to the wedges was not determined. This serves as
another signiﬁcant limitation of this test.
6.2.5
Freeze Thaw Test
The freeze thaw tests did not produce the expected results. It was hypothesized that a decrease
in the sample UCS would occur after the samples had been subjected to freeze thaw tests. The
lack of ability to make the samples air tight during the freezing period was the most signiﬁcant
limitation in this test. The lack of ability to make the sample airtight in the bag means that
the sample was not fully going through the freeze thaw process. It is possible that only the
outermost parts of the sample that showed saturation were the only parts of the sample that
were saturated at the point of freezing. In this case, the only part of the sample subjected to the
freeze thaw cycle would be the part most near surface. Unless the rock was extremely porous,
little damage could be done to the sample from the freezing process.
6.2.6
UCS Test
The results of the UCS test show that all samples had a UCS in the range of 195.3−197.3 MPa.
This is a very narrow range of results, considering it was expected that the samples subjected to
freeze thaw testing would have lower UCS as a result of the degradation from the freeze thaw
cycles. The results of sample one and sample two show no signiﬁcant change in UCS from
the control group and therefore, the freeze thaw cycles conducted on this rocks did not have an
effect on the strength of the rock.

6.3
Numerical Modelling
The numerical modelling program Swedge that was used for modelling of the two locations
along the road cut was intended to be used to model the conditions of the slope with ice pressure,
in addition to water pressure and the dry slope conditions. Different iterations were attempted to
model the ice pressure, but none of them yielded the desired outcome. This led to an analytical
approach being taken to explain the accumulation of ice within a joint and how this leads to
movement of a wedge within a slope over time.
6.3.1
Inputs
This model is only as strong as its weakest input. This model is severely limited by the general
rock mass inputs used. The density of the rock mass provided an adequate general estimate.
However, the actual friction angle of the this rock mass is likely higher than the value used in
the modelling exercise. While the standard friction angle given by the program is a good place
to start for the friction angle of any rock mass, it is not representative of the ﬁeld conditions.

the tilt test combined with the average joint roughness coefﬁcient obtained during the ﬁeld

Chapter7
Conclusion
The laboratory investigation of the fracture propagation of intact rock samples showed that
signiﬁcant ice accumulation within the cut slot was required for fracture propagation to occur.
In the tests of intervaled inﬁlling of water, no fracture propagation occurred because the amount
of ice that formed during the ﬁrst inﬁlling interval did not exert enough pressure on the sample
to lead to fracture propagation through the tip of the cut slot.
The ﬁeld work investigation determined areas that could be susceptible to the effects of frost
wedging. It was initially thought that in combining the structural mapping with the winter
photos showing ice build up on the front of the slope would be able to highlight some areas of
susceptibility. Due to limitations of photography in the winter months it was not possible to
coordinate the two. Further studies at this site are required to determine if the ice accumulation
in the winter does in fact lead to movement within the slope. The analytical approach used
to determine the effects of frost wedging shows that the stability of a slope will be gradually
compromised with small displacements of blocks at each freeze thaw cycle that result in a
slow downward movement of the block along the sliding plane. This analytical approach was
supported by the ﬁndings from the laboratory study of the unstable and stable wedge where
small movements were observed in both blocks.
The effects of freeze thaw cycles on intact core require additional cycles of freeze thaw testing
to determine its effect on the Iddefjord Granite. From this study it is clear that six cycles of
freeze thaw testing do not lead to a reduction in the uniaxial compressive strength of the core
samples.
From this research it is evident that the presence of ice within hard rock slopes in cold regions
results in a decrease in the stability of the slope. Fracturing of intact rock, and movement
of blocks within the rock mass due to ice pressure accumulation will decrease the stability of a
rock mass composed of hard rock. Due to some signiﬁcant limitations within the testing, further
recommendations for future work have been made to quantify the role that these processes play
in destabilizing slopes.

7.1
Recommendations for Further Work
Recommendations for further work surrounding this study are as follows:
• Interval inﬁlling tests for fracture propagation should be tried again with a longer fracture.
This test should also have additional cycles performed on a sample of the same fracture
length.
• Freeze thaw tests on hard rock with an increased amount of freeze thaw cycles and appa-
ratus that is more effective in retaining saturation during testing.
• Comprehensive ﬁeld investigation of a slope over multiple winters combining structural
mapping, photography and installing temperature probes, accelerometers and extensome-
ters within joints. Potential to pair with remote sensing methods to monitor movement of
slopes.

C
Tilt Test
This appendix includes the methods and results for the tilt test on the Fauske Marble and Id-
dafjord Granite.
C.1
Methods
The tilt testing has been performed to determine the inherent friction angle of the samples.
This test has been conducted following the ISRM tilt test recommendations. The testing has
been carried out on the Iddefjord Granite and Fauske Marble using two of the blocks of each,
the dimensions of which have been provided in TABLE 7.1. Four tests of different sample
orientation were conducted for each rock type, with ﬁve tests within each orientation. An
average of the 20 tests conducted for each rock type was used to determine the friction angle.
The sides of the samples were labeled A and B. The ﬁrst orientation was the friction between
side A of specimen 1 and side A of specimen 2, referred to as A −Ai. The second orientation
was the same, but the samples were rotated 180 degrees to determine the friction angle with
sliding in the reverse direction, referred to as A −Ar. The next two tests were conducted with
the sample principle but using the B sides of the samples, and are referred to as B −Bi and
B −Br, respectively. The temperature in the room during testing was 21.9◦C, and the humidity
in the room was unavailable. The tilting rate of the tilt table was 23◦/minute. This rate above
does not follow the ISRM recommendations, but it was the lowest possible rate the table could
move at (Alejano et al., 2018).
