Abstract
Due to the increasing penetration of the wind power and other renewable energies, the
electricity markets are exposed to new sources of uncertainty and variability.
One
potential solution to cope with those drawbacks is to increase the system flexibility
by adding conventional flexible producers such as gas-fired generating units. However,
these fast-ramping generators are prone to act strategically when submitting their offers
to the electricity market.
Therefore, it might be interesting for these generators to
withhold their ramp capability in certain occasions if that maximizes their profit. This
master thesis models the strategic behaviour of a flexible strategic producer that trades
electricity in a pool-based market. Therefore, it derives the optimal offering strategy
that this strategic producer should submit to the electricity market. In this framework,
the market operator collects offers in terms of price, quantity and ramp and then, clears
the market based on the maximization of the social welfare. The formulation of these
two highly interrelated optimization problems relies on a bilevel programming. Here, the
profit maximization of the strategic producer comprises the upper-level problem while
the lower-level represents the market clearing. Then, this bilevel model is transformed
into a mathematical program with equilibrium constraints by replacing the lower-level
problem with its Karush-Kuhn‚ÄďTucker optimality conditions. Then, a mixed-integer
linear programming problem is derived by using the strong duality theorem, some of the
previous Karush-Kuhn‚ÄďTucker optimality conditions and linearization techniques. Both
deterministic and stochastic approaches of this model are considered. After that, the
performance of these models is demonstrated on an illustrative example and on a more
realistic case study. The results obtained from those simulations show the ability of the
strategic producer to exercise the market clearing in its own benefit. It has also been
proved that the variability of the wind power and the congestions on the transmission
lines have a significant effect in the strategy followed by the flexible producer. Finally,
the influence of the time horizon in which the ramp offer applies is analysed. These
simulations show that longer time horizons reduce the proneness of strategic behaviour
form flexible generators.
Key words: Electricity markets, offering strategy, market power, ramp capability,
wind power, linear programming, stochastic programming

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and aim
Over the last decade, the share of non-dispatchable renewable sources, especially wind
power, has rapidly grown. For example, Danish wind turbines supplied 42.1% of load in
average in 2015, while this figure was 39.1% in 2014 and 33.2% in 2013 [9]. Moreover,
the penetration of the wind power and other renewable sources in the electricity systems
is expected to continue increasing worldwide [15]. Even though this leads to a more
sustainable future as well as reduces the dependency on fossil fuels, the impact of the
increasing penetration of wind power has been submitted to much discussion in regards
of both power system and electricity market operation.
It is well-known that the scheduling and dispatching of the generating units must
follow the demand and maintain the power balance in the system at every moment.
With the increase of renewable production, there is also a higher rate of variability in
the system. Because of that, achieving the supply-demand equilibrium is becoming a
much more difficult duty. Hence, the inherent variability of the wind power exposes
the electricity markets, and inevitably the producers, to a new source of uncertainty.
As an example of such variability, the electricity coming from Spanish wind turbines
dropped from 8 GW at 10pm of February 18th, 2012, to 2 GW at the same hour of
the subsequent day. This drop is almost equivalent to full production of six Spanish
nuclear power plants. Even if accurate forecasts are available, the wind power output
may depart suddenly from the predicted levels. The intermittency associated with wind
power is unavoidable. This non-dispatchable production faces the electricity markets to
a new phenomenon, which is really challenging.
In addition to that, power systems are subjected to some other contingencies such
as load variation, interchange transactions, line availability, deviations of the generating
units from their set points and so on. These events also have an impact in the power
balance control. After such an event, the power system must be readjusted and driven
to a new state of equilibrium. Traditionally, this readjustment is achieved by ramping
up or down some generation. Load-shedding can also be an option, but it should be
considered as a last resort. Then, the increasing non-dispatchable production such as
wind power and other renewable sources, is introducing much more variability into the
systems. One potential solution to cope with this problem is to increase the system
flexibility through adding conventional flexible producers such as gas-fired generating
units, demand-side flexibility resources (for example, demand response programs), and
so on. The former is the focus of this proposal. In this way, these fast-ramping generating

units will try to ensure that no load is lost if the wind production drops in the future
high wind-penetrated systems.
However, the electricity market is cleared based on bids and offers submitted by all
consumers and all producers, respectively.
The offering decisions consist on a price,

Then, the market operator clears the market based on

In an electricity market with significant share of wind power, a conventional flexible

to make a business based on its ramp capability, which allows that unit to quickly
change its production level (either up or down). Hence, it might be interesting for this
producer to withhold its ramp rate capability in certain situations if with that its profit is
maximized. Accordingly, this flexible producer can strategically offer its ramp capability
to the market as well as its strategic price and quantity offers. These three offers may
alter the market clearing procedure, so that the flexible producer‚Äôs profit is maximized.
This master thesis looks to the electricity markets from the conventional flexible
producer‚Äôs point of view, and its aim is to develop a mathematical tool that assists that
producer to maximize its expected profit through making informed offering decisions.
The main contribution of this proposal is to derive the most beneficial ramp offers for
the flexible producer, which has not been maturely addressed in the technical literature.
1.2
Electricity markets and renewable resources
This section briefly describes the key mechanism of electricity markets and the impacts
renewable resources have on them.
First of all, it is necessary to start with the traditional day-ahead market, which is
an auction that takes place the day prior to the energy delivery on an hourly basis. It
is usually based on an electricity pool where producers submit their production offers,
which consists on blocks of production at minimum selling prices and, on the other hand,
consumers and retailers submit their consumption bids, which also consist on blocks of
energy but at maximum buying prices. Then, the market operator clears the market.
This means that all bids and offers are sorted by price and an optimization model is run
to find the cheapest generating combination that can supply the forecasted demand and
meet all system constraints (transmission capacity of the lines, ramping limits of the
generating units and so on). As a result, the market operator stablishes the day-ahead
clearing-price and the production and consumption schedules. Nevertheless, and due to
transmission constraits, it is usual to talk in terms of locational marginal prices (LMPs)
instead of an unique clearing-price. Then, one LMP is associated with each node of
the network. In case of transmission congestion, different LMPs are expected amog the
nodes of the networks [21].

In a competitive market, it is expected that producers offer at the marginal costs
of their units.
These marginal costs are usually given by the fuel costs.
Hence, all
the scheduled generators will be paid at the marginal cost of the last generator that
is needed to meet the demand. This marginal cost of the last scheduled unit is what
was referred as clearing price. Nevertheless, electricity markets are mostly imperfect in
terms of competition. Then, some producers, so-called strategic producers, are prone to
exercise the market clearing in their own benefit by submitting strategic offers to the
market.
Moreover, due to the fact that the marginal costs associated with the wind power are
zero (or even negative if premiums apply), wind power producers enters near the bottom
of the supply curve, and thus, they are scheduled before conventional generators. This
circumstance is referred in the technical literature as merit-order effect. Therefore, higher
the wind production, more to the right the supply curve presented in Figure 1.1 is shifted.
This also means that it is possible to achieve lower prices in the system and higher
amounts of energy scheduled. On the other hand, lower the wind power production,
higher the market-clearing prices and lower the energy scheduled. An example of these
situations can be found in Figure 1.2. Then, regions with high wind production tend

transmission lines are not unusual in periods with high wind power generation.

After the day-ahead market, there are some other energy markets that allow to
make adjustments on the contracts forwarded in the day-ahead market.
These are
known as intra-day markets or adjustment markets and are mainly based on bilateral
contracts. Here, it is possible to correct the decisions made in the day-ahead market
due to unexpected events or mistakes made by producers or consumers when submitting
their offers. Later on, usually several minutes before the energy delivery, it is possible
to find the balancing, real-time or regulation market, in which the system operator tries
to restore the system from imbalances. Usually, this market is also cleared based on an
auction and it is also really important for those generating units that cannot accurately
predict their production [21].
Wind power also has an influence on these markets. For example, if in the balancing
market there is a deficit in the power production and the wind power production is also
lower than expected, other producers have to provide up regulation. In this situation,
wind power will be penalized. Similar reasonings can be applied to some other situations
to analyse the influence of the wind power in the balancing market. This is summarized
in Figure 1.3. One important consequence of the increase in the penetration of wind
power is that the share of the balancing costs in the overall system operation increases
as well. This increase can even become critical if there is not enough flexible generation
capacity in the balancing market to deal with uncertainty [21]. However, this master
thesis is not focused on balancing markets, even tough all these markets are highly
interrelated, and thus, it is necessary to understand the way they all work. Further
details can be found in [1] and [21].

As the penetration of renewable generation increases, so do the uncertainty and
variability associated with the net load, which is the difference between the forecasted
demand and the expected production from non-controllable resources. The changes in
the net load between consecutive time intervals define the required ramping capability.

Then, several circumstances such as variations in the demand or renewable production,
interchange transactions, deviations from the set points and so on may lead to ramp
==PAGE:24==
6

shortages. In this cases, real-time markets do not have enough ramp capabilities among
the online generators to deal with the ramp necessity of the system. Also in this cases,
offline generators are not capable to respond fast enough. Then, when there is not enough
ramp capability, the system will lose the equilibrium between supply and demand and
imbalances will take place.
Therefore, system flexibility indicates that sufficient ramp capability is reserved in
order to respond against unexpected changes in the net load. Hence, the market clearing
process has the challenge to ensure that there is enough flexibility among the generating
resources at any time [22]. One potential solution for increasing the flexibility of the
system is adding conventional flexible producers, such as gas-fired units.
These are
peaking units that have high ramp rates, relatively low minimum generation levels and
they can also shut-down and start-up rather fast. Nevertheless, they have high operating
costs.
This aspect together with the merit-order effect makes these units not really
attractive for investors. What is more, if these units are used to provide extra flexibility
into the systems, they will be operating out of their optimum, resulting also in higher
costs. Therefore, some incentives are needed to encourage the investment in flexible
generation [21] [22].
In addition, it is worth mentioning that wholesale electricity markets have several
practices to compensate situations of ramp shortages: increasing reserves, withholding
certain generation capacity, starting some fast start-up generating units (such as gas
turbines).
However, these practices tend to be costly and may cause some market
distortion. Therefore, it is important for the Independent System Operators (ISOs) to
achieve additional flexibility from dispatchable generating through the market clearing
process. In this sense, it is interesting to cite the new flexible ramping products (which
will be discussed in the following section) and the look-ahead economic dispatch [22].
This look-ahead economic dispatch basically considers the system condition for several
intervals and prepares some generators for possible ramping situations [36].
Finally, some other important facts have to be considered.
Firstly, wind power
(the renewable energy with higher penetration in the current power systems) tends to
ramp in the opposite direction as load does and this increases even more the need of
ramping capability [23]. Secondly, smart grids or demand response initiatives may help
ISOs dealing with the variability of the wind power but, at the same time, they are
introducing another source of uncertainty in the demand side. Thirdly, hydro can also
be considered as a flexible resource.
Nevertheless, this technology is limited by the
environmental legislations and water availability. Lately, the study presented in [34]
states that the quality of long-term predictions has a significant effect on the need of
fast-ramping generating units and provides further details about impact of the wind
power in the power system and the energy markets.

1.3 Ramp products overview

1.3
Ramp products overview
In order to assure real-time operation, some ISOs are proposing flexible ramping products
in their electricity markets. In this sense, it is necessary to cite the flexiramp products
proposed by the California ISO (CAISO) and the ramp capability submitted by the
Midcontinent ISO (MISO). Both of them are quite similar and aim to increase the energy
and operation reserves markets in order to schedule extra amounts of flexible capacity
and to motivate the investment in flexible generation [31]. Other american ISOs also have
interesting proposals. Even the main scope of the Pennsylvania-New Jersey-Maryland
Interconnection (PJM) is different from CAISO, its capacity performance product also
provides incentives for more reliable capacity as well as flexible ramping capacity. In
the same sense, the Electric Reliability Council of Texas (ERCOT), the New York ISO
(NYISO) and the ISO of New England (ISONE) are trying to improve their existing
capacity incentive mechanisms to motivate reliable and flexible ramping capacity [18].
Figure 1.5: ISOs of the United States. Source: Federal Energy Regulatory Commission
(FERC) website.
In general, it is possible to define a ramp product as the designation of capacity in a
time interval t to meet an unexpected net load variation in the subsequent time intervals,
either up or down. It is important to highlight that up-ramp and down-ramp capabilities
are different products and that the purpose of this procedure is being able to optimally
dispatch this reserved capacity in advance.
Nevertheless, these products should not
be confused with frequency regulation or spinning reserves.
Frequency regulation is
==PAGE:26==
8
1 Introduction
provided by Automaic Generation Controllers (AGC) and allows to react quickly to
real-time deviations in the frequency due to small imbalances between generation and
demand. For its part, spinning reserve is designed to face specific contingencies such as
line tripping or generator outages.
Opposed to these infrequent large net load variations, flexible ramping products
are designed for dealing with variations in the forecasts from the non-controllable assets
(such as load, variable generation and scheduled interchange) that happens continuously.
Therefore, it is expected that ramp products are called more often than the traditional
spinning reserves [31].
Flexible ramping products are achieved through an optimal
dispatch that positions them to be able to respond in a better way to variations in the
forecasts. This procedure may have additional costs but, at the same time, it reduces
the frequency of scarcity events and increases the responsiveness of the system. For that
reason, attempting to erase all scarcity events with this approach can be very expensive
and might not be practical. MISO states in [23] that there could be infrequent events
with high variations beyond the level of ramp capability that can be just achieved at
reasonable expense.
Moreover, these American ISOs stand up for these new flexiramp products instead
of the traditional regulation services because the fact that the increasing regulation
will lock up more capacity which will not be available in the energy dispatch, resulting
in more system imbalances. In addition, regulation services are rewarded by penalty
prices, even if there is no operational issue, whereas flexiramp products will influence
the electricity prices based on an optimization model. Something similar happens in
the case of increasing the spinning reserve for dealing with uncertainty. CAISO states
in [35] that it will have two sources of payments: the opportunity cost and the energy
payment if the reserve is dispatched in the real-time operation.
The previous paragraph shows that one of the objectives pursued by the ISOs when
developing ramp products was, indeed, achieving cheaper ramping capability. Besides,
it should also be pointed out that adjustments made by system operators to ramp up or
down some generation in real-time operation are not based on optimization algorithms,
and thus, these decisions are not the most economical ones. In addition, if ramp products
are not reserved in advance, the chances to find time intervals with insufficient ramp
capabilities are more likely. Moreover, this ramp market also tries to send sufficient
market-based price signals to provide incentives for conventional flexible generators
who provides fast ramping capabilities. Furthermore, these American ISOs state that
ramping products will also lead to fewer instances of scarcity pricing because they can
improve the positioning of resource dispatch to raise the amount of deployable energy
when needed [23].
Now, it is interesting to take a look to some of the ISOs proposals for ramping
products. Firstly, the Midcontinent ISO (MISO) [23] [24] has defined a ramp capability
to cover the net load uncertainty and advocates for implementing ramp capability
products in all energy markets (from day-ahead to real-time dispatch).
MISO also
stands up for using historical statistical data from net load to quantify the need of ramp
==PAGE:27==
1.4 Approach
9
capability in the day-ahead market. Moreover, MISO clarifies that the ramp capability
model works together with the multi-interval dispatch. First, the ramp capability model
reserves rampable capacity for possible net load variations. Then, the multi-interval
dispatch allows prepositioned ramping units to maximize the economic efficiency in
meeting the expected future interval requirements. Finally, the pricing of these ramping
products is based on opportunity costs.
CAISO proposal for flexiramp products [35] [17] is more focused on the real-time
dispatch. It came up because CAISO found that, in certain occasions, there was not
enough ramping capability and flexibility in the real-time economic dispatch to cope
with 5-minute changes in the net load. Actually, CAISO was running a multi-interval
optimization model able to look several intervals ahead in order to forecast the ramping
needs. Nevertheless, in real-time operation it happened that the current need of ramping
capability was greater than the forecast.
Therefore, flexible ramping products were
created to provide some margin on top of that forecast. Finally, it is worth mentioning
that flexiramp products prices are also based on marginal opportunity costs.
1.4
Approach
In order to guess the most beneficial ramp offer the strategic producer should submit to
the electricity market, an optimization model must be developed. This model is settled
from the strategic producer point of view. This producer is considered to be a price-taker,
which means that his decisions have an influence in the generation of the market clearing
prices. It is worth mentioning that just the day-ahead market is considered here. Even
tough there are different trading floors for electricity, the largest volume is traded in the
day-ahead market in most of the real-world energy markets. Besides, congestion-based
strategies can take place due to transmission constraints. In case of congestion in some
of the lines, the LMPs may differ from one bus to the next.
All of this is achieved thanks to a bilevel programming approach. Here, two different
optimization problems are considered. On one hand, the upper-level problem represents
the maximization of the strategic producer profit and, on the other hand, the market
clearing is considered as a constraint of the upper-level problem and it defines the
lower-level optimization problem. Even tough these are two different problems, they
are interdependent and must be solved jointly.
However, bilevel optimization problems cannot be directly implemented in general
software.
Therefore, and due to the fact the lower-level problem is presumed to be
continuous, linear, and thus, convex, it can be replaced by its Karush-Kuhn-Tucker
(KKT) conditions and then, transformed into a mathematical program with equilibrium
constraints (MPEC). Nevertheless, this MPEC is not linear, so it must be linearized
before being implemented in the software. For that purpose, the strong duality theorem,
some of the KKTs and the Big M linearization technique are going to be used. This
finally results in a mixed-interger linear programming (MILP) problem which is the one
==PAGE:28==
10

that is going to be implemented in a conventional branch-and-cut solver. Further details
can be found in Appendix A.
This procedure is based on a deterministic approach.
Nevertheless, a stochastic
model is also going to be developed in this thesis in order to assess the variability of the
wind power. This stochastic version is achieved by incorporating uncertainty into the
deterministic model. This leads to multiple lower-level problems that represent all the
possible realizations of the uncertain parameters considered.
Despite the fact uncertainty helps us studying the influence of the wind power
variability, the consideration of different scenarios also increases the computational
burden of the problem and might lead to intractability. Therefore, different linearization
techniques will be considered as well as non-linear programming methods.
Finally, it should be considered the way this master thesis is organized. Chapter 1
introduces the topic and describes the learning objectives the student must fulfil at the
end of the project. Chapter 2 presents the bilevel problem formulation and its further
mathematical development together with the main assumptions and simplifications that
have been considered.
Besides, this chapter also provides the results obtained after
running the model based on an illustrative example. Chapter 3 includes the extension
of the previous model to a stochastic version and the verification of that model with the
illustrative example presented in the previous chapter. Chapter 4 provides results from
a realistic case of study. Chapter 5 presents a deterministic multi-period simulation, and
the influence of the time horizon in which the ramp applies is analysed. Finally, Chapter
6 concludes this thesis and discusses the future work and further improvements.
1.5
Literature review
Even tough the possibility of submitting strategic offers in terms of ramp has not been
maturely addressed in the technical literature yet, the ramping products have been
widely studied.
First of all, it is worth mentioning that the exercise of the market power on ramp rate
in wind-integrated power systems has been analysed in [20]. The methodology followed
in this paper was based on ramp-rate Stackelberg game model with several leaders, which
are the strategic producers, and one follower, which is the market operator running a
security-constrained economic dispatch. Despite the fact that this is not the approach
followed in this master thesis, the study presented in [20] provides some interesting
conclusions. It states that generators are prone to exercise their market power in terms of
ramp in case of major contingencies and in the presence of network constraints. Moreover,
it also proves that strong intermittencies associated with the wind power can introduce
contingencies into the system, and thus, a higher penetration of the wind power carries
a proneness to bid strategically as well. On the other hand, the authors of [25] propose
different penalty approaches that aim to discourage bidders from strategic behaviour in
terms of ramp offers. They also remark the influence of the time period in which the
ramp offer applies.

Apart from that, some other papers face the topic of ramping products but from

focuses on the decisions in the real-time operation and discusses how flexiramp products
can increase market efficiency by implementing both deterministic and stochastic models.
The authors also state that the system has to be simulated under different conditions
in order to determine the optimal level of flexiramp requirements. Finally, it is worth
mentioning the robust optimization economic dispatch model proposed in [29]. Here,
the authors compare the results obtained with this model and a deterministic one and
conclude that the probability of insufficient ramp capability in the system is lower when
using a robust optimization approach.
Until here, an overview of the current state of art related to ramping products has
been presented. Apart from that, it is worth mentioning some papers that have studied
the wind power impact in the current power systems. These studies present interesting
methods and conclusions that we can use as guidelines for our current analysis.
First of all, a security-constrained unit commitment with volatile wind generation
is presented in [32].

considered by implementing different scenarios and transmission constraints are also

2.1
Model description and assumptions
2.1.1
Description
The model has been settled from the point of view of the conventional flexible producer.
This conventional producer trades in an electricity pool by making hourly offers for the
day-ahead market and seeks to maximize its profit through making strategic offering
decisions in terms of price, quantity and ramp offer. Prices and quantities are offered
hourly meanwhile the ramp offers apply for longer periods of time such as months,
trimesters, semesters and so on.
On one hand, this producer is able to impact the market clearing outcomes by acting
strategically. On the other hand, the conventional producer‚Äôs profit comes from selling
the production of its generating units in the electricity market so its revenues depend
also on the market clearing.
Figure 2.1: Structure of the bilevel model.
==PAGE:34==
16
2 Base model
Therefore, guessing the optimal offering strategy and the market clearing are two
highly interrelated but different problems that must be solved jointly.
Despite of that, the market clearing can be considered as a constraint of the problem
that search the optimal offering strategy. Nevertheless, the market clearing is itself an
optimization problem in pursuit of the maximization of the social welfare subjected to
several constraints. Because of that, the overall problem is modelled by using a bilevel
programming approach. Hence, the search of the optimal offering strategy represents
the upper-level problem, which is subjected to the market clearing, and that is the
lower-level problem. This structure was schematically represented in Figure 2.1.
Then, the upper-level problem is an optimization problem so it is comprised by its
objective function and several constraints.
Firstly, the objective function seeks to maximize the strategic producer‚Äôs profit
and has two different terms: revenues from the electricity market and costs of energy
production. Secondly, the constraints of this upper-level problem are the non-negativity
conditions of the offers (prices and ramps), the limitations in the maximum ramp offers
and, of course, the lower level problem.
Moreover, it is worth to mention that by
considering the market clearing as a constraint for the upper-level problem, the producers
are actually anticipating the impact of their offers on the pool.
On the other hand, the lower-level problem represents the clearing of the market
and the price formation. The objective function in this case equals to the maximization
of the social welfare (which is the same than minimizing the minus social welfare) and
the constraints correspond to the power balance per node, limitations on the power
generation and transmission capacities, bounds for the voltage angles and ramping limits
for the different generating units. The solution of this problem provides the upper-level
problem with the LMPs and the production levels on an hourly basis. In this way, the
upper-level problem depends on the lower-level decision variables.
This structure of the bilevel problem is summarized in Figure 2.2.
Then, as it was explained before, the procedure of solving this bilevel program implies
the formulation of the MPEC. If the lower-level problem is assumed to be continuous
and convex, the MPEC can be obtained by replacing the lower-level problem by its
KKT conditions. After that, the non-linearities must be linearised to obtain the MILP
problem, which can be solved by GAMS.
Finally, it should be noted that this base model has a deterministic approach and
does not consider uncertainty.
In the following chapter, a stochastic model is proposed by the addition of different
scenarios and some uncertain parameters.

2.1.2
Assumptions
In order to clarify the mathematical formulation of the model, here are listed the main
assumptions considered:
1. Forward and bilateral contracts are not modelled and are assumed to be considered
by both producers and consumers before submitting their offers to the market.
In fact, producers and consumers consider these contracts when defining their
capacity limits. This assumption is not far from the real world because this kind
of contracts are signed before the day-ahead market takes place.
2. The network topology of the system is modelled by using the dc approximation
without losses [13] and it does not change over the time period of study. This
formulation allows us to obtain a continuous and linear problem.
==PAGE:36==
18
2 Base model
3. The power production of the generating units can be divided into blocks. In that
case, the summation of the different blocks cannot be higher than the overall
capacity of the plant. Moreover, different operating costs can be defined for each
block. In any case, the operating costs are given by linear expressions.
4. The ramping limits apply for the overall production of the generating unit, without
distinguishing different blocks of energy.
5. The output of the upper-level problem is the offering strategy of the conventional
producer in terms of price and ramp. The quantity offer is considered to be fixed
and already defined. This enables us to study the quite indirect benefit a producer
can obtain by acting strategically in terms of ramp offers but, on the other hand,
it implies losing generality. Then, it must be remarked that all the conclusions
presented in this thesis are subjected to this assumption and it must be kept in
mind that the possibilities of acting strategically are even greater in the real- world
electricity markets.
6. Regarding the strategic producer‚Äôs price offer to the electricity market, it should
be pointed out that it will not equal, in general, to the marginal cost of production.
Nevertheless, the ramp offer has a greater chance of being equal to the maximum
ramp capability and will only differ from that value in specific cases, as it will be
discussed in the results section.
7. A strategic behaviour is only assumed for the generators the strategic conventional
producer owns. The rest of producers behave non-strategically, and thus, they offer
to the market at their marginal production costs and maximum ramp capabilities.
8. The expected outcomes from the lower-level problem are the LMPs in the different
nodes of the network and the production levels.
The hourly prices from the
electricity market are obtained as the dual variables associated with the energy
balance constraints. These LMPs represent the increment of the social welfare as
a result of a marginal increment in the demand at a certain bus.

Despite the fact that this MPEC is a single-level mixed-integer problem, it is far from
being solved since it includes some non-linearities:
1. The second term of the objective function (2.14a): X = őĽtnP S

2. The complementary conditions (2.16g - 2.16u).
Different procedures must be applied in each case to solve the non-linearities. For the
first one, the strong duality theorem [3] is going to be put into practice. After that, the

overall solution of the model due to the fact that the use of binary variables exponentially
increases the computational time. As a result, a MILP problem is obtained and this is
the final model that is going to be implemented in GAMS.

With this toy-model, it has been proved that a generator capable to offer strategically

that decision it rationally expects a higher profit.
On the other hand, it has also been proved that the price offer is much more flexible
than the ramp offer so, from now on, just three cases are going to be analysed: the
non-strategic situation, the situation in which it is possible to offer strategically just in
terms of price and the one in which it is also possible to offer a strategic ramp.

3.2.3
Preliminary discussion
In this section, the deterministic model has been upgraded to an stochastic one. With
this model, it has been proved that the extra profit a strategic producer can obtain from
the market when offering strategically in terms of ramp is greater when considering the
wind variability.
Moreover, if the different wind scenarios are considered separately, the average of
the ramp offers that are found with them is not necessarily the same than the ramp
offer found when considering the four scenarios as a whole. Therefore, the model finds
a trade-off among the most profitable ramp for each scenario.
This also applies for
the price offer although in this toy-model there was a common price offer for the four
scenarios considered.

CHAPTER 5
Multi-period model
As it was stated in the first chapter, ramp-rates are defined for longer time horizons
than quantity and price offers. Therefore, a multi-period simulation must be carried out
in order to analyse the influence of the time horizon in which the ramp offer applies.
5.1
Model description
It is important to recapitulate here that the market operator clears the market for the
whole period through an optimization algorithm. That means, the strategic producer
needs to find the most suitable ramp offer for a certain time horizon and it is not possible
to change this offer until the whole time horizon is over. Longer the time horizon, less the
flexibility the strategic producer has to submit its ramp offer. Therefore, it is expected
that a longer time horizon avoids greater exercise on the market clearing [25].
In order to carry out this multi-period simulation, the deterministic base model has
been updated to a multi-period version by the addition of a new index (r) that gathers
every 24-hour period in different days.
Nevertheless, and in order to avoid computational intractability, the different days
have been grouped together into what has been referred as representative days. These
are days with similar characteristics and thus, they can be treated as blocks of days.
Hence, months are divided into different blocks of days instead of having 30 days.
Different weights are assigned to each representative day to represent the number of days
that form the block. These weights are represented by the term őďr in equation (5.1a).

5.3.2
Results
With the conditions defined in the previous section, several multi-period simulations
have been carried out. Remember that the purpose is to analyse the influence of the
ramp‚Äôs time horizon in the strategic producer profit. For that reason, different time
horizons for the ramp offer are going to be used to define the cases of study of this
section: one, two, three and six months.
In the first one, there is a time horizon of six months and that means that it is just
possible to submit one ramp offer to the the market in the whole period. The second
time horizon is established in three months, and thus, it is possible to submit two ramp
offers along the total six-month period, one each trimester. The third one is a two-month
time horizon offer, so it is possible to submit a ramp offer in the beginning of January,
March and May. Finally, in the last option, it is possible to submit a ramp offer at the
beginning of every month. Logically, longer the time horizon in which the ramp offer
applies, lower the flexibility the strategic producer has, and thus, lower revenues are
expected as well.
The total profit the strategic producer can obtain when a six-month ramp applies
is 4523219 $. For example, this is the optimal revenue the strategic producer should
expect in a CAISO scenario after six months.
Then, the next simulation has been defined based on a three-month time horizon.
In this case, two multi-period simulations have been carried out, one for each trimester.
The optimal profit the strategic producer can expect after the three first months is
2487106 $. Then, the productions of the last hour of the trimester are used to feed
the multi-period simulation for the second trimester, in which a profit of 2042162 $ is
expected. Therefore, the profit after the six months is given by the summation of the
previous two: 4529268 $. As it was anticipated, the profit has increased due to the
higher flexibility provided by the shorter time horizon.
The third case of study allows to change the ramp offer at the beginning of each
two-month period. Therefore, it is necessary to run three different simulations and to
transfer the last-hour power data twice. Then, 1702629 $ are obtained as profit in the
first two-months block, 1437675 $ in the second block and 1413734 $ in the last one. In
total, this makes a 4554038 $ profit after six months. Again, this profit is higher than
the results obtained in previous simulations.
In the last case of study, it is possible to submit a different ramp offer at the beginning
of each month. The profits obtained at the end of each month are 974331 $, 758456 $,
859240 $, 688589 $, 643521 $ and 890941 $. Then, the total profit after the six months
of study is 4815080 $, which much bigger than those obtained in the previous cases.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
6.1
General conclusion
This master thesis presents a mathematical tool that assist a strategic producer to
maximize its expected profit through making informed offering decisions.
First of all, a description of the key mechanism of the electricity markets is provided.
This sets out the basis on which the optimization model is built. Then, the impacts
of renewable resources on the electricity markets are discussed. This analysis is mainly
focus on the wind power, the renewable energy with the greatest penetration in most
of the energy systems.
Straightaway, the need of additional flexibility and ramping
capabilities in the electricity market is highlighted and that leads to the development
of flexible ramping products. Therefore, an overview about how these new products are
implemented in different markets is provided. All of this establishes the framework in
which the strategic producer is making its business.
In order to assist the strategic producer to maximize its expected profit, a bilevel
optimization program is developed. With this model, the strategic producer is able to
find the most profitable ramp and price offer for a given market situation.
The procedure to implement the bilevel model to different systems starts with its
transformation into an MPEC. This can be done due to the fact the lower-level problem
is continuous and linear (and thus convex) so it can be replaced by its KKT conditions.
Then, the MILP is obtained by applying the strong duality theorem, some of the KKTs
and the Big M linearization technique.
In a first stage, the bilevel model is based on a deterministic approach and is run on
a two-bus illustrative example. Within this illustrative example, the model is run with
different degrees of flexibility in the way the strategic producer can offer to the market.
By doing this, the objective of evaluating the model against a benchmark of a completely
inflexible producer and a competitive flexible one is fulfilled. Here, it has been proved
that, indeed, it is possible to earn an extra profit when it is possible to offer strategically
in terms of ramp to the market. Moreover, the simulations performed with this two-bus
system demonstrate that the strategic producer does not always need to exercise the
market clearing in order to achieve higher profits. In fact, in this example, when the
possibility of offering strategic ramps is added to the price offer, both the social welfare
and the strategic producer profit are increased. Finally, the degree of flexibility of both
offers (price and ramp) is examined. Logically, the price offer is much more flexible than
the ramp offer because the second one applies for longer periods of time. Then, the
==PAGE:86==
68
6 Conclusion
results show that higher revenues are obtained when offering strategically in terms of
price compared with the situation in which it is just possible to offer strategic ramps.
On a second stage, the bilevel model is applied to the same illustrative example but
with a stochastic approach. Now the idea is to show the influence of the variability
of the wind power on the decisions made by the strategic producer. The uncertainty
associated with the wind production is incorporated to the model by a set of plausible
scenarios. Here, the simulations prove that, when the influence of the variability of the
wind power is taken into account, the extra profit that can be obtained when both ramp
and price offers apply is higher. Moreover, the average of the strategic offers found when
considering the different wind scenarios separately is not necessarily the same than when
considering those scenarios as a whole.
After that, on a third stage, the previous two models are applied to a more realistic
example. More precisely, this study case consists on a 14-bus system where there is a
total of five generators: one wind farm and four conventional generators, two owned
by the rest of producers and the other two are property of the strategic producer. The
deterministic example shows similar conclusions to the ones obtained in the illustrative
example. On the other hand, the complexity of the stochastic version of the model
is such that it is not possible to deal with the huge number of binary variables and
the corresponding computational burden. Then, different linearization techniques are
analysed in order to reduce the number of binary variables, and thus, solve the problem.
Nevertheless, the performance of these alternative methods is not better than the Big M
approach.
Therefore, a non-linear version of the stochastic model is developed and
used to obtain a reasonable optimal solution.
It has been said reasonable due to
the fact that it is not possible to assure that is a global optimum. However, several
simulations are performed, so it is possible to conclude that the solution obtained is a
local optimum equal to or close enough to the global one. The main conclusions obtained
with the deterministic simulation in this 14-bus system confirm the results discussed in
the previous stage. Nevertheless, and due to the fact that it is not possible to assure
that a global optimum was obtained in the stochastic version, the comparison between
both model versions is not conclusive.
After that, the fifth stage performs a multi-period simulation that shows the influence
of the time horizon of the ramp offer. As expected, longer time horizons dramatically
decrease the flexibility the strategic producer has when submitting its ramp offers to the
market, and thus, lower profits are obtained.
Finally, it is possible to conclude this thesis by mentioning that the obtained results
are really important for the future energy systems, in which the penetration of wind
power and other renewable energies is expected to continue increasing. Therefore, more
nimble power systems and electricity markets are required [4] in order to deal with
the increasing variability and uncertainty. Wholesale market design becomes critical for
facing these circumstances due to the fact that good protocols for the electricity dispatch
will result in more reliable and economic energy systems. As it has been shown in this
master thesis, flexible ramping products can be an interesting solution when dealing
==PAGE:87==
6.2 Future work
69
with wind variability and other uncertainties. However, this master thesis also shows
the proneness of flexible generators to submit strategic offers to the market in order to
increase their profits by means of exercising the market clearing. Nevertheless, these
strategic offers can even decrease the social costs as the same time they increase the
profit of these flexible producers, especially when the ramp offers are allowed. Therefore,
flexible ramping products must be designed carefully without forgetting the fact that
they can improve the overall performance of the energy markets.
6.2
Future work
The results obtained with this master thesis open several interesting proposals for future
works.
First of all, it is necessary to look for a more accurate solution for the stochastic
model applied to the 14-bus system. This can be achieved by going into decomposition
techniques. In this sense, Benders decomposition can serve our purpose. Moreover, once
this technique is working on the 14-bus system, it would be interesting to apply it to
the IEEE 24-bus test system.
On the other hand, it is also interesting to incorporate the strategic performance of
the rest of producers. That can be achieve by transforming the price and ramp offers and
the productions of the rest of producers into uncertain parameters and then, defining
them as a set of plausible scenarios as it was done when representing the wind power
variability. Nevertheless, and due to the computational issues faced in this thesis, it
would not be easy to obtain a solution to this problem. It is much more attractive to
consider multiple MPECs, one representing each strategic producer and then, solve the
model as a whole. Thereby, the strategic decisions made by one of the producer can
influence the rest of producers, and thus, all the strategies are interrelated by the market
clearing algorithm. This combination of MPECs is referred in the technical literature as
an equilibrium problem with equilibrium constraints (EPEC).
Apart from these mathematical issues, it might also be interesting to investigate
the effect of flexible ramping products in the different electricity markets. Therefore,
this master thesis may be extended by the consideration of bilateral contracts, future
markets and the intra-day and balancing markets.
