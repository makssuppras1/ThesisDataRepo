Preface 
The present document is a Master thesis project of 30 ECTS, which is obligatory 
requirement for completing the MSc Program of Environmental Engineering at the 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The MSc thesis project was conducted at 
DTU, in the department of Environment from February 2017 to June 2017 under the 
supervision of Senior Researcher Anders Damgaard and PhD students Giorgia Faraca 
and Susanna Andreasi Bassi.  
The MSc thesis investigates the environmental impacts of waste management 
alternatives for Polyvinylchloride (PVC) plastics.  
The document is divided into 5 main chapters. Chapter 1 presents the introduction and 
the objectives of the MSc thesis, Chapter 2 provides background information on PVC 
plastics. Chapter 3 provides the waste management scheme for PVC plastics waste in 
Europe and Denmark and Chapter 4 is a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of three common 
waste management options in Denmark. Chapter 5 presents the conclusions obtained 
from the study. A separate document named Supporting information (SI) contains all 
detailed values and processes of the LCA chapter.

Abstract 
PVC is a plastic with high popularity due to its advantageous properties amongst other 
durability and high range of applications. On the other hand, its production and disposal 
has been associated with harmful effects for human health and the environment. Except 
from PVC itself, the main impacts were identified on its additives, which include heavy 
metals and phthalates. Even if some improvements in production are made, namely 
removal of cadmium and lead and gradual replacement of phthalates that were proven 
toxic to reproduction, the disposal phase is still problematic due to the very long lifetime 
of PVC products. The PVC waste collected at the moment, was mostly manufactured 
with the old standards. With other words, the harmful additives are still present in the 
waste. A Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) was applied to define the most environmentally 
preferable end-of-life option considering the special case of Denmark. 
Three main waste management options were investigated. Mechanical recycling, 
incineration with energy recovery, and landfilling. The recycling scenario includes 
recycling of hard PVC in the Netherlands and landfilling of soft PVC in Denmark. 
Moreover, some of the recyclable impurities namely, PP and PE plastics, steel and 
aluminum metals, are separated from PVC and recycled as well. This scenario 
represents the main route of PVC treatment in Denmark. In incineration both soft and 
hard PVC are incinerated in a Waste to Energy Incineration (WTEI) plant in Denmark. 
Additionally, steel and aluminum are recovered from the ashes and recyled. The last 
scenario is a simple landfilling scenario of the entire amount of PVC in Denmark.  
Modelling was performed using EASETECH software developed at the Technical 
University (DTU). The Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) method was based on 
ILCD, recommended by the European Commission.  
The obtained results show that recycling is the preferable option in almost all impact 
categories excluding Depletion of Elements (DepE), Human Toxicity non Cancer 
(HTnc), and Ionizing Radiation (IonR). Incineration is most preferable option in IonR 
and DepE while Landfill is the preferable option in Human Toxicity non Cancer 
(HTnc). The Landfill has almost no impacts in all impact categories thus, it was not 
studied extensively.   
A sensitivity analysis showed that for recycling, the most sensitive parameters are the 
substitution ratio of PVC and the efficiency of the Material Recovery Facility (MRF). 
In Incineration the parameters characterized by high sensitivity were heat and power 
efficiencies of the WTEI plant. Moreover, it was observed that the form of electricity 
is a very important factor that can substantially affect the results in almost all impact 
categories. As a matter of fact, for global warming incineration has savings only when 
fossil electricity is substituted. When the energy substituted is renewable, the direct 
emissions of the plant overcome the savings from energy substitution. However, even 
if the choice of electricity affected the results, it did not affect the ranking of the options, 
indicating a generally robust conclusion on the most preferable disposal method 
A combined sensitivity analysis with renewable-based electricity showed that for global 
warming, recycling performs better than incineration independently of the MRF

efficiency or the substitution ratio. For the rest of the categories, excluding DepE, 
incineration was favored for low values of these factors.  
The results were proven robust when applying a difference LCIA method (ReCipe), as 
the ranking did not change. A change in the time horizon of the global warming 
potential from 100 to 20 years, did not affected the ranking in Global Warming (GW) 
as well. Last but not least, the ranking in DepE remained unchanged for the three 
different definitions of the reserve of elements i.e. reserve base, reserve ultimate, and 
reserve economic.  
In conclusion, the current scheme of recycling is the most effective option from an 
environmental point of view. The study also highlighted the importance of the recycling 
process technologies. This indicates that except from focusing in the collection of high 
amounts of PVC, technological efficiency should also be improved in order to increase 
the benefits of recycling. Moreover, looking at a renewable energy future, incineration 
should be gradually faced out as recycling performs better in most of the categories. 
Last but not least, new technologies should also focus in the removal of the harmful 
additives from the recycled material in order to avoid contamination of new material. 
This is essential in order to make the benefits of recycling even higher, as well as to 
avoid legislative and economic disadvantages in the sales of recycled material.

1. Introduction and Objectives 
Polyvinyl Chloride or PVC, as most commonly known, is a plastic with increasing 
popularity especially in the construction sector. PVC is an invaluable material in 
construction due to its versatile nature, its high resistance against chemical and 
environmental weathering and its long life durability. On the other hand, many concerns 
have arisen from its production and disposal. GreenPeace and other stakeholders have 
continuously expressed their opposition to PVC and its additives, which can be harmful 
to human and the environment (GreenPeace, 1997).  
Even if PVC has been characterized in the past as “the poison plastic” by GreenPeace 
(GreenPeace, 2004), a significant effort on eliminating its hazards has been made the 
past years. A combination of legislation, initiatives from the PVC industry and scientific 
research have laid the grounds for an improved and more environmentally friendly 
production and disposal. 
The most suitable disposal method can be controversial. According to the waste 
hierarchy recycling shall be preferred as it is an asset for avoiding the resources and the 
impacts that the virgin production entails. The disadvantage however is that hazardous 
additives that are now banned but can still be found in disposed material that were 
produced with prior legislations, linger longer in the life cycle. Moreover, legislations 
in some countries (e.g. Denmark) on additives’ concentrations will not allow an import 
and utilization of recycled material (Miljøstyrelse, 2015) leading to an economic and 
legislative disadvantage for recycling. Last but not least, it requires specific 
management schemes on collection, sorting and transportation. 
On the other hand, incineration of PVC requires no special collection schemes and 
pretreatment. Moreover, it could be a way to remove the hazardous elements used in 
the past from the loop. However, incineration requires modern incineration plants that 
can manage neutralization of excess hydrochloric acid, corrosion of equipment and 
release of dioxins in the flue gas, common challenges of PVC incineration (Rijpkema, 
1999).   
Finally, landfill is not preferred as a disposal method, by the waste hierarchy or by a 
sustainable point of view as it offers neither energy nor material recovery benefits. 
However, not significant impacts have been observed by the landfilling of PVC (EC, 
2000). 
The preferable option is case sensitive and a generic answer cannot be given. The 
ranking of the different options is likely to change depending on the system studied. 
For example, impurities, available technology, PVC application etc. can be defining 
factors. 
This study aims to give a contribution to the PVC debate, mainly focused in the case of 
Denmark. More specifically, the objectives of this study can be listed as the following: 
 1. Provide background information around PVC plastics  
2. Investigate the PVC waste management schemes in the EU and in Denmark.

for Denmark; namely mechanical recycling, incineration with energy recovery and

2. Background Information on PVC 
2.1 PVC History 
PVC or Polyvinyl Chloride was accidentally discovered multiple times (in 1839 for the 
first time) but it was patented in 1913 and started being used more widely in the 1950s. 
PVC became quickly an indispensable part of the construction industry due its many 
benefits: a vast variety of applications (pipes, window profiles, fittings, roof etc.), high 
durability and resistance to light and chemicals degradation etc. (PVC.org, 2017).  
2.2 PVC Structure & Manufacturing 
PVC is a polymer that is manufactured via polymerization of the monomer Vinyl 
Chloride (VCM) (PVC.org, 2017) also called chloroethylene 
(PubChem, 2017). The structure of PVC can be seen in Figure 1. PVC 
resin production can be acquired by three main methods: emulsion (E-
PVC), suspension (S-PVC) and bulk polymerization (B-PVC), 
depending on the product (PVC.org, 2017). The most common 
production is suspension polymerization (Saeki & Emura, 2002). VCM 
and consequently PVC consist of 43% hydrocarbons (ethylene) and 
57% rock salt (chlorine) (VinylPlus, 2014). Hydrocarbons are provided 
by the petrochemical industry while chlorine is provided by the chloro-
alkali industry (PVC.org, 2017).  
2.3 PVC Applications 
Two thirds of PVC is used in the construction sector with applications such as: window 
profiles, pipes and fitting, flooring, cables, coated fabrics, membranes, linings. Medical 
equipment etc. A smaller fraction is used in other applications like: packaging blisters, 
automotive, furniture, medical equipment, and rainwear (VinylPlus, 2014).

2.4 PVC Additives 
PVC is versatile and it additives can be used in many applications thanks to its additives. 
The commonly used additives include heat stabilizers, plasticizers, pigments, fillers, 
and lubricants but also anti-microbial and UV filters for some specific applications ( 
Wilkes, et al., 2005). The most important additives, in terms of applications, are heat 
stabilizers and plasticizers.

The most common elements used as heat stabilizers are Lead (Pb), Cadmium (Cd), tin 
(Sb) and a combination of Barium (Ba) with Zinc (Zn) or Calcium (Ca). The role of 
heat stabilizers, is to prevent the production of hydrochloric acid (HCl) during 
processing (Mesch, 2010). Processes like extrusion or molding need high temperatures

2.4.2 Plasticizers 
Plasticizers are used to make PVC flexible. PVC is rigid without the plasticizers, and 
they therefore play an important role on expanding its applications. Plasticizers are 
esters, usually phthalates and the most commonly found are diisononyl phthalate 
(DINP), diisodecyl phthalate (DIDP), Di(2-propylheptyl) phthalate (DPHP) and di(2-
ethylhexyl)phthalate (DEHP)  (Vinylplus, 2012).  
2.7 Positive aspects of PVC  
PVC can be used in a range of applications as it can be combined with a variety of 
additives. It is durable mechanically and it has a good resistance towards chemicals and 
weather phenomena (EC, 2000). These properties give PVC products a very long 
lifetime. The high thermal conductivity of PVC is also a considerable advantage 
(VinylPlus, 2014). It can be an asset for manufacturing of doors or windows, as its 
insulation properties can bring saving of energy for indoor heating (VinylPlus, 2014). 
Moreover, due to its high chlorine content, PVC is inherently a flame retardant and 
therefore performs better than other plastics in terms of flammability and flame 
propagation (Wright & Azapagic, 2005). 
As mentioned before, less than half of the PVC resin consists of oil, while other plastics 
base their production entirely on oil or natural gas. Under this perspective, PVC has a 
lower carbon footprint in production and it is considered a more sustainable plastic 
(VinylPlus, 2014).  
As long as disposal is concerned, tests on technical performance of PVC have shown 
that it can be recycled up to 7 times without any quality losses (Kristiansen, et al., 2010).  
The combination of its long service life, versatility and properties make PVC a very 
suitable material in the construction sector, while its lower needs in non-renewable 
resources and recyclability performance gives it an advantage within the sustainability 
concept. These possibilities have kept PVC in the market and regardless of its negative 
aspects. 
 
2.6 PVC Problems 
PVC has been criticized for being harmful to human and the environment. The aspects 
that makes it problematic on its production, use and End of Life (EoL) are the added 
stabilizers and its plasticizers (so called legacy additives) as well as its high chlorine 
content.  
2.6.1 Heat Stabilizers 
The most problematic stabilizers used in PVC are lead and cadmium.  According to 
REACH  and the Harmonized Classification and labelling (CLH) lead has be classified 
as follows “may damage fertility or the unborn child, causes damage to organs through 
prolonged or repeated exposure, is very toxic to aquatic life with long lasting effects, is 
==PAGE:14==

very toxic to aquatic life and may cause harm to breast-fed children.” The properties 
of concern are that is a PBT chemical (Persistent, Bioaccumulative and Toxic) and it 
has been classified as toxic to reproduction (R). For Cadmium the classification is “fatal 
if inhaled, is very toxic to aquatic life, is very toxic to aquatic life with long lasting 
effects, may cause cancer, causes damage to organs through prolonged or repeated 
exposure, is suspected of causing genetic defects, is suspected of damaging fertility or 
the unborn child and catches fire spontaneously if exposed to air”. The property of 
concern is that it is carcinogenic (C) (ECHA, 2017b).  
Moreover for rigid PVC produced before 2001, incineration could lead to a high amount 
of heavy metals in the residues like cadmium and lead. It was for example estimated 
that in 1993 PVC was responsible for 11% of the wastes cadmium and 1% of the lead 
( Johnsen, 2017).   
2.6.2 Plasticizers 
Other challenges involve the phthalates used in plasticizers for flexible PVC. Concerns 
have been raised that phthalates can pose risks to human health. More specifically, in 
2008 the main phthalates used at the time, DEHP, DBP, DIBP and BBP, were classified 
(Regulation (EC) No 1272/2008) as reproductive toxicants (DEPA, 2010).  
2.6.3 Chlorine 
As mentioned before, high temperatures can cause degradation of the PVC polymer 
which leads to the release of HCl. Heat stabilizers can prevent HCl during processing 
temperatures (170-180 oC) (PVC.org, 2017) however during incineration, where the 
temperatures are higher, chlorine is mainly transformed to HCl gas (Vehlow & Dalager, 
2010) which is a potent pollutant that can acidify both surface water and soil when 
deposed ( Evans , et al., 2011), it can be harmful to human and aquatic organisms 
(PubChem, 2017) and corrosive for metals (Aho, et al., 2008).  
The efficiency of the flue gas cleaning depends on the Air Pollution Control (APC). 
Modern incinerations are equipped with APC that can neutralize HCl and keep HCl gas 
emissions under the safety limits (Vehlow & Dalager, 2010). Problems only occur when 
high chlorine content waste is burnt in an uncontrolled manner ( Johnsen, 2017).  In 
addition, the correlation of the chlorine and the increased dioxin/ furan emissions (poly-
chlorinated-dibenzo- para-dioxins and -furans or PCDD/F) was a matter of debate in 
the past. However modern APC systems can abate PCDD/F and no concerns arise from 
it. 
On the other hand, corrosion is still a considerable limitation in the incineration of PVC 
waste. The HCl content limit that would not cause corrosion, is not known with 
certainty, as corrosion in not solely affected by chlorine. Some studies have shown that 
corrosion starts in volumes higher than 0.1 % wt of the waste (Rijpkema, 1999).  
During incineration the most important residues are bottom ash, fly ash and APC 
residues (Vehlow, 2002). The amount of bottom ash is technology specific and will not 
be affected by a higher content of chlorine in the waste. Moreover, since the larger share 
of chlorine is transferred to the flue gas the chlorine effect on the bottom ash is limited.  
Finally, the amount of APC residues due to the increased chlorine in the waste depends 
on whether the cleaning system is wet or semi-dry/dry. The wet system is the most

2.7 Legislation Concerning PVC 
2.7.1 Europe 
As far as packaging is concerned, the 2002/72/EC Directive for monomers in packaging 
applications allows the use of VCM in an EU level. However a standardized test that 
ensures that the transfer to food is non detectable (less than 0.1 ppm) should be 
performed first (EC, 2002). 
Lead stabilizers used in PVC are included in the Substances of Very High Concern 
(SVHC) candidate list (ECHA, 2015). Members States of ECHA can suggest a 
substance is identified as SVHC by preparing a dossier, upon European Commission 
(EC) request (ECHA, 2017c). Companies whose products are included in that list are 
legally obliged to immediately prepare some articles e.g. safety sheet for customers, 
sufficient information on the safe use, alternatives proposal etc. when the SCHV is 
present in a share of more than 0.1% (ECHA, 2017d). If the SVHC are not authorized, 
these substances cannot be placed in the market after a certain date (ECHA, 2017e). 
Cadmium stabilizers might not be used in mixtures and articles produced from PVC, 
with some exceptions. It can be present only when the mixtures are from recovered 
PVC and only up to an amount of 0.1% by weight. Moreover this rule applies only to 
specific applications in the construction industry only (e.g. non-drinking water pipes, 
cables, windows, rigid sheets etc.) (ECHA, 2012). The mentioned facts are part of the 
commission regulation (EU) No 835/2012. 
The 2005/84/EC directive on “Phthalate-containing soft PVC toys and childcare 
articles” banned the use of three specific phthalates DEHP/DOP, DBP and BBP which 
are classified as human reproduction toxicants used in toys for children (EC, 2005). The 
same phthalate were additionally regulated by the Restriction of Hazardous Substances 
(RoH) directive in 2015. Effective by 2019, all electric and electronic equipment (e.g 
PVC wires) might contain no more than 0.1% of the above mentioned phthalates. 
Moreover, DEHP/DOT is a SVHC, and therefore ECHA must be informed if it is 
present in more than 0.1% in any substance. Other than that, phthalates are not restricted 
all together but specific regulations apply to different applications. (Voskian & Syska, 
2016) 
2.3.2 Denmark 
Except from the above mentioned regulations, Denmark has an additional restrictions 
for lead. Even if REACH allows lead in concentrations less than 0.1% (1000 ppm), the 
Danish government has a stricter regulation were imports and sales of product with 
more than 100 ppm lead is not allowed1.

2.7 PVC Industry Initiatives 
2.7.1 Vinyl2010 
The most significant initiative towards the design of a sustainable path for PVC, was 
Vinyl2010 voluntary initiative (now VinylPlus). It was formed in 2000 by the European 
PVC industry and it created a 10-year plan for enhancing the sustainability of PVC. The 
commitment included PVC manufactures, stabilizer/plasticizer producers and 
converters, represented by their respective European associations2. The most important 
goals of Vinyl2010 were the following:  
 Compliance of European Council of Vinyl Manufacturers (ECVM) with the 
emissions standards for PVC Production 
 Phasing out of cadmium and 50% reduction of lead 
 Recycling of 200,000 tons of post-consumer PVC waste  
 Founding of research in the development of recovery and recycling technologies 
 Development of a social charter with the European Mine, Chemical and Energy 
Worker’s Federation (EMCEF) to improve training, health, safety and 
environmental standards.  
In 2010, Vinyl2010 had achieved the following (VinylPlus, 2017): 
1) Phasing out of cadmium from the production (2001) 
2) External verification of the EVCM E-PVC production (2005) 
3) Publish Environmental Product Declarations (EPD) for E-PVC and S-PVC. (2007) 
4) Decrease the lead in production by 50% in the EU-27 (2008) 
5) Environmental Impact Assessment of the major phthalates in plasticizers (2008) 
6) Recycling of 260,842 tons of post-consumer waste (2010)  
After the successful outcome of the initiative, Vinyl2010 decided to collaborate with 
the Natural Step and launch the new 10-year initiative, “VinylPlus” (VinylPlus, 2017). 
VinylPlus issues an externally audited yearly report describing the challenges and the 
goals of the initiative.  
2.7.2 Recovinyl 
An important achievement of Vinyl2010 was the establishment of Recovinyl. A 
monitoring committee with representatives from the European Commission, the 
European Parliament, trade unions, consumer associations and the European PVC 
industry (VinylPlus, 2017). It was formed in 2003 and its role is to encourage and assist 
PVC waste collection and recycling by acting as a mediator between recyclers and 
converters (Recovinyl, 2017). Recovinyl not only ensures that the yearly targets are 
reached, but also tries to improve the efficiency of the recycling process. To achieve 
that, a certification system is implemented for both recyclers and converters. Recyclers 
can be certified in the volume and quality of the recyclable material they deliver but

PVC waste in Denmark is either recycled or landfilled (Wellendorph, 2017). There is 
not an official law that bans incineration however waste operators try to avoid it as 
much as possible (Hansen, 2017). Rigid PVC is mostly recycled (Hansen, 2017) while 
soft PVC like credit cards, rubber boots, etc. which contain a high amount of phthalates

PVC comes from the construction industry and therefore the degree of impurities or 
misplaced plastics is not high (Hansen, 2017). An exception to this rule is PP or PE 
pipes which sometimes can be misunderstood as PVC even by professionals (Hansen, 
2017).

4. Life Cycle Assessment 
This chapter is a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of three main End of Life (EoL) 
treatment methods of PVC waste. The points discussed in this chapter are Goal 
Definition (4.1) Scope Definition (4.2), Life Cycle Inventory (4.3), Life Cycle Impact 
Assessment (LCIA) Results and Interpretation (4.4) and Sensitivity and Uncertainty 
Analysis (4.5). 
4.1 Goal Definition 
 
4.1.1 Intended Application 
This LCA is a comparative study, and its intended application is to compare and assess 
the environmental impacts of three different EoL options for PVC waste; namely 
mechanical recycling, incineration with energy recovery and landfilling in Denmark.  
4.1.2 Method, Assumptions and limitations 
The LCA is documented in accordance to the ISO 14040/44:2006 standards for Life 
Cycle Assessment (ISO, 2006a, ISO, 2006b) 
Moreover, it is focused on the waste management stage of PVC and therefore the Zero 
Burden approach is considered. This means that the burdens from the production of the 
PVC, that is thereafter going to be disposed as waste, are not included. The assessment 
starts at the point of disposal. 
Data are tailored to a Danish case study and therefore, the results cannot be used to 
draw general conclusions for other countries.  
4.1.3 Reasons for Carrying out the LCA Study and Decision-Context 
The study aims at decision making of the most preferable waste management option for 
PVC in Denmark. A decision on a management scheme alteration at a national level 
could entail structural changes. Therefore, the decision context could be characterized 
as situation B (EC-JRC, 2010). 
4.1.4 Target Audience 
The target audience of the study is Danish authorities and waste operators like recycling 
centers, landfill owners etc. 
4.1.5 Commissioner of the LCA Study and Other Influential Actors 
The commissioner of the study is the department of Environmental Engineering of the 
Technical University of Denmark. The LCA is conducted as part of the obligatory MSc 
thesis project at the Technical University of Denmark within the MSc program in 
Environmental Engineering. The research has purely a research scope and the author 
has no conflict of interests on the results.

4.2.4 Life Cycle Inventory Modelling Framework  
Two different frameworks are available for the modelling of the Life Cycle Inventory 
(LCI), attributional and consequential (EC-JRC, 2010).  
An attributional LCA models a specific or average supply-chain and EoL value chain 
for the entire life cycle of a product in a static technosphere (EC-JRC, 2010). This 
means that this type of modelling tries to quantify the impacts of a life cycle of a 
product, by artificially isolating it from the rest of the economy, assuming it does not 
interact with other process system (Bjørn, et al., 2016). In addition, it means that it uses 
either producer specific data or mean market mix for the background processes (EC-
JRC, 2010). 
A consequential LCA on the other hand, tries to model a generic supply-chain based on 
how the market would theoretically respond to a decision (EC-JRC, 2010). In other 
words, it models the changes in demand or supply of a technology/resource, as a result 
of a decision (Bjørn, et al., 2016). For example, secondary production of steel, would 
affect the market of production of primary steel in China, as the Chinese market has 
had a significant growth the last years ( Allegrini, et al., 2015). A generic supply-chain 
is built by using marginal processes. Meaning, technologies/producers that are most 
likely going to be affected by the decision. In this particular case, the decision of 
production of secondary steel would decrease the demand of primary steel in China that 
is therefore the marginal technology/producer.   
Figure 10: System boundaries Scenario Landfill Baseline (SIB). The blue boxes represent inputs to the 
process 
==PAGE:26==

The key to consequential modelling is identifying those marginal technologies. This 
way, the actual consequence of the decision can be modelled ( Weidema, et al., 1999).  
Moreover, one should also be diligent on demining whether the chosen technology is 
constrained, as a constrained market can never be the marginal technology ( Weidema, 
et al., 1999).  A technology is constrained when it is unable to change its supply as a 
result of a change in the demand. That could happen due to political goals, unavailable 
resources, limited capacity and others ( Weidema, et al., 1999).  
Consequential modelling is advisable in a situation B decision context (meso/macro 
level decision support that can entail structural changes), and it will therefore be used 
in this study. 
4.2.5 Handling of Multifunctionality  
It is common for industrial process to have multiple products/services (ISO, 2006b) 
however, some of the products are intentional, while other are co-products which arise 
unintentionally and are usually not valuable to the main production. Products or 
services that arise intentionally from a process system are called primary functions, 
while functions that are produced unintentionally but they cannot be avoided are called 
secondary functions. In this case, a process system is subject to multifunctionality 
(Bjørn, et al., 2016).  
In the present study, the different EoL options can also be multi-functional, as their 
primary function is to handle the waste, however secondary functions can arise during 
the process e.g. heat and power production during incineration.  
In order to be able to make comparisons between alternatives, multifuncionality must 
be solved to make the alternatives equivalent. This is achieved by following the 
ISO14044:2006 Hierarchy for handling multifunctionality. According to ISO hierarchy 
subdivision or system expansion shall be used for solving multifunctionality, while 
allocation shall be avoided (ISO, 2006b) .  
During subdivision, one must be able to identify the exact energy and raw material 
inputs for the product/process of the primary function and the co-product of the 
secondary function and their respective emissions/ waste. Meaning that one should be 
able to divide the process into unit processes, and then exclude the ones that refer to 
secondary functions (Bjørn, et al., 2016).  Subdivision cannot be performed for this 
study as it is impossible to determine, the exact inputs and emissions that are connected 
with the waste management and the ones that stem from the co-functions, as they 
happen simultaneously.  
As subdivision is not possible system expansion will be applied. System expansion can 
be achieved either by adding a non-provided function to make the alternatives 
comparable, or by subtracting a process/product that the secondary function would 
replace (ISO, 2006b) . The method applied here, is the second one; system expansion 
with substitution or avoided burden method (Finnveden , et al., 2009). During this 
method, system expansion is conducted by crediting the system for the avoided 
products. In other words, the burdens from a conventional way of producing the 
secondary function, are subtracted from the burdens of the production of the primary 
function. This way the systems become equivalent.

4.2.7 Identifying the Marginal Technology 
In the present study, the marginal producers were identified for electricity as well as the 
substituting markets for PVC and metals. 
 
The long term marginal technology for electricity in Denmark and the Netherlands was 
calculated according to Weidema (2003). The market for electricity is growing for both 
Denmark and Netherlands (EC, 2014), and therefore an increase in the demand will 
lead to an increase in the installed capacity of the technologies planned to be built in 
the future. The future technologies are the ones that are increasing in the projections. A 
decreasing form of energy cannot cover an increase in the demand and therefore, the 
marginal electricity is only the mix of the growing technologies. The marginal mix for 
each country is calculated in detail in section 4.3.2.3, and it comprises mostly of wind 
energy for both countries. 
 
In theory, the marginal producer for metals (steel and aluminum) is China, as it has 
been a growing market the last years ( Allegrini, et al., 2015).  However, data for China 
were unavailable in Ecoinvent and metals were therefore assumed to substitute an 
average global production. A global average was assumed due to the global nature of 
metal procurement. 
The marginal producer for PVC was also determined to be China, as it has the greatest 
share of the global market with a growing trend (Merchant Research and Consulting 
Ltd, 2014). However, PVC-resin production was only available for Europe, Switzerland 
and rest of the world in Ecoinvent. Since the third biggest producing country is 
Germany i.e. Europe (Merchant Research and Consulting Ltd, 2014), average European 
production was chosen.  
The marginal heat is hard to identify as it is local and it comprises of many different 
networks (Rigamonti, et al., 2014).  However, it is expected that oil and coal will be 
eliminated by 2035 and natural gas or biomass will be the marginal heat Natural gas 
was chosen as baseline while heat from biomass will be tested in scenario sensitivity. 
For both countries natural gas-fired Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants are used 
for heat production (IEA, 2008,IEA, 2008b)

4.3.2.5 Modelling of Recycling 
As mentioned in section 4.2.5, system expansion requires crediting of the avoided 
production in order to make all scenarios equivalent. In order to do that for recycling, 
two factors for defining the amount of substituted material are required (DTU 
Environment, 2016).  
The first factor (that will be referred to as A) is the technical substitution ratio. This 
factor accounts for material losses during the recycling process. The other factor (that 
will be referred to as B) is the avoided virgin production or market response. This factor 
determines the amount of virgin material that can be substituted by the recycled 
material. This ratio is not always 1-to-1, as during the recycling process the material 
can be subject to quality or properties losses (e.g. material grade) (DTU Environment, 
2016). The multiplication of A and B gives the final substitution ratio.

4.3.2.7 Modelling of Landfill 
For the modelling of landfill, an EASETECH generic landfill process with a 100 years 
life span was used. The module contains construction and maintenance of the landfill, gas 
collection/treatment as well as leachate collection/treatment at Avedøre WWTP in 
Denmark. The PVC, aluminum and steel are not producing methane so modules for gas 
production can be ignored. For leachate production, the only substance that can be leached 
is DEHP plasticizer. Heavy metals are fixed in the polymer’s matrix and are thus not 
expected to leach (EU comission, 2000). Therefore leaching of other substances was 
removed. The leachate collection efficiency of the landfill is 99.9% the first 80 years and 
80% the next 20. A study on 10 different landfills in Denmark ( Baun, et al., 2004 ) 
showed that concentrations of DEHP in the leachate were from 1 to 3 μg/L. In order to 
stay on the conservative side, a 3 μg/L concentration was considered throughout the 
landfilled period.  DEHP emissions to soil, air and water after the leachate treatment in 
the WWTP, were available in EASETECH.

4.4 Life Cycle Impact Assessment Results & Interpretation 
The results were generated using the EASETECH software that was developed in the 
department of Environmental Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The 
Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) method used in EASETECH is based on the 
International Reference Life Cycle Data System (ILCD) i.e. it contains the impact 
categories that are recommended by the European Commission in the ILCD handbook 
(EC-JRC, 2011) . Some differences between the ILCD and the EASETECH LCIA 
method are the following: 
1. The version in EASETECH does not use the official ILCD method for the impact 
category “depletion of abiotic resources”, but the CML method (Institute for 
Environmental Sciences, Leiden University) updated in 2016. In CML, depletion of 
abiotic resources is split into “depletion of abiotic resources-fossil” and depletion of 
abiotic resources-elements (reserve base)” (DTU Environment, 2016).  
2. The EASETECH version does not include three additional impact categories found 
ILCD. Namely, “ionizing radiation for ecosystem”, “land use” and “resource depletion 
water” (DTU Environment, 2016).  
Moreover, it does not consider the “long term” compartment, meaning emissions that 
exceed 100 years (DTU Environment, 2016)

(BPVC) as well as the efficiency of the MRF are the most sensitive parameters, or at least 
quite important in all the relevant impact categories.  
Aaluminum and B aluminum are the most important parameter in DepE, however this does not

wind power.

and mainly in the HTnc. Additionally to MRF efficiency in HTnc, lead and cadmium 
emissions are also important.  
MRF electricity and transport, do not seem to be sensitive in any category, and MRF 
electricity is in any case very small.  
All in all, for achieving results with low uncertainty in PVC recycling, one should be 
careful when defining A and B coefficients as well as the MRF efficiency. In addition to 
that, in cases where HTnC is a category of interest emissions for mercury and cadmium 
should be precise, as they influence the results significantly.  
These parameters are quite certain as they were obtained by personal communication with 
the manager of the recycling facility (Labots, 2017), but in any case they will be further 
tested by scenario sensitivity and combined sensitivity with different alternatives.

4.4.3.5 Time Frame Uncertainty in GW 
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has developed three different 
alternatives for the GW impact category. The difference between these three versions is 
that they have different life frames for the calculation of Green House Gas (GHG) 
emissions, namely within 20, 100 and 500 years (EC-JRC, 2011). This is necessary due 
to the different residence time of GHGs in the atmosphere. For example, GHGs with short 
residence time can have considerable impacts in a 20-year life frame, while they could be 
negligible aggregated within a 500-years’ time horizon. On the other hand, some GHGs 
have residence times of hundreds of years, thus a 20-years’ time frame would not illustrate 
their full impact (EC-JRC, 2011). A default time frame of 100 years is recommended by 
the ILCD Handbook (2011), however testing of 20 and 500 years as a sensitivity analysis 
is also recommended. The use of the 500 years is considered very uncertain (DTU 
Environment, 2016b) and therefore only the 20 year life time will be tested.  
CO2 emissions are the same for all time frames (DTU Environment, 2016b), as CO2 is 
used as a reference for calculating the other GHG potentials. Normalization factors are 
also the same for all time horizons (DTU Environment, 2016b).

4.4.3.6 Abundance of Elements Uncertainty in DepE  
Depletion of elements can be calculated in three difference concepts in the CML LCIA 
methodology. The natural reserve-ultimate, natural reserve-base and natural reserve -
economic (Van Olers, et al., 2002). Natural reserve-ultimate is the total amount of 
elements in the Earth’s crust that could eventually be extractable by humans. Natural 
reserve-base represents the share of the elements in the Earth’s crust, for which there is a 
reasonable potential of extraction, economically and technically. The last category refers 
to the part of the elements that is economically extractable at the moment (Van Olers, et 
al., 2002). The version used in the results as base is natural reserve-base, but since DepE 
has significant impacts in this study, the natural reserve-ultimate and economic were 
tested to ensure the robustness of the results in this category. Normalization references 
are not available in EASETECH, thus characterized results were used.

5. Conclusions  
PVC plastics were assessed from an environmental perspective. The main challenges 
identified in the literature were the emissions in the production/disposal of PVC and its 
additives, which can cause human toxicity and ecotoxicity. The ban of some of the 
additives i.e. cadmium and lead, as well as reproduction toxic phthalate, has improved 
the environmental performance of PVC production. However, the latter is still a 
bottleneck for disposal. The long life of PVC products implies that these additives can 
linger in the waste for many years to come.  Recycling is even more problematic, as the 
possibility of at least 7 life cycles of PVC, indicates that the concentration of the additives 
will need a very long time in order be removed from the life cycle. Last but not least, 
legislative limits in the concentration of additives can result to an economic disadvantage 
for PVC recycling. 
The comparative Life Cycle Assessment of recycling, incineration, and landfilling in 
Denmark yielded the following conclusions:  
 Recycling is the most preferable option in almost all impact categories. 
 Recycling of PVC has very high savings in human toxicity and ecotoxicity impacts, 
underlining the contribution of PVC production in these categories. 
 Incineration has benefits only in the case that it substitutes fossil electricity. 
 The substitution ratio and the sorting efficiency are the most important parameters 
for recycling. 
 The electricity efficiency and heat efficiency are the most important parameters for 
incineration. 
 The electricity type is a crucial parameter that influences significantly the 
scenarios. 
 In a renewable electricity scenario, recycling is the best option for global warming 
independently of the substitution ratio or the sorting efficiency. Whereas with fossil 
electricity low values of these factors can change the ranking between recycling 
and incineration.  
 In the rest of the tested categories i.e. human toxicity (cancer and non-cancer), 
ecotoxicity, and fossil depletion, incineration can be preferable to recycling even 
in the case of renewable electricity. To avoid that, the substitution ratio and the 
sorting efficiency should be high. For depletion of elements on the other hand, 
incineration performs always better than recycling due to the high element demand 
in the wind power. 
Considering all the above, it can be concluded that PVC recycling is the most preferable 
option, especially in the context of a renewably generated energy. In any case, in order to 
ensure its superiority in any environmental impact, sorting and reprocessing technologies 
should also be of high efficiency. Moreover, in the case of PVC, new technologies should 
additionally focus on removing the harmful additives. This can prevent contamination of 
new products and promote recycling from a legislative/economic point of view.
