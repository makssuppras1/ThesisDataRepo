Abstract 
 
The aim of this study was to develop a protocol for freeze-drying of probiotic strain, E. coli Nissle 
1917, and test the protocol by testing the short-term shelf life and robustness. The freeze-dried 
and powdered bacteria should be able to be loaded into microcontainers, which is an essential part 
of the MIMIO project at DTU. 
 
The strain has shown low survival after spray drying. For this reason, there is a need for 
optimization of the survival by the freeze-drying method. The strategy was to focus on the 
formulation of the lyoprotective medium, which has shown to have a great impact on bacterial 
survival during freeze-drying. The medium consisted of a matrix-forming component, 
Reconstituted Skim Milk, Bovine Serum Albumin and/or Mannitol was used for this purpose. 
Trehalose or Sucrose was applied for prevention of osmotic pressure. Six combinations of the 
lyoprotective components were tested for survival rate directly after freeze-drying, the survival 
rate after one month and the growth phases was evaluated. The formulation with the greatest 
potential was further optimized and evaluated 
It was possible to develop a suitable protocol for freeze-drying of E. coli Nissle 1971 and it was 
possible to apply the resulting powder to the intended microcontainers. The lyoprotective medium 
containing 5% RSM, 5% mannitol and 10% sucrose resulted in a survival rate of 68.8% However, 
the survival rate is speculated to be highly affected by ambient temperatures.

CHAPTER 1  
Introduction 
Microorganisms in the human gut, called the gut microbiota, are essential for a functional 
digestive system and profoundly influences the host health (Maus & Ingham, 2003). 
According to The World Health Organization (WHO), probiotics are defined as "live 
microorganisms which when administered in adequate amounts confer a health benefit on the 
host". The United Nation (UN) has created 17 goals for a better world by 2030. One goal is to 
ensure good health and well-being (UN Global Goals,). Probiotics may be a part of the solution 
for obtaining this goal.   
Probiotics consumption may provide health benefits including reducing lactose intolerance, 
inhibit of pathogenic bacterial growth within the gastrointestinal tract, synthesis of Vitamin B, 
among others (Maus & Ingham, 2003). A dysfunctional microbiota has been associated with 
intestinal and extraintestinal diseases (Scaldaferri et al., 2016). For this reason, probiotics may 
partly fulfill the UN goal by preventing diseases and thereby promote good health. 
E. coli Nissle 1917 (Nissle) is a non-pathogenic and non-toxic probiotic strain isolated from 
human origin. Nissle is used as the active compound in the microbial drug, Mutaflor, used for 
treatment in many gastrointestinal disorders including diarrhea, uncomplicated diverticular 
and inflammatory bowel disease (Scaldaferri et al., 2016). However, Nissle can currently not 
efficiently colonize the intestinal tract long-term in adults with normal intestinal microbiota 
(Joeres-Nguyen-Xuan, Boehm, Joeres, Schulze, & Kruis, 2010).  Optimization of the freeze-
drying process might increase the survival, which might lead to an increased colonization 
efficiency of the human intestinal tract and give long-term effect. Nissle is preferably taken as 
an orally administered drug when used in adults (Henker et al., 2007). For oral administration, 
the bacteria should be desiccated, powdered and encapsulated (Mazzoni et al., 2017). The 
microcontainer-mediated delivery system can be used for encapsulation and may potentially 
increase bacterial gut colonization compared to conventional probiotic delivery. The 
microcontainer has shown to improve oral availability by increasing specificity and ensuring 
a drug delivery directly to the intestinal mucus layer (Mazzoni et al., 2017). A combination of 
improved freeze-drying and drug delivery might increase the survival, colonization and the 
long-term effect of Nissle in the gut.

Scientific background 
2.1 E. coli strain Nissle 1917 used as probiotic 
The strain was isolated in 1917 by Alfred Nissle and is used in many gastrointestinal 
disorders including acute diarrhea, uncomplicated diverticular and inflammatory 
bowel disease (Scaldaferri et al., 2016). Nissle may prevent gastrointestinal disorders 
by preventing invasive pathogenic E. coli strains from invading human intestinal 
epithelial cells (Gronbach et al., 2010). 
Probiotics should be able to be tolerated by the human immune system and it should 
be able to survive and be metabolic active during In Vivo conditions. Nissle lacks 
defined virulence factors and no production of any known toxins, which results in 
toleration towards the immune systems (Scaldaferri et al., 2016). An efficient 
colonization of Nissle in the human colon occurs within few days (Scaldaferri et al., 
2016). However, the bacterial strain only remains a part of the microbiota a few weeks 
after administration (Joeres-Nguyen-Xuan et al., 2010; Scaldaferri et al., 2016). 
Various In Vitro and animal studies have demonstrated that Nissle can reduce intestinal 
inflammation (Scaldaferri et al., 2016). Nissle has shown to have a direct antimicrobial 
effect on pathogenic E. coli colonization in animals and inhibit synthesis of Shiga 
toxins in co-cultivation experiments (Scaldaferri et al., 2016). The results indicated 
that Nissle reduces the intestinal inflammation by preventing pathogenic E. coli strains 
from adhering and invading the human intestinal epithelial cells (Scaldaferri et al., 
2016).  
Nissle is preferably taken as an orally administered drug when used in adults (Henker 
et al., 2007). Oral administration reduced invasiveness and minimal need for trained 
personnel. Following oral administration, the adsorption of the drug will occur in the 
gastrointestinal tract (Henker et al., 2007). For a functional delivery to the 
gastrointestinal tract, the probiotics must be able to survive and be metabolic active in 
a specific location to be an effective and successful probiotic (Morgan, Herman, White, 
& Vesey, 2006). The probiotic must be able to survive and grow in the In Vivo

2.2 Preservation of microorganisms by desiccation 
Preservation of microorganisms by desiccation is the preferred method used for long-
term storage and for increasing shelf life of the product. Desiccation methods have 
been used in food and pharmaceutical industries for preserving a multitude of different 
drug preparations and food products (Morgan et al., 2006). A variety of techniques can 
be used for desiccation of microorganisms including spray- and freeze-drying. All 
desiccation techniques have advantages and disadvantages (Morgan et al., 2006). 
Freeze-drying is a time-consuming, expensive and ineffective process compared to 
spray-drying. However, freeze-drying is a gentler process which can be applied heat 
sensitive products, such as microorganisms, and it has a higher yield. The major 
advantages and disadvantages for spray- and freeze-drying are listed in Table 1 and 
Table 2, respectively (Morgan et al., 2006).

Desiccation of Nissle can be achieved by freeze- or spray-drying. However, 
unpublished experiments performed by the research group for Intestinal Bacteria and 
Health at DTU Food has shown low survival of Nissle after spray-drying. The results 
showed a loss of 40% and a survival rate of less than 0.1% directly after desiccation. 
For this reason, there is a need for optimization of the survival, possible by the freeze-
drying method.  
2.3 Freeze-drying principle 
Freeze-drying is a desiccation method commonly used for preservation and storage of 
biological material, among others. The preservative effect is obtained by reducing 
water activity without heating the material, which results in minimum damage to the 
quality (Carvalho et al., 2003; OPS Diagnostics, a). However, when applied on 
microorganisms there are undesirable side-effects including decreased viability of 
many cell types and denaturation of sensitive proteins. As illustrated in Figure 1:

Generic drying phases layout., the freeze-drying technique can be divided into five 
phases: pretreatment, freezing, two drying phases and the posttreatment (Carvalho et 
al., 2003; Tsinontides et al., 2004). 
 
Figure 1: Generic drying phases layout. 
The phases and their purpose can shortly be described as: (1) Pretreatment includes 
any treatment of the product before freezing. This may include choice of growth phase 
and cell concentration and formulation of lyoprotective medium (Morgan et al., 2006). 
Sucrose, trehalose, Reconstituted Skim Milk (RSM), Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) 
and mannitol can be used as lyoprotective compounds (OPS Diagnostics, a). (2) The 
freezing phase includes freezing of the material below the triple point to ensure 
sublimation (Millrock Technology, 2019). (3) Primary drying is the phase where the 
pressure is lowered, and sublimation occurs. During sublimation frozen liquid is 
converted directly into vapor without entering the liquid phase (Millrock Technology, 
2019). (4) Secondary drying is the final drying. During this phase, the temperature is 
increased, and the remaining water evaporates (Millrock Technology, 2019) The 
freeze-dryer used in this project is a manifold system and does not perform a secondary 
drying. (5) Posttreatment is the treatment of the product after freeze-drying. These 
treatments include packaging and storage (Morgan et al., 2006). 
All phases can influence survival in a different aspect. However, the main optimization 
can be obtained during the pretreatment (Morgan et al., 2006). 
After freezing of the product, the pressure and temperature must be adjusted so ice can 
be removed from the sample by sublimation (Labconco Corporation, 2004). The rate 
of sublimation depends upon the difference in pressure between the ice collector and 
the product container. The water will immigrate from the high-pressure area towards 
the low-pressure area in the ice collector (Labconco Corporation, 2004). During the 
freeze-drying process, it is important that there is a balance between pressure and 
temperature in order to get below the triple point and obtain sublimation (Labconco 
Corporation, 2004). A typical phase diagram containing a schematic presentation of 
the triple point is shown in Figure 2. After freezing the product will be well below the

successful freeze-drying (Labconco Corporation, 2004). LyoQuest -55 Plus laboratory

The freeze-dryer is a compact desktop version with a separate vacuum pump. The

The setup is illustrated in Figure 4. The setup and machinery consist of seven essential 
parts. (1) A vacuum pump ensures a free flow of water from the product. The vacuum

from the pump. (6) Monitoring screen to control and monitor pressure and temperature.

A variety of factors can affect the effectiveness of a freeze-drying process. These 
factors include bacterial species, temperature, formulation of the lyoprotective 
medium, crystal size, among others (Hubalek, 2003). The main optimization can be 
obtained during the pretreatment and will be the focus of this project (Morgan et al., 
2006). Notable is that no one method works for all microorganisms and not all 
microorganisms can successfully be freeze-dried. A method developed for one specific 
freeze-dryer will not necessarily work for another (OPS Diagnostics, a). 
2.3.1 Pretreatment 
Pretreatment includes any treatment of the product before freezing. The main influencer 
includes the growth phase, cell concentration and formulation of the lyoprotective 
medium (Carvalho et al., 2004; Carvalho et al., 2003). These factors can be modified to 
increase the long- and short-term survival and thereby optimize the final product.  
Growth phase 
When growing bacteria in a batch culture there are four phases: lag, exponential, 
stationery and death phase. These four phases can affect freeze-drying in different 
ways (Carvalho et al., 2004). During the stationary phase, the cells induce various 
physiological states. Carbon starvation and exhaustion of available food sources are 
normally present in this phase, which induces a stress response allowing survival of 
the cell population (Carvalho et al., 2004; Carvalho et al., 2003). In the early stationary 
phase, the cells are still active, in combination the survival response this can protect 
the cells towards other adverse conditions including desiccation. The optimal growth 
phases for desiccation survival is largely dependent on the organism and is even found 
to be species dependent (Carvalho et al., 2004; Carvalho et al., 2003). 
Cell concentration 
The cell concentration can also influence the survival of the bacteria. If the cell 
concentration becomes too high there is an increased risk of cell aggregation which 
can lead to cell wall destruction and product collapse (Carvalho et al., 2004; Carvalho 
et al., 2003). 
==PAGE:16==
2 Scientific background

Lyoprotective medium 
One of the most important factors to ensure high bacterial survival after desiccation is the 
composition of the lyoprotective medium used for suspending the organisms before 
freezing (OPS Diagnostics, a).  
The lyoprotective medium should contain a lyoprotectant protecting against osmotic 
pressure, along with matrix-forming agent which allows the sample to retain its shape, 
avoid collapse and limit cell aggregation (Hubalek, 2003; OPS Diagnostics, a). Skim 
milk, bovine serum albumin, trehalose, mannitol, and sucrose can be used as matrix-
forming agents, which can increase survival for several species (Hubalek, 2003). 
Reconstituted Skim Milk (RSM) has shown to increase cell viability during desiccation 
(Maciel, Chaves, Grosso, & Gigante, 2014). It is suggested that during water removal, 
lactose interacts with the polar part of phospholipids and proteins in the membrane. 
Membrane damage is reduced during desiccation by encapsulation and forming a matrix 
to prevent the cells from aggregation (Maciel et al., 2014). 
Another component for forming a matrix is Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA). BSA forms 
a matrix and protects the cells during desiccation by encapsulation of the cells (Al-Azzam, 
Pastrana, & Griebenow, 2002). BSA has shown to result in minimal physical variation 
after desiccation by spray-drying (Al-Azzam et al., 2002; Tsukamoto et al., 2012). 
Mannitol is a useful matrix-forming lyoprotectant (OPS Diagnostics, c). Mannitol 
provides a non-crystalline matrix which allows water to escape, prevent the sample from 
collapsing and minimize crystal size (OPS Diagnostics, c). Normally, crystals will form 
a network within the product during freezing, promoting fast removal of water vapor 
during sublimation. However, if the crystals in biological material become too large, they 
may break the cell walls, which will lead to reduces viability and less efficient freeze-
drying (Fellows, 2017; Millrock Technology, 2019). 
When applying the freeze-drying process, the ionic strength of the medium increases 
substantially, and the microorganisms are vulnerable towards osmotic stress as a result of 
the decreased water activity (Kets, 1994). Most microorganisms are able to synthesize 
and/or accumulate small water-solute compounds within the cell in order to maintain 
osmotic equilibrium. This action can be used to maintain and stabilize the cell membrane. 
==PAGE:17==
2 Scientific background 
 
 11
The accumulated compatible compounds can include sucrose, trehalose and other 
disaccharides (Kets, 1994).  
Trehalose is a non-fermentable disaccharide which will accumulate within the cell, 
stabilize the cytoplasmic membrane during desiccation, increase the tolerance towards 
osmotic pressure and prevent cell collapse (OPS Diagnostics, b; Streeter, 2003). 
Sucrose can be used as a lyoprotectant protecting against osmotic pressure. Sucrose has 
shown to provide good viability and furthermore acts as a matrix-forming component at 
10% (OPS Diagnostics, a). 
After freezing the primary dying proceed. About 93-97% of the water within product is 
removed during the primary drying. The primary drying can be a slow process where 
temperature and pressure can influence the product quality. Too much heat can result in 
altering the structure of the material (Millrock Technology, 2019; OPS Diagnostics, a). 
2.3.2 Posttreatment 
Posttreatment is the treatment of the product after freeze-drying. These treatments include 
packing and storage. 
The method of storage and packing will influence the shelf life of a dried product. The 
product should be packed in a material that prevents moisture and oxygen to react the 
product from outside (Teixeira, Castro, Malacata, & Krisby, 1995). The packing material 
can include glass vials or high barrier plastic bags. Plastic bags are not effective for long-
term storage (Teixeira et al., 1995). Oxygen can affect the shelf life by accumulating 
within the cells. If the cell is not able to metabolize it or transport it out of the cell, it may 
lead to irreversible damaging process within the cell. The damaging process could 
permanently damage the cell wall, cell membrane and DNA in some bacteria species 
(Teixeira et al., 1995). A glass vial is sealed with a stopper, and are good for storage (OPS 
Diagnostics, a). The shelf life of a freeze-dried product is highly dependent on the storage 
temperature and atmospheric water level (OPS Diagnostics, a)

2.4 Microcontainer application 
For oral administration, the probiotics need to be encapsulated, which may increase the 
bacterial survival rate (Mazzoni et al., 2017; Scaldaferri et al., 2016). Microcontainer-
mediated delivery system can be used for this purpose. 
The microcontainer is a micrometer size container with a pH-sensitive lid. The pH-
sensitive lid provides a more specific bacterial release (Maciel et al., 2014). Nissle needs 
to colonize and be metabolic active in the colon to have a beneficial effect. The 
microcontainers need to release Nissle in the early colon or in the caecum to obtain a 
beneficial effect. Furthermore, the microorganisms need to be actively growing before 
the transit in order to increase the chances of colonization.  
Nissle can currently not colonize the gut long-term (Joeres-Nguyen-Xuan et al., 2010). 
Optimization of the freeze-drying process might increase the survival rate, which might 
lead to an increased colonization efficiency of the intestinal tract and give long-term 
protection against pathogenic E. coli. The microcontainer has shown to improve oral 
availability by ensuring a more specific and functional drug delivery directly to the 
intestinal mucus layer (Mazzoni et al., 2017). A combination of improved freeze-drying 
and drug delivery might increase the survival rate, colonization and the long-term effect 
of Nissle.

CHAPTER 3 
Aim of Study 
The overall aim of this project is to develop a suitable protocol for freeze-drying of E. 
coli Nissle 1917. To ensure that this aim is fulfilled the protocol will be tested for 
robustness.  
 
The protocol will be developed by optimizing the formulation and concentration of the 
lyoprotective medium. Reconstituted Skim Milk (RSM), Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA), 
Mannitol (Man), Trehalose (Tre) and Sucrose (Suc) will be used for the formulation of 
the lyoprotective medium. The optimization is defined as increased survival and short-
term shelf life of the freeze-dried bacteria and ability to functional growth after freeze-
drying. Shelf life is measured survival after 1 month of storage.

Materials & Methods 
4.1 Experimental plan 
The experimental plan was designed before the experimental performance. The aim was 
to ensure an overview and understanding of the experiments since a specific protocol for 
freeze-drying of Nissle using LyoQuest freeze-dryer is not available.  
 
To ensure high quality of the technical performance and functionality of the equipment, 
along with initial knowledge about the freeze-drying, the following initial studies were 
performed: A growth curve experiment at 37°C and initial freeze-drying where the yield 
and water were tested for the freeze-dried product.  
  
Followed the initial experiments multiple freeze-drying were performed with different 
formulations of the lyoprotective medium. The lyoprotective medium consisted of a 
matrix-forming component, 10% RSM, 5% BSA or 10% mannitol, and a component 
preventing osmotic pressure, 10% trehalose or 10% sucrose. These lyoprotective 
substances and their concentrations were chosen based on information from OPS 
Diagnostics (OPS Diagnostics, a). 
Six combinations of the matrix and osmotic pressure preventing compounds were tested 
with three replications. A growth curve was performed on all samples and compared to 
non-freeze-dried bacteria. The scope of the growth curve is to investigate the lag- and 
exponential phase. 
Based on the percentage of survival directly after freeze-drying, one month after freeze-
drying and results from the growth curve experiment, the sample with the greatest 
potential was further optimized. This optimization included optimization of the 
formulation of the lyoprotective medium. 
  
Finally, the robustness of the protocol was evaluated. This was tested by performing a 
triple test three times. Performing a triple test will make it possible to apply statistic 
among the individual triple tests. Performing the triple test three times makes it possible 
to evaluate the significance of interbacterial differences and/or external factors between 
each one of the triple tests.

4.3.2 Initial freeze-drying 
The initial freeze-drying was performed to ensure functional freeze-drying and learn how 
the freeze-dryer works.  
  
E. coli Nissle 1917 was streaked on an LB agar plate and incubated at 37°C overnight. A 
few colonies were transferred to 200 mL LB medium in a 250 mL blue cap flask. The 
flask was placed in a steady incubator at 37°C overnight. The overnight culture was 
shaken to ensure homogeneity and OD600 was aimed to be between 0.780 and 0.960, 
which is in the early stationary phase. 25 mL was transferred to 50 mL centrifuge tube 
and centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 10 minutes at 4°C. The supernatant was removed, and 
the pellet was resuspended in 5 mL lyoprotective medium containing 10% RSM/10% Tre. 
50µL was transferred to 50 mL 0.9% NaCl. This was used as a `before sample´. The 
`before sample´ was diluted using a dilution row (100 – 10-8) and 20µL was spotted on an 
LB agar plate. The plate was placed at 37°C overnight and the CFU was counted the 
following day. 
 
The sample was transferred to a freeze-drying flask and placed at dry ice for at least 20 
minutes. The freeze-drying was performed using LyoQuest -55 Plus laboratory freeze-
dryer with a manifold system from Holm & Halby. The collector conditions were -55°C 
and 0.3 mbar. The sample was freeze-dried for 9-11 hours or until the flask temperature 
was equal to the ambient temperature. The freeze-drying is dependent on the ambient 
temperature.  
 
The dried product was crushed to a fine powder using a grinder in ceramic material. 1/5 
of the powder weight was transferred into an Eppendorf tube and MilliQ water was added 
until a total volume at 1 mL and resuspended. The solution was used as an `after sample´. 
Samples were taken before and directly after freeze-drying to calculate CFU/mL and 
percentage of survival. 50µL `after sample´ were transferred to 50 mL 0.9% NaCl. The 
sample was diluted using a dilution row (100 – 10-8) and 20µL was spotted on an LB agar 
plate. The plate was placed at 37°C overnight and the CFU was counted the following 
day.

6.1 Initial experiments  
6.1.1 Initial growth curve 
From Figure 6 the standard deviation for each individual point is low, which indicates 
that the performance has high precision. A high precision leads to higher insurance of 
exact results.   
 
The four growth phases might affect freeze-drying. In order to eliminate the growth phase 
as a variable parameter, the absorbance was used as an estimation of the growth phase. 
During the stationary phase, the cells might induce a stress response induced by 
starvation. A stress response induced by starvation can increase the survival of the cell 
during desiccation (Carvalho et al., 2004; Carvalho et al., 2003). The scope was to obtain 
an overnight culture in the early stationary phase and thereby increase the chances of 
survival. Based on the results in Figure 6 the early stationary phase was estimated to be 
between 0.780 and 0.960 at 37°C steady conditions. A culture within this range would 
possible increase survival rate after desiccation due to the induced stress response.  
6.1.2 Yield 
The yield was calculated based on three replicates. The average yield was calculated from 
Equation 3 to be 76.7%. In general, the freeze-drying method results in a high yield 
compared to other bacterial desiccation methods. Spray-drying result in a 20-70% yield, 
where 70% is a relatively high percentage for this method. The spray-drying performed 
by the research group for intestinal bacteria and health at The National Food Institute 
resulted in 60% yield.  
 
Telstar LyoQuest -55 resulted in an average yield of 76.7% with a relatively low standard 
deviation of 4.4%. This yield is almost two-fold higher than the yield obtained from the 
spray-drying method performed by the research group for intestinal bacteria and health at 
The National Food Institute. Spray-drying involves product contact with a greater surface 
area, which leads to a greater loss compared to freeze-drying. Furthermore, the yield from

the Telstar freeze-drying is relatively high. One reason for this could be that the contact 
area with the product is limited. An increased volume could result in a reduced loss. The 
reduced relative relationship between the volume and the contact with the surface will 
lead to reduces loss. However, an increased volume could potentially affect the survival 
rate of the bacteria. Increased volume can result in a longer sublimation time and an 
increased risk for melting and a collapsed product.    
6.1.3 Moisture 
During the primary drying, the water content can be expected to be 3-7%. However, the 
water content can be affected by temperature and pressure. Telstar LyoQuest -55 does 
only perform primary drying and depends on the ambient temperature. The water content 
with the freeze-dried product was calculated to be 2.46% r 0.095%. The results from 
freeze-dryings performed at room temperature at 21°C was in accordance with the 
expected result of 3-7%.  
 
The freeze-dryer was located in a room without temperature regulations. During the 
experimental period, there was a significant increase in the temperature within the room. 
The temperature increased to a maximum of 28°C, an increase of 7°C. A manifold system 
is dependent on the ambient temperature, an increase in the ambient temperature could 
lead to changed water content.  The water content was later measured on the samples 
freeze-dried at 28°C. The water content was calculated to be 1.02% r 0.16%. This could 
possibly result in changed properties. Decreased water content would lead to increased 
osmotic pressure, which could influence the survival rate.

6.2 Freeze-drying 
6.2.1 Survival directly after freeze-drying 
The results from Figure 7 indicate that sucrose leads to an increased survival rate for all 
lyoprotective media tested. Sucrose is used as an agent protecting against osmotic 
pressure. However, sucrose has shown to act as a matrix-forming component at 10%. This 
could explain the tendency of a general higher survival when using 10% sucrose in the 
lyoprotective media. This could also indicate that matrix formation serves great 
importance for survival. The matrix prevents cell aggregation and cell membrane 
destruction by crystals. Increased matrix stability could potentially lead to a reduces 
amount of destroyed cell and thereby an increased percentage of survival.  
 
The freeze-dryings resulted in Mannitol/Trehalose having the lowest survival of 1% and 
RSM/Sucrose having the highest survival of 48%. Other studies from the literature found 
a survival rate of 11-70% for other E. coli strains. However, functional freeze-drying is 
highly dependent on bacterial strain, freeze-drier and method applied (Leslie et al., 1995; 
Louis, Trüper & Galinski, 1994; Miyamoto-Shinohara et al., 2006). 
 
The samples containing BSA or Mannitol in the lyoprotective media have a generally 
lower survival rate and are not significantly different from each other. Samples containing 
RSM in the lyoprotective media seem to have a generally higher percentage of survival 
and is significantly different from the samples containing either BSA or Mannitol. This 
indicates that RSM leads to better protection against desiccation during freeze-drying. 
RSM interacts with the cell membrane, thereby encapsulating the cell and reducing 
damage to the membrane during desiccation. This might explain the increased survival 
when applying RSM in the lyoprotective media.

concentration of 107 cells/g to survive In Vivo conditions. From Table 5 it is seen that the 
average cell concentration (CFU/g) is above 107 CFU/g. During oral administration a 
portion of cell will not survive the In Vivo conditions, a cell concentration above 107 
CFU/g will ensure an efficient number of surviving microorganisms.

6.2.2 Growth curve 
The results presented in Figure 8 indicates that all samples are able to have active cell 
division after 3 hours. The exponential phase for the control starts at 1.5 hours. However, 
the experiment was performed using LB medium with a starting temperature of 
approximately 15°C. The relative cold starting temperature leads to slow cell division, 
which could cause an increased length of the lag phase. The optimal growth temperature 
for E. coli is 37°C. However, the same conditions applied for all samples, which makes 
it possible to compare the phases between the samples.  
 
Samples containing BSA have a generally longer lag phase, which can be caused by an 
increased cell membrane destruction. A reconstruction of the cell membrane is necessary 
before the cell divisions and exponential phase can begin, which can lead to a longer lag 
phase. The relative low survival and the extended lag phase could indicate cell membrane 
destruction.  This can be caused by poor protection by BSA against crystals during 
freezing. The survival is 4.4% and 14.3% for samples containing 5% BSA/ 10% 
Trehalose and 5% BSA/10% Sucrose, respectively. 5% BSA/10% Sucrose is significantly 
higher than 5% BSA/10% Trehalose, which could be a result of the sucrose acting as a 
matrix-forming component.  
 
The results from Figure 7 indicated that samples containing Mannitol have a relatively 
low survival of 0.4% and 4.9% for 10% Mannitol/10% Trehalose and 10% Mannitol/10% 
Sucrose, respectively. However, the results in Figure 8 shows a shorter lag phase 
compared to samples containing BSA. This could be due to the fact that mannitol provides 
a non-crystalline matrix which allows water to escape and prevent the sample from 
collapsing. The results could, for this reason, indicate that mannitol provides a low 
survival, but the cell structure of the surviving cells remains without great damage. It 
could also be a result of E. coli ability to metabolize mannitol and apply it as an easily 
accessible carbon source (EcoCys, 2019) 
 
For further investigation of the lag and exponential phase, a linear regression analysis was 
performed on the logarithm transformed growth curve. The slope was used to calculate 
the growth rate, while the intercept with the dotted line was used to indicate for the length 
of the lag phase. A short lag phase will lead to an earlier exponential phase and thereby 
increase the chances of In Vivo survival and growth.

The results from Figure 9 A indicate that there is no significant difference between the

Lack of crystals can prevent cell membrane destruction and an effective cell division can 
thereby occur earlier. Mannitol can also be degraded and possibly used as an easily 
accessible carbon source, which can lead to a short lag phase (EcoCys, 2019).  
6.2.2 Survival after one month 
The survival was tested after one month in order to estimate the short-term survival of the 
freeze-dried samples.  
 
In Figure 10 the percentage of survival is illustrated. The pattern is similar to the pattern 
directly after freeze-drying. Samples containing RSM have significantly higher survival 
than the remaining samples. Samples containing BSA or Mannitol did not differ 
significantly from each other directly after freeze-drying. A similar pattern is seen after 
one month. Likewise, is there a tendency to have a generally higher survival for samples 
containing sucrose. 
 
Figure 11 A-F compares the percentage of survival directly after freeze-drying with the 
percentage of survival after one month. The survival is significantly decreased for all 
samples, except for the sample containing 10% Mannitol/10% Trehalose. This lack of 
significance is possibly caused by a relatively high standard deviation. There is a rapid

decrease in the percentage of survival after one month. This could be caused by incorrect

drying method, which would indicate great importance of a specific freeze-drying

6.3 Optimization of freeze-drying 
6.3.1 Survival directly after freeze-drying 
Based on the results from the previous tests, 10% RSM/10% Sucrose showed the greatest 
potential as lyoprotective medium. This lyoprotective medium resulted in significant 
highest survival directly after freeze-drying and one month later. Furthermore, the growth 
curve indicated a functional growth. Different concentrations of RSM and Sucrose was 
used for further analysis. The analysis of the growth curve in Figure 8-10 indicated that 
the sample containing Mannitol and Sucrose resulted in a growth curve similar to the 
control. A lyoprotective media containing both RSM and Mannitol as matrix-forming 
component was therefore tested.  
 
The results from Figure 13 indicate that 5% RSM/5% Mannitol/10% Sucrose results in 
the significant highest percentage of survival of 68.5%. The high survival can be a result 
of mannitols ability to prevent crystal formation; the cell protection by RSM; and the 
prevention of osmotic pressure and matrix-formation by sucrose. The remaining samples 
containing all three components did results in a relatively low survival of less than 17%. 
This can be a result of incorrect lyoprotective formulation. Despite the relatively low 
survival, other studies found a similar survival rate for Nissle (Louis, Trüper & Galinski, 
1994). Furthermore, from Table 6 it can be seen that all samples have a cell density above 
107 CFU/g and which indicates the potential to survive In Vivo. During oral 
administration a portion of cell will not survive the In Vivo conditions, a cell concentration 
above 107 CFU/g will ensure a sufficient number of surviving microorganisms. 
6.3.2 Growth curve 
From Figure 14 it can be seen that all samples are able to grow after desiccation. The 
exponential phase begins after 2 hours for all samples. The experiment was performed at 
a starting temperature of approximately 15°C. This could cause an increased length of the 
lag phase. The relative cold starting temperature leads to slow cell division, which could 
cause an increased length of the lag phase. The optimal growth temperature for E. coli is 
37°C. The same conditions applied for all samples, which makes it possible to compare 
the phases between the samples.

The results from Figure 15 A indicate that the slopes are not significantly different. This

The generation time was calculated to be about 27 minutes for all samples, which is longer 
than the general generation time for E. coli of 20 minutes, in the laboratory under aerobic, 
nutrient-rich conditions at 37 °C (Gibson, Wilson, Feil & Eyre-Walker, 2018). The

A progressive growing bacterial can lead to increased immune response and a decreased

virulence and toxic expression may lead to an increase In Vivo survival (Legget,

caused by damage towards the cell membrane, which have to repair before an active cell 
division can occur.

6.4 Robustness test 
To evaluate the protocol, a robustness test was performed. Based on the survival rate and 
the growth curve, 5% RSM/5% Mannitol/10% Sucrose was chosen to have greatest 
potential. The survival rate was significant highest, and the growth curve indicated 
functional growth. The robustness test was conducted by performing three replicates of 
the freeze-drying protocol, all of which consisted of three technical replicates. 
 
Figure 16 A indicates a significantly higher percentage of survival for replication A-C 
compared du replication D-I. The percentage of survival is 68.5% for replication A-C, 
compared with 9.9% and 11.8% for replication D-F and G-I, respectively. As mentioned, 
there was a notable change in temperature within the room. The room temperature 
changed from 21°C to 28°C during the experiential period. A manifold freeze-drying 
system dependent on the ambient temperature. For this reason, the water content within 
the sample was tested. Increased ambient temperature can cause an increased product 
temperature during freeze-drying and therefore a decreased water content.  
 
The results from Figure 16 B indicate a significant decrease in water content in replication 
D-I, performed at 28°C. By comparing the pattern in Figure 16 A and B there seems to 
be a correlation between water content and survival.  The decreased water content can 
possibly lead to a decreased percentage of survival. However, increased survival in 
replication A-C could also be caused by caused by inaccuracies from measuring small 
amounts of or other external factors as humidity.   
 
From Table 7 there is no significant difference in cell concentration before freeze-drying 
between any of the replicates. It is therefore not possible to determine whether or not the 
cell density influences the survival rate during desiccation.

CHAPTER 7  
Conclusion 
 
It can be concluded that it was possible to develop a suitable protocol for freeze-drying 
of E. coli Nissle 1971. It was possible to freeze-dry Nissle using a lyoprotective medium 
containing Reconstituted Skim Milk, Mannitol and Sucrose. 5% RSM and 5% Mannitol 
was used as matrix-forming components and 10% Sucrose for prevention of osmotic 
pressure. The final protocol resulted in up to 68.8% survival. However, the survival rate 
seemed to be highly affected by ambient temperatures. The external environment or 
incorrect performance resulted in a protocol with low accuracy and low precision. This 
results in low robustness. Despite the great variations and low robustness, the resulting 
protocol resulted in a successful freeze-drying 
 
The resulting freeze-dried products had a significantly low shelf life. It cannot be 
concluded if the short shelf life is caused by the lyoprotective media or incorrect storage 
is the cause. The extern freeze-drying performed by DTU Vet did not result in 
significantly higher survival after one month. 
 
Lastly, it can be concluded that the resulting freeze-dried and powdered Nissle was able 
to be applied to the microcontainers and that the method can be used for further 
investigation.

CHAPTER 8  
Further perspective 
 
The survival of Nissle after freeze-drying has been investigated using Telstar LyoQuest -
55 Plus. This freeze-dryer is a manifold system and depends on the ambient temperature. 
The freeze-dryer should be placed in a temperature regulated room to investigate the 
effect of ambient temperature on accuracy and precision. The robustness of the protocol 
should be evaluated after elimination of temperature as an external factor.  
 
The storage of the desiccated product should be further investigated. Proper storage will 
ensure long shelf life. The product should be packed in a material that prevents moisture 
and oxidation within the product. The growth and survival should be tested after and 
during storage. 
 
The final lyoprotective formulation should be found and the final protocol is developed, 
before further investigation of the properties. In Vitro test of the growth in synthetic gut 
fluid should be tested before continuing to In Vivo testing. In vivo tests could include 
testing Nissles ability to colonize and be metabolic active in mice both short- and long-
term.
