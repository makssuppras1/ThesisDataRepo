Abstract
The Danish Environmental protection Agency (miljøstyrelsen) have provided a subsidy program for landﬁlls in
Denmark to provide ﬁnancial aid to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. The greenhouse gas emissions come from
the produced landﬁll gas (LFG) which is produced over several decades after the waste has been landﬁlled.
The LFG contains high quantities of methane (CH4) which is a greenhouse gas 28 times stronger than carbon
dioxide (CO2). The subsidy program aims to mitigate these emissions on a long-term basis by the construction
of biocovers, passive bioﬁlters & active bioﬁlters. These systems use biological oxidation by methanotrophic
bacteria to convert CH4 into CO2 under aerobic conditions. These systems are generally constructed with a
CH4 oxidation layer (commonly matured compost) and with a gas distribution layer beneath. An active bioﬁlter
uses a pump to extract the produced LFG and transfer it to the bioﬁlter. This active system can provide a
steady ﬂow of LFG and the possibility to introduce oxygen into the LFG, to create more favourable conditions
for CH4 oxidation compared to passive systems.
The aim of this study was to analyse the performance of an active bioﬁlter system constructed in 2019 at Reno
Djurs I/S, Nymandsvej 11, 8444 Balle, Denmark. The system consists of three bioﬁlters with similar design,
which treats LFG produced from shredder waste. This study consisted of an initial CH4 surface screening
campaign (March 2020) to establish the foundation for four measurement campaigns (May - June 2020). The four
measurement campaigns measured the LFG composition prior to entering the three bioﬁlters (LFG composition
at the exhaust manifold), measurement of the LFG composition & temperature in the CH4 oxidation layer
at diﬀerent depth and surface ﬂux of CH4 and CO2. The results showed a general trend of decreasing CH4
content in the gas proﬁles with increasing distance from the exhaust manifold. CH4/CO2 ratios suggest that
CH4 oxidation starts in the gas distribution layer and continues up through the oxidation layer. These results
also indicates that the CH4 oxidation zone covers most of the CH4 oxidation layer close to the exhaust manifold,
but the zone gradually decreases with distance, due to decreasing CH4 content with distance. The temperature
proﬁles show statistical lower temperatures with increasing distance from the exhaust manifold.
The ﬂux
measurements of CH4 show that an increase in LFG ﬂow resulted in statistical higher emissions of CH4. The
higher CH4 emissions were calculated with 95% conﬁdence to be in the range of 0-0.25 g CH4/m2*day. The
ﬂux measurements show in general low emissions across the bioﬁlter and no hot spot areas were located. The
eﬃciency of the bioﬁlter was estimated to be at least 95% by using the carbon mass balance.

Introduction
Landﬁlls have for many decades been used to discard various kinds of waste. In recent decades, with new waste
technologies emerging such as waste incineration, recycling of materials, anaerobic digestion and composting of
organic waste, landﬁlls have become the least favoured option for waste handling. Landﬁlls in industrialized
countries like Denmark, is in present time primary used for inert materials and waste with low organic content,
such as shredder waste, which cannot be treated any further of the above-mentioned technologies. Although
the technologies of waste treatment in recent decades have evolved, both newer and older landﬁlls still contain
organic waste, which due to the anaerobic conditions in the landﬁll, creates landﬁll gas (LFG). This gas contains
a mixture of methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2) as well as trace gasses. CH4 is a greenhouse gas, with a
global warming potential of 28 kg CO2-eq over a 100-year period (GWP100) (Green House Protocol 2015). The
two main problems with the unintentional production of LFG, is that CH4 has a much higher GWP than CO2
and the production of LFG can last several decades after the waste have been landﬁlled. Diﬀerent mitigation
options exist to utilize the produced LFG, if the volume of LFG and CH4 content is high enough. LFG can
be utilized in a combined heat and power plant (CHP) to produce electricity and heat. Eventually, the volume
and CH4 content will decrease to such degree, that there is no economically feasible option to utilize the LFG
(Pedersen et al. 2012, Ahmed et al. 2007, Haubrichs & Widmann 2006). The several decades with production of
LFG contribute heavily to the anthropogenic emissions of greenhouse gasses, which is one of the larges drivers
of global warming. Furthermore, untreated LFG emissions is not only contributing to global warming, but can
also have severe eﬀects on more local scales such as; ﬁre and explosion risks, health risks, groundwater pollution
and crop/vegetation damage (Kjeldsen 1996).
The most promising long-term solution to mitigate those emissions are through biological oxidation by methan-
otrophic bacteria. In the oxidation process, the CH4 in the LFG is converted to biogenic CO2, which is not
accounted as a greenhouse gas (Kjeldsen & Scheutz 2016). The biological oxidation of CH4 can be done by the
use of a bioﬁlter/biocover solution (Cabral et al. 2010, Scheutz et al. 2017a). The general construction of a
bioﬁlter consist of a gas distribution layer, which is constructed to distribute the LFG over the whole surface.
Over the gas distribution layer, a methane oxidation layer is placed. The methane oxidation layer generally
consists of matured compost, which contains special bacteria, known as methanogens, that utilize the CH4 in
the LFG by microbial methane oxidation. Both passive and active bioﬁlters exist, where the main diﬀerence
is how the LFG is transported to the bioﬁlter. In active bioﬁlters, a pump is used to extract the produced
LFG and transfer it below the gas distribution layer through a series of gas distribution pipes. Active bioﬁlters
have the advantage, that it is possible to introduce atmospheric air into the LFG mixture. The advantage of
introducing atmospheric air into the LFG mixture is the oxygen (O2) present in the air. O2 is used in the
microbial methane oxidation process. For passive bioﬁlters, the methane oxidation process is limited by the
diﬀusion of O2 from the surface and into the bioﬁlter. With the counter gradient for LFG from below and
O2 from above, creates narrow zones within the bioﬁlter, which provides the necessary condition for methane
oxidation to occur. With the introduction of O2 from below in active bioﬁlters, the bioﬁlter is no longer limited
by diﬀusion of O2 from the surface alone. This should provide an overall higher eﬃciency, due to more O2 is
introduced into the bioﬁlter system (Kjeldsen & Scheutz 2016).
Diﬀerent types of bioﬁlters exist, depending on the emissions patterns at the landﬁll (Kjeldsen & Scheutz 2016).

2019, gathered information regarding the diﬀerent projects which have currently been granted this subsidy and
types of bioﬁlters & biocovers constructed/planned (Hansen 2019). Some of the main results from this study

2.1
Landﬁll gas production
Generally, LFG mainly consist of a mixture of CH4 (50-60 %), CO2 (40-50 %) and small amount of trace gasses
such as nitrogen, oxygen, sulphides, carbon monoxide etc (Christensen et al. 2010). The production of LFG
can be divided into three diﬀerent processes; Bacterial decomposition, Volatilization and Chemical reactions
(ATSDR 2001).
The composition of the LFG develops over time depending on diﬀerent factors. The development of the LFG
composition is divided into a total of eight phases (ﬁgure 1). Phase I-IV are the most well-known phases and
represents the ﬁrst 20-35 years of the landﬁll (Christensen et al. 2010).
Figure 1: Development of LFG composition over time (Christensen et al. 2010)
Bacterial decomposition:
The majority of LFG is produced by bacterial decomposition (ATSDR 2001). This decomposition occurs natu-
rally when the bacteria breaks down the organic fractions in the waste and the soil used to cover the landﬁll. The
bacteria decomposition is divided into four diﬀerent phases, each phase changes the gas composition (ATSDR
2001).
Phase I:
During this phase, aerobic bacteria, consume O2 when breaking down the complex molecules in the organic
waste. The primary by-product is CO2. Nitrogen content decreases over the four diﬀerent phases. Phase I will

continue until the available O2 is depleted. The length of Phase I is dependent on how much O2 is available
when the waste was land ﬁlled. Phase I usually lasts between several days and months. The O2 content is
depending on how compressed the waste was at the time of land ﬁlling (ATSDR 2001). More compressing
will result in less pore volume for the O2 to occupy, hence less O2 will be available. A larger compressing will
simultaneously reduce the diﬀusion of O2 from the ambient air.
Phase II:
Phase II will be initiated when available O2 has been consumed. This will change the conditions from aerobic
to anaerobic. The bacteria present in this anaerobic environment will convert compounds generated by the
phase I bacteria (aerobic bacteria) into compounds such as; acetic acid, lactic acid, formic acid and alcohols
such as methanol and ethanol. The moisture present in the landﬁll will mix with the generated acids, which
results in certain nutrients to be dissolved. This dilution of nutrients makes nitrogen and phosphorus available
to the diﬀerent bacteria in the landﬁll. The main gaseous by-products of these phase II processes are CO2 and
H2. If any disturbances cause the conditions to change to aerobic, phase II will revert back to phase I. Thereby
stopping the conversion of compounds into sugars and alcohols.
Phase III:
Phase III is initiated when anaerobic bacteria forms acetate from the organic acids produced in phase II. Acetate
is an organic acid, and the production of acetate will create a more neutral environment, which allow the
growth of methane producing bacteria. The acid and methane producing bacteria has a symbiotic relationship.
Methanogenic bacteria consume the produced CO2 and acetate while acid producing bacteria creates compounds
which can be consumed by methanogenic bacteria. Too much CO2 or acetate would be toxic for the acid bacteria,
hence comes the symbiotic relationship for both kinds of bacteria.
Phase IV:
Phase IV is initiated when the landﬁll environment reaches steady state (composition and production remain
relatively constant). It is at this last stage, that the usual composition of LFG is generated, which is 60 % CH4
and 40 % CO2. The production of LFG at this stage can last several decades (ATSDR 2001). Due to the energy
release by the anaerobic processes, large landﬁlls have been shown to achieve temperatures higher than 40◦C.
Phase III and IV is where the CH4 in the LFG is produced by methanogenic processes.

Volatilization:
When certain wastes, in particular organic compounds, change stage from liquid or solid into vapor (i.e.
volatilize), nonmethane organic compounds (NMOCs) might be found in the LFG (ATSDR 2001).
Chemical reactions:
LFG can be created by reactions occurring between diﬀerent chemicals present in the waste (ATSDR 2001).

2.1.1
LFG from shredder waste
In the following sections, the origin and composition of shredder waste is described. It also describes how the
knowledge of LFG production from shredder waste have changed over the last couple of decades. Prior to Olsen
& Willumsen (2013) surveillance program, were shredder waste was believed not to produce any signiﬁcant
amount of LFG, to Mou et al. (2014, 2015) which demonstrates that shredder waste produces LFG but much
lower volume than the ﬁrst order decay models predicted with default parameters. Lastly, Fathi Aghdam et al.
(2017) which assessed how temperature, moisture and various metals inﬂuence the produced CH4 gas from
shredder waste.
Composition:
Shredder waste (SW) originate from larger objects such as end-of-life vehicles (ELV) and appliances. In the
shredding process, the objects are shredded to smaller pieces and sorted in diﬀerent steps. In general, around
65% of the waste is iron, 15% other metals (both fractions are recycled). The fraction after the recovery of
dismantled parts and various metals is categorized as shredder waste (Fathi Aghdam et al. 2017) and accounts
for around 20-25 % of an ELV’s weight. Some part of the waste is incinerated, but the majority of the waste
is landﬁlled (Fathi Aghdam et al. 2017). The waste fraction from SW consist of a range of materials such as;
plastic, textiles, rubber, iron- and metal residues, rocks, dust, broken glass, rust etc (Syddjurs og Norddjurs
kommuner 2014, Fathi Aghdam et al. 2017). Although the metals in the SW are recovered for the purpose of
recycling, the recovery technology is not 100 % eﬃcient, which leads to metal residues in the SW. The most
abundant metals in SW is: Fe, Al, Zn and Cu. Research on the metal content in SW is described in Fathi
Aghdam et al. (2017), which shows, that even though the content of iron (Fe) vary greatly (1.78-15.40 %), it
is still the most abundant metal in SW. To seperate the SW further, sieving can be applied to divide the SW
into a coarse and ﬁne fraction. The coarse fraction would mostly consist of metals and combustibles whereas
the ﬁne fraction would mostly be minerals and inert materials. Applying sieving technologies to treat the SW
and could potentially lead to a higher amount of recovered materials, both for recycling and incineration in the
coarse fraction. The ﬁne fraction would subsequently be landﬁlled (Fathi Aghdam et al. 2017).
There exist two shredder companies in Denmark, which has a total of three facilities, one of them in Balle. The
shredder waste from the facility in Balle and Roskilde have been landﬁlled at Reno Djurs. In the past ﬁve years,
Reno Djurs have received approximately 150,000 tonnes of shredder waste from those two facilities (Syddjurs
og Norddjurs kommuner 2014).
Gas production:
In the early 2000, it was believed that landﬁlled shredder waste, produced little or no CH4 gas, due to the small
amount of heavily degradable materials. Hence, no special measures were introduced to mitigate and collect
the produced LFG. In summer 2011, Odense Nord Environmental Centre (ONEC) launched a surveillance pro-
gram, were part of the program focused on the temperature in diﬀerent depths as well as the quality of the gas
produced in the shredder waste (Olsen & Willumsen 2013).
ONEC had since the early 2000 operated under a environmental license dating back to 2003, which states, as
described above, no special measures were needed for the potential produced LFG from the shredder waste.
ONEC had been landﬁlling shredder waste separately, with a total amount of 979,992 tonnes by the end of 2012
(Olsen & Willumsen 2013).
The surveillance program conducted by Olsen & Willumsen (2013) measured, that the CH4 & CO2 concentration
in the produced LFG at 10 m depth reached on average 56.5 % & 5.6 %, respectively. At 19.5 m depth, the
concentrations decreased a bit. The average concentration for CH4 and CO2 were 53.4 % & 3.6 %, respectively.

The authors modelled the CH4 potential and concluded that the maximum CH4 potential would be 150-300
Nm3 CH4/h by 2014 (Olsen & Willumsen 2013). The produced volume would afterwards decrease, but still
produce signiﬁcant volumes of LFG in several decades.
In order to estimate a landﬁlls combined CH4 emissions, ﬁrst order decay (FOD) LFG generation models have
been widely used to give these estimates (Mou et al. 2014). In these models, the two primary parameters to
estimate the produced LFG from waste biodegradation is the biochemical methane potential (BMP) and the
CH4 generation rate constant (k value, yr−1) (Mou et al. 2015). One of the main issues with the FOD models, is
the standard parameter values used for BMP and the CH4 generation rate constant, when applying these models
to low-organic waste, such as shredder waste (Mou et al. 2014). The models and the parameters have been
based on values for traditional municipal solid waste (MSW), which have a much higher organic content than
for example shredder waste, resulting in a overestimation of the generated LFG from these kind of low-organic
waste landﬁlls (Mou et al. 2014).
Mou et al. (2014) & Mou et al. (2015) wanted to look at the two key parameters, BMP and the CH4 generation
rate constant and determine those for four landﬁlls in Denmark, all containing diﬀerent kinds of low-organic
waste. The experiment setup was to collect waste from four danish landﬁlls; AV Miljø, Reno Djurs, Odense and
Audebo. Four diﬀerent categories of waste were collected; Combustible, Shredder, Sludge and Bulky mixed.
These four categories were then analyzed in batch test to calculate the BMP ((Mou et al. 2014)). For the CH4
generation rate constant ((Mou et al. 2015)) the four categories were slighty diﬀerent: street cleansing, mixed
bulky, shredder and sludge waste. Duplicate Anaerobic degradation experiments were conducted over a total
of 405 days, were as the accumulated CH4 were continuously monitored.
The calculated BMP for shredder waste were between 6.2 and 9.1 kg CH4/ton waste, which is signiﬁcantly lower
than MWS. Applying the new BMP data to the FOD models, would result in a decrease in the estimated LFG
generation in the range of 60-90 % (Mou et al. 2014). For the generation rate constant, k, this parameter was
in the range of 0.013-0.017 yr−1 for shredder and mixed bulky waste. These values for k is also lower than the
default values used in the diﬀerent FOD models (Mou et al. 2015).
Mou et al. (2014) & Mou et al. (2015) have looked at how to increase the accuracy to model the CH4 generation
from landﬁlls, which contain low-organic waste. The general problem with the models considering low-organic
waste, was the parameters used in the model, specially the biochemical methane potential (BMP) and the CH4
generation rate constant (k value, yr−1).
Fathi Aghdam et al. (2017) studied the CH4 production rates from SW, which were determined by incubat-
ing waste samples over a total period of 230 days. The experiment was conducted under diﬀerent operating
conditions and the CH4 generation rate constant, k, were calculated. One of the hypothesis in this research
was that biocorrosion of some metals could produce H2, which are used in reactions such as hydrogenotrophic
methanogenesis, which supports the conversion of CO2 to CH4. If this hypothesis was right, it could explain
why SW produces unusual LFG compositions, with high CH4 concentrations and low CO2 concentrations, com-
pared to a conventional LFG composition of 60 % CH4 and 40 % CO2 (Fathi Aghdam et al. 2017). The authors
concluded that H2 could indeed be produced by biocorrosion of Fe, Al and Zn. The authors also showed that the
production of H2 could be utilized by the hydrogenotrophic methanogens, which convert CO2 into CH4. The
authors also concludes, that the most likely scenario of the unusual LFG composition in landﬁlled SW is due
to a consumption of existing CO2 in the produced LFG and the produced H2 by biocorrosion (Fathi Aghdam
et al. 2017).

2.2
Gas transportation processes
Gasses produced under the landﬁll surface generally tend to move away from the landﬁll and migrate through
the pore volume of the waste (ATSDR 2001). Gasses lighter than air, such as CH4, will tend to move vertical
unless compacted waste or daily landﬁll covers restrict this movement. In general, lateral migration of LFG is
more common than vertical migration. This is mainly due to waste is commonly land ﬁlled in layered formation,
which subsequently produces horizontal zones, which has a higher compaction and lower permeability at the
surface of each layer. This creates a larger probability that the LFG migrate horizontal between these zones
rather than vertical (Kjeldsen 1996). Generally gas migrate through areas with least resistance (ATSDR 2001).
The governing factors for gas transport at landﬁlls are divided between transport processes; diﬀusion, advec-
tion and dilution and sink processes; dilution of LFG constituents in water, sorption of LFG to soil particles
and oxidation (Kjeldsen 1996).
Diﬀusion is caused by the variation in gas concentration within the porous medium and will migrate from high
concentration areas (such as within the landﬁll) to areas with low concentration (surrounding area). Advective
transport is caused by a pressure gradient (Kjeldsen 1996). Accumulating gasses within the landﬁll can result
in areas with high pressure where movement of gasses is restricted due to low permeability, soil covers or
compaction of waste. Other areas within the landﬁll will have a low pressure due to unrestricted movement.
These diﬀerences creates a pressure gradient and gasses within the high pressure area will migrate towards the
low pressure area (ATSDR 2001).
The gas transport within the landﬁll is mainly driven by advective forces due to pressure build up. Gas venting
installation or the installation of gas recovery wells can have a great eﬀect on the lateral migration and vertical
migration of LFG. The extraction of the produced LFG, will eﬀectively lower the pressure build up, and hence
reduce the advective forces within the landﬁll (Kjeldsen 1996).
Diﬀerent factors can inﬂuence these processes, and can be divided into three classes (Kjeldsen 1996):
Meteorological:
Barometric pressure, precipitation, temperature and wind.
Soil and geological:
Cracks and ﬁssures in the landﬁll soil, top cover, methane oxidation layer etc. The permeability, porosity, water
content and organic matter content of the soil.
Waste:
Diﬀerent factors of the waste can also inﬂuence the transport of the LFG such as: the gas production rate, the
rate of volatile organic carbon release, internal barriers, the installation of gas vents as well as lateral migration
areas.

In the review done by Scheutz et al. (2009) the major mechanisms for CH4 emissions from landﬁll sources are
mentioned. These includes diﬀusion, advection (Darcy ﬂow) as well as advection induced by wind. The diﬀusive

gradients.
These pressure gradients can be induced by wind, changing barometric pressure or by pressure
build-ups in the waste due to gas production.
Prior to the construction of the bioﬁlters at Glatved landﬁll, two campaigns were conducted to estimate the total
emissions of CH4 from the landﬁll. This was necessary, to estimate the area of bioﬁlters necessary to mitigate
the produced LFG. The two measurement campaigns were conducted under diﬀerent atmospheric conditions. A
decreasing barometric pressure were present under the ﬁrst campaign, whereas the second campaign experienced
increasing barometric pressure. The emissions measured under the ﬁrst campaign were 35.6 kg CH4/h, whereas
the second campaign, the total emissions were 21.5 kg CH4/h. Rosendal (2017) concluded that the two total
emissions measurement done by the tracer method, indicated that the barometric pressure had an inﬂuence on
the CH4 emissions which are emitted from stage I at Glatved landﬁll.
Gebert et al. (2011) assessed the temporal variability in microbial oxidation conditions in landﬁll cover soils
and the driving variables. This was done by measuring the gas composition at non-emissive and strong-emissive
locations. The locations were monitored on a seasonal, daily and hourly time scale. The measurements were
conducted on an old, unlined landﬁll in Northern Germany. Soil temperature, soil moisture, barometric pressure
and air temperature were monitored with a frequency of 1 h. Soil gas probes were installed in seven depths,
between 5 cm and 120 cm below the surface for the non-emissive areas and between 5 cm and 90 cm at the
strong-emissive areas.
The authors found that the gas composition in hotspots areas (mainly governed by
advection) was inﬂuenced by a change in barometric pressure. For the non-emissive areas, which were mainly
governed by diﬀusion, no direct relationship between barometric pressure change and soil gas composition were
observed. The authors also concluded that wind and changes in barometric pressure mostly aﬀects the gas
transport on shorter time scales, down to hours or minutes.
Xu et al. (2014) investigated the impact changes in barometric pressure had on landﬁll CH4 emissions at Bluﬀ
Road Landﬁll in Lincoln, Nebraska, USA. The emissions from the landﬁll were continuously measured using
the eddy covariance method and took place between June and December 2010. During the campaign, on June
7th 2010, during a cold front passage, an increase in barometric pressure resulted in CH4 emissions decreasing
from 50 ppm to 7 ppm in 3 minutes. The pressure continued to increase, leading to further decrease in CH4
emissions, which at that time decreased to atmospheric background levels of around 2 ppm. The decrease from
50 ppm to 2 ppm occurred over a 6 minute period. Xu et al. (2014) concluded that CH4 emission rates were
strongly inﬂuenced by changes in barometric pressure. Rising barometric pressure suppresses emissions whereas
decreasing pressure enhances the emissions. The authors also found up to a 35-fold variation in day-to-day
emissions of CH4, due to changes in barometric pressure.

2.4
Microbial methane oxidation
Microbial methane oxidation is a process that is possible in a special kind of bacteria, known as methanotrophs,
a subset of methyltrophic bacteria. Methylotrophic bacteria are aerobic bacteria that utilize a broad range of
one-carbon compounds as their source for carbon and energy. Methylotrophic bacteria assimilate formaldehyde,
which is a major source of cellular carbon. The one-carbon compounds include methane, methylated amines,
methylated compounds which contains sulfur, methanol, and halomethanes (Hanson & Hanson 1996). A subset
of methyltrophic bacteria, called methanotrophs, possess the unique ability to utilize methane as their sole
carbon and energy source. Methanotrophs have been found in a variety of aquatic and terrestrial environments
where CH4 and O2 are present (Kjeldsen & Scheutz 2016).
Methanotrophs have two separate pathways to assimilate formaldehyde: The ribulose monophosphate (RuMP)
pathway, and the Serine pathway.
Methanotrophs have been categorized into two types: Type I and type
II, depending on the assimilation of formaldehyde pathway. Type I use the RuMP pathway and are mostly
incapable of ﬁxing N2 (Scheutz et al. 2009). Type II use the Serine pathway and are capable of ﬁxing N2
(Scheutz et al. 2009). The two distinct pathways and assimilations of formaldehyde are presented in ﬁgure
3. Methanotrophs either assimilate all or one-half of their cell carbon from formaldehyde, depending on the
pathway used (Madigan et al. 2015). Type I methanotrophs contains the following genera; Methylocoddaceae,
Methylobacter and Methylomonas. Type II methanotrophs contains the following genera; Methylocystic and
Methylosinus (Hanson & Hanson 1996).

Almost all bacteria that utilize methane are obligate aerobic methanotrophs (Hanson & Hanson 1996), which
means that they require oxygen for their cellular respiration and are unable to survive for extended periods
without oxygen (Madigan et al. 2015).
The aerobic oxidation reaction of CH4 is catalysed by the enzyme
methane monooxygenase (MMO). One atom of O2 in the MMO reaction is introduced into CH4, which in
turn forms CH3OH (second step in ﬁgure 3). The second oxygen atom of O2 is reduced to H2O. CH3OH is
oxidized by an alcohol dehydrogenase, which yields CH2O and NADH. CH2O is then either used to make new
cell material or oxidized to CO2. In the serine pathway, acetyl-CoA is synthesized from one molecule CH2O
and CO2. This pathway, for type II methanotrophs, requires a reducing power as well as energy. The reducing
power and energy come from two molecules of NADH and ATP, respectively. Meaning, two NADH and two
ATP is needed to synthesize one acetyl-CoA. The RuMP pathway, utilized by type I methanotrophs, is more
energy eﬃcient compared to the serine pathway, because all carbon for the cellular material derives from CH2O.
CH2O has the same oxidation level as the cellular material, meaning no reducing power is needed. All the
NADH from the CH4 oxidation can be oxidized in the electron transport chain (Madigan et al. 2015). The
overall aerobic methane oxidation process, occurs according to the following reaction (Scheutz et al. 2009):
CH4 + 2O2 →CO2 + 2H2O + heat

2.4.1
Factors inﬂuencing methane oxidation
Temperature:
Studies have shown that diﬀerent soil types experience diﬀerent methane oxidation responses, in regard to
temperatures, which indicates that methanotrophs are capable of adapting to diﬀerent temperatures. In Hanson
& Hanson (1996), the optimum temperature for methane oxidation in peat soils were found to be around 25◦C.
However, it was also found that methane oxidation is capable of taking place at temperatures as low as 0-10◦C.
Rates of methane oxidation at 0-5◦C were 10-38% of the maximum rates at 25◦C (Hanson & Hanson 1996).
A literature review done by Scheutz et al. (2009), found that the optimum temperature for CH4 oxidation in
soil environments were in the ranges of 25◦C and 35◦C. The optimum temperature range for methanotrophs
corresponds with that of mesophilic bacteria (ﬁgure 4). This classiﬁcation is similar with the ﬁndings in Hanson
& Hanson (1996), which classiﬁed methanotrophs in pure cultures as mesophiles. Jäckel et al. (2005) studied
thermophilic CH4 production and CH4 oxidation in compost which varied in age.
The authors isolated a
methanotrophic strain which were shown to have a optimum oxidation capacity in the temperature range 45◦C-
55◦C. The study also showed that up to 98% of the produced CH4 was oxidated by the methanotrophs. These
studies indicate that methanotrophs are capable to perform CH4 oxidation under diﬀerent conditions such as
soil type and in a large range of temperatures.

Soil moisture:
The optimum soil moisture content for methanotrophic bacteria in landﬁll cover soils is in the range of 10-20
% w/w (Scheutz et al. 2009), although higher optimum has been observed in some cases. Moisture levels below

5% w/w decreases CH4 oxidation activity drastically (Scheutz et al. 2009). Scheutz & Kjeldsen (2004) found
soil moisture in the range of 18-24% to be optimal for maximum CH4 oxidation rates. Scheutz & Kjeldsen
(2004) also tested moisture content at 50% which shows signiﬁcantly reduced oxidation rates, comparable with
the oxidation rates observed at 5% moisture content. The study concluded that soil moisture is a governing
factor for CH4 oxidation.
pH:
Scheutz & Kjeldsen (2004) studied the impact of pH on CH4 oxidation rates. The study concluded that the
optimum pH value was around neutral pH (6.5-7.5). A pH of 3 and 10 showed oxidation rates close to zero. pH
around 8 showed a small decline in the oxidation rate compared to pH 6-7. It was stated by the authors, that
most landﬁll cover soils have a high buﬀer capacity, therefore concluding that pH will mostly stay in optimal
range for CH4 oxidation and therefore the pH in landﬁll covers is not thought to have a large inﬂuence on the
oxidation capacity.
Copper & Ammonium:
Scheutz & Kjeldsen (2004) tested the inﬂuence of diﬀerent concentrations of copper (Cu) and Ammonium (NH+
4 )
on CH4 oxidation. The soil used in the experiment contained 4.7 mg Cu/kg and 2.3 mg NH+

2.4.2
Factors inﬂuencing competition between type I and type II methanotrophs
In passive bioﬁlters, there is an opposing gradient of O2 and CH4. O2 diﬀuses from the top of the biocover and
downwards, whereas CH4 will diﬀuse from the bottom of the gas distribution layer and upwards. At the top of
the biocover, the O2 concentration will be high, and CH4 concentration will be low. The opposite will be present
towards the bottom of the methane oxidation layer. Amaral & Knowles (1995) made column experiments with
opposing gradients of CH4 and O2 in order to isolate the type of methanotrophic bacteria present, in diﬀerent
zones of the column. The gradients of CH4 and O2 resembled the gradients found in the natural environment.
The results from the DNA extracted from the zones near the top of the column, indicated that the majority of
the methanotrophic bacteria present, were of type I. DNA results from zones near the bottom of the column,
indicated that the majority of the methanotrophic bacteria present were of type II.
The results from Amaral & Knowles (1995) indicates, that type I outperformed type II under low CH4 concen-
tration, while low O2 and high CH4 concentration favoured the growth of type II. Type I methanotrophs have
the tendency to outperform type II methanotrophs, due to the more energy eﬃcient pathway for assimilation of
formaldehyde (RuMP pathway). Inorganic nitrogen or copper limitations could provide an advantage towards
the growth of type II methanotrophs (Scheutz et al. 2009).
2.4.3
CH4 oxidation zone
The majority of methanotrophic bacteria are obligate methanotrophs and strict aerobes (Hanson & Hanson
1996). The activity of methanotrophs thus depends on suﬃcient concentrations of CH4 and O2. Due to the
opposing gradient of CH4 and O2, CH4 oxidation tends to be conﬁned in narrow horizontal zones within their
habitat (Scheutz et al. 2009).

Kightley et al. (1995) studied core samples of landﬁll cover soil, in order to determine at which depth, the
majority of CH4 oxidation takes place.
The maximal oxidation rate was found to be 20-30 cm below the
surface (b.s). At this depth, the CH4 oxidation rate was around three times higher than the other depths of
the core samples. De Visscher et al. (1999) made a similar experiment with soil columns mimicking landﬁll
topsoil layers. They concluded that CH4 oxidation took place, mainly in the top 30 cm of the soil columns.
The maximum CH4 oxidation rate was calculated to take place at around 20 cm depth. Scheutz et al. (2004)
studied the potential for natural attenuation of several diﬀerent volatile organic compounds in landﬁll covers
and simultaneously studied the CH4 oxidation and at the depth CH4 oxidation took place. Soil samples was
collected from Skellingsted Landﬁll in Denmark. High activity of CH4 oxidation was observed down to 50 cm
b.s, with a maximum oxidation rate 15-20 cm b.s. Scheutz et al. (2004) also concluded, that 15-20 cm b.s, is the
zone that allowed optimal supply of both CH4 and O2, resulting in the maximum CH4 oxidation rate. Scheutz
et al. (2009) reviewed the state-of-the-science regarding CH4 oxidation at landﬁlls and natural environments.
The review shows that the active zone where CH4 oxidation occur is 30-40 cm b.s. Maximum CH4 oxidation
rates were observed in a narrow zone 15-20 cm b.s.

Biocovers & bioﬁlters in Denmark
In table 2 the planned and established bioﬁlters/biocovers in Denmark are presented. Three of the projects
(Fakse, Klintholm and AV miljø) were constructed prior to the subsidy provided by the Danish EPA to the
construction of bioﬁlters. The experience from those three projects have resulted in some of the general guidelines
used to construct and plan the other projects (Scheutz, Fredenslund, Chanton, Pedersen & Kjeldsen 2011,
Scheutz et al. 2017a,b, Pedersen et al. 2012). All the projects consist of a 30 cm gas distribution layer (singels)
and a minimum of 80 cm methane oxidation layer, which in all cases consist of matured compost.
In all
cases, the general guidelines from the Danish EPA on using compost as methane oxidation material have been
followed. This includes that the compost have been matured for at least six months, exposed to suﬃcient oxygen
(compost have been turned multiple times) and sorted in order to insure the compost materials have the correct
size (<15mm). The only exception is Horsens, which consist solely of a 150 cm thick layer of matured compost
acting as the oxidation layer, which can explain the low emissions from this landﬁll compared to the others.
There is 23 projects in total, 15 of those uses a biowindow system (BWS), 12 uses passive open bioﬁlters (BF-
PO), 6 uses active open bioﬁlters (BF-AO), 1 (Gerringe) uses a bioactive trench (BIT) and 1 (Horsens) uses
a full surface biocover (FSB). Although, an FSB would normally consist of a gas distribution layer as well as
a methane oxidation layer. For the Horsens project, it is assumed that the ﬁnal soil cover of 150 cm compost
closely resembles a full surface biocover. 11 of the projects uses a combination of diﬀerent systems, mostly a
combination of BF-PO and BWS. Further description of the diﬀerent systems types can be found in (Kjeldsen &
Scheutz 2016). The area necessary to mitigate the produced LFG from the diﬀerent landﬁlls vary greatly. The
area of biocovers/bioﬁlters depends upon the CH4 emissions from the landﬁll as well as the expected methane
oxidation capacity of the matured compost. Kjeldsen & Scheutz (2016) recommends that the necessary area of
the biocover/bioﬁlter is based upon a methane oxidation capacity of 50g CH4/m2/day for the matured compost.
Kjeldsen & Scheutz (2016) also points out, that this number could be increased to 100g CH4/m2/day, when
more experience have been gathered in order to avoid hotspot areas at the biocovers/bioﬁlters. Several of the
projects presented in table 2, mentions that the Danish EPA have concluded, that a oxidation capacity of 75g
CH4/m2/day can be used, if gas distribution pipes are used in the gas distribution layer, to insure the produced
LFG will be distributed more evenly.

In the following two sections, general information regarding Reno Djurs, Glatved landﬁll and its solution to
mitigate landﬁll gas are presented. This study will solely focus on the shredder cells (II-A, II-B and II-M) and
the corresponding three active bioﬁlters at cell I-c. This is mainly due to the fact, that the three bioﬁlters are
active bioﬁlters, which makes estimating the eﬃciency more accurate than for a passive solution, which is the
case for the bio window system (BWS) installed at cell I-b, and simultaneously, the bioﬁlters at cell I-c treats
the majority of the produced LFG.
3.1
General information
The area at Reno djurs Glatved, were until 1981 an area where limestone was industrial recovered. In 1981, a
landﬁll facility was established in the former mining area. From 1981 until 1996, a total of two million tonnes
of primary construction, refuse collections and non-recyclable bulky waste were landﬁlled. In 1996, Reno Djurs
Glatved was established. One of the main drivers for the establishment, were stricter requirements regarding
landﬁlling of waste, and a prohibition regarding landﬁlling of combustible waste. In 2009, stage I of the landﬁll
were closed, due to stricter environmental legislation. The new landﬁll area, opened 16th June 2009, and has
clay and plastic membrane, as well as a leachate collection system (Reno Djurs 2020).
23th June 2016: Renodjurs applied the danish Environmental Protection Agency (miljøstyrelsen) of funding to
perform a baseline investigation and subsequently for the construction of a biocover project at stage I.
14th July 2016: Funding granted by the danish EPA.
14th October and 15th December 2016: FORCE technology measured the combined emissions of CH4 at Reno
djurs (baseline investigation).
Based on the baseline investigation, an average emission of 28.5 kg CH4/h were measured. It was determined
that approximately 10 kg CH4/h could be utilized by a gas engine to generate electricity, whereas the rest must
be treated through a biocover/bioﬁlter solution. It is expected, that the construction of biocovers will reduce
the emissions of CH4 by 80-90% (RMR 2018a).
At area I-b, near the slope, a passive biocover (BWS) have been constructed, to treat the produced LFG
originating from the landﬁlled mixed waste. It is estimated that the produced LFG from the mixed waste
accounts for 4.5 kg CH4/h. At area I-c, three active bioﬁlters (BF-AO) are constructed, to treat the produced
LFG originating from the landﬁlled shredder waste. It is estimated that the produced LFG from the shredder

4.1
Surface screening of CH4
The CH4 emissions, measured in ppmv, at the surface of the three bioﬁlters were measured with a hand-held
Laser One Portable Methane Leak Detector (Huberg, Italy). The Laser One (ﬁgure 10) measures CH4 emission
in the range of 0-10,000 ppmv. The Laser One was bought with a GPS, which could map the emissions with a
speciﬁc location. Unfortunately, the GPS had a malfunction, and could therefore not be used in combination
with the Laser One. The Laser One was not able to save the measurements, meaning that the only available
option was to conduct point measurements.
The point measurements were conducted by standing at each
location and have the probe around 5 cm from the surface. Each location was measured approximately for 20
seconds. This provided multiple readings which were averaged on the spot due to the Laser One not being able
to log measurements.
Figure 10: Laser One used to conduct surface screenings of CH4 and probe connected to the instrument
To optimize the surface screening, the bioﬁlters were divided into grids, each measuring 2m x 2m (4 m2), 294-350
grids in total. Scheutz et al. (2017a) used a grid system (2 m2 pr. grid) when conducting their surface screening
of CH4 and CO2 at a pilot scale bioﬁlter at AV miljø, which had an area of around 500 m2. In this study,
the same procedure is used, although with grids twice as large. Next to the bioﬁlters, spray paint was used to
mark every 2 m in the width and length to help establish the grids. At the bioﬁlters, tent pegs were used as
guidelines, to ensure a straight path was taken during screening. The tent pegs were placed at the surface of
the bioﬁlters, to ensure minimum disturbance of the CH4 oxidation layer.
Before conducting the measurements, the Laser One was tested with a test gas, to ensure that the instrument

4.7
CH4 oxidation by the carbon mass balance method
One of the main objectives of this thesis, is to evaluate the CH4 oxidation eﬃciency for the active bioﬁlters
constructed at Reno Djurs. To get the best estimates of the CH4 oxidation eﬃciency, with the measurements
conducted, is by using the carbon mass balance approach. This approach assumes stoichiometric conditions,
which means that the combined ﬂux of carbon into the bioﬁlter must equal the ﬂux of carbon out of the bioﬁlter.
The carbon ﬂux into the bioﬁlter will depend on the concentration of CH4 and CO2 in the LFG. The carbon
ﬂux out of the bioﬁlter will be based on the CH4 and CO2 ﬂux measurements as well as the CO2 production
from respiration, which must be accounted for.
The carbon mass balance approach have previously been used in other studies (Scheutz, Fredenslund, Chanton,
Pedersen & Kjeldsen 2011, Christophersen et al. 2001, Scheutz et al. 2017b, Kjeldsen & Scheutz 2019).
This approach is based on the following assumptions:
• The methanotrophs are considered to be in steady state condition. Meaning decay equal growth.
• CO2 are not dissolved in the seepage water.
• There exist no gas production in the substrate soil (CH4 under anaerobic conditions).

Technical University of Denmark
• The vertical gas ﬂow is uniform in the area of the sampling point

5.1
Surface screening
The screening of CH4 surface emission at the three active bioﬁlters (IC-1, IC-2 and IC-3) were conducted by
the use of point measurements, that divided each bioﬁlter into a series of grids (4 m2 each). The density of the
measurements is therefore one measurement pr. 4 m2. The raw data for the surface screening are presented
in ﬁgure 17.
To get a better overview of the results, each grid has been coloured based on the measured
concentration. All values measured with a concentration < 10 ppm is green, values in the range of 10-30 ppm
is orange and values measured above 30 ppm is red.
It can be seen in ﬁgure 17 that there are some similarities between the three bioﬁlters. Both IC-1 and IC-3
have low CH4 emissions from 48-84 m (IC-1) and 44-100 m (IC-3). It can also be observed that the majority
of the higher emissions, >10 ppm, occurs in the ﬁrst 40 m (IC-1) and ﬁrst 30 m (IC-3). The highest emissions
observed, > 30 ppm, seem to be close to the gas inlet and the surrounding grids also show elevated emissions.
The grids with very ﬂuctuating emissions (bold and underlined numbers) are also quite close to each other.
For IC-2, the results show a diﬀerent emission pattern compared to IC-1 and IC-3. It can be seen from ﬁgure
17 that most of the elevated emissions occur in the ﬁrst 56 m but also at 80-96 m. The areas with elevated
emissions are also more scattered throughout the whole bioﬁlter, than what was observed from IC-1 and IC-3.
The data presented in ﬁgure 17 has been used to create the surface emission gradient graphs presented in ﬁgure
18. For IC-1, the areas which have the highest emissions start from around 10 m in the length and continues to
around 40 m in the length. At the start of the bioﬁlter, as well as from 50 m in length until the end, the emissions
from the bioﬁlter is similar with the measured background concentration (2.4 ppm CH4). From ﬁgure 17, it
can be observed that the area which has the highest emissions in IC-1, is in a narrow zone between 16 m and
22 m in length and the whole width. Especially at grid B22-G22 the emissions measured were very ﬂuctuating.
The seven grids showed emissions in the range of 90-210 ppm CH4, before decreasing to the presented values.
The emission pattern for IC-2 is quite diﬀerent compared to IC-1 and IC-3. Firstly, the elevated emissions are

Gas composition in the CH4 oxidation layer
In the following section, data for the gas proﬁles and CH4/CO2 ratio for the individual stations will be presented
and discussed, as a change in the ratio strongly indicate CH4 oxidation according to Pedersen et al. (2012).
Data for the gas proﬁles will be used to explain these ratios. The gas composition at the ﬁve stations have been
measured throughout the four measurement campaigns. The gas composition in the diﬀerent depths have been
used to construct a series of gas proﬁles which are presented in appendix D.5 (ﬁgure 40-59). The data for the
individual gas proﬁles can be found in the attached excel sheets:
• Excel document name: Campaign 2, sheet name: gas proﬁles, contains data for ﬁgure 40-44.
• Excel document name: Campaign 3, sheet name: gas proﬁles, contains data for ﬁgure 45-49.
• Excel document name: Campaign 4, sheet name: gas proﬁles, contains data for ﬁgure 50-54.
• Excel document name: Campaign 5, sheet name: gas proﬁles, contains data for ﬁgure 55-59.
The gas composition from the exhaust manifold (section 5.2) will be used as the baseline for the gas composition
and CH4/CO2 ratio. According to section 2.4, CH4 & O2 will be consumed and CO2 produced under CH4
oxidation. It is therefore reasonable to assume that the ratio will approach zero when oxidation takes place.
Furthermore, the O2 concentration will decrease. Production of CO2 and consumption of O2 might also be
explained by respiration in the compost but would fail to explain the consumption of CH4.
If CH4 oxidation does not occur, CH4 and CO2 would diﬀuse from the bottom towards the surface with no
signiﬁcant changes in the ratio (Pedersen et al. 2012). O2 would diﬀuse from the bottom towards the surface
but also vice versa due to intrusion of atmospheric air into the bioﬁlter.
The general observations for the measured gas proﬁles across the four measurement campaigns can be summa-
rized to:
• CH4 & O2 concentration decreases and CO2 concentration increases at the deepest probe compared to
the exhaust manifold.
• The CH4 concentration is highest at station E8 and lowest at B90
• The CO2 concentration tends to increase towards 0.3 m b.s and then starts to decrease towards 0.1 m b.s.
• O2 intrusion from the atmospheric air usually reach down to 0.3 m b.s.
It is important to notice, that the measurements conducted at the 2nd & 3rd campaign were done while the
combined LFG ﬂow to the three bioﬁlters was 78 m3/h. For the 4th & 5th campaign, the combined ﬂow was
increased to 95 m3/h. The increased amount of CH4 entering the bioﬁlters might have inﬂuenced the results.
To evaluate to what extend CH4 oxidation takes place, the CH4/CO2 ratio was calculated for each station and
depth throughout the four measurement campaigns. All data on the CH4/CO2 ratio for the ﬁve gas probe
stations as well as the exhaust manifold are presented in the attached appendix excel document: ﬁnal results,
sheet name: CH4 CO2 ratio V2.

All measurements throughout the four measurement campaigns showed the same two trends.
Firstly, the
CH4/CO2 is decreasing from the exhaust manifold to the deepest probe. Secondly, the CH4/CO2 ratio tends to
decrease with distance from the exhaust manifold. The ratio is much higher at E8 than for B90. One possibility
for this diﬀerence at B90 could compared to E8 be a decreasing LFG ﬂow with distance from the exhaust
manifold. If the bioﬁlter is operated below its maximum capacity, it is likely that most of the CH4 in the LFG
is oxidized before reaching the end of the bioﬁlter, which could explain the general low CH4 concentration at B90.
The trend with decreasing CH4/CO2 ratio from the exhaust manifold to the deepest probe strongly suggest that
CH4 oxidation must take place in the area between these two locations. This is based on the observation of a
decreasing CH4 & O2 concentration from the exhaust manifold to the deepest probes. Furthermore, an increase
in CO2 concentration has been observed in nearly all instances from the exhaust manifold to the deepest probe.
The only three exceptions to these observations are the measurements at B90 in the fourth campaign, which
will be further elaborated in the end of this section.
At the exhaust manifold, the O2 concentration ranges between 7.2% and 9.2%.
Of the 84 measurements
conducted at the ﬁve stations, only a few cases showed a O2 concentration higher than 0.5% at the deepest
probe. Station E8 had overall the highest O2 concentrations at the deepest probe, which were in the range of
0.2%-2.0%, which corresponds to minimum 70% reduction compared to the 7.2% in the exhaust manifold. The
decrease in CH4 & O2 and the increase in CO2 suggest that CH4 oxidation has taken place between the exhaust
manifold and the deepest probe. Between the exhaust manifold and the deepest probe, the gas distribution
layer is located.
The gas distribution layer should not be capable of performing CH4 oxidation due to its inert properties.
However organic materials from the CH4 oxidation layer could be present in the pore volume, making CH4
oxidation a possibility, which is assumed to be the case. This intrusion of organic materials from the CH4
oxidation layer might also explain why the depth is less than 0.7 m compared to the initial 0.8 m.
Station E8 and C12 is in close proximity to each other (6 m between the stations) and it would therefore be
assumed that the LFG ﬂow and the CH4/CO2 ratio at these two locations are very similar, which is not the
case. The ratio at the deepest probe is much higher at E8 compared to the ratio at C12, which is observed in
table 5 as well as in tables 14-16.
The average CH4/CO2 ratio at E8 is approximately 3 times higher than the average at C12. In comparison, the
ratios at E8 is only around 1.6 times higher than the ratios at F22 (14 m from E8). The ratio at station D60
also seems to suggest a lower LFG ﬂow at C12. on average the ratio at D60 is 1.2-1.4 times higher than for C12,
even though there is around 48 m between the two stations. It is unknown why there seems to be such a vast
diﬀerence between E8 and C12 given the short distance between those two stations. One possible explanation
could be a lower LFG ﬂow at station C12. With the assumption that the gas distribution layer can oxidize a
ﬁxed amount of CH4, a lower ﬂow to a certain area would result in a lower amount of CH4 present and would
hence inﬂuence the ratio to a higher degree. Another possible explanation for the lower ratio observed at C12,
would be a higher oxidation eﬃciency within the gas distribution layer.
The low CH4/CO2 ratios observed at B90 is assumed to be related to a low LFG ﬂow due to CH4 oxidation prior
to this station and not because of poor gas distribution. If the majority of gas probes measurements at B90
showed a CH4 concentration of 0.0% at the deepest probe, it would have been harder to distinguish between a
poor gas distribution in the region or if all CH4 in the LFG is already oxidized before reaching B90. With CH4
concentrations up to 10% (ﬁrst campaign) at the deepest probe, strongly indicates a good gas distribution. A
way to test this, would be to introduce a tracer gas to the LFG. The tracer gas would have to be a inert gas,

spots, due to the CH4 not being fully oxidized. This incomplete oxidation at 0.1 m b.s was observed in ﬁve

5.4
Temperature proﬁles in the CH4 oxidation layer
The factors governing the temperature in the CH4 oxidation layer is the temperature of the LFG, heat generated
from CH4 oxidation and compost respiration and the ambient temperature (Pedersen et al. 2012).
The respiration rate has been assumed to be equal across the bioﬁlter when estimating the oxidation eﬃciency
(section 5.7) and the heat production from this process is therefore also assumed to be equal across the bioﬁlter
and will not be discussed further.
The average temperature proﬁles for the two last measurement campaigns are presented in ﬁgure 22.
The
discussion will only be based on these results, due to a larger sample size for the last two campaigns. The
results suggest, that throughout the four measurement campaigns, there was a trend with overall decreasing
temperatures in the CH4 oxidation layer with an increasing distance from the exhaust manifold. These results are
in accordance with the results presented in section 5.3 which showed less CH4 oxidation with increasing distance
from the exhaust manifold. The CH4 oxidation produces heat (section 2.4), and the decreasing oxidation would
therefore suggest that less heat will be produced.
Station E8 & C12 experienced an increasing temperature with depth which is similar to the observations done
by (Pedersen et al. 2012). The same trend was observed for station F22, D60 and B90 until a depth of 0.3 m
b.s. At station F22, the temperature was stable beneath this depth, whereas the temperature for D60 & B90
were decreasing. The temperature at 0.1 m b.s at the diﬀerent stations seems to deviate less than depths below
0.1 m b.s. These results were similar with the observations done by Pedersen et al. (2012) which observed the
top layer temperature (down to 0.2 m b.s) was to a higher degree the result of the ambient temperature and
was less inﬂuenced by CH4 oxidation.
The inﬂuence of the LFG temperature is assumed to be greatest at the bottom of the bioﬁlter and will decrease
towards the surface. It is also assumed that some of the heat from the LFG is lost due to heat conduction to
the surroundings. The temperature of the LFG at the exhaust manifold & the gas distribution layer was not
measured as there were no places of doing so. If it were possible to demonstrate that the LFG temperature
decreased with distance from the container, it could partly explain the diﬀerences in temperature at 0.6 m b.s
observed in ﬁgure 22.
The temperatures for E8 and C12 show no statistical diﬀerence at all depths but both stations show statistically
higher temperatures at 0.5 m b.s and 0.4 m b.s compared to station F22 (table 9). If CH4 oxidation was the
only governing factor for the temperature diﬀerences it would require that (1) F22 had lower CH4/CO2 ratios
than both E8 and C12 and (2) The similar temperatures observed at E8 and C12 had similar ratios.

be compared to see if there exist any statistical signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the temperature proﬁles.

if there existed a statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the measured temperature. The calculated p-values are
presented in table 9. It is observed that with increasing depth, temperatures between stations deviate from one
another. At 0.1 m b.s, the temperature between E8 and B90 as well as F22 and B90 is the only two comparisons
that showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence (p-value of 0.02 and 0.04 respectively). With increasing depth, more of the

as well as D60 & B90 is the only two comparisons that suggested no signiﬁcant diﬀerence (p-value of 0.16 and
0.07 respectively).
These results could indicate that towards the bottom of the CH4 oxidation layer, the CH4 oxidation, CH4 con-
centration and LFG gas temperature has a large inﬂuence on the temperature, whereas the ambient temperature
plays a more signiﬁcant role towards the surface. The increasing inﬂuence of ambient temperature towards the
surface are likely when comparing the p-values presented in table 9, where nearly all cases at 0.1 m b.s showed
no signiﬁcant diﬀerence, which is similar to the observations done by (Pedersen et al. 2012). It would also be
assumed, that the ambient temperature has a similar eﬀect across the whole bioﬁlter.
Based on the analysis of the temperature at the ﬁve stations, it is concluded that the diﬀerences observed in
the temperatures across the ﬁve stations is to some extend governed by the CH4 concentration present which
subsequently leads to how much can be oxidized. The signiﬁcant diﬀerences in temperatures between the ﬁve
stations decreases towards the surface. At 0.5m b.s, nearly all stations showed statistical diﬀerent results from
the other stations. At 0.1m b.s, only two comparisons showed a statistically diﬀerence. It therefore seems likely,

5.5.1
Stationary ﬂux - CH4
Most of the ﬂux measurements in ﬁgure 23 is observed to be negative. The negative CH4 ﬂuxes suggest that
there is a net uptake of CH4 in the oxidation layer while conducting the ﬂux measurement. These results suggest
that the CH4 emissions from the ﬁve locations are low or near ambient concentrations. For station F22, three
ﬂux measurements were observed to be positive while the remaining measurements were negative. These results
suggest that the surface emissions from bioﬁlter is dynamic and can change on diﬀerent time scales.
At the third and ﬁfth campaign, only negative ﬂuxes were observed, which suggest that the increase in LFG
between the third and fourth campaign did not exceed the maximum CH4 capacity for the bioﬁlter. As it
has been presented in section 5.3, the vast majority of the measurements from the gas probes showed a CH4
concentration of 0.0% at 0.1 m b.s or deeper, which further support the statement of a highly eﬀective bioﬁlter
and it operates below its maximum capacity. If the maximum capacity where exceeded, it would be expected
to see an increase in surface emissions and subsequently, see an increase in CH4 concentration at the 0.1m b.s
probe.
It has been concluded by several authors, that the barometric pressure inﬂuences the surface emissions on a
landﬁll. There is although some major diﬀerences between their studies and this study. The largest diﬀerence
being that this study focuses on measurements on an active bioﬁlter, whereas the studies presented earlier are
mostly considering landﬁll cover soil. Nevertheless, this study wanted to see, if there is a relationship between
the measured surface ﬂuxes and the barometric pressure for the active bioﬁlter. If there is a relationship between
the surface ﬂuxes and the barometric pressure, it is assumed to follow the same relationship as presented earlier.
Namely, a higher barometric pressure leads to a lower surface emission ﬂux.
Due to the increase in LFG ﬂow between the 3rd campaign and 4th campaign, it is not considered valid to
include samples from all four campaigns, because of diﬀerent conditions, which could inﬂuence the conclusion
on this matter.
To assess the potential relationship between surface emission ﬂuxes on the active bioﬁlter
and barometric pressure, data from the 4th and 5th campaign is considered, because of a larger sample size.
Czepiel et al. (2003) presented a strong inverse relationship between the measured CH4 and the corresponding
barometric pressure, and linear regression showed, that the barometric pressure explains approximately 93%
(r2 = 0.93) of the variation in the data. Plotting the CH4 stationary ﬂux data to the barometric pressure in
the same manner as Czepiel et al. (2003) resulted in the following r2 values: E8 (0.59), C12 (0.32), F22 (0.23),
D60 (0.0028) and B90 (0.52). These results suggest that the barometric pressure could have an inﬂuence on the
surface emissions but more measurements are needed in order to determine a stronger relationship.

Stationary ﬂux - CO2
The stationary ﬂux measurements for CO2 are presented in ﬁgure 25. There seems to be no obvious trend
between the stations and the observed ﬂuxes. Across the four measurement campaigns, the highest ﬂux was
measured at B90 ﬁve out of ten times and at F22 ﬁve out of ten times. The lowest CO2 ﬂuxes is divided between
D60 (ﬁve times), B90 (two times) and C12 (three times).
Considering the increased LFG ﬂow to the bioﬁlters, it would be expected that the average CO2 ﬂux would
increase with a higher ﬂow, because more CH4 can be oxidized and will subsequently produce more CO2. To
determine if there exist a statistical diﬀerence between the campaigns, the Welch two sample t-test was applied
to the results.
This test calculates a p-value based on a 95% conﬁdence interval. If the p-value is above 0.05, no statistical
signiﬁcance can be concluded based on the samples (Brockhoﬀet al. 2017). Based on this, no statistical diﬀerence
can be concluded for station E8, C12 and B90, which has p-value in the range of 0.26-0.39. The p-value for
F22 is 0.056 which is near the limit but still above. For D60, the p-value is 0.03 which means that based on
the samples obtained, there is a statistical signiﬁcance between the CO2 ﬂuxes. It must be taken into account,
that these results are based on a small number of samples when doing the comparison. It is likely, that if the
stationary ﬂux chambers had been measured 2-3 times a day instead of only once, that there would be a larger
indication on whether or not there existed a signiﬁcantly diﬀerence in the CO2 ﬂux between the two LFG ﬂows.
This apparent dynamic system, as described with the great changes observed with the CH4 ﬂuxes at F22, is
further demonstrated when looking at the surface ﬂux for CO2 at station B90. Especially when combining the
CO2 ﬂuxes with the results in section 5.3 where no CH4 were found at June 11th. The surface ﬂux of CO2
up to this date have been in the range of 204 to 350 g CO2/m2*day for B90 and in the range of 120 to 350 g
CO2/m2*day for all ﬁve stations. On June 11th, the CO2 surface ﬂux decreased to 23 g CO2/m2*day at B90
(ﬁgure 25). Based on these uncommon results, it was determined that at B90, no CH4 oxidation could have
taken place. It was therefore concluded that the measured CO2 ﬂux must be from compost respiration.

5.7
Eﬃciency based on carbon mass balance
To determine the oxidation eﬃciency of the bioﬁlter it was necessary to know the CO2 produced by respiration
in the compost.
A constant respiration rate of 23 g CO2/m2*day was used when calculating the oxidation eﬃciency based on
the mobile and stationary ﬂux chamber measurements. This particular respiration rate has been determined
based on the CO2 emission rates measured at the stationary ﬂux chamber at station B90, campaign 4, June
11th. Prior to the ﬂux chamber measurement, the gas probes at the station were measured. The results were
vastly diﬀerent compared to earlier measurements done at this station. First oﬀ, no CH4 were measured in
any of the ﬁve gas probes. Secondly, the O2 concentration were 15-20% in all depths and simultaneously, there
were no inverse relationship between the CO2 concentration and O2. This inverse relationship between CO2
and O2 have normally been detected, and demonstrates CH4 oxidation, due to the consumption of O2 and the
production of CO2 in the process. All this indicated that CH4 oxidation did not take place around this time.
It was therefore concluded that the CO2 emissions from the oxidation layer must be a product of compost
respiration.
The assumed CO2 emissions from the respiration accounts to 8-12% of the total CO2 emissions measured at
the ﬁve stations.
With the respiration rate determined, it was possible to calculate the oxidation eﬃciency based on the stationary
and mobile ﬂux chamber measurements.
For the stationary ﬂux chambers, the oxidation eﬃciency was in the range of 98%-100.7%. For the mobile ﬂux
chambers, the eﬃciency was in the range of 95%-100.9%. The CH4 surface emissions is in the range of 0-0.3 g
CH4/m2*day (table 26).
These results correspond with the results from another study conducted at AV miljø. Filippo (2013) measured
the CH4 mitigation capacity at a pilot scale active bioﬁlter at AV miljø under his master thesis. His ﬁndings
showed an average CH4 oxidation eﬃciency of 99.7% and low CH4 surface emissions in the range of 0-0.16 g
CH4/m2*day.
Estimations of eﬃciency based on ﬂux chamber measurements is not without uncertainties. One of the main
uncertainties with this estimation is the covered area compared to the whole bioﬁlter with the mobile ﬂux
chambers. It was only approximately 50% of the whole bioﬁlter area that was covered and consisted of one ﬂux
chamber measurement for every 10 m2. The bioﬁlter have been shown to be very dynamic in terms of surface
ﬂuxes and LFG composition within the bioﬁlter. For the gas probes, it is especially the sudden disappearance of
CH4 at B90 and D60 and high CH4 concentration at E8 at 0.1 m b.s (3.5%-6.9%), all three instances occurring
the same day.
Even though the bioﬁlter seems to have a very dynamic system, the overall results for the performed measure-
ments described in earlier sections, all seems to indicate a highly eﬀective system which can be explained by:
Most gas probes measurements (77 out of 84) showed a CH4 concentration of 0.0% (detection limit) at 0.1 m
b.s or lower. The majority of the stationary ﬂux measurements (44 out of 50) showed negative ﬂuxes, indicating
a uptake of CH4 in the surface soil while measuring the ﬂux. The average CH4 emissions based on the mobile
ﬂux measurement have been estimated with 95% conﬁdence to be in the range of 0-0.3 g CH4/m2*day which
also indicate (1) low surface emissions and (2) low ﬂuctuation between the measurements.

Conclusion
The initial assumption that the similar design of the three bioﬁlters would result in similar surface emissions
of CH4 could not be veriﬁed due to a lack of surface screenings. Multiple CH4 surface screenings of the three
bioﬁlters could not be performed due to the equipment used in the ﬁrst campaign was not available for the
remaining campaigns. The performance monitoring was conducted on bioﬁlter IC-3, which was the largest of the
three bioﬁlters. The LFG composition in the exhaust manifold was analysed and plotted against the changes in
barometric pressure. Based on these results it is concluded that the LFG composition in the exhaust manifold
is not inﬂuenced signiﬁcantly by the barometric pressure.
The gas proﬁles analysed at the stationary ﬂux placements at IC-3 show in general a larger quantity of CH4 in
the gas proﬁles closer to the inlet of LFG. The CH4/CO2 ratios for the gas proﬁles and the exhaust manifold
indicate that CH4 oxidation already happens in the gas distribution layer. This could be an eﬀect of a possible
intrusion of compost into the layer allowing the presence of methanotrophs. The ratios also show that most
of the CH4 oxidation layer is active closer to the inlet of LFG. The ratios and gas proﬁles indicate that the
majority of CH4 in the LFG is oxidized before reaching the end of the bioﬁlter. This can partly explain why
higher temperatures in the start of the bioﬁlter is observed. The temperature of the LFG is also believed to
have a larger impact on the temperature proﬁles near the exhaust manifold compared to the temperature proﬁle
in the end. This is although only a hypothesis, as the temperature of the LFG could not be measured.
The stationary ﬂux measurements show in general very low ﬂuxes of CH4 which indicate a high eﬃciency. The
barometric pressure seems to have a correlation with the surface emissions, but more measurements are needed
to determine whether this relationship is causal or not.
The mobile ﬂux measurements showed a statistical increase in surface emissions of CH4 with the increased LFG
ﬂow. It is unknown if this signiﬁcant diﬀerence is the result of changes in barometric pressure or changes in
LFG ﬂow, although the LFG ﬂow is believed to be a governing factor for the measured surface emissions.
The mobile ﬂux measurements of CH4 show with 95% conﬁdence an average CH4 emission rate of 0-0.25
g CH4/m2*day which is comparable with another study which also analysed the performance of an active
bioﬁlter. Even with the increased CH4 emissions, the eﬃciency based on the carbon mass balance approach
showed a minimum eﬃciency of 95% which exceeds the estimated eﬃciency of 80%-90%. The measurements
did not locate any areas with substantially higher emissions which provide greater certainty to the estimated
minimum eﬃciency.
The high eﬃciency in the bioﬁlter is concluded to be largely due to the introduction of O2 in the LFG before
it enters the bioﬁlter. This allowed CH4 oxidation to take place in most of the CH4 oxidation layer compared
to the literature, which have shown the optimal CH4 oxidation zone to be conﬁned in a narrow zone at 0.2 m
b.s - 0.3 m b.s.
Throughout the campaigns, there have been multiple observations indicating that the bioﬁlter is dynamic in
terms of observed surface emissions and gas composition, especially with changing emission rates at the same
locations and changing LFG composition in the gas probes. It is therefore a possibility that there exist areas
within the bioﬁlter that have higher emissions than what has been reported and would hence have inﬂuenced
the estimated eﬃciency. The results from the gas proﬁles also seems to indicate to some degree, that further
increases in LFG ﬂow could result in the area close to the exhaust manifold being over-loaded and would hence
result in insuﬃcient CH4 oxidation and create unintentional hot spots.

Installation of gas probes
A series of ﬁve gas probes were installed at two places at IC-1 (C20-C22 & C70-C72) and at two places at IC-3
(B10-B12 & B70-B72). As seen from ﬁgure 17 C20-C22 at IC-1 and B10-B12 at IC-3 are some of the areas with
high emissions compared to C70-C72 and B70-B72. The decision to place the gas probes in those areas, was to
get some initial data regarding the gas composition in diﬀerent depths of the bioﬁlters. The gas composition in
the exhaust manifold was not measured that day, meaning the presented data for this campaign can not be used
to compare the composition between the diﬀerent depths and the exhaust manifold. For future campaigns, this
will be done, in order to see how the gas composition changes from the exhaust manifold to the deepest gas probe.
The probes were placed 40 cm apart in a straight line (ﬁgure 36). The probes were installed in ﬁve diﬀerent
depths; 70cm, 60cm, 50cm, 40cm and 20cm. Initially, it was decided to have a probe in 80cm below the surface,
given that it should be the bottom of the methane oxidation layer (RMR 2018a). In all four places, the probes
could not get deeper than 70cm, which was here the gas distribution layer was located. It therefore seems that
the methane oxidation layer have decreased to 70cm in height from the initial 80cm. The decrease in height
is not surprising, given that compost is a unstable material and will continue to mature over a prolonged time
(Kjeldsen & Scheutz 2016).

The results from the four gas proﬁles are presented in ﬁgure 37. The initial assumption for the bioﬁlters was that
methane oxidation took place in the upper 30cm of the methane oxidation layer. Due to the oxidation process
requires a presence of both O2 and CH4 in suﬃcient concentrations, it was thought that the methane oxidation
zone could occur deeper than 0.3m below the surface, primarily due to the active inﬂux of O2 in the bioﬁlters.
Looking at the high emission area at IC-1, O2 is present at 0.7m (2.2%) and decreases to 0% at 0.5m. At 0.2m
the O2 concentration is at 0.2%. For CH4, it seems like very little methane oxidation takes place through out
the oxidation layer. At 0.7m the concentration of CH4 is 15.4% which almost steadily decreases to 14%. This
small decrease in the concentration of CH4 is more likely to be primary diﬀusion than CH4 oxidation. The
same is observed at IC-3 in the high emission area, were CH4 is almost constant through the diﬀerent depths
(15.1% - 14.4%). CH4 oxidation is characterized by, among other things, the sharp break in the curve, which
are observed at IC-3 in the low emission area, which would have been expected in all four places, but is only
observed at IC-3 B70-B72.
