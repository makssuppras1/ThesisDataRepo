ABSTRACT 
Despite more than a decade of designing and testing genetic circuits, the process of making new circuits to 
perform a specific task remains a laborious process due to our inability to accurately predict gene and 
protein expression. High-throughput and multiplex methods to characterize genetic circuits offer a 
potential solution to this by testing and collecting data from thousands of circuits in a single experiment. 
Here, we synthesized a library of 135,016 unique oligonucleotides designed to systematically explore 
promoter architecture and protein expression from simple genetic circuits. Each oligonucleotide contained 
a genetic circuit consisting of two regulatory elements: One element regulated one of 13 different 
transcription factors, the other a fluorescent report. Here, we show that a method termed FlowSeq can be 
used to examine the output of thousands of genetic circuits differing in their transcription factor expression 
and the number, location and strength of their operator. We also quantify the effect of all possible single 
nucleotide mutations in three different promoters. This allows us to map the mutations most important for 
transcription and find that there are subtle differences between promoters. We attempt a simple additive 
model to predict the effect of mutations, but find a tendency towards an inverse relationship between the 
number of mutations and the accuracy of our prediction.  
This study shows that DNA synthesis in combination with FlowSeq is a powerful technique for multiplex 
characterization of genetic circuits and elucidating the sequence-structure relationship of DNA.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Engineering Biology 
In 1972, Jackson et al. published the first study on recombining DNA, where they inserted the galactose 
operon and genes from the λ-phage into a Simian Virus 401. The following year, Cohen et al. published a 
different method for molecular cloning using restriction enzymes and DNA ligase2  which continues to 
remain a widely used tool for molecular cloning even today. With Cohen’s technique, scientists were given 
the first tool to engineer biology at a whole new level by recombining DNA to create novel sequences, not 
seen in Nature.  
Today, molecular cloning remains a central technique in biology. Two disciplines in particular benefit from 
molecular cloning as a tool to alter the physiology of the cell: Synthetic biology and metabolic engineering. 
Although there is no official definition of synthetic biology3, the discipline is characterized by a focus on 
constructive approaches to understand and modify biological systems for almost any imaginable purpose4. 
The discipline of metabolic engineering is about designing, engineering and optimizing pathways for the 
production of various products such as fuels, chemicals and pharmaceuticals5. Although the two disciplines 
may share some, if not several overlaps, they are of interest here as both benefit from improved design and 
understanding of genetic circuits as a means to control a particular output (i.e. protein production).  
A major goal of metabolic engineering is converting traditional microorganisms into highly efficient cell 
factories for chemicals/protein production at industrial scales. This involves redesign and optimization of 
enzymatic pathways and metabolic fluxes in cells, as their physiology is completely repurposed towards a 
“non-natural” purpose (although the aim may be to produce a natural compound, the production titers 
aimed for are higher than wild-type organisms would sustain). This has led to a number of breakthroughs 
including the production of artemisinic acid in engineered yeast (a precursor that is easily converted to the 
antimalarial drug, artemisinin)6, various types of alcohols useful as biofuels or polymers7–9, D-glutaric acid10, 
succinate11, p-hydroxybenzoate12 and styrene13 to name but a few.  
Based on one’s perspective, synthetic biology may involve testing biological hypotheses, creating novel 
molecules and molecular systems (i.e. for diagnostic assays or cell-based drugs), “rewriting” and thereby 
optimizing biological system for almost any imaginable purpose, or making the design and construction of 
engineered biological systems easier14. Common to these perspectives is that genetic circuits are at the 
core of synthetic biology. The first genetic circuits engineered to carry out a specific function were 
developed in early 2000 and included a genetic toggle switch15 (cells could toggle between two genetic 
states based on an external input signal) and an oscillating system termed the repressilator16. Today, more 
advanced circuits have been created, including all elementary logic gates17 and a genetic counter18. Such 
genetic circuits have been used to engineer cells to form patterns19,20, detect edges21 and move towards 
specific chemicals22,23 as a few examples. 
Another aspect of synthetic biology is to rewire biological entities for therapeutic uses by making them 
sense and respond to diseases. In an early case, E. coli was engineered to invade various cancer cells in vitro 
by sensing cell densities or hypoxic conditions24. Combined with proteins able to rewire cells25, this can 
prove a promising strategy for treating diseases. Another approach to treat cancer using synthetic biology 
involves a genetic circuit that senses miRNAs to describe a specific cell state. This circuit can determine if a 
cell is of a specific cancer type and if so, trigger apoptosis26. Synthetic biology has also been used for small-
molecule responsive control of T-cell proliferation27 and lowering blood glucose levels in mice by

subcutaneous implantation of HEK293 cells engineered for light-inducible glucagon-like peptide-1 
secretion28. Cell therapies have also been developed against bacterial pathogens such as Pseudomonas 
aeruginose29 and Vibrio cholerae30. 
1.2 Regulatory Elements in E. coli 
The central dogma of molecular biology describes the flow of genetic information in a biological system as 
it passes from DNA, through RNA and to proteins. This flow is central when engineering biology and some 
of the core elements regulating the flow of genetic information will be described below.  
1.2.1 Anatomy of Promoters and Ribosome Binding Sites 
The first step of the central dogma in molecular biology is transcription of DNA to RNA and is initiated from 
a promoter sequence. This sequence is typically 100 bp or less, surrounds the transcriptional start site 
(+1)31 and is recognized by a σ-factor. Although E. coli can express multiple σ-factors, each recognizing a 
different consensus promoter sequence, this paper will only focus on promoter sequences recognized by 
σ70 (RpoD), E. coli’s housekeeping σ-factor.  
By tuning the promoter region, especially the -10 and -35 regions, the affinity of the σ-factor for the 
promoter can be changed leading to differences in transcription rate32,33. The promoter may also contain an 
UP element located upstream of the -35 region34. The UP element can interact with the C-terminal domain 
of the α-subunit (αCTD) of RNA polymerase and increase basal transcription by 1.5-90-fold35.  
For transcription to initiate, a σ-factor needs to associate with the RNA polymerase forming the RNA 
polymerase holoenzyme (referred to here as RNAP), then bind a promoter region. Recognition of the 
promoter is facilitated by the σ4.2- and σ2.3/2.4-domain of the σ-factor which bind the -35 and -10 promoter 
elements, respectively36. Having recognized and bound these, the RNAP can form an open complex, melting 
the DNA from position -11 to +2 creating a “transcription bubble”37. This makes the AT-rich -10 element 
particular important as DNA melting and formation of the transcription bubble is paramount for 
transcription initiation. Transcription from σ70 promoters typically initiate with ATP or GTP and continues 
down either abortive or progressive paths36. The abortive path is a result of excess stress from expanding 
the transcription bubble caused by elongation of the growing RNA chain, and results in the release of short, 
nascent transcripts and a return of the RNAP to the open complex. The progressive path is similar however, 
when the growing RNA transcript reaches a critical length of ≈11 nucleotides the stress from the expanding 
transcription bubble is relieved by disruption of the contacts between the promoter DNA and the RNAP. 
Releasing the promoter allows the RNAP to change into an extremely stable and processive elongation 
complex that continues transcription down the template strand38. This complex may or may not retain the 
σ-factor36. Elongation continues until the RNA polymerase elongation complex reaches a terminator 
sequence characterized by the formation of a strong hairpin followed by a stretch of several U residues. 
The weak A:U interaction pauses transcription, allowing the RNA hairpin to form and ultimately releasing 
the newly synthesized RNA strand and the RNA polymerase38. 
Following transcription, the newly synthesized RNA strand may be translated, a complicated process 
involving multiple different proteins and RNAs. The mechanism of protein synthesis is not a focus of this 
thesis and will not be covered. Instead, I will briefly mention a few of the core RNA elements important in 
translation. 
The location and rate of translation initiation in prokaryotes is determined by the Shine-Dalgarno sequence 
(SD) and the presence of a nearby start codon, typically located 5-13 nucleotides downstream of the SD39,40. 
==PAGE:8==
5 
 
The SD sequence shows complementarity to the 3’-end of 16S rRNA, although the SD consensus sequence 
may change as a consequence of differences in spacing between the SD and the start codon40. The 16S 
rRNA is part of the 30S subunit of the ribosome which, along with other factors, bind the mRNA initially and 
enables assembly of the entire ribosome41. Deviations from the 16S rRNA consensus sequence may 
therefore decrease translation efficiency as 16S rRNA binding becomes less favorable39. Translation 
initiation is also affected by RNA secondary structure which can prevent effective translation or occlude 
either the SD or the standby site41–44. Variations in the 5’ untranslated region (5’-UTR) may therefore greatly 
affect translation42,45,46. The region upstream of the initiation codon will be referred to as the ribosome 
binding site (RBS), and is defined by the DNA/RNA sequence containing the information affecting ribosome 
binding. 
Promoters and RBSs constitute the core regulatory elements necessary for carrying information from DNA 
to protein. However, this only allows for constitutive expression at a defined expression level. To respond 
to external and internal signals, cells have developed additional elements that ultimately enable dynamic 
adjustments of the type and amount of proteins expressed in a cell at any given time. This often involves 
transcription factors (TFs): DNA binding proteins that can regulate the expression from a promoter. 
1.2.2 Transcription Factors  
Prokaryotic TFs can be divided into two types: repressors and activators, which either decrease or increase 
gene expression, respectively. This is facilitated by the protein binding to DNA in regions known as operator 
sites, where they can affect the RNAP. These sites are typically located in close proximity to, or even 
overlapping the promoter, where it ideally positions the TF to interact with the RNAP. Operator sites 
generally differ between TFs which is crucial for orthogonal gene regulation as different operators can 
recruit different TFs.  
For repressors, the operator site and thus their binding to DNA, often occurs between the -35 and +1 
region, where they sterically hinder interactions between the σ-factor and the promoter. Two other 
mechanisms for repression includes preventing formation of the open complex necessary for transcription 
initiation, or by preventing the RNAP from clearing the promoter (either by binding in the upstream region 
anchoring the RNAP, or binding downstream of the promoter blocking its movement)47. The exact 
mechanism of repression depends on the repressor, although all have the potential for steric hindrance. 
Auxiliary and weaker operator sites may also increase repression by weakly binding the repressor in close 
proximity to the stronger operator site thereby increasing its local concentration47.  
Activators typically bind between -30 and -80 where they can interact with the RNAP without preventing its 
binding to the promoter48. In principle, there are two ways in which activation can occur: 1) by altering the 
conformation of the promoter and improve its quality, or 2) interacting with the RNAP to compensate for a 
low-quality promoter49. The effect of activation is often stabilization of the promoter-DNA complex or 
acceleration of the transition of the RNAP-promoter into the open complex49; the latter being a rate-
limiting step in transcription36 and the mechanism with which cI from the λ phage increases transcription49. 
Nonetheless, simply recruiting the RNAP to the promoter may be sufficient for activation, and extensive 
conformational changes to the RNAP induced by the activator may be unnecessary49. 
The exact interaction between the activator and the RNAP can have important consequences for the 
operator location. For instance, the αCTD subunit is joined to the RNAP through a flexible linker. Activators 
binding to the αCTD are therefore allowed flexible positioning relative to the RNAP. The opposite is true for 
==PAGE:9==
6 
 
activators that interact with the σ4-domain of the RNAP such as cI50–52. This interaction requires careful 
positioning and binding to the same face of the DNA as the σ4-domain49 as the positioning of σ4 with 
respect to the rest of the RNAP is critical and with little room for flexibility32. 
Activators also hold the potential for repression: In a study by Lee et al., they fused the cyclic AMP receptor 
protein (CRP) to zinc-finger proteins (ZFP) which bound either upstream (-67 to -50) or downstream (+24 to 
+41) of the Ptac promoter48. When binding upstream, the ZFP-CRP fusion acted as an activator, but when 
bound downstream, it acted as a repressor. 
The most common TFs used today originate from natural sequences, and are often subjects of protein 
engineering in attempts to have them sense and respond to compounds of i.e. medical, industrial or 
environmental interest. Moreover, since many TFs are not orthogonal to the organism in which it is being 
expressed (i.e. LacI expressed in E. coli), evolving and changing the DNA binding is also an attractive area of 
research. This is not easily done as the TFs rarely have a modular design allowing for easy modification of 
their DNA-binding region53. An exception to this are zinc-fingers and TALEs (transcription activator-like 
effectors)54–56. The CRISPR-Cas9 system has also been used to control transcription and enables almost any 
DNA sequence to be targeted with little to no need for protein engineering57.  
A lot of work has gone into evolving and designing TFs, but little has gone into engineering operator binding 
sites for tuning genetic circuits; often, the consensus sequence or promoters with a known binding site are 
used. Changing the operator site location, number and strength holds the potential to change the dynamic 
and sensitivity of promoters towards the TF without the need for complicated and laborious protein 
engineering.  
1.3 Characterizing Genetic Circuits 
Approaches to engineer genetic circuits with a desired systems-level function can roughly be divided into 
three different approaches: 1) using known regulatory elements for which there are quantitative 
descriptions available, 2) using computational tools to design or select the necessary elements (DNA 
sequences) for a defined output, and 3) using libraries of relevant DNA elements to screen for the desired 
output.   
The first approach is the most widely used and is a cornerstone of synthetic biology. It attempts to provide 
quantitative descriptions of genetic elements under various, well-defined conditions58. This approach relies 
on a high and predictable degree of modularity for each element, allowing for “technical datasheets” for 
each part as is custom from other engineering disciplines (i.e. electrical, mechanical and structural 
engineering)58. This approach is limited by the number of well-characterized biological components, the 
requirement to extend biological standards for different elements and the concern about modularity in 
biology59. The latter can be particularly challenging: Promoters are affected by the surrounding genetic 
context which may cause changes in transcription rates60,61. Using pre-defined DNA sequences as insulator 
elements, this variability can be reduced61. As the 5’-UTR can affect translation, different approaches have 
successfully helped improve RBS modularity, often by cleaving this sequence from the transcribed mRNA 
allowing for more accurate quantitative models62,63. A statistical approach has also been taken, where a 
reliability score is used to identify part robustness across varying genetic context64. Despite challenges, 
“plug-and-play” biology remains an attractive area by promising easier, faster and better construction of 
biological systems14.   
==PAGE:10==
7 
 
The second approach is based on computational design of regulatory elements and attempts to relate 
sequence with function using biophysical models. As an in silico approach, it holds the promise of high 
speed and incredible low cost in designing sequences with a desired output. Nonetheless, the reliability of 
these models is only as reliable as the data used to build them: Studies to build biophysical models examine 
only a tiny amount of the vast sequence space, and are often confined to small sequence changes. This 
limits their potential to reliably predict the output of sequences as their homology to the training set 
decreases. Biophysical models has been built describing both promoters65,66 and RBSs46,67. Another problem 
with these models arises from genetic context. As previously discussed, the genetic context in which a 
regulatory element is placed can significantly alter its output. Most biophysical models fails to take this into 
account which can diminish their reliability or demand additional design considerations (such as the use of 
insulators68 or the abovementioned reliability score). 
The third method can be considered an extension of the first. It aims at using preexisting regulatory 
elements, but rather than relying on quantitative descriptions and modular behavior, it utilizes the strength 
of DNA synthesis and high-throughput techniques for multiplex characterization of a library of regulatory 
elements. For instance, by synthesizing all possible combinations of 114 promoters and 111 RBSs Kosuri et 
al. could quantify the transcription and translation levels of ≈12,653 different elements45. This was possible 
by using Fluorescence Assisted Cell Sorting (FACS) to sort the regulatory elements based on fluorescence. 
Transcription and translation from each oligonucleotide (oligo) could then be estimated by next-generation 
sequencing (NGS) of each bin. It allowed for multiplex and high-throughput characterization of thousands 
of regulatory elements without the need to consider modularity of the assembled circuit, and provided 
insight into the mechanics of transcription and translation. A similar method was used in yeast to measure 
the resulting expression from a promoter library designed to systematically probe the effect of changes in 
location, number, orientation, affinity and organization of TF binding sites and nucleosome disfavoring 
sequences69. 
Although a library-approach can prove powerful for characterizing regulatory elements, it is limited by the 
length of oligos that can be accurately synthesized. This is a significant limitation when it comes to testing 
larger circuits, but may be alleviated by synthesizing smaller DNA sequences and cloning them into multiple 
different locations. Such an approach has been used on a small-scale library consisting of various 
promoters, RBSs and terminators to improve the production of lycopene70.  
1.4 De novo DNA Synthesis 
As the carrier of genetic information, DNA is at the core of synthetic biology. The DNA used for the various 
purposes of synthetic biology – or almost any other biological discipline – are typically copied from 
metagenomic libraries, cancer cell lines, viruses, bacteriophages, etc. However, copying DNA only allows us 
to look at a very narrow window of the sequence landscape, and typically only sequences selected for by 
evolution (Figure 1A). Even using various techniques for directed evolution of DNA such as error-prone PCR, 
we are still left with an undirected approach that requires luck, patience and countless hours of lab-work 
for a thorough exploration of sequence space. In synthetic biology, the work is often performed at low 
throughput and only the smallest fraction of the mutational landscape is explored (Figure 1B). DNA 
synthesis allows for a targeted approach to test a given hypothesis and can be used for a systematic search 
of the mutational landscape (Figure 1C). This search is of course limited by the sequence space and can 
quickly make even DNA synthesis unfeasible for a fully exhaustive search (for a stretch of 30 nucleotides, 
there are 430≈1.15×1018 different combinations). Nonetheless, DNA synthesis allows us to guide our search 
in sequence space, looking only at the sequences we deem most relevant.  
==PAGE:11==
8 
 
Today, DNA synthesis has been used for a variety of purposes in synthetic biology. For instance, Goodman 
et al. showed how DNA synthesis can be used to understand how N-terminal codon bias affects protein 
expression43. Both bacterial regulatory elements and yeast promoters have also been systematically 
explored using synthesized DNA sequences to control expression of a reporter gene45,69. On a genome 
scale, DNA synthesis has been used to create genome-wide knockout or overexpression libraries to select 
for traits of interest71. Moreover, it has been used to synthesize genes to probe the limitations of genetic 
recoding in E. coli72.  
Among the biggest challenges for DNA synthesis is the length and cost of high-throughput oligonucleotide 
synthesis. Although it is possible to synthesize genes and even entire chromosomes, they often involve 
multiple steps for assembly, and are not done at a high-throughput scale (likely due to the cost)73–76. It can 
therefore be expected that technological improvements and reduced cost of DNA synthesis is likely to have 
a drastic impact on biology, as testing hypotheses will be as simple as the design and analyses allow them 
to be73. Synthesizing larger and cheaper constructs will not only allow for careful dissection of the function 
of larger genetic elements, but also increase the library size and thus the scope of experiments.  
1.5 Aim of Study 
This study aims to demonstrate the extent at which FlowSeq can be used for multiplex characterization of 
genetic circuits. A method that combines the high throughput of FACS to measure a fluorescent output 
with the power of NGS to identify the sorted cells. We hope this can be used to increase both development 
speed and circuit quality when engineering genetic circuits, i.e. for the applications of synthetic biology and 
metabolic engineering. FlowSeq has previously been applied to the study the composability of regulatory 
elements as described above45. This provided a model that accurately predicted RNA levels and moderately 
predicted protein levels. However, unexpected levels of protein and RNA could deviate greatly from the 
model, illustrating a limitation of a predictive approach for circuit design. Therefore, FlowSeq provides a 
valuable alternative for precise tuning of protein expression in lieu of standardized elements and prediction 
algorithms. 
With the previous success of FlowSeq, we try to expand the applications of this technique. Using a synthetic 
library of constructs, we explore the dynamics of genetic circuits. For this, we synthesize a library of DNA 
oligos, each containing two regulatory elements separated by a terminator (Figure 2). On each oligo, the 
regulatory elements are varied by changing the promoter, RBS and operator number, location and strength. 
The oligo library is cloned into a plasmid where one regulatory element controls TF expression while the 
other controls superfolder GFP77 (sfGFP) expression. Expression of the TF may affect sfGFP expression and 
its own expression by binding to possible operator sites located on one or both regulatory elements leading

2. RESULTS & DISCUSSION 
2.1 Promuter as a Tool for Oligo Design 
The oligos synthesized by Agilent Technologies were designed using Promuter, a Python-based software 
package written by Daniel B. Goodman (unpublished results). Using this software, we first defined multiple 
sets of design constraints, each predicted to yield a different expression phenotype. This involved varying 
promoter strength, RBS strength, and the number, location and strength of operator sites (Figure 3.1).  
 
Next, Promuter translates these constraint sets into a mutational landscape by predicting the linearly 
independent effect of each single base pair change on each constraint (Figure 3.2). When introducing a TF 
binding site, it uses a position weight matrix of the TF binding motif to estimate the effect of each mutation 
to calculate an operator strength. A promoter model is used to estimate effects of base changes in the 
promoter (due to placement of an operator site in the illustrated case, Figure 3). The model is based on 
that of Shultzaberger et al. which similarly calculates a score based on a position weight matrix of the -35 
Figure 3: Workflow of Promuter. 1) Constraints are identified; in this case the removal of a TetR operator and addition of a LacI 
operator. Position weight matrices provide the information content of bases and positions and are used as proxies for the 
strength of TF binding or promoter strength. 2) A mutational landscape is created based on the constraints, identifying the effect 
of all possible base-changes at all possible locations. 3) Mutations sets are created from the landscape and ordered by their 
priority. 4) The mutations are applied to the sequence and the constraints are tested until a sequence is found that satisfies
them.  
==PAGE:15==
12 
 
and -10 half-sites81. Next, Promuter generates a priority queue of sets of increasing numbers of mutations 
generated from the highest points in the mutational landscape (Figure 3.3). It applies the mutation set from 
the top of the queue, tests if the criteria are met, and repeats this until it succeeds. Once complete, it 
returns the sequence that best satisfied the constraints (such as “place operator between -10 and -35, 
reduce operator strength to x% of consensus sequence; keep promoter expression constant”) (Figure 3.4). 
With this software, we designed 135,016 different oligos. Oligos are divided into 14 different sets; 13 sets 
for the different TFs and one control set used with a plasmid that contains no TF. Each set varies in the 
number of oligos and uses an operator sequence matching the TF the set is designed for. 
2.1.1 Oligo Design 
The oligos we designed all follow a well-defined pattern that can be split into three main components: A 
left sequence, a right sequence and a spacer to separate them (230 bp total). The left sequence controls TF 
expression while the right sequence controls expression of sfGFP (Figure 4). The two sequences are 
separated by a hairpin structure (terminator) which has a 4 nucleotide barcode in the loop. Because we do 
not sequence the TF region itself, this barcode is used to identify the TF for which each oligo is designed. An 
overview of the different oligo types are given in Figure 4 and all combinations of left and right sequence 
are summarized in Table 2. 
The promoter and RBS are varied on the left sequence: three different RBSs and four different promoters, 
ranging from weak to strong, are paired in all possible combinations for each right sequence. Moreover, a 
blank RBS and blank promoter combination is included (“blank” implying that no translation and 
transcription, respectively, should occur).  
For the right sequence, three “base” promoters are used (low, medium and high strength; base implying 
there are no operator) and mutations are applied to these to generate new promoters with operators. 
Similarly to the left sequences, a blank RBS and blank promoter are also included and the blank promoter is 
in some cases paired with the working RBS. Each set also contains right sequences with the cannonical 
promoter for the relevant transcription factor. The cannonical promoter is a naturally occurring promoter 
the TF is known to regulate; the zinc-finger proteins (ZFP1, 2 and 4) are the exception as they use the 
promoter designed to have them evolve towards a specific DNA motif54. 
To estimate the strength of the left promoter, we use a plasmid with no TF. In this control set, for each left 
sequence in the other 13 oligo sets, the left sequence switch place with a blank right sequence. Moreover, 
the first 23 amino acids of the appropriate TF are placed at the 3’-end of the oligo (in extension of the left 
sequence which is now placed on the right arm) and fused in front of sfGFP when cloned. This N-terminal 
fusion is used to mimic the TF’s codon bias when expressing sfGFP, as this can have a significant impact on 
expression43. We expect this to provide a good estimate of protein expression from the left sequence. 
We examine the effect of operator site location by placing them at various locations: upstream (of the -35), 
downstream (of the -10) or inside (between -10 and -35; termed “repress”). There may be up to two 
operator sites, but placed at different locations (upstream, repress or downstream). Moreover, we vary 
their strength by changing the sequence away from consensus (Figure 2B). This is done for both the right 
and left sequence potentially building feed-forward/feedback loops (termed “Feedback” when an operator 
is placed on the left sequence).

For each TF, oligos are also designed to investigate the importance of each base in a promoter by 
synthesizing all possible single base mutations (SBPs) in a 45 bp window (Figure 4, Single-basepair-mutant). 
This is done for the three right promoters and the cannonical promoter. We pair these with two different, 
blank left promoters and three different RBSs generating a total of 3240 unique oligos.  
At the 3’ and 5’ end of the oligos are primer binding sites and BsaI cut sites. The primer binding sites are 
designed such that each library set (each designed for a different TF) is amplified by a separate primer set. 
The BsaI cut sites are designed such that it generates unique overhangs at each cut, preventing the oligo

and were uniquely designed with a hamming distance of at least 3. We also ensured they were amplified by 
the correct primer set and that the control carried the first 23 amino acids of the TF in the correct reading 
frame.

2.2 Library Construction and Cloning 
Each library set was cloned into a modified version of pGERC45 carrying the appropriate TF (Supplemental 
Figure 1 and Table 1); or none in the case of the control. The cloned oligo is orientated such that the right 
sequence controls sfGFP expression and the left sequence controls one of the 13 different TFs. The plasmid 
also expresses mCherry82 from the constitutive promoter PLtetO-1
83 to act as a control for intrinsic noise. We 
first transformed the library into NEB Turbo electrocompetent E. coli to maximize transformation efficiency. 
Plasmids were then extracted and transformed into ECdbg3. ECdbg3 was chosen as it does not express any 
of the TFs we use, nor does it produce proteins known to interfere with them.  
To successfully explore the designed library, we required multiple detections of each unique oligo when 
sequencing. Each transformation is therefore a potential bottleneck as we risk losing unique constructs or 
introduce bias. The first transformation into electrocompetent NEB Turbo E. coli is particularly important as 
we need the entire library set cloned with a minimum of bias. Any bias introduced here will only be 
amplified further downstream in the workflow. As we have to prepare the library sets individually prior to 
FACS, we carefully designed and optimized the workflow to ensure sufficient library coverage with a 
minimum of bias. By minimizing the number of cycles used to amplify the library and terminating the PCR 
immediately once we no longer observed exponential amplification, we neutralized a major source of initial 
bias in our method.  
Each library set holds between 7,423 and 12,460 unique oligos with ≈20-25% expected to be synthesized in 
full length and with the correct sequence. We estimated this by sequencing individual clones after the first 
transformation into NEB Turbo cells (data not show). Both cloning steps used in our method were done 
with a minimum of 250-fold coverage to increase the chance each correctly synthesized oligo is observed at 
least once and accounting for the large fraction of incorrect sequences. The NEB Turbo cells were grown in 
a large volume of LB with kanamycin selection to ensure we kept cells in the exponential phase when 
isolating plasmid. For transformation into ECdbg3, we recovered cells on large LB plates with kanamycin 
selection under the hypothesis that it would minimize growth bias between oligos.  
2.3 Library Growth and FlowSeq 
We inoculated cultures, each expressing a library set, and grew them separately O/N in preparation for 
FACS. We performed three different FlowSeq experiments over a total of 4 days: an initial FlowSeq run on 
the LacI library only (LacI-FS) to investigate and troubleshoot the experimental setup; two independent 
FlowSeq runs with all libraries (Full-FS); and a FlowSeq run with the inducible TFs only (TetR, LacI, AcuR, 
CdaR and TtgR; referred to as Inducible-FS). When doing the Full-FS and Inducible-FS, we grew cultures 
separately (with or without inducer depending on the experiment) to avoid growth biases between 
libraries. Two independent sorts were done for the Full-FS to investigate reproducibility. For the Inducible-
FS, we chose four different concentrations (Table 5) which we expect to cover the full dynamic range of 
each TF84 (Srivatsan Raman, personal communications). For acrylate (AcuR) and naringenin (TtgR) we were 
limited in our highest inducer concentration by their toxicity (both) and solubility (naringenin). We grew 
and sorted two inducer concentrations per day for the Inducible-FS due to the time it took to sort cells. 
Once the libraries reached early log-phase, we pooled them at an equal cell ratio and halted growth by 
chilling them on ice immediately, keeping them there until all bins had been sorted. For the Inducible-FS, 
we only pooled libraries with the same concentration number. The pooled libraries were subjected to FACS 
as the first step in FlowSeq and sorted into 12 log-spaced bins. The number of cells sorted into each bin 
(Table 3) reflects the distribution of cells for the entire library sorted. After sorting, we grew each bin at

2.4.1 Elucidating Promoter Structure 
The LacI library holds a large number of oligos with a blank regulatory element cloned in front of the TF, 
which allows us to investigate promoter structure, as we can assume unregulated expression. These oligos 
are also ideal for modeling promoter expression. Since we keep the RBS on the right sequence constant, we 
assume only the promoter contribute to variations in protein expression.   
2.4.1.1 Promuter as a Tool for Predicting Promoter Strength 
Promuter was used to predict the effects of base-changes in the regulatory sequence, providing parameters 
based on the sequence that can be used to estimate the effect. These parameters are used in conjunction 
with a linear model to estimate the strength of the promoter. The parameters fed to the model holds 
scores for the -10, extended -10 and -35 region; the spacer, the GC-content of the promoter, the 
discriminator element87,88, the presence of UP-elements34 and the expected strength of RNA polymerase 
binding.  
We attempt to predict the log10 of the measured protein (log10[pi]) using either the prediction generated 
by Promuter (Figure 6, top) or a new linear model based on Promuter-generated scores (see section 5.3.4; 
Figure 6, bottom). However, it is evident the model performs poorly, indicating a significant need for 
significant refinement.

A striking result from this experiment is the ease with which we can identify the -35 and -10 region. The 
consensus sequences for these regions are TTGACA and TATAAT, respectively89(Figure 7, letters on x-axis 
highlighted in red). Changes away from consensus have an obvious detrimental effect and can almost 
completely abolish protein expression. For instance, in the R0 promoter, protein expression is reduced 38-
fold by changing the last T in the -10 to a C (TATAGT → TATAGC, mutation is underlined). This is not 
surprising as it is a particularly conserved site81.  
The R0 promoter has the -35 TTTACG and are two mutations away from consensus (underlined). Changing 
these back individually increases protein expression by ≈2.5-6-fold. The -10 is one base away from 
consensus, and mutating it to the consensus A will at least double expression; a response we would expect. 
Surprisingly, changing it to a C provides an almost equally fold-change and in some cases even higher 
although it still reflects the sequence logo, albeit at a less conserved position81. The results also find that 
the first base upstream of the -35 is important for determining promoter strength. More interesting is the 
benefit of having T’s in the downstream region of the spacer for R0. This seems to be a more unique 
feature to the R0 promoter and may correlate with the higher GC-content in the spacer of this promoter. 
Especially as the R1 promoter seems to benefit more from G’s in the upstream vicinity of the -10. R2, the 
strongest of the three promoters, has a GC-content in between R0 and R1. The GC-content is important in 
determining the melting temperature of DNA and may therefore be important in easing formation of the 
transcription bubble. Unfortunately, these experiments cannot conclude between the hypotheses that GC-
content is responsible for the observed effect versus a transcriptionally more favorable interaction 
between the promoter and RNAP, such as a stronger extended -10.

For the R1 and R2 promoter, it is again obvious that mutating the -35 and -10 away from consensus results 
in drastically reduced transcription. For R1 as for R0, the base immediately upstream of the -35 is important 
for transcription, but is not so for R2. Close to the middle of the spacer in R1, T seems to greatly increase

phenomenon and we will benefit from more read counts as we sequence the Full-FS. This particular data 
point only holds 48 reads divided between adjacent bins.  
Certain locations in the -35 and -10 of the R2 promoter show more robustness against mutations. This 
implies that these regions are not entirely dominating in deciding promoter strength, and binding of the 
promoter to the σ-factor is likely to involve the combined effort of the entire promoter sequence. Although 
the first base in the -10 is highly conserved81, it appears to be of little importance in determining promoter 
strength of the R2 promoter. Overall, the R2 promoter shows more variance in the importance of each 
position in the -35 and -10 region compared to the R0 and R1 promoters, where each base are closer to be 
of equally high importance.  
In the absence of a -35, an extended -10 region has been shown to enable transcription90,91.  The extended -
10 of R0 has the sequence AGGTATAGT where the extension is shown in bold and the -10 is underlined. The 
R0 promoter benefits by an A→T mutation in accordance with the extended -10 consensus sequence 
(Figure 8). For the R1 promoter, the sequence is ACGCATAAT, but expression increases more moderately; 
for the first base (A), it increases only when mutated to a G, and for the second base (C) when mutated to G 
or, to a lesser extent, T. In most cases, the second base has the greatest impact on expression levels in 
accordance with the consensus sequence81. For the R2 promoter, there is no obvious extended -10, and 
mutations towards the extended -10 consensus sequence appears to have negative or no effect. As the 
extended -10 was proposed to enable transcription in the absence of a -35, it is likely its function is 
undetectable in the presence of a strong -35, and the mutations will only interfere with the already 
optimized promoter-RNAP interaction.  
One of the most obvious differences between R2 and the weaker promoters R0 and R1 is the difficulty by 
which it can be improved. Few changes elicit an increase in expression, and when they do, it is minor (<1.2-
fold) compared to an almost 6-fold change in expression for a single mutation in R0. The R2 promoter also 
seems harder to break than R0 and R1. As the -10 and -35 region interacts with conserved domains in the 
σ-factor32, it is no surprise these positions remain the most important in determining promoter strength, 
yet their effect is more varied in the R2 promoter compared to R0/R1. Although the R2 promoter still 
requires the transcription bubble to form and the RNAP to bind with the correct positioning it appears the 
promoter is of a higher quality which greatly improves robustness.  
Overall, by synthesizing all single base mutants, we effectively uncover the importance of each base in the 
promoter. Although single mutations can help shed light on the importance of individual bases, it is unlikely 
they will reveal the full story. It is almost certain that combinations of mutations interact in an 
unpredictable manner the SBPs do not capture. Synthesizing rationally designed constructs can help guide 
hypotheses and answer these questions.

2.4.1.3 An Additive Model Based on Single-Base Contributions to Promoter Strength 
With the data from the LacI-FS, we try to predict a protein score,   , for all oligos that have a blank left 
sequence, excluding those of the type SBP, using an additive model (see section 5.3.5). All of these 
sequences are based on one of the three right promoters, R0-2, and contains various numbers of mutations 
due to the placement of operator sites. We expect several but not all mutations to be in the 45 bp window 
the SBPs cover. In this model, we choose to ignore the contribution of mutations outside this window as we 
lack data to quantify their effect.  
Figure 9 shows the prediction using an additive model while highlighting oligos based on the number of 
mutations outside the 45 bp window covered by the SBPs (top) or the total mutations (bottom). Again, it is 
obvious the model fails to predict the majority of constructs. However, we are left with some clues as to 
why: Figure 9 (top) highlights that our prediction generally performs better if we can use the SBP data to 
model all the mutations. As the number of mutations outside the SBP window increases, our prediction 
performs worse. Nonetheless, in several cases, the prediction performs poorly even when all mutations are 
within the SBP range of mutations. Figure 9 (bottom) and Supplemental Figure 3 provides an explanation to 
this, as they highlight how an increase in the number of mutations reduces prediction accuracy. In the vast 
majority of cases, the protein level is overestimated. If we look at the predictions for promoters R0 and R1, 
the log10(pi) tends to drop to ≈2. This is close to the minimum we can measure, indicating we likely break 
the promoter in these cases. Even though most single mutations have little effect (refer Figure 7), there is 
clearly interactions between them which can completely abolish transcription. This is also the case even 
when the mutations are beneficial and the prediction increases promoter strength above the reference.  
Another observation is the effort it takes to break the promoter. At log10(pi) ≤ 2, we are close to the 
minimum detectable range of FlowSeq, as it becomes harder to distinguish sfGFP fluorescence from 
background fluorescence. Although promoters may still have a minimum amount of transcription, we will 
consider them broken at this low level of protein expression. Supplemental Figure 4 shows that R2 is less 
likely to break completely compared to R0 and R1 in the sense that fewer oligos have log10(pi) ≤ 2. Part of 
the reason is likely due to the much higher “quality” of the promoter compared with the others. We expect 
a strong unregulated promoter to have all aspects of transcription optimized, such as recognition and 
binding of the RNAP, formation of the open complex and a quick progression from open complex to the

2.4.2 Effects of Tuning Operator Location and Strength 
For all libraries, we vary the location and strength of the operator site to examine the effect it has on 
repression/activation. Activators can be made to serve a dual purpose as both repressors and activators, 
exemplified by the cI repressor from bacteriophage λ which either repress or activate depending on its 
position relative to the RNAP92,93. LacI is thought to act as a repressor by competing with the RNAP for 
binding to the promoter and thereby blocking transcription94. With this simple mechanism, we expect LacI 
repression to be conserved when sufficiently strong operator sites are placed at the Repress or 
Downstream position (i.e. any location downstream of the -35) or close enough upstream of the -35 to 
influence RNAP binding.  
Although we do not have direct measurements of the strength of the left regulatory elements and the 
amount of TF they express, we can infer them based on the changes a left sequence impose on all the right 
sequences it is paired with, and rank them accordingly. The fold-change in protein expression yields an 
arbitrary number for TF expression.  
When varying the operator strength, location and number, we get a mix of results (Figure 10). Looking only 
at instances where we have at least 3-fold repression, we find that all right sequence types are present 
except “Upstream + Downstream”. Since LacI depends on steric hindrance for repression, this is not 
particularly surprising as these positions are the least optimal (the type “Repress” where the operator is 
between -10 and -35 is, theoretically, most optimal for this). By looking at the slope of the linear regression 
(Figure 11 and Supplemental Table 2) we can compare operator-variations more quantitatively. Even in the 
presence of a predicted strong operator binding site, LacI repression is relatively weak at best when placed 
upstream of the -35. The same is the case when it is placed downstream of the -10, indicating that the 
RNAP can relatively easily transcribe through the operator-bound enzyme. In cases where we see 
repression, the operator is typically predicted to be strong in accordance with steric hindrance requiring 
LacI to effectively compete with RNAP for binding in the promoter region.  
As we would expect, placing the operator between the -35 and -10 results in very efficient repression, 
especially when using the consensus sequence95 5’-TGTGAGCG-3’ (only one half-site present in the 
promoter). The efficiency of the cannonical promoter in enabling repression is exemplified by the SBPs 
derived from it.  These show that LacI may still be present in the cell, possibly due to the blank sequence 
allowing for trace amounts of transcription (Supplemental Figure 5, compare Figure 7). We base this 
hypothesis on the observation that changing RBS strength affects LacI sensitivity; a stronger RBS makes the 
promoter more robust towards changes in the LacI operator, indicating an increased LacI concentration 
neutralize the effect of a weaker operator. Our hypothesis is strengthened by the lack of changes in protein 
expression when mutating the -10 and -35 leading us to conclude that the promoter does not respond to 
the RNAP. It also appears that the B0 promoter more efficiently prevents lacI transcription than L2. These 
observations points towards LacI being expressed from the plasmid rather than the genome (which we 
expect to have a lacI deletion).

the -35 and -10, and benefits from a second operator either upstream of the -35 or downstream of the -10 
in accordance with previous observations31. We observe a maximum of 46-fold repression, although the 
vast majority of constructs shows little response towards LacI (Figure 12). We also find that constructs with 
strongly transcribed promoters correlate well with greater repression as has previously been found31. This 
only applies when the right sequence is responsive to LacI. These observations are well in accordance with 
a previous study where it was concluded that repression is determined by the rate of complex formation of 
the competing LacI-operator and RNAP-promoter systems and the rate in which the RNAP leaves the 
promoter96. It was also found that the location of the operator within the promoter region could change 
repression by up to 70-fold, supporting our observations, albeit at a higher level of repression. 
Although the results discussed so far are only preliminary without the control to more accurately quantify 
TF expression, they show how FlowSeq can be used to probe the rules governing operators and genetic 
circuits. Moreover, the SBPs derived from the cannonical promoter hints at LacI having a very high affinity 
for its operator (assumed it is only expressed at trace amounts as we would expect from a promoter that is 
a random sequence assumed to have no transcription!).  
LacI can be induced by IPTG causing LacI to lose its affinity for the operator. We do not yet have data from 
the Inducible-FS, but those will allow us to answer questions such as whether operator strength can be 
used to adjust sensitivity and dynamic range of promoters. It will also confirm our suspicion that LacI 
represses the SBPs derived from the cannonical promoter as it will be affected by adding IPTG.

3. PERSPECTIVES 
3.1 Uncovering Promoter Architecture 
The SBPs allowed us to scratch the surface of the rules governing promoter sequences, although they 
warrant further studies. We have tried two simple models, using either Promuter or an additive model; the 
latter sums the contribution of all mutations in each sequence. Although our analysis is mostly exploratory, 
we find that the SBP data may prove useful in elucidating the rules governing promoter design. Among the 
biggest flaws in the additive model is its failure to take into account complex interactions between 
mutations, especially those causing the promoter to break. Applying machine learning algorithms may 
provide a method to capture patterns in the data resulting from combinations of specific mutations and 
their respective fluorescent output. This has been successfully applied to predict the sequence specificities 
of DNA- and RNA-binding proteins97; a task similar to ours, but with the addition of quantitative data. 
Moreover, the oligos we test our prediction on contains mutations outside the limited area covered by the 
SBPs. As we sequence the Full-FS, we acquire a larger test set where all mutations are restricted to the 
region covered by SBPs. This will allow us a more appropriate evaluation of the additive model, although a 
more dedicated effort at dissecting the promoter may be necessary for accurate modeling. One obvious 
step would be to synthesize all possible two-nucleotide mutations. 
Our data provides an initial attempt at understanding promoter structure, however the dataset cannot be 
considered complete. For instance, we only look at three different promoters, a significant limitation in our 
training set. We also lack variations in spacer length. The spacer can have a large impact on transcription, 
and we will need quantitative measurements of this, i.e. by expanding the SBPs to include insertions and 
deletions, as well as a larger variety of promoters with different spacer lengths.  
An interesting application for the SBPs is tuning of natural promoters in E. coli. We find that the normalized 
effect of mutations is relatively constant across our three promoters. If this holds true for most natural 
promoters, we may be able to predictively modify their relative expression by introducing very few 
mutations. A possible task even on the genome scale with the advent of MAGE (multiplex automated 
genome engineering) for genome engineering in E. coli98,99. 
Aside from promoter strength, a promoter may also be more or less noisy in its expression, causing 
different levels of agreement between cell-cell fluorescence. Our dataset allows us to measure this noise as 
the distribution of sequence counts across the bins we sort into. As different promoters may allow for the 
same expression level45, noise may be an interesting parameter when making a choice about which 
promoter to use for a given construct. We may find that different circuits exhibit different degrees of noise, 
providing another important aspect of circuit design. 
3.2 Operator Effects on Protein Expression 
The control library, where the left sequence of each oligo is switched so it drives sfGFP expression instead, 
allows a more direct quantification of TF expression level. This has the potential to improve our analysis on 
TF repression conducted above, and allow us to look at the feed-forward/feedback circuits too. Moreover, 
with the Full-FS we will also have access to information on activators. The most interesting results to wait 
for however, are the results from the Inducible-FS: with that, we will examine if FlowSeq can be used to 
examine response curves for the TFs. We chose the inducer concentrations to cover the entire response 
curve of the inducible TFs84 (Srivatsan Raman, personal communications), enabling us to detect if a given 
construct is more or less sensitive towards inducer concentrations.  
==PAGE:30==
27 
 
An obvious experiment we have not performed is dynamic measurements of induction: In the LacI-FS and 
Full-FS, we expect protein expression to have reached steady-state. We test this hypothesis indirectly by 
performing two independent Full-FS runs to examine reproducibility, assuming we are most likely to 
observe this at steady-state protein levels. FlowSeq has already been shown to produce reproducible 
data45, yet we consider it a necessary control. For the Inducible-FS, we allow cells to grow for exactly 
5h20min in the presence of inducer. This should allow us to reach maximum induction in most cases84. 
However, we are unable to answer if FlowSeq can be used for multiplex dynamic measurements of induced 
constructs. There are two major reasons we did not perform the experiment necessary for this: 1) We do 
not know the most interesting inducer concentrations to perform this experiment at. The Inducible-FS will 
hopefully give us data for that. And 2) we are limited by the cost of sequencing. As it is, we are already 
sequencing on a full HiSeq2500 v4 flow cell. Dynamic measurements would require sorting and barcoding 
cells at multiple time points; with just four time points, the experiment would be the same size as the 
Inducible-FS, which again is larger (in terms of both sequencing and total cells sorted) than the Full-FS.  
3.3 FlowSeq and DNA Synthesis as a Tool for Probing Genetic Circuits 
Our analysis so far, reveals that FlowSeq can be used for probing genetic circuits at their steady-state. It still 
remains to be seen if the method can be used for dynamic measurements of genetic circuits such as an 
oscillating system16. We believe this approach is useful as a research tool in synthetic biology to better 
understand the relationship between sequence and function. FlowSeq also holds great promise in areas 
such as metabolic engineering, where it can select the right promoter-RBS combination45, and type of 
genetic circuit for a fine-tuned protein response. 
One disadvantage of FlowSeq is its dynamic range: Weak promoters may be indistinguishable from 
background, requiring a protein that either fluoresces at a wavelength where the cells do not or is orders of 
magnitude brighter. In metabolic engineering, it may also be favorable to clone and express genes on the 
chromosome rather than a plasmid. Adapting FlowSeq to be located on the chromosome instead of a 
multicopy plasmid will cause difficulties when expressing sfGFP from a weak promoter as we expect a 
weaker fluorescent signal.  
As a last note, we expect the majority of synthesized sequences to contain one or more errors. Rather than 
discarding these sequences, we may choose to keep them assuming they have enough sequence reads to 
appear valid. Doing so will allow us to expand our dataset, albeit with sequences that do not systematically 
test our hypotheses. Nonetheless, they may, in our case, prove particularly useful for probing the structure-
function relationship of promoters, as we can easily modify our R code to accommodate them and increase 
our dataset.

4. CONCLUSION 
In this study, we designed and synthesized 135,016 of unique oligos to examine promoter architecture and 
genetic circuits in E. coli. Our oligos contained back-to-back promoters such that the left sequence 
controlled expression of one of 13 different TFs and the right oligo controlled sfGFP; the fluorescent output 
was used to quantify the effect of each oligo. We performed FlowSeq on three different experiments: the 
LacI library only, all libraries without induction and the inducible libraries with four different inducer 
concentrations. At the time of writing this thesis, we have only sequenced and analyzed the LacI library 
while the others are being sequenced.  
Our oligos were designed to systematically probe promoter architecture by mutating each nucleotide in a 
45 nucleotide window in the promoter sequence. We showed that this is a powerful method to quantify 
the importance of individual bases in a promoter. The quantitative data was used to build a simple additive 
model that shows how protein expression from a slightly mutated promoter can be predicted. However, an 
accumulation of mutations tend to break the promoter. We suggest using more advanced prediction 
methods that take into account interactions between mutations.  
We examined genetic circuits by designing oligos with variations in location, strength and number of 
operators in the regulatory element responsible for sfGFP expression and/or TF expression. Despite lacking 
quantitative measurements of TF expression, we find the LacI represses up to 46-fold, and that strong 
repression typically occurs when a strong operator is placed in the spacer region of the promoter. We show 
that FlowSeq can be used to select genetic circuits across a range of repression and protein expression.  
In conclusion, FlowSeq enables multiplex characterization of genetic circuits and probing of regulatory 
sequences. The analysis in this thesis has not yet exhausted our dataset, and we conclude that FlowSeq 
coupled with DNA synthesis is a powerful tool to help uncover the effect DNA sequence has on cellular 
function.

5. MATERIALS AND METHODS 
5.1 Library Design, Construction and Cloning 
The library was designed using Promuter (section 2.1), tested they satisfied all requirements (section 5.3.1) 
and synthesized by Agilent Technologies using their oligo library synthesis process yielding a total mass of 
1050 ng DNA with ≈50-130 ng of full length DNA (≈5-12%). Each library set was amplified separately based 
on their corresponding TF, using ≈21 ng of DNA (≈1.1-2.5 ng full-length DNA), 600 nM each of forward and 
reverse primer (see Supplemental Table 1 for primers and the library they amplify) and KAPA SYBR Fast 
qPCR Kit Master Mix. The reaction was amplified using a CFX96 Touch™ Real-Time PCR Detection System 
(Bio-Rad) until we no longer observed exponential amplification (3 min initial denaturation at 95°C; ≈15 
cycles of denaturation for 3 sec at 95°C with annealing/extension at 63°C for 30 sec). Library sets were 
purified individually using Agencourt AMPure XP magnetic beads and eluted in TE buffer. 
We used 14 different low-copy plasmids derived from pGERC45 (Supplemental Figure 1), differing only by 
their TF. A total of 13 different TFs were used (Table 1) all with an N-terminal FLAG-tag (DYKDDDDK)100 to 
reduce the effects of codon bias on protein expression43 between different TFs. The last plasmid acts as a 
control and does not have a TF, but only the FLAG-tag. We amplified the backbones individually using 500 
nM of primers eccn110 and eccn111, ≈1ng template and the KAPA HiFi HotStart ReadyMix PCR Kit. The 
reaction was initiated with a 5 min initial denaturation step at 95 °C; 25 cycles of denaturation (98°C for 20 
sec), annealing (62°C for 15 sec) and extension (72°C for 3 min); and a final extension step at 72°C for 5 min. 
The amplified backbones were purified individually using the QIAquick PCR purification kit (QIAGEN).  
Overhangs for the ligation were generated by digesting the amplified and purified library and backbones 
with BsaI HF (New England Biolabs) for 5 hours at 37°C. The backbone was treated with Antarctic 
Phosphatase (New England Biolabs) for the last 15 min of digestion to prevent self-ligation. Following 
digestion enzymes were deactivated by heating to 65°C for 20 min. The oligo library sets and backbones 
were purified using AMPure XP magnetic beads and the QIAquick PCR purification kit, respectively.  
The purified and digested backbone (>1 μg) and insert (≈100 ng) were mixed at a 1:3 molar ratio. Using a 
high mass of backbone was critical for successful transformation. Prior to ligation, the mixed backbone and 
insert was heated to 50°C for 5 min and allowed to slowly cool down to RT. High-concentration T4 DNA 
ligase and fresh T4 DNA ligase buffer (New England Biolabs) was added and the ligation proceeded for 1 
hour at 23°C followed by a 10 min deactivation at 65°C. The ligated product was purified and concentrated 
by ethanol precipitation (SI Materials and Methods) and eluted in a small volume of water. The entire 
concentrated ligation was transformed directly into NEB Turbo Electrocompetent E. coli (New England 
Biolabs) as described by New England Biolabs. A tiny volume was plated on regular LB plates with 
kanamycin selection (referred to as LB Kan) to estimate the total number of CFUs in the transformation; the 
remainder was grown in 50 mL LB Kan O/N with shaking. Both the liquid culture and the plate were 
incubated at 30°C. Plasmids were isolated from the O/N culture using a QIAGEN Miniprep kit. 
To prepare the library in ECdbg3 (Table 4), we concentrated 1 μg of isolated plasmid by ethanol purification 
into 4 μL water. This was transformed directly into electrocompetent ECdbg3. Transformed cells were 
recovered in SOC for 1 hour with shaking at 37°C, and plated on large LB Kan plate (240mm x 240mm). 
Plates were incubated O/N at 30°C to minimize growth differences between different library members. 
After O/N incubation, cells were scraped off the large LB Kan plate, resuspended in a small volume of LB

5.2 Library Growth and FlowSeq 
We performed three different FlowSeq runs on different days: one with the LacI library only (LacI-FS), one 
with all 14 libraries without inducers (Full-FS) and one with inducers for the inducible libraries (TetR, LacI, 
AcuR, CdaR and TtgR, referred to as Inducible-FS). For all FlowSeq runs, we separately inoculated 20 mL LB 
Kan with 100 μL of the library sets (from glycerol stock) and grew them O/N (≈14 hours) at 30°C with 
shaking. For LacI-FS and Full-FS, we did a backdilution into 20 mL fresh LB Kan in the morning using 70 μL of 
each O/N culture and grew at 30°C with shaking until early log phase (OD600 = 0.15-0.3) after which cells 
were immediately placed on ice. For the Inducible-FS, we instead backdiluted into LB Kan containing the 
appropriate inducer (Table 5) using 3 μL culture except for AcuR and TtgR concentration #4 where we used 
100 μL and 15 μL, respectively to compensate for the reduced growth rate due to inducer toxicity. All 
inducer libraries irrespective of inducer concentration were grown for exactly 5h20min at 30°C with 
shaking, enough time for the inducer to affect transcription and capturing cells in early log-phase (OD600 = 
0.15-0.3). The cells were immediately placed on ice when the time was up.

5.3 Data Analysis and Computational Tools 
At the time of writing, results were unavailable for the Full-FS and Inducible-FS, and the data analysis had 
only been done for LacI-FS. All scripts and methods have been designed to easily accommodate the 
additional data once it becomes available. We used R to perform all statistics and the ggplot2 package in R 
for generating all graphs.  
5.3.1 Error Correction of Oligo Design 
Custom Python software was used to check all oligos prior to synthesis to ensure they satisfied our design 
criteria. We ensured they all had the correct length (230 bp) and two BsaI cut sites at the correct position 
generating the correct overhangs (Supplemental Figure 2). The entire library was also tested for duplicate 
sequences and we ensured a minimum hamming distance of 3 between any two sequences; the latter not 
applying to the single-basepair-mutants. The control library set was checked to ensure they had the first 23 
N-terminal codons of the correct TF (including the FLAG-tag) added at the 3’-end. We ensured the codons 
and library set matched and that they had the correct reading frame. Finally, we ensured each library set 
was amplified by their correct, orthogonal primer set.  
5.3.2 Forming, Trimming and Counting Contigs for Analysis 
We used custom Unix scripts in combination with FLASh85 to merge paired-end reads and trim off adapter 
sequences. Using Bowtie86 and custom Python scripts, we matched the aligned sequences to the reference 
sequences from the LacI-FS library set, counting the number of reads for each unique sequence and only

7. SI MATERIALS AND METHODS 
7.1 Ethanol Precipitation 
Ethanol precipitation was performed on 1 volume DNA-containing sample by adding 1/10th volume sodium 
acetate (pH 5.2) and 3 volumes ice-cold 99.9% ethanol. The sample was quickly mixed and frozen for at 
least 1 hour at -80°C. After freezing, salt-bound DNA was spun down in a tabletop centrifuge at max speed 
for 30 min at 4°C. Supernatant was removed by decanting and 100 μL ice-cold 70% ethanol was added, 
taking care not to disturb the pellet. The mixture was spun at max speed at 4°C for another 15 min and 
supernatant was removed again. The pellet was air-dried completely and eluted in TE buffer (if stored at  
-20°C for later use) or water (if used immediately). 
7.2 Preparing ECdbg3 for Electroporation 
ECdbg3 was streaked out on an LB plate with kanamycin selection (LB Kan) and grown O/N at 30°C; a single 
colony was used to inoculate 10 mL of LB without antibiotics which was grown O/N. 2 L pre-warmed LB, 
split into 4 flasks, were inoculated with 2 mL of the O/N culture each and grown with shaking at 30°C. At 
OD600 = 0.4-0.5, the cells were put on ice and chilled for 15 minutes with occasional swirling for even 
cooling. The culture was split into 333 mL portions and spun down at 2000×g for 10 minutes at 4°C. 
Supernatant was removed and the cells were resuspended in 250 mL ice cold, double-distilled water (ddH-
2O). The cells were spun down as before and resuspended in 125 mL ice cold ddH2O. The resuspended cells 
were spun down again (2000×g, 10 min, 4°C). Supernatant was carefully removed, and cells were 
resuspended in 20 mL ice cold 10% glycerol. The 20 mL of resuspended cells were transferred to a conical 
tube and spun down as previous. The supernatant was carefully aspirated and the pellet was resuspended 
in 1 mL of ice cold 10% glycerol. Resuspended cells were split into 100 μL aliquots and snap-frozen by 
dipping into liquid nitrogen. The now electrocompetent cells were stored at -80°C for later use.
