Abstract
The scope of this project was to investigate the modal parameters of a flexible shaft
mounted in an active gas bearing in different configurations both under hydrostatic and
hybrid lubrication. Apart from examining the shaft-bearing system, the results found
were meant to be used as references for future tests using other bearings on the same test
rig.
A previously manufactured active gas foil bearing was examined and modified to overcome
some of the known practical issues. However, it was no tested due to time constraints.
The work included an update of the test rig and installation of an electrical motor to
drive the shaft. This enabled easier testing compared to the previous drive train of a gas
turbine.
After the update, it was necessary to identify unusual interfering natural frequencies
occurring in the frequency response function of the shaft. These were assumed to be as-
sociated to the update, as the same shaft-bearing system had been examined in previous
projects, so natural frequencies belonging to the shaft were known. The other natural
frequencies discovered proved to originate from the test rig foundation and measures to
eliminate these were taken.
Some of these natural frequencies were successfully eliminated, but at the cost of others
appearing.
After identifying the unusual natural frequencies, tests were made with the shaft mounted
in the active gas bearing in several configurations of air injections and air pressures un-
der hydrostatic and hybrid lubrication. This included estimation of natural frequencies,
damping factors and other modal parameters using a least square fit to the frequency
response function.
The results agreed well with previous results of the same bearing with some parameters
slightly changing supposedly due changes in the air injection components. The horizontal
response of the shaft was difficult to evaluate due to interfering natural frequencies. It
was concluded that these interferences should be further examined before precise results
from the horizontal response can be achieved.

1
Introduction
Bearings are crucial parts in rotating machinery as they enable components to rotate
independently of other components. Examples of uses are wheels, turbines and engines.
Typically, ball bearings are used, but they cause friction, may get worn or require constant
lubrication. Another type of bearing is fluid bearings. These have some significant ben-
efits compared to ball bearings. Most importantly, the friction is reduced, as the metal
to metal contact is completely avoided. This is achieved by fluid bearings creating a film
of typically gas or oil between the shaft and the bearing. Using gas bearings additionally
decreases the need for viscous lubricants as oil or grease, which may conflict with the
operating environment or require maintenance.
Fluid bearings are becoming more widely used, however, they still have limitations in
regard to stability and load carrying capacities. An example of use is air conditioner
systems [1]. Different kinds of gas bearings exist and gas foil bearings (GFB) seem to
have advantages over usual gas bearings. Also, active gas bearings (AGB) exist, where
pressurized air is injected into the bearing. A hybrid bearing, an active gas foil bearing
(AGFB), using both foils and controllable injection was designed some years ago at DTU
during another project [2], but it was not tested due to practical issues. Additionally, the
test rig meant for testing the bearing was outdated. These issues were addressed during
this project, creating a solid foundation for the testing of the AGFB.
The test rig had previously been used to test an AGB, but an unfamiliar natural fre-
quency appeared during a previous project [3]. The cause of it was desirable to source as
it could interfere with the natural frequencies of the AGFB, which would complicate the
characterization of the new bearing. It was also desirable to create reference data from
the AGB, which was known to work properly, so natural frequencies occurring in new
bearing would be easier to identify.
To investigate the AGB, Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) was utilized. EMA is the
study of a structure’s vibration response to excitations - this could be momentary impacts
or oscillations. The natural frequencies and damping factors are important parameters
of a vibrating structure, which can be estimated using EMA. The natural frequencies are
desired to avoid as these are operational conditions where significant vibrations occur,
which makes the structure unstable. Damping counteracts these vibrations by lowering
the vibration amplitude and thereby stabilize the structure.
In EMA, a structure’s response in the frequency domain is established, where the natural
frequencies can be identified. This method is used in the industries of automobiles, air
crafts and spacecrafts among others. The natural frequencies of rotating machinery are
examined to see if these are within the rotational operational range of the product in use.
In case they are, measures are typically taken to either change the natural frequencies or
introduce damping.
Often, the natural frequencies are difficult to change as these are constrained by the de-
sign of the machinery, which makes the implementation of more damping the desirable
choice. Thus, damping is a crucial part of rotating machinery.

1.1
State of the Art at DTU
Bearings of different designs have been researched at DTU for some years. Examples
are magnetic bearings, gas foil bearings and active gas bearings. The test rig mentioned
above was originally built in 2008 with magnetic bearings and an active gas foil bearing
[4]. The test rig was modified in 2010 [5], where a controllable active gas bearing using
piezo actuators was installed. In the following years, the magnetic bearings were removed
and the active control of the gas bearing was studied [6].
In 2019, the hybrid bearing (controllable active gas foil bearing) was constructed, but it
did not work due to practical issues, so the controllable active gas bearing was reinstalled.

The friction between the shaft surface and the gas forces the gas into rotation around
the shaft. Due to the small gap, the gas is retained in the bearing and the pressure of
the gas increases. The highest pressure occurs in bottom of the bearing, where the gap
is smallest, and the pressure increases enough to carry the shaft - acting like a cushion.
3
==PAGE:23==
Depending on the direction of rotation, the shaft will move horizontally as well due to the
pressure profile, as the gas is drawn from one side and forced into the other. The colored
gradient of the gas film in the figure reflects the varying pressure profile in the gas film.
As rotation creates the film, the rotational speed is critical for the functionality and can-
not be too low, else the gas film will not be sufficiently strong to carry the shaft. This
also implies that the shaft is laying on the hub when it is stationary, which causes friction
at startup, wearing the bearing. The principle of using only the rotation of the shaft to
create the gas film is denoted aerodynamic lubrication.
To increase the pressure at low speeds and avoid the startup friction, gas can be injected
actively into the bearing - between the shaft and hub, see Figure 2(b). This allows the
bearing to work without rotation, which is the concept of the AGB. This principle is
denoted hydrostatic lubrication. Yet, rotational speed still increases the strength of the
gas film, which is the principle of hybrid lubrication. Additionally, the gas injection can
be turned on or shut off quickly during operation in different locations to control the
bearing at instabilities, which would otherwise make the shaft hit the hub. This defines
the controllable AGB.
Another way to improve the basic gas bearing is to add top foils and bump foils as
shown in Figure 3(a), which is the GFB. The foils are thin pieces of metal. The bump foil
acts like a spring, softening the start up of the shaft, reducing wear. The uneven surface
of the bump foil prevents the air film build up, why the top foil is placed on top of the
bump foil. The top foils are typically coated with friction reducing materials, like Teflon.
At standstill, the shaft fully rests on the foils. As the pressure increases during startup,
the bump foils are pushed back, and the shaft is suspended by the gas film as in the
basic gas bearing. If instabilities occur and the shaft displaces radially, the spring action
from the bump foils acts on the gas film, damping the vibrations. If the shaft displaces
significantly, it would hit the foils instead of the hub. As the foils may deflect, wear is
reduced.

2.2.5
Mode Shapes
The way in which the shaft vibrates at a natural frequency is known as the mode-shape
with the most simple mode-shape belonging to the lowest natural frequency. With relation
to the shaft-bearing system, the first mode shape of it may be a bending mode shape or a
rigid body mode shape. This depends on the stiffness of the system. A rigid body mode
shape is when the shaft vibrates up and down like a stiff "line". A bending mode shape
is when the shaft vibrates like an arc, bending up and down.
Mode shapes have stationary points, nodes, where the shaft does not displace in the mode
shape. See Figure 13 for illustrations of the two types including nodes related to the shaft
with a disc, where the green line reflects the rigid body mode shape and the blue line
reflects the bending mode shape.
Figure 13: Example of a rigid body mode shape (green line) and a bending mode shape
(blue line). The black points reflect nodes, which are stationary points in the mode shape.
The rigid body mode shape only has one node, so the left most node is not related to the
rigid body mode shape.
The same accounts for the disc mounted on the shaft, which could have mode shapes of
its own and affect the vibrations of the shaft. Assuming the disc is mounted as a point
mass on the shaft, simplifies the mode shapes to the given example.

shaft, which is equal to the rotational speed of the shaft. The unbalance response is in-
evitable, as no ideal components exists, so the mass cannot be evenly distributed within
a component. This makes the component heavier at some point, which creates a greater
centrifugal force in that direction from the center. The force then affects the system with
the frequency of the rotational speed.
At the intersections of the red line and one of the other, a critical speed occurs, where the
rotational speed is equal to the natural frequency of either the backward or forward mode
shape. However, the backward mode can only be excited by the unbalance response, if
the system is asymmetrical.
In a asymmetrical system, the modal parameters of the horizontal and vertical directions
are different from each other, so the vertical and horizontal mode shape have different
natural frequencies. The difference in stiffness makes the forward and backward mode
shapes become elliptic movements rotating around the shaft center line. The frequency
15
==PAGE:35==
of the higher mode shape becomes the frequency of the forward mode shape and the
frequency of the lower mode shape become the frequency of the backward mode shape.
The major axis of the elliptic movement is in the same direction as the corresponding
non-rotating mode shape. For example, if the vertical direction has the higher natural
frequency when not rotating, the forward mode shape when rotating will be an ellipse
with the major axis in the vertical direction, rotating forward.
When exciting a system, a superposition of mode shapes occurs. Exciting the rotating
shaft results in an alternating movement between the forward and the backward mode
shape as the superposition of the two. This is represented in Figure 15.
Figure 15: Alternating movement of a shaft during the forward and backward mode shape
where the movement is in one direction at first, then changing to the other.
As the two natural frequencies also differ for a non-rotating asymmetrical system, the
alternating movement can be seen from exciting a non-rotating asymmetrical system as
well.

2.2.7
Filtering
When sampling data, signal noise may interfere with the measurements. Noise can be
caused by electronics switching on and off, electromagnetic waves etc. Typically high
frequency noise is introduced from electrical components. If it is not possible to remove
the source of the noise, a filter may be applied to the measured signal. The filter can
remove undesired frequencies, while not affecting desired frequencies.
Different filters exist with the most general examples being low-pass, high-pass or band-
pass. The low-pass filter allows frequencies lower than a set limit to pass while removing
higher frequency components, like noise. A high-pass filter works oppositely by allowing
higher frequencies than a set limit to pass while removing low frequency components. A
band-pass filter allows a range of frequencies between an upper and lower limit to pass.
The principle of the filters are sketched in Figure 16, where the frequencies are multiplied
by the corresponding amplitudes. Thus, frequencies with an amplitude of 1 are allowed
17
==PAGE:37==
to pass while frequencies with an amplitude of 0 are eliminated. The transitioning fre-
quencies are reduced rather than removed.
There are different methods for creating the filters. The following are some examples;
Kalman, Butterworth and Chebyshev.

2.2.9
Runout Compensation
When doing EMA on a rotating shaft using displacement sensors, the sensors are mounted
in place, so they do not move with the rotating shaft. This means, the sensors measure
along the circumference of the shaft and as nothing is ideal, radial variations, runout,
along the circumference exist.
In Figure 19, an ideal and actual shaft are sketched with the corresponding displacement
measurements during rotation.
If the radial variations are great, they may interfere with the displacements caused by the
impacts. To eliminate the variations, runout compensation can be performed, where the
variations are measured prior to the experiment and then subtracted from the acquired
data from the experiment. This done by measuring both runout and angular position.
Then the runout at each angular position during a rotation is known and may be sub-
19
==PAGE:39==
tracted from the displacements measured at each angular position during an experiment.
Thus, only the displacement from the impacts are visible in the processed displacement
data.

Pipe-Foil Connection
One of the primary issues of the AGFB was the pipe-foil connection, which allowed air to
be injected through the top foil. Previously, the connection was done using epoxy glue,
but it did not work.
Different solutions were considered and discussed with various people. These solutions
are described in the following.
Pulling-Expansion Method
The pipe-foil connection was discussed with Chris Valentin Nielsen who proposed the so-
36
==PAGE:56==
lution of pulling the pipes through the holes in the foils to expand the holes and fasten
the pipes in the process. This is sketched in Figure 40.
1

rod, 4: foil pipe. Left: the pipe is being pulled through the top foil. Right: the pipe has
been pulled through and is fastened in the top foil.
This would work by placing the foil in a rig with a hole. A wire or rod would go through
the rig, foil and pipe, to pull the pipe downwards through the foil. As the hole in the foil
would be smaller than the pipe’s outer diameter, the hole would be expanded, fastening
the pipe in the process.
As the foils were hardened when manufactured, this process might not work, as cracks
could occur due to the hardened material. However, it could be beneficial to use this
process before hardening the foils in the future.

The data was shifted to center around 0 to result in a mean value of 0. A low pass But-

into thirty segments centered around each impact time. A Hamming window was applied
to each segment. The autocorrelation and crosscorrelation were calculated for each win-
dowed segment. These were Fourier transformed to yield the PSD and CPSD of the force
and displacement data. The thirty PSDs and CPSDs were averaged to yield two final
PSDs and two final CSPDs from the force and displacement data. These were then used
for H1(ω) and H2(ω).
The method used is known as the Welch Method. The processing was done using Mat-
Lab and the built in functions of [pxx,f] = pwelch(x,window,noverlap,f,fs) and
[pxy,f] = cpsd(x,y,window,noverlap,f,fs). The script used was based on the
frf_general.mat script provided by Ilmar Ferreira Santos.

Force Transducer calibration
The force transducer used for the test, B&K Type 8200, seemed to be relatively old, as
the data sheet found for it was published in 1995 [10].
When the force transducer was bought, a calibration chart should have been included,
but it was not possible to source this document. Additionally, there were no records of
previous students calibrating it.
42
==PAGE:62==
The data sheet claimed calibration should not be necessary due to the stated extreme
stability of the piezoelectric element. However, regarding the age of the force transducer
and to increase reliability of the results, calibration was carried out.
This was done using a mass of 3.88 kg mounted with an accelerometer hung in strings and
hitting it with the impact hammer, see Figure 47. The impact hammer was connected
as described previously, as the best calibration is achieved using the exact setup used for
the actual test rig i.e. the actual wires and components used on the bearing test rig. The
accelerometer was connected to dSpace similar to the connection of the impact hammer.

This

Discussion
7.1
Sources of Error
The general precision of the proximity sensors, which was about ±2 µm denote the pre-
cision of the displacement measurements. However, it was noted that the displacement
measurements slowly shifted about ±5 µm over time.
Further sources of error are listed below.
• Bearing clearance measurements were done by hand.
• During force transducer calibration impacts, the mass hit was stopped by hand
rather than naturally.
• The sampling frequency may not be able to pick up all oscillations or peaks.
• Impacts were done by hand making it possible they were done in an angle instead
of completely vertical of horizontal. The force may also have varied.
• Mode shapes from other components may interfere with the response of the shaft.
7.2
Bearing Clearance
It was difficult to make sure the shaft follow the circumference instead of skipping some
points. This would cause a linear movement instead of the shaft following the curve of
the bearing. The force applied by hand varied, which may have bend the shaft a little.
This would make the shaft displace more, than that of the clearance distance.
The nominal clearance was stated in 2016, so the current measurements of 33-35 µm could
indicate some wear, which is realistic regarding the time period.
A greater clearance could indicate lower stiffness and higher damping as the pressure
inside the bearing would be lower, making the air film less firm.
7.3
Force Transducer Calibration
The calibrated test using the calibration factor yielded a deviation of 3.51 % from the
actual mass. This indicates the accuracy of the test and the data. However, the order of
magnitude is more important, so the little deviation may not have great influence. Also,
the natural frequency and damping ratio should not be affected by an incorrect force
magnitude, as the frequencies depend on the shape of the data and not the size. The
modal mass, stiffness and damping should be affected though.
7.4
Response of the Aluminum Plate
The two natural frequencies at 32 Hz and 52 Hz that were discovered when the aluminum
plate was examined, seemed to change to one natural frequency around 63 Hz when the
plate was tightened. It then decreased to 46 Hz when the shaft was mounted. The first
change may be related to different modes of the plate, e.g. a vertical mode shape that
was restrained by the bolts so it was eliminated. The frequency decreasing to 46 Hz could
relate to the mass of the shaft being added to the system, but it seems unlikely, as the
mass of the shaft was low compared to the mass of the plate. This was about 3.8 kg and
about 40 kg respectively.
The increase in natural frequency was also seen after the hybrid lubrication tests, where
90
==PAGE:110==
the plate was tightened again. So, the tightening may have increased the stiffness in
horizontal direction, which could explain the cause.
The fact, that the mode shape could be set up, indicated that the natural frequency
around 50 Hz actually was from the aluminum plate, but the number of points measured
makes this a little unreliable. If more points were measured, different movements may
have been discovered which may not relate to the first mode shape of the plate, thus prove
it was not a mode shape of the plate.
7.5
Response of the Shaft under Hydrostatic Lubrication
The vertical responses to vertical impacts were very clean and seemed only to be af-
fected by the horizontal mode of the shaft a little. As the horizontal mode could be seen,
it makes the SDOF LS fit less reliable. However, the deviations between the visual and
calculated natural frequencies were low and the fit often coincided very well with the curve.
The mode shape and elliptic trajectory being extracted easily support that the method
works properly and the measurement are good. If the mode shape did not look as ex-
pected, something essential could be different than expected.
The horizontal response was affected quite a lot by other natural frequencies and the
responses did not look very clean. The two loose bolts indicate how sensitive the response
of the shaft is to the foundation. The occurrences around 160-170 Hz had not been seen
in previous projects [9]. This could be related to the foundation, which was moved during
the installation of the electrical motor. Especially the aluminum plate seemed to affect
the results in the horizontal direction. This could be caused by the plate being more
or less stiff in the horizontal direction when adjusting the bolts. This may change the
stiffness of the shaft like two springs i series or a 2 DOF system with 2 masses moving in
the same direction. This could be likewise for the damping.
The interference of other natural frequencies make the SDOF LS fit less reliable. The
orthogonal mode was quite visible at some points, clearly proving the system is not a
SDOF system. How much the other natural frequency affected the results seem to vary
among the configurations.
The horizontal mode being more visible in the vertical response when the sideways injec-
tors were open could relate to an increased cross-coupling.
The tests performed before the aluminum plate was isolated by rubber showed gener-
ally a lower stiffness in vertical direction than the tests afterwards. During the isolation,
all bolts were tightened, including bolts fastening the piezo actuators. The air injected is
very sensitive to the mounting of the piezo actuators [9]. Tightening these could result
in more or less air being injected, potentially closing of air injection in some injectors or
causing an asymmetrical injection in the horizontal direction. This could cause changes
in stiffness and damping or make the shaft hit the bearing.
The change in natural frequency could perhaps also relate to the alignment of the shaft,
as it was re-aligned after the isolation. The alignment could make the shaft be closer to
one injector than another, thus affecting the pressures.
91
==PAGE:111==
Comparing the extracted damping factors of the vertical mode shape with the previ-
ous projects showed a similarity in order of magnitude of the parameters. The difference
between the natural frequencies of the vertical and horizontal mode shape in this project
were greater than the difference found by Ask Finderup and Vanja Wylie. The same ac-
counted for the damping ratios. This indicates a greater difference in stiffness in the two
directions during this project, which could relate to the fastening of the piezo actuators.
7.6
Response of the Shaft under Hybrid Lubrication
The low quality of the test series with only the bottom injector open could relate to very
little air being injected into the bearing. The rotation of the shaft could force this little
amount of air to be distributed in the bearing instead of in the bottom. This would make
the vertical response less stiff and the horizontal response more stiff, why the horizontal
response was more clean.
The data used for the runout compensation being of low quality could also affect the
result. This could for example be caused by noise or variations in the rotational speed.
However, only the vertical data should be of low quality then, as the horizontal response
was relatively clean. This seems improbable as the same method and test was used for
both the vertical and horizontal runout compensation data.
The runout compensation not working perfectly, did also not seem to be significant for
the other cases.
The response with all injectors open at 7 bar was very clean compared to the other
case, which indicates the method and measurement being suitable. This leads to thinking
something must cause the other response to turn out like it did.
A greater peak of the occurrence around 160-170 Hz for the rotational speed of 5000
RPM agrees with the multiple of 83.3 Hz coinciding with the occurrence at 166.7 Hz. In
other cases, the unbalance response did not seem to affect the results significantly.
The cross-coupling increasing with the rotational speed could explain the increased visi-
bility of both mode shapes in the orthogonal response. For example, the forward mode
shape would be less significant in the horizontal response, as the movement is primarily
in the vertical direction. The cross-coupling then excites the backward mode shape with
the primary movement in the horizontal direction with almost the same amplitude as the
forward mode shape in the horizontal direction.
The little peak between the backward and forward also only being visible in the orthog-
onal response, but independently of the vertical and horizontal direction, indicates it is
related to the cross-coupling.
The damping seemed to decrease based on the visual inspection, and was in some cases
confirmed by the LS fits from the horizontal responses. The natural frequency did not
seem to change. Perhaps, a greater difference in rotational speed would provide a greater
difference. This would however be difficult as the natural frequency of the shaft would be
approached.
92
==PAGE:112==
As the shaft was misaligned after the test series, it indicates some changes happened
during the tests. This may have affected some results if the shaft was hitting the bearing
after impacts as it was not known when this happened. This could relate to the case of
the open bottom injector at 3 bar, which did not yield very good results.
Regarding the modal parameters, comparison with the previous projects showed a similar
tendency as in the hydrostatic case with the difference between the backward and forward
natural frequencies being greater in this project.
This could relate to the same cause of the piezo actuator mountings as stated earlier,
which could have been more open in the vertical direction than in the horizontal direction
during this project.

8
Conclusion
The initial corrections of the test rig proved the 100 Hz natural frequency being caused by
two loose bolts. The 130-140 Hz natural frequencies were found to be caused by the table
supporting the test rig. The vibrations from the impact on the shaft were transferred to
the table, making the bars in the table vibrate and transfer their vibrations back to the
test rig.
The elimination of these two natural frequencies in turn caused other natural frequencies
to appear. The natural frequencies seemed to originate from the horizontal mode shape
of the aluminum plate. The other unusual natural frequencies were not identified.
Apart from the the two interfering natural frequencies at 130-140 Hz, the responses in fact
seemed more clean before the installation of the rubber mats. A general higher natural
frequency was seen after the installation, so it can be concluded something related to the
bolts, affects the bearing properties.
The vertical responses to vertical impacts seemed very clean and the trajectories being
easily extracted and looking as expected indicate the method and instrumentation were
not an issue or main cause of poor FRFs. The same accounts for the increased clearance,
which then seems not to be an issue.
Damping factors and natural frequencies are in the right order compared to previous re-
sults, but especially the horizontal direction was difficult to investigate. This was proved
by the LS fits not coinciding well with the curves and the visible interfering natural fre-
quencies.
If the other natural frequencies are not eliminated the LS fit should be adjusted separately
for each horizontal response to choose points reflecting a SDOF system properly. For this
project, a generic pattern was used by the
√
2 method.
The damping factor decreasing without the natural frequency increasing during the hy-
brid lubrication tests is notable. This indicates the damping is more sensitive to rotation,
than then natural frequency.
The very low damping makes the bearing vulnerable. To improve, damping should be
increased, which is exactly what the active gas foil bearing should add.
It is not straightforward to identify the causes of the natural frequencies.
The foun-
dation and the other components have a significant influence on the response of the shaft
rather than simply incorrect methods or bad instrumentation.
The bearing properties are sensitive to the tightening of the the aluminum plate and the
alignment of the shaft. Also, the piezo actuators have influence on the air injection, which
was proved by Fabian Pierart who tested the shaft-bearing system with different piezo
settings.
Hence, further examination of the foundation and mountings is appropriate to examine
the horizontal response - the interfering natural frequencies, how the aluminum plate
affects the bearing and if the air injection is adjusted properly. Care should be taken
for testing other bearings if the natural frequencies of the foundation are not examined
further before as these may interfere, especially in the horizontal direction.

8.1
Future Work
Some recommendations for future work are given below based on the thoughts and results
during this project.
• Examine the adjustment of the piezo actuators.
• Find solutions for isolating the aluminum plate from the table while not making the
plate susceptible to its own mode shapes.
• Measure more places with more sensors along the shaft to examine the unusual
natural frequencies.
• Fasten the aluminum plate in horizontal direction to see if this affects the assumed
natural frequency of the horizontal mode shape of the plate.
• Investigate the influence of the aluminum plate and the alignment on the shaft-
bearing properties.
• Find a better, more systematic way of aligning the shaft, maybe a feeler gauge could
be used.
• Consider if it is feasible to install a steel plate instead of the aluminum plate to
eliminate the unusual natural frequencies and ease shaft alignment as a steel plate
is more stiff.
• Examine the source of the occurrence around 160-170 Hz.
• Look into different flexible couplings as the hose used gets worn by time.
• Isolate the electrical motor on a separate foundation to avoid possible interference
from a vibrating motor during rotation.
• Test pipe-foil connection of the AGFB before mounting the bearing in the test rig.
• Test the AGFB.
• Implement multi-DOF fitting to estimate the damping factor.

Bottom plate adjustment to align ball bearing and air bearing 
When adjusting the alignment of the ball bearing and air bearing, the rotor and motor should be 
disconnected to see when the rotor spins freely. The pressure should be 3 bar (as lower pressures seem 
to make the rotor touch the bearing when the top and bottom valves are opened). The valves in the air 
bearing are all opened, and the six bolts connecting the bottom plate to the base are tightened and 
loosened by trial and error until the rotor spins freely. Then the sideways valves are closed, and it is 
ensured the rotor still spins freely. Else the bolts should be adjusted and the configuration with all valves 
opened should be checked again. When the rotor spins freely in both configurations, the top and 
sideways valves are closed, and the rotor should still spin freely – if not, the bolts should be adjusted, 
and the other configurations should be checked. 
 
Piezo 
The piezo actuators are controlled by the E617 amplifiers in the cabinet. The displacements of the 
actuators are measured by the E621 amplifier / controller using half bridge strain gauges inside the 
actuators. The E621 amplifier / controller has built in feedback control, but this seems not to be 
implemented, as the Analog Input and PZT ports should be used for this purpose. However, feedback 
control is perhaps implemented in dSPACE. 
Piezo actuators can be set to a constant offset by setting the amplitude to 0 and the DC value to the 
desired offset. 
The piezo position unit and sensitivity may not be right – it was not relevant for Ask and Vanja to 
investigate. 
 
Disc proximity sensor 
The disc proximity sensors are used with the IQS450 signal conditioners to produce an electromagnetic 
field used for measuring the proximity.

The signals from the flow meter are read by dSPACE through ADCH4.
