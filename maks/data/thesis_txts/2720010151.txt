Abstract
This thesis presents methods to quickly obtain near-optimal solutions for the asymmetric Travelling
Salesman Problem with Time Windows (TSPTW). It is a collaboration with the company AMCS,
which provided real-life instances from one of their projects.
Chapter 1 introduces the TSPTW and the three data sets used in this thesis, together with a
standard data representation and mixed-integer programming formulation. Chapter 2 describes the
state-of-the-art metaheuristic approaches to the TSPTW. Chapter 3 presents the metaheuristic im-
plemented in this thesis, which is based on Variable Neighborhood Search (VNS). The performance
of the metaheuristic is quantified by the various lower bound algorithms described in chapter 4. The
lower bound algorithms include a subgradient algorithm with a Lagrangian relaxation sub-problem,
a relaxation into a normal TSP solved with the Concorde solver, and a simple cutting-based algo-
rithm. The overall results are very satisfactory with an average gap between metaheuristic solutions
and best lower bounds of 3.63% for the AMCS instances, provided a 5 second time limit for the
metaheuristic. The lower bound algorithms perform vastly different dependent on the data set, but
together the algorithms provide good lower bounds for all data sets. For the AMCS data set the
best lower bounds were found by relaxations to normal TSPs solved with Concorde.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP) is a famous problem in combinatorial optimization. Yu
et al. [23] describe it as "(...) the prototype of the hard combinatorial optimization problem".
Kara and Derya [12] write that the TSP "(...) lies at the heart of routing problems and serves
as one of the most attractive combinatorial optimization problems." These formulations show the
importance and relevance of the TSP. The TSP is an NP-hard problem and can be stated as: A
salesman starting at a depot must visit n stops one by one and return to the depot. What is the
minimum cost tour?
The Travelling Salesman Problem with Time Windows (TSPTW) is an important variant of the
TSP. It extends the original problem of finding a minimum cost tour with additional constraints of
time windows. In the TSPTW each stop is associated with an interval of permissible times in which
it must be visited. The salesman must wait to arrive at a stop until its time window opens, and if
he arrives after its time window has closed, the tour is infeasible. The TSPTW is NP-hard since
it has the TSP as a special case. Furthermore, Savelsbergh showed in 1985 [19] that even finding
a feasible solution to the TSPTW is NP-hard. The TSPTW has many practical applications in
logistics and manufacturing [16], with this thesis’ collaboration with AMCS being a clear example.
The TSPTWs in this thesis are asymmetric, such that the cost of going from A to B is not necessarily
the same as going from B to A. Many real-life applications are asymmetric, and asymmetries may
easily occur in routing problems. Asymmetries may arise from simple concepts, such as differences
between left and right turns, or from more complex road designs.
The objective function for a TSPTW is not as unambiguous and straightforward as for a TSP. In
most of the literature the objective function is the travel-time objective, which minimizes the total
cost of travelling the arcs between different stops. However, alternative objective functions exist
such as the makespan objective [12]. The makespan objective minimizes the completion time of the
tour, that is, the time at which the travelling salesman returns to the depot. This thesis will, in
line with most literature on the topic, consider the travel-time objective. The goal of this thesis
will be to implement algorithms to quickly obtain good solutions for the TSPTW with travel-time
objective, but also to quantify and validate the performance of the implemented algorithms from a
theoretical point of view.

1.1
Data description
A standard way to represent data for a TSPTW with n vertices is to have the following 3 data
structures:
1. A n × n cost matrix, c, where entry cij represents the cost of going from stop i to stop j.
2. A n × n time matrix, T, where entry Tij is the time it takes to travel from stop i to stop j.
3. A list of time windows, w = [a b] with n entries, where each entry, wi = (ai bi), denotes the
opening time ai and closing time bi of the i’th time window.

1.1.2
Secondary data sets
Two secondary data sets are used in this thesis to test and validate the implemented algorithms.
Data was downloaded from the web page https://lopez-ibanez.eu/tsptw-instances [14], which con-
tains information and data sets for the TSPTW. These data sets are well-studied and have been
tackled with many different approaches. Therefore, it is likely that the best-known objective values
for these data sets are close to, if not, optimal. The two data sets are:
1. The SolomonPotvinBengio data set, which is described as: "30 asymmetric instances originally
provided by Potvin & Bengio [18] and derived from Solomon’s RC2 VRPTW instances [20].
These instances are very diverse in structure. The number of customers (n) ranges from 3 to
44 customers." From now on, this data set will be referred to as the SPB data set.
2. The AFG (rbg) data set, which is described as: "50 asymmetric TSPTW instances introduced
by Ascheuer [2]. These are real-world instances derived ’from an industry project with the aim
to minimize the unloaded travel time of a stacker crane within an automated storage system.’
We have removed instance rbg021.tw because it is exactly the same as rbg019c.tw." I have
also removed the redundant rbg019c.tw instance, so there are a total of 49 instances. This
data set will be referred to as the AFG data set.
The SPB and AFG data sets do not have separate cost and time matrices, but time and cost
matrices are simply the same, c = T. The downloaded files are text files which provide the number
of stops, a cost/time matrix and a list of time windows.
Finally it should be noted that for both the AMCS data and the secondary data sets, all entries in
c, T and w are non-negative, real numbers.

- The assignment constraints make sure that all stops are arrived at and left exactly once.

- The time precedence constraints are ti + Tij ≤tj + (1 −xij)M, where M is a big, positive

They formulate the relation that, if the salesman travels from stop i to stop j,
he can arrive at j no sooner than he arrived at i plus the travel time from i to j. If the
salesman does not travel from stop i to stop j, then M is chosen sufficiently big to make
the inequality trivially satisfied. Additionally, the salesman must arrive at a stop in its time
window, ak ≤tk ≤bk. Thus, the salesman is allowed to wait at stop i, before he uses arc (i,j)
to travel to stop j and arrive at tj ≥aj.
Since all values in c, T and w = [a b] are non-negative values, the time precedence constraints prevent
sub-tours, which in a traditional TSP need separate constraints, such as the Miller-Tucker-Zemlin
formulation seen in [23].

Chapter 2
Literature review
The scientific literature on the TSPTW is quite extensive and contains both exact and heuristic
approaches. An early exact approach was Dumas et al.’s 1995 dynamic programming algorithm
proposed in [6].
This algorithm uses elimination tests before and during the execution of the
algorithm to reduce the search space. In 1998 Pesant et al. [17] proposed an exact algorithm based
on constraint logic programming. 2002 saw Focacci et al. [8] present a hybrid exact algorithm;
hybrid in the sense that it utilizes constraint programming for the feasibility aspect and operations
research techniques for the optimization aspect. 10 years later, in 2012, Baldacci et al. [3] proposed
an algorithm based on state-space relaxations and dynamic programming which outperformed all
earlier exact algorithms in the literature.
Metaheuristic approaches have also been studied intensively. In 1998 Gendreau et al. [9] suggested
a generalized insertion heuristic which rely on insertion or removing vertices and continuous re-
optimization. 2007 saw Ohlmann and Thomas [16] propose a version of simulated annealing called
"compressed annealing" for the TSPTW. They extend simulated annealing and its temperature
analogy with a concept of "pressure" and a variable penalty method to relax time windows. In 2010
da Silva and Urrutia [5] presented a two-phase GVNS heuristic with random restarts. This meta-
heuristic proved to be very efficient and significantly cut down computation times while producing
great results. In 2017 Kóczy et al. [13] put forward a novel metaheuristic for the TSP and TSPTW
based on a bacterial evolutionary algorithm and local search techniques. Kóczy et al. compared
their results with what they deemed the state-of-art algorithms for the TSPTW, the GVNS heuristic
by da Silva and Urrutia and the compressed annealing heuristic by Ohlmann and Thomas.
Table 2.1 shows an overview of the performance by the different metaheuristics mentioned in this
section. Kóczy et al. already perform a comparison of compressed annealing, GVNS and their
bacterial, evolutionary algorithm, so for these three heuristics the two right-most columns are based
on this comparison (page 398 in [13]). This comparison is made based on instances with 20 to 100

Chapter 3
Metaheuristic
Metaheuristic optimization is used in this thesis to tackle the problem of finding good solutions to
the TSPTW quickly. But why use metaheuristic optimization? Glover and Kochenberger write in
their 2003 preface to the first edition of the Handbook of Metaheuristics [10] that "(...) metaheuristic
methods have proved to be remarkably effective, so much so that metaheuristics have moved into the
spotlight in recent years as the preferred line of attack for solving many types of complex problems,
particularly those of a combinatorial nature." They define metaheuristics as "solution methods
that orchestrate an interaction between local improvement procedures and higher level strategies to
create a process capable of escaping from local optima and performing a robust search of a solution
space”. This characterization highlights some important aspects of metaheuristics. Often times, the
local improvement procedure is performed a massive number of times, so a crucial component of the
metaheuristic is the computational efficiency of the local improvement procedure. The higher level
strategy will guide the local improvement procedure through the solution space, often incorporating
some kind of stochasticity, to ideally perform a robust, thorough and efficient search. An important
aspect of a metaheuristic framework is its adaptability to different problems. Another key feature
of metaheuristics, and one of their primary advantages over exact algorithms, is their speed and
ability to make trade-offs between accuracy and computation time. Many real-world problems are
incredibly complex and more concerned with the speed at which a high-quality solution can be
obtained than optimality.
Metaheuristics find use in a diverse range of fields. One is transport optimization as in this thesis,
but a quick search reveals that they are also utilized in e.g. thermal systems [21] and economics [1].
This chapter will present a metaheuristic framework in section 3.1. An outline of the GVNS al-
gorithm used in this thesis is given in section 3.2, whose neighborhoods, construction phase, op-
timization phase and implementation issues are discussed in sections 3.2.1, 3.2.2, 3.2.3 and 3.2.4
respectively.

3.2.1
Neighborhoods
A neighborhood operator defines which solutions can be reached from a given solution, x. It is
necessary to limit the search and not consider all solutions in the solution space at once, since the
solution space is often overwhelmingly large. Furthermore, there are empirical observations that for
many problems, local optima are often relatively close to each other with respect to one or more
neighborhoods [10]. Hence, it may not be necessary to search the whole solution space to find good
solutions.
In the case of a TSPTW there are O(N!) different routes to consider. To see this, consider that a
route can be viewed as a string of stops starting and ending at the depot (stop 1). Let’s say there
are 5 stops arranged in the following way: s1 →s2 →s3 →s4 →s5 (of course also returning to the
depot with s5 →s1, which will not be written explicitly). The depot is fixed in the route, but there
are (5 −1)! = 4! ways to arrange the other stops. For large instances this solution space is way too
big to search all possibilities.
The two-phase heuristic utilizes two different neighborhoods, 1-shift and 2-opt which will be pre-
sented in sections 3.2.1.1 and 3.2.1.2.
3.2.1.1

From here on the convention will be that a move forwards is to a later spot in the route (larger
index), and a move backwards is to an earlier spot in the route (smaller index).

address the feasibility issue posed by the time windows constraints.", so the 2-opt neighborhood is
reserved for the optimization phase.
The perturbation function in Algorithm 5 performs a total of level 1-shifts, disregarding if these
1-shifts positively or negatively impact Zconstruct. 1-shifts are well-suited as perturbation operators,
since a 1-shift perturbs the solution roughly the same amount no matter the deletion and insertion
points. That is, the amount of deleted and inserted arcs are constant, regardless of the deletion and
insertion points. On the contrary, the amount of deleted and inserted arcs by a 2-opt operation in
an asymmetric TSPTW depends a lot on the deletion and insertion points. If deletion and insertion
are close, a 2-opt perturbs the solution relatively little compared to if they are far apart. In this
way, 1-shifts make sense as perturbation operators, as ten 1-shifts will perturb the solution roughly
ten times more than one, so the strength of the perturbation is easily controlled. Thus, 1-shifts are
chosen as perturbation operators in both the construction and optimization phase.

Note about pre-processing and predecessors
Some stops are simply forced to be visited before other stops because of their respective time
windows. If it is not possible to go from a stop i to a stop j and arrive before j’s time window
closes at bj, even if departing at the earliest possible time from i, then j must be visited before i.
That is, for all pairs i and j where the following equation holds
ai + Tij > bj,
j is a forced predecessor of i. Figure 3.2.3 illustrates the concept. The time window for stop 1
opens at a1 and closes at b1 and similarly for stop 2. T12 is the time it takes to travel from stop 1
to stop 2. No matter when you leave in the time window for stop 1, it is impossible to reach stop
2 before b2. Even if you leave at the earliest possible time from s1 at a1 as shown in the Figure,
you can’t make it to s2 in time. Thus, s2 is a forced predecessor to s1. When reading-in data, the
necessary predecessors for each stop is calculated and saved, so one can avoid trivially infeasible
routes.

adds a random stop which either

until all stops are added to the route.

3.2.4
Implementation issues
According to [5], "Two implementation issues are determinant in the efficiency of local search proce-
dures: the number of neighbor solutions evaluated and the fast computation of the neighbor solutions
costs". In order to evaluate as few neighboring solutions as possible, all local search procedures are
of first improvement type, which means the incumbent solution is updated as soon as an improve-
ment is found. This is in contrast to the best improvement type, where the whole neighborhood is
evaluated and the incumbent is then updated to the best solution in the neighborhood.
It should be made clear that the construction heuristic is a variant of classic VNS described in
section 3.1, much like the optimization heuristic is a variant of classic GVNS, using VND⋆instead
of VND. How the construction heuristic resembles and differs from VNS is addressed at the end of
section 3.2.4.1.
3.2.4.1
Implementation - construction
Before a neighboring solution is evaluated with respect to the objective function, it is first evaluated
if the solution is trivially infeasible with respect to predecessors, since this can be checked in O(n)
time. For example, if stop s is moved from index i = 5 to index j = 2 as in Example 3.2.2, it is
first checked if any of the stops from index 2 to index 4 is a predecessor to s. If any of them are
predecessors, the move is trivially infeasible and is not evaluated.
If a move is not trivially infeasible, it is evaluated with respect to Zconstruct. The constructive phase
uses a partitioning of the neighborhood when performing 1-shift local search to evaluate promising
neighbors before less promising neighbors.
The neigborhood is partitioned into the following 4
segments
1. moving violated stops backwards (to an earlier position),
2. moving non-violated stops forwards (to a later position),
3. moving non-violated stops backwards, and
4. moving violated stops forwards.
[5] claims that the first part of the neighborhood has the biggest chance of resulting in an improve-
ment in Zconstruct, followed by the second, then the third, and lastly the fourth. Therefore, they
are evaluated in this order.
The construction heuristic is a variant of VNS as the 1-shift local search uses these 4 segments as
4 separate neigborhoods when performing local search. However, the perturbation operator varies
from classic VNS, as it does not use 4 separate neighborhoods, but uses the united neighborhood
to provide as much randomness as possible.

the complete solution. Generally, the local searches in the optimization phase follows the evaluation

4.1.1
Lagrangian relaxation as a bound
Before we move on to the subgradient method, let’s turn the attention towards a very useful fact
about the Lagrangian relaxation. Namely, that it indeed is a relaxation and thus provides a lower
bound for a minimization problem and an upper bound for a maximization problem. From Wolsey’s
Integer Programming[22], theorem 2.1 states that a problem, (RP), is a relaxation of another prob-
lem, (OP), if
1. the set of feasible solutions to the original problem (OP) is a subset of the feasible solutions
to the relaxed problem (RP), and
2. for all feasible solutions in (OP), their objective value must be lower or equal (for minimization)
or greater or equal (for maximization) in (RP) compared to in (OP).
Consider our problems (P) and (LRP) from section 4.1, and let XP be the set of feasible solutions
to (P) and XLRP be the set of feasible solutions to (LRP). Then it is clear that XP ⊆XLRP , since
the only difference in constraints is, that Ax = b is removed from (LRP).
The second condition is also fulfilled, since for all x ∈XP it holds that Ax = b, so
ZLRP (λ) = min cx −λ(b −Ax) = min cx −λ · 0 = min cx = ZP ,
which means that all x ∈XP will have identical objective value in (P) and (LRP).
Since (P) and (LRP) are minimization problems, this means that ZLRP ≤ZP , since the optimal
solution to (LRP) must be at least as small as (P), and maybe smaller if there is an optimal solution
to (LRP), x⋆, where x⋆∈XLRP but x⋆/∈XP .
In our examples we have chosen Ax = b, since it matches the ATSPTW constraints. Similar results
for the Lagrangian relaxation can be shown for constraints Ax ≤b and Ax ≥b.

4.5.2
Time limit for sub-problems
One very important remark is, that often the sub-problems in the algorithms described in this sec-
tion are not solved to optimality due to their complexity and time requirements. Therefore, the
28

Master thesis

algorithms take as input an overall time limit and a time limit for their sub-problem. If a sub-
problem is not solved to optimality, the algorithms will use the lower bound provided by Gurobi as
objective value. This of course affects the performance of the algorithms and some of the conver-
gence results. For example, the convergence result for the α-step size only holds if the Lagrangian
relaxation sub-problem is solved to optimality. Nonetheless, it was deemed necessary to restrict
the sub-problems to only run for a specified amount of time, otherwise some sub-problems could
easily take hours to solve. The hope is that the lower bound and its corresponding solution will be
sufficiently close to the optimal value to still provide good results.
4.5.3
Concorde and integer input
Another implementation detail is, that Concorde only takes cost matrices with integer values as
input.
Consequently, when solving instances with non-integer values, all values are floored to
provide integer values to Concorde. To see that this is still a lower bound, consider the fact that
the set of feasible solutions, X, remains the same, but each solution in the set, x ∈X, has a lower
or equal objective value with ⌊˜C⌋as cost matrix instead of ˜C. A consequence of the floor operation
on the cost matrix is, that what may seem like a lower bound by Concorde may actually be the
optimal solution - that is, the optimal route just with a smaller objective value, since the costs of
traversing the route have been rounded down.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The main goal of this thesis was to implement algorithms to quickly obtain good solutions for the
asymmetric TSPTW with travel-time objective, but also to validate and quantify the performance
of the algorithms. With this goal in mind, the overall findings of this thesis are very satisfactory.
On average, the GVNS metaheuristic performs great for all 3 data sets. For the AFG and SPB data
sets, a time limit of 5 seconds results in an average gap to the best-known solution below 0.1% as
seen in Table 5.4. Thus, the GVNS metaheuristic is very reliable in finding near-optimal solutions.
For the AMCS data set there are no best-known solutions, but a time limit of 5 seconds results
in a average gap of 3.63% to the best lower bounds. This is also a satisfactory number, especially
considering that the best bounds were provided by Concorde for 72 out of 73 AMCS instances. For
a lot of instances, the solutions by Concorde may be infeasible with respect to time windows, which
means the optimal value is higher than the lower bound, and so the true gap is smaller.
The different approaches to lower bounds also perform well. This is mostly due to the variety of
lower bound algorithms and not one single, superior algorithm. Some algorithms perform well for
one data set and badly for others. The clear example of this is the relaxation into an ordinary
TSP solved by Concorde, which only performs well for the AMCS data set. For the AFG and SPB
data, Concorde only manages to find the best bound for one instance. An overview of the ability
of different LB algorithms to find the best lower bound for each data set was presented in Table
5.2. However, it should be noted, that the average gap between best-known solution and best LB
for the SPB data set is 5.71%, which can not be described as good, only acceptable.
The reason why Concorde performs so well for the AMCS data set is most likely due to the very
large time windows in the data set as seen in Table 5.1. The size of the time windows make the
solution space bigger and more difficult for a MIP solver like Gurobi to efficiently search through
the solution space. Since the subgradient algorithm and cutting algorithm rely on Gurobi to solve
sub-problems, this makes the AMCS data computationally difficult to handle. However, the benefit
of these very wide time windows is, that it makes the instances similar enough to a normal TSP, to
a point where the optimal TSP and TSPTW objectives (and solutions) aren’t very different.
Regarding the parameters for the different algorithms, it was found that the α-parameter in the
subgradient algorithm greatly impacted its performance. The α-parameter very explicitly controls

the step size, and it was found that different values of α performed best for different data sets. The
value of the β-parameter did not impact the performance of the subgradient method much, which is
a bit surprising. For the GVNS metaheuristic different combinations of levelMax parameters were
tested. The results slightly deviate from the results of da Silva and Urrutia in [5], but this may also
be affected by the kind of instances solved. The findings in Table 5.3 regarding levelMax suggests,
that perhaps levelMaxoptimize should be set higher than the proposed 8 in [5]. However, the results
are not crystal clear, and considering the raw results in Table 9.4, no one combination stands out as
the clear best. The results for different parameter combinations are very similar, and more testing
would need to be done before deciding to change parameters from the ones suggested by da Silva
and Urrutia.
Time is also a very interesting parameter. For the GVNS algorithm the time limit was set to 5
seconds. For most instances, e.g. the AFG and SPB data sets, this was more than enough. Also,
for most AMCS instances this time limit is sufficient, as 46 out of 73 have an average GVNS vs.
LB gap below 1% over 10 runs. However, some instances stand out as more difficult to solve. A
difficult instance, Set03, was for example shown in Figure 5.4.2, where Set03 benefit from a greater
run time. The AMCS instance Large01 would also clearly benefit from a greater run-time as it is
the only data set for which GVNS performs decidedly bad. Large01 is discussed in detail in section
6.1. Still, in the grand scheme of things, a greater time limit is not justified. Runs with a time limit
of 15 seconds were performed, and on average the increase in performance is very small, below 0.5
percentage point. Such a small increase does not justify a tripling in run time.
6.1
Further work
The ACMS instance Large01 is the only instance for which the metaheuristic does not obtain good
solutions. Large01 is artificially created by merging some other instances, so it is in some sense also
a natural outlier. Still, it is frustrating. It was tested that the reason for the bad performance for
Large01 was, that it took on average 35.7 seconds to obtain a feasible solution. So, a clear way
forward would be to implement a special construction heuristic to tackle instances such as Large01,
if similar instances are expected. An option could be to take advantage of the structure of the time
windows in the AMCS instances. One could partition the stops into sets with wide and narrow
time windows. Since a small percentage of the stops have narrow time windows, these are likely the
ones causing problems - maybe there are only a few feasible permutations of these. A specialized
construction heuristic might tackle the set with narrow time windows first, thereby greatly reducing
the size of the problem. Once a feasible permutation of the stops with narrow time windows are
found, it should be relatively easy to insert the stops with wide time windows and obtain a feasible
solution.
Another interesting option, not with regards to Large01, but in general, is to base the metaheuristic
off of the TSP solutions provided by Concorde. Concorde provides optimal solutions to the TSP
relaxations, and for some instances it provides them extremely fast. Run-times for Concorde for the
AMCS instances can be seen in the appendix, Table 9.5. For 39 out of 73 AMCS instances Concorde
solves the TSP relaxation within 2 seconds. It can be checked quickly, in O(n) time, if the solution
is feasible - and we’ve already seen in Figure 5.4.2 an example, Set03, that the TSP solution might
also be feasible with regards to time windows. In the case of a feasible TSPTW solution, this is a

close to optimal starting point for a heuristic (I write "close to optimal", since it is likely optimal,
but due to the rounding of input to Concorde, optimality is not guaranteed). If a few time windows
are not respected, the TSP route can still be used as a starting point for a metaheuristic, which
then tries to achieve feasibility while maintaining a good objective value. However, this approach
has the drawback that Concorde might use a long time to find an optimal TSP solution, e.g. 15
minutes for AMCS Set05-09, and Concorde doesn’t take a time limit as input.
While the metaheuristic generally performs really well, it would still be interesting to try and
optimize it further. Time window feasibility checks are implemented with complexity O(n), but
[19] suggests methods to reduce such feasibility checks to O(1) for TSPTWs with symmetric cost
matrices satisfying the triangle inequality. The cost matrices in this thesis are not symmetric, and
it is unknown if they satisfy the triangle inequality, but it would be interesting to see if some of
the concepts could be applied. However in this thesis, the main struggle has not been to find good
metaheuristic solutions, it has been to obtain good lower bounds. Therefore, the focus has not been
on this kind of advanced optimization.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In conclusion, the GVNS metaheuristic described in chapter 3 is able to quickly obtain good solutions
for the asymmetric TSPTW. For the AFG and SPB data sets, the implemented metaheuristic has
an average gap to the best-known solutions below 0.1% for 10 runs with 5 seconds each. For the
AMCS data set, the gap between average GVNS and best lower bound is 3.63% averaged over all
instances.
Regarding the lower bound algorithms presented in chapter 4, it can be concluded that these perform
vastly different depending on the data set. In total, the lower bound algorithms perform well, with
an avg. GVNS vs. best LB gap of 0.3% averaged over all AFG instances, 3.63% averaged over all
AMCS instances, and 5.76% averaged over all SPB instances. The relaxation into a normal TSP
solved with Concorde should be highlighted as an approach that works better than all other lower
bound approaches for the most important data set, the AMCS data.
Even though the overall performance of the implemented algorithms was good, it is also clear
that there are instances for which the algorithms don’t perform satisfactory. The clear outlier is
the artificially created AMCS instance Large01, which has an average GVNS vs. best LB gap of
91.53%. Ways to improve performance for instances such as Large01 was discussed in section 6.1.
