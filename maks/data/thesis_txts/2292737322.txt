Abstract
Studies have concluded that the climate is changing, which have an impact on
the future precipitation patterns. Denmark will experience an increase in extreme
weather events in the future, which will have impact on the infrastructure in relation
to the sewage systems and treatment plants. For design and operation purpose it is
essential to know the frequency and magnitude of the future rain events. The aim
of this study is to describe the future precipitation by a time series with sub-hourly
time steps, which can be used for future simulations.
In this study,
three diﬀerent historical observations are investigated at
representative spatial sites across Denmark.
SVK stations measuring intensities
in 10 minutes intervals and SYNOP stations accumulate the precipitation over
10 minutes intervals by weighing.
Furthermore, Climate Grid Denmark (CGD),
a nationwide grid divided by cells of 10x10 km, with interpolated values within
time resolution of 24 hours.
The hypothesis is that the SVK data leave minor
measurements out and therefore underestimates the extremes for the aggregated
precipitation between 1 and 24 hours.
Four areas across Denmark is chosen according to the annual mean and means of
daily extremes. The areas covers, North and West Jutland, Odense and Copenhagen.
The SYNOP data in Odense is subsequently excluded from further studies because
it was attached with errors.
For the present climate, the historical data are compared according the statistics;
mean, variance, skewness and Probability of Dry Days (PDD). An extreme value
analysis is done for the historical data with two methods. An empirical analysis
of a 100 year weather generated time series by the software RainSim and a model
distribution method by the software MIKE EVA.
Fifteen Regional Climate Models (RCM) are applied to the historical data to
predict the future precipitation patterns by the delta change method. A stochastic
disaggregation by simple random cascade is used to disaggregate the future time
series from 24 hours intervals down to a 11.25 minutes time resolution. The future
time series have been compared with the Regional Rain Curve (RRC) provided by
the Danish sewage committee.
For the present climate, small variations in the diﬀerent locations and data
observations are seen for both the extreme analysis and the basic statistics.
In
general, the mean and variance are largest during the summer, the skewness has
smallest variations across the year and PDD show the opposite trend than the mean.
For the future climate the change factor increase in the mean from Sept. - June,
and the opposite trend is seen for PDD. An increase is seen for the variance, and
for the skewness an increase occurs during the summer. The extremes increases by
a factor, which corresponds well to the sewage committee’s recommendations, and
most in North Jutland.
A linear relation for the moment scaling was found, which is in agreement with
earlier studies. The seasonal trend in Copenhagen was opposite than for the other
sstations which should be investigated in further studies. The disaggregation was
concluded to most suﬃcient for the SYNOP data down to 11.25 min time steps,
though less bias was seen for 22.5 minute time steps. The disaggregated time series
showed a cloud of future simulations which include the values of the RRC estimations
with a standard and high climate factor. Two time series were chosen to describe
the future precipitation in Denmark with standard and high climate factor. In the
future an increase for the annual precipitation was found and an increases of the
variance and skewness during the summer.

Abstrakt
Klimaændringer har en klar indvirkning p˚a fremtidens nedbørsmønstre, viser
forskning. I fremtiden vil Danmark opleve en stigning af ekstreme vejrhændelser.
Disse vil f˚a stor indvirkning p˚a infrastrukturen - og især aﬂøbssystemer og
rensningsanlæg. For at sikre den bedst mulige drift og det mest optimale design,
er det vigtigt at kende størrelsen og frekvensen for fremtidens nedbørshændelser.
Form˚alet med dette studie er at beskrive fremtidens klimanedbør med tidsserier
med tidsintervaller p˚a under en time, for p˚a den m˚ade bedre at kunne simulere
fremtiden.
I dette studie er tre forskellige historiske datasæt undersøgt i forskellige
repræsentative dele af Danmark. SVK-stationer m˚aler nedbør som intensitet over
10 minutter, og SYNOP-stationer akkumulerer nedbøren over 10 minutter. Klima
Grid Danmark (CGD), som er et nationalt grid opdelt i celler af 10x10 km,
hvor nedbøren bliver interpoleret over tidsintervaller af 24 timer.
Hypotesen
er, at SVK-m˚alerne registrerer for mange mindre m˚alinger som værende nul og
underestimerer derfor sammenlagte intervaller mellem 1 til 24 timer.
Fire omr˚ader p˚a tværs af Danmark er blevet undersøgt p˚a baggrund ˚arlig
nedbør og middelværdierne for ekstrem døgnnedbør. De valgte omr˚ader er Nord
-og Vestjylland, Odense og København. SYNOP-stationen i Odense viste sig dog at
være behæftet med fejl, og omr˚adet blev derfor udelukket for videre studier.
For det nuværende klima er de historiske data blevet sammenlignet i forhold til
middelværdier, variansen, skævheden og sandsynligheden for en tørvejrsdag (PDD).
Desuden har jeg brugt følgende to metoder til at estimere ekstremværdier:
en
empirisk analyse af en regnvejrsgenereret 100-˚ars tidsserie med software programmet
RainSim og en metode baseret p˚a modelfordeling af regnvejret med MIKE EVA.
I specialet er der brugt 15 regionale klimamodeller (RCM) til at forudsige
fremtidens nedbørsmønstre med delta-forandringsmetoden.
Fremskrivningen af
klimaet er anvendt p˚a alle de historiske datasæt.
Til at opdele fremtidens
nedbørsmønstre fra tidsintervaller p˚a et døgn ned til 11.25 minutter, har jeg brugt en
stokastisk model med en simpel tilfældig kaskadeopdeling. De fremtidige tidsserier
er sammenlignet med regionale regnvejrskurver, som er beregnet i samarbejde med
Spildevandskomiteen.
For det nuværende klima er sm˚a variationer set for de forskelige historiske
stationer og beliggenhed b˚ade med hensyn til de statiske værdier og i ekstrem
analysen. Konklusionen er at middelværdierne og variansen er højest om sommeren,
skævheden varierer mindst over ˚aret, og PDD viser den modsatte trend i forhold til
middelværdien.
For fremtidens klima stiger middelværdien fra september til juni, og det modsatte
ses for PDD. Variansen og skævheden stiger om sommeren. For ekstremerne har det
vist sig, at de øges med en faktor svarende til spildevandskomiteens anbefalinger. I
Nordjylland har stigningen af ekstrem nedbør vist sig at være større end i de andre
regioner. En lineær sammenhæng er set for moment-skaleringen, hvilket stemmer
overens med tidligere studier.
For København viste undersøgelsen en modsat
sæsonbaseret trend end de andre stationer, ˚arsagen burde undersøges nærmere i
videre studier. Opdelingen af tidsskridt viste sig at være tilstrækkelig korrekt ned
til 11.25 minutter for SYNOP stationerne, dog med en mindre skævridning af data
ned til 22.5 minutter. Den statistiske opdeling af tidsskridt vidste en sky af tidsserier,
som dækker de regionale regnvejrs kurver, b˚ade med høj og standard klimafaktor.
To tidsserier blev udvalgt til at beskrive fremtidens nedbør i Danmark med henhold
til høj og lav klima faktor. Disse tidsserier viser, at ˚arsnedbøren stiger og variansen
og skævheden stiger hen over sommeren.

Introduction
The climate is changing and so is the pattern of global precipitation.
In the 21st
century the precipitation will increase in some areas and decrease in others (IPCC,
2012).
In Denmark we will most likely experience an increase, especially in case of
extreme precipitation. Such extremes have been seen several times in Denmark the last
few years (Vejen, 2010; DMI, 2011, 2014c). These events have caused problems in urban
areas as the capacity of the drainage systems have been exceeded. Adaptation according
to the climate change are essential to cope with such extremes in the future, but it is
challenging as those systems have a long technical lifetime and high construction cost
(Gregersen and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2011).
The risk of ﬂooding in Denmark is often illustrated as a product of the probability
multiplied with the value of the aﬀected area (Jensen et al., 2014). In this study the
future precipitation is studied to clarify the probability of the extreme events for future
climate. The increase in precipitation, especially in case of extreme events, will lead
to problems in regards to ﬂoods in mostly urban areas where the majority are paved.
Furthermore, it is in the urban areas where the density of the economic value are largest
due to the high density of private and cooperate buildings and important infrastructure.
Knowledge about the future precipitation is essential for adapting the urban
infrastructure to the climate.
This knowledge is used as input for sewage system
simulations to verify where existing systems should be improved and for designing new
systems. To minimize or avoid ﬂooding knowledge of the most probable future rainfall
is essential to plan the urban sewage systems most suﬃcient.
The simulations can
furthermore designate alternative ﬂood areas with low economic damage such as soccer
ﬁelds or other green areas. Through a better estimation of inﬂow to treatment plants,
the operation can be improved according to diﬀerent levels of performance. The future
prediction of the rainfall, with sub-daily time resolution will improve the design of the
treatment plant, according to the future climate in accordance with precipitation input.
Ideally, time steps at 30 min or even 10 min resolutions would be optimal for drainage
systems (Sunyer et al., 2014).
In this study, the historical precipitation is studied across Denmark (North Jutland,
Western Jutland Odense, and Copenhagen). In all areas three diﬀerent measurements
(SVK data, SYNOP, and Climate Grid Denmark (CGD)) are studied to see how
they describe the precipitation patterns with diﬀerent time resolutions.
The three
measurements are compared to see which describe the precipitation patterns in the best
way and which should be used for future climate prediction. The analysis of the present
climate were used to choose the best observation for predicting the future rainfall.
For the future climate, ﬁfteen RCMs are used as input to generate time series with
a resolution of 24 hours. A stochastic disaggregation by simple random cascade were
used to disaggregate the time series from time steps at 24 hours down to 11.25 min. The
aim is to choose a historical time series, which describe the present climate best and
a future simulation which describes the future pattern the best. With those criteria I
have studied how a future time series can be constructed to give the best estimation of
extremes in the future. Earlier studies showed both good and bad estimations of extremes
and basic statistics with the disaggregation method (Onof and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2009;
2
==PAGE:9==
Sunyer et al., 2014). This is the background of studying the three diﬀerent historical
measurements to conclude how a future time series can be estimated most accurately.
1.1
Objectives
Three main objectives form the basis of this thesis are listed below, together with the
sub objectives.
Furthermore, this project builds on earlier studies by Sunyer et al.
(2014), who showed that the disaggregation method did not perform suﬃcient when using
historical SVK data to disaggregated future CGD. Whereas Onof and Arnbjerg-Nielsen
(2009) have shown good results for the disaggregation method on SVK data. With these
conclusions in mind it was an aim to discover which data describe the future climate best
according to the Regional Rain Curve developed by the sewage Committee of Denmark
(IDA, 2014), and how a future time series with sub-hourly time steps, can be estimated.
The three objectives are as follow:
1. Compare the three types of historical data (SVK, SYNOP and CGD), according
to their description of the present climate at representative spatial locations in
Denmark.
2. Invest the ﬁfteen regional climate models description of the future, according to
basic statistics and extremes for the chosen spatial distribution of Denmark.
3. Test stochastic disaggregation by random cascade model for SVK and SYNOP
historical data.
- Compare the future disaggregated time series with the Regional Rain Curve
with standard and high climate factor.
- Select the best future time series of sub-hourly time resolutions to describe
the future precipitation in Denmark by spatial distribution.

Precipitation Data
In this thesis, three diﬀerent observations in four areas of Denmark were studied (SVK
data, SYNOP, and CGD). The reasons for selecting the four areas are described in section
2.1, where the diﬀerent spatial patterns in precipitation of Denmark is elaborated upon.
The diﬀerent historical data are described in section 2.2. The SVK and SYNOP data
are intensities and accumulated at 10 minutes resolution respectively, and the CGD is
daily accumulated values. The hypotheses of the diﬀerence between the two stations is
that the SVK data does not measure all small rain events, and therefore rainfall events
with a duration of 1 hr - 6 hr is under estimated Sunyer et al. (2014).
2.1
Area selection
The precipitation patterns vary across Denmark, according to Mikkelsen et al. (1999);
Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al. (2006) the Waste Water Committee in Denmark has developed a
tool to construct a regional rain curves and CDS rain series for design purpose. Earlier
Denmark was divided into an east and west part based on mean annual precipitation
patterns.
New calculations by Gregersen et al. (2014), conclude that the annual
precipitation cannot be expressed by a mean average for west and east Denmark. By
looking into the annual precipitation and means for the average daily extremes, divided
in 10x10 km grid in Denmark, patterns for speciﬁc regions in Denmark are seen. Figure
2.1 shows how the two measurements are distributed across Denmark.
The annual
precipitation, Denmark can be divided more or less into three areas: West Jutland, Mid
Jutland and Fyn, and Zealand, Lolland and Falster. The annual precipitation is ∼850,
700 and 600 mm respectively, see Figure 2.1a. For the mean daily extremes the north and
eastern part of Denmark has a higher level than the rest, except areas of the southern
islands, see Figure 2.1b. By looking at data for North Jutland, Western Jutland, Fyn
and Zealand (Copenhagen) the diﬀerent patterns of the precipitation would be covered
due to annual precipitation and daily mean extremes. The four chosen SVK stations
are placed in the diﬀerent regions and plotted in Figure 2.2 and information about the
data are listed in Table 2.2.
These stations have also been a focus in Sunyer Pinya
(2014) PhD thesis, and the same extracted data are used. The closest SYNOP stations
to the respectively SVK stations are chosen to compare the two diﬀerent measurements.
DMI has also measured accumulated daily precipitation in a 10x10 km spatial resolution
across Denmark. The grid data for where the stations are located is used for this study
(Scharling, 2012). For the data in West Jutland, the grid data between the two stations
are used, and for North Jutland data for two grids are used. An overview of the name
of the diﬀerent stations and grids are listed in Table 2.1 and Figure 2.2 showing the
placement of the stations.

2.2.1
SVK Stations
The SVK stations are installed by the cooperation between the Danish committee of
sewage (Spildevandskomite´en, (SVK)) and the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI).
The ﬁrst stations were installed in 1979 and covered more than 40 urban areas in high
time resolution (Mikkelsen et al., 1999). The main usage the data have been design and
analysis of the sewage system response to extreme weather events in Denmark. Before
the end of 2012 the total amount of measuring station in 143, and the operation reliability
was 99.7 % (Thomsen, 2013).
The rain gauge in the SVK stations is a RIMCO tipping bucket containing what
corresponds to 0.2 mm rainfall and measures the rain intensity.
The gauge registers
the number of tips every second and records to DMI. The accuracy is ± 1 mm/hr and
3 % at 200 and 380 mm/hr respectively, useful up to 500 mm/hr (Mikkelsen et al., 1999;
Observator, 2014). In appendix B.1 the station and rain gauge are illustrated. In this
study the data is calculated as accumulated mm with 10 minutes resolution. There has
only been extracted data when two tips in a row are measured. The SVK data are

3
Methodology
The aim oﬀthis study is to compare precipitation patterns with diﬀerent spatial and time
resolution for three diﬀerent sets of data (SVK, SYNOP and CGD). The methodology
describes how I have evaluated the data. First, the basic statistics, for the historical in
the four areas of Denmark, are evaluated through RainSim (standalone software). Then,
an extreme analysis evaluation is studied by the empirical method and the distribution
models by the software MIKE EVA. Furthermore, RainSim is used to simulate a 100 year
synthetic stochastic rainfall to estimate a 100 year event by an empirical method.
To project the future climate a change factor for ﬁfteen RCM’s, provided by DHI
from the research done by van der Linden P. and Mitchell (2009), are used to predict
the future time series. The projected time series are disaggregated by a random cascade
model to describe sub-daily time steps.
The methods are described in the following
sections:
1. The software RainSim, described in Section 3.1, is used for statistic analysis of the
data and simulating synthetic stochastic rainfall series.
2. Extreme value analysis for both present and future climate, described in section
3.2.
3. Loading CF’s for diﬀerent grids of Denmark and multiplying them to the basic
statistics (Section 3.3).
4. The disaggregation of the time resolution of future time series is described in
Section 3.4.
3.1
Statistic analysis of rainfall and Simulation of time series by the
weather generator RainSim
In this study the weather generator software RainSim is used for generating synthetic
rainfall for the future and present climate by analysing basic statistics for time series
(Burton, 2008).
The analysis of basic statistics is used to compare diﬀerent spatial
resolutions for the diﬀerent historical data. The simulation of synthetic rainfall is used
to generate 100 years of rainfall for both present and future climate. For the present
climate the 100 years simulations are used to estimate larger events by the plotting
position method described in Section 3.2. For the future, all the time series are simulated
as a 100 year time series including the CF described in Section 3.3.
The simulation of precipitation time series are estimated using the statistic
properties: mean, variance, skewness and probability of dry days (PDD). RainSim have
a lower limit for the time resolution at 1 h.
The RainSim software consist of three functions, which require three syntax (-a, -f, and
-s):
1. Statistical analysis of the precipitation observations (-a)
2. Fitting the model to a set of statistics (-f)
3. Simulating a time series from a set of ﬁtted parameters (-s)

Extreme value analysis and distribution models
The objective for an extreme value analysis of a certain rainfall is to quantify the
probability of an extreme precipitation event exceeds a given time interval.
The
probability exceedance (p) for a T-year event every year is given as:
p = P{X > xT} = 1
T
(3.1)
where xT is the magnitude of the T-year event measured in mm per time unit and T
is the return period in years for the xT level.
In this study, two methods to estimate p are used: the empirical and the model
distribution. The main advantages of using the model distribution is that it can estimate
the return period beyond the time series record length. This is important because the
probability for 100 year event is wanted to describe, and the maximum record length
of the stations are 34 years. For the empirical method the 100 year RainSim simulation
of present climate is used to describe how RainSim simulates the return periods. The
simulation in RainSim is described in Section 3.1.
The ﬁrst step in the extreme value analysis is to extract the extremes in the time
series for the diﬀerent stations. In this study an average of three events per year is used,
i.e. Partial Duration Series (PDS). One extreme is extracted for each event and the time
of disaggregation between to events are the time resolution for the time series. A script
(Extreme evets extraction.py) developed by Hjalte J. D. Sørup are developed to extract
the extreme events by these criteria. The script is uploaded to CambusNet together with
the report, see Appendix A for the structure of uploaded ﬁles.

3.4
Stochastic disaggregation by a simple random cascade model
Disaggregation of the future precipitation time series is important to describe time series
of sub-daily time resolutions.
Earlier studies by Onof and Arnbjerg-Nielsen (2009);
Sunyer et al. (2014) have shown diﬀerent results for the stochastic disaggregation by
the simple random cascade model.
Onof and Arnbjerg-Nielsen (2009) showed ﬁne
results for the disaggregation for a rain gauge located in Holbæk, approximately 60 km
west of Copenhagen.
The rain gauge was a SVK station with a time resolution of
10 minutes.
Though, it was recommended to look into spatial scaling, and how the
method performs according to future climate simulations. Sunyer et al. (2014) found
15
==PAGE:22==
that the disaggregation underestimate the high extremes and that the cascade parameters
from diﬀerent areas have minor impacts on the extremes. Furthermore, it was found that
the disaggregation method perform badly according to the variance, regional average and
standard deviation. In Sunyer et al. (2014) the CGD were used to generate the 100 year
simulation by RainSim and the SVK data for the moment estimation. I will use the
SYNOP and SVK data for both the 100 year simulation in RainSim and for the moment
estimation for disaggregation to evaluate which data gives the most realistic values. The
disaggregated data for a 100 year simulation by the weather generator RainSim will be
referred to as WGD (Weather Generator Disaggregation). The results are compared
with the Regional Rain Curve ver. 4.0 based on the recommendation from Gregersen
et al. (2014), provided and downloaded from IDA (2014).
The method builds on the assumption that the relation between the moments of
the historical data between diﬀerent aggregated time resolutions will remain in data
simulated. The moment estimation is carried out on all spatial stations for the SVK
and SYNOP data at 10 minutes resolution.
To test whether the method is working
the disaggregation was done on the aggregated historical data, and afterwards on the
100 year simulations by RainSim for the present climate on daily time resolution.
The method used in this study is a stochastic disaggregation by a simple cascade
model and the aim is to disaggregate the time resolution from 24 hours values down to
a sub-hourly time resolution. I have chosen to use a Canonical cascade model rather
than Micro-canonical cascade, since Molnar and Burlando (2005) found that this cascade
model describes precipitation better, especially for extremes.

belong to the same statistical distribution. Furthermore, the canonical model preserves
the mass on the average in the disaggregation and for this study the intermittent

Extreme value analysis
The results of the extreme value analysis for both the empirical method and the
distribution models are presented in this section. For the distribution model methods
the largest advantages are that large return period can be simulated by ﬁtting the data
to a distribution model, though uncertainties are attached to the values.
Empirical method
An extreme value analysis of the three SVK stations with the empirical method is
illustrated for the diﬀerent durations in Figure 4.3 for a 100 year time series simulated in
RainSim. It is seen that for the 10 minutes and 1 hour storm the intensity of the return
period is quite similar up to 15 years in all areas. For the 6 hours and 24 hours duration
the changes appear from 10 and 3 years respectively.
RainSim has a limit of 1 hour
to analyse a rainfall, therefore the historical data is plotted for 10 minutes resolution.
The empirical method describes return periods up to the length of time series, where
the highest T is 23 and 34 years for North Jutland, and West Jutland and Copenhagen
respectively for the 10 minutes resolution. The largest diﬀerence between the diﬀerent

4.5
Change factors for the future climate
In this study the change factor (CF) is used for calculating the projected climate with the
Delta Change method. In this study 15 diﬀerent climate models are invested and a set
of CF is extracted from each model corresponding to the Climate Grid Denmark (CGD)
for each station (Sunyer et al., 2014). The CF is extracted in MATLAB from the RCM
in respect to the grid closest at the station described in Section 3.3. The 15 models are
listed in Table 2.5 where it is described which global and regional model there is used.
The CF generated by the ﬁfteen models are based on daily time resolutions and so are
the future generated time series. Section 4.7 describes the result from disaggregating the
resolution into sub-daily time steps.
The CF for each indices are plotted in Figure 4.14 for all three areas (North and
West Jutland, and Copenhagen). The CF’s change over the year and are diﬀerent for
the ﬁfteen RCMs, but similar according to the location. For the mean-CF the majority
of the models increase over the year, with the highest increase from Feb. - May. By
looking at the average mean for all models the same trend is seen, a decrease in mean
precipitation in August and the highest values Nov. - Feb. with a smooth change down
to August.
Some of the models have a decrease of the mean value for few or most
months, especially RCM 2, 3, and 11 describe a decrease in the mean precipitation. The
largest decrease appears from July - Sept. The trend for those RCM’s according to mean
precipitation and PDD are plotted in Figure 4.15.
The average variance-CF for all models are larger than one in except July and Aug.
at West Jutland and Copenhagen respectively. For average CF the maximum increase
is seen from Sept. - April. A CF larger than one will result in larger deviations from the
mean precipitation, which could cause in more and/or severe extremes. The diﬀerent
models do not follow the same trend as the average variance-CF. For the single models
the maximum and minimum occur at diﬀerent months, and for the average the minimum
occurs from June - Aug. with a gradual increase and decrease from these months. It is
not the same models which describe the general minimum and maximum values at the
diﬀerent areas as for the mean-CF.
For the skewness the single models have the largest variations, though the average
values have the smallest variations over the year, compared to the other CF’s. From Oct.
to May the average values are very close to one which indicate zero or minor changes
in the skewness. During the period from May - Sept. the CF is above one which would
occur in a more skewed precipitation for the future climate during the summer. The
largest single values in each model also occur during the summer period.
The average CF for probability of dry days have more or less the opposite trend as for
the mean-CF. A decrease from September to May and an increase the opposite month,
for North Jutland it is only in August an increase appears for the average PDD-CF.
From Sept. - May it will result in less chance of a dry day for most months. During the
summer the more dry days will appear which will increase the precipitation over fever
days. As with the mean-CF where RCM 2, 3 and 11 describe the largest decrease it
is the same models which forecast the largest increase of PDD. The trend for the three
RCM’s is plotted in Figure 4.15, where the clear relation between low mean result in
high PDD. None of the other RCM’s has a clear trend in presenting the lowest or highest
value for any indices and the CF for PDD have smallest change of the four indices.

4.7
Disaggregation of future time series
The disaggregation method is used on the SVK and SYNOP data to describe the future
climate for smaller time resolution than 24 h, which are available for the CF. A test of
the method’s validation is shown to see how the disaggregation performs. The present
test using the SYNOP stations 6031 and 6068 performed the best according to the RRC
and therefore these historical data are used to project the future climate as described in
Section 4.7.2.
4.7.1
Test of method for present climate
To test if the method describe the disaggregation of the future time series well, a test
of the present climate is constructed. First the disaggregation is constructed with the
historical data and then with the 100 year weather generated time series by RainSim
(WGD). The results from Station 6031 are presented and plots of the statistics for the
other stations are shown in Appendix I.3 and I.4.
The disaggregation is made from a time resolution of 24 hours to 6 hours, 90, 45, 22.5
and 11.25 min. The log-moment, as a function of the log-time resolution and tau as a
function of the moment, is plotted in Figure 4.18 and 4.19. The parameter values for
β, σ and τ are listed in Appendix I.2. The moment scaling is found to be linear with
an upward trend for q with range of [2:4] and opposite for q = 0 and almost constant
for the ﬁrst moment.
The se results are similar to the studies done by Molnar and
Burlando (2005). The moment scaling during the winter is smaller than the summer for
the four last moments and opposite for the zero moment. For the two stations located
in Copenhagen an opposite trend is seen, which indicate the stations measure less rain
in the summer, and they underestimate the extremes, which appear during the summer.
The plot also shows a good relation between the ﬁve moments and the tau estimation
done by the MKP function (EQ 3.17). In this thesis a constant parameters through n
is assumed. A better ﬁt could possibly be achieved with a variable parameter. With
a moment estimation from 24 hours down to 45 minutes a better linear relation is seen
which indicate that varying the parameters could optimize the method. The moments
estimation plots down to a time resolution of 45 minutes and the parameters are listed
in Appendix I.1. For station 30314, with the moment estimation down to 45 minutes
resolution the same seasonal trends are seen as for the stations in Jutland, see Figure I.1.
This indicates that the method starts having a large bias at time steps of 11.25 minutes.
Statistics for the disaggregation of the historical data
Figure 4.20 shows how the disaggregation simulations describe the mean, variance and
skewness by monthly averages for the two time resolutions (22.5 min and 11.25 min).
The method simulates the mean values most accurately for both time resolutions. For
the variance more variations between the simulation are seen. The average skewness
simulations diﬀer the most from the observations of the three statistics. The best ﬁt
of the skewness is seen for a time resolution of 22.5 min. The simulations overestimate
the skewness and underestimate the variance which in general are the main trends for
all stations and all time resolutions.

4.7.2
Disaggregation of the future climate
In this section the disaggregation, of the projected future climate by the delta change
method for all 15 RCMs and for the two SYNOP stations 6031 and 6068, is described.
The 24 hours time series for the future is simulated as described in Section 3.3 and
presented in Section 4.6. The two stations are chosen on behalf of the results in Section
4.7.1 and are used to estimate the future time series with the time resolutions 11.25,
22.5, 45, 90 minutes, 6 and 24 hours. The time resolution of 11.25 minutes has larger
uncertainties. Two time series are chosen according to less diﬀerence from the future
RRC with the standard and high climate factor according to 9 criteria with the least
squared error as the selection criteria. The time series is ﬁtted to a 2, 10, and 100 years
return period with a duration of 45 minutes, 6 and 24 hours with lowest error calculated
as ∑((RRCi −Xi)2/RRCi). In Figure 4.24, the future simulations, of the WGD data from
station 6031 and 6068, are compared with the future RRC with the standard and high
climate factor. The chosen time series are plotted in the Figure, marked with  and 
depending on the ﬁt for the standard or high climate factor respectively. The spectrum of
simulations increase as the return period increases, which support the notion that short
record lengths have diﬃculty in projecting large return periods. For North Jutland RRC,
with the high climate factor of T = 2 and 10 years, is higher than the spectrum describes.
This indicates that the RCMs, which are used in this thesis are more conservative than
RRC simulates. For West Jutland it is only the high RRCs with a duration of 6 and
24 hours for a T = 2 year which are larger than the cloud describe and the diﬀerence is
small. The precipitation as a function of the time period plotted for all time resolutions
for both stations in Appendix I.7. The spectrum for station 6068 is larger and therefore
includes the RRC data with long durations better and the average future WGD is very
close to the high RRC data as well.
In Figure 4.25, the 150 WGD data for both locations are plotted with the nationwide
RRC data, calculated with an annual precipitation of 686 mm and an average climate
grid value of 26.15 mm/day. As more WGD time series are included the cloud expands
and only the 24 hours duration with a T = 2 year is outside the cloud. The same time
series for the standard and high climate factor in North and West Jutland are the best
to predict the nationwide climate as well.

5
Discussion
In the discussion the results are discussed according to the objectives of this thesis. The
three spatial locations with the three diﬀerent historical data are discussed according to
their description of the present climate. The data for the present climate is discussed
in relation to the basic statistics and extreme events. In the future climate the diﬀerent
RMC’s models are discussed and how they predict the future.
For the disaggregation the method’s performance is discussed and how SVK and
SYNOP data describe the present and future climate. Furthermore, it is discussed how
the disaggregated simulations perform according to earlier studies, and how the results
can be used.
5.1
Spatial resolution and historical data
As seen in Figure 4.11 the basic statistics do not vary much from the diﬀerent
measurements (SVK, SYNOP and CGD). Some exceptions were seen, especially for
the variance which diﬀers a up to four times in the SYNOP data for West and North
Jutland. To predict the future climate on behalf of the diﬀerent historical data short
record length has large inﬂuence on the two statistics variance and skewness. According
to the estimation of extremes, few large events have a major inﬂuence on the extreme
value analysis. Therefore, these events were excluded from further analysis, though the
risk of underestimating the extremes would increase. Though the results show a better
estimation of extremes by excluding the values compared with the RRC.
Some variation in the statistics is seen between the diﬀerent locations in Denmark.
Therefore, it is reasonable to describe the RRC according to the location. Though, the
statistics cover the same spectrum of values, and one time series to predict the rainfall
in Denmark is suﬃcient, with some biases.
For the extreme value analysis by MIKE EVA it is clear that estimations with a
100 year return period are attached with major uncertainties for the historical data with
a record length <13 years (Station 6068 and 6184). Weather generated time series of
100 years describe large return periods better compared with the RRC. For the extreme
analysis plotted as an IDF curve for the three areas in Figure 4.13, very small diﬀerences
in between the areas are seen. For estimation of extreme events, an average time series
for Denmark would therefore be suﬃcient.
By looking at the diﬀerent historical data, the SVK data underestimate the extremes
with a duration of 1 and 6 hours. This was not observed in the SYNOP data. The
SYNOP data should be used for estimating the extremes even when large uncertainties
is seen for the 100 year events. To describe precipitation in Denmark more suﬃcient it
is required to ﬁnd a SYNOP station with longer record length or combine the diﬀerent
SYNOP stations, as the spatial diﬀerence are small. If estimations of extremes are done
as an average across the spatial distribution, the uncertainties concerning T = 100 year
extreme events could decrease. Then biases for the basic statistics will probably increase,
as they vary across the spatial distribution.

==PAGE:62==
5.2
Future Climate
In this thesis ﬁfteen RCMs were used to predict the future rainfall. Some assumptions
are attached to these models according to the future development. The question is if
the models predict the future with a spectrum suﬃcient to cover extremes which are
considered as reasonable. In this thesis the future extremes are compared with RRC, as
recommended by Arnbjerg-Nielsen (2008) for design purposes. It is seen from this study
that the 15 RCMs have diﬃculties with describing the events corresponding to RRC with
a high climate factor, especially in West Jutland. When choosing a future time series
describing extremes equivalent to the RRC estimations, it would be more suﬃcient to use
climate models that describe the future less conservative. Though, large uncertainties
are always attached to simulations when trying to predict the future climate.
The diﬀerence of the change factor (CF) according to the spatial distribution across
Denmark is minor. Therefore, it could be reasonable to assume a set of average CFs for
the whole of Denmark to predict one time series for the future rainfall. A nationwide
time series, together with a deviation formulae for diﬀerent parts of Denmark, could
be a suﬃcient assumption to predict future climate. It would simplify the results, but
advantages of only using one time series in the future are considerable.
5.3
Disaggregation
The disaggregation of time series is done to see if future time series, with sub-hourly
time steps, can predict reasonable statistics and estimations of extreme events.
In
comparison with the present climate, the statistics, mean, variance and skewness, have
the same yearly variations and magnitudes down to a time resolution of 11.25 minutes as
observations. Some biases are seen in the small time steps, but the disaggregated data
describe the statistics suﬃciently.
By comparing the estimated extremes of the WGD present data, the SYNOP data
estimate the 90 min and 6 hours durations best according to the RRC. The assumption
was that the SVK data underestimate those extremes described by Sunyer Pinya (2014)
as a ’belly’ on the curve. In that study CGD data were used for the aggregated 24 hours.
I have argued that the method is suﬃcient if the disaggregation are used on the same
aggregated data as the moment estimation. However the method, is not suﬃcient to
describe areas of Denmark without sub-hourly historical data by disaggregate CGD
which is a nationwide grid. By looking at the results from Figure 4.23, the SYNOP data
describe the data best down to 22.5 minutes. Therefore, it would be optimal not to use
the time series of smaller time steps without considering the attached uncertainties. For
the estimation of a return period for T = 100 year, the SYNOP data have diﬃculties as
well. It would be more adequate to investigate a method to combine several historical
observations to lengthen the historical record period, which would lower the estimated
uncertainties for less frequent storms.
In Copenhagen an opposite trend is discovered for the estimated moments between
summer and winter, for both the SVK and SYNOP data. It should be addressed why
this trend is seen. If it is caused by the selection of measurements and too many large
measurements from the summer is excluded by the selection criteria. Or if the Stations
are measuring wrongly due to calibration or placement errors, or the trend in Copenhagen
56
==PAGE:63==
in fact diﬀer from the rest of Denmark. This investigation is beyond this study, but it
is essential to describe the future rainfall in Copenhagen more suﬃciently. The moment
scaling is not completely linear with a constant set of parameters for all n’s in the
disaggregation. Therefore, it would probably improve the method if constant parameters
from the 24 hours time steps down to 11.25 minutes is not assumed. Investigation of how
many steps the parameter estimation should be divided in, to improve the estimation
signiﬁcantly is not done in this study, but is recommended for future investigations.
Even some biases are seen, the results show that the extremes are located appropriately
within the spectrum of disaggregated extremes. With these limitation in mind the chosen
time series can apply to the two stations 6031 and 6068 up to T = 100 year and a time
resolution down to 22.5 minutes.
In the future WGD simulation, the standard and high RRC are almost covered by the
’cloud’ of simulations, though the RCMs did not cover these extremes completely. The
RRC with high CF are located in the upper part of the cloud and outside the spectrum
for few long durations. As discussed earlier, the ﬁfteen RCMs predict the climate more
conservatively than the RRC with a high Climate factor. For a more accurate time series
with high climate factor, it would be advantageous to use climate models with larger
change factors. Furthermore, the time series are selected by a certain set of parameters,
and they will predict the future more accurately for these parameters. If other return
periods, or durations are chosen the time series should be selected by those criteria
instead. A weighting of the diﬀerent parameters could also be discussed according to
the usage of the future time series.
The selected time series to describe the future with a high climate factor estimate
the extremes in West Jutland better than for the whole Denmark. Therefore, it would
decrease the biases if a present time series which describes the precipitation better
nationwide, than station 6068, was found.
For this it would be necessary to study
the 41 SYNOP stations in Denmark to choose this present time series. As argued the
change factors from the three locations have minor variations, which advocates that one
future time series could be constructed for disaggregation into sub-hourly time steps.

6
Conclusion
By looking at the accumulated monthly precipitation in the four areas across Denmark
the conclusion is that the measurements for Odense are attached with error in the
SYNOP station and Odense was excluded for further investigations.
For the three other locations, the three diﬀerent types of historical data all describe
the accumulated precipitation well, compared with the international climate normals.
The trend regarding the basic statistics, mean, variance, skewness and Probability of
Dry Days (PDD), across the year, does not vary signiﬁcantly between the diﬀerent types
of historical data or spatial locations. In general, the mean precipitation and variance
were showed to be largest during the summer.
The skewness has a less clear trend
across the year, but the largest values were measured during the summer period. The
PPD have the smallest variations across the year. The SYNOP stations had less dry
measurements than SVK for a time resolution larger 1-2 hours, the CGD had less dry days
in West Jutland and Copenhagen. Furthermore, I found that few large measurements
have major inﬂuence of the variance and skewness for data with small record lengths.
In the extreme value analysis, data with shortest record (SYNOP stations) estimated
the extremes worst, for T = 100 years, with a clear overestimation compared to the RRC,
especially for the longest durations. The WG method estimated the extremes adequately
except for the 100 year events at a 24 hours duration in station 6031 and at 6 and 24 hours
duration for station 6068. The advantages of using the WG method can be attributed to
the fact that it generates time series for future and disaggregation simulations as well.
As earlier studies have concluded, the SVK stations underestimate the durations of 1
- 6 hours because some small measurements are recorded as zero. Furthermore, minor
diﬀerences are seen for the return period due to spatial distribution.
A clear trend is seen for the average, of the ﬁfteen Regional Climate Models (RCM),
future simulations. For the mean precipitation, an increase from October to June are
seen and a decrease or no change are seen from July - September. An increase of up
to 20 % are seen for the variance, highest during the summer. For the skewness none
or minor increases are seen from September to May and a larger increase during the
summer. The PDD show the opposite trend as the mean, and the trends across the year
are more or less equal for all stations. Though West Jutland shows the largest increase
for the skewness during the summer. The extremes increases for all the return periods
and stations except station 6031 at T = 100 year. The largest average increase is seen
in North Jutland and all changes correspond well with the recommendations from the
Sewage Committee of Denmark. The ﬁfteen RCMs do not completely cover the extremes
correspond to the RRC with high climate factor, therefore less conservative models could
be studied for better ﬁts of future disaggregated time series with a high climate factor.
The weather generated disaggregation (WGD) show that the if the same historical
data are used for the moment estimation and the disaggregation it is possible to
disaggregate down to 11.25 min with minor biases.
A linear trend for the moment
scaling are seen with higher values for the summer. At the two Copenhagen stations,
the opposite trend was seen which should undergo further investigation.
With the
disaggregation, the SYNOP stations show the best results up to a return period of
100 year and for time steps down to 22.5 minutes.

Appendix B
The measurements systems
In this appendix are the three types of historical data described according to the used
rain gauge and etc.
B.1
The SVK stations
DMI and the sewage committee monitors 143 SVK stations in Denmark (Thomsen,
2013). The rain gauge in the SVK stations is a RIMCO tipping bucket containing what
corresponds to 0.2 mm rainfall and measures the rain intensity.
The gauge registers
the number of tips every second and records to DMI. The accuracy is ± 1 mm/hr and
3 % at 200 and 380 mm/hr respectively, useful up to 500 mm/hr (Mikkelsen et al., 1999;
Observator, 2014). The Station consist of a rain gauge as which are located as optimal
as possible and a recording box to send the data to DMI. In Figure B.1 a picture of a
SVK station is present and the rain gauge. The rain gauge consist of a funnel and the
tipping bucket to measure the rainfall.
(a) A SVK station
(b) RIMCO rain gauge

the recording box is located (IDA, 2015). To the right a RIMCO rain gauge is illustrated, it
consist of a funnel and a tipping bucket (Observator, 2015).

Appendix C
RainSim Syntaxes
In this Appendix the examples for each syntax is shown to clarify the methodology using
the software RainSim.
C.1
Statistic analysis (-a)
The analysis syntax requires two input ﬁles: *.ts and *.template which both are *.txt
documents. The *.obs ﬁle is the precipitation time series for the given station with a
certain time step.
Syntax for the Analysis Argument:
-a
obs.ts
<yyyy> <mm> <yy> <mm> <t> statsTemp.template stats.obs.sta -dir demo
The obs.txt indicate the given analysed observations. <yyyy> <mm>: indicates
the beginning of the time series <yy> <mm>: indicated the length of the time series,
<t> indicates the time step in hours. *.template are the template input and *.sta is
the generated output statistic ﬁle. ’-dir demo’ indicates the folder where the ﬁles are
placed.
Syntax for the statistics template:
Statistics

The ﬁrst column show which statistic there is analysed, third is the time step in
hours and the fourth is the priority of which statistic are most important. Column 7 -
