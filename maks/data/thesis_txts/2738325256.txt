Abstract
In recent decades, the photosynthetic pigments known as carotenoids have been shown to pos-
sess substantial benefits for human health. Among these, fucoxanthin has risen as a promising
candidate for commercial production, as it is the most abundant carotenoid and a variety of its
positive effects have been discovered in numerous trials. Unfortunately, the large-scale produc-
tion of fucoxanthin is hindered by the lack of knowledge regarding its biosynthesis. However,
Cao et al. (2023) have recently described the enzyme responsible for the last step of its synthe-
sis: the fucoxanthin synthase CRTISO5. This step forward opens the way for the production
of fucoxanthin in heterologous hosts, using synthetic biology to improve its feasibility.
In this work, CRTISO5 has been introduced into the orthologous organisms Phaeodactylum
tricornutum, Nannochloropsis oceanica and Saccharomyces cerevisiae in order to characterise
its expression and assess the suitability of these three organisms as potential fucoxanthin cell
factories. To ensure a thorough exploration, various engineering techniques, such as protein
truncation, genomic integration and episomal expression, have been applied to four different
CRTISO5 homologs. After successful engineering of the three hosts, several hurdles hindering
the analysis of fucoxanthin production have been discovered. Possible regulatory effects may
have disrupted the expression of CRTISO5 in P. tricornutum and N. oceanica, while S. cere-
visiae showed consistent expression of CRTISO5. As S. cerevisiae does not natively synthesise
carotenoids, a feeding assay was designed to assess in vitro activity of CRTISO5 in cell lysates.
All in all, this work examines the challenges that face the production of fucoxanthin in het-
erologous cell factories, highlighting the need for further research focused on the elucidation
of regulatory mechanisms upcoming cell factories.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Novel applications of photosynthetic pigments
Awareness of the detrimental effects on human health of chemically synthesised compounds
has had an impact on the current setup of food production. Thus, natural pigments that are
able to modify the colour of food products, the utmost important food quality, have received
much attention in recent decades (Janiszewska-Turak et al., 2016).
This has sprung an increase in natural colour research, which has brought insight into the
effects of these compounds on human health (Di Salvo et al., 2023).
It has been found
that the majority of compounds responsible for pigmentation in plants and microorganisms
present activities that can range from anticarcinogenic to antidiabetic, with their antioxidant
capabilities being the most renowned. Amongst these, the pigments known as carotenoids are
clearly highlighted.
1.1.1
The relevance of carotenoids
Carotenoids are a group of coloured lipophilic molecules present in all photosynthetic organisms
and some non-photosynthetic organisms (Zhang et al., 2023). They range from yellow to red
in colour and are the most abundant pigments in nature, with more than 1200 individual
structures described in The Carotenoid Database (Yabuzaki, 2017). Almost all carotenoids
originate from phytoene, a C40 molecule, through the condensation of several isoprene units
and both cis and trans isomers can be found in nature (Figure 1.1). Despite this, carotenoids
can be generally classified into carotenes, which are plain hydrocarbons, and xanthophylls,
which contain at least one oxygen molecule (Gong and Bassi, 2016).
Additionally, carotenoids can be classified based on their main function. Primary carotenoids
are tightly associated with the light-harvesting mechanisms to expand the spectrum of ab-
sorbable light by photosynthetic organisms. On the other hand, secondary carotenoids are
accumulated in lipid vesicles under stress conditions, dissipating, for example, the excess en-
ergy in the case of light stress (Begum et al., 2016).
Irrespective of the particular role of each carotenoid in the cell metabolism, isopentenyl py-
rophosphate (IPP) and dimethylallyl pyrophosphate (DMAPP), the common carotenoid pre-
cursors, may be synthesised through two distinct, and well known, pathways (Gong and Bassi,
2016). In the case of some animals, yeasts, archaea and some Gram-positive bacteria, these
compounds are obtained by means of the mevalonate (MVA) pathway. Alternatively, in algae,
cyanobacteria and most Gram-negative bacteria, the methylerythritol phosphate (MEP) path-
way is followed (Figure 1.2) (Ren et al., 2020). Interestingly, certain marine algae and higher
plants have both pathways at their disposal, though the MVA pathway is cytosolic, while the

From IPP and DMAPP, phytoene is synthesised through a series of condensations, and, after

Humans are not able to generate our own carotenoids, yet they are necessary for the proper
function of the metabolism, such as the role of β-carotene as provitamin A (Olson, 1989).
For this, they are obtained through the diet. Furthermore, since the late 1990s, the potential
beneficial bioactivities of carotenoids have been studied, showing they possess a variety of
positive effects on several human cell lines, model mice and clinical trials (Chuyen and Eun,
2017). It has been shown that fucoxanthin possesses an anticarcinogenic effect, with a dosage
of 45.2 µM reducing the cell viability of human leukaemia cells (HL-60) to 17.3% (Hosokawa
et al., 1999). Moreover, Hosokawa et al. (2004) later compared the anticarcinogenic effect of
fucoxanthin to β-carotene and astaxanthin, and found it to be superior. This work supported
the previous efforts by Kim et al. (1998), who found that fucoxanthin is chemopreventive in
mouse colon carcinogenesis models. A dosage of 0.01% fucoxanthin from Hijikia fusiforme
to drinking water reduced the number of putative pre-neoplastic aberrant crypt foci, one of
the end-point marker lesions, by 25%. On the other hand, the potential anti-obesity effect of
carotenoids has been illustrated in vivo in mice. Ikeuchi et al. (2007) showed that astaxanthin
supplementation in a mouse high-fat diet decreased liver weight, liver and plasma triglycerides,
and total cholesterol levels. Additionally, it is known that astaxanthin increases insulin sensi-
tivity through the activation of the β insulin receptor in male Swiss albino mice (Arunkumar
et al., 2012). Furthermore, the effect of one of the first carotenoids, lycopene, has also been
thoroughly studied, showing that it reduces blood and urine glucose levels, and pancreatic
damage in the STZ-induced diabetic rat model, amongst others (Zhu et al., 2020). Lastly, fu-
coxanthin has proven to inhibit the development of lipopolysaccharide-induced uveitis in rats.
This has been linked to an inhibition of inflammatory mediators, like nitric oxide, and the
suppression of pro-inflammatory marker expression in RAW 264.7 cells (Shiratori et al., 2005).

1.1.2
Fucoxanthin and its elusive synthesis
Fucoxanthin is a primary xanthophyll first described in 1914 by Willstätter and Page in Dictyota,
Fucus and Laminaria, three brown macroalgae. In fact, brown algae, Phaeophyceae, are the
main producers of this compound, though it is also found in some green algae (Lourenço-Lopes
et al., 2021). The main function of fucoxanthin is to aid light harvesting during photosynthesis
as its vital role in the fucoxanthin-chlorophyll proteins (Schaller-Laudel et al., 2015).
It can be seen that fucoxanthin has received much attention in recent years, due to the range
of beneficial bioactivities it possesses. Additionally, through several in vitro and in vivo studies
with model animals, fucoxanthin has also been found to be an antioxidant, cytoprotective,
anti-obesity, neuroprotective and antidiabetic (Lourenço-Lopes et al., 2021). Sangeetha et al.
(2009) showed that fucoxanthin is a better alternative to β-carotene to relieve lipid peroxida-
tion in cases of retinol deficiency in rats, modulating the activity of catalase and glutathione
transferase in the liver. Furthermore, the cytoprotective activity of fucoxanthin has been ex-
plored in vivo through its protection of the retina against photoinduced damage, proving better
than lutein (Y. Liu et al., 2016). Moreover, Hu et al. (2018) have demonstrated the neuropro-
tective potential of fucoxanthin against cerebral ischemic/reperfusion injuries in rats. Lastly, as
with astaxanthin, the antidiabetic and anti-obesity potential of fucoxanthin has been explored
in high-fat fed mice (Maeda et al., 2009). With all these studies, it should be noted that no
substantial evidence indicates that fucoxanthin is toxic, showing that the mean lethal dose is
well over 2 g/kg of body weight (Beppu et al., 2009).
All in all, this carotenoid has a great number of positive effects on human health. Together
with the fact that fucoxanthin comprises 10% of the total amount of carotenoids in nature, it
becomes clear that it is a molecule with a lot of potential for applications that will positively
impact our society (Matsuno, 2001). In fact, some preliminary studies on the economics of
fucoxanthin highlight the high value of this compound, estimating its price to range from
USD40 000 to USD80 000 per kilogram (Joel, 2016). Furthermore, its market is projected to
reach USD113.5 million by 2026 (H. Sun et al., 2023).
1.1.3
Current advances in fucoxanthin production
Despite this great focus on its bioactivities, the current production of fucoxanthin is quite
challenging. The traditional way of producing carotenoids, chemical synthesis, is not available
for a molecule as complex as fucoxanthin (Pajot et al., 2022). Furthermore, this strategy
presents serious drawbacks concerning the safety of the generated compounds and the observed
shift towards the use of natural products by consumers (Gong and Bassi, 2016).
Currently, commercial fucoxanthin is sourced from various macroalgae, including Laminaria
japonica, Undaria atifida, Eisenia bicyclis, Alaria crassifolia, Ishige okamurae and Laminaria
ochotensis (Peng et al., 2011; H. Sun et al., 2023b). However, this production scheme is not
fully economically feasible at a large scale, due to the slow growth of these algae, the low fu-
coxanthin productivity they possess, the toughness of their cell wall, which hinders extraction,
and their susceptibility to seasonal fluctuation (Terasaki et al., 2009). Therefore, research has
focused on other potential avenues for fucoxanthin production. Microalgae, in particular, show
potential to fill this niche, since they are fast-growing and can be separated from seasonal
effects (Khaw et al., 2022). Furthermore, species like Phaeodactylum tricornutum, Tisochrysis
lutea and Cylindrotheca closterium present a high fucoxanthin productivity, which makes them
suitable candidates for commercial production (S. Wang et al., 2021). It should also be noted
that microalgae offer a simplification of downstream processing in comparison to macroalgae.
In fact, P. tricornutum, a model diatom, has been highlighted as a potential cell factory for the
production of secondary metabolites (Butler et al., 2020). Algatechnologies Ltd. has success-
fully introduced a fucoxanthin food ingredient based on P. tricornutum: FucoVital (Abu-Ghosh

However, despite the clear advantages of microalgae, research efforts are still focused on the
improvement of fucoxanthin production, as it is still not fully viable. The effect of various
culture conditions, such as light, CO2, nitrogen and salinity have been explored (Leong et al.,
2022; McClure et al., 2018). Furthermore, different culture modes such as heterotrophy and
mixotrophy are proposed (R. Yang and Wei, 2020). Lastly, different alternatives for downstream
processing, mainly focused on fucoxanthin extraction, such as conventional solvent extraction,
supercritical extraction or ultrasound assisted extraction, have been tested (Kanda et al., 2014;
Saini and Keum, 2018).
Apart from these traditional optimisation strategies, synthetic biology provides an additional
avenue for the improvement of fucoxanthin production. Some preliminary studies have shown
that random mutagenesis is able to increase fucoxanthin production by 69.3% in P. tricor-
nutum (Yi et al., 2018). Moreover, the utilisation of a heterologous host, such as yeast or
alternative microalgae, can further exploit the advantages of these microorganisms in an in-
dustrial setting. Thus, the genetic engineering of strains or other production chassis stands as
a viable path towards a better production platform.
1.2
Recombinant production of secondary metabolites
As it can be seen, the production of fucoxanthin by conventional means, either chemical
synthesis or natural extraction, shows various challenges towards its implementation in the
market. Thus, a third alternative can be explored, that of recombinant production. Through
the expression of the necessary pathway in a better characterised organism, the synthesis
of fucoxanthin can be finetuned to yield a commercially viable system.
Furthermore, the
advantages of a controlled fermentation provide a simplification of the downstream process,
which allows for better recovery of the product (Cataldo et al., 2020).
1.2.1
The road to fucoxanthin production: challenges
Despite the immense amount of research destined to carotenoids and their function, a commer-
cially feasible fucoxanthin producer has yet to be found or engineered. This is due to several
challenges arising in this particular case.
First and foremost, there is a lack of knowledge regarding the native mechanism of synthesis
for fucoxanthin. Its proposed synthesis starts with β-carotene, which is transformed into zeax-
anthin by β-carotene 3,3’-hydroxylase (BHY) (Figure 1.3). Zeaxanthin epoxidase (ZEP) then
generates violaxanthin, followed by violaxanthin de-epoxidase like 1 (VDL1) that tautomerizes
it to neoxanthin. From this point on, the enzymes responsible for the subsequent transfor-
mations until fucoxanthin have not been fully elucidated. However, the enzyme responsible
for the conversion of diadinoxanthin to allenoxanthin has been identified as VDL2, a paralog
of VDL1. Furthermore, the conversion from haptoxanthin to phaneroxanthin is attributed to
zeaxanthin epoxidase 1 (ZEP1) (Bai et al., 2022).
Recently, the fucoxanthin synthase, CRTISO5, was described by Cao et al. (2023), together
with a proposal for an almost complete pathway for fucoxanthin synthesis (Figure 1.3). CR-
TISO5 is a 58 kDa globular protein closely related to carotenoid isomerases (CRTISO) that
catalyse the isomerisation of cis C=C bonds from 7,9,9’,7’-tetra-cis-lycopene to form all-trans-
lycopene (Figure 1.4A) (Breitenbach et al., 2001). However, CRTISO5 is not able to perform
this reaction, only facilitating the hydration of phaneroxanthin to fucoxanthin with the use of
the reduced flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) cofactor. Additionally, it is notable that, despite
previous hypothesis considering dinoxanthin as an intermediate, CRTISO5 does not seem to

Evidently, the limited knowledge regarding fucoxanthin biosynthesis severely hinders any biotech-
nological efforts towards a fucoxanthin cell factory. Not only this, the regulation controlling
the underlying pathway is also unexplored, as evidenced by some initial attempts at increasing
fucoxanthin production with substantial differences in outcomes (McClure et al., 2018; Seo

1.2.2
Main achievements in carotenoid synthesis
All in all, fucoxanthin production in a heterologous host has not been yet achieved. With
this in mind, one must look at the previous attempts made towards carotenoid production
in heterologous hosts. As it is common, the first attempts were carried out in Escherichia
coli. Misawa et al. (1990) seem to be the first to achieve production of zeaxanthin in E. coli
when they characterised several genes responsible for bacterial carotenoids. From here, further
optimisation attempts have been conducted, aiming at improving titres with several metabolic
engineering strategies (Table 1.1) (Chen et al., 2021). Most notably, Yang and Guo (2014)
achieved a β-carotene titre of 3.2 g/L through the combination of both the MVA and MEP
pathways.
However, it appears that a substantial research focus has been placed on the development of
the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae as the main chassis for carotenoid production, possibly due
to its already established use in the food industry (Table 1.1). Thus, Yamano et al. (1994) were
able to introduce the bacterial carotenogenic genes in S. cerevisiae to achieve a β-carotene
producing strain.
Again, further strategies have been explored and systems with a better
control of carotenoid production have been developed. Su et al. (2020), for example, propose
a growth-phase-dependent regulatory mechanism that reduces metabolic burden to allow a
maximum lycopene concentration of 1.48 g/L. Moreover, violaxanthin, one of the precursors
in the fucoxanthin pathway, has been successfully synthesised by Cataldo et al. (2020).

Host candidates for fucoxanthin production
As it can be seen, the previous work conducted with S. cerevisiae establishes a foundation for
carotenoid expression in this host. Moreover, the status of S. cerevisiae as a model organism,
which has been widely used across industry, makes it a safe regulatory choice (Parapouli et
al., 2020). Lastly, the genomic engineering of S. cerevisiae is relatively easy, especially with
CRISPR-Cas9 mediated toolkits such as EasyClone MarkerFree (Jessop-Fabre et al., 2016).

On the other hand, P. tricornutum also provides a promising host, given its native fucoxanthin
production capabilities, as discussed previously (Butler et al., 2020). In this case, the main
challenge for fucoxanthin production, that of its biosynthesis, can be avoided, focusing only
on the optimisation of its yield. Furthermore, novel engineering strategies such as the uLoop
assembly, which use standardized type IIS DNA cloning methods to establish modular assembly
procedures, have been developed to successfully engineer this model diatom (Pollak et al.,
2020).
Lastly, in order to make use of the robust genetic background that microalgae offer, together
with their inherent carotenogenic properties, the heterokont Nannochloropsis oceanica ap-
pears as a suitable alternative. This microalga’s potential for lipid accumulation has made
it a focus of synthetic biology strategies, making it a model organism for microalgae chas-
sis (Ye et al., 2024). Furthermore, recent advances have successfully improved its engineering
through electroporation, paving the way towards its establishment as a chassis for carotenoid
production (Poveda-Huertes et al., 2023).
1.3
Objectives and scope
The objective of this work is to study and characterise the new fucoxanthin synthase, CR-
TISO5, in order to pave the way for a fucoxanthin producing cell factory. Thus, its activity
will be assessed in three promising heterologous hosts: P. tricornutum, N. oceanica and S.
cerevisiae. Apart from assessing the feasibility and complexity of fucoxanthin production in
these organisms, this work also aims at comparing four closely related homologs of CRTISO5
in order to determine their activities within the hosts. The CRTISO5 of Fragilariopsis cylin-
drus, Phaeodactylum tricornutum, Prymnesium parvum and Thalassiosira pseudonana will be
studied.
Moreover, given the existence of various approaches available for recombinant expression,
several cloning strategies will be tested.
For N. oceanica, an integrative strategy will be
compared with episomal expression. Furthermore, adjusting for the fact that S. cerevisiae does
not have a chloroplast, expression of the full-length protein will be compared to a truncated
version without the signal peptide in this host. Additionally, CRTISO5 will be fused to the
green fluorescent protein (GFP) so that subcellular location can be determined in yeast. Lastly,
due to the lack of native carotenoid production in S. cerevisiae, the activity of CRTISO5
will be assessed through a feeding assay with the hypothesised precursor dinoxanthin. For
this, Amphidinium carterae extracts will be used, as this algae substantially accumulates this
compound (Johansen et al., 1974).

for high-light selection of high-light resistance mutants at 26 ◦C and air supplemented with

2.5
Feeding assay
Yeast lysates were prepared according to Geisler et al. (2016). Yeast strains were grown for 72 h
in SC-URA media. Cultures were concentrated 10 times in TE buffer (50 mM Tris HCl, 1 mM
EDTA, pH 8) with cOmplete protease inhibitor (Roche Diagnostics). Afterwards, aliquots were

at 400 rpm. Immediately after the reaction, the lysates were freeze dried and extracted as
described below.
As a positive control for this assay, a S. cerevisiae strain capable of transforming β-carotene
into zeaxanthin was engineered with a plasmid provided by Dany Liu. This strain was fed with
5 mg/mL β-carotene (SimgaAldrich) in methanol following the protocol described above.

Carotenoid extraction
Carotenoids from P. tricornutum were extracted via the acetone method (Jinkerson et al.,
2024). Biomass equivalent to 10 OD750 was harvested and extracted with 700 µL pure acetone.

Carotenoids from N. oceanica were extracted through following an internally validated proto-

Carotenoids from S. cerevisiae were extracted following a chloroform protocol internally es-

roform:methanol. The organic phase was evaporated and the pigments were resuspended in
8:1:1 methanol:DCM:water with apocarotenal via sonication.
Carotenoids from A. carterae were extracted with either the acetone method or the chloro-
form:methanol method as described above. Additionally, either a freezing-thawing cycle or
freeze drying until full water removal were used as pretreatment steps to break the cells.

Analytical methods
2.7.1
Fluorescence measurements
Absorbance and fluorescence measurements were conducted in a SpectraMax M Series plate
reader with clear and dark 96-well plates, respectively. For all measurements, OD was kept
below 0.5 for adequate fluorescence reading. mVenus was excited at 510 nm and emission was
read at 530 to 535 nm, tdTOMATO was excited at 554 nm and emission read at 570 to 581 nm
and mEGFP was excited at 485 nm and read at 520 to 525 nm. Chlorophyll content was also
measured through autofluorescence by excitation at 440 nm and reading the emission at 565
to 670 nm.
Additionally, marker fluorescence was assessed through confocal microscopy with a Leica TCS
SP5 X microscope. Laser power and detector gain were adjusted between samples to achieve
adequate imaging.
For all samples, an x63 objective with immersion in water was used.
Additionally, optical microscopy was conducted in a Leica DM100 microscope, with a x40 air
objective.
2.7.2
Liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LCMS)
Pigment extracts were analyzed using an Ultimate 3000 UHPLC+Focused system (Dionex
Corporation) coupled to a Bruker Compact APCI-QTOF-MS (Bruker) system. The method of
analysis was adopted as previously described (Bijttebier et al., 2014). Briefly, samples were sep-
arated on a ACQUITY UPLC HSS C18 SB Column (100 Å, 1.8 µm, 2.1 mm x 100 mm; Waters)
maintained at 35 ◦C with a flow rate of 0.5 mL/min. Injection volume was 5 µL. Mobile phase
consisted of A: 5 mM ammonium acetate dissolved in 50:22.5:22.5:5 water:methanol:acetonitrile:
ethyl acetate and B: 50:50 acetonitrile:ethyl acetate. The liquid chromatography (LC) pro-
cedure was outlined as following: 0–0.1 min, 10% B; 0.1–0.8 min, linear increase from 10 to
30% B; 0.8–20 min, increase 30% to 91% B; 20–20.1 min, increase from 91% to 100% B;
20.1–20.4 min isocratic; 20.4–20.5 min linear decrease from 100% to 10% B; 20.5–23 min iso-
cratic. Mass spectra (MS) were acquired in positive ion mode over a scan range of 50–1200
mass-to-charge ratios (m/z) and 2 Hz sample rate, with the following settings for MS and
atmospheric pressure chemical ionization (APCI): Capillary voltage, 4000 V; endplate offset,
500 V; corona, 4 µA; vaporizer temperature, 400 ◦C; dry gas temperature, 250 ◦C; dry gas flow,
5 L/min; and nebulizer pressure, 3 bar. Acquired chromatogram data were calibrated using an
internal standard, apocarotenal. Data analysis was performed with DataAnalysis 4.3 (Bruker).
All standards used can be seen in Supplementary Figure B.1.

Statistics
All measurements were done with technical triplicates. ANOVA and Tukey’s statistical tests

TukeyHSD/). Results are presented as average with error bars denoting one standard deviation.
In all cases, ** considers a p-value<0.01 and * considers a p-value<0.05, higher p-values are
considered as outside of the statistical significance.

Chapter 4
Discussion
4.1
Overexpression of CRTISO5 in P. tricornutum
The expression of CRTISO5 in P. tricornutum presented certain challenges. Despite previous
reports on the adequacy of the uLoop system to assemble and express target genes in this
diatom, only two out of four homologs were successfully cloned (Pollak et al., 2020). The
setbacks experienced in this assembly may be attributed to an incorrect curing of SapI and
BsaI target sites in the sequences of the genes. Alternatively, the lack of positive colonies
found after assembly may also be due to a low ligation eﬀiciency, which is supported by the
low amount of E. coli colonies observed after transformation. Subsequent literature research
showed that Diner et al. (2016) describe how certain genetic elements within a plasmid can
drastically change its conjugation eﬀiciency. This may explain the low eﬀiciency observed when
conjugating the PpCRTISO5 and TpCRTISO5 constructs into P. tricornutum.
Regarding the confirmation of CRTISO5 expression, mVenus has been proposed as a good
fluorescent marker for extrachromosomal expression of genes, supporting its use (George et
al., 2020). Moreover, confocal microscopy has been previously used to successfully determine
mVenus expression in P. tricornutum (Windhagauer et al., 2021).
However, these reports
use liquid cultures when determining fluorescence. In this work, the TpCRTISO5 strain was
sampled from a plate, due to time constraints. This might have had an effect on extrachro-
mosomal expression due to a physiological changes, especially when taking into account the
clear morphology change in comparison to the WT (Borowitzka and Volcani, 1978). In fact,
the pc49202 promoter is the native polymerase II promoter in P. tricornutum and, thus, low
fluorescence intensity might be linked to genetic regulation of the promoter, which may be
susceptible to growth conditions (Taparia et al., 2022).
When looking at the carotenoid profiles of P. tricornutum, the lack of pigments in the WT
stands out, especially given that the final extract possessed a distinct colour. Here, it must
be noted that the only available LCMS system used throughout this work has been recently
installed and undergone several troubleshooting services. Therefore, the lack of results may
be due to problems within the analytics. Furthermore, despite some pigment detection in the
TpCRTISO5 samples, similar issues may have occurred in this sample. Nevertheless, the peak
corresponding to fucoxanthin can be observed, though quantifiable comparison with the WT
is not possible.
4.2
Equipping N. oceanica with the fucoxanthin synthase
The integrative strategy towards CRTISO5 cloning proved successful in introducing the gene
of interest. The setbacks experienced with the cloning of the native TpCRTISO4 gene may
Characterization of CRTISO5 in orthogonal heterologous hosts
27
==PAGE:36==
have been related to the fact that it was directly extracted from T. pseudonana biomass,
thus affecting the quality of the biobrick.
Sequencing results showed appropriate biobrick
amplification, though degradation may have occurred (Supplementary Figure A.3). On the
other hand, the episomal strategy did not yield similar results. Liu et al. (2020) have showed
that N. oceanica can be acclimated to resist a hygromycin B concentration of up to 350 µg/mL.
Thus, the working concentration of 50 µg/mL used for transformant selection in the protocol
might be too low for adequate screening. Possibly, during successive culturing, the N. oceanica
used as parent strain may have acquired tolerance to hygromycin B. Of course, a longer
investigation is necessary to find the underlying causes and set up an optimised protocol.
When looking at CRTISO5 transformants, a reduced chlorophyll a content may be due to
growth under the pressure of the zeocin antibiotic, as this marker has an effect on photo-
synthesis (Lin et al., 2017). Notwithstanding, the significant increase in chlorophyll a of the
Tp_int strain differs considerably from the other strains. Moreover, Tp_int is the only strain
that presented significant tdTOMATO fluorescence through the plate reader. These two re-
sults may have been caused by the fact that the Tp_int liquid culture was inoculated with
significantly more biomass than other cultures, in order to establish adequate growth. This
was due to the very slow growth of Tp_int ni plates. On the other hand, the inoculation of
certain N. oceanica cultures with variable biomass may cause the loss or limitation of construct
expression due to certain regulatory mechanisms, possibly activated by light stress (Wei et al.,
2020).
The more detailed overview obtained through confocal microscopy points to a certain level
of tdTOMATO expression in all strains, including the WT and strain Fc_epi. Given that the
latter do not contain the fluorophore, the observed intensity may be an artifact. However, in
the subsequent round of imaging, one cell with clear tdTOMATO expression can be observed.
Thus, once again, CRTISO5 expression may be confirmed in N. oceanica, though consistent
and widespread expression may not occur.
The absence of fucoxanthin in the carotenoid profile of the strains can be explained as a
consequence of this variable expression. It may be the case that fucoxanthin production does
occur in certain cells, but this remains below detection levels in the greater culture. On the
other hand, fucoxanthin biosynthesis may have been hindered by the lack of the confirmed
precursor, phaneroxanthin, as this carotenoid has not been identified in N. oceanica. In fact,
the only compounds found in N. oceanica that belong to the proposed fucoxanthin biosynthesis
pathway are neoxanthin and diadinoxanthin (M. Liu et al., 2022; Y. Sun et al., 2022). Lastly,
it must be noted that, most probably, the peak coeluting with fucoxanthin in Fc_epi is an
analytical error, given that no new compounds are observed, together with the circumstances
regarding LCMS analysis discussed above.
4.2.1
Culture conditions can trigger carotenoid accumulation in N. oceanica
N. oceanica has been reported to shift its carotenoid profile under suboptimal growing condi-
tions, such as light or salt stress (Solovchenko et al., 2014; B. Wang and Jia, 2020). Despite
carotenoid content decreasing under these conditions, the proportion between carotenoids
shifts. It appears that this may have been the case in the described plate. Given that other
plates stored under the same conditions did not present similar phenotypic changes, light stress
may be discarded as a possible trigger. Apart from this, a proper experiment to determine the
cause of this phenomenon must be conducted.
4.3
Assessment of CRTISO5 functionality in S. cerevisiae
As shown before, the EasyClone system provides a toolbox to quickly and eﬀiciently engineer S.
cerevisiae. The issues experienced with the cloning of the truncated FcCRTISO5 gene were due
28
Characterization of CRTISO5 in orthogonal heterologous hosts
==PAGE:37==
to unsuccessful Gibson assembly, possibly due to degradation of the backbone. Nevertheless,
integration for all other constructs was successful.
It can be seen that expression varies greatly, and significantly, between colonies when integra-
tion in the same site occurs, as exemplified by the three positive controls. Thus, confirmation
of adequate gene integration does not seem to be enough for adequate strain selection. Fur-
ther screening of positive colonies might yield strains with even more mEGFP fluorescence.
Nonetheless, adequate integration entailed significant fluorescence in the plate reader for some
colonies, and the more thorough confocal imagery showed expression in all strains.
Regarding mEGFP expression through time, previous studies have shown gene expression in-
creases through time (Brejning and Jespersen, 2002; Li et al., 2000). These works only describe
protein expression during the first 30 hours at most. Additionally, S. cerevisiae biomass report-
edly only increases during the first 30 hours of a batch culture (Coppella and Dhurjati, 1989).
Therefore, it seems that maximum protein content for the feeding assays was achieved before
the 48 h mark. Nevertheless, this work shows that the stability of expression is very high in
most cases, since mEGFP expression remained constant for all strains except Tp_c and Pp_tr.
In spite of the adequate engineering of S. cerevisiae, the validation of the feeding assay pre-
sented several significant setbacks. Firstly, LCMS issues can be clearly seen in the run of the
positive control, as absorbance drastically dropped momentarily. As mentioned before, this
might be due to the troubleshooting with the equipment that occurred concurrently with this
project. Secondly, the purified β-carotene solution used as the feed does not show a peak for
β-carotene. This might also be related to the aforementioned LCMS error. However, posterior
search of the Certificate of Analysis showed that the used batch was released in November
2022 (Product number: C9750, Batch number: MKCR9474, Purity: 92.9%, SigmaAldrich).
Product purity was determined by absorbance, which is susceptible to interference from com-
pounds with a similar structure. As the positive control only accepts β-carotene as a substrate,
the presence of other carotene-like structures may have had unexpected results. Moreover, it
must be noted that the use of the pathway in the β-carotene strain has been validated before
in S. cerevisiae.
The last setback is, of course, the absence of dinoxanthin in the A. carterae extract. Looking
more closely at its growth and extraction, it can be seen that, in case of no LCMS issues, the
growth of A. carterae on marine media is not adequate for carotenoid production. In fact,
the contamination later discovered in the culture might have significantly displaced the algae.
Thus, F2N proves to be adequate for further use. Moreover, it appears that freeze drying
may have been more effective as a pretreatment than a freeze-thawing cycle during carotenoid
extraction.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this work, the newly discovered fucoxanthin synthase, CRTISO5, has been characterised in
three heterologous hosts. The challenges encountered in establishing a fucoxanthin cell factory
have been explored in order to assess the complexity of future progress. For this, CRTISO5
has been introduced following different engineering strategies in the desired hosts, quantifying
its expression and determining its impact on carotenoid composition.
It has been found that the uLoop system shows limited eﬀicacy in the engineering of P. tricor-
nutum. Possibly due to current expression mechanisms relying on genetic elements which lack
characterisation, CRTISO5 expression has not been confirmed. Further advances in synthetic
biology tools for P. tricornutum will undoubtedly reduce the limitations of diatom engineering.
On the other hand, the integration of CRTISO5 has not equipped N. oceanica with fucoxanthin
biosynthesis. Despite the successful engineering of this microalgae, variable expression may
have hindered the production of fucoxanthin. Additional research focused on the characterisa-
tion of fucoxanthin biosynthesis may provide further insight into the constraints underlying its
production in N. oceanica.
Lastly, S. cerevisiae has been proven to be a very good host for synthetic biology, demonstrating
why this choice of organism is favoured for new developments in biotechnology. Due to this,
CRTISO5 expression has been achieved and confirmed in most desired strains.
However,
challenges in the development and analysis of the feeding assay have obstructed the assessment
of CRTISO5 activity.
5.1
Further research
Regarding the work conducted in this project, further research pursuits will need to focus on
tackling the challenges that have been discussed. As fluorescence appears to vary substantially
between strains, irrespective of host, CRTISO5 expression would need to be confirmed through
a more robust technique, such as Western blot.
Additionally, the issues related to LCMS
analysis would need to be solved, establishing a protocol that ensures reliable detection of the
compounds of interest. Lastly, the feeding assay for S. cerevisiae would need to be repeated.
Here, a good source of precursor would need to be established, possibly by means of a clean
A. carterae culture. Furthermore, the positive control would need to be improved. To ensure
the maximum accuracy, a new strain that also uses dinoxanthin, or the A. carterae extract, as
a substrate would need to be designed and constructed. It must be noted that this was not
feasible in this work due to its already broad scope.
To conclude, this research opens the path to a better understanding of carotenoid biosynthesis
