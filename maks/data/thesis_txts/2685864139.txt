Abstract
Current power supply to offshore measuring devices and machinery are very impractical
and unsustainable. Resen Waves has developed a solution, with their wave energy
Smart Ocean Buoy. Resen Waves wants to know how the buoy behaves in waves (the
motions and forces of the buoy) to predict the energy absorbed and the forces acting on
the buoy. The aim for this master’s thesis was therefore to (I) develop a weakly
nonlinear numerical model for the motion of the buoy in ocean waves, including
nonlinear cable forces, and (II) conduct wave tank tests of a model scale buoy at AAU,
to collect motions and forces to verify the numerical model and get an indication of the
power generated by the buoy.
First a weakly nonlinear numerical model was developed, by implementing the linear
frequency response, given as the solution to the linear potential flow using WAMIT, into
the DTUMotionSimulator tool and adding nonlinear cable forces from the power take off
(PTO) system. This was done both for the old geometry and the optimized geometry
where the mass and position of the drum was updated to match the model scale buoy in
the wave tank test. The numerical model was verified for the linear solution, by
comparing the response with the solution from WAMIT.
Physical experiments were performed with a model scale buoy in a wave tank at Aalborg
University, examining the response of the buoy with a range of different waves. The
undisturbed wave signals were decomposed into incident and reflected components up
to third order, where the linear part was used to generate wave inputs to the numerical
model. When looking at the simulated cable forces, the results from the non­linear
simulations show good agreement with the measurable cable forces. This means that
the model can be used to estimate the power production from the buoy.

1
Introduction
“Resen waves is the first company in the World to provide renewable and continuous
power and real­time data connectivity to autonomous instrument and machinery in the
sea, as a plug and play solution.” [1].
Figure 1.1: Picture of the Resen Waves Smart Ocean Buoy. [1]
Historically it has been a troublesome task to provide power at remote offshore
locations. Usually instruments, measuring devices and machinery in the sea run on
electricity provided by batteries. Those batteries need replacement periodically to keep
the equipment running, which is a very time consuming and thus an expensive repeated
task. Furthermore, if the equipment is submerged in the water or placed on the sea bed,
it is possible to use underwater acoustic communication, between submerged
equipment and a buoy on the free surface, but the bandwidth is very limited, and the
communication system can be unreliable in bad weather conditions. Consequently the
data is often stored locally and will need to be retrieved. This results in a delay of the
data collection and no real time measurements can be conducted. In some cases, it is
not even possible to change the batteries or collect data due to harsh weather. The
need for more sustainable and better solutions for power supply for offshore instruments
and machinery resulted in the development of the Resen Waves Smart Ocean Buoy.
Resen Waves has developed a wave energy buoy that solves both of these problems.
The buoy provides renewable and continuous power to offshore instruments, together
with real time data connectivity through e.g. satellites or cellular. This means no more
changing of batteries and delayed data collection, the instruments can be accessed at
all times. This is an important feature for live measurements where time is of the
essence, for instance surveillance of earthquakes and tsunamis.
Resen Waves wants to know how the buoy behaves in waves (the motions and forces of
the buoy) to predict the energy absorbed and the forces acting on the buoy. A way to
investigate this can be through real life testing. For large structures in full scale, a large
wave tank would be required to provide a controlled environment to perform testing.
Moreover, in an iterative design phase, changing the model several times can be very
costly and time consuming. Real life testing is therefore highly impractical. A numerical
simulation on the other hand, can provide fast and accurate answers, can be easily
scaled and multiple different design cases can be run simultaneously. A computer is
also relatively cheap. Furthermore, it is possible to get results for complex and rare

cases, that would be very difficult to emulate in a wave tank. The goal is therefore to
develop a numerical model for the wave­structure interaction and to predict the
response and energy capture of the wave buoy.
The aim of this master’s thesis is therefore:
­ Develop a weakly nonlinear numerical model for the motion of the buoy in ocean
waves, including non­linear cable forces.
­ Conduct wave tank tests of a model scale buoy at AAU, to collect motions and forces
to verify the numerical model and get an indication of the power generated by the buoy.
This will allow the user to compare and verify a simulated response of the buoy, against
the response measured in the wave tank at Aalborg University (AAU). In order to do so,
WAMIT [2] will be used to calculate the linear potential flow solution. The linear and/or
weakly non­linear response of a floating marine structure to ocean waves will be
simulated using the DTUMotionSimulator [3] where nonlinear cable forces will be
implemented and the DTUMotionSimulator will be corrected to take in waves from two
headings in order to implement the reflected waves in the wave tank. The waves
examined in the wave tank at AAU will be decomposed to identify the incoming and
reflected waves for the simulation. Finally, the power generated by the buoy can be
predicted directly from the nonlinear cable forces, implemented in the model.

2.1
Assumptions
The flow is assumed to be ideal, meaning that the flow is irrotational, incompressible and
inviscid. The velocity field is defined as the gradient of the velocity potential
(u, v, w) = ∇ϕ.
The model is symmetric about the y = 0 plane, and the body is only free to move in
Surge, Heave and Pitch.
The water waves are assumed to be linear, with the wave crest being perpendicular to
the x­axis.
2.2
Frequency domain solution
The frequency domain solution is calculated using the program WAMIT [2]. It consists of
two subprograms POTEN and FORCE. The POTEN program solves the potential flow
problem for a defined range of frequencies. The FORCE program calculates the added
mass and damping coefficients, the excitation forces and the response of the body.

6.2.1
Delay in PTO rotation.
When the direction of rotation changes, the sign on the brake force changes as well.
This means there will be a jump in cable force of 2 times the brake force, over a short
amount of time. The resulting cable force will look like the sum of a sinusoidal wave and
a square wave, as shown in Figure 6.4.
To understand what is happening when a brake or generator with constant torque is
added to the system, let us first consider the system without the brake. In this case the
tension in the mooring line is created by the spring pulling on one end of the cable and
the buoy at the other end pulling in the opposite direction with the same force. This
gives us the balance of forces
|Fbuoy| = |Fspring|

Lets first consider the case when the buoy is ’lifted up’ by the incoming wave. In this
case the brake Fbrake will team up with the spring Fspring to oppose the pulling force from
the buoy Fbuoy. At the peak of this motion, the PTO shaft will stop rotating.
In order for the PTO shaft to begin rotation in the opposite direction, the spring Fspring
now has to overcome both the pulling force from the buoy Fbuoy and the brake Fbrake.
Since there is a difference in force of two times the brake force, going from (6.2) to (6.3),
and the spring force is constant when the shaft is at rest, the buoy force has to decrease
this amount ∆Fbuoy = 2Fbrake, before the shaft begins to rotate in the opposite direction.

7
Conclusion
This project describes the tool for simulating the response of the Resen Waves Smart
Ocean Buoy in waves. This includes the theory on the equations of motion in the
frequency domain and time domain, nonlinear external forces, and decomposition of the
measured wave elevation. A weakly nonlinear numerical model was developed, by
implementing the linear solution obtained from WAMIT into the DTUMotionSimulator tool
and adding nonlinear cable forces from the PTO system.
The model was verified for the linear solution, and simulated results were compared
experimental data, which was collected by conducting wave tank tests with a model
scale buoy at Aalborg University.
The numerical model makes it possible to simulate different design changes to the buoy
in a fast and easy way, for example what impact changes to the geometry of the float will
have on the motions and power production of the buoy.
The simulated nonlinear cable forces show good agreement with the measured cable
forces. The power analysis also confirm that the power curve for the buoy plateaus,
making the power produced by the buoy more or less constant within a given range of
operation.
7.1
Recommendations
It is recommended to add more volume to the float, to increase the buoyancy of the
buoy. This makes it possible to add more pretension to the cable and in turn generate
more power.
It is also recommended to perform a more thorough experiment with the damping brake,
in order to get more exact brake force measurements, and verify that the brake force is
independent of the rotational velocity.
7.2
Future work
Extending the model to take the non­linear hydrostatic forces into account. This is
probably the top priority in order to get a more accurate prediction.
Implement the time delay in the non­linear cable force calculations.
The position of the drum. It would be interesting to look at the effect on turning stability.
At the experiments in Aalborg we moved the drum forward 2cm and the buoy seemed to
be more stable. The buoy is turning around the mooring line intersection point, so by
moving this point forward, the buoy should have a more stable yaw motion and align
better with the waves.
In order to simulate the turning motion, the buoy should be free to move in all degrees of
freedom, in particular Yaw. If turning is happening in the simulation, the heading angle of
the incident wave should be updated at each time step. I am not sure how exactly to
implement this, but a good start would be to have the RAO’s calculated for a lot of
headings.
