Abstract
As in all other areas, we humans wants to push things close to the limit, and sometimes over
to gain knowledge. The same are the case within the ﬁeld of turbo machinery. Within this vast
area, a small part is the understanding of the seal’s inﬂuence, because this are one of the limiting
factors, for turbo machinery.
This thesis is focused on making a mathematical model that can be slowed fast (compared to
current CFD models) by a ﬁnite diﬀerence scheme, but not necessarily be highly accurate, but
give clear indication of the performance of the selected seal design. The model, that it is based
on, is the one by Reinhardt and Lund. This is originally derived for bearings, and as a result of
that based on laminar ﬂow theory (Reynolds equation).
It were possible to validate the model against diﬀerent types of bearing theories. The model did
however not fare well, when it were compared to diﬀerent seal models and measurement made
by numerous people.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
A turbo machine is a machine where, a ﬂow of a ﬂuid is either driving or driven by one ore more
fans. When the machine is driven by the ﬂuid, it is called a turbine, when driving a ﬂuid, it is
either called a compressor, pump or a fan [22]. It is the latter that this thesis will be focusing
on. These three types, will from now on be gathered under the notion pump.
Figure 1.1: Schematic of a pump and the seal [32]
An example of a pump can be seen in Figure 1.1. As seen it consist of a pump wheel (im-
peller), that is driven by a motor. Right behind it is the seal, between the housing and the
driving shaft (rotor), the shaft is often supported by magnetic or ﬂuid ﬁlm bearings.
1
==PAGE:16==

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Pumps can work with pressures in excess of 300 bar and speeds at 15000 rpm [32], these speci-
ﬁcations makes it impossible to use normal ball or roller bearings∗, hence the magnetic or ﬂuid
ﬁlm bearings. This again makes impossible to use normal sealing methods, since the shaft should
be able to move in radial direction. This leads to an open type of seal, therefore there is a leakage
ﬂow, which one wants to minimize. One way to reduce the leakage ﬂow through the seal is to
make the seal longer, but then the shaft has to be longer. This again inﬂuences the stability of
the pump. Instead grooved or labyrinth seal have been made to reduce the leakage ﬂow. The
reduction in leakage ﬂow can increase the mechanical losses, this must be considered when design
the pump.
The ﬂow through the seal has the disadvantage, that it inﬂuences and often determines the
operational speed limit for the machine because it causes self excitations [38], especially for short
shafts the inertia eﬀect inﬂuences the stability [31]. The leakage ﬂow also directly inﬂuences the
eﬃciency of the pump, other factors that inﬂuences the eﬃciency of the pump is mechanical or
frictional losses between stationary and rotating parts. The design of the pump wheel and the
housing around it, also determines the eﬃciency of the pump.
The ﬂow through the seal is primarily driven by the pressure diﬀerence across the seal (be-
tween the pump wheel and the internals of the pump). Secondly there is a ﬂow around the shaft
that is driven by the rotation of the shaft. Other factors that inﬂuences the ﬂow behaviour, is
the temperature, type of ﬂuid and the geometry of the seal. For high viscous ﬂuids, the forces in
the seal can be as high as the ones in the bearing. This means that the seal eﬀectively is a loaded
path for the ﬂuid and it alters the stability limit of the bearing [1]. It should also be noted the
seal experience larger deformations from the shaft, due to their position relative to the bearing.
Pumps is used in a variety of industries e.g. reﬁnery, power, mining, water [17]. So there is
a interest in making the machines more eﬃcient, to reduce the running cost. It is also industries
where quantity matter, meaning that a minimum of service where the pumps are shut down and
design where the MTBF† is important. In pursuit of getting better performance, some pumps
is designed to operate, at speeds higher than their the ﬁrst eigenfrequency. This means that
there has to be enough damping near the critical speed to pass through it, or enough power
to accelerate through the critical speed. It is critical to predict/know the critical speeds when
determine the operating speed. Therefore the inﬂuence from the seal is interesting.
1.2
Objectives
The scope of this thesis, is to develop a fast and eﬃcient computational tool, that supports
parametric studies on seal’s in pumps. It should not necessarily be highly accurate in the sense
of absolute accuracy, but it should give reasonable results. So when using the tool, one will be
able to get an understanding of how diﬀerent seal geometry‡ will inﬂuence the seal forces, the
overall rotating machine stability and thereby its performance. The tool should be able to work
with compressible§ and incompressible ﬂuids. The tool should give estimations on the stiﬀness,
damping and the added mass from the ﬂuid ﬁlm. This means that it should include the ﬂuid
inertia.

Chapter 2
Seal and modelling options
There is developed a variety of diﬀerent methods, to calculate the inﬂuence of the ﬂuid in a seal.
A short introduction to the diﬀerent methods will be made. Diﬀerent physical phenomena will
be brieﬂy discussed and how they inﬂuence the design of the seal. Finally the assumptions for
doing the calculations will be listed.
2.1
Seal
There is diﬀerent types of seal’s this thesis will be focusing on the plain journal type. They can
be tapered, cylindrical or have another type of simple design. Some that is relatively close in
design, is the labyrinth seal that can lead the oil through a more complex geometry. A plain
journal seal is similar to a plain journal bearing however the key diﬀerence is that the seal often
has a large pressure gradient in the axial direction.
It also has large clearance between the
shaft and the seal itself. These facts makes it interesting to have inertia terms included in the
calculations, due to the large axial ﬂow.
The plain seal’s damping is normally only eﬀective until twice the critical speed is reached, any
higher and whirling starts to occur [10]. Pumps that run over the critical speed is therefore
limited by whirling of the shaft in the seal.
Therefore [10] made investigations into surface
roughness and how it could stabilize the shaft. Another possibility to reduce the impact of the
ﬂuid ﬂow in the seal, is to use a back to back conﬁguration as opposed to the ﬂow through, both
are shown in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: On top a ﬂow through compressor, and below a back to back [7]
It is known that an annular straight seal always have a positive stiﬀness if the eccentricity is
low, this is regardless if the ﬂuid is compressible or incompressible (Lomakin eﬀect). The inertia

2.2
Physical phenomena
2.2.1
Swirl
Swirl (pre-rotation) is that the ﬂuid, is all ready ﬂowing/rotating with a circumferential velocity,
close to the one of the shaft. This especially inﬂuences the cross coupling of the coeﬃcients and
therefore the stability. The higher swirl, the lower the stability limit becomes. This is a more
pronounced problem in long seal’s, to minimize this a ﬂuid is sometimes forced in, in the opposite
direction of the rotation, this then reduces the circumferential ﬂow and velocity. Other ways to
minimize the swirl, is to install swirl brakes at the entrance of the seal. These swirl brakes can
have a variety of designs, that is determined by the swirling.

2.2.4
Losses
There are multiple areas that inﬂuences the eﬃciency of a pump, some of them are losses due to
friction between moving parts, design of the pump wheel/house and e.t.c.. The ﬂow through the
seal, is directly inﬂuencing the volumetric eﬃciency of the pump. If one percent of the pumped
ﬂuid ﬂows through the seal, then volumetric eﬃciency of the pump is lowered by one percent.

2.2.5
Oil whirl
Oil whirl is a phenomena that happens when the shaft is located in the equilibrium position,
and a large enough shock occurs (Figure 2.5). This then leads to an increase in the oil ﬁlm
thickness, which increases the carrying capacity of bearing. This again means that the forces
is not in equilibrium, meaning that the shaft will start to move around. This will continue, if
the damping is to low. This is most pronounced in plain journal bearings, since there is only
"one pressure zone", this is also the way to minimize the problem, namely make a non uniform
geometry. This is something that primarily happens in bearings, but as mentioned earlier a
viscous oil, would make the bearing act somewhat like a bearing.

2.3
Methods
2.3.1
Bulk ﬂow
In bulk ﬂow theory, the idea is that the ﬂow is assumed to be a lump of ﬂuid that, passes through
the bearing. The losses is then generated by the friction between the wall and the shaft. The
reason for the abandonment of the Bulk ﬂow model, is that it is more complex, than the model
presented in this thesis, and that there is multiple empirical, coeﬃcients which can be rather
diﬃcult to get correct/ﬁnd. If more information about the Bluk Flow theory is need, one could
study publications by D. Childs, G. G. Hirs and/or the thesis by Grauballe [12].
2.3.2
CFD/FE modelling
The problems with these methods is that they are computational heavy, which excludes them as
a possible solution for this thesis. They are currently the best tool for making precise predictions
of the coeﬃcients, and general behaviour of the seal. There is a list in [12], where diﬀerent types
of software for calculation seal coeﬃcients is listed.
2.4
Reynolds equation
The chosen method for solving the ﬂow through the seal, is the thin ﬁlm model, also known as
the Reynolds equation. The advantage is that the equation itself, is relatively simple and well
understood. Another advantage is, that is does not contain any empirical obtained factors. The

2.5
Assumptions
Here the assumptions necessary to drive the governing equations is listed.
• Thin ﬁlm-thickness: This allows for the curvature in the diﬀerent directions to be as-
sumed negligible, since the clearance to radius ratio is very small. This also leads to the
pressure changes in the redial direction can be neglected.
==PAGE:22==
8
CHAPTER 2. SEAL AND MODELLING OPTIONS
• No body forces: The above assumption allow one to neglect the gravitational forces,
since the ﬂuid ﬁlm is so thin.
• Incompressible: The mathematical model can only be used for ﬂuids that can be assumed
to be incompressible, this can however be changed in the model by introducing a extra term.
• No-slip condition: The ﬂuid is "glued" to the wall, meaning that the ﬂuid has the same
velocity as the walls.
• Fully laminar & developed ﬂow: The ﬂow is laminar at all times, and the ﬂow is always
has a fully developed ﬂow proﬁle, even at the entrance and exit.
• Perfect geometry: The seal’s geometry is perfect cylindrical, and so is the shaft. This
means that small deviations, that will occur from the production of the seal and shaft is
not possible to evaluate.
• Isothermal: There can be no change in temperature in the ﬂuid. This means that the
density, viscosity (which is assumed to be isoviscous) and etc. is constant.
• Aligned: The shaft and seal is at all times parallel, meaning the shaft cannot have any
angle relative to the seal, due to load, or bearing misalignment or due to bending of the
shaft.
• Velocity proﬁle: The velocity proﬁle is not inﬂuenced by the inclination of the seal.
• Pressure: The pressure through the ﬁlm thickness in constant.
• Little movement: It is assumed that the shaft moves very little up and down. This
makes it possible to make linear perturbation.

and poiseuille ﬂow. This is because there is a pressure that is forcing the movement of the ﬂuid,
and that the shaft is rotating. Figure 2.8 shows that it is only a Poiseuille ﬂow in the z-direction.
Due to the possibility of an inclining seal, there is an risk for some turbulence could emerge
before the theory can predict it. A Reynolds number or an equivalent method could not be
found for such a case.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The presented model in this thesis has been successfully derived, and implemented in a numerical
solution method. The numerical model were implemented with relative ease, the only real time
consuming problem, were the integration limits when using Gümbel boundaries. The model were
also veriﬁed against long and short bearing theory, with both Gümbel and Sommerfeld bound-
aries. This were done with very little deviation between the models.
A lot of time and eﬀort were spend to ﬁnd articles, with good and enough data to do comparison
with, especially in the beginning, where the inﬂuence of ﬂow in the axial direction for the pre-
sented model were unknown. Therefore articles with low axial Reynolds numbers were sought,
it later proved that it did not inﬂuence the result signiﬁcant (for the presented model). It were
also a problem, that numerous articles were lacking information, to recreate their results with
this model. The model did however not do well against experimental, CFD or bulk ﬂow for the
results that were found. Therefore this model is currently not working for seal’s.
It was rather surprising that the model did perform so badly, when it were compared to ex-
perimental data and other models. One would of cause expect some diﬀerence, maybe in the
order of factor 4-7∗, but atleast some of the tendencies were expected to be recreated with the
presented model, this were not the case. The high factor is also because, some of the assumptions
are on the limit to be acceptable. For instance the assumption about laminar ﬂow, and that
is is fully developed is not true. However the signiﬁcance is still unknown, but the error of a
factor of around 4.6 indicates that it is signiﬁcant. The inﬂuence of the assumption, that the
velocity proﬁle is fully developed at the entrance of the seal is still unknown. The assumption
that the velocity proﬁle is unaﬀected by the inclination is probably also wrong†. It were however
not tested, but it should be noted that [15] did derive an equation (by using stream lines), that
indicated that some backﬂow could occur. In that derivation there were however not a pressure
diﬀerence in the axial direction of the seal. The other assumptions are more or less the same as
for the bulk ﬂow model.
In the articles found, the axial pressure diﬀerence is probably including, the inlet pressure drop
and outlet pressure recovery. Also many of the results include inlet/pre swirl which the presented
model can not. This means that it is diﬃcult to do comparisons with the articles. The most fair
comparison is probably with [1], this is with a smooth seal with a negligible inlet swirl, there are
however no information about inlet and outlet pressure drop and recovery. These results does

Chapter 7
Conclusion
In this thesis a derivation of the Reynolds equation were made, which is including the inertia
terms. The Reynolds equation were implemented in a ﬁnite diﬀerence scheme, which (dependent
on the grid size) were able to solve the Reynolds equation fast (in a few seconds). The equation
were validated against short and long bearing theory. It were however not possible, to validated
it against any experimental data or mathematically sealing theories.
It has proven diﬃcult to conduct a root cause analysis, and therefore ﬁnd the point(s) where the
error(s) might occur. This has not been helped, by the diﬃculty of ﬁnding relevant literature,
which also presents sub-results. Only two articles were found, where relevant sub-results are
presented. The data from the articles, indicates that it is the velocity, that is (atleast a part
of) the source of error. It where also speculated that the viscosity of the ﬂuid, could help the
presented model in getting better results. The boundary conditions Gümbel and Sommerfeld
also has to be investigated, to ﬁnd if they suits the conditions in a seal, or a third boundary
condition should be used. Even though hard work and much eﬀort were put into this project
in order for it to succeed, no conclusive results can be drawn from the presented model, with
respect to the coeﬃcients and the inﬂuence of these parameters for seals.
Nevertheless the Reynolds equation including the inertia eﬀect were successfully derived. There
after a perturbation analysis of that equation were also done successfully, along with the imple-
mentation of the code. The derivation, including the perturbation, is shown in its full length
with a description of each step. The model does however work perfectly for plain journal bear-
ings, but not for seals. This raises the question, whether it is feasible to continue using eﬀort on
the model presented in this thesis, when the bulk ﬂow seems to work in most cases. The ﬁnal
conclusion must be made after an extensive literature review, of cases where Reynolds equation
is "evaluated". This review combined with a qualiﬁed guess of how many, if any, empirical data
based constants are needed to get good results. The beneﬁt could be that one model, could be
used for both bearings and sealings.

Chapter 8
Future studies
First of all a study of the whether the Reynolds equation is suited for seals should be made. This
means that a thorough literature study, for articles of the Reynolds equation used for calculating
the coeﬃcients, for a seal with and without success should be made. The two articles ([21, 11])
indicates that it is possible to get good results with the Reynolds equation, even if the axial
ﬂow is turbulent. If this proves to be the general case, the source of error should of cause be
determined, but then the following subjects could be interesting to include.
• Compressible ﬂow: It should be relatively simple to include in the model. It can be
inserted into the momentum equation, this does however mean that the derivation should
be made again. Most of the steps should however be the same and therefore relative easy
to do.
• Inlet pressure loss: For making more accurate predictions, the inlet loss could be in-
cluded. It could be based on the work made by Childs, the general problem with the inlet
loss, is that it is based on empirical values.
• Velocity proﬁle: It could be interesting to investigate how diﬀerent velocity proﬁles
inﬂuence the results. This is could however also a part of the solution, for getting the
Reynolds equation to work on seals.
