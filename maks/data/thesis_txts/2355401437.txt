Abstract
A prototype of a multi degree of freedom, bio inspired robotic joint was designed. The aim was for it
to be used as an experimental setup for Spiking Neural Network controllers. Design goals were a human
inspired design, agonist-antagonist actuation, position, force and torque sensing capabilities and a range of
motion similar to the shoulder of a human.
The resulting module is actuated by ﬁve actuators. Each actuator can manipulate the joint by winding up
a tendon. An elastic element with non-linear characteristics is placed in series with a DC-motor in order to
add stiffness to the actuation and the joint end effector.

1.2
Background
For decades, robots have been fascinating people in many ways and made people dream about the
possibilities they create. In popular culture robots have been portrait from household helpers to ruthless
killing machines and from mindless factory slaves to hyper intelligent well doers.
At the moment, only one of these is a reality and that is the the mindless factory slave. Robots are being
used at a large scale in the production and precision industry, but a robot that can autonomously perform any
conceivable household task has sadly still to be invented.
And exactly this is a growing ﬁeld of research. With an aging society, the need for robots as semi
intelligent helpers is growing, and advancing technology in ﬁelds such as computation and material sciences
open up new possibilities for robotics engineers.
In the last decades, robotics researches have increasingly studied biological systems in general, and
humans speciﬁcally in order to get inspired in their designs. Fields such as soft robotics, where the focus is
to design robots using materials and working principles that are intrinsically highly ﬂexible, versatile and
adaptive, take most of their inspiration out of looking at natures examples.
The reason for this interest in biological systems is the extreme measures of versatility, the robustness
and the efﬁciency that many biological mechanics show. Existing robots are nowhere near these levels
compared to even the simplest organisms. Instead they are currently designed to do one speciﬁc task with
high speed and high accuracy, but if we want robots to do our laundry and dry and fold our clothes, there is a
huge leap still to be made in terms of both mechanics and control.
1.3
Biologically inspired control at DTU
At DTU, a research group is currently investigating modular self learning control methods, that can
be applied on (modular) robots. A focus of this research is to develop self learning, bio inspired control
methods. In this research, inspiration is drawn from the working of the Cerebellum as the cerebellum has an
important role in human motor control and motor learning.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
This research is partly conducted within the framework of the neurorobotics group within the EU-funded
Human Brain Project1, which aims to develop robotics controllers inspired on the human brain and test them
using robotic conﬁgurations with human like characteristics.
As of now, the testing of the controllers is performed using conventional robotics Baira Ojeda (2016) or
robot simulators Garrido et al. (2016).
The used conventional robotic modules are rigid structures, actuated by several servo motors and using
rotational encoders in order to obtain limb orientations and velocities (Pacheco (2012)). However, the
functionality of these conventional modules does not correspond to the functionality of the human muscu-
loskeletal system in terms of sensor output and mechanical (viscoelastic and inertial) properties.
The functionality of a robotics simulator is inherently limited since it is impossible to account for all
environmental variables.
Therefore, robots with mechanical characteristics resembling that of humans are of high interest for this
research.
Therefor, the topic of this thesis will be the design and implementation of such a robot. A robotic module
has functionality resembling that of human musculoskeletal mechanics. This leads to the following problem
deﬁnition:
The existing conventional robotic modules of the Fable platform, used for testing bio inspired, self learning
controllers, do not provide an optimal experimental environment; i.e. they lack human-like functionality. A
Fable module inspired on human mechanics does not exist.
1.4
Design goal
The design goal can now be formulated as follows:
Design a prototype of a robotic limb, which has mechanical and sensing properties resembling a
multi degree of freedom human joint, such as the shoulder joint.

2.1
The human musculoskeletal system
Four parts of the human musculoskeletal system will be discussed. Firstly actuation, secondly mechanical
properties, thirdly sensing and ﬁnally joint properties.
2.1.1
Skeletal muscles and agonist-antagonist actuation
The actuators of the human musculoskeletal system are our skeletal muscles. The following is a short
overview of the working principals of the musculoskeletal system as discussed in van der Kooij et al. (2008).
Skeletal muscles consist of groups of muscle ﬁbres distributed over the muscle. These groups of muscle
ﬁbres are called motor units and are all simultaneously activated. Activation is triggered by neural (efferent)
signals coming from the central nervous system. These neural signals activate a chemical process inside the
muscle ﬁbre, making them contract. Muscle force is regulated by changing the number of active motor units.
Skeletal muscles are connected to the bones by tendons, which are though and elastic. When a muscle
contracts, the tendon is tensioned and a force is exerted on the bone.
The actuation of the joints in the human musculoskeletal system is characterised by its agonist-antagonist
actuation principle. The agonist muscle(s) contract, causing a rotation of the joint, while the antagonist
muscle(s) are relaxed. Reversing the order reverses the direction of rotation of the limb.
If during agonist contraction the antagonist muscle is co-activated instead of relaxed, the stiffness of
the joint is increased. By co-contracting muscle pairs the stiffness of joints can be regulated. A stronger
co-contraction of the muscles results in a stiffer joint.
Furthermore, Vanderborght et al. (2013) proved that when a joint is actuated by agonist-antagonist
actuators in series with elastic elements with a constant stiffness, the resulting joint stiffness is constant as
well. However, they showed that when the elastic elements have a length dependent stiffness, the resulting
joint stiffness can be controllable. This is further explained by ﬁgure (2.1), which shows a schematic of such
a joint, and equations (2.1-2.4).

2.1.2
Mechanical properties
The mechanical properties of our musculoskeletal system give us great advantages. For example, they
allow us to store and release energy during locomotion in the form of elastic energy, thus improving our
efﬁciency, or allow us to deal with sudden impacts on our bodies.
The mechanical properties of human joints are for a large part the result of the mechanical (viscoelastic)
properties of the muscles and the tendons.
The viscoelastic properties of muscles and tendons are often modelled using so called Hill-type muscle
models, for which Hill (1938) laid the foundation by modelling a muscle as a passive, undamped, non-linear
elastic element in series with a damped contractile element.
A more elaborate version of this model has an extra parallel non-linear elastic element. A schematic
of this model is shown in ﬁgure 2.2. Hill-type muscle models are lumped parameter models and are not a
physical representation of the muscle properties, but rather empirically found.
Several more complex models have been proposed, increasing the accuracy of the approximation of
muscle behaviour as well as the complexity to implement them (Winters (1990)).
Finally, the musculoskeletal system has some interesting inertial properties, as pointed out by Richter
et al. (2016). Due to the use of tendons, the actuators do not have to be placed close to the joints. This allows
for relatively light end-effectors and a low inertial mass.
2.1.3
Proprioceptive sensing
Proprioceptive sensors are those sensors in the human body that measure posture and motion, as discussed
in van der Kooij et al. (2008). The proprioceptive sensory system consists four types of sensors:

7
Figure 2.2: Schematic of the Hill-type muscle model. CE = (damped) contractile element, PE = parallel
(elastic) Element, SE = Serial (elastic) element.
• Vestibular organs
• Muscle spindles
• Golgi tendon organs
• Joint receptors
The vestibular organs
are located near our ears. They measure the orientation and linear and rotational
acceleration of the head. This is the only proprioceptive sense that gives us information of the position and
motion of our body with respect to the outer world.
The muscle spindles
are part of the muscles and measure muscle length and velocity. This allows us to
determine, maintain and control our posture as well as the motion of our body and limbs relative to each
other.
The Golgi tendon organs
are located in the tendons and measure the tension in the muscles and tendons.
This muscle force feedback signal has an important function in controlling the position of our limbs (van der
Kooij et al. (2008)).
The joint receptors
are sensors located in the joint ligaments and capsules measuring joint angle and/or
motion. There are four different types of joint receptors: free nerve endings, golgi type endings, rufﬁni
endings and paciniform endings.
According to van der Kooij et al. (2008) it is unlikely that these sensors play a big part in human motion
control, other than limit detection.

Proske and Gandevia (2012) wrote a review of the historical discussion about the role of joint receptors.
They come conclude that they only play a minor role as limit detectors and that the principle proprioceptive
sense is the muscle spindle. They present two interesting arguments for this:
1. A study by Grigg et al. (1973) showed that patients could still determine joint position accurately after
the replacement of the hip joint with an artiﬁcial joint. During the operation, the joint capsules and
ligaments containing joint receptors of the patients were removed.
Similar ﬁndings were produced by Clark et al. (1979), who anaesthetised the knee joint and skin of
test subjects in order to impair the function of joint receptors.
2. Eklund (1972) and Goodwin et al. (1972) showed that by applying 100Hz vibrations to tendons, the
illusion of movement of the corresponding joint could be triggered in a patient. In contrast, vibrating
the joint directly did not produce a similar illusion. This indicates that it is the muscle spindles and
not the joint receptors that form our primary source for joint movement detection.
From the above it is concluded that the primary senses involved in human movement control are the
vestibular organs, muscle spindles and Golgi tendon organs.

Existing biomimetic robotics platforms
Several universities have been developing biomimetic robotic platforms over the last two decades.
At the Inria research center in Bordeaux, France, Lapeyre et al. (2013) designed a robot with some human
like characteristics. Although it was actuated using conventional actuation methods, namely servo motors in
the joints, it used morphology aided walking, making walking a more dynamic process. Furthermore, it used
special materials in the toes, which gave it a functionality similar to that of human toes. Also it used springs
parallel to the knee joint to aid walking and store and reuse kinetic energy.

Its latest sibling, Kengoro (Kozuki et al. (2016)), is similar but now it sweats in order to cool the high
number of motors used inside the body.

This chapter is divided into two parts. In the ﬁrst part I will extract the design criteria from the design

3.1
Design constraints
In chapter 1.4 the design goal of this project was stated as:
Design a prototype of a robotic limb, which has mechanical and sensing properties resembling a
multi degree of freedom human joint, such as the shoulder joint, and integrate this design into the
Fable modular robotics platform.
Throughout the rest of this section, this design goal will be dissected in order to extract a set of design
criteria. These criteria will be the corner stones of the project and the resulting prototype.
3.1.1
Agonist-antagonist actuation
When studying human mechanics, one of the ﬁrst things to stand out is the actuation method of the limbs.
As was explained in chapter 2, humans (and many other animals) use agonist-antagonist muscle pairs to
actuate limbs.
This gives great freedom in the placement of the actuators, which now do not have to be inside the joint.
It is in sharp contrast with most conventional robotics actuators, such as DC or stepper motors, which actuate
the limbs from inside the joints, functioning as both actuator and hinge point.
From this I extracted the ﬁrst design criterion, which is that the module will be actuated by agonist-
antagonist actuator pairs. Additionally these actuators will only be able to pull and not push, to further
mimic human muscles.
3.1.2
Visco-elastic properties
As discussed in section 2.1.2, Hill-type muscle models are used to approximate visco-elastic muscle
behaviour. In order to avoid over complicating the design, I will start with a simple model as a design
criterion. If desired at a later stage, more complexity can be introduced into the design.

3.1.3
Joint type
The human hip and shoulder are multiple DoF joints. In these complex joints, the bones are held inplace
by the muscles, tendons and ligaments. These joints resemble ball socket joints, where a ball like bone
end rotates in a socket. In human joints, these elements are not exactly spherical. However, for the sake of
simplicity I will consider the joint a ball-socket joint.
3.1.4
Joint angles
Of all the joints in the human body, the shoulder joint has the widest overall range of movement.
According to Paulsen and Wasche (2013) the ranges of this joint are:
• Adduction/Abduction (to and from the body): 90◦−0 −40◦
• Anteversion/Retroversion (forward/backwards): 90◦−0 −40◦
• External/Internal rotation (around the longitudinal axis): 60◦−0 −70◦
• the neutral (0◦, 0◦, 0◦) position is when the arm is hanging down along the body, with the palm facing
outward
The shoulder joint has three DoF. However, I chose not to incorporate external/internal rotation (twisting)
into this design. The reason for this is that the incorporation of the third DoF brings a lot more complexity to
the design. This makes it not feasible for the limited time set aside for this project.
From the previous I extracted two more design criteria: the joint will have two DoF: both with a
movement range of 130◦.
3.1.5
Sensing properties
In order to give the robotic limb sensing capabilities resembling those of humans, the following three
muscle/tendon parameters have to be measurable: length, contraction velocity and force.
This has to be done at a rate equal to the control loop speed of the SNN controllers, which is 200Hz.

3.1.6
Over actuation
Human limbs are further characterised by over actuation. I.e. there are more muscles present than strictly
necessary to actuate the DoF available. This feature introduces an interesting optimisation problem into the
control of the limbs, where the brain has to chose between many, if not inﬁnite, options when controlling the
limbs.
As the purpose of this module is to be used as an experimental setup for controllers inspired by the
functioning of the human brain, I chose this as another design criteria. Theoretically, two DoF can be
actuated using three agonist-antagonist actuators, therefore adding two more makes the module over actuated.
An additional advantage is that it increases the range of motion, as is pointed out further on in this chapter.
3.1.7
Design criteria concerning Fable
The designed module has to be compatible with the Fable platform. Therefore it will aim to have at least
similar capabilities in the form of:
• Fable module measurements (The 2 DoF Fable module is 244.2 mm high and has a maximum diameter
of 86.9 mm Pacheco (2012)).
• Fable module load characteristics. The Dynamixel AX-12A servo motors used in the Fable modules
have a stall torque of approximately 1.6Nm accordingto the data sheet. Dynamixel advises to use
them at no more then 20-30% of the stall torque during normal operation, which is between 0.3 and
0.45 Nm.
• Fable control loop speed. This was clocked at 200 Hz (5ms) for the SNN controllers.
3.1.8
Inertial properties
As I discussed earlier, a characteristic of human musculoskeletal mechanics is the relatively low inertial
mass of its end effectors. This is an advantage for both safety and energy consumption as large moving
masses are inherently dangerous. They require higher torques to move them and a higher energy consumption.
For these reasons, I will take minimising the inertial mass as a guideline throughout the design.

3.2.1
Actuation principles
Table 3.2 lists several actuation principles, divided into two categories. The ﬁrst category are options
that use an external elastic element (EE) to add elasticity to the output of the actuator. They consist of an
actuator, such as a DC-motor, that is connected in series with the EE like in the muscle model of ﬁgure 3.1.
The contractile element in this model is the actuator.
The second category are options that have intrinsic elastic properties and therefore would not need an

The second category are mostly experimental actuation types that still have large shortcomings, such as

unify with a modular robotics platform like Fable. It might be possible to implement some of them, but this
would require extensive research outside the scope of this project. Therefore, they are not looked into any
further.

3.2.3
Early prototype
Figure 3.6 shows the ﬁrst prototype of the actuator. This early prototype only had the non-linear pulley, a
torsion spring and a tendon on a small pulley. The small pulley was inspired by Richter et al. (2016).
The working principle is that when the rope leaving the actuator in ﬁgure 3.6(b), from now on ’the outer
tendon’, is displaced it can freely move trough the small pulley assembly. In the ﬁnal prototype, the outer
tendon is wound up by a DC-motor, however not in this early version.
As long as there is no force obstruction the movement of the other end of the tendon, it can keep moving,
giving the actuator a large stroke. However, when there is a force on the other end of the tendon, the whole
pulley assembly will be forced to move towards the mouth of the actuator. This causes the torsion spring
inside the non-linear element to wind up and exert a force in the opposite direction.
I chose the tendon material to be stiff, so that it does not interfere too much with the stiffness characteris-
tics.
As soon as the force on the outward tendon is released, the accumulated potential energy in the spring
element will be released, and the system will be restored to its original state.

Control, update speed and data size
The arduino boards were equipped with simple controllers for the position and torque. This allows
the user to switch between position control and force control. The position control is done by a simple,
experimentally tuned PD-controller. The torque is controlled using a P-controller.
The actual loop duration of the on board Arduino is less then one millisecond. However, it is coded
to wait for a millisecond to pass before starting a new loop cycle. This is done with an accuracy of ±4
microseconds.
The advantage of doing this, is that it guarantees a hard real time loop of one millisecond, which is
important in order to be able to guarantee sensor update rates.
The position and torque measurement data are stored as 16 bit integer values. The velocity data is stored
as a single byte. The controller commands have a data size of 16 bits.

The end effector is actuated by agonist-antagonist actuator combinations. By assigning one or more

tendons at the same time.
The stiffness of the end effector is controllable. Changing the stiffness can be achieved by tensioning all
the actuators simultaneously with in the same amount, making the end effector more rigid.
I dimensioned the dish, ball and socket such that the movement range is 130◦. Further more the dish
guides the tendons, ensuring that the end effector can be actuated over the entire range of motion. The guides
are equiped with plain bearings to reduce friction between the body and the tendons.
Inherent to the design using tendons and a ball socket joint, is that the moment arm between the tendon
force and the point of rotation of the end effector is not constant.
The minimum moment arm occurs when the end effector is fully rotated away from the tendon guide.
The maximum moment arm is where the end effector almost touches the guide. The maximum moment arm
is Rmax = 40mm and the minimum moment arm is Rmin = 24.5mm.
3.3.1
Number of actuators
Figure 3.18 shows a photo of the body with the actuators installed. Three were placed at the top of the
body and two at the bottom. This way the shape of the module can gradually get thicker towards the top.
I designed the module to be over actuated, as is desired for the SNN controllers for its more realistic
resemblance of human limb mechanics. In theory a setup of three actuators could be able to rotate the
end effector in 2 DoF. In this module, however, there will be ﬁve actuators. This makes the module over
actuated. Additionally it allows for a larger range of motion, with the actuators still packed closely. Figure
3.19 schematically clariﬁes this.
As can be seen in ﬁgure 3.17, I did not space the actuator guides equally over the dish. I did this because
this way the actuators could be packed side by side in the body, reducing the overall body size. This only has
small consequences for the range of movement.
The actuators are all placed in the lower part of the module, making the end effector relatively very light.
3.3.2
Power consumption
I estimated the total power consumption, using the data from section 3.2.8. At nominal usage and with
ﬁve actuators, the power consumption is approximately 12 W. At stall torque, this is approximately 48 W.
Since stall of all the motors is not a realistic nor a desirable scenario, the power source used will not
need to be able to produce 50 W. Instead, I will use a power supply with rated power of 21 W, which is well
over nominal use and therefore should be sufﬁcient.

The internal communication between the actuators is done via a central micro controller, which is another
Arduino Pro-mini.
It has three functions. Firstly, it is used to interface with the robot. Secondly, it distributes commands to
the actuators. Thirdly, it receives sensor data from the actuators. Another possibility is to preprocess the

The internal communication is done using the I2C protocol set in full speed mode. This allows for a
maximum data transmission of 400 kbit/s. The data size per actuator per loop of 1 millisecond is 56 bits,
as was explained in section 3.2.6. With 5 actuators, this amounts to a total of 280 bits per millisecond.
Therefore, the I2C protocol is sufﬁcient to guarantee an internal sensor update interval of one millisecond,
leaving 120 bits for start/stop bits, message receival conﬁrmation, etc.
A large advantage of the I2C protocol is that it uses just two wires for all the actuators.
The external communication is done trough a serial half duplex protocol at a baut rate of 115200 baut/s.
This allows for a sensor update speed of one millisecond when the data is preprocessed internally and two
milliseconds in the case of full sensor feedback.

4.4
Manual functionality tests
In this section I will discuss a few functionality tests, performed manually on the module body. These
will be qualitative, rather than quantitative results for most features.
The functionality to be tested is:
1. Range of motion
2. End effector stability
3. Ball socket stiction and friction
4.4.1
Range of motion
To test the range of motion, I will set the actuators to torque holding mode. In this mode, a proportional
controller keeps the torque on the elastic element constant and thereby the tendon force as well. If the tendon
is then pulled, the actuator releases the tendon (it unwinds) in order to maintain a constant force. Inversely,
when the tendon is slackened, the motor will wind up the tendon in order to maintain constant torque.
In this way I can test if the actuator tendons are able to follow the range of motion of the end effector
and can maintain a torque. It was found that the end effector can move in the full designed range of 130
degrees and that the actuators can follow it over most of its range. However, at extremes the tendons slip out
of the roller guides, causing high friction.
4.4.2
End effector stability
As the end effector is only held in place by the tendons, it is important to know whether they are able to
keep the end effector in place and the ball in the socket at all times. During the previous test, it was found
that this is indeed the case. The end effector is ﬁrmly in place without a way of falling out. Only when all
tendons are slackened, the end effetor can come out of the socket.
4.4.3
Stiction and friction
A problem with ball socket joints is the large amount of friction due to the large contact area. I tested the
movement of the end effector in the socket, by moving it around under different forces. When no or little
force is applied by the tendon, the end effector moves around easily. However as the force increases, friction
increases drastically.
Also stiction phenomena occur. This means that the ens effector initially is very stuck, but once it is in
motion it the friction is greatly reduced.
4.4.4
Tendon guiding
During the movement of the end effector, it was checked whether or not the tendons stayed in their
guides. Although they did, they frequently slipt out from the single guiding plain bearing, getting caught in
between the bearing and the walls of the guide. This causes the tendons to stall.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
In this chapter I will discuss the test results from chapter 4. In the ﬁrst section, I will discuss the friction
test results. In the second section, I will discuss the elastic behavior of the actuators. In the third section, I
will discuss the hysteresis present in the actuators. In the fourth section, I will discuss the communication
speed tests and ﬁnally I will discuss the manual functionality tests of the body.
5.1
Friction testing
The stall torque tests showed that the advertised stall torques of the DC-motors of 0.28 Nm are by no
means met. Instead, the maximum torque measured was 0.102 Nm. Installed into the actuators, this number
further dropped to a maximum of 0.055 Nm which is bellow the designed nominal torque. This causes great
problems in the actuation of the limb as the actuators are simply not strong enough to produce acceptable
torques on the end effector.
The friction inside the actuators was substantial. All the friction torques were in the same order of
magnitude as the produced torque.
In order to trace the origin of the friction, the separate moving parts inside the actuator were tested. It
was found that the bearings had a friction coefﬁcient of about 0.12 - 0.15. This is in the range of the expected
values from chapter 3.
However, it was also found that the pulley system tilted when the tendon tensioned, causing excessive
friction forces. The reason for this tilting was that the inner tendon was attached to the pulley with a noose.
This inherently pulls tilts the pulley when a force is applied.
Finally, it was found that the friction inside the elastic element itself was very high, making measurements
using weights virtually impossible. This indicates a large friction inside the elastic element.
A possible explanation for high internal friction is the usage of torsion springs with tightly wound coils.
This causes the coils to slide alongside each other during operation which produces friction.

The range of motion was found to be as designed and therefore satisfactory. Furthermore, the end effector
was stable in the socket, as long as the tendons were slightly tensioned. Rotation was easy when little tendon
force was applied, however at higher forces stiction and friction became evident.
An issue found during the testing, is that the tendons need proper guidance in order to stay in the guides.
The single plain bearing used was not sufﬁcient to achieve proper guiding of the tendons.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions and future work
Looking back at the design goals, I have found that during testing a lot of them are not met. Due to the
high friction in the system, the force characteristics of the actuators are nowhere near the design criteria in
terms of magnitude. However, these are issues that are ﬁxable.
More importantly, the main achievement of the project is that it shows a prove of concept. On a
conceptual level the actuators and the module are promising. The working principle of a tendon actuated,
3D-printed limb with stiffness characteristics proves to be a feasible one. If friction in the system is reduced
and stronger motors are installed, the module can be made operational.
To emphasize this, below is a list of successful implementations together with some remarks on possible
improvements:
• The actuators show elastic characteristics
• The agonist-antagonist setup is a feasible one
• The range of motion is feasible
• The size is within the order of magnitude of the Fable joints
• Over actuation is easily achieved
• Inertial mass is minimized
The actuators show elastic behavior and I have good indications from the testing that the behavior is
correspondent with the designed behavior. However, testing on this level is still inconclusive. To get a
better insight into the elastic characteristics of the actuators, more extensive tests should be done using
a higher accuracy force measuring. Also variables like extension length during testing can be controlled better.
The agonist-antagonist actuation setup is a feasible one, as the module testing did not show any indications
that the module can not be actuated. However it is important that the tendons are properly guided on all sides,
in order to avoid them getting stuck in the guide. The current guides are not capable of ensuring smooth
behavior and should therefore be redesigned.
The range of motion is achieved as designed and can theoretically even be extended by reducing the end
effector shaft diameter.
Over actuation was implemented successfully. The actuators are all identical, making it easy to design
different modules with different amounts of actuators if this is desired.

The inertial mass of the end effector is very low, as all the actuators are installed inside the module.
Leaving the end effector with a very low mass.
Concluding, if the module were to be made operational, the next step in this design is to reduce the
internal friction inside the actuators. As a starting point for this, the pulley system and the elastic element
should be redesigned, with the focus on reducing friction.
