Abstract 
IV 
Abstract 
Biomanufacturing is put forward as a sustainable alternative to the fossil-based production of 
fuels and chemicals. Currently, this process relies on sugar-based fermentations that inevitably 
produce CO2 as a byproduct, resulting in low conversion yields. One promising solution is the 
use of acetogenic bacteria, which can ferment C1 compounds via the Wood–Ljungdahl 
pathway. While they are mainly studied for their ability to grow on CO2 and H2, this gaseous 
substrate uptake is a major process limitation. This work investigates the potential of growing 
acetogenic bacteria on soluble C1 compounds instead. This work combines stoichiometric 
modeling, soil enrichments, phylogenetic analysis, and growth experiments to identify and 
domesticate novel production hosts, thereby pushing acetogenesis beyond gas fermentation. 
Here, methanol combined with formate is predicted to be a favorable combination for 
acetogenic growth to facilitate a synergistic effect, through a constructed metabolic model. 
Hence, an enrichment of microbes from environmental samples was performed and 
characterized. Here, it is shown that only with both substrates, no substrate is lost as gaseous 
byproducts, facilitating a full liquid-to-liquid fermentation, while outperforming single substrates 
regarding biomass formation and acetate production. Only with co-feeding, a full liquid-to-liquid 
is facilitated. These findings confirm the model-based prediction and demonstrate that 
methanol is a key player in gas reassimilation. Additionally, based on phylogeny, an 
engineering strategy gives future perspectives regarding metabolic engineering.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Bioprocesses for the mitigation of climate change 
Climate change is primarily driven by industrialization, deforestation, and intensified 
agriculture, which have significantly increased emissions of greenhouse gases (GHG) such as 
carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrogen oxides (NOx). These gases trap heat in the 
atmosphere, causing a greenhouse effect, which results in climate change, where the 
temperature has risen about 0.13 °C per decade since 1950 (Masson-Delmotte et al., 2021; 
Rawat et al., 2024). If the current pace of high emissions is maintained, global temperatures 
could rise by as much as 4 °C by 2100. (Rawat et al., 2024) The consequences include more 
extreme weather events, such as floods and heatwaves, a rising sea level due to melting 
glaciers, desertification, and increasing water scarcity. Ecosystems are being disrupted, and 
many species face habitat loss and extinction. Economically, climate change threatens food 
production, damages infrastructure, and increases the burden on health and emergency 
systems. (Kikstra et al., 2022; Pfenning-Butterworth et al., 2024; Rawat et al., 2024) A scientific 
consensus agrees on the human cause of climate change. Industrial activities such as the 
extensive combustion of fossil resources have led to a disruption of the atmosphere’s near 
steady-state behavior over the last 10000 years. CO2 levels increased from about 280 ppm 
(pre-industrial) to 415 ppm today, a rise of approximately 135 ppm, or nearly 50% higher than 
pre-industrial levels in less than 200 years. (Fischer et al., 1999; Nunes, 2023)  
To tackle this problem, shifting from the current linear economic model to a circular approach 
is necessary, where the end product is not discarded as waste but used to its full potential 
(Mannina et al., 2023). Moreover, industry must transition away from fossil resources and 
towards renewable feedstocks. Such processes could build on catalytic chemistry for non-
fossil feedstocks, and bio-manufacturing is increasingly seen as a promising alternative. Here, 
the ability to valorize more sustainable feedstocks, such as biomass, at reduced chemical 
usage and milder operating conditions makes it an attractive option. (Sheldon & Brady, 2022) 
Still, their overall sustainability is limited when first-generation feedstocks are used, since these 
rely on food crops and contribute to food versus fuel issues and increased land and resource 
competition (Melendez et al., 2022). An approach that includes both criteria is a bioprocess 
that uses waste as a feedstock to produce chemicals. The high specificity and efficiency of 
bioprocesses also enable them to produce complex products with minimal energy input. 
Therefore, bioprocesses are economically promising for producing high-value goods or bulk 
chemicals, while there is still a need for improvement regarding yields to compete with the 
current fossil-based production. (Boodhoo et al., 2022)

A major challenge in heterotrophic bioprocesses (utilizing organic feedstock) is the inherent 
carbon loss that occurs primarily through decarboxylation steps in central pathways such as 
glycolysis, where 1/3 of the carbon from sugars is lost as CO2 as a by-product, reducing overall 
carbon yield (Iding et al., 1998). As an alternative, autotrophic bioprocesses are considered, 
where the use of inorganic carbon (CO2) allows for less waste. This approach combines 
solutions for issues such as waste management and climate change, where waste gas 
utilization could close carbon cycles and reduce future emissions, thereby mitigating the effects 
of GHGs. (Dürre, 2017) One of the most efficient carbon-fixating pathways is the Wood-
Ljungdahl pathway (WLP), where 2 CO2 are converted into acetyl-CoA through H2 as an 
electron donor. Here, an ATP requirement for CO2 reduction mostly limits the product formation 
to acetate and ethanol, as it must recover the invested ATP (Katsyv & Müller, 2020). 
Additionally, the use of gaseous feedstocks presents several challenges. Although large-scale 
implementations exist, these processes remain less efficient and economically viable than their 
petrochemical counterparts (Gorter de Vries et al., 2024; Redl et al., 2017). One of the primary 
limitations is the low solubility of gases, particularly hydrogen, which restricts the mass transfer 
of gases into the reaction medium. (Gorter de Vries et al., 2024) As a consequence, the need 
for more complex reactor designs to maximize the gas transfer further complicates the process 
(Bredwell et al., 1999). While integrating industrial off-gases into subsequent production 
processes appears promising, certain gas pretreatment steps are necessary. For example, 
NOx compounds, often toxic to cells, must be removed to prevent inhibition of fermentation 
processes. (Liew et al., 2016; Redl et al., 2017)  
As a solution, more reduced soluble C1 substrates, including formate, formaldehyde, and 
methanol, can be used to overcome the mass transfer issues and energy constrains. 
Processes building on soluble C1 substrates have the potential to bring down process costs 
and produce more valuable compounds, making the process more economically attractive and 
competitive. (Satanowski & Bar‐Even, 2020) Unlike CO2, which must first be reduced at an 
energy cost, methanol and formate are more reduced, bypassing energy-intensive reactions 
(Katsyv & Müller, 2020). This also avoids ATP-consuming steps required in CO2 fixation, 
improving overall energy retention. Methanol yields more ATP (~0.83 mol mol-1 acetate) than 
H2/ CO2 (~0.3 mol mol-1 acetate), supporting greater biomass and productivity (Kremp et al., 
2018; Schuchmann & Müller, 2014). 
Apart from their solubility, they can also be sustainably produced from waste CO2. Their 
synthesis from CO2 requires electrons and a suitable catalyst to drive the conversion 
selectively and efficiently (Álvarez et al., 2017). Capturing CO2 from concentrated sources like 
power plants is more practical and energy-efficient than direct air capture, primarily because

the higher concentration of CO2 in flue gases allows for more effective absorption and 
regeneration using solvent-based and enzyme-assisted methods (Peu et al., 2023). Lastly, 
hydrogen must be generated through water electrolysis powered by renewable energy sources 
such as wind, solar, or water to ensure sustainability (Álvarez et al., 2017; Patil et al., 2024). 
While formate is directly part of the methyl branch, it can be either activated or oxidized to CO2 
and then further integrated into the carbonyl branch. Methanol can be linked to the WLP 
through a methyltransferase system, acting as a methyl-group donor, bypassing the reductive 
methyl-branch. (Kremp & Müller, 2021).  
To identify a host capable of anaerobic methanol utilization under defined conditions, a 
targeted enrichment approach is a powerful starting point (Overmann, 2006). Environmental 
samples can be incubated with methanol as the sole carbon and energy source under 
anaerobic conditions, allowing native methylotrophs with the necessary metabolic capabilities 
to outcompete others. This strategy increases the likelihood of isolating organisms naturally 
adapted to methanol metabolism and the desired redox and energy constraints. While 
engineering model organisms like E. coli is attractive due to the availability of advanced genetic 
tools, most current efforts in synthetic methylotrophy have been limited to aerobic conditions 
(Sanford & Woolston, 2022). Oxygen as a terminal electron acceptor enables aerobic 
organisms to maintain redox balance more efficiently and extract greater energy yields by fully 
oxidizing carbon substrates to carbon dioxide, rather than producing fermentation by-products. 
This however translates to higher losses to CO2 as a by-product. (Antoniewicz, 2019; Sanford 
& Woolston, 2022) In contrast, native anaerobic methylotrophs already possess the necessary 
metabolic framework for methanol utilization and CO2 fixation (Kremp & Müller, 2021). Thus, 
starting with such an organism provides a good foundation, but it may also lead to challenges 
during later strain optimization required to improve product formation. This presents a key 
challenge, as a potential non-model native anaerobic methylotroph might lack a genetic toolkit. 
In this study, it is demonstrated that combining methanol and formate as co-substrates in 
acetogenic fermentations could enhance both carbon and electron flux through the WLP, thereby 
increasing product yield and process efficiency. Specifically, methanol can be used to directly 
supply the methyl-branch of the WLP as a fully soluble, reduced C1 source, which bypasses the 
ATP-intensive activation step. Meanwhile, formate can feed the carbonyl-branch at a 
comparable reduction state to carbon monoxide. This dual-substrate approach could balance 
the supply of reducing equivalents, minimize CO2 losses, and demonstrate a liquid-to-liquid 
conversion.

To illustrate the potential of soluble C1 substrates, two main approaches were outlined. First, 
a metabolic model is constructed, demonstrating the utilization of methanol and formate and 
showing a synergistic effect. Second, an enrichment of Danish soil samples is performed on 
methanol and formate. Here, a selective pressure to co-consume both substrates is aimed at

capability of a full liquid-to-liquid fermentation is demonstrated, using novel Lærke pressure

novel non-model organism and build on workflows obtained from previous studies, engineering

2 Background 
2.1 C1 substrate utilization 
CO2 can be utilized as a feedstock in various carbon-fixating pathways, including the Calvin–
Benson cycle, which plants and cyanobacteria use; the reductive or reverse TCA cycle, found 
in green sulfur bacteria and archaea; the WLP, used by acetogens and methanogens; and the 
hydroxypropionate cycle, employed by Chloroflexus and Thermoproteus (Peretó et al., 1999). 
However, the WLP is the most efficient regarding CO2 fixation and energetic input, with the 
lowest ATP requirements, but is overall energetically limited due to its ATP neutrality 
(Claassens et al., 2018; Ragsdale & Pierce, 2008). The pathway is divided into two branches: 
the methyl branch and the carbonyl branch. In the methyl-branch, CO2 is first reduced to 
formate through a formate dehydrogenase, which facilitates the reaction through electrons 
provided by the reduction equivalents NAD(P)H/NAD(P)+, Fdred/Fedox or H2/2 H+, or a 
combination (Figure 1A.). Formate is then further activated and bound to the cofactor 
tetrahydrofolate (THF) through an ATP dependent addition of the formyl-group to THF, 
resulting in 10-Formyl-THF. This is followed by several reductive steps where THF remains as 
the C1 carrier, where the formed 5-methyl-THF is lastly transferred onto the cobalamin-
containing corrinoid iron-sulfur protein (CFeSP). Simultaneously, another molecule of CO2 is 
reduced to CO in the carbonyl branch through the carbon monoxide dehydrogenase (CODH), 
through H2, or Fdox/FdRed, where CO remains bound to the CODH. This is subsequently used 
to carbonylate the CFeSP-bound methyl group to form an acetyl group carried by coenzyme 
A (CoA) to form acetyl-CoA. The produced acetyl-CoA must then be converted to acetate, 
regenerating the ATP needed for the formate activation. (Katsyv & Müller, 2020; Ragsdale, 
1991, 2008; Ragsdale & Pierce, 2008) (See Figure 1 A-B.) Due to this ATP-neutrality, 
acetogens conserve energy through membrane-bound enzyme complexes such as the Rnf or 
Ech complex. Both couple the electron transport to ion translocation and ATP synthesis. 
(Katsyv & Müller, 2020) (Figure 1D.) Generally, electron bifurcation serves to balance redox 
equivalents between carriers such as NADH and ferredoxin. For instance, the Rnf complex, 
found in species like A. woodii, transfers electrons from reduced ferredoxin to NAD⁺, producing 
NADH. This process is coupled to the extrusion of Na⁺ or H⁺ across the membrane into the 
periplasm, generating an electrochemical gradient resulting in ATP synthesis. Additionally, Rnf 
can reverse direction depending on redox demands, providing metabolic flexibility. (Katsyv & 
Müller, 2020; Westphal et al., 2018) In contrast, the Ech-complex, used by organisms like 
M. thermoacetica, oxidizes reduced ferredoxin and transfers electrons to protons, releasing 
hydrogen (Katsyv & Müller, 2020; Schoelmerich & Müller, 2020) (See Figure 1D). Like the Rnf, 
this electron flow is coupled to ion translocation and ATP generation, however, with lower 
==PAGE:13==

energy efficiency due to fewer ions pumped per electron. (Katsyv & Müller, 2020) Additionally, 
multiple electron bifurcation enzymes are responsible for energy conservation by coupling 
exergonic and endergonic redox reactions through the simultaneous reduction of two electron 
acceptors, where reactions are coupled to multiple reduction equivalent exchange, for 
example, the methylene-tetrahydrofolate reductase (metF/metV) in the methylbranch (Katsyv 
& Müller, 2020; Poudel et al., 2018)(Figure 1 F.). Lastly, hydrogenases balance the redox-state 
of the cell with by coupling the oxidation of H2 to the reduction of low-potential electron carriers 
such as ferredoxin and NAD+/NADP+ (Katsyv & Müller, 2020) (Figure 1E.).

Methanol and formate are so far not as widely explored as feed, due to their inhibitory or toxic 
effects on most common production hosts as well as their limited ATP yields compared to 
carbohydrates (Cotton et al., 2020). Although acetogens are typically cultivated with these 
substrates, high concentrations have shown inhibitory effects (Cotton et al., 2020; Sharak 
Genthner & Bryant, 1987). Despite this, formate may be less toxic to acetogens than to aerobic 
==PAGE:14==
Background 
7 
organisms, as they lack respiratory proteins such as cytochrome c oxidase, which is typically 
affected by formate (Cotton et al., 2020; Nicholls, 1975). 
Methylotrophy, the trait describing the ability to utilize methanol as a substrate, is rare 
(Antoniewicz, 2019). During aerobic utilization, the ribulose monophosphate pathway (RuMP), 
xylulose monophosphate pathway (XuMP) or serine cycle are common (Antoniewicz, 2019; 
Sanford & Woolston, 2022). All three aerobic utilization pathways are dependent on the initial 
methanol oxidation to formaldehyde, which is then further utilized. This initial step is causing 
methanol utilization to be uncommon and challenging. Formaldehyde is extremely reactive, 
crosslinking proteins or DNA, resulting in cellular death (Heck et al., 1990; Zhan et al., 2021). 
To counteract toxicity, cells must rapidly detoxify or dissimilate formaldehyde. Many organisms 
activate dissimilatory pathways to oxidize formaldehyde to formate and eventually CO2, which 
provides NADH for energy but results in carbon loss. For example, in P. pastoris and other 
methylotrophs, upregulation of these pathways is essential during methanol metabolism. (Zhan 
et al., 2021) Key enzymes like dihydroxyacetone synthase (DAS) or the RuMP pathway 
enzymes have high substrate affinities and rapidly convert formaldehyde to less toxic 
intermediates (Antoniewicz, 2019). Methylotrophs often harbor multiple detoxification systems 
to ensure minimal formaldehyde concentrations (Paredes-Barrada et al., 2025; Zhan et al., 
2021). However, while this decreases toxicity, it increases carbon loss as CO2. In essence, 
while aerobic methanol metabolism yields higher ATP production due to oxidative 
phosphorylation, resulting in faster growth rates, it often results in partial carbon loss as CO2 
(Cotton et al., 2020). 
In contrast, anaerobic methanol utilization, carried out by acetogenic bacteria using the WLP, 
enables direct incorporation into acetyl-CoA without formaldehyde formation. For instance, as 
described in A. woodii, a methyltransferase system consists of two consecutive steps, 
facilitated through 2 enzymes. The reaction starts with the methyltransferase I (MTI), which 
transfers the methyl group to a corrinoid-protein (CoP), and a second methyltransferase II 
(MTII) that transfers the methyl-group onto THF, resulting in the integration into the WLP. 
(Kremp et al., 2018; van der Meijden et al., 1984) (See Figure 1C.) Overall, even though 
energetically less efficient and slower-growing, anaerobic pathways offer the distinct 
advantage of net CO2 fixation and high carbon efficiency, where methanol also enables 
reduced fermentation products like acetate, butyrate, ethanol, and, upon metabolic 
engineering, also butanol and acetone. (Kremp & Müller, 2021) For these reasons, anaerobic 
methanol utilization represents a compelling route for developing carbon-efficient 
bioprocesses. Therefore, an optimal production host would need to be a native methylotroph, 
possessing the WLP and, optimally, even ABE production pathways.

2.2 Energetic barriers in C1 utilization 
When utilizing the WLP with C1 gases as a feedstock, the main restriction is the energetic 
limits regarding ATP. Here, the stoichiometry of C1 gases and soluble substrates differs in 
relation to acetate production, where the reaction is balanced through byproducts or co-
substrates such as CO2, H2O, or H2. (see Table 1) However, theoretical stoichiometry often 
doesn’t represent feasibility based solely on theoretical considerations. As an alternative, 
metabolic models can be utilized, which illustrate the stoichiometry of a reaction, which also 
considers co-factor balances, all based on process parameters and an objective function. 
Table 1: Basic Stoichiometry of different feedstocks with their degree of Reduction [y] during 
anaerobic WLP. 
Feedstock [y] 
Stoichiometry (to Acetate[8]) 
H₂ + CO₂ [0] 
4 H₂ + 2 CO₂ → CH₃COOH + 2 H₂O 
CO [2] 
4 CO + 2 H₂O → CH₃COOH + 2 CO₂ 
Formate [2] 
4 HCOOH → CH3COOH + 2 CO₂ + 2 H₂O 
Methanol [6]  
2 CH₃OH → CH₃COOH + H2 + H₂O 
 
When aiming for a full liquid-to-liquid fermentation, using only one of the substrates, methanol 
or formate, might not be sufficient. Formate, with a degree of reduction of 2, needs to be 
balanced with CO2 as a by-product when producing acetate. As shown in A. woodii, during 
acetogenesis from formate, three out of four moles of formate are oxidized to CO2 and H2 to 
provide reducing equivalents. The fourth is assimilated into acetate via the WLP directly. Of 
these oxidized, only one CO2 is reincorporated, and the remaining two may be lost as 
byproducts unless the cell is able to reincorporate them into the WLP. In essence, if the 
reducing equivalents are limited, the CO2 will be lost. (Table 1) (Moon et al., 2021) Similarly, 
methanol cannot be fully converted to acetate by acetogenic bacteria without an external 
electron acceptor or an excess production of hydrogen to balance the reaction. When balanced 
with CO2, methanol supplies methyl-groups, and is partially oxidized to provide electrons for 
CO2 reduction, which is used as carbon in the carbonyl-branch. Without external CO2 or a 
carbonyl-branch-counterpart, stoichiometrically, only 1 mol of acetate can be produced from 4 
mol of methanol, which makes the process unbalanced. (Kremp et al., 2018; Kremp & Müller, 
2021) Alternatively, since methanol has a high degree of reduction (6), the production of 
==PAGE:16==
Background 
9 
acetate from 2 moles results in a redox reaction, which ultimately results in hydrogen 
production. 
In both cases, the cell must carefully balance the distribution to account for the regeneration 
of reduction equivalents and energy conservation, while fulfilling its stoichiometric obligations. 
Based on the degree of reduction from methanol (6) and formate (2), the production of acetate 
(8) is feasible without the production of gaseous byproducts. Hence, a synergistic effect can 
be expected, and this substrate combination could be feasible to substitute the WLP, which 
would be fully powered by soluble C1 substrates.  
2.3 Metabolic engineering of the Clostridium spp.  
Clostridium spp. is a diverse group of anaerobic, spore-forming, Gram-positive bacteria that 
thrive in oxygen-free (anaerobic) environments. They are widely found in soil, water, and the 
gastrointestinal tracts of animals and humans. A key feature of Clostridium species is their 
ability to produce endospores, which are highly resistant to heat, desiccation, and 
disinfectants, allowing them to survive in harsh conditions. (Stackebrandt & Hippe, 2001) The 
industrial application of Clostridium spp. is widely known for their application in ABE 
fermentation, where (most commonly C. acetobutylicum) convert sugars into a mixture of 
acetone, n-butanol, and ethanol. (Kujawska et al., 2015) 
Genetic engineering in Clostridium spp. presents significant challenges compared to more 
established model organisms due to factors such as complex restriction-modification systems, 
high nuclease activity, and the recalcitrance of many species to DNA transformation (Lütke-
Eversloh & Bahl, 2011; Minton et al., 2016). Overcoming these issues, the development of 
specific genetic tools and strategies, such as conjugative DNA transfer, electroporation, 
pseudo-suicide vectors, gene knockout/knock-in strategies, and the use of Group II intron-
based mutagenesis systems like ClosTron, was developed. (Heap et al., 2007, 2010a; Joseph 
et al., 2021; Minton et al., 2016)  
For instance, Heap et al. (2009) established a robust, genus-wide modular shuttle vector 
system for clostridia, which made engineering widely available (Heap et al., 2009). The 
modular shuttle vector system enables the flexible and standardized construction of plasmids 
that can replicate in both E. coli (for easy DNA manipulation) and various Clostridium spp. (for 
functional expression). This system consists of interchangeable modules for essential 
functions: a Gram-positive replicon (for Clostridium spp.), a Gram-negative replicon (for 
E. coli), an antibiotic resistance marker, and an application-specific module (such as a multiple 
==PAGE:17==
Background 
10 
cloning site or reporter gene). Each module is separated by rare restriction sites (AscI, FseI, 
PmeI, SbfI), making it easy to switch modules in a single step. (Heap et al., 2009) 
Additionally, the ClosTron technology allows for efficient mutagenesis, where it utilizes a 
reprogrammable group II intron (from Lactococcus lactis, Ll.LtrB) that can be modified to insert 
into any target gene in the Clostridium genome. Once integrated, the intron disrupts gene 
function, creating a stable knockout mutant. The system uses a modular plasmid backbone 
(compatible with the modular shuttle system) that allows easy customization, including 
replacement of origins, markers, and the intron targeting sequence. Selection of mutants is 
facilitated by an antibiotic marker that becomes active only after successful intron insertion 
(RAM, retrotransposition-activated marker). (Heap et al., 2007, 2010a; Minton et al., 2016)

3.1.2 Cultivation of enrichments  
Multiple soil samples in the greater Copenhagen area were collected to enrich for bacteria from 
environmental samples. To extract the soil sample, soil from approximately 10 cm underground 
was recovered, transferred to a 25 ml test tube, filled with water, and closed with a rubber 
stopper (See Table A 1). After solid particles have settled, 1 mL of the supernatant was used 
to inoculate a test tube with 5 mL base medium and the respective substrate combination. The 
samples were subsequently incubated at 30 ºC and 60 ºC, respectively. 
Once growth was observed through an increase in turbidity, 2 mL of the sample was 
transferred to 50 mL of fresh base media. This was repeated twice, using the second round, 
to store the enrichment as glycerol stocks, containing 45 % (v/v) glycerol, 5% (v/v) YE, and 
50% (v/v) cell culture at -20 ºC until further use.

Pre-cultures for growth experiments were inoculated with 100 µL of enrichment glycerol stock 
in 5 mL acetogenic base medium, containing the specific substrate combination. All vials and 
materials were prepared either in an anoxic environment (anaerobic chamber) or flushed with 
100% N2 before use, to prevent oxygen inhibition. Once exponential growth was reached, 2 mL 
of the 5 mL preculture were used to inoculate a 50 mL culture under the same conditions. After 
reaching exponential growth, 50 mL of this culture was transferred to a reaction tube and 
centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 5 minutes. The supernatant was discarded, and the cell pellet was 
resuspended in 1 mL of fresh base medium. This suspension was then used to inoculate a 
prepared 50 mL vial to reach an optical density (OD) of 0.01. 
For monocultural growth, the respective glycerol stock was streaked on a TSA-agar plate 
(15 g L-1 tryptophan, 5 g L-1 Soy peptone, 5 g L-1 NaCl, 15 g L-1 Agar) and incubated for 3 days. 
After colony formation, a single colony was picked and transferred into a 5 mL vial containing 
the prepared base medium. The vial was incubated overnight at the appropriate temperature. 
The culture was then centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 5 minutes, the supernatant discarded, and 
the pellet resuspended in 1 mL base medium to inoculate the main culture at an OD of 0.01. 
Growth was monitored by measuring optical density, and the vial was equipped with a pressure 
sensor. Samples were at the start and end of the fermentation and stored at -20 °C until further 
use. 
3.2 Strain isolation 
For the strain isolation from an enrichment, 5 mL of a preculture with the respective conditions 
was inoculated from a stored enrichment glycerol stock overnight. The suspension was then 
streaked on an LB-agar plate (10 g L-1 tryptophan, 5 g L-1 YE, 10 g L-1 NaCl, 15 g L-1 Agar) and 
incubated until colonies formed. The plate was then used to restreak single colonies on a 
master plate and incubated under the same conditions. After sufficient cell material had 
formed, the isolates were used to inoculate a 96-well plate containing 200 µL of base medium 
with the original enrichment substrate combination. The plate was then cultivated at 30 ºC. 
Subsequently, colonies able to grow on the substrates were identified. The positive identified 
colonies were used to inoculate an overnight culture and stored as glycerol stocks.

3.3 Hardware Pressure sensors 
The Laerke.eu pressure sensor board was developed in collaboration with FabLab RUC to 
monitor pressure changes in microbial experiments conducted in closed vials. The sensor 
board is a custom-built printed circuit board (PCB) that integrates the MS5837-30BA pressure 
sensor from TE Connectivity. It is connected to a syringe, which can be inserted into an airtight 
vial to detect the headspace pressure. (Unpublished Data, M. Borch, 2024) 
The pressure sensors use Wemos D1 Mini ESP32 microcontrollers for continuous monitoring 
and real-time data logging. They detect gas pressure changes with digital pressure 
transducers, transmitting data via Wi-Fi to the ‘ThingSpeak’ platform for central storage and 
live observation. The system allows data collection every 15 seconds, facilitating detailed 
monitoring of gas consumption or production. Each sensor is configured with unique API keys 
and ThingSpeak channels for efficient data management.

3.4.4 Metabolic Model 
The metabolic model was constructed in Python on jupyter notebooks accessible on Github 
(Github Repository, n.d.). A reduced model was derived from an existing genome-scale 
metabolic model of Clostridium ljungdahlii using the COBRApy (Constraint-based 
Reconstruction and Analysis) toolbox (Ebrahim et al., 2013; Nagarajan et al., 2013).  
The reduction was done by transferring all reactions of the WLP to a new and empty model. 
After manual curation, additional uptake and conversion reactions were added, including 
formate and methanol. A formate transporter was added, as well as the methyltransferase 
reaction (MtaA) to incorporate methanol into the WLP. 
The metabolic model was further refined and customized using Python in Jupyter Notebooks. 
Specific manipulations and adjustments to the model were implemented to simulate key 
metabolic processes and optimize for acetate production. Boundary conditions were defined 
to represent metabolite sinks and exchanges using the COBRApy toolbox. This included 
defining ATP, ADP, inorganic phosphate as sinks and external metabolites such as water, 
hydrogen, acetate, carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, formate, and methanol as exchange 
reactions. All reactions in the model were set to be bidirectional by adjusting their flux bounds 
to [-1000, 1000]. The medium composition was modified to define environmental conditions, 
setting uptake and secretion rates for key metabolites. Acetate production was defined as the

conditions. Flux distributions were visualized through a visual map of the metabolic network 
using Escher, integrating the model, flux data, and a JSON representation of the reduced 
Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway (King et al., 2015).

constraints under a specific objective function. With different parameters, the result would 
differ. Additionally, they do not reflect enzyme kinetics, regulation, or thermodynamic feasibility 
beyond basic mass balance. As a result, predictions made by the model may not fully reflect

(methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase) catalyzed oxidation is stoichiometrically possible but 
kinetically hindered. The reaction is highly endergonic, which makes it unfavorable and 
requires a coupled redox partner. Specifically, the oxidation of methyl-THF is coupled to a 
positive standard free energy change (ΔG°’ ≈ +23 kJ/mol), and does not efficiently mediate 
this reaction on its own, nor is it stimulated by typical co-reductants such as reduced ferredoxin 
(Bertsch et al., 2015; Maden, 2000). However, when a cell extract or a protein fraction enriched 
in methylene-THF dehydrogenase (MTHF-DH) is added, significant NAD⁺ reduction as a 
coupled redox partner is observed, indicating the need for another protein to drive the reaction 
forward by quickly consuming the methylene-THF product (Bertsch et al., 2015). Therefore, an 
oxidative methyl branch is possible in the cellular context, but it requires an additional 
mechanism or metabolic partner to overcome the energetic and kinetic barriers inherent to the 
reaction. This illustrates the major limitation of purely stoichiometric predictions, they do not 
guarantee physiological feasibility.  
During simultaneous methanol and formate utilization, a flux of 0.1 methanol is oxidized, 
contributing to a 0.2 flux loss of CO2. This can be linked to the need to oxidize formate first to 
CO2 for it to be reduced to CO. These steps use different redox carriers meaning that some 
energy is lost. Since this value represents the approach of balancing redox and carbon fluxes 
based solely on stoichiometric constraints, as a consequence of methanol oxidation, the 
kinetics in vivo couldn’t drive methanol oxidation. This highlights the importance of testing 
theories experimentally to account for kinetics. Although genome-scale models have 
limitations, they can reveal interesting substrate targets, as demonstrated here with methanol 
and formate. This model predicts that, indeed, a simultaneous uptake can be expected, where 
methanol substitutes for the methyl branch and formate substitutes for the carbonyl branch. 
While CO2 is predicted to be produced under this mixed feed, it is a favorable combination 
compared to solely formate or methanol, as it conserves more of the carbon. Although 
methanol appears to be the best substrate for acetate production, it presents an unbalanced 
scenario with an excess of hydrogen and no ATP production. Additionally, the oxidation is 
initiated by MTHFR, which is kinetically hindered.

4.3 Clostridium toolbox development 
From a previous enrichment on methanol, a new strain was identified. Its genome was 
assembled from metagenomic sequencing of the mixed enrichment culture and included in a 
phylogenetic analysis of the Clostridium genus.  
4.3.1 Phylogenetic analysis of Clostridium genus  
Out of 97 proteomes analyzed, 76 single-copy orthologs were identified, with their 
corresponding protein IDs, names, and gene names listed in Table A 4. A multiple sequence 
alignment (MSA) of the concatenated ortholog sequences was used to evaluate 224 
evolutionary models, with the best-fitting model determined to be LG+F+I+G4. This 
LG+F+I+G4 model is an Le and Gascuel (LG) amino acid replacement model derived using 
maximum likelihood from a large and diverse set of protein alignments, incorporating rate 
heterogeneity across sites. The +F component uses empirical amino acid frequencies 
calculated directly from the input alignment, rather than relying on default frequencies from the 
LG matrix. The +I component accounts for a proportion of sites that remain evolutionarily 
invariant, while +G4 accounts for rate variation across sites using a gamma distribution divided 
into four rate classes. Together, these components provide a robust framework for modeling 
evolutionary relationships. (Le & Gascuel, 2008; Nguyen et al., 2015) 
The resulting phylogenetic tree, representing inferred evolutionary relationships, was 
optimized through several rounds and evaluated for robustness using 1000 ultrafast bootstrap 
(UFBoot) replicates. UFBoot is a rapid approximation method for assessing branch support, 
offering reliable confidence estimates with significantly reduced computational time. (Minh et 
al., 2013; Nguyen et al., 2015) Most branches showed high support, with UFBoot values close 
to 100, and only a few branches exhibited lower support, the lowest being 29.7. (Figure 7) 
Methylotrophy is a rare trait, found only in C. luticellarii, C. magnum, M. Thermoaceticum, and 
C. formicaceticum (excluded from this dataset). Carbon monoxide dehydrogenase is widely 
distributed, but the reductive methyl branch of the WLP is less abundant, as shown by the 
scarce presence of the Methylene-THF reductase. ABE solventogenesis and its acetoacetate 
decarboxylase marker gene is also relatively uncommon in the genus. Notably, the 
combination of all four traits, methylotrophy, WLP, chain-elongation, and ABE, is exceedingly 
rare, highlighting the potential value of exploring non-model or novel organisms that possess 
these unique metabolic capabilities. 
It is important to note that every phylogenetic tree is a hypothesis of evolutionary relationships, 
based on limited data and specific methods used for analysis. Here, the tree was constructed 
using a specific set of input data and selected single-copy orthologs, and therefore reflects one 
==PAGE:36==
Results 
29 
possible interpretation rather than an absolute truth. In this case, the tree was constructed 
using a concatenation of single-copy orthologs within a taxa and is supported by strong 
bootstrap values. Therefore, while it remains an interpretation, it reflects a well-supported and 
robust tree. The marker genes used to identify metabolic traits reflect the presence of genes 
identified by sequence homology to belong to the same gene family. This does not exclude the 
possibility that the gene has acquired a new function or that an unrelated gene fulfils that 
function. Each strain would need to be individually examined to confirm that the phenotype 
matches. Nevertheless, the tree provides a valuable overview, highlighting the fact that the 
combination of all four traits: Mehylotrophy, the WLP, chain elongation and, ABE production 
pathways, is rare. These are valuable traits that enhance the industrial applicability of 
acetogenic bacteria by enabling the use of alternative feedstocks, efficient carbon fixation, and 
solvent production. M. thermoacetica, a key model organism, possesses both methylotrophy 
and the WLP, but lacks the native ABE fermentation pathway and remains genetically 
challenging to engineer, limiting its use for solvent production (Jia et al., 2023). This supports 
the concept of selecting strains based on desired native features. This framework enables 
identifying the closest genetically characterized relatives and exploring how they have been 
successfully engineered as a guide for future strain development of C. magnum.

4.3.2 Analysis and tool development strategy for the non-model organism C magnum 
Based on the phylogenetic analysis, the strain isolate from a previous enrichment is a close 
genetic match to C magnum, indicating that the isolate is indeed C. magnum. Although 
sequencing results are still pending, the isolated species from this study is a mesophilic, 
anaerobic firmicute capable of methylotrophic acetyl-CoA synthesis. Given that methylotrophy 
is such a rare trait, it is possible that the presented enrichment again isolated this same species 
or a closely related one. As established, C. magnum harbors a combination of genes that make 
it a promising candidate for investigating as a platform host for chemical production. The 
illustrated evolutionary connections allow for the use of C. magnum as a reference point to 
explore successful engineering strategies and assess their applicability to the isolate. Since 
there are no existing tools for C. magnum, its closest engineerable relative can be used as a 
reference to investigate an in silico engineering strategy.  
4.3.2.1 Cloning strategy 
As shown in the phylogenetic tree, the closest relatives of C. magnum, in this prediction, are 
C. acidotolerans and C. thailandense (Figure 7B). Both were characterized (Chaikitkaew et al., 
2022; Y.-J. Lee et al., 2007), but have not been genetically engineered yet. The next closest 
relative is predicted to be C. carboxidivorans (Figure 7C). This species was successfully 
engineered for the overexpression of enzymes relevant for solvent production (alcohol 
dehydrogenase) (Cheng et al., 2019). Here, the modular shuttle vector pMTL82151 was used, 
as previously constructed by Yu et al. (2011) for the modification of C. tyrobutyricum. This 
expression plasmid belongs to the pMTL80000 series, established by Heap et. al (2009), 
specifically designed for Clostridium spp. (Heap et al., 2009) Following the naming of this 
modular shuttle vector system, pMTL82151 contains the Gram-positive replicon pBP1 (2), the 
chloramphenicol-selectable marker catP (1), the Gram-negative replicon ColE1+tra (5), and 
an application-specific module consisting of a multiple cloning site (MCS) (1). (Heap et al., 
2009) Due to the phylogenetic proximity of C. carboxidivorans and the nature of this modular, 
proven to work in a broad range of Clostridium spp., system, it can be postulated, that this 
pMTL80000 series can be applied for C. magnum.  
The plasmid pMTL82151 was originally designed for C. tyrobutyricum (Yu et al., 2011). 
However, for C. carboxidivorans, a specific Gram-positive replicon called p19 has been 
described (Kottenhahn et al., 2023). To adapt the vector for C. magnum, pMTL88151 was 
selected, which replaces the original Gram-positive replicon with p19 (number 2 change to 8), 
while the other modules remained the same.

4.3.2.2 RM systems in C. magnum and a suitable transformation strategy 
C. magnum harbors restriction-modification systems (RM), which could complicate the 
transformation of circular DNA. Here, a putative "BsuMI modification methylase subunit YdiP" 
for C. magnum was found using REbase (rebase.neb) (refer to Table A6). RM systems 
degrade unmethylated double-stranded DNA and hinder transformation methods like 
electroporation (Kottenhahn et al., 2023). Conjugation avoids this issue by transferring 
plasmids as single-stranded DNA, improving transformation efficiency (Purdy et al., 2002). Like 
C. magnum (and most Clostridium spp.), C. carboxidivorans harbors RM systems as well 
(Kottenhahn et al., 2023). However, this species has been successfully transformed with 
recombinant plasmid via conjugation, utilizing a common donor strain E. coli CA434 (Cheng et 
al., 2019; Williams et al., 1990). This can be similarly applied to C. magnum.

4.3.2.3 Knock-out strategy 
As part of the genetic engineering strategy for C. magnum, a knockout approach is proposed 
to selectively remove specific genomic features. For this, the ClosTron system for a genome-
targeted knock-out can be adapted by retargeting the group II intron to insert into a specific 
DNA sequence, chosen using the TargeTron algorithm (provided by Clostron.com). As a 
potential target for a knock-out, methylase could be chosen. This could enable an 
electroporation protocol, making future strain engineering easier. When chosing the putative 
"BsuMI modification methylase subunit YdiP" as a target, a ClosTron score of 7.438 indicates 
a suitability for group II intron insertion (using ClosTron.com online tool). The scoring is based 
on a log-odds calculation comparing how well a DNA segment matches the preferred group II 
intron target site relative to random genomic sequences, where 7.438 means that this particular 
sequence is a strong predicted target, about 27.438 (roughly 173 times) more likely to be 
recognized for successful intron insertion than expected by chance (Perutka et al., 2004) The 
Intron binding site (IBS), Exon binding site (EBS) 1d, and -S2 primers bind to and mutate 
specific subdomains within the Ll.LtrB intron sequence when used in a splicing by overlap 
extension (SOE) PCR (Figure 9). These mutations change the intron’s recognition elements 
to match the gene target, ensuring site-specific integration. The IBS primer includes a HindIII 
recognition site, and the EBS1d primer a BsrGI recognition site. The EBS Universal primer is 
used during PCR to amplify the modified intron (exon target and recognition sites), which is 
then cloned into a ClosTron plasmid through a restriction digest by HindIII and BsrGI. Lastly, 
the plasmid is introduced into the host for targeted intron insertion and gene knockout.(Heap 
et al., 2010b) (Table 7, Figure 9)

4.3.2.4 Towards successful in vivo cloning for Clostridium spp. 
The preliminary genetic engineering toolbox for C. magnum presented in this work will need 
experimental data to confirm the effectiveness of the shuttle system. For instance, each strain 
is different regarding the effectiveness of antibiotics and promoter strengths (Bienick et al., 
2014; Woodford, 2005). Therefore, it is crucial to assess these features before implementing 
the cloning strategy and if necessary, replace the antibiotic module on the plasmid backbone. 
The use of antibiotics as selection markers in Clostridium spp. is generally challenging due to 
the limited range of effective antibiotics, the nature of their gram-positive cell wall and 
difficulties maintaining plasmid stability without constant selection pressure (Heap et al., 2009). 
Additionally, relying on antibiotic selection can leave behind unwanted marker genes, which 
complicates further genetic work and limits the application of the strain (Boudignon et al., 2023; 
Foulquier et al., 2019). Furthermore, to test for a suitable antibiotic, the promoter strength 
necessary for governing the expression of the gene of interest is required. To assess the 
promoter strength, the expression level of a reporter gene, such as a fluorescent protein, 
governed by the promoter to be tested, can be evaluated. The fluorescence intensity relative 
to cell density is then normalized to obtain a standardized relative fluorescence value. The 
result is further normalized against a negative control strain lacking the reporter, allowing for 
direct comparison of the promoter’s ability to drive gene expression under different conditions. 
(Feng et al., 2024) For Clostridium, both constitutive (e.g., thlA, adc) and inducible promoters 
(e.g., bgaR-PbgaL in C. perfringens; xylose-inducible in C. acetobutylicum) for controlled gene 
expression are common (Feng et al., 2024; Hartman et al., 2011). Similarly to promoters, a 
range of ribosomal binding site (RBS) for optimal protein expression could be tested in the 
host. Commonly used RBSs in Clostridium include the native RBS from the thlA gene in 
C. acetobutylicum, which has been extensively characterized, is frequently used, and presents 
a strong starting point (Gyulev et al., 2018).

5 Discussion 
The metabolic model’s central hypothesis that co-feeding methanol and formate enables a 
synergistic metabolism is supported by the experimental data. Methanol is only consumed 
when both substrates are supplied. More importantly, the co-feeding condition leads to 
improved performance compared to the single substrates across all metrics, with greater 
acetate production, higher biomass formation, and the ability to reassimilate metabolic gases. 
This validates the metabolic model’s prediction of a synergistic effect experimentally. Although 
the exact stoichiometry of uptake differed from the simulated scenario, the model's core 
prediction that combining the two substrates enables enhanced growth and product formation 
is shown. The model outputs reflect possible metabolic states that are not necessarily feasible 
in vivo, due to thermodynamic, kinetic, or regulatory limitations. However, the experimentally 
confirmed synergistic effect, the products formed, and the identification of the isolate as a 
Clostridial species support the hypothesis that the underlying metabolism is the methylotrophic 
acetyl-CoA synthesis. This shows that metabolic modeling can effectively guide experiments, 
even if the exact results differ from predictions. Notably, a main difference is the observed 
substrate uptake of equimolar usage. Experimentally, formate was primarily metabolized, while 
methanol uptake was limited. This highlights the importance of considering biological context, 
such as substrate accessibility, gene regulation, and bacterial composition. 
Their underlying metabolism could explain the observed substrate preference for formate over 
methanol. Formate is oxidized in a single core enzymatic step to CO2, providing an immediate 
energy source. In contrast, methanol metabolism requires the induction of an auxiliary 
methyltransferase system and draws from the membrane potential (Kremp et al., 2018). This 
results in delayed methanol utilization, explaining why formate is prioritized during early growth 
(Humphreys et al., 2022; Wood, Marcellin, et al., 2022). This aligns with the gas pattern 
observed during co-feeding, where gas production begins within 5 hours, a much shorter lag 
phase than typically reported for methanol-based growth, which can exceed 45 hours (Kremp 
et al., 2018). The early gas production in this experiment suggests that formate is consumed 
first, where an initial metabolism driven by formate oxidation. This is followed by a second 
phase, characterized by mixed substrate consumption, where gas is reassimilated, likely 
corresponding to delayed methanol metabolism and the need for primary formate oxidation. A 
similar sequential substrate utilization has been observed in defined cultures. For instance, 
Wood et al. (2022) demonstrated that E. limosum first consumes formate, with methanol 
uptake beginning only after formate depletion (~25 hours) (Wood, Marcellin, et al., 2022). Here, 
the shift initiates a second growth phase, which enables the production of more reduced end-
products, where they report butyrate and small amounts of butanol. The extent of this shift 
==PAGE:45==
Discussion 
38 
depends on the methanol-to-formate ratio, where higher methanol availability leads to greater 
production of reduced compounds, supporting redox balancing and consistent with predictions 
from genome-scale models. (Wood, Marcellin, et al., 2022) This behavior is also reflected in 
B. methylotrophicum, where formate consistently acts as the preferred substrate, resulting in 
an acetate-dominated product profile when methanol is limited (Humphreys et al., 2022). The 
observed enrichment behavior exhibits a similar pattern, underlining that initial formate 
metabolism is a common strategy among acetogens. Even when methanol uptake is minimal, 
its presence can still provide a metabolic advantage. Growth on formate alone results in 
significant carbon loss due to CO2 accumulation, where typically three out of every four formate 
molecules are oxidized and lost (Moon et al., 2021). Co-feeding methanol, even in small 
amounts, can partially offset this by enhancing carbon retention and increasing acetate yield. 
This supports the idea that methanol plays a critical role in redox balancing, even if its uptake 
is delayed or low. (Kremp & Müller, 2021; Wood, Marcellin, et al., 2022) The limited methanol 
uptake observed in this experiment may also be due to the relatively short fermentation time. 
In other systems, such as B. methylotrophicum grown with CO, formate, and methanol, 
methanol is fully consumed over longer timeframes (~240 h) (Humphreys et al., 2022). 
Alternatively, regulatory delays or kinetic limitations of the methyltransferase system may 
restrict methanol usage during growth. In E. limosum, methyltransferase genes are strongly 
upregulated during methanol growth, suggesting substrate-induced activation (Wood, 
Gonzalez-Garcia, et al., 2022). Kinetic data further show that MtaB, the core methyltransferase 
enzyme, is not inherently slow, and in principle should support efficient methyl-group transfer 
once the system is active (Hagemeier et al., 2006).  
While methanol and formate co-feeding demonstrates strong potential for efficient carbon 
conversion, the results reveal that acetogenic systems can also enhance carbon retention 
during glucose fermentation. Specifically, 79.7% of the glucose carbon was recovered in 
measurable products, exceeding the typical 66.7% yield observed in standard ethanol 
fermentations. This suggests that the enriched culture may facilitate improved carbon 
conservation, which is possibly through reassimilation of the produced CO₂ during carbon-
fixing pathways (Hill et al., 2025). This underlines the broader applicability of acetogenic 
metabolism beyond one-carbon compounds. Hence, mixed-acetogen systems may offer 
potential for high-efficiency fermentation across a variety of carbon sources, not just one-
carbon compounds.

Conclusion and Outlook 
39 
6 Conclusion and Outlook 
This work gives a comprehensive overview of a workflow towards the optimization of carbon-
neutral fermentations. To achieve this, a metabolic model of the WLP was developed and 
tested for a potential synergistic combination of C1 substrates. Additionally, microbes were 
enriched on methanol and formate from Danish soil, to identify capable candidates. After 
characterizing a successful enrichment, the synergistic effect predicted by the metabolic model 
was confirmed in growth experiments. Here, methanol uptake enables reassimilation of 
produced gas, while only on formate or glucose, gaseous byproducts are lost. In contrast to 
the metabolic model, the uptake of methanol and formate was found to be neither equimolar 
nor simultaneous. While an initial gas-producing phase is based on formate, the gas 
reassimilating growth phase is enabled by methanol utilization. 
To further validate the insights gained from this work, it is essential to follow up with 
experimental work. For instance, the enrichment and the strain isolate should be identified 
through metagenomics analysis. This would enable a more detailed analysis of methylotrophy 
and potential missing genes. Also, to assess whether methanol is the prerequisite for gas 
reassimilation and can improve growth performance in the strain isolate, the growth experiment 
should be repeated in triplicates and be grown on formate as a control. To address the poor 
quantification of methanol, a colorimetric assay could be introduced to increase accuracy in 
determination. Additionally, after comparing the gained insights to the literature, it would be 
interesting to assess different ratios of methanol to formate to investigate the influence on 
product formation and byproduct loss. Overall, a better overview of the metabolite production 
and consumption during the different growth phases could be achieved by sampling during the 
fermentation, besides the initial and final time points. Importantly, all current findings were 
obtained under batch cultivation, where a steady state is not achieved. Transitioning to 
continuous fermentation may facilitate complete L2L conversion over time and enhance control 
over substrate utilization and gas dynamics.
