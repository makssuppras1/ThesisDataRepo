Abstract
This paper introduces a compiler to automate the translation process from choreographies
to local behaviors of individual agents in security protocols. The syntax for both languages
and translation semantics are provided by the underlying theoretical work of Mödersheim
et al. [1]. In contrast to common security protocols, choreographies enable specifications
to include a tree-like decision structure for agents to choose from and follow. Local behav-
iors can be derived from choreographies and include individual actions of a participating
actor useful for attack analysis processes, with e.g. protocol verification tools, that can
provide more assurance in the security of a protocol. The compiler respects given rules
while handling non-trivial tasks, such as term de-, construction and composition, check
generation, and frame branching. Alongside examples, internal classes of the compiler
are highlighted and the translation process is explained. Also, decisions slightly altering
the underlying approach are discussed.

Introduction
In 2021 Bruni et al. [2] informally introduced a language for describing choreographies
in security protocols. Mödersheim et al. [1] are taking this approach further and define
two formal languages. First, using a choreography language agents can be defined that
each have an initial knowledge, a set of terms, and can perform actions, such as sending
another actor a message. In comparison to existing specification languages, the chore-
ography language enables writers to define transmission steps with multiple messages
actors can choose from and belonging steps depicting how the choreography should con-
tinue. Second, with a local behavior level language actions by a particular agent in a
choreography can be listed. It includes the actors initial knowledge and holds steps the
actor is part of in the original choreography. On receiving a message, local checks are
made to first validate the message and second distinguish how to continue afterwards.
The compiler this paper presents is made to derive local behaviors from a given choreog-
raphy. The translation process is based on semantics theoretically specified by Möder-
sheim et al. [1] and involves further foundational tasks, such as term de-, and construc-
tion, check generation, and check and choice node positioning. During translation, the
compiler is responsible to keep track of known terms by the agent being translated and
must offer the actor choices when the choreography demands it. Furthermore, the com-
piler offers extensibility of formats, such as functions that need to be handled in a certain
way. In this work algebraic theory used for modular exponentiation is not considered since
terms could otherwise not be seen as equal. Also, this work is based on an older draft of
Mödersheim et al. [1] and it does not include the recent addition of atomic operations.
In chapter 2 relevant information is taken and explained from the work of Mödersheim et
al. Chapter 3 goes over the design and implementation of the compiler while explaining
a translation procedure. Then, chapter 4 goes over the next steps and states how the
compiler can be part of a larger process. Lastly, chapter 5 concludes this paper.

Background
In their paper Mödersheim et al. [1] define semantics for a choreography and a local
behavior level language as well as rules on how to derive and properly translate the former
to the latter. Syntaxes for both levels are using the Alice and Bob language by Almousa
et al. [3] as a foundation.

Local Behavior
In listing 2.3 the syntax of local behaviors is displayed. First the initial knowledge of
an actor in a choreography must be stated, see line 1. Then, the behavior of a local
role L mirrors the individual actions of the actor and additionally handles the verifica-
tion of received messages using assignment checks, line 6, and equal checks, line 7.
Based on checks made by an actor it is also decided with which step to continue af-
ter a received transmission. Assignment checks include a verification and a destructor
operation in one line. Using try l1 = dcrypt(inv(pk(A)),crypt(pk(A),msg), for ex-
ample, will automatically verify crypt(pk(A),msg) removing the necessity of an extra if
vcrypt(crypt(pk(A),msg)) and assigns msg to l1 increasing the readability of the follow-
ing steps wherever the decrypted message is used. Equal checks are used if a received
term or subterm can be composed with terms the actor already knows since it is expected
that they are equal. Mödersheim et al. [1] require that all possible checks are made in a
local behavior of an agent after receiving a term from another actor.

The compiler is developed in the Java programming language and uses the ANTLR library
[4] to parse and transform a choreography from an input file into a compilable abstract
syntax tree (AST) structure. Its goal is to produce files for each involved agent containing

Here the start rule summarizes the entire specification. Each agent needs to be specified
alongside their initial knowledge which is stated by a knwl+. Afterwards a choreography
step is expected fulfilling the rule choreo. It can either be empty, a transmission of one or
more messages to choose from alongside a continuation choreo, see rule choice, or a
definition of one or more constants by an actor. A transmission message as well as any
items in the actors initial knowledge follow the term rule. A term can either be a function
with terms as arguments, a constant, or a MAC which is a shorthand for a specific function.
We allow terms, choices and choreos to be surrounded by parenthesis.
3.2
Abstract Syntax
With the help of Java classes we define structures the ANTLR visitor can use to store
pieces of a given choreography after successful parsing.
Start
This class is used to hold values matching the start rule in the ANTLR grammar for
initializing the translation process. It contains all actors alongside their initial knowledge
as well as the root choreography step.
Attributes: List<Knowledge> knowledges, Choreo choreo
Knowledge
A knowledge instance belongs to an agent and holds a list of known terms to the agent.
Attributes: String agent, List<Term> knowledge
Term
We use an abstract class Term as a superclass for all possible types of terms.

Attributes: String x
Function extends Term
A function consists of a name and one or more terms as arguments. It provides getKey

Attributes: String name, List<Term> args

We use an abstract class Choreo as a superclass for all possible types of choreography

Empty extends Choreo
This class is used to place the end of a choreography branch in the AST.
Message extends Choreo
A message instance holds information about a transmission made by agentFrom to agentTo.
Since more than one message can be offered as a choice to the transmitting actor a list

Attributes: String agentFrom, String agentTo, List<Choice> choices, String label
Definition extends Choreo
When an actor defines new constants, this class is used to store the involved agent, the
defined constants, and the choreography step with which to continue afterwards.
Attributes: String agent, List<Constant> constants, Choreo choreography
Frame
A frame instance holds a state of knowledge for an agent and provides functions for term
composition and check generation. It is explained further in section 3.4.
Attributes: Map<String, Term> knowledge, Map<Term, Term> recipes, List<String> la-

Choice
A choice is always a message to send alongside a continuation Choreo instance. It is
mainly used in Message instances.
Attributes: Term message, Choreo choreography
Additionally, we use the following helper classes during translation.
Check
The Check class simply holds two terms, one left and one right of the equal sign as well
as a boolean deciding if the check is an assignment (try x = dstruct(y) do ..) or an
equal check (if x = y then ..).
Attributes: Boolean isAssignment, Term left, Term right
Node<T>
A simple node class allowing a payload with a generic type and storing a set of children

Attributes: T payload, Set<Node<T>> children

A custom node structure used as Node<ChoiceCheckNode> storing either a Check instance
or an Pair<Frame, Choreo> agentPair. If both are null, it is considered a root node.

3.4
Frame
A frame, implemented as a class, holds a state of knowledge an agent has at a certain
point in a choreography. Known terms are stored within a Map<String, Term> where keys
are unique labels pointing to unique terms. For adding a term to an agents knowledge the
add function is used. As a parameter the added term is expected. An optional preferred
label can be specified. The new term is only added if it could not have been composed with
known terms before. On addition, the frame generates a new unique label using a simple
local counter that increases with each added term. The function returns a Pair<Term,
Boolean> that holds a recipe composing the added term or a knowledge label pointing
to it and a boolean indicating if the term was added (true) or a recipe was given instead
(false). For example, the initial knowledge of actor A from the choreography example
in listing 2.2 would look like this inside a frame: [l1 →sk(A,s), l2 →A, l3 →s,
l4 →B, l5 →pk(B), l6 →msg, l7 →upd]. On calling add(M), the function will

create a new label and store M under l8 returning Pair(l8,true). However, when calling
add(sk(A,s)) the function will simply respond with Pair(l1,false) since the term is
already part of the knowledge. Also, when calling add(crypt(sk(A,s)),pair(msg,upd)
it will not be added directly to knowledge either. Instead a recipe is constructed using the
compose function explained below. Pair(crypt(l1,pair(l6,l7))) is returned.
The function compose expects a term that it tries to produce a recipe for with the knowl-
edge available in the frame. Recipes represent a composition only consisting of public
functions and knowledge labels. Given a frame F and a term t, a recipe r is composed
and returned such that F(r) = t, if it cannot be composed null is returned instead.
If t is not a public function, it cannot be composed unless it is known. If it is, F can
compose a recipe r if all arguments of t can be composed. For example, with a frame
containing the initial knowledge of agent A shown above, compose(B) would result in l4
while compose(C) would result in null since one is known and the other is not. The
same principle is valid for compose(inv(pk(B)) since inv is a private function. A term
like compose(crypt(sk(A,s),pair(msg,upd))) however is not directly known either, but
can be composed using public functions and known terms. The recipe would then be:
crypt(l1,pair(l6,l7)).
Lastly, the analyze function is used to generate all possible checks for knowledge labels
that have not been analyzed yet. To keep track of a labels status, a frame instance con-
tains three local lists of labels, namely labelsNew, labelsHold, and labelsDone. When
created in the frame, all knowledge labels are added to the labelsNew list. The algorithm
in the analyze function goes through all items in the labelsNew list. After the iteration
of a label has started is it removed from the labelsNew list. If the term belonging to the
iterating label is a simple constant, not a registered function, or a registered function but
the analyzable parameter is false, the knowledge label is immediately added to the done
list since no further checks are required and the iteration continues to the next label. In all
other cases, the term is a registered function and we can use the stored attributes, see
table 3.1, to check it further. If the function requires a key to verify and extract its contents,
which is the case for crypt, scrypt, and sign, we require the needed key to be compos-
able with the actors knowledge. If the key can be composed, the decrypted contents are
added to the frame using the add function and an assignment check is added to the list of
checks. If the key is not composable, the iterating label is added to the hold list and will
be analyzed again if a new term is added later that could possibly be the necessary key.
For all other registered functions, assignment and equal checks are constructed similarly
without requiring a key argument.
As an example, again using the initial knowledge of A from above, calling add(M) will gen-
erate and add label l8 to labelsNew and return Pair(l8,true) since M was not part of the
agents knowledge. As soon as analyze is called l8 is moved to labelsDone since no fur-
ther checks are necessary for a simple constant. But when adding scrypt(sk(A,s),K),
add will return Pair(l9,true) adding l9 to labelsNew. Calling analyze will result in
an assignment check try l10 = dscrypt(l1,l9) being part of the actors local behavior
since the decrypted contents of the message are added to the actors knowledge under the
condition the actor can compose the required decryption key. If a received message can
be composed by the actor, equal checks are used to validate that they are indeed equal
which we expect. If we would add pair(M,K) now, for example, considering the cur-
rent knowledge, the checks if (l8 = π1(pair(M,K))) and if (l9 = π2(pair(M,K)))
would be added to the local behavior since M and K already exist in the frame.
All checks are collected in a List<Check>. After analyzing outstanding labels, gathered
checks are sorted by assignment checks first and returned to the translation process.

3.5
Translation Process
With the help of the ANTLR parser, we identify involved agents, register their initial knowl-
edge and construct an AST of choreography steps as nodes. For every agent we now
instantiate a frame with the agents initial knowledge and pair it up with the root choreog-
raphy step. This results in a List<Pair<Frame, Choreography>> for each of the involved
agents. A compile function loops through each agent, renders their initial knowledge and
triggers the local behavior translation by calling compileAgent for each agent.
The compileAgent function gets passed the agent to compile as a String, the agents
pairs as a List<Pair<Frame, Choreography>> and a depth parameter used for indenta-
tion as an Integer. The function implements the translation semantics given by Möder-
sheim et al. [1] and returns the translated local behavior of the agent as a String. First,
we want to filter out any choreography steps the agent being compiled is not involved in.
Filtered out steps are replaced by their continuations. Second, we check of which type of
action the choreography steps in the pairs are. As described in chapter 2, we require all
choreography steps at the same level to be of the same type of action and to fulfill further
requirements dependent on the action type. Last, if a list of pairs is empty, consists of
choreography steps of different action types, or does not fulfill certain action type require-
ments, we declare the specification as ill-defined and end the translation process.
If all Choreo instances in the agentPairs are of type Empty, the function will simply return
a ”0” indented to the given depth.
If all Choreo instances in the agentPairs are of type Definition and define constant(s)
with the same identifier(s), we iterate through each agentPair, create a new Frame in-
stance based on the frame from the agent pair, add the defined constant(s) to the frame,
and create a new agent pair with the updated frame and the continuation Choreo instance.
With a list of new agentPairs, the compileAgent function is called recursively appending
it to the current translation string.
If all Choreo instances in the agentPairs are of type Message, hold the current agent
as agentFrom, have the same number of choices across all Message instances, and all
messages are composable by the frame from the same pair, we iterate through each
agentPair and inner loop the List<Choice> of the Message. For each Choice, the mes-
sage for the send step is composed and is added to the translation string. After each
send statement the following steps are translated by calling compileAgent with the cur-
rent frame and the continuation choreography. If two or more choices exist in a pair, a
plus sign is rendered in-between clarifying from which choreography steps the actor can
choose.
If all Choreo instances in the agentPairs are of type Message and hold the current agent as
agentTo, we produce a list of agent pairs to choose from and belonging checks needed
(List<Pair<Pair<Frame, Choreo>, List<Check>>>) named choicesChecks using the
frames analyze function after adding the choices message. We expect the numbered la-
bel of the added message to be the same across all agent pairs and their choices. Thus,
we render the receive statement once using the new label. After receiving a message,
as previously mentioned, it is required to perform all checks possible to on the one hand
ensure the validity of the message and on the other distinguish which choreography steps
to continue with. Using the collected choicesChecks, Node<ChoiceCheckNode>s are con-
structed positioning checks and choices in a tree structure. It is important to note that
the helper class Node only allows unique children solving the challenge of combining the
same checks one the same depth of the tree. Passing the current agent, a set of nodes,
and a depth parameter, a function named renderNodes is called to render all checks and
choices in a given tree recursively and returning a translation String. Initially the root
children are passed. renderNodes separates nodes containing checks and choice agent

Future Works
In order verify procedures defined using the choreography language, protocol verifica-
tion tools can be used, such as the Open-Source Fixed-Point Model-Checker (OFMC)
by Basin et al. [5]. However, a translation process is required to transform the informal
language into a readable format verification tools understand. After deriving local instruc-
tion sets for each actor in a choreography using the compiler this paper presented, future
works could include a translation of individual behaviors to the High-Level Protocol Spec-
ification Language (HLPSL) that is directly or indirectly supported by multiple verification
tools [6], like OFMC. Protocol verification tools can then provide possible attack traces on
choreography steps if found and help the specification writers to validate security goals
in their work. An ANTLR grammar for parsing local behaviors our compiler produces can
be found in listing A.3.
In addition to choreography verification, an improved visualization of specifications might
help writers and readers understand the steps and choices involved agents can follow. A
future step could therefore involve creating a tool for displaying defined procedures, simi-
lar to the graphical user interface (GUI) developed for OFMC by Hoffman and Edvardsson
[7]. Possibly the usage of custom labels allowed in the choreography level grammar, see
listing 3.1 line 36 parameter l, could help identifying transmission decisions in a GUI.
Combining both ideas, a visualization application could include attack traces analyzed by
verification tools alongside a displayed choreography easing the process of writing more
secure procedures.

Conclusion
All in all, this paper has presented the workings of a compiler that effectively translates
choreographies into local behaviors for specified agents, adhering to the translation se-
mantics defined by Mödersheim et al. [1] based on the formal semantics of the AnB
language by Almousa et al. [3]. The development of the translation process involved
solving multiple challenges, including term construction and deconstruction, check gen-
eration, and correct placement of check and choice nodes for local branching. Despite
the scope limitations, such as the exclusion of modular exponentiation, the compiler of-
fers extensibility, e.g. in handling specific function formats. The background detailed in
chapter 2 served as a foundation for the design and implementation outlined in chapter 3.
The envisioned future work discussed in chapter 4 presents how this compiler can fill a
useful part in the tool-chain for creating analyzable choreographies in security protocols.

This paper introduces a compiler to automate the translation process from choreographies to local
behaviors of individual agents in security protocols. The syntax for both languages and translation
semantics are provided by the underlying theoretical work of Mödersheim et al. [1]. In contrast
to common security protocols, choreographies enable specifications to include a tree-like decision
structure for agents to choose from and follow. Local behaviors can be derived from choreographies
and include individual actions of a participating actor useful for attack analysis processes, with e.g.
protocol verification tools, that can provide more assurance in the security of a protocol. The compiler
respects given rules while handling non-trivial tasks, such as term de-, construction and composition,
check generation, and frame branching. Alongside examples, internal classes of the compiler are
highlighted and the translation process is explained. Also, decisions slightly altering the underlying
approach are discussed.
