Abstract 
We are today facing the world’s biggest global refugee crises, and half of the refugees are under the 
age of 18, education is thus a critical element of the international refugee response and it is one of the 
most important ways to solve today’s world’s crises. In 2015 the UN set “Quality Education” as one of 
the 17 Sustainable Development Goals, and it is the aim of this project to investigate how to make this 
goal more achievable through provision of quality learning environments.  
The research outlined in the thesis investigates the feasibility of user involving (participatory) design 
tools to evaluate the learning environment of semi-permanent classrooms in emergencies. This is done 
through the evaluation of classrooms of the Danish company Lifeshelter’s pilot project at Undugu 
Primary school in the Nduta Refugee Camp in the Kibundo district in North-West Tanzania.  
Through a field study at Undugu Primary school, it is also the project’s aim to inform the further 
development of the Lifeshelter classroom solution and thereby the construction of Lifeshelter’s next 
project in Baidoa. The research demonstrates how the Lifeshelter Classroom still face multiple 
shortcomings, preventing it from providing a quality learning environment for education in 
emergencies. The research suggests a need for understanding the local context, as well as including the 
community, in order to provide quality products. It is the argument of the project that engagement of 
beneficiaries when building learning environments has the potential of increasing both motivation and 
ownership among users, all increasing the impact of the product. It is thereby concluded that the 
quality of the learning environments and the user satisfaction could be dramatically increased, if 
Lifeshelter Aps change their focus from a “one solution fits all” mentality into a holistic, user-inclusive 
and context-specific approach.

1. Introduction 
The current global refugee crisis is the biggest ever recorded with more than 68.5 million forcefully 
displaced people. Internally Displaced People (IDP) make up 40 million whereas 25.5 million are 
refugees displaced outside their home country (UNHCR, 2019a). On average it takes more than 10 
years before these people can return to their home (Devictor and Do, 2016). Despite this substantial 
time span most refugee camps are dominated by temporality, often due to 1) fear from the host 
country that the refugees will settle permanently, and 2) the humanitarian aid sector being notoriously 
underfunded (Fairs, 2017). Furthermore, the majority of the funding is only based on one-year scopes, 
reinforcing the temporal nature of the camps through short sighted aid programs. Consequently, this 
provides short-term fixes instead of more durable shelters and solutions (UNHCR, 2004a), which is 
particularly problematic as the number of long-lasting refugee situations are increasing (UNHCR, 
2019a). 
Currently more than 420 million children live in areas of conflict. This is nearly one fifth of all the 
children worldwide, and more than half of the refugees today are under the age of 18. Only 61 per cent 
of the refugee children attend primary school whereas the global average is 92 per cent. In secondary 
school the number goes down to 23 per cent of refugee children enrolled, compared to 84 per cent 
globally (UNHCR, 2019a). 
In 2015 the United Nations General Assembly set the 17 Sustainable Development Goals as guidelines 
for a global sustainable development towards 2030. Goal number four is “Quality Education”, or more 
elaborate: "Ensure inclusive and equitable quality education and promote lifelong learning opportunities 
for all” (SDG, 2015). This is a goal that must be acted on now, as education is one of the most 
important ways to solve current refugee crises. Education is a critical element of the international 
refugee response, as it protects children from being forced into child labour, sexual exploitation, child 
marriage and armed groups. Education also strengthens community resilience as it improves children’s’ 
ability to break the poverty cycle and protects them both physically and mentally (SDG, 2015). 
As the world biggest child rights organization, Save the Children (SC) fights for children’s rights and 
helps them fulfil their potential (SCI, 2019e), and one of their main mandates is to fight for educations 
for those who need it the most. In the fall 2018 Save the Children Denmark (SCD) formed a strategic 
partnership with a small Danish company called Lifeshelter. Lifeshelter has designed a semicircle 
shelter concept for emergency situations and fragile contexts. They have two different types of 
shelters: A housing shelter; Lifeshelter Family Shelter and a classroom shelter (image on the frontpage); 
Lifeshelter Community Shelter, which is essentially a scaled-up version of the Family Shelter. The aim of 
the SCD-Lifeshelter partnership is to explore the potential of the Lifeshelter classroom solution in 
providing quality learning environments in emergency situations. During the months of May and June 
2019, Lifeshelter is building 10 of its classrooms for SC in the Baidoa Region in Somalia. The Lifeshelter 
==PAGE:8==

Classroom solution is still very new and the only place the Lifeshelter Classrooms is currently used is in 
the Nduta Refugee Camp in the Kibundo district in North-West Tanzania. The classrooms were set up in 
April 2018 but have not yet been evaluated. To assists in the further development of the Lifeshelter 
classroom solution and thereby the construction of the classrooms in Baidoa the aim of this thesis is to 
evaluate the pilot project in Nduta Refugee camp (see appendix 1 for the Terms of Reference with SC).  
With this in mind, the problem statement for this project will be presented in the following section.  
1.1. Problem statement 
While providing Lifeshelter Aps and Save the Children new insights on the performance of the 
Lifeshelter Classroom, the objective of this thesis is at the same time to investigate the feasibility of 
user involving (participatory) design tools to evaluate the learning environment of semi-permanent 
classrooms.  
This leads to the following problem statement:  
Can participatory methods, applied in the evaluation of physical learning environments in 
refugee camps, provide insights to the decision makers (i.e. Lifeshelter) which may help them 
improve their products and services? 
The above problem statement will be answered through a field study at Undugu Primary school in 
Nduta refugee camp in Northwest Tanzania. The case study will serve two purposes; 1) Make a holistic 
comparison of the classroom provided by Lifeshelter Aps with alternative solutions, and 2) test the 
suitability of user involving design methodology as a tool for formative evaluations.  
To answer the problem statement four research questions have been formulated. The first two aims at 
1) defining the research parameters and 2) lay out the methodology.  
1. What are the relevant parameters to assess the quality of a physical learning environment? 
2. Which participatory methods are suitable when involving the specific users of the classrooms in 
the evaluation of the physical learning environment? 
Research question 1) and 2) will be applied and tested through the field study in Nduta. Research 
question 3 serves as a guideline for the activities in Nduta. 
3. How is the quality of the physical learning environment provided by the Lifeshelter Classrooms 
compared to other classroom solutions at Undugu Primary School? 
Lastly the analysis of the findings will lead to a list of recommendations for Lifeshelter, guided by the 
following research question:  
4. How can the Lifeshelter Classrooms be improved to provide a better learning environment for 
education in emergencies?

1.2. A Formative Evaluation 
Since the aim this thesis is to evaluate already existing structures that is currently used in a real-life 
context, the project has many of the characteristics of a summative evaluation. However, it is 
important to note that the Lifeshelter classrooms in Nduta are only a proof of concept, and not 
intended to be the final product. As the development of the Lifeshelter classroom is still ongoing, the 
aim of the evaluation is not to assess the final quality of the product but to identify areas of 
improvement to assist the further design process. The evaluation can thereby best be described as 
formative, but will also draw on elements from what is typically defined as a summative evaluation 
(Hartson et al., 2003). E.g. the other classrooms at Undugu Primary School will be used as a baseline for 
the assessment and a list of predefined parameters will be used to guide the evaluation (chapter 5) The 
evaluation is purely qualitative and no fixed quantitative goals will be used to rate the quality of the 
classrooms. Since the Lifeshelter Classrooms at Undugu are still at a relative early state, making such 
quantitative comparisons against other products would serve little sense.  
1.3. Content of Thesis 
In chapter 2 the stakeholders and actors involved in the research project are presented, following a 
description of the current refugee crises and education within emergencies. A short description of 
Nduta Refugee Camp will set the scene for the field research. The chapter ends with an introduction to 
the importance of the physical learning environment. Chapter 3 introduces the evaluation of physical 
learning environments and argues for the importance of participatory and holistically oriented 
frameworks for evaluation. The chapter shows that there is a gap in the literature when it comes to the 
evaluation of the physical learning environments in emergencies. For the schools to be well-designed 
and well-functioning it is argued that they should be rooted in the needs of the specific community. At 
the end of the chapter, Post-Occupancy-Evaluation is explained and neglected issues in the evaluation 
process are identified. In chapter 4, a lack of participatory approaches within the development and 
humanitarian sector are framed. Subsequently, design thinking is introduced as an overall framework 
to help putting the user at the centre of the activities, while generative methods are introduced as a 
means to access the experiences of the users. Based on the literature presented in the previous 
chapters, chapter 5 introduces the research parameters guiding the fieldwork. In Chapter 6 the 
projects methodology and methods are presented, such as selecting location and informants, setting 
up interviews and workshops as well as ethical considerations. Chapter 7 discusses the qualities of 
various participatory tools. It introduces the exercises used during the workshops and presents the 
workshop guide. Chapter 8 looks at how the methods worked and explores dilemmas concerning 
power structures, giving gifts and understanding cultural differences. The challenges of conducting 
participatory workshops through a translator are also discussed in this chapter. In chapter 9, the data 
collected during the field study is analysed and presented. The Lifeshelter classroom proves to have 
substantial shortcomings in a range of the research parameters. Based on these findings, a list of

2. Background information 
2.1. Stakeholder Presentation 
Multiple stakeholders have played central roles during this project. As described in the introduction the 
background for the project is the SCD-Lifeshelter partnership, aiming at innovating the provision of 
sustainable education spaces for children in fragile and remote areas. The solutions are targeting host 
communities, refugee camps and IDP settlements. The partnership is a part of SCD’s strategic work 
with the UN Sustainable Development Goal number 4, namely to give all children the right to quality 
education. At the same time, the partnership exemplifies SDG 17, which is creating new partnership for 
the goals. 
Initiated by this partnership between SCD and Lifeshelter I Have collaborated with both parties around 
making an evaluation of the Lifeshelter Classrooms. As mentioned in the introduction the only place 
the Lifeshelter Classrooms are currently being used as a classroom is in Nduta Refugee Camp in 
Kibondo District, North-West Tanzania. Nduta Refugee Camp is run by the Tanzanian Ministry of Home 
Affairs (MHA) in collaboration with UNHCR. The two Lifeshelter Classrooms are part of the Undugu 
Primary School and were constructed in April 2018 by Lifeshelter in collaboration with Danish Refugee 
Counsel (DRC). After finished construction the classrooms were handed over to Caritas International

access to the refugee camp. (see appendix 2 for ToR with SC Tanzania on the field visit and appendix 3

2.2. Global refugee and IDP situation 
The Refugee Convention’s (1951) legal definition of a refugee is any person who owns “well-founded 
fear of being persecuted for reasons of race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social 
group or political opinion, is outside the country of his nationality and is unable or, owing to such fear, is 
unwilling to avail himself of the protection of that country; or who, not having a nationality and being 
outside the country of his former habitual residence as a result of such events, is unable or, owing to 
such fear, is unwilling to return to it.” (UN, 1951). This definition excludes Internally Displaced People 
(IDP) and people who flee war, natural disaster, general violence, and other forces causing 
displacement. The more general understanding of refugees, however, includes these people 
(RefugeeCities, 2015). IDP’s are defined by the UNHCR as: “persons who have been forced or obliged to 
flee or leave their home or place of habitual residence, in particular as a result of or in order to avoid the 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
effects of armed conflict, situations of generalized violence, violations of human rights or natural or 
human-made disasters, and who have not crossed an internationally recognized border”  (UNHCR, 
2004b). UNHCR define a refugee situation as people from a country of origin exiling in a country of 
destination. A situation is considered major if it involves more than 25.000 people. While a protracted 
situation is defined as one that has lasted for more than five continuous years.  
Presenting clear numbers on the amount of people displaced globally proves to be some of a challenge, 
on top of this comes the challenge of predicting the duration of the crisis. In a study based on data 
from UNHCR’s Population Statistics Database, Xavier Devictor and Quy-Toan Do (2016) compute 
statistics on the duration refugees stay in exile and argue that knowing the length of the refugees stay 
can play a crucial role when planning response to the situation. With short crisis humanitarian aid 
might be enough, while development interventions will be needed for the longer stays (Devictor and 
Do, 2016). With the length of a crisis increasing, temporary solutions need proper assistance, as e.g. 
temporary lack of schooling in a humanitarian crisis turns into illiteracy in a longer lasting refugee crisis 
(Devictor and Do, 2016). In the end of 2015 there were 15.5 million refugees under UNHCR’s mandate, 
and according to the UNHCR. In 2019 the number has increased to 25.4 million refugees, over half of 
whom are under the age of 18 (UNHCR, 2019a). Only 61 per cent of the refugee children attend 
primary school whereas the global average is 92 per cent. In secondary school the number goes down 
to 23 per cent of refugee children enrolled, compared to 84 per cent globally. 
At the end of 2017, 13.4 million refugees were in protracted refugee situations, an increase on the 11.6 
million from the year before. Of this 3 million were in a situation lasting 38 years or more (UNHCR, 
2017). A protracted refugee situation is defined by the UNHCR (2004a) Protracted Refugee Situations 
report as: “one in which refugees find themselves in a long-lasting and intractable state of limbo.” The 
refugees in these situations are often not able to break free of reliance on external assistance. Their 
situation is defined by a lack of basic rights and needs; economically, physical and psychological. 
Protracted refugee situations are not unavoidable but stems from political impasses in the host country 
and in the country of origin (UNHCR, 2004a). When looking into the protracted refugee situations 
about 42,3 % of the total number of refugees have stayed in exile for more than five years. The average 
duration of the protracted situations has slowly increased and are now more than 20 years. To get an 
overview of the problems at hand it is therefore necessary to look into the individual situation and not 
just judge on the figures alone.  
The protracted refugee situation has three main consequences; it wastes lives, it squanders resources 
and it functions as an incubator for future problems. Depending on the political situation in the host 
countries and the rights given to the refugees their conditions will vary widely. In some situations, the 
refugees are purely dependent on continued provision of assistance on a daily basis, where in others 
the refugees are economically self-sufficient. The access to land and labour market is key to this 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
development (UNHCR, 2004a). A combination of the continued need of external assistance and the lack 
of funds and declining donor commitment leads to “stop-gab solutions”. This leads to what has been 
termed “the plastic sheeting syndrome”. Instead of providing more durable shelters and solutions in 
general, the lack of assets leads short-term fixes and shelters made of plastic sheeting (UNHCR, 2004a).  
2.3. Global Education in Emergencies  
Education is a critical element of the international refugee response, as it protects children from being 
forced into child labour, sexual exploitation, child marriage and armed groups. Education also 
strengthens community resilience as it improves children’s ability to break out of the poverty cycle and 
protects them both physically and mentally. It enables them to grow up to contribute to the society, 
both in their host society and in their homelands when peace allows them to return. 
Education is fundamental to the way we think refugee emergency today, as refugees now spend many 
years, even decades, in exile. Nevertheless, with over half of the world’s refugees being children, and 
more than half of those are not getting an education – a number which has increased by 500,000 in the 
last year alone (UNHCR, 2019b) – levels of illiteracy will keep rising, consequently having major 
negative impact both the individual and the society. To name a few; unemployment or low quality and 
low paid jobs, low self-esteem, negative impact on health, and low levels of community involvement 
and civic participation (Wofford and Tibi, 2018).  
In 2015 the United Nations General Assembly set the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as 
guidelines for a global sustainable development towards 2030. The goals are interdependent, and each 
has a set of targets, summing up to 169 targets in total. Goal number four is “Quality Education”, or 
more elaborate: “Ensure inclusive and equitable quality education and promote lifelong learning 
opportunities for all (SDG, 2015). This is a particularly important goal, as the number of out-of-school 
children in emergencies is still increasing both in total and relative numbers, and it is predicted to 
continue so, due to large-scale protracted crises (Bashir et al., 2018a). The Inter-Agency Network for 
Education in Emergencies’ (INEE) Minimum Standards for Education is a standard for education in 
emergencies, made to ensure people the right to quality education and safe learning environment in 
emergencies. It is a standard SC use, as well as many other NGOs, such as International Rescue 
Committee (IRC), UNESCO, UNICEF and World Bank, to name a few (INEE, 2010). The INEE report notes 
that by following these minimum standard, NGOs can offer safe spaces for learning in emergencies and 
assistance to identify and support the students’ needs. The Quality Learning Framework (QLF) 
describes SCI’s understanding of what makes quality basic education, more on the QLF can be found in 
chapter 3.  
To shortly sum up, quality education is life-saving, which is why education is one of the most important 
ways to solve today’s world’s refugee crises.

2.4. The Camp, the Schools and the Classrooms.  
Nduta refugee camp is located approximately 15 kilometers outside Kigoma city and houses around 
100.000 predominantly Hutu people from Burundi. More than half of the population in the camp are

a lack of classrooms. Hope Secondary School are the only Secondary school in the camp.

2018 120 UNICEF semi-permanent classrooms were constructed to meet a detrimental lack of

2.5. The Importance of the physical learning environment 
According to the INEE minimum standards learning spaces can be defined as: “Places where teaching 
and learning happen. Examples include private homes, child-care centres, pre-schools, temporary 
structures and schools.” (INEE, 2010). In the following section the importance of the physical learning 
space will be described through various examples from the literature.  
Research on school effectiveness have often neglected context and ignored the physical learning 
environment (OECD, 2017), thereby creating a blind spot when focusing on the relationship between 
the built environment and learning. However, in the past decade, focus on the physical environment 
seems to be receiving more and more attention, as many economically developed countries with a 
history of public-funded education have large school buildings to maintain and improve. This has led to 
discussions on the priorities for new schools, as well as thoughts on what influence the physical 
environment might have on learning (Woolner et al., 2007). 
Following this discussion, many scholars have started studying school designs. One of them is Mary 
Filardo, who noted that poorly designed schools can have a damaging impact on teacher and student 
engagement, and thereby impact negatively on the students’ outcomes (Filardo, 2008). In 2005 a 
group of scholars (Higgins et al., 2005) conducted a literature review mainly based on USA and the UK, 
looking at the evidence of the impact of physical learning environments. They concluded that elements 
such as air quality, temperature, lighting and sound have significant impact on the students’ 
concentration, mood, well-being, attendance and ultimately attainment (Higgins et al., 2005).  
According to a study by Shield and Dockrell (2008), the acoustic properties of the classroom can have 
detrimental effect on student performance, with both internal and external noise negatively 
influencing memory, motivation and reading ability (Shield and Dockrell, 2008).   
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
Another important finding from Higgins et al. (2005), was that  ‘ownership’ of space by both teachers 
and students is important, as it empowers, and thus encourages creativity and raises motivation 
towards both academic and social goals. Building on these findings, Pamela Woolner et al. (2007), 
argued that while it is worthwhile bringing the core physical elements up to an adequate level, it is 
crucial to focus on the context when building learning environments. They note that the evidence is 
consistent with regard to the benefits of engaging students and teachers in the design process. 
(Woolner et al., 2007). It is necessary to focus on the meaning of schools in different contexts, as they 
need to be rooted in the needs of the specific community. By localizing the design process the 
ownership of the created space is given back to teachers and students. In order to do this, 
understanding the complex relationship between people and the built environment is essential. 
Environmental psychologists such as Gabriel Moser (2009) stress the importance of the fit between 
humans and their settings and emphasize that we should not only examine the built environment or 
the person, but the interrelations between them, noting that too often the fit is poor and needs 
improvements (Moser, 2009). Ahrentzen and Evans (1984) did a study on 65 4th-6th graders and 13 
teachers from 5 schools from an upper middle-class population in Florida and found that higher ceilings 
in the classrooms produced a decreased sense of crowding by both teachers and children. They also 
found that higher ceilings were related to less visual distraction from student movement (Ahrentzen 
and Evans, 1984). However, Read et. al. (1999) did a similar study on 30 Anglo-American preschool 
children in Illinois and found that higher ceilings in classrooms affected negatively cooperative 
behaviour among the children, whereas the children showed higher levels of cooperative behaviour in 
classrooms with lower ceiling (Read et al., 1999). This is not to say that general examinations of 
physical school environments are pointless, but rather that if it is to be more fruitful, we must carefully 
examine how each specific environment is supposed to produce effects on its teachers, students and 
other users.  
As part of the OECD Learning Environment Evaluation Program Series (LEEP), Belfali and Velissaratou 
argues that further research is needed to better understand how the physical environment affects 
students and teachers, as well as how results from this research can be used for improvements of the 
physical school environment (OECD, 2017). In line with previous statements, the report also concludes 
that improved student learning is most likely to happen if there are certain physical conditions. Well-
designed buildings and facilities can be a catalyst for teachers developing innovative pedagogies with 
positive outcomes for student learning. In order to better map out elements in the physical learning 
environment, which contributes to improved student outcome, Belfali and Velissaratou propose to 
divide these influential aspects into three categories. The first is basic conditions for learning, which 
includes aspects such as temperature, ventilation and safety. The second is relative aspects, such as 
colours, plants and aesthetics. The third one is space design, which are aspects determining how 
flexible and adaptable the physical space is (OECD, 2017). Ideally all three categories are considered 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
when providing physical learning environments, it being both in the economically developed and 
developing part of the world. 
To describe the quality of the learning environment OECD LEEP uses the concepts of Adequate, 
effective and efficient. These three concepts are to be understood as: “ 
- 
Adequate learning environment is the learning environment that meets the minimum 
requirements to ensure users’ comfort, access, health, safety and security.  
- 
Effective learning environment is the learning environment that supports the varied demands of 
teaching and learning to enable a school to achieve its educational objectives. 
- 
Efficient learning environment is the learning environment that maximizes the use and 
management of space and resources to achieve maximum output in terms of student and 
teacher outcomes” (OECD, 2017).  
As we will experience through this thesis, just providing an adequate learning environment can be a 
challenge in the situation of education in emergencies.

3. Evaluation of Physical Learning Environments 
3.1. MEAL within the Humanitarian and Development Sector 
To improve, achieve and document results in programmes, Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E) is a 
fundamental part of the work of most NGOs. Traditionally the M&E has primarily been focused on 
donor documentation. Did the programme reach the indicators? Did the donors get what they paid 
for? Did the beneficiaries get the expected aid? Within the humanitarian and development sector an 
ever-growing emphasis on evidence-based programming is seen. However, there has been a general 
tendency to associate evidence with quantitative evaluation only (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015). The 
M&E is often dominated by a narrow focus on reach and scale of the programmes, and not necessarily 
on the quality (Bamberger et al., 2010), (Aly, 2016). It is the numbers of classrooms constructed, text 
books delivered, children receiving education and teachers trained that are measured instead of the 
actual learning among the children (Bashir et al., 2018a). This can partly be explained with a dominant 
donor focus on easy to present quantitative indicators and partly with an ever-present lack of 
resources and capacity among the NGOs (Aly, 2016). On top of this comes an, until recently, scepticism 
towards qualitative methods among those working with MEAL (Bamberger et al., 2010).  
However, in the recent years there has been an increasing focus on what could be learned from 
monitoring and evaluation instead of solely focusing on documentation. On a more systematic level 
NGOs have started to analyse the data, looking at how the programmes could be improved. At the 
same time accountability has turned to be an integrated part of the former M&E departments, 
ensuring community engagement and feedback from stakeholders (IASC, 2011). This increased focus 
on accountability has pushed the need for more qualitative data in a mixed methods approach. To 
underline this focus, many NGOs have changed their data collection strategies from M&E to MEAL 
(Monitoring, Evaluation, Accountability and Learning), integrating accountability and learning as a 
strategic part of their activities (SCI, 2019b). With an effective MEAL plan NGOs are able to track 
progress, make adjustments, determine the impact of programmes and discover unplanned effects of 
the programme. The MEAL system also provides feedback mechanisms, so that the NGOs become 
aware of the stakeholder’s thoughts and complaints, which can help to guide programme 
implementation (Walden, 2013).  
Monitoring and evaluation are different, but related, processes. Monitoring is the systematic 
assessment of a programme’s performance over time, and it provides an ongoing feedback of the 
programme, indicating the programme’s progress and achievements. Monitoring helps the decision-
making by providing information on how well the project is performing on different indicators, making 
it possible to take proper actions to reach the set goals (INEE, 2010). Evaluation is the systematic and 
objective assessment of the programme and its design, implementation and results (OECD, 2006). The 
evaluation is usually conducted in the middle of, or at the end of the programme and is often carried 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
out by external actors. It complements the ongoing monitoring activities by providing more in-depth, 
objective assessment of the relevance, efficiency, effectiveness, impact and sustainability of the 
programme (Walden, 2013). The overall aim with the evaluation is to determine if the target indicators 
are met and provide evidence on the quality of the program. What is meant by accountability is that 
the community should be involved in the progress and should be able to influence relevant decisions. It 
also means giving the community the power to hold the NGOs accountable for ways in which they 
influence the community’s policies, priorities and actions at local, national and global levels (INEE, 
2010). The term learning refers to the systematic incorporation of observations into the programme’s 
design (SCI, 2019a). 
3.2. Evaluation of Learning Environments at Save the Children 
At Save the Children MEAL is an essential and integrated part of the everyday work, ensuring 
systematic and continuous feedback and learning from the programmes they run (SCI, 2018). In the 
following a short case example from Save the Children in Kibondo, and a brief analysis of the ILET 
framework will be used to cast light on evaluation of learning environments within the organization.  
3.2.1. Evaluation at Save the Children Kibondo 
At the Save the children Kibondo office in Tanzania there is a dedicated MEAL department, ensuring 
accountability and collection of evidence on Save the Children’s activities in the refugee camps and 
host community. The programmes are monitored through predominantly quantitative data reported 
on a weekly basis by the staff members responsible for the different activities. The regular evaluations 
are done by the MEAL team themselves, and for the bigger evaluations enumerators are hired in, 
helping to reach representative sample sizes (Mkomwa, 2019). Though doing interviews and focus 
group interviews these are most often based on close ended questionnaires. Though the approach 
might be qualitative the statements and findings are confined by predefined indicators. This allows the 
answers to be filled into a spread sheet, making it easy to process the large quantities of data but 
hiding the details of each interview.  
When looking into the SC Kibondo department’s evaluation of the learning environment at their 
facilities in the Nduta Refugee Camp, a lack of focus on the physical learning environment becomes 
evident. Periodic evaluations of the learning environment are carried out according to the Save the 
Children Quality Learning Environment (QLE) guidelines.

3.2.2. ILET 
Among the newest, most participatory and holistically oriented frameworks for evaluation are ILET 
(Improving Learning Environments Together in Emergencies), developed by Save the Children Norway

The high degree of community involvement combined with the big focus on not only assessing the 
quality of the learning environment but also improving it, is what makes ILET unique. At the same time 
it deals with the narrow focus on output level data mentioned earlier (Bashir et al., 2018a).

of learning environments has a very limited focus on the actual classroom. The data management

3.4. Post Occupancy Evaluation of Schools in the Developed World 
The following section explains and discusses methodologies for Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) of 
learning environments, focusing on a series of approaches used to evaluate school learning 
environments. Subsequently, neglected issues in these evaluation processes are identified. POE has 
been defined in a number of ways; e.g. professor of architecture Wolfgang Preiser, (2002 p. 42) defines 
POE as ‘‘a process of systematically evaluating the performance of buildings after they have been built 
and occupied for some time’’, and Karim Hadjri and Carl Crosier (2009 p. 22) define POE as a ‘‘process 
that involves a rigorous approach to the assessment of both the technological and anthropological 
elements of a building in use’’. Nevertheless, what they all seem to have in common is the 
understanding that it is an evaluation taking place after the building is in use – post-occupancy.  
Benjamin Cleveland and Kenn Fisher, both leading learning environment specialists, note in an article 
on the subject of POE that the term “learning environment” is often used to describe the conceptual, 
social and psychological environment at schools, rather than the actual physical learning environment 
(Cleveland and Fisher, 2014). They also note, as stated earlier in this thesis, that there is an increasing 
focus on physical environments’ influence on the educational activities, both among researchers, 
teachers and design professionals. Again, it is especially in the developed world this has become 
increasingly discussed, with a growing focus on architecture that supports contemporary educational 
practices (OECD, 2009). And since creating better learning environments is expensive, the focus on 
evaluation is in order to assert what works and what does not work for future building projects, as the 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
information from the evaluation can potentially inform new building projects on how to create better 
learning environments. This is where POE comes into the picture.  
Karim Hadjri and Carl Crosier underline the importance of this evaluation, since it can be valuable to a 
big range of stakeholders (Hadjri and Crozier, 2009). It can be used to evaluate occupants’ needs, 
reduce space and energy, reduce operational expenses, etc. It can also support the given building by 
making improvements based on the findings. To sum up, feedback from already build learning 
environments can add value to the next building project.  
3.4.1. Post-occupancy methodologies and methods 
Three general POE methodologies are defined by Preiser, (1995), these are; indicative, investigative 
and diagnostic. He argues that these three methodologies together encompass the full spectrum of 
building projects. Indicative POE includes a quick walk-through evaluation, involving inspections, 
interviews with key personnel, and group meetings with building users. Investigative POE involves the 
more in-depth analyses with interviews and questionnaires, usually across a number of similar 
buildings. Diagnostic POE is the evaluation of a number of comparable facilities, using a broad range of 
technological and anthropological perspectives (Preiser, 1995). 
With regards to the collection of data, Leaman et al., (2010) recommends the use of multiple methods, 
to ensure that the weaknesses of different methods are compensated for, as the different methods will 
strengthen each other. With this in mind, following methods for data-collection are recommended; 
interviews, checklists, questionnaires, systematic observation, participant observation, critical incidents, 
archival record analysis, expert walk-through, measuring technical performance of building fabric, 
services and systems, assessing environmental performance, occupant survey and visual recording 
(Leaman et al., 2010). Furthermore, Cohen et al., (2001) notes the importance of carefully considering 
the amount of time needed to collect and analyse data.   
As a response to the growing recognition that physical learning environment evaluation need to be 
context specific the Higher Education Funding Council for England (HEFCE) in 2006 produced a guide to 
POC (Blyth et al., 2006). The guide divides the evaluation into three main areas: process, functional 
performance and technical performance. Correspondingly these areas will be used in this thesis to 
categorize the different parameters influencing the physical learning environment (see chapter 5). 
3.4.2. Neglected issues in the Evaluation Process 
In the Benjamin Cleveland and Kenn Fisher literature review (2014), they present an extensive number 
of tools used to evaluate learning environments, and point out that across all educational sectors, new 
building evaluation methodologies are required if a deeper understanding is to be attained. Their 
findings are that the majority of these tools focus predominantly on the physical features and the built 
environment, and that few tools seek the opinions of the students on the learning environment. They 
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
argue that this clearly indicates that approaches to evaluation of learning environments require further 
development (Cleveland and Fisher, 2014). More studies are required to develop the methodologies 
and methods, in order to make sure that the evaluation of physical learning environments includes 
teaching desires, learning practices, activities and behaviour. Following this argument, Cleveland and 
Fisher (2014) point to the potential of an interdisciplinary approach, involving people from a variety of 
backgrounds. They suggest including, but not exclusively focusing on, the education, human geography, 
environmental psychology and architecture, with the argument that different epistemologies tend to 
be favoured by different disciplines. An interdisciplinary approach might thus provide the space needed 
to generate new knowledge regarding learning environment evaluation (Cleveland and Fisher, 2014). In 
line with previous arguments presented in this thesis, they also point to the importance of context 
situated evaluation, using the argument of Walbe Ornstein et al. (2009) who concludes that ‘‘user-
informed assessments increase the likelihood that a given school building fulfils its intended educational 
purposes to the greatest degree possible’’ (p. 364). A highly important factor, which too often is being 
neglected.  
3.5. Participatory Evaluation of Physical Learning Environments in Emergencies  
As seen in the above chapter no method has given enough attention to the importance of including the 
users’ perspective when building learning environments. This is in spite the many arguments for doing 
so. The presented literature in this chapter shows a variety of scholars presenting arguments for why 
user-informed assessment is so important, and for why physical learning environments need to be 
context specific. At the same time, it has been shown how the development and humanitarian sector 
are still struggling with inclusion of their beneficiaries. Building on these arguments I will do exactly this 
during my fieldwork. Namely making a context specific assessment of the physical learning 
environment while bringing the users to the centre of the research.

4. Participation and Design Thinking 
4.1. Participatory Approaches within the Humanitarian Sector 
Throughout the last decade, there has been a growing focus on involving the beneficiaries of the 
development and humanitarian aid in the programs. The focus on engaging communities and aid 
receivers has been formalized into a long list of standards, commitments and NGO policies and 
strategies. In 2011 the Inter-Agency Standing Committees (IASC) endorsed the Commitments to 
Accountability to Affected populations (IASC, 2011). Among the total of five commitments is the 
commitment to participation which aims to enable the engagement of the beneficiaries in decision-
making processes that affect them. Hereby, accountability and the clear aim of engaging the affected 
populations were brought into the higher level of the UN system for the first time (UNICEF, 2013).  
With the introduction of the Core Humanitarian Standards in 2014, the inclusion of the beneficiaries 
was given a vital position stated most clearly by commitment no. 4: “Communities and people affected 
by crisis know their rights and entitlements, have access to information and participate in decisions that 
affect them.” (CHS, 2014). The organizations and individuals involved with humanitarian response 
thereby commit themselves to put the communities at the center of their actions. However, according 
to focus group interviews carried out in the Middle East in 2015, the Humanitarian sector was losing 
track of its core function (Aly, 2016). When rated on their ability to consider the opinion of the affected 
communities, the aid agencies only scored 2,5/10. This, along with other indicators, drew attention to 
the problematic fact that the beneficiaries still were not at the center of the humanitarian response. 
Though far from the only solution to these grave problems, involving the people affected by crisis in 
every stage of the relief work has been highlighted as one of the possible steps forward. With the 
Grand Bargain, introduced during the World Humanitarians Summit in Istanbul in 2016, a new set of 
commitments were endorsed by 18 of the primary donor countries and 16 of the biggest aid 
organizations (IASC, 2016). Commitment no. 6 in the Grand Bargain sets the stage for the new strategy 
on participation: “A participation revolution: include people receiving aid in making the decisions which 
affect their lives.” Though not completely novel, the inclusion of people affected by humanitarian crisis 
in the decision-making process is now emphasized as a central aim. By involving the beneficiaries 
already in the early stages of the campaigns the aim is to ensure relevant, timely, effective and efficient 
humanitarian responses (IASC, 2017). Drawing on the above commitments, the release of the fourth 
edition of The Sphere Handbook; Humanitarian Charter and Minimum Standards in Humanitarian 
Response takes an extra step in the direction of involvement of the beneficiaries: “We offer our 
services in the belief that the affected population is at the center of humanitarian action, and recognize 
that their active participation is essential to providing assistance in ways that best meet their needs…” 
(Sphere, 2018). It is hereby underlined that the participation of the affected population should be 
“active”, and thus it no longer suffices to simply ask the affected populations for their opinions when 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
projects are evaluated. The active inclusion and influence on decisions prove fundamental in 
supporting the right to life with dignity (UN, 1948) (CHS, 2014). 
With the increased focus on active participation we also see a growing need for new tools. With a 
participatory approach, the ILET framework, described in chapter 3.2.2 strives to develop School 
Improvement Plans in collaboration with the affected communities (Bashir et al., 2018a). ILET thereby 
serves as an example of the new participatory tools developed within the humanitarian and 
development sector. During the last couple of years, a global trend has emerged with most of the 
larger NGO’s establishing innovation departments, supporting and promoting innovation internally in 
the organizations and creating new partnerships with the private sector (Fejerskov and Vestergaard, 
2018). The partnership between Save the Children Denmark and Lifeshelter is one such example. 
According to the Sustainable Development Goals, partnerships that cross the sectors are needed to 
meet the goals and solve the ever more complex global challenges (SDG, 2015). However, Fejerskov 
and Vestergaard describe how this new type of partnership presents new challenges as organizations 
and companies with different goals, working practices and ethical standards meet. This makes it 
increasingly important for the NGO’s to stand their ground and ensure the focus on and inclusion of 
the beneficiaries (Fejerskov and Vestergaard, 2018). At Medicins Sans Frontiers Sweden Innovation 
Unit (MSF SIU) a human-centered design approach helps to ensure that the needs of the end user are 
put first (MSF, 2018). The innovation process practiced at MSF SIU is very much inspired by the 
approach of design thinking (IDEO, 2019) and keeps the users of the products in focus throughout all 
the phases of Inspiration, Ideation and Implementation.

4.2. Design Thinking 
Throughout this thesis, I will seek inspiration within the methodology of design thinking. Even though 
the scope is to carry out an evaluation of an already existing product and not to go through a full 
design cycle, the theoretical framework of Design Thinking can still be applied. Fundamental for Design 
Thinking is a user centered approach where the user is involved throughout the different phases (IDEO, 
2015). In the context of this thesis, the participatory methodology and the way of seeing the world 
offered by Design thinking is used as a framework to collect valuable ethnographic insights within a 
confined timeframe.  
With the focus on the user, it is my belief that Design Thinking methodology supports the goal within 
the humanitarian and development sector of active participation of the beneficiaries as phrased in the 
Sphere Handbook (chapter 3) (Sphere, 2018). Throughout the last decades it has been demonstrated 
how Design Thinking can help to solve dynamic, multifaceted and complex issues within multiple 
sectors such as IT, Business, Education, Medicine and Development (Dorst, 2011; IDEO, 2019, 2015; 
Larsen and Flensborg, 2011; MSF, 2018; Tange, 2017; Vianna et al., 2012). Design Thinking is no longer 
an activity reserved for designers and can be applied by various professionals if trained in the 
methodology and embracing the creative mindset needed. 
As figure 4 shows, design thinking resembles an iterative process that oscillates between Inspiration, 
Ideation and Implementation. In the inspiration phase the scope of the project is formulated, the initial 
user insights are gathered and analyzed, and the problem is defined. In the ideation phase a catalogue 
of ideas are created, prototyped and tested while the concept is piloted, planned and diffused in the 
implementation phase (IDEO, 2019; Spinuzzi, 2005). The iterative nature of the process results in a co-
evolution of the problem and solution space (Dorst and Cross, 2001). When ideas are developed and 
tested with users, it brings about new insights that reframe the problem space. The process is brought 
back to the ideation phase or it is even made clear that more user insights are needed to open up the 
solution space. The designer thus constantly seeks to reinterpret and restructure the problem in order 
to come up with a viable solution (Dorst, 2011). 
As IDEO describes it: “To think like a designer requires dreaming up wild ideas, taking time to tinker and 
test, and being willing to fail early and often. The designer's mindset embraces empathy, optimism, 
iteration, creativity, and ambiguity. And most critically, design thinking keeps people at the center of 
every process.” (IDEO, 2019). At the same time, the practitioner must adopt a “beginners mind” 
allowing him- or herself to stay open, keep curious and assume nothing. From the onset of this project I 
intended to bring this open and rather playful mindset into the process of involving users and 
conducting participatory design. 
Design is often described as a way of organizing complexity and attempt to make sense in a chaotic 
world (Kolko, 2010). Jon Kolko (2010) describes the work of the designer as a process of sensemaking. 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
It is about the synthesis of market needs, available technologies and business needs. This synthesis was 
what I hoped to realize when I did field research at the school in Nduta. The question was: How can we 
make the technology (the classrooms) fit the needs of the affected communities in the best possible 
way? In process of synthetization the collected ethnographic insights are transformed into concrete 
problems that again lead to new ideas and concepts. This is brought about through an abductive 
sensemaking process: “Through efforts of data manipulation, organization, pruning, and filtering, 
designers produce information and knowledge.” (Kolko, 2010) The aim is to uncover hidden meaning in 
the behavior of the users and to find themes and relationships in the data, all supporting the design 
task at hand. However, due to the scope of this thesis I will not go into the actual idea generation, but 
will delimit the synthesis to highlight the issues and potentials of the investigated classrooms (chapter 
9) 
 
Figure 5 – The Landscape of Human-Centered design research as portrayed by Sanders and Stappers in 
2008 (Sanders and Stappers, 2008) 
As mentioned above, it is fundamental to Design Thinking to keep the user at the center of the process. 
It is however worth keeping in mind that user involvement can be achieved in many ways. Elizabeth

actual co-designing (figure 5) They draw a distinction between the user as subject and the user as 
partner (Sanders and Stappers, 2008). Participatory design is often confused with user-centered design 
that leaves the user to be a subject of research rather than an actual partner in the design process. 
When the humanitarian and development sector speak about participation of the affected population, 
there has been the same tendency to see the user as a subject rather than as a partner (chapter 3). The 
design process has to be a collective activity with a heterogeneous team of both designers, users and

researchers in order to qualify as participatory design research. In participatory design, the user is

4.3. Understanding Experiences through Generative Sessions 
A core aim of involving participants in the design process is to learn from their experiences, hear their 
opinions, understand what they do and how they do it and to learn about what they know, feel and 
dream. Further, participatory design should provide the users with an actual vote in the design process 
and a way to directly influence the outcome, rather than just treating them as the subjects of study. 
Additionally, letting users participate actively by generating solutions has another substantial benefit: 
Uncovering knowledge.  
According to Sleeswijk Visser et al. (2005) human experience is often based on tacit knowledge and 
latent needs. Tacit knowledge can be described as knowledge that can be acted upon but is hardly 
expressed with words. The latent needs are the needs people are not aware of themselves. Accessing 
the tacit knowledge and latent needs are therefore of high interest when doing research on user 
experience. It goes without saying that the researcher cannot simply ask the informants about this 
knowledge and these needs, since they are defined by the fact that the user is not aware of them. 
However, through generative techniques it is possible to access this information. As the name implies, 
generative techniques are about having the participants express themselves in a creative way by 
creating various artefacts. These may be anything from collages, drawings, playdough models, Lego 
structures or a combination of them all. The participants are guided through the sessions in small steps 
helping them express memories, routines and dreams. The physical representation facilitates access to 
the deeper levels of knowledge and makes it possible to express experiences that are otherwise hard 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
to put into words (Sleeswijk Visser, 2009): “Such sessions produce varied and rich views, anecdotes, 
and explanations about the explored context which include the use situation and the users’ concerns, 
memories, feelings, and experiences surrounding it” (Sleeswijk Visser et al., 2005). As seen in figure 7 
the generative tools are among the participatory methods and they serve as yet another argument for 
involving the user as a partner rather than a subject of research. 
 
Figure 7 – Different knowledge is best accessed by different techniques (Sleeswijk Visser et al., 
2005) 
 
4.4. Benefits of the Participatory Approach 
There are many reasons to involve the affected populations in the design process. To mention a few: It 
enhances impact by increasing local ownership, commitment and buy-in, and it ensures that the 
development activities resonate with local resources and needs (IDEO, 2015; Skovdal and Cornish, 
2015). Aid agencies have become increasingly aware of how crucial user involvement is to success, and 
they have recently articulated clear aims of increasing the involvement of beneficiaries (IASC, 2016).  
However, evaluations of recent efforts reveal that the current state of user engagement is poor. It is 
my belief that the frameworks of participatory design and design thinking can serve as a tool to 
improve the state of user involvement. In this thesis I test this assumption in practice through the 
application of design thinking in the evaluation of the classrooms at Undugu Primary School.

6. Methodology 
In the following the methodological considerations guiding the thesis will be presented.  
As described in chapter 1.1, the core aim of this thesis is: 1) to provide insights on the quality of the 
learning environments at Undugu Primary School, and 2) to investigate how the evaluation can benefit 
from the use of participatory methods. 
The primary purpose is thereby to make a formative evaluation of the classrooms at Undugu. Following 
Preiser's (1995) categorization of POE methodologies, this study can also be seen as an Indicative POE 
(see chapter 3.4). In limited time the aim is to get indications on the quality of the structures post 
occupancy, thereby allowing for eventual alterations and improvements. As it is typical for the 
indicative POE, this study will also make use of interviews, group meetings and observations to collect 
the qualitative data in an efficient manner.  
6.1. Selecting Location and Informants 
As described in chapter 2.1 the only place the Lifeshelter classrooms have been taken into use, are at 
the Undugu Primary School in Nduta Refugee Camp in Tanzania, where they have two of Lifeshelter’s 
classrooms. Since the main purpose of this study is to gather insights on the user experience of the 
classrooms, there is only this one possible location for the study to take place. At the same time the 
user group is relatively confined. The primary users are obviously the students and the teachers using 
the classrooms. Additionally, is there the headteacher and service personnel at the school, the parents 
and the organizations running the school. And as on most other schools also at Undugu the parents

chapter 2.1 Undugu Primary school is run by Caritas while DRC are responsible for the conditions of the 
buildings. Making the education officers at Caritas and the construction technologists at DRC relevant 
informants for the study, too (see chapter 8.4 for overview of informants).  
The access to the school and the refugee camp was negotiated by SC Tanzania. The SC education team 
are collaborating closely with the Caritas education team and gaining access to Undugu Primary school 
therefore only required a quick formal introduction to the Manager of Education; Julias Jeremia. As one 
of the operational partners in the camp SC could apply the Tanzanian Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA)

6.2. Research Design 
As recommended by Leaman et al., (2010) multiple data collection methods have been used to 
mitigate for each their weaknesses and strengths. The use of mixed methods thereby allows for 
triangulation of the findings generated by the different methods, looking at the topic from different 
angles (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015). 
The methods used during the field study in Tanzania where; key informant interviews, participant 
observations and workshops. 
6.2.1. Interviews, Informal Dialogues, Discussions and Meetings 
To gather invaluable background knowledge, one to one conversational interviews have been key for 
the project. Depending on the context and the interviewee the interviews have had many different 
formats. In most situations the aim has been to conduct semi structured interviews under a more 
formal setting; sitting at a table in a relatively silent room with a timeframe agreed upon in advance. In 
many cases this has however been hard to achieve forcing the “interviews” to be more dialogue based 
under informal settings. As an example, the 45 minutes’ drive from Kibondo town to Nduta refugee 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
camp served as a valuable possibility to insights from the SC staff also going to the camp. To get the 
most out of these - sometimes unforeseeable - possibilities of speaking to important stakeholders, 
topic guides was prepared. Closely related to the research parameters presented in chapter 5 the topic 
guides helped to make sure that the relevant issues were covered. To make sure that the topic guides 
were always available, they were stored on both my phone and in my notebook. 
For each of the more formal semi-structured interviews, an interview guide was prepared in advance 
guiding the interview while still leaving space for new ideas, issues and interesting subjects to be 
discussed and explore (Boeijen et al., 2014).  
6.2.2. Workshops / Focus group Discussions  
A corner stone in the research has been participatory workshop involving the users of the classrooms. 
The aim of these workshops was to carry out focus group discussions, inspired from the design thinking 
methodology these should be assisted with various generative tools. As described in chapter 4.3, the 
generative methods should help gain insights on the experiences, the tacit knowledge and the latent 
needs of the users. By giving voice to both teachers, students and parents the participatory workshops 
gives insights on a range of views, helps to understand norms and typical behaviour while exploring the 
users’ experiences of the classrooms (SCI, 2019d). In chapter 7 the design of the workshop and the 
used tools are described in detail.  
Sleeswijk Visser et al. (2005) describes how the quality of the information learned from the workshop 
can be enhanced greatly if the participants are sensitized prior to the activities. The aim is to have the 
participants start reflecting on the subject of research before attending the workshop. The students 
and teachers are asked to think about the quality of the physical learning environment and will start 
being aware of their surroundings. The participants might start to think about elements of the learning 
environment that they were not conscious about before. Thereby the sensitization grants access to the 
tacit knowledge of the participants. When sensitizing the participants it is however important not to 
prime them, making them overly focused on certain parameters or making them think that one answer 
is better than another. 
6.2.3. Observations 
The third pillar in the research has been observations of the classrooms and the teaching situations. 
Though sitting in a classroom full of students the observations has been primarily non-participant 
(Skovdal and Cornish, 2015). I was sitting in the classroom experiencing what the room was like to be in 
on my own body, but I did not directly interact with neither the students or the teachers.  
The observations were structured around the research parameters, using them as a guidance on what 
to look for. By following these predefined parameters observations were thereby less exploratory than 
what is the case with the unstructured participatory observations (SCI, 2019d). However, the 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
observations were not narrowly confined to the parameters and an open mind was kept for new and 
important observations.  
Though the methodology best can be described as non-participatory, the users of the classrooms 
played an important role. A key indicator for the quality of the physical learning environment is still the 
interactions and the verbal and non-verbal behavior of the students and teachers (SCI, 2019d). Many of 
the physical and technical characteristics of the classroom can be observed without the presence of 
any students. However, not before seeing the classroom full of students with an ongoing lesson the 
actual qualities of the classroom is shown.  
The observations thereby serve as a supplement to the interviews and workshops verifying if what has 
been described is also what is happening. At the same time the observations made it possible to 
discover the unspoken behavior of the classroom’s users.  
6.3. Translator 
The majority of the population in Nduta Refugee Camp speak Kirundi as their primary language, while 
many also speak some French and Swahili. The amount of English speakers is however very limited, 
especially among the children. Unfortunately, I speak neither French, Kirundi or Swahili making it 
necessary with a translator during both interviews and workshops with the users of the classrooms. In 
the Terms of Reference for the project, SC Tanzania had agreed to provide a translator whenever 
needed during the research. It was made clear that the translator should be used to work as a 
translator, used to work with children, speak a good English and be available throughout the five days 
field study in the camp.  
6.4. Ethical considerations 
Ethical concerns should always be part of any fieldwork and are primarily directed towards the study’s 
participants. An important question to always ask, is how the participants are benefitting from the 
research - how the researcher can justify spending peoples’ time and energy. Participants in the 
outlined project in Tanzania will unfortunately not personally benefit directly from their assistance in 
this research project. Rather, the project will qualify future decision makers to improve classroom 
conditions in similar situations. It is often seen that participants do not benefit directly from a fieldwork 
by e.g. receiving improved livelihood conditions, however, a way to then justify a given fieldwork is 
often by arguing that listening to the participants worries and wonderings itself is a way the fieldwork is 
benefiting the participants. The researcher is someone who will listen to their stories and give the 
participants an outlet for any thoughts. In a situation as a refugee camp, where multiple different 
NGO’s operates, it is however important to be aware of what could be referred to as notebook fatigue.  
As described in chapter 3.1 the organizations carry out continuous monitoring and evaluation of their 
programs. If this is solely done to live up to donor requirements on documentation there is a danger 
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
that the refugees experience being part of tedious evaluations without seeing any feedback or 
improvements. The danger of making the refugees feel that they were speaking to, yet another 
notebook is particularly present in a project like this. As a researcher it is therefore critical to be 
accountable, make the purpose of the research clear to the informants and respects their limits. In line 
with this, “avoidance of harm” (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015), is also very important. The activities 
following the project have in no way done harm to the participants involved, as is in accordance with 
the SC’s guide for Protecting Children in Conflict Centenary (PCIC) guideline (PCIC, 2018). 
 
Any decisions during the fieldwork have been made with the intent to encourage the children’s 
“happiness, safety, health, and emotional development towards adulthood” (PCIC, 2018), as SC’s PCIC 
guide note that any of their work should. By following the SCI Child Safe Guarding Policy, I also made 
sure that any risks of re-traumatization of a child was minimized. This can e.g. be done by framing the 
discussion around children’s experiences in general instead of the story of a specific child. What is 
meant by “minimal risk”, is that “…the probability and magnitude of harm or discomfort anticipated... 
are not greater... than those ordinarily encountered in daily life or during the performance of routine 
physical or psychological examinations or tests.”(HHS, 2007). I also had to make sure that the research 
was fair to all participants and did not put the objective of the research before the needs of any 
individual. This includes ensuring that all relevant parts of society are included, and participants have 
equal access to the assessment.   
In terms of “informed consent” (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015), I made sure that the participants in the 
project gave their informed consent. For one thing, this implies that the participants were informed in 
an easy and accessible manner before giving their consent to participate in the research process. It also 
meant that any participant, at any time, could stop the participation if they felt the need to do so. 
Especially working with children, considerations were made to ensure this, since the power balance 
between adults and children can make the child feel that he or she must participate because an adult 
says so. It was also very important that the participants understood that they had the right to make 
their own autonomous decisions based on own values and preferences. 
During the entire field research, I also made sure to follow the nine principles for basic requirements 
for meaningful and safe child participation, as outlined in the “Committee on the Rights of the Child 
General Comment on Article 12”. The following nine principles are outlined for the participation: 1) 
transparent and informative, 2) voluntary, 3) respectful, 4) relevant, 5) child friendly, 6) inclusive, 7) 
supported by training for adults, 8) safe and sensitive to risk and 9) accountable. (UNICEF, 2005) 
6.5. Sampling 
When deciding on the sample size for qualitative research, a simple and often mentioned guideline is 
that data should be collected until saturation is reached. Here saturation should be understood as the 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
time where no new information are uncovered during the analysis, and the same themes are recurring 
during the interview or research activity (PCIC, 2018). A list of studies has tried to uncover when 
saturation is reached in qualitative research. As a general guideline this is the case after 6-12 interviews 
or 3-6 focus group interviews with a specific sup-population of interest (Namey, 2017). When studying 
a highly homogeneous group it can thereby be assumed that saturation will be reached around these 
numbers. For each specific sup-population of interest (gender, age, role, etc.) it will be necessary to 
carry out a similar number of interviews. At the same time the sample size should comply with the 
what is realistic possible within the timeframe and funding constraints of the project (Skovdal and 
Cornish, 2015).  
6.5.1. Students 
At Undugu Primary school the Lifeshelter classrooms are only used for teaching by the 10 first grade 
classes. Each class has an approximate size of 60 students, meaning that around 600 students have 
experience from teaching situations in the classroom. Apart from receiving teaching in the Lifeshelter 
classrooms, the 1st grade students also have classes in the UNICEF semi-permanent classrooms. The 
TME Classrooms are reserved for the 4th-9th graders. The Lifeshelter and UNICEF classrooms have been 
at the school for less than a year, meaning that none of the students have yet experienced teaching in 
all three types of classrooms.  
The duration of the field study, the availability of a translator, and the practicalities in general allowed 
for a total of 4 workshops with students at Undugu Primary School. During all workshops a total of 6 
students participated, 3 girls and 3 boys. Three of the workshops were with 1st grade students using 
primarily on of the Lifeshelter Classroom, but also the UNICEF semi-permanent classroom. One 
workshop was with 9th grade students using the permanent classrooms. Ideally the workshop around 
the TME permanent classroom would have been with students around the same age as the ones using 
the Lifeshelter classroom, this was however not a possibility because of the distribution of the classes 
at the school. The workshops with the 1st grade students turned out to be particularly challenging (see 
chapter 8), this was especially due to the language barrier and the age of the students. It was assessed 
that the difference between 1st grade and 4th grade students would be so big that their responses 
would not be directly comparable. In order to get some more qualified discussions on the quality of the 
learning environment I therefore decided to do the workshop in the permanent classroom with the 
oldest student. During the workshops it turned out that most of the children had knowledge about the 
other types of classrooms than the ones they normally used, from doing homework and other after 
school activities in the rooms and through the experiences of siblings and friends.

6.6. Delimitations  
Due to the scope of the project the focus will only be on the actual classrooms – the structure and the 
learning environment they provide. Limiting the experience of the physical learning environment to the 
classroom alone is of course a rough simplification. The teacher, the pedagogy, the learning materials, 
the childrens’ background etc. will all be highly influential on the user experience. As described above 
the students are however assumed to be of a relative homogeneous group. Since all participants 
attend the same school, it is assumed that the pedagogy and the standard of teaching are 
approximately the same.   
The quality of the school itself also has a high impact on how the classrooms are perceived. Factors 
such as WASH facilities, the size of the school, security and fencing, playground etc. all have an 
influence on the wellbeing of the students, and how they perceive the school and the classroom (INEE, 
2010; SCI, 2017). However, since all the workshops were carried out with children from the same 
school, the external factors should have the same impact on how the students perceive the classroom.  
6.7. Data management 
When conducting workshops it is normally recommended to have both a dedicated facilitator and 
notetaker (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015) (PCIC, 2018). The notetaker should do observations on what the 
participants say and do, take pictures and help the facilitator keep track on time and workshop plan. 
Having a dedicated notetaker was however not a possibility during this research, since I was in 
Tanzania on my own. SC Tanzania was already providing both translator and transport and could not 
spear an extra person for my project. I therefore had to both facilitate the workshops and record the 
generated information. The fact that the workshops took place through a translator slowed down 
everything, giving only a little extra time to take notes and photos while the translator or the 
participants were speaking.  At the same time all workshops were recorded with a dictaphone. Lastly 
the output from the generative workshop would help store some of the created data. After each 
workshop, extra notes and photos was taken to supplement the collected information.  
Though the workshops were recorded, only a small part of the recordings were in English. Most of the 
discussions happened in Kirundi and I only got relatively short resumes from the translator, transcribing 
the interviews therefore made little sense. To mitigate for this, I developed a diary-based data 
management method. Every evening I wrote all my handwritten notes into my computer 
supplemented by other observations and analysis of the visual output of the workshops. Looking 
through the photos and listening to the recordings taken during the day helped discover hidden layers 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
and get a more complete picture of the workshops. All observations, reflections and interpretations 
were assembled in the Finding’s Diary (appendix 12).  
As described in the above sections a lot of the most valuable information was gathered under informal 
settings. In these situations, it would often be inappropriate to start recording the conversations and 
notetaking could be difficult. As soon there was the time for it, this information was also recorded in 
the Finding’s Diary.  
As researcher I to play a central role in the recording of the “Finding’s Diary methodology”. The findings 
are to a greater extent based on my memories instead of immediate recordings of the events and 
statements when they happened. This unavoidably introduces a danger that I interpret and remember 
the events different from the reality. To mitigate for this potential bias the Finding’s Diary has always 
been founded in notes, recordings and pictures while staying open to write out own reflections on the 
events under separate headings. Keeping a constant reflexivity towards my own role both among the 
informants and as interpreter of the findings has been central throughout the process.  
6.8. Data analysis 
The aim of analysis is to offer explanations and interpretations on the descriptive information collected 
through the field study helping to answer the research questions (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015). The 
insights collected during the formative evaluation should thereby help Lifeshelter improve their 
product portfolio. At the same time the findings should inform SCD when deciding on future projects 
providing learning environments for emergency situations.  
This clear predetermined focus on assessing the quality of the physical learning environment provided 
by the classrooms at Undugu makes it possible to follow a top-down approach in the analysis (Skovdal 
and Cornish, 2015). In order to get an overview, a preliminary grouping of the data is made according 
to the areas of process, functional and technical performance presented by HEFCE (Blyth et al., 2006) 
(see chapter 3). Subsequently the data was systematically indexed according to the research 
parameters presented in chapter 5 - Selection of Research Parameters.  
Following this top-down analyses the data was investigated for other relevant parameters and findings 
than those defined within the scope of the predefined research parameters. This more exploratory 
bottom-up approach thereby allowed for the discovery of parameters neglected when the research 
parameters were created (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015). The bottom-up part of the analysis is crucial in 
order to let the participatory methodology live. It is the bottom-up part that makes space for the values 
and experiences of the participants to influence the research and its indicators.

7. Workshop Design  
In the following chapter research question 2) will be answered:  
2. Which participatory methods are suitable when involving the specific users of the classrooms in 
the evaluation of the physical learning environment? 
7.1. Selection criteria’s  
There are countless participatory methods meant to engage users. The main goal of these methods is 
to facilitate the production and exchange of knowledge between the informants and the 
researcher/designer (chapter 4). In the relevant literature, these methods are often described as tools, 
and the two terms are used interchangeably. This interchangeability is mirrored in the following 
chapter in order to navigate flexibly through the literature. Universal Methods of Design (Martin and 
Hanington, 2012) and Delft Design Guide (Boeijen et al., 2014) exemplifies books dedicated to present 
methods for all the different stages of the design process – inspiration, ideation and implementation. 
Market Creation Toolbox (Larsen and Flensborg, 2011), Qualitative Research for Development (Skovdal 
and Cornish, 2015) and The Field guide to Human-Centered Design (IDEO, 2015) are all examples of 
publications focused on the developing world, presenting a broad scope of methods for qualitative 
feedback and community engagement. Also, within the humanitarian and development sector a long 
list of toolsets is available. Within Save the children some of these are; A Kit of Tools for Participatory 
Research and Evaluation with Children, Young People and Adults (SCN, 2008) and the previously 
mentioned; Improving Learning Environments Together in Emergencies (Bashir et al., 2018b) 
framework. In total these presents hundreds of different methods all centered around the user but 
serving a variety of different purposes.  
The methods can be categorized in different ways and most of the tool books each have their own way 
of doing so; according to the design phase they are meant for (Boeijen et al., 2014), (IDEO, 2015), by 
the role of the researcher and participant (Martin and Hanington, 2012) or as presented by Morten 
Skovdal and Flora Cornish (2015) according to the primary purpose of the method:  
1. “Mapping tools seek to develop maps that contain information about local realities and 
practices.  
2. Time analysis tools focus on temporal aspects of community life, looking, for example, at 
changes over time or between seasons.  
3. Linkages and relationships tools seek to visualize the connections between different factors 
promoting or undermining well-being or health.  
4. Experiential tools seek to bring forward community members’ experiences.  
5. Prioritization and quantification tools help community members seek consensus through ranking 
and scoring.  
6. Action planning techniques systematize the planning and evaluation process.  
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
7. Training tools prepare facilitators to use the tools in a flexible, engaged, inclusive, and 
participatory way.” 
(Skovdal and Cornish, 2015) 
The main focus of the workshop was to uncover the user experience of the physical learning 
environment in the classrooms at Undugu Primary School. As the name implies the experiential tools 
are specifically aimed at investigating the experiences of the participants. Building on the theory 
presented in chapter 4.4 the experiential tools will often be generative helping to access the tacit 
knowledge and the latent needs that are formative for the experiences (Sleeswijk Visser et al., 2005). 
To get an overview of how users evaluate the importance of subjects produced by other tools 
prioritization and quantification tools can be useful. The prioritization helps narrow down the 
discussion and highlight the most relevant subjects.  
Often the subjects of interest will be found in or related to multiple locations making the mapping tools 
effective to both get an overview and to discover new subjects of importance. As an example, the 
mapping tools are often used for the evaluation of schools. However due to the classrooms’ confined 
space and indeed very stationary nature, the mapping tools were not found to be relevant for their 
evaluation.  Linkages and relationships tools were also among the considered tools. These are effective 
to get an overview of the connection between various stakeholders and/or artefacts. However, the 
network around the classrooms were found mainly to be relevant on a management level. This 
information was provided by Caritas and Save the Children Education officers while the workshops with 
the daily users of the school were directly related to the learning environment.  
When choosing tools for participator workshops it is however not only the type of tools that should be 
taken into consideration. Other factors such as skills of the facilitator, location, timing, available 
materials, participants and ethics will all influence the suitability of the methods (see chapter 6) 
(Skovdal and Cornish, 2015).

7.3. Description of the selected tools. 
In the following section the tools selected for workshops with students, teachers and PTA will briefly be 
described as they were intended to be carried out during the sessions. The tools are inspired by the 
reviewed publications but altered to fit the context.  
7.3.1. Yes / No / Maybe 
The Yes / No / Maybe tool is a warmup exercise aiming at catching the participants attention, get a less 
formal vibe and get an impression of the group.  
The room is divided into three zones. One symbolizing yes, one symbolizing no and one symbolizing 
maybe. The participants are told to stand up. Different questions are asked, and the participants are 
instructed to “vote with their bodies” walking to the zone with the statements fitting best to their 
observation. When everybody has positioned themselves, the different groups are asked why they 
placed themselves as they did.  
7.3.2. Drawing Exercise 
The participants are asked to help each other draw the UNICEF Semi-permanent classroom, the 
permanent classroom and the Lifeshelter classroom on separate flip charts. The classrooms should be 
drawn one at a time, and the participants are prompted to add details – doors, windows, roofing 
structure, decorations and accessibility. 
After the drawing session the participants are asked to present their drawing and go through the 
different details one at a time. The facilitator asks clarifying questions ensuring that all the research 
questions are answered. It is especially important to ask for the non-visual items – How is the room 
temperature and ventilation? How is the quality of light in the classroom? What is it like in the 
classrooms when it is raining? The facilitator asks the participants to think about critical incidents – 
experiences in which the classroom has been particularly good/bad. The participants are asked to 
envision what it is like in the classroom during different times of day, different seasons and different 
types of whether.  
7.3.3. H-Assessment 
A huge “H” is drawn in the middle of a flipchart. The participants are asked to draw a happy smiley in 
the top left corner and a sad smiley in the top right corner. The name of the classroom should be 
written in the top of the paper, between the two vertical lines in the H. In the lower part of the H, a 
light bulb is drawn, leaving space for ideas on how to enhance the classroom. The participants are 
asked to think about and list the strengths and positive aspects of the classroom in the left side of the 
paper. Clarifying questions are asked to understand why they rate the aspects as they do. The 
participants are now asked to fill in the right side of the paper with the negative aspects, challenges 
and weaknesses of the classroom. Once again clarifying questions are asked and when solutions to 
some of the aspects come up, they are to be filled in to the idea section of the paper. Open questions 
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
are presented to spark discussion. The different details of the classroom are gone through. Questions 
related to whether, time of day and year are asked. When someone disagrees, it is important to 
understand their reasons. 
 
Table 7 - Illustration of the H-assessment template (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015) 
7.3.4. Dot Voting 
Following the H-assessment exercise the participants are handed 3 red and 3 green sticky paper dots 
each. The participants are now asked to vote in silence on 3 enablers and 3 disablers for a good 
classroom. After the voting a discussion should be started around the question: Why did the 
participants vote as they did? 
7.3.5. Ranking Values 
Building on the Dot Voting exercise, participants are asked to rewrite the five statements with the most 
votes on. The aim is to phrase parameters influencing the quality of the classroom. Only the core of the 
statement should be written using as few words as possible, leaving out the negative and positive 
adjectives (e.g. “there is too little space in the classroom” should be translated to: “space”). Each 
parameter should be written on three post-it notes.  
A matrix is drawn on a flip chart, with the three classrooms along one axis and the numbers 1-5 
representing the importance of the different parameters along the other. The participants then place 
the parameters on the 1-5 continuum, rating the quality of each of the classrooms.  It is important to 
get clarifications on why they prioritize as they do. If disagreement occurs, the participants should be 
invited to explain their standpoints.

8. Field Studies – How did the methods work 
This chapter reflects on the methods used for data collection with a primary focus on the workshops, 
as they form the theoretical backbone of the thesis. Furthermore, the chapter will elaborate on my 
reflections on the participatory part of the research, as this part was found particularly challenging.  
8.1. Planning and preparation  
In the following section some of the power structures, cultural differences and other challenges 
surrounding the participatory research will be presented.  
8.1.1. Motivation of stakeholders 
In an evaluation study, where stakeholders do not benefit directly from the outputs of the research, 
the researcher is extremely dependent on the goodwill of the stakeholders (chapter 2.1). Save the 
children Tanzania are settled on their permanent classroom solution and are not planning on taking in 
new designs. Their main concern was therefore on supporting another country office in the 
organization, but they did not have any direct interest in my project as such. To the other stakeholders 
such as Caritas, and the incentive staff on Undugu Primary School, their gained benefits of assisting me 
were even more vague. Nevertheless, they were of significant help to me and my work. Caritas aided 
me because I came as SCI staff and was introduced by SC Tanzania. Caritas is the employer of the staff 
at Undugu, which meant that they were my key contact to arranging meetings with the headteacher, 
who then assisted me with getting in contact with the PTA, teachers and students. Curios on who I was 
and what I wanted, and in the hope that I would give gifts or incentives, as well as a general willingness 
to help are all part of the explanation of why the participants were attending the workshops. But at the 
end of the day, they were there because they were told to be. When introducing the workshops, all I 
could do was to ask them to do me a favour by participating and answering my questions. I made it 
clear from the beginning that the findings of my research would not benefit their school directly, and 
that I would not be giving out gifts or other incentives as “payment” for their participation. The reason 
behind my decision for not giving incentives, is elaborated in the next chapter.  
8.1.2. Gifts and Incentives 
The level of the monetary incentives for the refugees working at the schools have been established by 
MHA, UNHCR, Caritas and SC Tanzania. Giving financial incentives to people participating in data 
collection is against SCI policy (SCI, 2018), and could destabilise the agreement between the education 
agencies in the camp. Among the refugees there was a general dissatisfaction with the incentives they 
received, but at the same time there was an understanding that SC have more money than Caritas. This 
ongoing argument on incentives combined with my role as white male representing SC on a Caritas 
school, created expectations of donations from my side. As an example, the headteacher asked me for 
money and goods whenever other staff from Caritas or SC were not around. When consulting SC Staff, 
they were clear on the matter that we could not give out any more money than the agreed incentives, 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
especially not on a Caritas School. The headteacher’s request for subsidies was therefore kindly 
rejected. 
The last day at the school was dedicated to observations in the classrooms. After having finished the 
last workshop, I decided to donate all the non-used materials (flipchart, post-it notes, pens, pencils and 
tape) to the headteacher. By waiting till the last day of my fieldwork before giving any “gifts” to my 
informants, ensured that the “gifts” would not affect the data collection. Deciding not to give anything 
was also an option, however, I felt the need to do so, as I wanted to thank them for their time and 
effort. The donation completely changed the collaboration of the headteacher. The lazy eyes and 
hanging shoulders disappeared and he took initiative to link me up with different classes to do 
observations of the teaching. In retrospect, a strategic donation of e.g. learning materials at the 
beginning of the visit might have helped to better gain the support of the headteacher. This can 
however be a difficult field to manoeuvre, and as mentioned, it could potentially affect the data 
collection. Furthermore, I was representing SC at a Caritas school and had to refrain from stepping in to 
the ongoing discussions on incentives for the teachers, as well as avoid confusing the relationship 
between the two organizations for the refugees in the camp. 
  
The role of gift-giving to informants is often discussed in the literature, with many arguments both for 
and against. Marie Larsen and Andreas Flensborg (2011) propose bringing small gifts for the 
participants when conducting field studies, with the argument that when the researcher gives 
something back as an appreciation for the received information, it can balance the reciprocity. On the 
other hand, by introducing gifts there is a danger of biasing the informants, making them reply what 
they think the researcher would like to hear rather than what they experience. In the context of my 
research on a school with more than 2500 students, giving gifts would be particularly difficult. Should 
every child receive a gift, or only those who participated in the workshops? The ILET Data Management 
Handbook describes how you should try to treat the kids evenly and not make promises that you 
cannot keep (Bashir et al., 2018b). As I was already the centre of attention and could not go anywhere 
on the school without having 30-50 kids around me, handing out small presents would likely have 
made this much worse. It would have made it even harder to conduct observations without disrupting 
the teaching further, as the students were often more focused on me than on the teacher.  
When giving gifts it has to be thought out carefully and aligned with local customs and expectations. 
Giving a small donation to get the support from a valuable stakeholder might be beneficial in some 
situations, however, it is important to be careful in order to avoid destabilizing internal power relations 
or create expectations from other stakeholders that cannot be fulfilled.  
8.1.3. Planning, Sampling and Sensitization 
Despite having travelled, lived, and carried out previous projects in the Sub-Saharan part of Africa, it 
keeps being a challenge for me to adapt to the cultural differences of planning and making 
agreements. Due to the great number of different stakeholders involved in the research in Nduta, this 
==PAGE:54==
53 
 
turned out to be particularly challenging. First of all, my planning was dependent on when the SC cars 
went back and forth between Kibondo town and the Nduta refugee Camp, when my translator was 
available, and on the timetable at Undugu. To the newcomer most of these activities seemed to 
happen ad hoc without following any written or formalized plan. At the same time most of this internal 
communication happened in Swahili or Kirundi making it challenging to take part in. With none of the 
stakeholders benefitting directly from the outputs of the research I was lacking leverage when making 
appointments. It was necessary to find a balance between being persistent and clear about what I 
wanted while being conscious that I was asking favours from people. This requires a great deal of 
awareness and flexibility from the researcher. You have to be constantly ready for unexpected 
possibilities and always have your materials ready in case potential participants for an interview or a 
workshop suddenly shows up. You never know if you will get the chance to interview them again, so it 
is better do it while you can. In general, sensitivity for the cultural costumes are key while being 
patient, persistent and prepared.  
A combination of not being able to obtain a clear timetable for neither the SC cars, my translator nor 
the activities at the school made the planning and sampling of the workshop particularly difficult.  
Multiple times I sat down with both Caritas and SC education officers, the headteacher and my 
translator to schedule when different activities should be carried out. However, if just one of the 
stakeholders did not follow the plan everything needed to be rescheduled. The headteacher at Undugu 
Primary school was gatekeeper for the workshops, because he was the one knowing the timetables 
and creating contact with PTA and teachers. Consequently, the sampling of the workshop groups was 
coordinated through him. For each of the workshops the headteacher or one of his assistants collected 
the students according to my instructions on gender, grade, use of classroom and based on a 
randomized sample of the class.  
As described in chapter 6.2.2, the aim was to sensitize the participants a few days prior to the 
workshops, preparing them for what was going to happen and making them think about their learning 
environment. Due to the challenges on planning described above I however never managed to do so. 
Apart from the sampling criteria’s given to the headteacher I did not know who was going to attend the 
workshop before we were all in the room. I could have partially mitigated this by going into each of the 
grade 1 classes on the first day at the school, presenting myself and my purpose of research. 
Furthermore, this would have increased my accountability, letting everybody know what a Caucasian 
man was doing at their school.

8.2. Facilitating with a Translator  
In the following section some of the challenges related to facilitating workshops with a translator will 
be described. At the same time, the section presents various recommendations on how these 
challenges can be met to improve the outcome of the workshops.  
Normally SC would use the best English teachers among their Incentive staff as a translator. However, 
the usual translator had just stopped working for them a few weeks prior to my arrival. After one day 
with Alexander, the “second-best” English teacher, as a translator it was clear that his level of English 
was not sufficient. Too much information got lost in translation and too much time was wasted 
explaining the meaning of the different words to him. Instead the Manager of Education at SC Kibondo 
told one of his Kirundi speaking Education Officers to assist me. As a young educated teacher Petronia 
seemed like the perfect translator for the job. However, the internal power relations turned out to be 
challenging. As an education officer Petronia found that working as a translator was of low status. At 
the same time, she was given the job, without any consideration for what other tasks and plans she 
might have had throughout the week. Though never outspoken, this resulted in a subtle resentment 
from her side. She had no interest in understanding my methodologies and aim of research but just 
wanted to finish off the workshops so she could get on with her own tasks. However, she did 
everything she was told and helped both arrange and facilitate the workshops, but having a translator 
who was actually engaged in the project would have improved the project. 
Not surprisingly, facilitating a workshop in collaboration with a translator completely changed the 
dynamics of the workshop. Especially establishing and maintaining discussions within the group turned 
out to be challenging. The translator could not both spark discussion and translate at the same time. 
Whenever the subject of the discussion was translated the discussion died out, while the participants 
waited for the translator and me. The translation dragged away the focus from the discussion and the 
arguments of the different participants. Thereby the workshop turned to be centered around the 
facilitator taking shape of a focus group interview instead of a focus group discussion (SCI, 2019d). 
Under normal circumstances the facilitator of a workshop needs to show attention and flexibility by 
picking up the different threads and leading the discussion in directions informing the research 
questions in the best way possible. When the facilitator does not understand the participants this role 
is left to the translator, which requires a close collaboration between the facilitator and the translator. 
The workshop guideline plays a crucial role in ensuring that both parts are aligned on what direction 
the workshop should take. Normally the workshop guide should only serve to point out the overall 
direction of the workshop, thereby making space for deviations by following the arguments of the 
participants, revealing new themes, subjects and concerns. In this study, the workshop guide almost 
ended up being a rigid framework with the guiding questions turning into a questionnaire format and 
thereby reinforcing the dynamics of a group interview instead of a group discussion.   
==PAGE:56==
55 
 
To successfully facilitate a participatory workshop without speaking the language of the participants a 
highly engaged and collaborative translator is required. The translator needs to not only understand 
the subject of interest, but also the methodological consideration on which the participatory 
workshops and group discussions is based. The translator is thereby turned into a co-facilitator 
changing the role of the researcher from facilitator into notetaker and observer.  
During the research at Undugu Primary school an attempt was made to give more ownership of the 
workshops to Petronia, in the hope that increased responsibility would result in greater engagement. 
However, I never managed to get Petronia to fully engage in the project and apply the participatory 
methods. As leader of the research I should have been more persistent, but as explained in the 
previous section finding the right balance between receiving assistance and putting up demands was 
challenging. The two workshops she facilitated with grade 1 students was subsequently dominated by 
close ended questions, and resembled an assessment of the students rather than a group discussion.  
8.3. Workshops 
In the following sections some of the most significant issues and qualities of the workshops will be 
highlighted. In table 8 in chapter 7.4 an overview of the workshops conducted at Undugu Primary 
School is presented.  
8.3.1. Grade 1 
To the grade 1 students I was a complete alien. It seemed like very few of them had ever been in room 
with or spoken to a Caucasian person before. On top of this, the workshops were held in the teacher’s 
office, which was not optimal in relation to making a comfortable environment for the students. But it 
was the only place where we would not get disturbed by all the other curios children. When the 
students entered the room they were all overwhelmed, silent and shy, making the warmup exercises 
increasingly important (chapter 7). Though the warmup exercises helped significantly loosen up the 
kids, the mood changed again when we sat at the table. The unfamiliarity of sitting at a table with two 
adults they did not know made them all silent. At the same time, my aim of speaking to the kids as 
equals was odd to Petronia. During a session Petronia was facilitating, all the students placed 
themselves at the one side of the table when we sat down. I asked Petronia to tell them to sit around 
the table instead. She did however prefer this setup and did not ask them to change seats (see image 
7). Our distribution around the table thereby reinforced the power structures and contributed to an 
atmosphere of assessment rather than dialogue.

In general, the workshops with the grade 1 students were characterized by the very teacher focused 
education, practiced at the school. Contrary students in the Nordic countries, schooled through a 
participatory learning form, the students were not used to reflect themselves and speak their own 
mind. Instead their answers looked very much as if they replied what they thought I would like them to. 
E.g. when asked about their favourite activity in school, all their replies were centered around cleaning 
and keeping the classroom nice and tidy. 
The aim of the Drawing Exercise was to have the students draw the different classrooms, thereby 
creating visual representations of the learning environment. The idea was that we should go through 
the drawing step by step, adding extra details, while speaking about the experiences with the room 
(see chapter 7). However, it turned out that the drawing skills of the students were not as good as I had 
expected when planning the exercise, making the drawings almost useless (see image 8). As the tool is 
described in the ILET Data Management Handbook (Bashir et al., 2018b) the students has to draw the 
whole school. Drawing the whole school is better suited as a group exercise as each student can draw 
each their building, fence or playground. Drawing only the classroom requires a much higher level of 
detail, demands better drawing skills, and makes it harder to collaborate.

The combination of the challenges caused by the collaboration with the translator, the students low 
age, my “weird” looks, and the non-participatory education tradition all made the outputs of the

be used as an indication of the student’s perceptions of the different research parameters, but should 
not be taken as finite statements. As previously described, the workshops never turned into an actual

8.5. Observations 
The observations have played a key role in the data collection process, supplementing the findings 
from the interviews and workshops.  
The observations of the teaching situations have allowed for something very close to a “fly-on the wall” 
observation situation. In most social situations sitting in a corner observing and taking notes of the 
activities will be somehow odd and unavoidably mediate the behaviour of the observed. This was also 
the case in the classrooms and on top of this my unfamiliar looks just increased the distraction I 
caused. However, unlike most other social situations all the actors were facing the same way, allowing 
me to sit in the back without being in the field of vision of the students. At first, the students constantly 
turned around to watch me, but after a while they got used to my presence. I did my best to ensure 
the teachers that I was not there to assess their performance, but was only interested in the quality of 
the classroom.  
The possibility to attend the classes myself and experience what the rooms are like, has been a unique 
source of insights. The observations grants access to what people actually do and not only what they 
say they do (Skovdal and Cornish, 2015). Addressing noise, sound levels and distractions turned out to 
==PAGE:62==
61 
 
be challenging in the workshops. The participants did either not have the right words to describe their 
experiences or they were not aware of the issues. However, during the observations it became clear 
that the level of noise was disturbingly loud in all classrooms. Not only due of the acoustics of the 
rooms but also because of the teaching form (see chapter 9.9). During the workshops it had been 
mentioned that the rooms were loud, but the participants did not have sufficient knowledge to know 
that this also had to do with the teaching. 
Due to the unpredictability of the planning, most of the observation sessions ended up being 
conducted on the last days at the school. Ideally more of the observations should have been carried 
out earlier in the process, creating a better background for the workshops. Having a better 
understanding of the classrooms could have allowed me to ask more in-depth questions during the 
interviews/workshops. Likewise it could have been interesting doing short interviews with the students 
after each class. Asking specific questions on certain behaviour and thereby supplementing the 
observations with the direct experiences of the students.   
8.6. Sub-conclusion 
Chapter 2.5, 3 and 4 highlights the importance of applying context specific research when creating new 
learning environments. The chapter above however describes how cultural practices connected to the 
specific context makes it challenging doing evaluations informing the decision making. The language 
barrier plays a fundamental role. If not speaking the language of the informants, it is crucial to have a 
dedicated, engaged and openminded translator. The translator is highly defining for the quality of the 
findings. At the same time the researcher must navigate the unfamiliar cultures with humility and 
respect while being persistent in his tasks. Patience, preparedness and flexibility are fundamental for 
the project to succeed.

9. The Quality of the Learning Environments 
In the following section, the data collected during the field study in Nduta Refugee Camp will be 
analysed. The research parameters presented in chapter 5 will be the backbone of the analysis to 
determine the quality of the physical learning environment provided by the classrooms. Following a 
top-down approach the parameters will be used to index the findings (chapter 6.8) (Skovdal and 
Cornish, 2015). However, the predefined research parameters will not be followed strictly. During the 
research some parameters turned out to be less relevant in the given context and will therefore be left 
out while others will be combined, and new ones will be added. Likewise, the findings will not be 
presented following the HEFCE areas of process, functional and technical performance strictly (Blyth et 
al., 2006). The functional and the technical performance parameters are interdependent, and the 
performance of the classroom is therefore presented better following the different subjects instead of 
these broad areas.  
The findings are based on triangulation between the data collected during the interviews, the 
observations and the workshops with students, teachers and PTA (see chapter 6.2).  
The technical data has been provided through interviews and informal dialogues with: Supply Chain 
Manager SC Tanzania; Bavo Malyango (Malyango, 2019), Construction Engineer SC Kibondo; Timotheo 
Hezro (Hezro, 2019), Architectural Technologist DRC; Donald Kambosha (Kambosha, 2019), 
Architectural Design Consultant at UNICEF and Plan International; Shedrack Bugeraha (Bugeraha, 2019) 
and COO & Founder of Lifeshelter; Jakob Christensen (Christensen, 2019). For a complete list of the 
technical parameters see appendix 10. 
9.1. Delimitations 
During an earthquake in the Kagera region in Northwest Tanzania in 2016 the TME classrooms proved 
not to be strong enough. Based on the TME classroom a new and earthquake proof classroom has 
been designed by SC. Both the Tanzanian government and the NGO’s have stopped building TME 
classrooms and are now doing SC classrooms instead. Since TME permanent classrooms are no longer 
made, SC permanent classrooms will be used as reference for the comparison of technical parameters 
and price of the classrooms. The structural reinforcements have made the SC classroom 30% more 
expensive than the TME classroom, while the dimensions, interior and looks are identical. The TME 
classroom can therefore be used as reference for the physical learning environment provided by the SC 
classroom. 
Since the two Lifeshelter Classrooms in Nduta refugee camp are the first of its kind to be used in a real-
life context, the pilot has already provided Lifeshelter with valuable learnings. Since the two classrooms 
were erected in spring 2018 various alterations has been made to improve the design.

changed the design the damaged roof at Undugu unavoidably influenced the overall impression of the

9.3. Cultural perception 
The overall perceptions of the classrooms were very much based on an unavoidable comparison with 
the TME Permanent classrooms who were perceived as “real classrooms”.  
The Lifeshelter classroom did not align with anything the informants had seen before. All the focus 
groups stated that it did not look like a classroom. This was however also the case for the UNICEF semi-
permanent classroom. Generally, the functionality of the rooms and not their looks was essential for 
the feedback the respondents gave.  
Architectural designer and Consultant for UNICEF; Shedrack Bugeraha found the structure appealing 
from a design perspective but pointed out how some people might associate it with vernacular huts 
and not a classroom (Bugeraha, 2019). No other respondents however stated this point. One grade 9 
student described that the classroom looked like the tents she had seen the military use in Burundi, the 
rest of the focus group agreed with this observation. They saw it as a negative factor but were not 
alarmed by the design.  Nobody else stated that they associated the Lifeshelter Classrooms with 
military structures.

management at Undugu Primary School tried to compensate for the lacking space by using the 
Lifeshelter classrooms for the smaller students. The normal class size at Undugu Primary school is 
around 60 students pr. class. If the size of the classes had been adapted according to the floorspace,

9.6. Privacy and distractions 
Both teachers and PTA highlighted how the Lifeshelter classroom provided a less distracting learning 
environment. The absence of windows in the side of the building ensured that the students would not 
get distracted by what was going on in the school yard. However, the opening at the gable behind the 
teacher was undesirable. I did not get any clear answer from the students on what they preferred.   
The insulating properties of the Rockwool makes the classroom less vulnerable to outside noise. In 
both the TME permanent classroom and in the UNICEF semi-permanent classroom noise from the

9.7. Air quality  
It was hard to get a clear overview of the experienced temperature in the classrooms. Generally, the 
respondents had a hard time putting words on temperature and ventilation. All rooms were 
experienced as cold in the morning and warm in the middle of the day. There was a tendency to 
describe the Lifeshelter as warmer both in the morning and in the middle of the day. 
Both teachers, PTA and headteachers described how the ventilation in the Lifeshelter classroom was 
insufficient. It was however not clear whether this observation stems from the overcrowded sessions in 
the classrooms. During the observations it was clear that the ventilation was far from sufficient under 
such circumstances. The air quality decreased, and the temperature rose throughout the sessions. 
However, it would not be expected from any other room of 41 m2 to sustain an optimal air quality 
while containing more than 100 kids. When less than 60 kids were in the room the air quality was 
satisfactory. It is worth noting that the observation was carried out in March during the rainy season on 
partly cloudy days with temperatures varying between 20-25°C. The ventilation might be scarce during 
the dry seasons.  
There is no doubt that both temperature and air quality have significant impact on the quality of the 
learning environment (Higgins et al., 2005). To get a better overview of the air quality, continuous 
measurements of temperature and CO2 levels should be carried out in the classrooms, in turn 
providing an overview of air quality fluctuation through days and seasons under different loads of 
students.

9.8. Light 
During sunny and partly cloudy days the lighting was sufficient in all the classrooms. The respondents 
however described how it could get so dark during heavy rains that it was not possible to clearly see 
the blackboard. This was an issue in all the classrooms.  
In the Lifeshelter classroom the blackboard is placed up against the windows. None of the participants

among the participants. 
Having the light coming in from the front and back of the classroom also seems less ideal for lighting up 
the tables. The students unavoidably shade the light coming in.  
9.9. Acoustics  
Based on the observations in the classrooms it was hard to determine their acoustic qualities. The 
noise inside all the classrooms was exceptionally loud, not necessarily due to the design of the rooms, 
but because of the style of teaching. Especially during the language classes the teaching style was 
based around the teacher saying a sentence which the learners repeated over and over again. The 
focus was not on correct pronunciation but on having the kids saying the sentence as loud as possible. 
Yet alone having more than 50 kids in a room of 41-68m2 will inevitably be loud. During the observation 
of teaching, non-scientific measurements of the noise level in the rooms were made with an iPhone. In 
all classrooms the background noise was measured to be around 60-70dB while peak noise reached 
110dB. It was not possible to determine if any rooms were more optimal than others.  
However, to get a basic overview of the acoustic properties of the classrooms M.Sc. in Audio design 
Morten Roar Berg was consulted. Berg is specialized in sound and acoustic design in relation to 
architecture and works as concept developer on acoustic solutions in schools and office buildings.  A 
quick assessment of the acoustic performance of the rooms was not uplifting. According to Berg, all of 
the rooms were very poorly suited for teacher centred education (Berg, 2019).  
The acoustic qualities of a room are defined by the surface area's ability to absorb sound (i.e. soft and 
porous materials that can be penetrated by air are preferable to hard materials). 
According to Berg, the TME Permanent classrooms seems to be the absolute worst. No surfaces are 
done in materials with any particular acoustic absorption. The room will thereby contribute 
significantly to the noise level. “The speech intelligibility will probably be so poor that the students just 
half way down the room will use most of their mental capacity on distinguishing the words from each 
other” (Berg, 2019). 
The opening along the side walls of the UNICEF Semi-permanent classrooms allows the sound to 
“escape” making the room a partly free space, in turn making the noise level less intense. Conversely, 
==PAGE:72==
71 
 
this could make it hard for the teacher to be heard. Had it been a closed room, the bamboo walls 
would probably have made the speech intelligibility better, but with the huge amount of open space, 
hard surfaces would have been preferable. The UNICEF semi-permanent classrooms probably have the 
best acoustic performance, but they barely have any sound insulation making them vulnerable to 
outside noise. 
The dome shape of the Lifeshelter classroom complicates the process of making an acoustic 
assessment. Due to the dome shape the acoustic will be complex and vary quite a lot throughout the 
room. Though Rockwool has some formidable sound absorbing properties the 5mm cement plaster 
makes the inner walls a hard surface. The complete lack of acoustic absorption in the room will make 
the acoustic performance very poor.  
As described in chapter 2.5  “The Importance of the Physical Learning Environment” the acoustic 
properties of the learning environment have great influence on the academic performance and 
attainment of the students (Shield and Dockrell, 2008). Morten Roard Berg confirms this: “If you dive 
into the literature on acoustics and learning the picture is clear, classrooms with bad acoustic 
performance can have long lasting damaging effect on the students” (Berg, 2019).

9.10. Hygiene 
Neither at the schools nor in the rest of the camp there is pavement to be found. All roads, trails, 
squares and yards are made from the bare soil. This is the source of a fine red dust entering 
everywhere while the rain turns it into a sticky clay, that builds up on shoes and is carried into any 
building. The lack of pavement is for that matter also the case in the host community, and in the town 
of Kibondo only a few hundred meters of tar roads can be found. Because of this, mud and dust are 
==PAGE:73==

everywhere, and make frequent cleaning a necessity. This is of course also the case in the classrooms 
with hundreds of kids passing in and out throughout a day. In most buildings in the host community the 
floors are made from tiles or smooth cement, making mobbing and cleaning easy. 
In both the TME permanent classrooms and in the Lifeshelter Classrooms the floor is made from 
concrete allowing it to be mopped. In the Lifeshelter classroom a curb at the doors however hinders 
the water to get out after cleaning resulting in the classroom often only being swept. The amount of 
soil accumulated on the floor made it hard to tell that there was concrete underneath (image 22). In 
the UNICEF Classrooms the floor is made from burned bricks making washing unsuitable. Both 
teachers, PTA and learners problematized how it was difficult to clean the Lifeshelter Classroom and 
the UNICEF classroom probably. Especially the fear that kids going bare feet should get Tunga 
Penetrans from the Jigger Flea was highlighted as a reason for good cleaning.

9.11. Protection from the environment 
Both learners, teachers and PTA described how noise from the rain drops hitting the corrugated iron 
roof makes it impossible to continue teaching during heavy rains in both the TME and the UNICEF 
classroom. In the Lifeshelter classroom the noise is not an issue and the teaching can continue even 
during heavy rains.  
During windy rain there were reported problems in all three types of classrooms with the rain entering 
the rooms through the windows. Forcing the children to move to the one side of the classrooms. In the 
UNICEF classroom this was particularly a problem and the water apparently even come in through the 
bamboo walls.  
==PAGE:74==
73 
 
In the afternoon the sun entered the Lifeshelter Classroom and hit the benches in the back of the 
room. The Lifeshelter classrooms in Nduta are placed east-west, so when the sun is low on the sky it 
can enter the classrooms through the openings in the end.  
As described in section 9.1 delimitations the earthquake proof SC Permanent classrooms are now being 
build instead of the TME classroom. Also, the Lifeshelter classrooms are made to be earthquake 
resistant. This is not the case for the UNICEF semi-permanent classroom.  
The Rockwool used in the Lifeshelter classroom are certified with Euro Class A fire safety (Lifeshelter, 
2019). The whole room as such however has no fire certificate as it is the case for the other 
classrooms. In the permanent classrooms it is only the roofing structure and the door that is made 
from combustible materials, while only the bamboo end walls are combustible in the Lifeshelter. Only 
the CGI roof are noncombustible in the UNICEF semi-permanent classrooms. All classrooms use the 
same wooden school tables. The TME and UNICEFE classrooms only has one door. There are burgle 
bars in front of the windows in the TME classroom while the windows are 170 cm above ground in the 
UNICEF classroom. The Lifeshelter classroom has a door in each end of the room.  
Lifeshelter states that the Lifeshelter classroom can resist wind of up to 32,6 m/s (Lifeshelter, 2019). 
There are no numbers on wind resistance for the other classrooms. According to the SC, DRC and 
UNICEF construction experts, the designs are based on local construction standards (Hezro, 2019), 
(Kambosha, 2019), (Bugeraha, 2019). Timotheo Hezro SC Construction Engineer explained that strong 
winds are no problem in the region and therefore not essential in the building design.  
9.12. Other Parameters 
The curb at the door in the Lifeshelter Classrooms makes it difficult to access for children with mobility 
disabilities. Generally, the desks used in all classrooms did not accommodate for wheelchair users and 
there was not enough space between them for a wheelchair to get around.  
On the TME permanent classrooms a water harvesting system was installed collecting water for 
cleaning of the rooms and watering of plants.  
The lack of doors and burglar bars in the Lifeshelter classroom makes locking the classroom impossible. 
At Undugu no learning materials were stored in the classrooms but at other schools locking the 
classrooms was essential.  
9.13. Process – Procurement and Construction 
In line with the recommendation in among others The Grand Bargain (IASC, 2016) both the Supply 
Chain Manager for SC Tanzania, Bavo Malyango (Malyango, 2019) and all three construction experts 
(Hezro, 2019), (Kambosha, 2019), (Bugeraha, 2019) highlighted the benefits of using as many local 
materials as possible. The two main reasons were avoiding unforeseeable, time consuming and 
==PAGE:75==
74 
 
expensive logistic challenges and enforcing the local markets, building capacity and economy within the 
host community. Both the SC and the UNICEF classrooms are designed around these principles, 
building on the same construction techniques and materials as used in the host community. Thereby

9.15. A short sum up 
Through the above chapter it has been shown how the Lifeshelter Classroom on several parameters 
are underperforming compared to the other classrooms at Undugu Primary School. Many of the 
challenges of the Lifeshelter classroom stems from a lack of understanding of both the context, the 
local standards and good practice when providing learning environments. The poor size of the 
classroom, the absence of an integrated blackboard and the difficulties of cleaning are all examples of 
issues that could have been easily avoided. Had Lifeshelter taken their time to get a better 
understanding of what is required from a classroom such evident failures could have been avoided.

10. Discussion 
As presented in chapter 1.1 the following problem statement has been guiding for this thesis.  
Can participatory methods, applied in the evaluation of physical learning environments in refugee 
camps, provide insights to the decision makers (i.e. Lifeshelter and Save The Children Denmark) which 
may help them improve their products and services? 
As shown in chapter 9, the field studies in Nduta indeed provided new and valuable insights on the 
physical learning environments in the classrooms at Undugu Primary School. The Lifeshelter classroom 
turned out to have shortcomings on surprisingly many parameters.  
Starting with the possibly biggest issue of them all; the amount of imported materials in the Lifeshelter 
Classroom. As described in chapter 9.13 the other classrooms are constructed from local materials by 
local companies. The Rockwool in the Lifeshelter classroom are shipped all the way from Europe. This 
compromises the principles of localization. As part of The Grand Bargain a wide range of the largest 
international NGO’s has committed to increased local ownership and procurement within their 
programs (IASC, 2016). With the use of international materials, a part of the money used on the 
classroom leaves the recipient country, limiting the projects positive economical side effects within the 
host community. When constructing classrooms in fragile contexts, the logistical challenges are many 
and unpredictable. In the Kibondo district the dirt roads were not passable for heavy vehicles during 
heavy rains. Whereas the SCD project with Lifeshelter in Baidoa, Somalia (chapter 1) has been 
significantly delayed due to Al Shabab (i.e. a rebel militia) takeovers of the main roads between 
Mogadishu and Baidoa. Lastly the Rockwool is an unknown material in the East African context. 
Nobody knows its properties, how to use it or how to maintain it. The primary qualities of Rockwool are 
its excellent insulation properties. In the Lifeshelter classroom this theoretically limits the heat 
radiation from the sun as well as external noise. Had the classroom performed significantly better than 
the other classrooms on these parameters, this might have justified the downsides of import and lack 
of local know-how. However, Lifeshelter did not perform convincingly with regards to indoor 
temperatures.  
Many of the challenges with the Lifeshelter classroom serves first and foremost as an example of how 
crucial it is to understand the context one is designing for (IDEO, 2015). Many of the recommendations 
presented in chapter 10 are both fast and easy to implement, and do not require any major redesign of 
the structure. These shortcomings are not the result of compromises made in the design process but 
stems primarily from simply not knowing the local/contextual constraints of user communities. 
The dimensioning of the classroom is a great example. Lifeshelter Aps were not aware that their design 
was 40% smaller than the Tanzanian standard before receiving the findings from this research. 
Investigating the quality of existing solutions would be one of the first steps in a pre-implementation 
==PAGE:79==
78 
 
assessment. Such an assessment has never been made, as indicated by Lifeshelter Aps’ lack of 
knowledge about the size of competing classroom solutions. The curb at the door, hindering both 
cleaning and access for mobility disabled students, is another example of how basic user-needs can be 
thwarted by accident, due to a lack of context-specific insight (image 23, chapter 9.10). The concrete 
curb runs all along the end of the room and works as support for the bamboo wall. Apart from making 
it minimally more convenient to cast the concrete curb, there is no reason why it should be in front of 
the door. Likewise, the absence of an integrated blackboard shows a substantial lack of understanding 
of the basic workings of a classroom. The blackboard is central to most shared activities in classroom, 
and yet it is not possible to have a blackboard in the Lifeshelter classroom without blocking the 
windows.  
As described in chapter 2.7, Pamela Woolner et al. (2007) argues that the inclusion of the users can 
improve the quality of the classroom while also increasing the users’ motivation towards academic and 
social goals. Making a pre-implementation assessment of user needs, would have been the first step in 
this direction, increasing both the acceptance of the classroom and the quality of the design. 
Furthermore, the community could be engaged in the actual construction and decoration of the 
classroom creating a sense of ownership and empowerment. Not only increasing the satisfaction 
among students and teachers but potentially also helping to avoid vandalism on the buildings.  
10.1.1. Only the basic conditions for learning? 
In chapter 2.7 it is described how Higgins et al. (2005) has documented how students’ concentration, 
mood, well-being and attendance are all significantly impacted by air quality, temperature, lighting and 
sound in the classroom. In the OECD LEEP report on physical learning environments (2017) these 
parameters are described as the “basic condition for learning” (see chapter 2.7). Neither the amount of 
light, the acoustics or the ventilation of the Lifeshelter classroom was convincing. Also, in the other 
classrooms the noise was unacceptably high, as described in chapter 9.9, and the rain came in during 
heavy showers. This draws attention to the general lack of funding within the development sector as 
described in chapter 2.2. Just providing the basic conditions for learning ends up being a challenge 
within education in emergencies. The same tendency is seen in the SC QLF (Quality Learning 
Framework) and in the evaluation frameworks described in chapter 3. The focus is on providing an 
adequate learning environment, one that meets the minimum requirements of its users by ensuring 
comfort, access, health, safety and security (OECD, 2017). During the research at Undugu it was seen 
how the classrooms at the school were just adequate on several parameters. Relative aspects 
influencing the quality of the learning environment such as colours, plants and aesthetics (OECD, 2017) 
were percent in in the TME permanent classrooms in form of wall painting and decoration, while 
neither the Lifeshelter or the UNICEF classroom had the like.  
==PAGE:80==
79 
 
By failing to include user communities throughout all phases of the project, Lifeshelter ultimately 
reproduce the humanitarian and development sectors’ long-lasting challenges with discrepancies 
between product utility and user needs (chapter 4.1) (Aly, 2016). As described above, this distance 
from their own beneficiaries, damages both quality and impact of their product. At the same time, the 
use of international materials compromises the principles of localization within the humanitarian and 
development sectors.  
The lack of context awareness and inclusion of the community combined with the import of European 
construction materials all resemble some of the most problematic downsides that can occur when 
western world companies operate in developing countries. Too many stories already exist, of well-
meaning companies pushing poorly designed products on the developing world.  
When designing products to the most disadvantaged, it is important to avoid making the problem-
solving too relative to the magnitude of disadvantage. If there is no classroom at all, almost any 
solution will be preferable to that, but if the goal is to not only make things better, but to make them as 
good as possible, this disadvantage-relativity must be abandoned and replaced by a strategic focus on 
maximizing product utility, while ensuring profit. Such a dedication to excellence is desirable partly 
because the competition is serious, and because staying ahead of competitors is vital to long term 
success, partly because Corporate Social Responsibility is becoming increasingly important for 
companies in the humanitarian and development sector. Such a shift in focus might even further the 
degree to which these classrooms are in accordance with children’s global rights to quality education 
(UNICEF, 2005), which again is a popular reference in CSR statements. 
By accepting this idea, companies specializing in providing learning environments for emergencies 
would transition from aiming for the adequate to striving for the effective or even efficient learning 
environment through the inclusion of both relative aspects and space design (see chapter 2.7) (OECD, 
2017). I believe that this thesis compellingly demonstrates that user-oriented, and possibly also 
participatory, approaches to pre-implementation assessment, holds the potential of quite substantial 
product improvement for very modest cost, and as such demonstrates the feasibility of the investment. 
10.2. Participatory or user centred evaluation 
Through the use of generative methods, the workshops were meant to engage the classroom users and 
get deep insights on their experiences with the learning environment. As described in chapter 4, this 
project thereby aims at positioning itself as generative design research, in the top right corner of The 
Landscape of Human-Centered Design Research by Sanders and Stappers (2008) (figure 5, chapter 4.2) 
Through the drawing exercise, the users were meant to express themselves creatively while generating 
visual representations of the classrooms, allowing for in-depth discussion of their experiences. While 
the research was design driven, the user was meant to be a partner, contributing with experiences and 
knowledge on the challenges and potential improvements of the classrooms. As described in chapter 8 
==PAGE:81==
80 
 
the workshops never really got to be generative or group discussion based (this was especially the case 
with the grade 1 students). Primarily the language barrier but also the age of the students and the 
cultural differences prevented this from happening. Instead the workshops ended up more like focus 
group interviews. The user was no longer a partner in the research but more of a subject of interest, 
while the workshop got to be more dominated by the predefined research parameters instead of being 
free and explorative. On The Landscape of Human-Centered Design Research the workshops has 
thereby moved away from the participatory section and into the area of user-centred design. Where it 
can best be described as contextual inquiry.  
The framework and mindset of design thinking as well as participatory methodology, has been used to 
prepare and facilitate the workshops. As described above it can however be argued that the workshops 
were not actually participatory. Anyway, it has been shown in chapter 9 and discussed above in section 
11.1 how multiple valuable insights has been generated throughout the research. The answer to the 
problem statement thereby becomes more complicated. Yes, the application of participatory methods 
has played a central role in providing insights on the physical learning environment. It is questionable 
however, to what extent the participatory element in the workshops is responsible for the provision of 
insights. Getting the users’ perspective on the research parameters has been vital to achieve a full 
understanding of the performance of the learning environments. Tentatively, this understanding 
should be primarily attributed to the user-oriented approach rather than to the participatory design 
method.

12. Conclusion 
The field study at Undugu Primary School in Nduta Refugee camp in Tanzania has shown how 
conducting participatory workshops through a translator, can be highly challenging. It is crucial that the 
translator is engaged in the facilitation while not only understanding but also accepting the purpose of 
both the methodology and the overall research. The translator is thereby turned into a co-facilitator 
while the researchers more have the roles of observer and notetaker. When navigating in unfamiliar 
contexts, the success of the project is dependent on researchers’ ability to be respectful, flexible and 
patient while demonstrating persistence and pushing for objectives to be reached. 
Through the research conducted in this thesis it has been highlighted, how the Lifeshelter Classroom 
still face multiple shortcomings, preventing it from providing an adequate learning environment for 
education in emergencies. Based on the findings from the field study, major improvements of the 
classroom are however readily available. The research has thereby demonstrated a need for 
understanding the local context, as well as including the community, in order to provide quality 
products. As the literature shows, the engagement of beneficiaries has the potential of increasing both 
motivation and ownership among users, all increasing the impact of the product (Higgins et al., 2005; 
OECD, 2017; Woolner et al., 2007). It is thereby concluded that the quality of the learning 
environments and the user satisfaction could be dramatically increased, if Lifeshelter Aps change their 
focus from a “one solution fits all” mentality into a holistic, user-inclusive and context-specific 
approach.
