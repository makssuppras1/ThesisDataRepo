Abstract 
Brewers’ spent grain (BSG) is the insoluble residue of barley malt that is byproduced 
in brewing process. Although it is an abundant waste product of the brewing industry, 
it is mainly used as animal feed and provides very low economic returns. Hence, it is 
of great interest to obtain knowledge on its potential value and this work focuses on 
one of the main constituents of BSG, i.e., the proteins. Until today, there is plenty of 
literature review on the protein content of the wort and the final beer but very little on 
the spent grain. 
Therefore, the purpose of this project is to gain a deeper insight on the protein 
composition of the spent grain and for that purpose BSG was fractionated by applying 
several methods such as a Dacofi filter machine, protein extraction with solvents and 
protein extraction with enzymatic hydrolysis. The derived fractions were later 
analyzed by applying SDS-Page, Isoelectric focusing (IEF), FPLC and LC-MS. 
Moreover, the polyphenol content along with antioxidant activity was examined in 
order to correlate this content to the nutritional value of the fractionated product.  
The results on protein and polyphenol content were evaluated to determine a potential 
nutritional value of BSG and its derived fractions. Specifically, samples of pressed 
BSG and the sludge showed high protein content and low antioxidant activity, 
whereas the pressed liquid was found to contain les proteins and higher amount of 
polyphenols with high antioxidant activity.

1. Introduction 
Barley (Hordeum vulgare L.) is the world’s most important cereal after wheat, maize 
and rice.  Its genus has existed and diverged for almost 12 million years.[1],[2] 
Barley crop is known for its wide adaptation in different climates, as it is grown 
annually on 48 million hectares in a wide range of environments. It can grow at the 
highest altitudes in the mountains, in the deserts of and also near the Arctic Circle.[2] 
However, most of the world’s barley is grown in Europe (60% in 2005), and world 
production of the grain ranks fourth behind maize, wheat and rice. [2],[3],[4] 
Germany has been the largest producer of barley for many years with reaching a 
production in 2010 of  13 million tons. Nevertheless, it should be noted that some 
cultures, mainly in North Africa and Asia, still depend heavily on barley as a food 
source and production has also increased. Turkey, the largest developing country 
producer in both 1980 and 2010, increased production from 1980 by 35% to 7.2 
million metric tons in 2010. Production is known to increase significantly in countries 
as Iran (+137%), Argentina (+1307%), Algeria (+89%), and Iraq (+67%) over that 
period.[3],[5]

Moreover, barley consists of three main parts: the germ (embryo), the endosperm 
(comprising the aleurone and starchy endosperm) and the grain coverings. The last 
may be divided into three fractions which can be seen also in Figure 1: the pericarp 
and the seed coat, the innermost layers surrounding the aleurone and the husk. [8]

The pericarp is waxy and waterproof, whereas the seed coat is a semi-permeable 
membrane, forming the exterior and the interior of the kernel. The husk consists 
mainly of lignocellulosic walls that provide external protection to the grain but also 
contains small quantities of proteins, resins and tannins. [6]

During the last decade there has been increasing interest in barley to improve human 
health, mainly in industrialized countries and major urban areas of developing 
countries, boosting barley food-products and consumer interest in such products. 
Barley’s beta glucans have been associated with lowering blood cholesterol and its 
low glycemic index has marked it suitable to diets for people with Type II diabetes. In 
addition barley is a rich source of tocols, which are known to be capable of reducing 
serum LDL cholesterol through their antioxidant action. [9] 
1.1 Diet Proteins 
Recent concerns regarding exhaustion of animal sources, expansion of global 
population, and its needs for nutrients such as proteins, has led to increased research 
interest in plant proteins.  
Proteins are essential in nutrition as they provide energy and valuable amino acids for 
the human organism. These amino acids are being decomposed during digestion and 
subsequently are utilized by the body for the synthesis of new proteins. Such proteins 
==PAGE:14==
DTU                                                                                                                       Angelos  Charalampidis 
National Food Institute                                                                                                              16-07-2016 
13 
 
could potentially contribute to the formation and replacement of tissues or intervene 
in several other processes in the human body. [10]  
WHO (2007), indicates a daily protein intake of 0.66 g/kg body weight as the best 
estimate of the average requirement for adults. It is nevertheless suggested that this 
quantity should be raised to 0.8 g/kg body weight to reach what is considered as the 
safe level. These values are not valid for children and pregnant or lactating women, 
who need higher intakes. Recommended intakes cover only the body’s needs for 
amino acids but not for energy, hence they are appropriate only in the framework of a 
complete and balanced diet. [11]  
In general, the quality of a protein can be assessed by (i) its composition in amino 
acids, (ii) the possible presence of an amino acid at a concentration that strongly 
differs from that found in a reference, high quality protein, and finally (iii) its 
digestibility. [12] 
 
1.2 Formation of malt  
Barley is also the most important raw material for beer production and that is due to 
the high enzymatic content, which is able to convert starch into fermentable sugars.  
To best exploit barley's composition in brewing, it is initially subjected into a process 
called malting. In that process barley is steeped or intermittently immersed in water 
for two to three days, which allows the grain to sprout. Sprouting the grain then 
generates the enzymes necessary to convert starches into sugars during brewing. Once 
the grain has reached the optimum moisture level, it is transferred to the malting or 
germination floor, where it is constantly turned over a four to five-day period to 
ensure consistent germination. The germination step promotes the synthesis and 
activation of enzymes in the aleurone and starchy endosperm, including amylases, 
proteases, b-glucanases and others. The action of these enzymes modifies the structure 
of the starchy endosperm. During the germination process, which normally lasts 6 or 7 
days, the endosperm is fully and evenly modified. The malted barley is dried (kilned) 
at 40–60oC to a 4–5% moisture content, to avoid microbial contamination and to 
generate flavour components. After this step, the dried malt is stored for 3 or 4 weeks, 
to reach homogeneity and equilibrium. [6],[13]  
The aim of the malting process is the production and activation of enzymes. These 
enzymes also contribute to the hydrolysis of β-glucans and hordeins (water insoluble

proteins), which would otherwise restrict access of enzymes to the starch granules. 
[14] 
 Barley to be used for malting must meet specifications for germination, kernel size 
and weight, grain protein, activity of several enzymes, and many other traits. [15],[16]

Proteins are among the barley components that are essential for the quality of malt 
and beer, representing approximately, 8– 15% of its total mass. 
Thus, the total protein quantity in the barley grain is a crucial factor for final beer 
quality. High protein contents decrease available carbohydrates, with a negative 
influence on the brewing process.  [17],[18]  
 
1.3 BSG production 
Brewer’s spent grain (BSG) is the major byproduct of the brewing industry, 
representing around 85% of the total by-products generated. This is equal to almost 40 
million tonnes of BSG in 2014 worldwide, of which all European and American 
brewing are based on more than 50% malt. [19] The remaining 15% of by products 
are trub, spent yeast and spent hops. [8] BSG is a lignocellulosic material that has a 
high content of protein, more than 20% of protein on dry basis, depending on the used 
grain. [6],[20] According to Townsley (1979), spent grain weights on average almost 
30% of the original malt weight, representing approximately 20 kg per 100 l of beer 
==PAGE:16==
DTU                                                                                                                       Angelos  Charalampidis 
National Food Institute                                                                                                              16-07-2016 
15 
 
produced. [21] Thus, the brewers’ spent grain (BSG) is produced in large amounts 
even by small breweries and it is available at low or no cost throughout the year. [6] 
BSG can be derived from barley malt or from other cereal grains after the mashing 
and consists of the husk that covers the original barley malt grain, a part of the 
pericarp and seed coat layers. 
The main application, that has been used widely, is as animal feed. In the past, most 
of the BSG generated by brewing activities was disposed of to the environment. 
Currently, breweries usually supply this material at low cost to local farmers for use 
as cattle feed. However, BSG is a raw material of interest for application in different 
areas because of its low price, large availability throughout the year and valuable 
chemical composition. [22] 
 
1.3.1 Chemical composition and physicochemical properties of BSG 
As it has already been mentioned, the BSG consists of the husk–pericarp–seed coat 
layers that covered the original barley grain. Depending on the malting process the 
starch content may vary and walls of empty aleurone cells may also remain. After 
mashing the starch content of the BSG will be negligible, and some remnants of hops 
added during mashing might appear In the BSG depending on the brewing profile 
used. Thus, the major components of BSG will be the walls of the husk–pericarp–seed 
coat, which are rich in cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin, and may contain some protein 
and lipid. The husk also contains considerable amounts of silica and much of the 
polyphenolic components of the barley grain. [23] According to Kunze (1996), 25% 
of the minerals present in barley are present as silicates. [8] Fig. 3 shows the 
appearance of a BSG particle by scanning electron microscopy, where the bright 
points in the second image of the husk are silicates. [6] BSG predominantly consists 
of the husk-pericarp-seed coat layers that are rich in cellulose, non-cellulosic 
polysaccharides, lignin, protein and fat. This is reflected in the composition of BSG 
(Table 3), and thus BSG can be regarded as a lignocellulosic material. In addition to 
the components detailed in Table 3, it has been shown that BSG is also a valuable 
source of vitamins, minerals and amino acids, particularly for animal feeding. The 
vitamins present in BSG are biotin, folic acid, niacin, choline, riboflavin and thiamine, 
pantothenic acid and pyroxidine. BSG is also reported to contain minerals such as Ca,

1.3.2 Potential applications of BSG 
Approximately 20 kg of BSG is generated for every 100 L of beer produced. [22] 
However, as a result of increased cost of transportation coupled with EU regulations 
on waste disposal, alternative uses for BSG are being studied. [26] Some of them are 
presented below.

1.3.2.1 Animal Consumption 
This abundantly available raw material has found a place in animal nutrition, not only 
reducing the cost of feeding but also creating an outlet for this material.[27] The 
presence of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin, and also the presence of essential 
amino acids provides an essential feed for ruminants.[28] Moreover, the ingestion of 
BSG or its derived products provides some health benefits, since dietary fiber has 
been generally related to affect some non-infectious diseases.[25] Both wet and dried 
BSG have been utilized as animal feed for many years. However, dried BSG is 
preferable, as high moisture content of BSG (80 to 85%) makes it particularly 
susceptible to microbial growth and subsequent spoilage in a short period of time (7 to 
10 days).[29]  Belibasakis and Tsirgogianni (1996) found that the protein content of 
BSG contains the limiting amino acids, which are lysine, threonine, tryptophan, 
methionine (and cystine), isoleucine, histidine, valine, arginine, and phenylalanine 
(and tyrosine). Their findings showed in fact that BSG supplementation (45% w/w) 
increased actual milk yield, milk total solids content, and milk fat yield, compared to 
control containing maize silage (45% w/w).[30] Since BSG is derived from materials 
utilized for humans, it can be incorporated into so many human diets, such as breads 
and snacks; especially where there is need to boost the fibre contents. [29]   
 
1.3.2.2 Metal Adsorption 
Plant wastes, agricultural and industrial by-products have been utilized also in the 
past, since they provide a cheap source for adsorbing heavy metals from aqueous 
solutions.[31]  BSG has also found application in removal of heavy metals from 
wastewater by adsorption. In fact, Lu and Gibb (2008) examined the removal of 
Cu(II) ions from aqueous solutions and they found a maximum adsorption capacity of 
10.47 mg g-1 dry weight at pH 4.2. [32] BSG has been also tested by Silva et. al as an 
adsorbent on acid orange 7 dye (AO7), a monoazo acid dye used in paper and textile 
industries. It has been proven that high levels of color removal (>90%) can be 
achieved with low contact and that BSG can successfully remove AO7 dye in aqueous

1.3.2.4 Growth medium for microorganisms  
According to Szponar, BSG was recommended as a suitable medium for isolation and 
maintenance of unknown strains and highly suitable for screening and production of 
new biologically active substances and fast spores production.[27] BSG was reported 
to be used for the cultivation of a great range of bacteria such as Bifidobacterium 
adolescentis 94BIM, Lactobacillus sp. (Novik et al., 2007), actinobacteria

(Panagiotou et al., 2006), among others.  [36],[27],[37],[38],[39] 
 
1.3.2.5 Bioethanol production 
BSG has been successfully used as media for fermentation and especially for 
production of bioethanol. The composition of brewer’s spent grain (BSG) as 
described in previous sections is of great interest as  the cellulose and hemicellulose 
fraction makes it a good feedstock for ethanol production.  Both Xiros et al. (2008) 
and Xiros and Christakopoulos (2009) reported the ethanol yield of 74 and 109 g/kg 
of dry BSG by N. crassa and F. oxysporum, respectively under microaerobic 
conditions (0.01 vvm), while Liguori et al. found  a yield of 260 g ethanol/kg of dry 
BSG by using the strain S. cerevisiae NRRL YB 2293 without nutrient 
supplementation. [40],[41],[42]

1.4 General Brewing Process 
To best understand the production of BSG as a waste stream, a brief introduction in 
beer brewing is presented below. There are four main ingredients in beer: water, a 
starch source such as malted barley or oat, a yeast able to ferment and hops. This 
project aims to analyze the protein content of malted barley and thus, it is of interest 
to summarize all steps of transformation. Before a starch source can be used to make 
beer, it must be malted, which involves a natural conversion process that has been 
described in section 1.2. After malting, the grain is crushed in a malt mill to gain 
smaller pieces from the husks and break apart the grain kernels. Subsequently, the 
process of mashing begins. During this process, sugar producing enzymes are released 
(such as alpha and beta-amylases) and break down much of the starch into sugars 
which play a vital role in the fermentation process. After the mashing, the process of 
lautering follows, where the mash is pumped to a lauter tun and the resulting liquid is 
strained from the grains. Brewer spent grains can be collected right after the mashing 
and lautering step. The liquid produced at that step is known as wort. The wort is 
moved into a kettle where it is boiled with hops and sometimes other ingredients such 
as herbs or sugars. The boiling process serves to terminate enzymatic processes, 
precipitate proteins, isomerize hop resins, concentrate and sterilize the wort. 
Furthermore, hops add flavor, aroma and bitterness to the beer. Cooling of the wort is 
followed after this during the process of inoculation, where the yeast is added, and the 
fermentation begins producing CO2, ethanol and other components. After the 
fermentation process the beer maturates for a week to several months as some 
fermentation byproducts like diacetyl and acetaldehyde need to be reduced by the 
yeast and that is being done through maturation of the beer. Finally after a week to 
three weeks, the beer is cooled down to freezing temperature, the yeast is removed 
and the beer is allowed to rest. The beer is also often filtered to remove remaining 
yeast and particulates so that it can be ready for serving or packaging. Several 
optional steps can be introduced to the process, such as pasteurization, 
addition/removal of CO2 etc. Some breweries can use 100% raw barley (unmalted) for 
mashing, by adding the right amounts of enzyme separately. Other small scale 
breweries produce beer without being able to control any form of sterile conditions in 
the equipment. [8]

1.5 Protein structure and function – from barley to beer 
In order to have a better insight on the proteins that appear on spent grains, a review 
of proteins that are present in beer is provided below. Beer contains mostly simple 
proteins (e.g. LTP1, protein Z), in contrary to conjugated proteins (nucleoproteins, 
phosphoproteins, glycoproteins, chromoproteins, lipoproteins and membrane 
proteins). Most of these simple proteins contribute to properties, such as foam 
formation or haze formation. Barley malt contains also endoproteases that are being 
produced through germination and remain intact after kilning. These endoproteases 
have been examined by Jones et al. with regard to their activity in relation to the 
temperature. It was found that the enzymes can be deactivated in temperatures higher 
than 60o C, however their proteolytic activity was not influenced in the final stages of 
kilning, where temperatures exceed the 80o C. [43] Jones examined those enzymes 
and their behavior during malting and mashing and found that cysteine proteases have 
a great impact in the hydrolysis of barley proteins during malting and mashing. [43] It 
has also been found that metalloproteases play a very significant role in solubilizing 
proteins, especially during mashing at pH 5.8–6.0. Other proteins are involved in 
protein folding, such as protein disulfide isomerase catalyzes the formation of protein 
disulfide bridges. Summarizing the most important factors for the protein composition 
in finished beer are barley cultivar and the level of protein modification during 
malting. [44]

Hordein is an alcohol-soluble protein family, found in barley endosperm, which has a 
low lysine, high-proline, and high-glutamine. It is the major nitrogenous fraction of 
barley endosperm composing 35–55% of the total nitrogen in the mature grain. They 
are storage proteins and are known to form a polymorphic series of polypeptides of 
considerable complexity.  These storage proteins form a matrix around the starch 
granules, and it is suggested that their degradation during malting directly affects the 
availability of starch to amylolytic degradation during mashing.  Shewry et al. divided 
the hordeins according to their size and amino acid composition in four different 
fractions (A-D), dependent on their size and amino acid composition. [46] A-hordeins 
(15–25 kDa) are not likely to be storage proteins and contain protease inhibitors and 
a-amylases. B-hordeins (32–45 kDa) are rich in sulfur content and  constitute 
approximately 80%of the hordein fraction. B-hordeins have a general structure, with 
an assumed signal peptide of 19 aminoacid residues, a central repetitive domain rich 
in proline and glutamine residues.  C-hordeins (49–72 kDa) are low in sulfur content, 
and D-hordeins (100 kDa) are the largest storage proteins. During malting, disulfide 
bonds are reduced and B- and D-hordeins are broken down by proteolysis. Hence, 
malt contains less than half the amount of hordeins present in the original barley.

Chemical methods for protein hydrolysis are difficult to be controlled and the derived 
products may present reduced nutritional qualities (destroyed amino acids, formation 
of unusual amino acids). Enzymatic hydrolysis occurs under mild conditions of pH

has limited effects on the nutritional properties of resulting protein hydrolyzates.

1.6 Polyphenols - Antioxidant activity 
1.6.1 Phenolic compounds in BSG 
As previously mentioned, BSG consists predominantly of the husk-pericarp-seed coat 
and is largely made up of cell walls. Malt contains different classes of phenolic 
compounds located in different tissues of the grain. Some of the most abundants are 
benzoic and cinnamic acid derivatives, proanthocyanidins, quinines, flavonols,

source of phenolic acids. There is evidence to suggest that ferulic acid and p-coumaric 
acid are present at relatively high concentrations in BSG. [24] It has been reported 
that following ferulic and p-coumaric acids, the next most abundant phenolic acids in 
BSG were found to be sinapic, caffeic and syringic acids.[24] Several types of 
polyphenol extraction have been described. The mixture of water with organic solvent 
is one of the most used. [52]

1.6.2 Antioxidant activity 
Polyphenols and phenolic acids present in malt are natural antioxidants, capable of 
delaying, retarding or preventing oxidation processes, and therefore thought to have a 
significant effect in malting and brewing as inhibitors of oxidative damage. About 
80% of beer polyphenols originate from malt and the remaining 20% come from hops.  
A lot of research has been also conducted looking at the antioxidant activity of 
hydroxycinnamic acids, particularly ferulic and p-coumaric acids. A commonly used 
method for quantification of antioxidant activity is the 1,1-diphenyl-2-picrylhydrazyl 
(DPPH) assay, which measures the ability of the test compound to scavenge the 
DPPH radical. Regarding antioxidant activity, antioxidant is named any compound 
that is able to slow or inhibit the oxidation of a substrate, when it appears in a small 
concentration. Downgrading of food ingredients rich in fatty acids is due primarily to 
oxidation reactions. Fatty acids consist mainly of triglycerides that are derived of 
glycerol and three fatty acids. Acids with two or more double bonds are named 
polyunsaturated fatty acids and are prone to oxidation in presence of oxygen. 
[52],[54],[55] The formed lipid peroxides constitute the main products of oxidation 
and are odorless or without taste. Lipid peroxides of polyunsaturated fatty acids form 
through isomerization of conjugated forms, specifically conjugated dienes and trienes. 
These primary products are unstable and can be degraded in secondary products of 
oxidation such as aldehydes, ketones, lactones, furan derivatives. At the same time, 
polymerization of lipid peroxide derivatives occurs. Finally oxidizing reactions of 
other ingredients such as vitamins and food colorings or compounds that contribute to 
the taste and odor occurs. [52] As a consequence to the formation of these products is 
the quality downgrading of the food, reduction of its nutrient value and increase of 
health damaging compounds. However, phenolic compounds have strong in vitro and 
in vivo antioxidant activities associated with their ability to scavenge free radicals, 
break radical chain reactions, and chelate metals. Moreover, a high phenol 
consumption has been correlated with a reduced risk of cardiovascular diseases and 
certain cancers. [52]  
Research on the effects of dietary polyphenols on human health has developed 
considerably in the past 10 years. It strongly supports a role for polyphenols in the 
prevention of degenerative diseases, particularly cardiovascular diseases and cancers.

1.7 Aims 
Brewers' spent grain constitutes an abundant waste-product of many breweries, which 
has low economic returns. Therefore, it is of great interest to obtain knowledge on its 
composition and examine potential benefits from it. 
Until today, there is an extended literature review on the protein content of the wort 
and the final beer but very little on the spent grain. Therefore, the overall aim of this 
project is to separate the protein content of the spent grain and identify it to gain a 
deeper insight on a potential nutritional value. For that purpose, several separation 
techniques and analytical assays were applied. In addition, more specific aims were 
set to determine most abundant proteins and a potential value of these proteins. To 
stimulate this perspective, the polyphenol content along with antioxidant activity will 
be examined in order to correlate this content to the nutritional value of the product.  
However, it should be noted that the present project does not aim in fully unfolding 
the nutrient value of the separated proteins. That would require further amino acid 
analysis to acquire knowledge on the value as dietary supplement.

Wort and spent grain were produced entirely from malted barley. The food-grade malt

2.2 Preparation of wort 
The malt (50 kg) was milled with a grain crusher Piccolo 15 400V, setting 1.2 mm

The mash pH was adjusted to 5.7 with citric acid. During brewing, the following 
temperature– time profile was followed: 15 min at 54oC, increase to 62oC (1oC/min); 
60 min at 63oC, increase to 74oC (1 oC/min); 5 min at 74oC. The wort was separated 
from the insoluble BSG in a traditional lauter tun. The spent grain was then collected.

2.3.3 Enzymatic Hydrolysis of Brewers spent grain 
2.5 grams dried BSG protein mixed with 22.5 mL deionized water was homogenized for 1 
min at 5000 r/min before hydrolysis. The homogenized mixture was adjusted to the required 
pH (8.0) with 0.01 mol/L NaOH and heated in a water bath to the required temperatures (60 
oC) and then 1 mL Alcalase was added. The hydrolysis reaction was performed in a shaking 
incubator. The pH of the mixture during the reaction was maintained constant by proper 
addition of 1 mol/L NaOH solution. At the end of the hydrolysis reaction, the mixture was 
heated in boiling water for 10 min to inactivate the proteases. The hydrolysates were 
centrifuged at 4,000 r/min for 30 min and the supernatant was lyophilized and stored in the 
freezer for further use.[57],[58] 
Alcalase 2.4L was from Novozymes, Bagsvaerd, Denmark and sodium hydroxide was 
purchased from Sigma Aldrich. 
 
2.3.4 Fractionation of low molecular weight proteins from gel filtration FPLC 
Gel filtration separates molecules according to differences in size as they pass through a gel 
filtration medium packed in a column. Unlike ion exchange or affinity chromatography, 
molecules in this method do not bind to the chromatography medium and the buffer

DTU                                                                                                                       Angelos  Charalampidis

composition does not directly affect resolution. Thus, a significant advantage of gel filtration 
is that conditions can be varied to suit the type of sample or the requirements for further

This method was applied in order to detect proteins that are below 10kDa and therefore all

The mobile phase consisted of water and all proteins were detected by monitoring absorbance

2.4.2.1 Bradford assay 
The Bradford total protein assay is a protein determination method, which involves 
the binding of Coomassie brilliant blue G-250 to protein.  The assay is based on the 
observation that the absorbance maximum  for an acidic solution of Coomassie 
Brilliant Blue G-250 shifts from 465 nm to 595 nm when binding to protein occurs. 
Both hydrophobic and ionic interactions stabilize the anionic form of the dye, causing 
a visible color change. The assay is useful since the extinction coefficient of a dye-
albumin complex solution is constant over a 10-fold concentration range. 
Bradford was used to determine the total protein concentration of the wort, pressing 
water (mud), pressed water supernatant. 
All needed chemicals were provided by thermo scientific.[60]

It has the distinct advantage of requiring only trace amounts of your valuable protein. 
In many cases the accuracy of the assay is more than sufficient. Another advantage of 
the assay is its relative ease and quickness. 
An important advantage of the dye-binding method is the lack of interference from 
many compounds. It shows tolerance to salts and to detergents in small amounts. 
Thus, the method was appropriate to use protein content determination on samples 
that contained solvents as described previously in the fractionation of proteins section.  
The method's drawback lies in a different affinity of a dye for several pure proteins 
that react or bind differently, according to their structural characteristics, which 
virtually requires adjustment of the method each time it is applied, for use with a 
given protein. [62] 
In this assay all samples were used for protein determination. Protein content on solid 
samples was determined after extraction with solvents or enzymatic hydrolysis. In 
addition the protein content of hordein, albumin, globulin and glutamin fraction was 
determined after the extraction. 
For this assay protein dye amido black 10B was used and was dissolved in a wash 
solution that consisted of 90% methanol and 10% acetic acid, to prepare the stain 
solution. Afterwards 50μg of all samples were mixed with 300μl of washing solution 
in eppendorfs at room temperature. The mixture was centrifuged for 5 minutes at top 
speed leading to precipitation of the protein. The supernatant was discarded and 500μl 
of washing solution were added. The new mixture was centrifuged and the 
supernatant was discarded, thus repeating the step until the supernatant became 
colorless. Lastly the pellet was dissolved in 250μl of 0.1N NaOH 
and the sample was measured in spectrophotometer at 615nm.  
More details on the protocol can be seen in the Appendix.

2.4.3.1 SDS-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (Sodium dodecyl sulphate)  
SDS-Page has been used to identify different protein bands on the samples based on 
their electrophoretic mobility. The samples have been identified according to their 
molecular weight by comparing with standard protein samples of known molecular 
weight. All samples have been reduced by using a reduced SDS buffer containing 
mercaptoethanol (Laemli).  
SDS is an anionic detergent applied to protein samples to linearize proteins and to 
impart a negative charge to linearized proteins. This procedure is called SDS-PAGE. 
In most proteins, the binding of SDS to the polypeptide chain imparts an even 
distribution of charge per unit mass, thereby resulting in a fractionation by 
approximate size during electrophoresis. 
SDS is a strong detergent agent used to denature native proteins to unfolded, 
individual polypeptides. When a protein mixture is heated to 100 °C in presence of 
SDS, the detergent wraps around the polypeptide backbone. It binds to polypeptides 
in a constant weight ratio of 1.4 g SDS/g of polypeptide. In this process, the intrinsic 
charges of polypeptides becomes negligible when compared to the negative charges 
contributed by SDS. Adding SDS solves this problem, as it binds to and unfolds the 
protein, giving a near uniform negative charge along the length of the polypeptide. 
The gels that have been used, where either single percentage (12%) or within a range 
of 4-16%. All gels used were from biorad and novex. MW markers for gel 
electrophoresis were from Bio-rad. 
Protein size analysis, performed in Bio-Rad Mini Protean and Novex electrophoresis 
system were conducted with Bio-Rad Mini-Protean gradient TGX gels (4-20%, 20μL 
wells) and NuPAGE™ Novex™ 12% Bis-Tris Protein Gels (20μL wells) respectively 
at 200V for 45 minutes. Samples were diluted 1:3 with Bio-Rad reduced SDS Buffer 
(Laemli). Precision Plus Protein standard from Bio-rad was used as protein size 
reference (10-250kDa).  
The gels were subsequently stained with Coomassie Blue or Instant Blue and stored in 
the fridge.  
The samples that have been used, were all the liquid water-soluble samples (wort, 
pressing water supernatant, pressed water) and also solid samples extracted with 
solvents or by enzymatic hydrolysis. Prior to running the SDS-page gels, all samples 
were precipitated by TCA or ethanol precipitation method and resuspended in water

 2.4.3.2 Isoelectric focusing 
Isoelectric focusing (IEF) is a technique for separating different molecules by 
differences in their isoelectric point (pI). The method is able to separate proteins that 
differ in their isoelectric points by as little as 0.01 of a pH unit. 
 Separation is achieved by applying a potential difference across a gel that contains a 
pH gradient. The pH gradient is formed by the introduction into the gel of 
ampholytes, which are complex mixtures of synthetic polyaminopolycarboxylic acids.  
Prior to running the IEF gels, all samples were precipitated by ethanol precipitation 
method and resuspended in water to adjust them into the needed concentration, which 
was approximately 5mg/ml.  
IEF gels were purchased from GE healthcare life sciences. 
2.4.3.3 Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC-MS) 
LC-MS is an HPLC system with a mass spec detector. The HPLC separates chemicals 
by conventional chromatography on a column. Usually the applied chromatography 
method is reverse phase chromatography, where the metabolite binds to the column 
by hydrophobic interactions in the presence of a hydrophilic solvent (for instance 
water) and is eluted off by a more hydrophobic solvent (methanol or acetonitrile). As 
the metabolites appear from the end of the column they enter the mass detector, where 
the solvent is removed and the metabolites are ionised. The metabolites must be 
ionised because the detector can only work with ions, not neutral molecules. The mass 
detector then scans the molecules it sees by mass and produces a full high-resolution 
spectrum, separating all ions that have different masses. 
All samples were analyzed with LC-MS method to gain information on the protein 
content. The results from the mass spectrometry graph were compared to data 
retrieved for barley from Uniprot database. The proteins were identified by using 
MaxQuant; a quantitative proteomics software package designed for analyzing large-
scale mass-spectrometric data sets.

4. Conclusion 
BSG constitutes an abundant waste product, as it accounts for 85% of the total 
amount of byproducts generated in beer brewing and is estimated to reach a quantity  
of 40 million tonnes a year. Until now, BSG is being used primarily as animal feed, 
leading to very low economic returns. Nevertheless, this lignocellulosic feedstock 
contains also a high amount of proteins and the present project aimed on identifying 
the proteinaceous material in brewers spent grain. For that purpose, fractionation of 
BSG was achieved by applying the Dacofi filter machine into fractions of pressed 
BSG and pressed liquid, which was later centrifuged and a supernatant and a sludge 
were obtained. Total protein concentration analysis showed higher concentrations for 
samples of pressed BSG and sludge reaching 6 and 7.7% w/w respectively, indicating 
that the Dacofi filter is an efficient equipment able to produce fractions with reduced 
water content and enriched protein content.  
Further analysis on all derived fractions took place with SDS-Page and Isoelectric 
focusing. Best results were obtained from SDS-Page method and a number of proteins 
were detected. Protein Z, lipid transfer protein , α-amylase inhibitor BDAI-1, avenin-
like protein-a (ALP) and α-, β- amylase have been reported in the literature with 
similar molecular weights, suggesting that they appear in the analyzed samples. The 
first four proteins are known to contribute to beer foam stability and therefore it could 
be of interest to incorporate them in the final beer and enhance its properties.  
Moreover smaller proteins have been detected and isolated from FPLC analysis in all 
samples. However, by the date this project took place it was not possible to conduct 
further analysis with mass spectrometry. These fractions also have not been detected 
or identified based on literature therefore it could be of interest to study any potential 
characteristics and function. 
Analysis conducted with LC-MS provided a deeper insight to many proteins. The 
results indicated that fractionation was not actually achieved, as most proteins 
appeared in all samples but with different intensities. BSG samples were found to be 
more enriched in structural proteins and enzymes that catalyze biochemical reactions. 
Lastly, the polyphenol content was determined and an attempt has been made to 
correlate these values to antioxidant activity. Although samples of BSG and pressed 
liquid in particular were found to be enriched in polyphenols, only the pressed liquid 
showed high antioxidant abilities even in small concentrations.

5. Final Remarks - Future perspectives 
Further research and studies on the quantities and the role of all identified proteins is 
needed in order to determine, whether it is possible to recover a valuable end-product 
from the spent grain. It is not yet unrealistic to say that the liquid fraction, derived 
from the spent grain can be incorporated into a food system such as beverage or 
alcoholic drink with increased protein content, as it provides a certain amount of 
proteins and a good antioxidant activity that prevents its deterioration. BSG could be 
also incorporated in food system and in fact a few attempts have been made to include 
it in commercial food such chocolate bars or cookies. Perhaps an alternative solution 
would be to perform enzymatic hydrolysis of the proteinaceous material and extract 
smaller proteins or amino acids that can be later add to food systems. 
Future perspectives concern the use of methods that would enable to quantify the 
isolated proteins. That can be done by applying a chromatography method such as ion 
exchange, size-exclusion chromatography (or gel filtration), affinity chromatography 
that would enable isolation of proteins with also high molecular size and later 
quantify the proteins with the appropriate assay.

Appendix 2 
 
Bradford Protocol 
For the bradford method, the Bradford reagent was produced as per following: 
100 mg Coomassie Brilliant Blue G-250 were dissolved in 50 ml 95% ethanol and 
100 ml 85% (w/v) phosphoric acid were added right after. Then the reagent was 
diluted to 1 liter, after the dye was completely dissolved, and finally it was filtered 
through Whatman #1 paper just before use. 
Then the assay was completed by taking into account the following steps: 
Warm up the spectrophotometer before use. 
Dilute unknowns if necessary to obtain between 5 and 100 µg protein in at least one 
assay tube containing 100 µl sample 
If desirred, add an equal volume of 1 M NaOH to each sample and vortex (see 
Comments below). Add NaOH to standards as well if this option is used. 
Prepare standards containing a range of 5 to 100 micrograms protein (albumin or 
gamma globulin are recommended) in 100 µl volume. See how to set up an assay for 
suggestions as to setting up the standards. 
Add 5 ml dye reagent and incubate 5 min. 
Measure the absorbance at 595 nm. 
Finally a standard curve of absorbance versus micrograms protein was prepared and 
the protein content were determined from the curve.

Samples of BSG, pressed BSG, pressed liquid and sludge were stored at -20oC prior

Solid samples were weighed on an electronic balance weight on nitrogen free 
weighing paper and transferred to the bottom of a Kjeldahl tube. Liquid samples were 
transferred directly into a Kjeldahl tube on a technical weight, carefully so that there 
would be no sample residue on the sides of the tube. Two Kjeldahl tabs were added to 
each tube and covered with 20mL sulfuric acid (96%). Two blind samples with

and Kjeldahl tabs were prepared and used as references. The tubes were placed in the 
Foss Tecator 2020 digestor set to 440oC for 1.5 hours, until the liquid becomes clear 
green. When all samples became clear and green, the samples were heated for 
additional half an hour. After cooling down for 30 min. at room temperature, 20mL

Kjeltec Distillation Unit and the cooling water valve, was turned on 10 minutes before 
the first analysis. For each sample, an Erlenmeyer flask was prepared with 40mL 
0.1M HCl solution and 5 drops of A.C. Andersen indicator. The Erlenmeyer receiving 
flask is placed with the outlet from the distillation unit inside without touching the 
HCl solution. The Kjeldahl tube is placed with condensation hose. The distillation 
then runs for 5 minutes. Then the remaining HCl is titrated with 0.1M NaOH until the 
color turns clear green. The volume of NaOH used for titration is used to calculate the 
protein content in the sample. The analysis gives as result the nitrogen content of the 
samples and a general N factor of 6.25 is applied for calculation of the protein

For the amido black method the following solutions were made: 
 wash solution: 10% acetic acid, 90% methanol 
 staining solution: 26mg Amido Black per 100ml Wash solution, allowed to 
stand at RT for a couple of hours 
 0.1 M NaOH 
50μl of protein extract were mixed with 300μl staining solution in eppendorf tube. 
The mixture was vortexed and allowed to rest in RT for 5 minutes. Then it was 
centrifuged for 5 minutes at top speed and the supernatant was discarded carefully. 
The pellet was washed with 500μl of wash solution, vortexed and centrifuged for 5 
minutes at top speed. The supernatant was discarded right after. The process was 
repeated 2 times until the supernatant becomes colorless (2 washes). 
The clear pellet is dissolved in 250μl 0.1 N NaOH and the solution was measured in a 
spectrophotometer at 615nm along with some standard samples. 
Finally a standard curve of absorbance versus micrograms protein was prepared and 
the protein content were determined from the curve.

Appendix 5 
 
1) Ethanol precipitation took place as per following: 
99.9 % ethanol was used, cooled at -20 °C by mixing 1 volume of sample and 9 
volumes of the cold (-20 °C) ethanol. The mixture was incubated for 60 minutes at -
20oC. Then it was centrifuged for 1 minute at  4 °C; 15000 g. Supernatant was gently 
removed and the precipitate was dried in air before solubilizing in relevant buffer 
(SDS-buffer). 
 
2) For TCA precipitation the following stock solutions were used: 
100% (w/v) Trichloroacetic acid (TCA): 500g TCA were dissolved into 350 ml 
dH2O, store at RT. 
1 volume of TCA stock was added to 4 volumes of protein sample. i.e. in 1.5ml tube 
with maximum vol., 250µl TCA were added to 1.0ml sample. The mixture was 
incubated for 10 min at 4°C. and was later centrifuged at 14K rpm for 5 minutes. The 
supernatant was removed, leaving the protein pellet intact. The pellet was washed 
with 200µl cold acetone and was centrifuged again at 14K rpm, 5min. The previous 
steps were repeated for two acetone washed. After that the pellet was collected and 
dried by placing the tubes in 95°C heat block for 5-10 min to drive off acetone.
