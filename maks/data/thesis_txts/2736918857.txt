Abstract
Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cell therapy has shown remarkable
results in the treatment of certain cancers, particularly haematological
malignancies. However, challenges such as low efficacy, particularly in solid
tumours, severe side effects and escape of cancer cells from the immune
system remain significant hurdles.
This is driving the development of
next-generation CAR-T cell therapies.
Here, valuable tools for engineering the CAR-T cell output module to enable
development of a novel CAR-T/protein delivery system are presented. The
principle is to use the natural granzyme-perforin pathway of cytotoxic
lymphocytes to deliver recombinant proteins to the cytotoxic granules, where
they are stored and subsequently released upon interaction of the CAR with
its target.
This generates a highly-specific and rapid cell-to-cell delivery
module.
Flow cytometry-based assays have been developed for protein localisation.
To assess whether the protein has reached the cytotoxic granules (acidic pH),
a pH-sensitive reporter (tandem mCherry-EGFP) has been established.
It
indicates location in the target compartment by a shift of the cell population
to mCherry due to the pH sensitive properties of EGFP. To further assess the
release of the protein into the immunological synapse and its delivery to the
target cell, a delivery assay has been established. It similarly indicates the
presence of the protein in the target cell by a mCherry shift of the targeted
Nalm-6 cell population.
Fusion of the reporter protein with Granzyme B
(GzB) was successfully demonstrated to deliver the protein initially to the
cytotoxic granules and subsequently to the target cell.
In addition, a search was initiated for a relevant transporter system to enable
delivery in a manner similar to the GzB fusion, but using a small DNA
sequence instead that does not encode a protein.
The GzB signal peptide
tested showed unexpected results in delivering the protein to the cytotoxic
granules in Jurkat cells and activated primary T cells. Initially, the signal
peptide should only direct the protein to the ER, whereas a combination
with a sorting motif should mediate further sorting, which was only seen in
Sup-T1 cells. However, the signal peptide itself and a combination with two
different sorting motifs (from the lysosomal membrane proteins LIMP-2 and
LAMP-2) failed to demonstrate subsequent delivery to the target cells.
Further research is needed to shed light on the seen results and to identify
relevant transporter sequences, e.g.
using a sorting motif screen.
The
successful development of the cell system will open up a wide range of
potential applications due to the provided flexibility for the integration of
proteins of interest for targeted delivery.

Introduction
The development of cell-based therapeutics offers promising new approaches
to the treatment of complex diseases such as cancer. This is due to the cell’s
ability to recognise and integrate a variety of signals, actively translocate to
specific tissue compartments and generate context-dependent responses [1].
Such cell-based approaches have the potential to overcome current obstacles
faced by small molecules and biologics, such as lack of target specificity,
unwanted localisation in tissue compartments, lack of personalisation and
limited ability to modify the effects of the drug after administration to the
patient. Cytotoxic lymphocytes, such as cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs),
can provide an excellent platform for the development of such cell-based
therapeutic systems for several reasons:
• They offer a unique molecular transfer system from the delivery cell to
the target cell via the granzyme-perforin pathway using endosomal
compartments [2].
• The T cell receptors (TCRs) or related chimeric antigen receptors
(CARs) used in this project endow the T cells with high specificity for a
population of cells presenting either major histocompatibility complex
(MHC)-bound antigen or, in the case of CARs, any surface antigen.
The latter provides an even broader and more flexible target panel [3].
• Activated T cells differentially express cytokine and tissue-specific
receptors, allowing selective lymphocyte homing throughout the body
to target tissues.
• Laboratory and clinical protocols for lymphocyte modification and
therapeutic delivery have been developed in the field of adoptive cell
therapy [4].
The development of such a cell-based delivery approach in the field of cancer
immunotherapy is the focus of this thesis project, which aims to improve the
efficacy of current CAR-T cell therapies by enabling the cell-to-cell delivery of
specific effector proteins to the target cell. The engineering of the CAR-T cell
output module is critical to this, harnessing the natural T cell mechanisms of
protein trafficking, storage and secretion for cytolytic killing of tumour cells.
1.1
CAR-T Cells in Cancer Immunotherapy
To emphasise the purpose of this project, it is important to illustrate the
relevance and state of the art of CAR-T cell therapies and to place them in
the context of other cancer treatment strategies.
1.1.1
Conventional Treatments vs. Cancer Immunotherapies
Historically, surgery, chemotherapy and radiotherapy have been the three
pillars of cancer treatment [5]. However, conventional therapies have certain
limitations.
Surgery is mainly effective in early-stage cancers and in cases
where surgical resection would not affect the function of vital organs.

Radiation and chemotherapy both significantly reduce morbidity, but these
treatments are generic and target both cancerous and healthy cells, which
can lead to many harmful side effects [6]. In addition, the possibility that
cancer cells may develop resistance to these treatments, leading to disease
relapse, underscores the urgency of finding more targeted and effective
alternatives [7].
In recent years, immunotherapy has emerged as a promising treatment for a
wide range of cancers [5]. Unlike conventional treatments, it uses the body’s
own immune system to fight cancer. The basic role of the immune system is
to protect the body from being infected or otherwise harmed by foreign
invaders. In addition, in most cases, pre-malignant and malignant cells that
can develop in healthy people are recognised and eliminated by the normal
immune function.
It is only when cancer cells acquire enough malignant
mutations that they gain the ability to evade immune surveillance and
develop into tumours.
This means that the immune system is initially
effective enough to keep these primary events under control [8] [7].
Immunotherapy works by boosting the immune system’s natural ability to
recognise and defeat cancer cells. By targeting specific features of malignant
or immune cells, the ipatmmune response can be directed specifically against
cancer cells while sparing healthy cells.
This highlights its potential for
long-term remissions and fewer side effects compared to conventional
treatments [9].
1.1.2
Main Fields in Cancer Immunotherapy
Since its proven efficacy, immunotherapy has emerged as a promising cancer
treatment, and several different strategies have been developed in recent
years.
They work either by co-stimulating immune cells or by blocking
immune-inhibiting pathways [7]. Active immunotherapy involves inducing an
immune response in the cancer patient to attack cancer cells [5]. This may
involve vaccination, non-specific immunomodulation or targeting antigen
receptors. Passive immunotherapy is the administration of immune molecules
that enhance existing anti-tumor responses, such as monoclonal antibodies
(mAbs), T cells or cytokines [5].
The best known and most developed approach, with over 100 FDA-approved
and
marketed
therapies,
focuses on the use of mAbs [10].
These
immunoglobulins can be directed against virtually any target, and come in
different types depending on their function.
• Immune checkpoint inhibitors: These mAbs work by blocking immune
checkpoint proteins that can be upregulated by tumour cells to inhibit
the immune system.
Examples include pembroluzumab, blocking the
PD1-PDL1 interaction and ipilimumab, targeting the CTLA4-CD80/86
inhibitory pathway [11] [12].
• Monoclonal antibodies targeting cancer cells: By specifically recognising
and binding to proteins on the surface of tumour cells, these mAbs
mark them for destruction by the immune system or directly inhibit
their growth, e.g.
trastuzumab targets HER2-positive breast cancer

• Antibody-drug conjugates: They work by attaching to chemotherapy

Another promising approach is cancer vaccines. Similar to classical vaccines,
where proteins from pathological organisms are delivered to the body to train
the immune system, antigens from tumour cells can be administered to
patients to stimulate or restore the ability of the immune system to fight
cancer.
Preventive vaccines aim to prevent cancer from developing,
particularly those associated with viral infections [17].
They are based on
antigens carried by infectious agents that the immune system can easily
recognise as foreign.
Stimulation occurs with tumor antigens, peptides, or
whole cancer cells. Two such vaccines have been approved by the FDA, the
hepatitis B virus and human papillomavirus vaccine [5]. Therapeutic cancer
vaccines are designed to treat existing cancer by stimulating the immune
system to recognise and attack cancer cells. They target immune responses
specifically against tumour antigens. A broadening of the immune response
can also be achieved by releasing additional antigens when cancer cells are
killed (antigenic spread) [5]. This can lead to increased effectiveness against
the tumour.
The last major group of immunotherapies are adoptive cell therapies (ACTs).
These therapies involve the administration of self-immune cells, primarily T
cells, and exploit their ability to induce cell death in identified cancer cells [7].
After isolation from the patient’s blood, the cells are treated in vitro to increase
their number, alter their phenotype or genetically modify them with chimeric
constructs to increase their specificity. The cells are then infused back into
the patient, often preceded by lymphodepleting treatment: a chemotherapy
treatment to deplete T regulatory cells (Tregs) to improve the efficacy of ACT.
The therapies are autologous, which means they are personalised for each
patient. Since the development of the first ACT began in the early 1990s [18],
three large groups have emerged over the years:
• Tumour-infiltrating Lymphocytes (TILs): TILs are immune cells that
have
infiltrated
the
tumour
microenvironment
(TME)
and
can
specifically respond to tumour antigens [17]. The basis of the therapy is
to isolate these lymphocytes from excised tumour lesions and generate a
product
consisting
of
expanded
tumour-recognising
lymphocytes.

Expansion is achieved with specialised in vitro culture techniques,
providing adequate signaling. Efficacy of TIL depends on the patient’s
previous lymphodepletion and the administration of high doses of IL-2
to maintain T cell function, which can cause severe side effects in
patients [7]. While several studies are investigating TILs as biomarkers
for tumour prognosis, their practical use in treatment is currently
limited
to
specific
cases
of
refractory
metastatic
melanoma
and
adenocarcinoma. For this reason, it has been proposed to improve the
therapeutic potential by genetically modifying peripheral blood T cells
[7].
• T Cell Receptor (TCR) Therapy: T cell activation occurs when the
TCR recognises peptides non-covalently bound to the MHC on the
surface
of
tumour
cells
after
they
have
been
activated
by
antigen-presenting cells (APCs) [19]. TCR therapy involves the genetic
modification of the patient’s T cells ex vivo to express a specific TCR
that can recognise and target cancer cells.
Exogenous TCR-encoding
genes are introduced via retroviruses or lentiviruses [5].
Challenges
include cross-pairing with endogenous TCR chains, affinity maturation,
which increases the likelihood of cross-recognition, and MHC restriction
(MHC is downregulated in many cancers). All this has led to further
improvements in this strategy.
• Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) therapy: CAR-T cells, which are the
focus of this report, are also a type of genetically modified T cells
derived from the patient’s blood.
Unlike TCR therapy, CARs have
antibody-like specificities that can recognise antigens on the surface of

1.2
Engineering the CAR-T Cell Output Module
For developing a useful cell-to-cell delivery system, three primary components
are required: input modules, control logic and output modules [2]. In CAR-T
cell design, the focus is on the first two, including a variety of transcriptional
regulatory circuits and the different generations of highly specific CARs [29].
The output modules are less developed. They rely mainly on passive, non-
specific secretion activated by a combination of sensory input modules [2].
In addition, the disadvantage of transcriptionally activated secretion is that
there is a time delay between activation and actuation. Therefore, for many
applications, an output module is desirable in which the deliverable payload
is pre-stored in the cell for rapid actuation and cell-specific delivery.
The development of such a system is the focus of this project.
1.2.1
Granule-Mediated Cytotoxicity
In order to harness the properties of T cells to engineer the CAR-T cell
output module, it is important to understand the natural mechanisms of T
cell cytotoxicity.
Apoptosis induced by the granzyme-perforin pathway is one of the major
pathways used by CTLs to eliminate virus-infected or transformed cells [47].
Its
main
components,
granzyme
and
perforin,
are
both
stored
in
membrane-bound secretory lysosomes or cytotoxic granules in the cytosol of
CTLs. Upon recognition of the target cell by the effector cell, the CTL forms
a tight apposition with the target cell, forming the immunological synapse.
Surface receptor signalling results in the trafficking of cytotoxic secretory
granules to the immunological synapse [48].
This is due to a pronounced
increase in cytosolic Ca2+ concentration,
leading to migration of the
cytotoxic granules along the polarised microtubule-organising centre, followed
by fusion with the presynaptic membrane and endocytic release of their cargo
into the synapse between the two cells.
Perforin and granzymes act

Perforin is a pore-forming protein that forms ring-shaped lesions that are
able to mediate ion flux and the uptake of larger molecules, such as
granzymes [44]. Studies in gene-disrupted mice indicate that perforin is vital
for cytotoxic effector function [47]. Once released across the synaptic cleft,
perforin inserts in the target cell membrane and oligomerizes to form
transient pores, allowing GzB and other granule proteins to enter the cytosol
of target cells.

Ca2+, perforin is active at the neutral pH of the immunological synapse, but
is inactive under acidic conditions (pH<6).

secretory granule (pH≈5) is a safe storage compartment for perforin, which
only becomes active during secretion [48]. The rapid osmotic flux through

Lysosomes rely on a large arsenal of acid hydrolases, transmembrane proteins

The majority of soluble lysosomal hydrolases use the mannose 6-phosphate

remain in the lumen during endosomal maturation and eventual fusion with
lysosomes. In addition, lysosomal proteins can undergo proteolytic processing
in acidic pH environments, which is often associated with their conversion to
active forms [55].

granule, which is another proposed mechanism for keeping them inactive
during storage [49].

Hypothesis and Objectives
The aim of this thesis project is to lay the foundations for the development of
next-generation CAR-T cells, enabling the controlled delivery of specific
recombinant effector proteins to the target cell. The delivery is desired to be
rapidly initiated by the interaction of the CAR with its target and to come
from a pre-positioned source, making it a tightly coupled and short process.
The unique features of the granzyme-perforin pathway, including the storage
of
granzymes
in
the
cytotoxic
granules
prior
to
secretion
and
the
pore-forming mechanism of perforin, fulfil these criteria.
Based on this, we hypothesise that by using the granzyme-perforin pathway,
we can deliver specific proteins to the target in a cell-specific manner (Figure
2.1).
The main objectives are:
• Development of a pH-sensitive reporter system to localise the protein of
interest within the endosomal pathway.
• Initial testing of short DNA sequences (signal peptide and sorting motif)
as a transporter system mediating entry into the cytotoxic granules.
• Validating the system‘s functionality by demonstrating the protein‘s
presence initially in the granules and subsequently in the target cell
(delivery assay).
Potential applications of such CAR-T/protein delivery system include: (i)
overcoming resistance to cytolytic killing in tumour cells by delivering killing
factors and alternative cytotoxic proteins; (ii) controlling and modifying the
death pathway, e.g. inducing a more pro-inflammatory CAR-T cell response
such as paraptosis rather than apoptosis; and (iii) delivering peptides as a
vaccine strategy to enhance naturally occurring anti-cancer immune responses
and mediate epitope spreading.

4.1.2
Optimization of the Primary T Cell Workflow for the Use of
the Tandem MCherry-EGFP Reporter
Since the ultimate goal is to use the reporter system in primary T cells to
generate CAR-T cells for cancer immunotherapy, it is crucial to optimise the
tandem reporter for use in primary T cells and to establish a workflow for
subsequent experiments.
It is suggested that the timing of transduction after T cell activation may
influence transduction efficiency and may help to obtain a more defined
population for the construct fused to GzB (construct #2, positive control).
This could be due to the degree of up-regulation of specific receptors after
activation that support efficient transduction.
To evaluate this, three different time points (24 h, 48 h and 72 h) of
transduction after T cell activation were tested. Two different T cell donors
were used and transduction was performed in a titration setup.
To assess the activation status of the T cells, CD25 and CD69, which are
early markers of T cell activation, were stained on the day of transduction at
the three different time points after activation.
Similarly, the low-density
lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) was stained to assess its expression.
LDLR
functions as a primary entry receptor for the vesicular stomatitis virus
envelope G glycoprotein used to produce lentivirus [65]. In particular, it has
been shown that T cell activation significantly increases LDLR expression,
which promotes lentiviral recognition on the T cell surface and influences
transduction efficiency [66] [67] [68].
Fluorochromes with distinct emission
spectra that do not significantly overlap were selected for the different surface
stains (Table 3.5).
In addition, compensation was performed using beads.
Unstained T cells and non-activated T cells from both donors were used as
negative controls.
The gating strategy is shown in the Appendix (Figure
A.5).

Evaluation of Signal Peptide and Sorting Motif
Sequences as Transporter
In order to develop a CAR-T/protein delivery system, the goal is to identify
a short sequence that allows protein delivery to the cytotoxic granules in a
manner similar to the positive control (construct #2, tandem + GzB). The
identification of short sequences (e.g. by a sorting motif screen) has several
advantages: (i) more flexibility on the transfer plasmid, i.e. more space for
the effector protein of interest and for other CAR elements (size limit for
lentiviral vectors), (ii) incorporation into the protein sequence without
significantly altering the structure or function of the protein, which can be
caused by fusion to a whole protein such as GzB, (iii) more control over the
cell system, as there is no additional up-regulation of GzB in the target cell,
which can falsify the effect of the actual protein of interest, and (iv) generally
gain
valuable
insight
into
cellular
sorting
mechanisms
to
cytotoxic
granules/lysosomes for research purposes.

The principle within this project is that the final construct will contain a
signal peptide, potentially guiding the protein of interest to the ER, and a

5
Discussion
When discussing the results, it is important to keep in mind the intention of
the thesis project.
The overall aim was to lay the foundation for the development of a
cell-to-cell delivery module based on CAR T cells, i.e. the engineering of the
CAR T cell output module. The delivery of the payload should be rapidly
initiated by the CAR, resulting in the transfer of the payload from the
effector cell to a target cell in a cell-specific manner. Ideally, delivery should
be from a pre-positioned source so that the time between target recognition
and completion of payload delivery is tightly coupled and short.
The
granzyme-perforin pathway, as a natural and unique cell-to-cell delivery
system of cytotoxic lymphocytes,
offers these properties,
with effector
proteins stored in cytotoxic granules prior to release.
The CAR would
provide specific and flexible target antigen recognition, which would naturally
activate a signalling cascade that initiates the release of the pre-positioned
molecules for cell-specific delivery, utilising natural T cell properties.
The potential applications of such a system are broad.
They include
overcoming
cancer
escape
strategies
by
delivering
killing
factors
and
recombinant cytotoxic proteins, modifying and controlling the death pathway
initiated
by
CAR-T
cells,
delivering
additional
antigens
to
enhance
anti-cancer immune responses, or increasing the local presence of specific
cytokines. The potential risk of incorporated cytotoxic proteins damaging T
cells is limited by using the natural T cell pathway for delivery and storage of
cytotoxic proteins, which is a closed system, but still needs to be considered.
Another limitation of the system may be the stability of the proteins of
interest at low pH, as they will be stored in the acidic environment of the
cytotoxic granules prior to release.
This will need to be evaluated when
selecting effector proteins for testing.
Here we present tools for the development of such a CAR-T/protein delivery
system, the key developments being (i) a pH-sensitive reporter system to assess
location within the endosomal pathway, (ii) a transporter system to enable
delivery to the cytotoxic granules and subsequently to the target cell, which
requires further work, and (iii) a delivery assay to assess successful delivery to
the target cells.
5.1
Development of a PH-sensitive Reporter System
The tandem mCherry-EGFP reporter with pH-sensitive EGFP has been
successfully established to determine whether it is located in the cytotoxic
granules (acidic pH) or not (neutral pH). This means that a protein of
interest or another short DNA sequence of interest can be attached to the
reporter and the location of the protein can be detected.

In an initial test in Jurkat cells and activated primary T cells,
the
functionality of the reporter was demonstrated using a positive and negative
control (section 4.1.1).
Due to the GzB fusion of the positive control, the
reporter
should

location in the cell with neutral pH, such as the cytosol, which was seen with
a population shift equally to EGFP and mCherry (double positive), meaning
that both fluorescent proteins were expressed at similar levels.
In addition, successful delivery of the positive control first to the granules
and then to the target cells (shown with the delivery assay; Section 4.3)
demonstrates the stability of the reporter protein at acidic pH, which is
critical for its use.
For the positive control, the generally low fluorescence signal seen in all

especially
compared

should
be
discussed.
One reason for this could be a generally lower viral strength, related to the
increased size of the expression cassette due to the added GzB sequence,
which may lead to reduced viral transduction efficiency. It is well known that
lentiviral vector titres decrease with increasing genome size, meaning that the
size of the delivered genetic material can influence the efficiency of viral
packaging,
production and transduction [37] [69].
However,
with an
expression cassette of 6,512 kb (construct #2, positive control) compared to
5,735 kb (construct #1, negative control), it is still within the reported
lentiviral packaging limit of 9-10 kb and the size difference between the two
controls is in fact rather small. In addition, the transduction efficiency of all
constructs should be comparable, as data from a range of 5 - 20 %
mCherry-positive cells were selected from the viral dilutions. The selection of
this lower range should also reduce the risk of multi-copy integration, which
can result from multiple lentiviral particles infecting the same host cell or
from a single viral particle integrating at multiple genomic loci, potentially
increasing transgene expression compared to single insertions.
Using low
multiplicities of infection (MOIs), or in this case, selecting data with lower
transduction efficiencies, should reduce the likelihood of multiple vector
integrations per cell.
To further ensure single copy integration, a more
targeted cell engineering tool such as CRISPR-Cas9 could be incorporated
into the vector system to mediate site-specific integration.
Another reason for the lower fluorescence signal could be the specific
compartment (cytotoxic granules) in which the reporter protein accumulates,
potentially leading to less signal being detected by the flow cytometer. This
is supported by the fact that other constructs, such as constructs #3, #5 and
#6 (Section 4.2.2), also resulted in reduced signal when targeted to a specific
compartment (ER or cytotoxic granules) compared to the negative control.
These constructs contain only short signal peptide and/or sorting motif
sequences (< 200 bp) that do not encode an additional protein.

Generation CAR-T Cell Development in Cancer Immunotherapy

==PAGE:76==
However, when comparing the MFI of these constructs with each other (not
with the negative control), the expression strength of the constructs without
GzB is still higher. This indicates that the fusion of GzB with the reporter
has an effect, possibly due to lower translation efficiency as the protein size
increases with GzB fusion. GzB adds approximately 28 kDa (236 aa) to the
55 kDa (496 aa) tandem reporter.
Potentially a combination of factors, including the size of the expression
cassette, the size of the protein and the target compartment, is responsible
for the difference and argues for considering positive and negative constructs
separately when looking at the MFI. For this reason, a split y-axis is used in
the bar charts.
The GzB insertion may also be a reason why the functionality of the mKeima
positive control could not be demonstrated. The viral load might have been
too low to result in a detectable signal, even though the virus production was
repeated several times.
The spread to EGFP seen in the positive control must also be considered
when evaluating the functionality of the mCherry-EGFP tandem reporter
(Appendix Figure A.6 and A.7). It is consistent for the three types of T cells
tested (primary T cells:
before optimisation) as well as for the other
constructs targeting a specific compartment.
It is suggested that the spread to EGFP is related to high levels of
expression, e.g. due to multiple integrations, as it increases with higher virus
levels (titrations).
Possible reasons could be that some of the reporter
protein escapes the endosomal pathway when protein levels are too high, that
some protein remains in other compartments (such as the Golgi or ER), or
that there is some spectral overlap between mCherry and EGFP at high
expression levels. In the latter case, compensation has been attempted, but
to no avail.
Nevertheless, the problem can be mitigated to some extent by selecting the

It decreased with increasing time after transduction. This can be explained
by upregulated receptors mediating lentiviral transduction depending on the

due to a staining error, the titration data clearly showed a correlation

reporter is actually in the ER (for Jurkat and primary T cells) as the pH

stable. An argument in favour of storage in a different compartment is that
no delivery to the target cell was demonstrated with construct #3, although
the reporter indicated location in the cytotoxic granules (in primary T cells
and Jurkat cells).
In a study of the structure and activity of EGFP, Dos Santos et al. report a
reversible loss of fluorescence due to the protonated and deprotonated states

suggests that some loss of EGFP may already occur in the compartments
upstream of the cytotoxic granules.

5.2
Development of a Transporter System
The goal of identifying a suitable transporter system in the form of short
DNA sequences to replace fusion to GzB has not yet been achieved.
However, important steps have been taken to make such a search possible.
Firstly, the reasons why GzB is not optimal as a transporter should be
highlighted.
Due to the aforementioned size limitation of the expression
cassette when using lentiviral vectors, it is desirable to have enough space on
the vector to allow flexibility for the inclusion of protein sequences of interest
and to accommodate any required CAR elements.
Fusion of the reporter
protein with GzB could also lead to changes in the structure or function of
the proteins involved. However, as both GzB and the reporter protein appear
to function well, this cannot be inferred from the results. On the other hand,
GzB fusion may be responsible for the low expression levels seen, and the
influence of GzB on the function of other proteins of interest is unclear. The
additional up-regulation of GzB would also limit the ability to study the
effect of other effector peoteins and could cause problems in in vivo studies.
Furthermore, it is of research interest to study the cellular functions of signal
peptides and sorting motifs and to identify novel motifs that mediate sorting
to cytotoxic granules in T cells. Therefore, a large-scale sorting motif screen
(including up to 10,000 or more sequences) was planned from the outset.
For this purpose,
a cloning workflow has been successfully developed,
implementing various optimisations to overcome initial cloning problems.
The development was carried out to allow upscaling for a future sorting motif
screen, e.g. by increasing cloning efficiency through the use of electroporation
and simplifying the process by incorporating restriction sites for faster
linearisation of the backbone. The Hifi cloning strategy chosen will allow the
ordering of inserts with appropriate overhangs to generate a large sorting
motif library for the screen. The final cloning strategy was successfully tested
with a generated test construct mimicking the inserts planned for the library.
Larger scale testing with more inserts would be the next step.

As mentioned above, the results of testing construct #3 (tandem + signal
peptide) left room for discussion, as this construct was originally included as
a negative control, but showed evidence of delivering the reporter protein to
the cytotoxic granules in Jurkat cells and activated primary T cells.
The
consensus from the literature is that (as described in section 1.2.2) a signal
peptide alone is not sufficient to mediate delivery to the lysosome [55] [49]
[54] [72] [73]. Its function is to direct newly synthesised polypeptides to the
ER for translocation across the ER membrane. After protein synthesis and
processing in the ER, it is typically cleaved. To direct protein localisation to
a specific intracellular compartment, such as the lysosome, a sorting motif is
required. Kapp et al. review that some signal peptides have been shown to
have post-targeting functions after insertion into the ER membrane and
cleavage from their pre-protein, either as a membrane-spanning peptide, as a
peptide released from the membrane, or as a signal peptide fragment [73].
However, most of the current examples are of viral proteins and none of the
post-targeting functions involve further targeting of proteins to another
compartment. The researchers themselves conclude that for cellular proteins,
it is likely that most signal peptides contain only the ER targeting
information and are then rapidly degraded.
No example or suggestion of
signal peptides that also have a sorting function to the lysosome/cytotoxic
granules or any other cellular compartment could be found in the literature.
If the results with the signal peptide are indeed confirmed, it will be a new
insight into the function of the GzB signal peptide. However, further research
is needed. To assess whether this is specific to the GzB signal peptide, other
signal peptides from proteins that target different compartments, such as the
Golgi, ER and membrane as their final destination, and signal peptides from
other proteins that target the lysosome and cytotoxic granules, should be
tested.
This will also further validate the functionality of the tandem
reporter or reveal any non-specificities. If other signal peptides do not show
such an effect in primary T cells, one of them could be used for a sorting
motif screen in primary T cells. A larger screen for signal peptides may also
be of interest to see if there is a pattern in the sequences that could mediate
lysosomal sorting in T cells.
An interesting hypothesis in a slightly different direction is that different
types of granules may be targeted by the signal peptide compared to the
common M6P tag (used by GzB). Chang et al.
have recently identified
distinct cytotoxic granules [74]. They show two classes of fusion-competent
granules, single-core granules (SCG) and multi-core granules (MCG), which
differ in diameter, morphology and protein composition. It is proposed that
CTLs use SCG fusion to immediately fill the synaptic cleft with active
cytotoxic proteins, and parallel MCG fusion to deliver latent SMAPs for
delayed killing of refractory targets.
However, the present results provide
little support for this hypothesis, particularly due to the fact that no delivery
to the target cell was observed in the delivery assay for the signal peptide
construct.

What is also unclear, and requires further discussion beyond the question of
reporter specificity, is why different patterns could be seen for activated
primary T cells and Jurkat cells compared to Sup-T1 cells, even though all
three are T cell types. It is well known that immortalised T cell lines derived
from T cell lymphomas, such as Jurkat and Sup-T1 cells, do not have the
same relevance as donor-derived primary T cells in reflecting in vivo T cell
behaviour. Differences such as their biology, activation status, differentiation
status and genetic composition (e.g. due to mutations) of the cell types could
lead to divergent patterns of protein sorting.
Jurkat cells are a very well
known T cell line, especially for modelling T cell signalling, whereas Sup-T1
cells are less well studied. While a genome-wide survey of mutations has been
performed in the Jurkat cell line, revealing mutations in many key molecules
involved in the TCR signalling pathway [75], such a study is lacking for
Sup-T1 cells. Further research is needed to determine the impact of genetic
differences in these cell types. Interestingly, however, it is not Sup-T1 cells
but primary T cells and Jurkat cells that show the unexpected effect of the
signal peptide alone. Further evaluation of this will be crucial in establishing
a functional transporter system for the proposed CAR-T/protein delivery
system.
Nevertheless, based on the expected patterns seen in Sup-T1 cells, they could
be used for the intended sorting motif screen. The signal peptide itself did
not appear to deliver the reporter to the cytotoxic granules, but the
combination with LAMP-2 showed an effect. In primary T cells and Jurkat
cells, the effect of the signal peptide did not allow conclusions to be drawn
about the functionality of LIMP-2 and LAMP-2.
In general, it was not expected that all lysosomal sorting motifs identified in
the literature would work, e.g. LIMP-2 showed no effect in Sup-T1 cells, but
a sorting motif screen would provide valuable insights into specific patterns
and functions through bioinformatic analysis. However, screening in Sup-T1
cells would leave the question of physiological relevance unanswered and
would require further functional testing, e.g.
using the developed delivery
assay.

Development of a Delivery Assay
To evaluate the ability to deliver the payload to the target,
a flow

anti-CD19 (CAR) Jurkat cells was successfully developed (Section 4.3).
The GzB fusion positive control was further demonstrated to function as
intended. GzB not only delivered the tandem mCherry-EGFP reporter to the
cytotoxic granules, but also to the target cells, as indicated by a population
shift of the wild-type Nalm-6 cells to mCherry after overnight incubation
with the transduced effector cells. This demonstrates the principle of fusing a
natural T cell effector protein to a protein of interest, thereby using the
natural T cell pathway to target the cytotoxic granules for storage and
secretion following CAR/target interaction.

However, for effector cells transduced with the construct containing the
signal peptide #3 (as well as signal peptide and sorting motifs #5 & #6),
protein delivery to the target cell could not be demonstrated as no clear shift
was seen. It is concluded that simple implementation of the signal peptide as
a transporter for the CAR-T/protein delivery system is not feasible.
Assuming that the tandem reporter is indeed stored in the cytotoxic granules
by the signal peptide, the question arises as to why it is not transported to
the target cells in the same way as when fused to GzB. It is suggested that
the 3D structure of GzB may have an influence on successful secretion and
delivery to the target cell.
Research into the exact mechanism of
perforin-mediated entry of the granzymes into the target cell is ongoing and
there is no consensus in the literature. At present, the two models described
in section 1.2.1 are direct diffusion of soluble GzB through perforin pores in
the target cell membrane and perforin-induced endosomal uptake [44] [76]. It
has also been suggested that perforin and granzymes can be secreted in
SMAPs [53].
Certain regions of the GzB protein may be involved in
interactions with perforin or other components of the cytotoxic granules. The
conformational changes that GzB undergoes on activation may also play a
role. However, further research is needed to elucidate the precise molecular
mechanisms underlying GzB entry into the target cell.
If short sequences for sorting are not sufficient for subsequent delivery to the
target, new challenges arise in the development of a CAR-T/protein delivery
system. Additional sequences may be required to mediate secretion and/or
entry, and whether the 3D structure of the protein is necessary will need to
be evaluated.
Another possibility is that the reporter protein in combination with the
signal peptide (with or without a sorting motif) is trapped in an endosomal
compartment,
preventing its release.
LIMP-2 and LAMP-2 are both
lysosomal membrane proteins, so their sorting motif could cause the reporter
protein to get stuck in the cytotoxic granules. Something similar could also
be the case for the signal peptide.
To evaluate this, more sorting motifs
should be tested, including those of proteins reported to be located in the
lumen of lysosomes/cytotoxic granules.

also be discussed. They have been selected to best mimic the interaction of

6
Conclusion and Outlook
In conclusion, this thesis project has laid the foundation for the development
of a cell-to-cell delivery module based on CAR-T cells.
The tandem mCherry-EGFP reporter can be used as a pH sensitive reporter
system to assess the location within the granzyme-perforin pathway. Upon
reaching the cytotoxic granules (acidic pH) as a target for payload storage, a
population shift to mCherry due to pH-sensitive EGFP can be detected by
flow cytometry.
Fusion of GzB with the reporter protein can serve as a
positive control, mediating protein delivery to the target.
In addition, the
search for a relevant transporter system has been initiated to enable delivery
to the cytotoxic granules and subsequently to the target cell. The GzB signal
peptide tested, with and without the combination of two different sorting
motifs (from the lysosomal membrane proteins LIMP-2 and LAMP-2),
showed targeting to the cytotoxic granules in Jurkat cells and primary T
cells, but not to the target cell.
A delivery assay to evaluate target cell
delivery was successfully set up involving overnight incubation of pre-stained
target cells (Nalm-6, CD19+) with effector cells (anti-CD19 CAR Jurkat
cells) previously transduced with the different constructs. Using the reporter
protein, a mCherry shift in the target cells can be detected by flow
cytometry, indicating successful delivery.
As a next step, a large-scale sorting motif screen can be performed to identify
short sequences that may function as a transporter system similar to the
GzB construct (positive control). Bioinformatic tools can be used to identify
relevant sequences from lysosomal and cytotoxic granule proteins in the
lumen and membrane. A library can then be generated and screened in a
scaled-up pooled experiment using the methods developed here. Positive hits
can be sequenced for identification.
Recommendations from this project
include: (i) performing the screen in Sup-T1 cells as the expected patterns
were seen from the signal peptide and sorting motif, (ii) using a low MOI for
transduction to avoid integration of multiple copies into the cell via lentiviral
vectors, (iii) using the developed cloning workflow including transformation
by electroporation and backbone linearisation by restriction enzymes, (iv)
verifying the functionality of the positive hits using the optimised delivery
assay, and (v) validating the top candidates in activated primary T cells
using the established T cell workflow.
Furthermore, the successful development of the CAR-T/protein delivery
system will enable the exploration of its many applications for rapid and
highly
specific
delivery
of
proteins
of
interest,
paving
the
way
for
next-generation CAR-T cell therapy with increased efficiency.
