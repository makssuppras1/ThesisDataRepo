Summary
Linear cryptanalysis is an important tool for analysing the strength of symmetric
cryptosystems. The method exploits linear approximations of a cipher which have
high correlation between input and output values.
We consider the case of using
multiple linear approximations simultaneously to create cryptographic distinguishers
and key recovery attacks for block ciphers. We develop a new model, multivariate
linear cryptanalysis, that uses multivariate statistics to analyse the joint distribution
of the approximations. We compare the multivariate model to the current models:
multiple linear cryptanalysis and multidimensional linear cryptanalysis.
We show
that these two models can be considered special cases of the new model. We consider
the problem of predicting the mean and variance of the test statistic used when
creating a distinguisher. We find that using the multivariate model improves our
ability to predict these parameters. The improved estimate potentially lowers the
strength of some linear attacks. For ChoвҖҷs attack on present, we obtain a slightly
larger computational complexity using the multivariate model, compared to ChoвҖҷs
original estimate.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Statistical distinguishers play an important role in modern cryptanalysis of symmet-
ric cryptosystems. One way to build such a distinguisher is using linear cryptanalysis.
Introduced by Matsui in 1993, achieving the first known-plaintext break of the DES
[27], linear cryptanalysis has been heavily developed since then. Linear cryptanal-
ysis uses a linear approximation of a cipher with high statistical bias. This bias is
measured using the correlation of the approximation. The perhaps most important
extension of traditional linear cryptanalysis is the simultaneous use of many linear
approximations. Two prominent models for using multiple approximations have been
developed: multiple linear cryptanalysis and multidimensional linear cryptanalysis.
1.1
Previous Work
Multiple linear cryptanalysis was considered in [24] by Kaliski and Robshaw, and in
[5] by Biryukov et al. This model builds on the assumption that the linear approxima-
tions are statistically independent. However, this assumption was shown by Murphy
to generally be invalid in [29]. To remedy this, multidimensional linear cryptanalysis
was developed by Nyberg, Hermelin, and Cho in [20, 19, 21]. In this model, the ap-
proximations must form a subspace of Fn
2. In [13], Cho used multidimensional linear
cryptanalysis to mount a key recovery attack on 26 rounds of the cipher present.
The strength of a multidimensional attack can be measured by the capacity of
the linear approximations.
Typically, it is not possible to obtain the exact value
of the capacity, and thus the estimated strength of an attack deviates from the ac-
tual strength. To solve this problem, signal/noise decomposition was proposed by
Bogdanov and Tischhauser in [10].
Recently, several links between linear cryptanalysis and other statistical attacks,
such as (truncated/impossible) differential cryptanalysis, integral attacks, and statis-
tical saturation attacks, have been discovered [11, 8, 7, 37]. Thus, advances in linear
cryptanalysis could lead to advances in other areas of statistical cryptanalysis.
1.2
Our Contributions
We present a new framework, multivariate linear cryptanalysis, in which the corre-
lations of many linear approximations are modelled as a multivariate normally dis-

tributed random variable. In this way, the dependence structure of the approxima-
tions is directly considered, as opposed to the approaches used in previous models.
Additionally, the structure of the linear approximations is not restricted, and no
assumptions about the individual distributions of the correlations are made. The
signal/noise model is extensively used to avoid the assumption of knowing the exact
correlation distributions.
We show that both the multiple and the multidimensional linear models can be
viewed as special cases of the multivariate model. Moreover, we propose a test statistic
given as a quadratic form in the multivariate distribution, which can be used to create
a statistical distinguisher. We show that this test statistic is equivalent to the one used
in the multidimensional model. We derive expressions for the two main parameters
of this statistic: the mean and the variance.
Through experimentation on SmallPresent [26], we show that the multivariate
model gives a better prediction of the main parameters of the test statistic compared
to the multidimensional model given in [6]. In particular, when the capacity is very
low, the multivariate models gives a much more reasonable estimate. The variance
estimate is especially improved.
In spite of this, we also show that the variance
prediction made by the multivariate model is still a large underestimate of the true
variance, which can lead to a large overestimate of the power of the attack. Similar
results were shown in [6], using the multidimensional model.
Finally, we investigate ChoвҖҷs attack on present [13]. Through direct trail enu-
meration, we estimate the capacity of the approximations used, and arrive at a result
close to ChoвҖҷs original estimate. We compare this result to the capacity estimate
obtained by Huang et al. in [22]. Using the multivariate model, we obtain a slightly
higher computational complexity than that given by Cho. However, the attack on
26 rounds of present is still valid.
This contradicts the conclusions reached by
Blondeau and Nyberg in [6].

Structure

setting we are considering, gives a high level explanation of statistical distinguishers,
and shows how these can be used to create key recovery attacks. Chapter 4 describes
the block cipher present.
Chapter 5 explains the theory of linear cryptanalysis,
presents the signal/noise model, and shows the most recent results on multidimen-

Finally, in Chapter 6 we present the new concept of

CHAPTER 3
Cryptographic Setting
We consider the setting of symmetric cryptography: two parties (sender and receiver)
wish to securely exchange information over an insecure channel in the presence of an
adversary. This adversary can observe all information on the channel. It is assumed
that the communicating parties have already exchanged some small piece of informa-
tion, called the key, in a secure manner. The goal is to use this pre-shared and secret
information to exchange messages over the channel, without the adversary being able
to understand these messages.
In order to do this, the sender encrypts the message, using the same key and an
encryption algorithm, before sending it to the receiver. The receiver then decrypts the
received message using the key and a decryption algorithm, recovering the original
message. The original message is called the plaintext, while the encrypted message
is called the ciphertext. The encryption algorithm together with the decryption algo-
rithm is called a cipher.
Importantly, it is assumed that the adversary knows exactly how the cipher works.
The only thing unknown to the adversary is the secret key. This is known as Kerck-
hoffsвҖҷs principle.

3.2
Adversaries and Attacks
In cryptanalysis, the cryptanalyst plays the role of the adversary in order to asses the
strength of a cipher. We will often refer to the cipher under investigation as the target
cipher. Two main types of attacks that an adversary can perform are considered.
вҖў Key recovery: The adversary learns the secret key, and can therefore decrypt
any messages exchanged on the channel encrypted using this key.
вҖў Distinguishing: The adversary is able to distinguish the target cipher from
an ideal cipher.
Intuitively, a key recovery attack is a much stronger form of attack than a distin-
guisher, but as we shall see later, a distinguisher can usually be used to built a key
recovery attack. Note that any block cipher can trivially be broken by exhaustively
decrypting with every key in K. We call this a brute force attack or exhaustive search.
However, the size of Оә is usually chosen such that this approach is infeasible.
We also consider different types of adversaries with varying strength, e.g. how
much information the adversary has access to or can control during an attack. We
list some of the usual attack types here, from weakest to strongest.
вҖў Chiphertext-only attack: The adversary can only observe the ciphertexts as
they are transmitted on the channel.
вҖў Known-plaintext attack: The attacker knows the corresponding plaintext of
each transmitted ciphertext. We further subdivide this attack type into random
know-plaintext (RKP) and distinct know-plaintext (DKP) attacks. In an RKP
attack, the plaintexts are drawn at random from P with substitution. In a DKP

and show how such a distinguisher can be used to develop a key recovery attack.
3.3
Distinguishing as Hypothesis Testing
The goal of a distinguishing attack is to make an algorithm that, upon receiving some
amount of data from a cipher, determines whether the cipher used for encryption was
an ideal cipher or not. Determined by the power of the adversary, the data given as
input to the distinguisher will be some number of ciphertexts or plaintext-ciphertext
pairs. The idea is presented in Algorithm 1. Of course, it is in no way clear or trivial
how to perform the assessment in line 1 of this algorithm.
In statistical cryptanalysis, the cryptanalyst exploits the fact that some statistical
property of the targeted cipher does not behave as it would for an ideal cipher. By
analysing the target cipher, the cryptanalyst attempts to predict the statistical be-
haviour of some value T , which is derived from the available data. We call this value
the test statistic. The test statistic usually depends on the value of the encryption
key. Assuming that the encryption key is chosen at random, T is then a random
variable. If the distribution of T is significantly different for an ideal cipher and the
target cipher, we will be able to distinguish the two.

Linear Cryptanalysis
In Chapter 3 we introduced the idea of statistical distinguishers and key recovery
attacks made from these. One way of building a distinguisher is using linear approx-
imations to analyse the behaviour of the target cipher. This area of cryptanalysis is
called linear cryptanalysis.
Linear cryptanalysis was first introduced by Matsui in 1993 [27]. The method
was used to obtain the first known-plaintext break of the DES. The attack was built
on what Matsui called вҖқAlgorithm 2вҖқ вҖ“ the idea of which closely resembles our Algo-
rithm 3. The central concepts of linear cryptanalysis are presented in this chapter.
The notation follows that of such works as [16, 10, 17], with some slight deviations.
In [27], Matsui used only a single linear approximation. Shortly after, Matsui
published an improved attack that used two approximations simultaneously [28]. The
idea of using multiple linear approximations to obtain better distinguishers has been
expanded considerably since then. We present the concepts of multiple linear crypt-
analysis [24, 5, 29] and multidimensional linear cryptanalysis [4, 20, 19, 21] in Sec-
tion 5.7. In an attempt to improve these methods, we introduce multivariate linear
cryptanalysis in Chapter 6.

5.4
Signal/Noise Decomposition
While Corollary 1 seems like an easy way to determine the correlation of a linear
approximation, the number of trails in a linear hull with non-zero correlation contri-
bution can be extremely high. If this is the case, we will not be able to determine
the correlation contribution of all trails, and thus we cannot calculate the sum in
Corollary 1.
Traditionally, two approaches to this problem have been used. One approach is
to assume that we somehow have knowledge of the true distribution of CОұ,ОІ, see for
example [6, 22]. The other approach is to assume that the main contribution to the
linear hull comes from a small number of dominant trails. These trails usually have
a high correlation value.
This approach was used in e.g.
[32].
For some further
discussion of this problem, see [25].

signal/noise model. Say we have enumerated t signal trails. The signal part of the

5.4.1
Experimental Verification
We have verified the validity of the signal/noise model on nine rounds of SmallPre-
sent-[8]. Similar experiments were performed in [10], although only on SmallPre-
sent-[5], with few enumerated trails, and over few rounds. Moreover, the experiments
measured the data complexity of an attack, whereas we directly inspect the variance
of approximations.
We considered two sets of approximations. The first set was all approximations
of the type

where y takes on any hexadecimal value. We enumerated part of the hull using the
enumeration algorithm in Appendix A with HW = 32 and B = 10. The maximum
number of trails found was 173,201, while the average number of trails found was
116,137 (ignoring zero correlation approximations).
The second set considered was all approximations of the type
(Оұ, ОІ) = (0x00y00000, 0x000000y0),

where y takes on any hexadecimal value. We enumerated part of the hull using the

For each approximation, we first measured the exact correlation value for 1000
random master keys. The signal part of the correlation was then computed for 5000
random master keys, using the enumerated part of the hull. Finally, the variances of

seen in Figure 5.1. For both sets, the approximations have been ordered according
to their variances.
For the first set of approximations, all variance values are relatively high. Fur-
thermore, we see that the signal part of the variance accounts for quite a large part
of the actual variance for all approximations. More importantly, when adding the
noise component, the result never exceeds the actual variance. For the second set of
approximations, the opposite situation occurs; All variance values are quite low, and
the signal part of the variance does not account for a significant part of the actual
variance. When adding the noise component, we overestimate the actual variance for
a few approximations, namely those with extremely low variance values. However,
the overestimate is not significant. For both sets of approximations, the signal/noise
model seems to be a good approximation to the actual variance values. This is espe-
cially the case for low variance approximations, in which the noise part contributes
significantly, and we achieve a large improvement over considering the signal part
alone.
While these experiments seem to verify the validity of the signal/noise model
for present, we emphasize that it is important to justify the use of the model for
different types of ciphers.

5.7
Multidimensional Linear Cryptanalysis
MatsuiвҖҷs original attack on the DES used only a single linear approximation [27].
The use of multiple linear approximations to improve the strength of the resulting
distinguisher was considered in [24, 5]. Both work under the assumption of statistical
independence between the correlation values of the linear approximations. In [29],
a more rigorous framework for using multiple linear approximations was developed,
and this assumption was shown to generally be invalid. Nonetheless, we shall call
the method of using many linear approximations, picked only on the basis of their
correlation values and assuming statistical independence, multiple linear cryptanalysis.
As multiple linear cryptanalysis has its obvious flaws, most research in recent times
has been done using a different model. However, we shall return to multiple linear
cryptanalysis in Chapter 6, as a special case of the new model we develop there.
In order to fix the problems with multiple linear cryptanalysis, multidimensional
linear cryptanalysis was developed. Multidimensional linear cryptanalysis builds on
the work presented in [4], while much of the main theory was developed by Nyberg,
Hermelin, and Cho in e.g. [20, 19, 21]. In the following, we present the theory of
multidimensional linear cryptanalysis as it is given in these and other works. As with
the multiple model, we shall see that the multidimensional model is a special case of
the model we develop in Chapter 6.

CHAPTER 6
Multivariate Linear
Cryptanalysis
Chapter 5 presented the concept of linear cryptanalysis. We briefly mentioned the
multiple linear model, in which it is assumed that the correlations of multiple linear
approximations are statistically independent, but no restrictions are placed on the
structure of these approximations. The assumption of independence is typically not
true, as shown in [29]. In Section 5.7 we presented the multidimensional model, in
which the approximations make up a subspace of Fn
2 in an effort to take the depen-
dence into account. Theorem 12, derived in [6], was used to predict the mean and
variance of the test statistic TH1, but uses the assumption that the multidimensional
probabilities are all identical, and that we know the exact expected capacity. Both
the experiments in [6] and our experiments show, however, that Theorem 12 fails to
accurately predict the variance of TH1. This can lead to a large overestimate of a
multidimensional attackвҖҷs strength.
In this chapter, we present a new model for using many linear approximations
simultaneously. Traditionally, all analysis of linear cryptanalysis has been done in
the setting of univariate statistics. We propose to move into the setting of multi-
variate statistics to make a more flexible and accurate model. Multivariate linear
cryptanalysis, or the multivariate linear model, as we present it in this chapter, aims
to have the benefits of both the multiple and the multidimensional model, without
the limitations.
In particular, the multivariate model does not assume statistical
independence of the correlation values, does not restrict the parameters of the corre-
lation distributions, and does not require that the approximations have a particular
structure. Furthermore, we shall see that the multivariate model actually contains
both the multiple and multidimensional models as special cases. Finally, we make
use of the signal/noise model to account for the problem of not knowing the exact
distributions of the correlations.

The multivariate linear model immediately addresses some of our criticisms of
Theorem 12, discussed in Section 5.7.
вҖў The model allows for arbitrary marginal distributions of the correlations.
вҖў It directly takes into account the dependence between the correlation values.
вҖў It directly deals with the fact that we rarely know the exact correlation distri-
butions, by using the signal/noise model.
вҖў The approximations do not need to make up a subspace of Fn
2.
вҖў It is true for both Theorem 13 and Corollary 2 that ВөR вүҘВөW and Пғ2

of an ideal cipher.
The immediate disadvantage of the multivariate model is that we need a way to cal-
culate ОЈвӢҶ. While we can do this algorithmically by enumerating part of the linear
hulls and using Corollary 1, doing this for ciphers where hull enumeration is difficult
poses a problem. In particular, more analytic approaches to calculating the expected
capacity, as for example the work done by Cho in [13], does not help to determine ОЈвӢҶ.
Currently, we are not aware of any analytic way to determine the covariances of the
linear approximations. Additionally, using Theorem 13 is clearly more computation-
ally intensive than Theorem 12. For large values of M, the matrix product required
to calculate Пғ2
R could easily become unwieldy.
Another concern is how we have applied the signal/noise model. Assuming that
we are able to calculate ОЈвӢҶ, we only use the signal/noise model to adjust the variance
values, as can be seen from Equation 6.7. We do nothing compensate for our limited
knowledge of the covariance values. Thus, we have to rely on the signal covariances
being good estimates of the actual covariances.

further details on the attack, we refer to [13].

6.4
Open Questions and Future Work
Although all our experiments indicate that the multivariate model is an improvement
over all previous models, we have also observed that it is still quite far from accurately
predicting the variance of the test statistic TH1. The model relies on two assumption:
the assumption that T is approximately normally distributed (Assumption 1), and
the assumption that the joint distribution of the correlation values is multivariate
normally distributed (Assumption 3).
To the best of our knowledge, Assumption 1 has surprisingly not been verified in
the literature. Our preliminary experiments show that it is not necessarily the case
that T is normally distributed. Research into the exact distribution of T could help
to improve the current models.
In the same vein, the validity of Assumption 3 should be verified. If the correla-
tion values are not multivariate normal, this could help to explain why Assumption 1
might be invalid.
Our experiments clearly show that the multivariate model im-
proves when we consider the covariance between the correlation values. However, the
true joint distribution of the correlations might have a more complicated dependence
structure, which is not taken into account by the multivariate model. Identifying this
dependence structure could go a long way towards predicting the true distribution of
T .
Finally, it would be interesting to expand the signal/noise model to also account for
a limited knowledge of the covariances between the correlation distributions. Doing
this might further improve the multivariate model.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this thesis, a new model for using many linear approximations simultaneously
to create a statistical distinguisher for block ciphers was presented.
This model
was compared to the multiple linear model and the multidimensional linear model.
The new model, called multivariate linear cryptanalysis, moves the analysis of linear
cryptanalysis from univariate statistics into the realm of multivariate statistics. To
this end, the correlations of the linear approximations were expressed using a joint
multivariate normal distribution. The test statistic used for the distinguisher was
then given by a quadratic form in this distribution. This test statistic was shown to
be equivalent to the one used in the multidimensional linear model.
We found the multivariate model to be less restrictive than the multiple and mul-
tidimensional models. Especially, the model does not assume independence of the
linear approximations, or that the approximations have a specific structure. How-
ever, the multiple and multidimensional models can be viewed as special cases of the
multivariate model. Additionally, the model allows for the marginal distributions to
have arbitrary parameters.
The signal/noise model was used to account for a limited knowledge of the true
distributions of the linear approximations. Our experiments show, that using this
model gives a much more realistic prediction of the true distributions. In some cases,
this can make a drastic difference between the multivariate and multidimensional
model.
Through experiments on SmallPresent-[8], we have shown that the multivari-
ate model gives a better estimate of the mean and variance of the test statistic, than
previous models. Especially, the estimate of the variance is markedly improved. How-
ever, this estimate is still much lower than the actual value, which could lead to a
large overestimate of the resulting attackвҖҷs strength.
Using the multivariate model, we found that the computational complexity of
ChoвҖҷs attack on present is slightly underestimated вҖ“ though not significantly so.
While the multivariate model seems to be an improvement, we still found that it is
far from optimal. We conclude that more research is needed into the joint distribution
of the linear approximations and the distribution of the test statistic. Especially, we
hypothesise that the dependence structure between the linear approximations is more
complicated than the linear dependence we have considered in this work.
