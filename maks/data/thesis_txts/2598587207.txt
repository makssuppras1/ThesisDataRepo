Abstract
The goal of the M.Sc. thesis project focuses on how to simulate recommender agent inter-
action based on reinforcement learning within hearing healthcare, by modelling a users’
audiological preferences in response to hearing aid settings generated by a recommender
agent within a changing soundscape environment. The aim is to evaluate and assess the
agent interaction by comparing different recommendation algorithms, intending to grad-
ually optimise the perceived listening satisfaction for different audiological types of users
exposed to a sequence of soundscapes environment throughout a day.

1.1
Background
Recommender systems have traditionally been focused on static suggestion of products
based on similarities of features, rather than modelling user preferences as dynamic and
evolving over time dependent on the context and the state of the user; google AI have
recently open-sourced a state of the art framework for simulating sequential interaction
between recommender agents and users within a changing context, by using reinforce-
ment learning to gradually infer preferences based on a trade-off between exploiting and
exploring the solution space in order to optimise a reward function over time.
Translating such a recommender agent simulation framework from e.g. social media sug-
gestions of YouTube videos, to learning personalised audiological preferences within a
hearing healthcare domain, is extremely challenging as the user state depends on hidden
features reflecting cognitive processing, which continuously has to adapt to the changing
context of soundscapes defined by multiple interacting parameters. Figure 1.1 illustrates
a simple overview of this project.
Figure 1.1: Project Overview.
1.2
Outline
The current M.Sc. project focuses on how to simulate recommender agent interaction
based on reinforcement learning within hearing healthcare, by modelling a user’s audio-
logical preferences in response to hearing aid settings generated by a recommender agent

interaction by comparing different recommendation algorithms, with a goal of gradually

1.3
Problem Definition
merge to background
Hearing loss1 is on the rise. According to the World Health Organisation [2], in the year
2018, approximately 466 million people worldwide have disabling hearing loss2. It is es-
timated that by 2050 the number of people with this condition will double, reaching over
900 million. This is a significant increase in hearing loss, which in consequence will lead
to high demands in hearing healthcare resources. Currently, hearing aids (HAs) are the
primary treatment of choice [3]. However due to the nature of hearing aids and users, two
main challenges are to be addressed:
(1) Users have unique hearing preferences.
Challenge: Hearing is as unique as a fingerprint [4]. Two people can have the same
degree and type of hearing loss, even share similar audiogram properties and still not
have the same hearing loss needs [5].
Research question: Is it possible to learn the pattern of hearing aid setting preference
for a unique user?
Solution: Since users perceive the sounds differently, the idea of integrating a recom-
mendation system (RS) with hearing aids potentially allows for fully personalised HA set-
ting for each user.
(2) Hearing aids have static settings.
1Hearing loss, also known as hearing impairment, is a partial or total inability to hear [1]. Hearing loss
may occur in one or both ears [2].
2Disabling hearing loss refers to hearing loss greater than 40 decibels (dB) in the better hearing ear in
adults and a hearing loss greater than 30 dB in the better hearing ear in children [2].
2

Challenge: When users are fitted with a new hearing aid, they are usually given a “one
size fits all” (static) setting, which does not reflect the varying needs of users during the
course of the day [6]. In reality, the users soundscape, also known as context, is constantly
changing (dynamically) throughout the day. For example, in the morning the users is home
where its quite, and in the afternoon the user is having lunch in a busy restaurant where
its noisy. Ideally, for each context it would be nice to have a specific HA setting to best fit
the users hearing loss needs. Conventional hearing aids often have a static setting. In
recent years, even it is possible to pair multiple settings with modern hearing aids due to
the development of hearing healthcare technology, the number of settings is still limited.
Research question: Is it possible to learn the pattern of hearing aid setting preference?
And how reinforcement learning can help to solve this question?
Solution: A possible means to address this issue, could be to dynamically change the
HA settings during the course of the day. Given the sequential nature of the change in
context that the user experiences, this issue can be framed as reinforcement learning
(RL) problem.
In this project, we will investigate a novel recommender system (using reinforcement
learning) to make hearing aids learn for each individual user for different contexts.

Pong Example
Reinforcement learning methods are used to create an AI agent that outperforms the hard-
coded agent in the classic Atari game, Pong. The proposed solution is to sample the Pong
environment, feed raw game frames to a deep-Q-network (DQN), then take actions based
on its output.
Figure 2.1: Complete architecture of the action selection model created for Pong on Atari
2600.
Computation
• Exploration Vs.
Exploitation.
Reinforcement learning is not restricted by the
amount of training data -> take unlimited random actions. The key to improving is
the epsilon-greedy algorithm that decreases the chance of taking random actions
over the first n epochs.
• SGD optimisation. Approximate the non-linear function Q(s, a) with NN -> Bellman
equation. However, observations are not independent and identically distributed
(i.i.d). Solution: replay buffer of experience and sample training data from it.
