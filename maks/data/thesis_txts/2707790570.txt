ABSTRACT
The transition to a sustainable power system through the integration of renewable energy
sources (RESs) can pose challenges for the optimal operation of the system. In addition,
the on-going electriﬁcation threatens to exacerbate the situation with their high demand
on energy and the increase on the peak demand magnitude [1].
For instance, electric vehicles (EV) integration is set to inﬂuence distribution networks
with their additional load [2]. Overloading of transformers and lines may lead Distribution
System Operators (DSOs) to reinforce the already existing infrastructure or acquire power
system ﬂexibility solutions to deal with the situation [3]. Such ﬂexibility can be provided
from EVs, by modulating or shifting their charging process.
The simplest EV charging strategy today, also mentioned as “dumb” charging, is the
uncontrolled maximum charging power as soon as the EV is plugged into the grid. This
thesis will focus on implementing smart charging strategies to provide ﬂexibility services.
Flexibility services provided by EVs can be divided into two categories, namely behind
the meter (BTM) and in front of the meter (FTM) [4]. BTM services refer to the users
and focus on coordinating the EVs consumption with other loads (e.g. buildings) and
distributed energy resources (DER) behind the point of common coupling (PCC). On the
other hand, FTM services can refer to the DSO being known as local services or to the
TSO known as global services [5]. This type of grid services includes charging at oﬀ-peak
times to assist with the reduction of peak loads, alleviation of transformer congestion
through charging power adjustments and wind power curtailment reduction through the
utilization of the excess wind power production for the charging process [6].
This thesis is focused on coordinated and autonomous charging control of electric vehicles
(EVs) within multiple parking lots cluster for the provision of FTM services.
The
investigation is going to be held into a virtual power plant scenario. Grid services include
responding to unique or concurrent demand-response signals with the aim of providing
services such as power limitation and wind power production matching [7]. Another aspect
to be investigated is assessing the ﬂexibility of EVs based on diﬀerent constraints such as
state of charge (SoC), driving needs and charging availability. Geographical constraints
reﬂected on the power availability (e.g. transformer congestion) of individual ﬂeets of
vehicles will be accounted for.
It was found that EVs have a potential for providing ﬂexibility services through smart
charging. Wind power production can be utilized for charging, avoiding curtailment in
this way. Moreover, it was showcased that the EVs can use only wind power to fulﬁll their
charging needs and a small amount of energy imported from the grid is needed in times of
low wind generation. Moreover it was shown that the charging power adjustment based on
a dynamic power reference imposed by a wind power timeseries is capable of absorbing
the ﬂuctuations of the wind power injected into the grid. Finally, through charging power
limitation it is possible to tackle overloading both on parking lot and grid level.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
As the world’s population rapidly increases, the energy needs are rising accordingly. At
the moment, the majority of the society’s energy needs (e.g industry, transportation,
electricity) comes from fossil fuels, such as oil, coal and natural gas. Thus, the carbon
dioxide (CO2) levels as well as the greenhouse gas emissions are rising at an alarming rate.
The requirements for decarbonization for the energy and transport sectors are becoming
stricter and new technologies that will enable the compliance with the new worldwide
standards are needed.
According to the Paris Agreement (2015) [14], countries are obliged to limit global warming
preferably to 1.5 degrees Celsius. In order to work towards this goal, the European Union
(EU) has set the milestone of reducing the emissions by minimum 55 % by 2030. A decisive
step towards the decarbonization of the energy and transportation sector can be done by
integrating a larger share of renewable energy sources (RES).
As the transport sector in Europe is responsible for 1/4th of greenhouse gas emissions [15],
it is easy to infer that electric mobility is a promising solution towards a more sustainable
transport sector. The main reasons are that it doesn’t involve any harmful emissions
to the environment and, with a state-of-the art charging control, it produces tangible
beneﬁts for the energy sector as well [15]. This realization has initiated the work towards
the development of EVs which will assist in the coupling of the transport and electricity
sectors.
The electric power system is deﬁned as the network which facilitates the uninterruptible
electricity exchange between producers-consumers and it ensures an equilibrium between
produced and consumed power [16]. The traditional power system is organized in a vertical
way where the generation is centralized and the consumption distributed. Additionally, the
power ﬂows from the top (large scale generation power plants) via high voltage transmission
lines to the bottom (end-users) via medium and low voltage distribution systems. The
high voltage system is called the transmission network and is managed by the transmission
system operator (TSO) while the medium and low voltage systems are managed by the
distribution system operator (DSO).
1.2
Motivation
In the recent years, along with the evolution of RESs, the power system itself has evolved in
terms of structure. Particularly, the large scale generating power plants are gradually being
replaced by distributed energy sources such as wind turbines (WTs) and photovoltaics

(PV). As a result, the power ﬂow is bidirectional and the actors in all levels (generation,
transmission and distribution) can be involved [8].
Figure 1.1: Power system evolution. [8]
In the traditional form of the power system, the electric loads are accountable for any
uncontrollability leading to imbalances. As the penetration of wind and solar power
increases, the uncontrollability shifts to the generation side due to the intermittent
and stochastic nature of RESs. This new reality imposes a paradigm shift from the
traditional "production follows consumption" to an evolved approach of "consumption
follows production". In order to attain this, the consumers are required to provide some
degree of ﬂexibility. Hence, it is vital that they transform to "smart" consumers, namely
adjusting their consumption habits in order to beneﬁt the power system [16]. These
ﬂexibility services have the form of consumption regulation according to a monetary
remuneration and they are known as demand response (DR) [8].
Following this new situation, EVs present a unique chance to oﬀer a certain degree of
ﬂexibility to the power system. At the same time their extensive use is a step towards the
"green" transformation of the transport sector, namely the shift from fossil-fuel dependency
to electriﬁcation. As it has been forecasted in [17], by 2030 there will be 140 million EVs
on the streets worldwide. Taking into account that the EVs charge in an uncontrolled
manner and their charging process mainly occurs in the evening, it is easy to infer that
the peak evening load will inevitably increase [18]. Therefore for the coming years, it is
highlighted that EVs can be an active load and become a ﬂexibility asset for the power
system by delivering ancillary services [19].
The mass electriﬁcation paves the way for considerable increase in the peak electricity
demand and the integration of variable RES in the power system increases the potential
imbalances between production and consumption. The increase of the peak demand will
lead to the need for reinforcing and upgrading the power system and the imbalances
will lead to the requirement of balancing ancillary services. This thesis will focus on the
investigation of two types of ﬂexibility services. Firstly, the potential for peak demand
shaving, namely adjusting the charging power levels of the EVs to provide services either
on local level (parking lot) or grid level (feeder overloading) and secondly the wind power
variability mitigation, which concerns the opportunity to balance the ﬂuctuations of the
wind power exported to the grid from a wind farm.

1. Introduction
1.3
ACDC project
This thesis is conducted as part of the research activities of the Danish-funded research
project Autonomously Controlled Distributed Chargers (ACDC).
The Danish government aims to suspend the sale of diesel and gasoline vehicles by 2030
thus the expected increase of EVs can either pose challenges or bring opportunity to the
power system.
The goal of the project is to showcase the opportunity for provision of ﬂexibility to the
power system by controlling the EV charging process. Another aspect investigated is the
support of the integration of more RES in the power system. The projects focuses on the
development of two new technologies: an EV autonomous smart charge controller and a
virtual aggregator. The content of the research focuses on three main areas: architecture
for autonomous chargers, large-scale management of clusters of electric vehicles, power
smoothing applications with hybrid power plants. Partners to the projects are Nissan,
Circle Consult, El-Net Øst and Vestas [20].
1.4
Thesis description and main objectives
The main thesis objectives are the design of Electric Vehicle (EV) smart charging strategies
for the provision of FTM services and the assessment of ﬂexibility of EVs based on diﬀerent
constraints such as SoC, driving needs and charging availability. Control algorithms will
be developed in order to respond to diﬀerent signals from the grid and perform the FTM
services. More speciﬁcally, the analysis of the simulation results will lead to answering the
following research questions:
• How can the EVs contribute to wind power production matching? The
main goal of this service is to balance the power output of a wind power plant by
adjusting the charging levels. This research question can be divided into 3 sub
questions and can be formulated as:
1. How can the surplus power be allocated to the diﬀerent EV driving
proﬁles? When the wind is high and there is a surplus in the wind farm power
production, the EVs are set to charge from the excess power and the rest of the
wind power can be injected into the grid . In this way wind power curtailment
can be avoided.
2. How can the EVs utilize only wind power to fulﬁll their charging
requirements? This charging strategy aims to maximize self-consumption
from a local wind power plant thus demanding power from the grid only when
the wind power production is very low.
3. How can the EVs assist in mitigating the variability of the wind
power injected to the power grid? This research question concerns the
possibility of utilizing the highly variable wind power output of a wind farm
to design a power reference that will be followed by the charging power of the
EVs in one or several parking lots. If the speed of the controller is high enough,
the charging power will follow the designed power reference and in this way the
ﬂuctuations of the power exported to the grid can be dampened.

EVS AND SMART CHARGING
The introduction of electric vehicles (EVs) has the potential to revolutionize the world’s
transition to sustainable energy, particularly renewable power generation. EVs oﬀer a
signiﬁcant chance to increase the amount of renewables in the entire power generating mix,
in addition to reforming the transportation industry [9]. However, integrating large EV
ﬂeets in the distribution system may be detrimental to the grid operation and planning.
The adverse eﬀects of EV integration can be mitigated by using smart charging technologies
[4]. This chapter aims to analyze the concept of smart charging as well as the ﬂexibility
services related to it. Consequently, the synergy between EVs and RES will be discussed.
2.1
EV current status and charging infrastructure
The shift to electric mobility minimizes the transportation sector’s carbon footprint while
also increasing vehicle eﬃciency. However, one popular argument is that there is a lack of
charging infrastructure due to the low number of EVs and the EVs are low in number due
to the lack of charging infrastructure. In order to be able to achieve a fruitful transition to
electric mobility, a balance between the number of EVs and the charging infrastructure
should be maintained [21].
The Danish government intends to decrease greenhouse gas emissions by 70 % by 2030
compared to 1990. Today, 4 out of 100 Danish citizens buy an electric vehicle, implying
that by 2030, everyone should choose an EV when it comes to buying a new car [22]. On a
global scale, EV sales exceeded 2 million in 2019 (2.1 million) just 2 years after having
surpassed 1 million in 2017. On a European level, EV sales in 2019 increased by 50%
compared to 2018 and the countries with the highest shares of EVs in overall car sales
being Norway (56%) and Iceland (22%) [17].
Moreover, EVs can be classiﬁed in the following categories [17]:
• Battery electric vehicles (BEVs): The battery is the only energy source.
• Hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs): They are equipped with gasoline engine, fuel tank
and an electric motor and a battery. However, HEVs cannot be charged from the
power grid.
• Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles (PHEVs): They are equipped with battery and/or
internal combustion engine and can either be charged by the power grid or the engine.
• Fuel-cell electric vehicles: They are powered from hydrogen or oxygen in fuel cells
inside the vehicle.
This thesis focuses on the ﬁrst category, the BEVs and for simplicity reasons the abbreviation
EV is going to be used.

2. EVs and smart charging
It is very important to highlight that EVs have eﬃciency levels of 80-90 % which means
that 80-90% of the input power is converted to power moving the car [23]. To showcase
the fundamentals behind the operation of EVs, the main elements are presented in Figure
2.1. The gray-colored area in the Figure represents the EV itself and as can be seen,
the only components lying outside the EV are the grid and the electric vehicle supply
equipment (EVSE). In brief terms, an EVSE is the protocol which establishes a bidirectional
communication between the EV and the charger in order to set the correct charging current
[24]. Moreover, the EVSE is responsible for the safety during the charging process making
sure that no current is ﬂowing when the charger is not properly connected. [24]. A brief
description of the components lying inside the EV is:
• Charging socket: It is the port that allows the EV to establish a connection with
the EVSE (external power source) in order to charge its battery.
• Charger: Receives the AC power coming from the grid and sends it to the AC/DC
converter which in turn supplies the battery. Furthermore, the charger communicates
with the battery management system (BMS) in order to keep track of the voltage,
current, temperature and state of charge (SoC) of the battery during charging.
• BMS: It is responsible for the secure operation of the battery, namely it is taking

• Battery: It is the unit that stores the energy which is being used by the electric
motor.
• DC-DC converter: Converts (lowers) the DC voltage of the battery in order to
operate some auxiliary loads that require lower voltage than the output of the battery.
• Motor Controller: It is a power electronics components which regulates the energy
the battery feeds to the motor by controlling the speed and torque produced by the
electric motor.
• Electric Motor: It is the unit that gives movement to the wheels of the EV. As
mentioned above, its energy needs are supplied by the battery. The battery is coupled
to the electric motor through an inverter which allows the feature of regenerative
braking. This means that when the car slows down, the electric motor becomes an
AC generator sending energy back to the battery through the inverter.

2.2
Uncontrolled and controlled charging
Uncontrolled EV charging refers to the charging of an EV with maximum power as soon
as it is connected to the charger. This behavior results in a total power demand increase
and may cause stability and security issues in the distribution network [26]. Moreover, the
additional power demand induced by the EV load and especially the increase of the peak
demand can overload the power system infrastructure. As a result, the distribution system
operators (DSOs) will be forced to upgrade the existing infrastructure (e.g transformers,
lines) to tackle congestion [4]. Additionally, the charging of EVs is accountable for phase
imbalances and voltage drops which can have a signiﬁcant impact on the grid depending
on the penetration rates and user behavior [27]. On the other hand, controlled ("smart")
charging can be deﬁned as the adaptation of the charging process of the EVs according to
both the power system condition and the needs of the vehicle users [9]. In this context smart
charging is beneﬁcial to both power system operators (technical beneﬁts) and customers as
they can participate in the demand response programs and receive monetary remuneration
[28]. Authors in [29] suggest that employing ﬂexibility solutions for the power system can
reduce the peak demand and congestion issues can be alleviated. In this way, expensive
infrastructure upgrades can be avoided resulting in signiﬁcant ﬁnancial savings for the
DSOs.
2.3
Flexibility services from EV smart charging
Smart charging has the potential to enable ﬂexibility both at the system and at the local
level. By adjusting their charging levels, EV charging patterns might be regulated with
V1G (unidirectional smart charging) to ﬂatten peak demand, ﬁll load valleys [6], and
provide real-time grid balancing [30]. EVs could provide ﬂexibility services to transmission
system operators (TSOs) by injecting electricity back into the grid via V2G (Vehicle to
Grid) [31]. Smart charging could assist customers monitor their energy use and enhance
their rates of renewable energy self-consumption, as well as DSOs manage congestion [9].
In addition, authors in [32] highlight that residential smart charging is economically more
feasible when compared to battery energy storage.
Flexibility services provided through smart charging can be separated into 2 categories,
namely behind the meter (BTM) and in front of the meter (FTM). BTM services refer to
the users and include load coordination among EVs, buildings and local distributed energy
resources at the point of connection (PCC). FTM services refer to ﬂexibility provided
to DSOs and can include reduction of peak loads, transformer overloading, reduction of
RES curtailment and consumption of low price power coming from RES [33]. Figure 2.3

2.4
Synergy between EVs and wind power
Grid-connected EVs can assist in increasing the penetration of wind power and RES in
general in the power system. Some ways to achieve this goal are by using the energy
storage capability of the EVs and by maximizing the consumption of local wind power at
times of excessive wind power production [34],[35]. Another aspect that can be mitigated
by EV smart charging strategies is the wind power production variability. It is well known
that the stochastic nature of wind leads to highly ﬂuctuating wind power plants’ power
output proﬁles. When a highly variable wind power output is injected to the grid there is
high possibility of stressing the system infrastructure and causing production-consumption
imbalances. When the charging pattern of the EVs is designed to "follow" the wind power
output from a local wind power plant, those ﬂuctuations can be dampened, seen from a
grid perspective. This means that the ﬂuctuating wind power output will be used for the
charging process of the EVs and the resulting power proﬁle that will be transmitted to the
distribution network will have a smoother behavior (less ﬂuctuations). This requires a fast
controller that will be able to respond to a highly variable power reference designed based
on a wind power timeseries. The controller will promptly adjust the charging power and
in this way, the ﬂuctuations can be absorbed through the charging power adjustment. One
of the goals of this thesis is to utilize the wind power production of a local wind farm and

METHODOLOGY
The main objective of the thesis is to design smart charging strategies for an EV ﬂeet (1
parking lot or multiple clusters) based on the maximum current capacity of the individual
parking lot (fuse limit), the wind power production of a local wind farm and on local
transformer loading constraints. This chapter provides an overview on the architecture of
the whole system as well as a general description of the developed model. More detailed
aspects of the modeling will be analyzed in Chapter 4.
3.1
System Architecture
This section concerns the control approach used in this investigation as well as the big
picture of the system architecture and the analysis of the proposed methodology.
3.1.1
Control Architecture
There are several forms of control architectures. They are divided in three categories,
namely centralized, decentralized and distributed. The centralized control architecture
is based on a single central control component gathering information from the grid and
distributing the decided power setpoints for power demand/supply of every electrical device
remotely. Some advantages of the centralized control approach are that it is a well-known
architecture, it gives the opportunity of monitoring the system from a single observation
point and that it has an operational transparency. However there are several drawbacks
associated with it, namely the amount of data to be processed in large, it requires a
complex and costly communication infrastructure and it is vulnerable to cyber attacks
so it compromises privacy. On the other hand, the decentralized control architecture is
based on local control components using local measurements. Some advantages of the
decentralized approach are that it is scalable and autonomous, requires less communication
infrastructure and that the charging control lies at the side of the user. There are several
disadvantages to this control approach as well, namely the uncertainty in the ﬁnal result and
the requirement of predicting the user behavior to a certain extent. Finally, the distributed
control architecture combines centralized and decentralized control architectures by utilizing
both a local and a central control component [28], [10], [36]. In this investigation, the
distributed control approach is used where the central control component is called cloud
aggregator (CA) and the local control component is the virtual aggregator (VA) and the
charger controllers [12]. The reason behind the choice of the distributed control architecture
is that it combines the advantages of both centralized and decentralized control approaches.
In particular, with a distributed control approach the communication cost decreases and
this type of control maximizes the autonomy and the scalability of the system [37]. Figure
3.1 illustrates the 3 described control approaches.

3.1.2
Cloud Aggregator (CA)
The CA is the centralized part of the intelligence which is being used in this investigation
and is responsible for communicating information regarding the global grid status. For
example, the CA is able to broadcast information about the power production of a local wind
farm, spot market electricity prices or transformer congestion information. Subsequently,
in cooperation with the VA ﬂexibility services can be provided.
3.1.3
Virtual Aggregator (VA)
The VA is the intelligence used in a decentralized control approach and is responsible
for the coordination of the chargers. The VA is connected to the cloud and is able to
receive signals from smart meters (e.g power, current and voltage measurements), power
setpoints from a cloud aggregator and the user (initial state of charge (SoC), departure
time). Subsequently, the VA propagates the control signal ∆P, which is the error between
the reference power and the measured power at the point of common coupling (PCC),
to a proportional-integral (PI) controller responsible for controlling the charging power.
According to the signals received, the VA can eﬀectively adjust the power reference in
order to limit the charging power and in this way provide ﬂexibility services to the grid.
Ultimately, the VA is equipped with a memory which is useful for the storage of the user’s
preferences and is able to translate them into charging schedule.
3.1.4
Virtual Power Plant (VPP)
VPPs are a type of clustering model that attempts to manage geographically scattered
electrical generation and demand as if they were one entity for the system operator. In
this context, the mission of a VPP is to network DERs in order to monitor, forecast and
trade their power [11]. Inside a VPP, EVs are able to provide ﬂexibility services to the

the connected EVs that fulﬁll the criteria to start charging will charge with at least the
minimum charging power they support (1-phase or 3-phase). Figure 3.6 illustrates the

4.1.4
Priority logic
The charging process of the EVs is scheduled according to a priority logic. After an EV
is connected to the charger and the user registers the inputs needed, a priority number
is calculated for the EV and based on this number the EV will either start charging
immediately or will be put in the queue because the EV on the same charger has higher
priority. Moreover, the power modulation is also dependent on the priority.
Figure 4.4 illustrates the priority logic developed. As can be seen, the priority value
calculated is a result of a summation of diﬀerent factors. The most important of them are
the following:
• State of Charge (SoC): The SoC component of the priority is implemented with
a look-up table which gives a linear output from 1 to 0 when the SoC is going from
0 to 100%. This means that when the SoC is 100% the result that goes into the
summation is 0.
• SoC urgent charge: If the SoC is lower than 25% then the result that goes into
the summation is 0.25. This positive number serves the purpose of increasing the
priority when an EV with low SoC is connected.
• SoC reduce charging: If an EV reaches a SoC of 90% then the result that goes to
the summation is -0.25. This means that the priority will be reduced since the EV
has reached a high SoC and does not need a high priority anymore.
• Desired kilometers: If the amount of available kilometers is lower than the amount

USE CASES
This chapter presents the use cases (scenarios) simulated in order to showcase how
the control logic works according to the various requirements (e.g wind power output,
transformer congestion constraints).
• Scenario 1: Power reference design based on local wind farm power output.
• Scenario 2: Power reference design based on local wind farm power output and
transformer congestion
5.1
Scenario 1
The ﬁrst scenario investigated is a study case of a power reference design solely based on a
wind farm power proﬁle. The objective is to utilize the power coming from the wind in
order to charge the EVs in the 4 parking lots. Moreover, from a control perspective, it is
desired that the charging power proﬁles of the EVs are able to follow the highly variable
power reference coming from the wind power proﬁle. At the same time, the primary control
objective is to provide power limitation at times when the available EVs for charging would
cause the total charging power to exceed the fuse limit of the parking lot, thus causing
overloading.
This scenario showcases a situation when the loading of a transformer is very low and
the congestion constraint is not present. Another situation showcased by this scenario is
when the wind farm and the parking lots are connected to the same feeder and thus the
transformer lies upstream.
In this use case the CA distributes the wind power reference to the parking lots based on
the PRDC given by Equation 3.3. After, the reference power is adjusted according to the
logic described in 3.3.2.
5.2
Scenario 2
The second scenario investigated is a case study of a power reference design based on
a wind farm power proﬁle and constraints coming from transformer overloading. The
objective is to utilize the wind power in order to charge the EVs in 4 parking lots respecting
the limitations regarding transformer overloading.
This use case is a more complete version and showcases the situation where a highly loaded
feeder needs an overloading mitigation in order to avoid the infrastructure reinforcement,
e.g replacing the already existing transformer with a larger one. The power limitation
oﬀered in this situation is crucial for the safe operation of the distribution network.

to follow the highly variable wind power reference in order to charge the EVs.

CONCLUSION
This thesis focused on the design of EV smart charging strategies for the provision of
FTM services and the assessment of ﬂexibility of EVs based on diﬀerent constraints such
as state of charge (SoC), driving needs and charging availability. The investigation was
held into a virtual power plant setup where control algorithms were designed in order to
respond to diﬀerent signals from the grid and provide the FTM services. In particular, the
signals coming from the grid are the power production of a wind farm and the aggregate
loading of the feeders where the proposed parking lots are connected. The analysis was
carried out in 2 scenarios where the ﬁrst one describes a situation where grid constraints
are not present i.e transformer congestion and the wind power production is the only
criterion for the power reference design. The second one is a case where the transformer
congestion constraints are taken into account and the power reference design is based both
on the wind power production and the constraints coming from the overloading of the
transformers on the feeders where the parking lots are connected. The model was designed
and implemented in Simulink and the results were plotted and analyzed in MATLAB. The
research questions of the thesis that are listed in section 1.3 are concluded in this chapter
relying on the results and the knowledge obtained.
• How can the EVs contribute to wind power production matching? The
main goal of this service is to balance the power output of a wind power
plant by adjusting the charging levels. This research question can be divided
into 3 sub questions and can be formulated as:
1. How can the surplus power be allocated to the diﬀerent EV driving
proﬁles? It was shown that a dynamic power reference based on a wind power
proﬁle can successfully be designed and the EVs can utilize the available wind
power for their charging process. The remaining power that cannot be allocated
to the EVs either due to fulﬁllment of their charging requirements or due to
lack of EV availability can be injected to the power grid. This result showcases
that the excess wind power can be utilized for EV charging purposes and in this
way the curtailment is prevented.
2. How can the EVs utilize only wind power to fulﬁll their charging
requirements? The resulting power proﬁle after subtracting the total EV
power demand from the original wind power timeseries shows that for the greater
part of the 24 hour simulation the EVs can use only wind power to fulﬁll their
charging requirements and only a small amount of energy needs to be imported
from the power grid at times when the wind power production is very low.
This result is reﬂected on the calculation of the assessment indices where it is
observed that the power imported from the grid is negligible compared to the
amount that is exported during the 24 hour simulation.
3. How can the EVs assist in mitigating the variability of the wind power
41
==PAGE:54==
7. Conclusion
injected to the power grid? It was shown that the speed of the controller
allowed the rapid adjustment of the charging power of the EVs by following
a highly variable power reference designed based on a wind power timeseries.
The fast adjustment of the charging power levels resulted on a smoother power
export from the wind power plant by absorbing the ﬂuctuations to a certain
extent. However, it was highlighted that with a higher availability of EVs at
times when the power reference allowed maximum usage of wind power, the
variability mitigation could have been improved.
•
How can overloading be alleviated through the EVs smart charging
strategies? This research question can be answered in two parts. Firstly, there
is the overloading on the parking lot level which refers to the hard constraint of
respecting the fuse limit, namely the maximum admissible current and secondly
there is the overloading on the grid level which refers to the constraint of the
transformer overloading. As it was shown, the control strategies implemented succeed
in respecting both the fuse limit of each parking lot and in the scenario where the
grid constraint was introduced, the transformer congestion was prevented through
the charging power adjustments.
7.1
Future work
This section highlights some topics for further research which have not been covered in
this thesis. In particular:
• Modeling of larger clusters for optimal control of the power export: An
adjustment in the already existing model regarding the number of EVs in one parking
lot or the addition of more parking lots could lead to a smoother power export
to the grid from the virtual power plant. Moreover another aspect that could be
investigated is the ideal dimensioning of the EV demand with respect to the wind
power capacity.
• Modeling 3-phase to 1-phase switching and disconnection of EVs: The
opportunity to switch EV charging from 3-phase to 1-phase or even disconnecting
speciﬁc EVs when needed can increase the ﬂexibility margin when providing the
service of charging power adjustment to avoid overloading. The model used in
this thesis does not include phase switching or EV disconnection and this addition
could provide better results in terms of overloading alleviation. Additionally phase
switching can provide the ﬂexibility service of phase balancing when needed.
• V2G: Another aspect to be modeled could be the opportunity for bidirectional power
ﬂow. With V2G the parking lots could provide other ancillary services related to
frequency regulation.
• Modeling of response to electricity prices:
Apart from the wind power
production and the transformer loading proﬁle, information regarding the hourly
electricity price could be processed by the CA. A control logic that will shift the
charging process to hours when electricity price is lower can optimize the charging
fee paid by the EV users.
• Modeling of response to signals regarding CO2 emissions: Another addition
to the model could be the processing of information regarding CO2 emissions by the

CO2 emissions are low can be developed.
