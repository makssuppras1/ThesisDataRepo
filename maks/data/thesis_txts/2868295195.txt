Abstract
This MSc thesis explores the use of profile Hidden Markov Models (pHMMs)
to compress and analyze immune repertoires in NSCLC patients using high-
throughput TcR CDR3b sequencing from different tissues. PCA results suggest
that pHMMs effectively separate peripheral blood mononuclear cells with other
tissues and project additional datasets into the same PCA space with correct
closest neighbour, highlighting the ability of pHMMs to capture immune pro-
files. A novel TcR diversity metric, based on SDV of forward scores, outperforms
traditional indices like the Shannon Index. However, pHMMs struggle to distin-
guish certain tissues and correlate with clinical variables such as age, sex, cancer
type and stage. While promising, further refinement and validation are needed.

1 Introduction
Probabilistic models like the Hidden Markov Model (HMMs) and profile Hidden
Markov Models (pHMMs) are powerful tools inside bioinformatics; Recent work by
Yu et al. [2] demonstrate the potential of HMMs in analysing immune repertoires in
blood.
However, pHMMs are not widely applied for analysis inside the human immune reper-
toire. The core hypothesis of this study is that pHMMs can effectively compress the
immune repertoire and reveal clinical differences, such as tissue types, age, sex, or can-
cer stage. This thesis also investigates whether pHMMs can provide a more reliable
approach to quantifying repertoire diversity compared to traditional single-number
indices like the Shannon Index and Simpson indices [3], which are limited in their
ability to capture the full complexity of immune diversity [4].
1.1 Immune system and immune response
The immune system is a complex network of different cells, tissues, and molecules.
It has two main branches: the innate immune system, which provides immediate and
non-specific protection against pathogens, and the adaptive immune system, which
offers specific and long-lasting immunity by lymphocytes. In this thesis, the primary
focus for the background knowledge of the immune system is the T-cell-mediated
immune response within the adaptive immune system [5].
T cells originate from hematopoietic stem cells inside bone marrow and develop inside
the thymus. Naive T cells will mature through positive and negative selection processes
inside the thymus. After the T cells are developed into CD4+ helper T cells and CD8+
cytotoxic T cells, they will be released into circulation. T cell receptors (TcR) are
protein complexes on the membrane of T cells that are responsible for recognizing
fragments of antigen as peptides bound to major histocompatibility complex (MHC)
molecules [6]. A detailed explanation of TcR will be shown in the next section.
Once T cells are activated, clonal expansion will occur, producing a large population
of T cells specific to the antigen that triggered the activation. The expanded T cells
are cloned from the same original naive T cell, which means that they share the same
TcR sequences; this allows them to bind specifically to the same antigen [7].
While all these T cells are identical, some of them will become effective T cells that
will either kill the abnormal cell or recruit and activate other immune cells and die
out after the original pathogen is cleared; others will become memory T cells, which
will form immune memory and provide a faster, stronger immune response if the same
antigen is recognized again in the future [4].
In our body, many immune responses are constantly happening against various
pathogens. And there are many types of memory T cells developed after each immune
response. There are central memory T cells (Tcm), effector memory T cells (Tem) [8],
tissue-resident memory T cells (Trm), regulatory T lymphocytes (Treg) [9], and mem-
ory T cells with stem cell-like properties (Tscm) [10]. Among these memory T cells,
9
==PAGE:10==
Tcm majorly appears inside secondary lymphoid organs like lymph nodes and ton-
sils. Tem, however, they mainly inhabit non-lymphoid peripheral tissue types and
are capable of recirculation between blood and tissue. Trm does not recirculate and
occupy specific locations inside the tissue. [11]
Due to the nature of the immune response, clonally expanded memory T cells can be
found in human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC), various organ tissues,
and lymphoid organs where the immune response originated. Each exposure to differ-
ent pathogens formed a unique immune environment within the PBMCs, making its
immune repertoire significantly different from that of other tissues.
1.2 T Cell Receptor and Complementarity-determining regions
TcR is composed of two different protein chains, in most cases, α and β. They are
expressed on the surface of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, unlike the β chains, the α chains
contain only the V and J segments [12]. β Chain rearrangement is a vital process
of T cell maturation; during T-cell development in the thymus, one Variable (V),
one Diversity (D), and one joining (J) segment are randomly selected from multiple
germline gene segments and then the recombination process brings the selected V,
D, and J segments together to build a TcR, the successful rearrangement of the β
chain is essential for progression to the next stage of thymocyte development. The
VDJ rearrangement is a random process, and the diversity of TcR is generated by
the random selection of V, D, and J gene segments. The extreme variability in CDR3
sequences enables the immune system to recognize a vast array of antigens.[13]
Fig. 1 Rearrangement of CDR3b, the β chain consists of V, D and J segments, one random gene
from each segment is selected and then recombined into one TcR. [1]
10
==PAGE:11==
This β chain rearrangement process is shown in Fig 1. On the beta chain, there
are complementarity-determining regions (CDRs). CDRs are hypervariable loops that
directly interact with peptide-MHC (pMHC) complexes. They are part of the variable
domain of the TcR and determine antigen specificity.[14]
The CDR3 region directly contacts the antigenic peptide presented in the MHC bind-
ing groove, making this region the most important for recognizing antigenic peptides.
During the rearrangement process, the CDR3b is generated not only by the variable
region but also by the diversity and joining regions, making the CDR3b the most
diverse region of the TcR and theoretically capable of binding to any possible antigenic
peptide [15].
Once the CDR3b region of a matured T cell is bounded with p-HMC, it will trigger the
intracellular signalling cascade and ultimately activate the T cell, triggering immune
response [16]. The activated T cell will then undergo clonal expansion and differentiate
into effector T cells and memory T cells as described in Section Immune system and
immune response.
Sequencing-wise, most CDR3b sequences are 5 to 25 amino acids long after removing
flanks. Usually, CDR3b sequences will start after C and end before F or W
[17].
1.3 NSCLC and Tumor Microenvironment
According to JR Molina et al. [18], Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer (NSCLC) accounts
for around 85% of all lung cancers in the U.S. Among all NSCLC patients, 25% are
current smokers and more than 60% are former smokers. According to the same study,
the 5-year survival rate for NSCLC patients is around only around 15%. There are
two major types of NSCLC: adenocarcinoma and Squamous cell carcinoma, according
to the American National Cancer Institute (NCI), the stage of NSCLC is described
as follows [19]:
• Stage 1: The cancer is confined to the lung and has not spread to lymph nodes.
• Stage 2: Cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes or structures within the chest.
• Stage 3: Cancer has spread more extensively within the chest, potentially involving
lymph nodes on the opposite side or other critical structures.
• Stage 4: Distant metastasis of cancer present.
The Tumor Microenvironment (TME) is referred to as a complex and dynamic ecosys-
tem spatially consisting of specific cellular and non-cellular components that surround
and interact with Tumor cells by Xia et al. [20]. There are various of cells inside TME,
we focus on the T cells inside the TME, the appearance of immune cells like T cells
inside TME can either suppress the Tumor growth or promote the Tumor growth [21].
According to the same research done by NM. Anderson and M. Simon, the CD8+
Cytotoxic T-cells detect the abnormal antigen presented by the Tumor cell on the
cell membrane and target Tumor cells to kill them. They also secrete interferon−γ
(IFN−γ), which also inhibits angiogenesis. Higher Th−1 levels caused by CD4+ T
cells and the presence of CD8+ T cells are correlated with positive prognosis in several
cancers.
11
==PAGE:12==
There are three major types of TME, immune infiltrated, immune excluded, and
immune silent. The immune infiltration means that immune cells like CD8+ are homo-
geneously distributed in the Tumor, which indicates an active immune response inside
the Tumor. Immune excluded means that the T-cells are at the periphery of Tumors
and have not infiltrated the TME. Immune silent means that there is no immune
response to the Tumor in the TME [21].
If CD8+ T cells experience chronic antigen stimulation, this will lead to CD8+ T cell
exhaustion, which means that the T cells will reduce cytotoxic activity thus decreasing
its ability to kill Tumor cells [22].
1.4 Profile Hidden Markov Models
Profile Hidden Markov Models are probabilistic models that are widely used in bioin-
formatics for sequence analysis. pHMMs are HMMs with a specific architecture that
is suitable for modelling sequence profiles.
Unlike usual Markov models, the observable events in HMMs are called symbols, and
the invisible factor behind the observation is called states. In our case, the thing ‘states’
are the underlying structural or functional features of the amino acid sequences, and
the ‘symbols’ are the amino acid sequences themselves.

repetitively uses three types of hidden states, namely, match states M, insert states
I, and delete states D, to describe amino acid probability, insertion, and deletion of
amino acids [23].

1.5 Umap and Adonis Test
In this thesis, besides well-known dimensionality reduction methods like PCA, UMAP
is also employed to explore the structure of the results. Additionally, the Adonis test
is used to perform statistical hypothesis testing based on the distances derived from
the PCA results.
1.5.1 UMAP
Unlike PCA, UMAP is non-deterministic; instead of rotating the data, UMAP maps
the high-dimensional data into a lower-dimensional space by preserving the local and
global structure based on a manifold learning approach. This enables UMAP to cap-
ture complex relationships in the data while maintaining meaningful clustering and
neighbourhood structures [29]. Because of this property, Umap is good for visualiz-
ing data and clustering, but not ideal for statistical hypothesis testing approach like
Adonis test.
1.5.2 Adonis Test
The Adonis Test is a non-parametric statistical test commonly used to assess the sig-
nificance of group differences in multivariate data, based on the PERMANOVA (Per-
mutational Multivariate Analysis of Variance) framework. Adonis evaluates whether
the variation in a distance matrix can be explained by one or more explanatory
variables [30].
The idea of the Adonis test is based on the null hypothesis H0. In this thesis, the
Adonis test is used to evaluate whether the multivariate differences between groups
(e.g., in the PCA results) are statistically significant. The null hypothesis H0 is that
there is no significant difference between groups. If the p-value obtained is less than
the threshold set in this thesis (p < 0.05), we reject H0 and accept H1, concluding
that there are significant differences between groups [31].
17
==PAGE:18==
In this thesis project, we will calculate the distance matrix using Euclidean distance.
The results of the Adonis test have tree major parts: F, R2 and p [30].
• p: According to definition by S.Greenland et al. [32], p-value indicates the degree
to which the data conform to the pattern predicted by the test hypothesis and all
the other assumptions used in the test. A smaller p-value (e.g.p < 0.05) indicates
stronger evidence against H0, suggesting that the explanatory variables significantly
explain the variation and rejects the H0.
• R2 (The coefficient of determination): Represents the proportion of variance in the
distance matrix that is explained by the explanatory variables. Higher R2 values
indicate a stronger relationship between the variables and the observed multivariate
variation.
• F: The F-value is used to determine if there is a significant difference between
groups. A larger F-value indicates that the variance between group means is greater
than the variance within the groups, suggesting a more significant difference between
groups. The p-value is calculated based on the F-value to assess the statistical
significance of this difference.
The low p value (p < 0.05) is an indicator that we can reject H0 but not necessary
accepts H1, if in the paired Adonis test result, the F value is high, R is high and p
is low, then we will conclude that this p value is valid, otherwise the result will be
treated as possible false-positive.
For example, if two groups share the same centroid (e.g. the group means are identical)
but differ in their within-group dispersion, this can lead to a low p value despite low F
and R2 values. This happens because the significance in Adonis can be influenced by
differences in within-group dispersion rather than differences between group means.
In such a situation, the low p-value might not indicate a true difference between
groups but reflects the disparity in within-group dispersion, this situation could be
considered a false positive result. To mitigate this issue, in this thesis, if p ≤0.05
and either F ≥5 or R2 ≥0.1, the Adonis test result is considered as valid, otherwise
considered invalid to avoid possible false-positives [33].

3.1 Data Filtration on PBMC Dataset
Before training the pHMMs, two separate filters are applied to all PBMC samples to
remove sequences, the first approach is singleton filter, a singleton sequence means
that this sequence only appears one time inside this unique combination.
The second approach is the 2.5% quantifier filter, we combine all the sequences in side
the Reuben dataset and calculate duplication count of all sequence, then the quantile
function calculates the lowest 2.5% position among all sequences (sorted by dupli-
cation count) and returns the duplication count at that position using interpolation
(q = 6.15). After the q is calculated, the frequency threshold T is calculated using
T = q/N where N is the total sequence count for the Reuben dataset, then we filter
out all the sequence with duplication count d < T.

more average sequence count (n = 37937, 10x compare to Tumor n = 3835) compare to other tissues
The reason for applying these filters is that PBMC samples contain not only matured
naive T cells but also memory T cells such as Tcm, Treg, and Tem [40, 41]. This unique
immune environment contains a large amount of low-frequency noise caused by the
matured naive T cells even in old-age samples where the TcR diversity has significant
24
==PAGE:25==
dropped [42]. Especially considering the sequence count of PBMC samples is much
higher than other tissue sources as shown in Fig 4, this noise will become the domi-
nate factor in further analysis and cover the biological signal if not removed, causing
the initial analysis using workflow without filtering failed to significantly distinguish
differences between PBMC and other Tissues.
The two filters have distinct biological implications. The singleton filter removes all
single-appearance sequences within each sample, retaining only clonally expanded T-
cells specific to each patient. This approach may reflect individual exposure history
and individual immune responses.
However, the 2.5% quantile filter removes global low-frequency sequences, this filter
focusing on common and shared immune profiles across individuals and potentially
filter out unique colonial expanded T-cells, this filter may show broader yet similar
immune responses between patients.

3.2 Derive and Train pHMMs
In this thesis, the package ‘aphid’ is used to derive and train pHMMs [43], the package
is based on the algorithms described in the book Biological Sequence Analysis by
Richard Durbin et al. [44]. In this thesis project, the Baum-Welch algorithm is used to
train the pHMMs and forward scores are used to describe how possible is a sequence
generated by the model. The Baum-Welch is already described in section 1.4.1, and
the forward score is described in section 1.4.1.

3.3 Score Matrix Establishment
In this step, we score all the sequences used to train the model against their own
model, then we arrange the scores from high to low to get the top 100 sequences with
the highest scores. Now we have 100 sequences for each sample and corresponding
model. We then combine all the sequences into a tidyverse dataframe and remove
duplicated sequences inside the dataframe. Then we transpose all the sequences to the
column, now the dataframe has sample id as rowname and sequences as colname, and
the content is the forward score of sequence (col) against the model (row) as shown
in the workflow in Fig 3.
After the initial score matrix is established, we can now add clinical variables to
the score matrix, by matching the sample id with the original Reuben dataframe,
patient, Tissue.Source, final.stage, Biological.Sex, Age and Cancer are added to the
score matrix. The final clinical matrix is now ready for further analysis.
3.4 PCA Analysis on Score Matrix
Then we are able to use the numeric part of the clinical matrix to perform PCA
analysis on the score matrix, the function ‘prcomp’ is used to perform PCA on the
matrix with parameters ‘center = TRUE’ and ‘scale = TRUE’.
PCA is a dimensionality reduction technique that is used to reduce the number of
variables in a dataset while preserving the variance of the data. It can be used to visu-
alize the data in a lower dimensional space, making it easier to identify patterns and
relationships between groups. In this thesis, PCA is used to explore the relationship
between the forward scores of the sequences and the clinical variables,
The centre and scale control the centering and scaling of the data before PCA. The
centering and scaling process is described as follows:
Centering is the process of removing the mean of each column from each element in
the column. This process is used to remove the offsets caused by the mean of the data,
making the data more centred. For example, if one column has a mean of 10, the data
points in PCA would be shifted by 10 at that dimension. By centering, this offset is
removed.
Scaling is the process of dividing each column by its standard deviation (SDV) after
centering. This step reduces the impact of different scales among variables. For exam-
ple, if one column has values ranging from -1000 to 1000 while another column ranges
from -1 to 1 after centering. By scaling, both columns are adjusted so that their SDV
become 1, ensuring they are on the same scale. [45]

==PAGE:30==
After the centering and scaling, the PCA is performed on the matrix, and the results
are shown in the Result section.
We can also use the rotation matrix from the PCA result to project the HC dataset
back to the PCA plot of the Reuben dataset, this is a way to explore if the HC dataset
samples are similar to the samples of the Reuben dataset in the PCA space and find
their closest neighbours using Euclidean distance. All results are also shown in the
Result section.
3.5 Umap Analysis on Score Matrix
As mentioned in the introduction, unlike PCA, Umap is a non-linear dimensionality
reduction technique that is used to reduce the number of variables in a dataset while
preserving the local structure of the data [29]. It can be used to visualize the data
in a lower dimensional space, making it easier to identify patterns and relationships
between groups. However, Umap is not a deterministic algorithm, the results may
vary between runs, but the general structure of the data should be preserved. The
Umap analysis is performed on the score matrix just like the PCA but with no need
for centering and scaling.
3.6 Adonis Test
After the PCA and Umap analysis, we can now use the Adonis test to check the
significance of differences between groups and pairs inside PCA results, we use the
Adonis2 from package vegan to perform all Adonis tests. The Adonis test is performed
on the score matrix with different clinical variables as the grouping variables. In order
to explore the detailed pair separations, pairwise-Adonis2 from package ‘pairwise-
Adonis’ is used to perform the Adonis2 test on detailed pairs for finding certain groups
separate (p ⩽0.05) and significantly separate (p ⩽0.01).
3.7 Analysis TcR Diversity using SDV approach
The forward scores of the pHMMs will be affected by the diversity of sequences during
the training as described in Appendix C. We can try to use this property of pHMMs
to quantify the diversity of immune repertoire by calculating the standard deviation
(SDV) of forward scores for the sequences that belong to the model. If the model is
trained on more diverse sequences, the SDV of scores for new sequences will be lower,
and vice versa.

5 Discussion
In this project, we set out to investigate if we are able to use probabilistic models like
pHMMs to compress the immune repertoire and observe clinical variable separation
in the PCA space, we also explored possible approach to quantify TcR diversity using
pHMMs instead of single one-number diversity metrics.
5.1 PCA and Umap Analysis Results
The PCA analysis results in the Reuben dataset and the Yusko Dataset shows strong
tissue separation between PBMC and other tissues (p = 0.001). In the Reuben dataset,
we explored two different filtering methods, singleton and 2.5%, all of these two filters
are applied based on the observations of significant differences between unfiltered and
filtered PBMC samples in the PCA analysis.
The PCA analysis of the singleton filter demonstrates that the pHMMs can effectively
capture individual differences while maintaining the ability to distinguish PBMC tis-
sues from other tissue types. On the other hand, the results of the 2.5% filter reveal
that the pHMMs can capture the similarities in the immune repertoire across different
individuals while still preserving the ability to distinguish PBMC tissues from other
tissues.
The UMAP analysis for the singleton filter does not show a visible cluster of filtered
PBMC samples, which is expected and align with the PCA result, supporting the
hypothesis that the singleton filter amplifies individual immune profiles, leading to
limited similarity between samples.
In contrast, the UMAP analysis for the 2.5% filtered PBMC in Reuben Dataset shows
a distinct cluster of PBMC samples, consistent with the clear and separate clusters
observed in the corresponding PCA result. This indicates that the samples after 2.5%
filtering show similarity within their group while remaining vastly different from other
tissues.
Tissue wise, the Adonis test results show the PBMC samples are significantly sepa-
rated from Control, Tumor, NAT and LN in the Reuben dataset using singleton filter
(p = 0.001, F > 5.15, R2 > 0.02) but not between COPD and PBMC (p = 0.183).
The 2.5% filtered PBMC samples are significantly separated from all other samples
(p = 0.001, F > 45, R2 > 0.25). Yusko dataset using singleton filter applied to PBMC
samples also shows significant differences between Tumor and PBMC samples (p =
0.001). These results are consistent with the PCA and Umap results, showing that
the workflow of using pHMMs to compress the immune repertoire of each sample is
feasible and there are significant difference between filtered PBMC and other tissues
except with COPD group using singleton filter, the difference between PBMC and
other tissues align with current research [46].
For tissues other than PBMC, significant differences between groups (p = 0.009) are
observed in the Reuben dataset. However, when examining detailed pairwise com-
parisons, most pairs, despite having low value of p (0.009 ≤p ≤0.038) indicating
significant separations, are considered invalid due to extremely low value in both F

(3.1 ≤F ≤5.5) and R2 (0.019 ≤R2 ≤0.038), which could suggest a risk of false
positives.
The only two pairs with higher R2 = 0.149 or higher F > 5.0 and low p2.5 ≤
0.073, psingle ≤0.038 are the LN−COPD and NAT−Control group, between these
two groups, NAT−Control group has lower p value (p2.5 ≤0.010, psingle ≤0.009).
Due to the random nature of Adonis test for each test, the same pair may show
different p result between runs. The p value of LN−COPD group is above significant
threshold (p = 0.073 > 0.05) in the 2.5% filtered analysis but in the singleton filter
the p value is below threshold (p = 0.038 < 0.05), thus we still consider this pair a
valid separation.
The separation between NAT and Control samples is particularly interesting. Accord-
ing to D. Aran [47], NAT in NSCLC patients is biologically distinct from both healthy
tissues (Control) and Tumor tissues, however, our analysis only shows a significant
difference between NAT and Control. In contrast, the differences between Tumor and
Control and NAT and Tumor are less clear, with low F-values, R2, and borderline
p-values (0.056 ≤p ≤0.079). While these p are close to the threshold of signifi-
cance, they do not provide strong evidence of separation. This result suggests that
the current workflow has some capacity to distinguish tissue types other than PBMC,
particularly between NAT and Control, but may require optimization to amplify the
differences, such as those between Tumor and NAT.
To summarize the analysis result for Tissues besides PBMC, these results indicate
that the pHMMs are capturing some differences in the immune repertoire between
tissues but are unable to amplify these signals effectively, potentially due to noise or
limitations of the model. As a purely mathematical EM-based model, pHMMs lack the
ability to capture the physical structural relationships within CDR3b sequences like
deep learning methods. Besides, the discrete nature of pHMMs states further limits
their capacity to represent the overall complexity and continuity of CDR3b sequence
patterns.
For other clinical variables, the PCA result of Reuben dataset does not show significant
separation between variables of age group, sex, cancer stage and cancer type (p >
0.1), this indicates the current workflow is not sensitive enough to distinguish these
clinical differences alone, but for these clinical variables, they separate from the non-
cancerous group in the pair-Adonis test (Control and COPD, p = 0.004 −0.008),
which indicates significant difference between cancerous and non-cancerous samples.
Besides, the PCA result of Yusko dataset shows separation to certain degree (p =
0.047) between different states of the patient but due to low F-value and R2, together
with the PCA plot, the separation is considered as false positive. This result in Yusko
dataset reinforces the conclusion that current workflow is not sufficient to distinguish
clinical variable. No differences observed in the PCA analysis result for PBMC samples
along using both filters.

5.2 Findings in HC dataset projected back to Reuben dataset
The 10 closest neighbour analysis for the HC dataset primarily consists of PBMC
samples, with some NAT and Tumor samples. This result is expected, as the HC
dataset originates from PBL samples which is a subset of PBMC, and the same sin-
gleton filter was applied to both datasets. This finding suggests that even when using
an individual-focused filter, PBMC tissues retain distinct immune profiles compare
to other tissues, resulting in PBMC samples being the majority of the closest neigh-
bours. This result shows the potential of projecting new samples using the pHMMs
and rotation matrix, and by analysing their closest neighbours, it is possible to identify
whether the samples are PBMC tissues.
The 2.5% filter may perform better than the singleton filter as they have the tendency
to form clusters outside other tissues, but this remains to be verified due to the time
limitation.
5.3 Findings in SDV Approach
The SDV approach originates from the experiment on simulated dataset as explained
before and shown in Appendix C. If the model is trained on highly diverse sequences,
it will be less sensitive to sequences, losing its ability to recognize if the sequences are
generated by the model, as now the model has become a generic model for CDR3b
sequences. On the other hand, if the model is trained on highly clonal sequences, it
will be more sensitive to sequences, and the sequences will get varied forward scores
compared to the highly diverse models.
This property of pHMMs brings new ideas, since the forward score distribution is
impacted by the sequence diversity, and in many cases, noticeable impact, we can
use the forward score distribution to explore the sequence diversity of the immune
repertoire. Usually, the sequence diversity is calculated by simple one-number diversity
metrics like the Shannon Entropy. However, the Shannon Entropy or Shannon index
has many limitations, for example, as shown in Table 9, the Shannon index failed
to rank the diversity of HighDiversity and SingleClonal datasets, they have a higher
Shannon index compared to less diverse datasets.
We calculated the Standard Deviation of the forward scores (SDV) for each dataset,
the more diverse the dataset is, scores should be more similar and SDV of scores
will be lower. The results are shown in Table 9. Compared to the Shannon index,
in the simulate dataset, the SDV approach shows the diversity rank correctly, this
approach is just to demonstrate that the model can be used to quantify the diversity of
sequences in a more sensitive way compared to Shannon index. However, considering
the training time of the model, it is not practical to use the forward score distribution
to quantify the diversity of the immune repertoires if they are not compressed into
pHMMs in advance.
Additionally, the SDV approach result shown in Fig 20 indicate the PBMC has lowest
SDV and highest TcR diversity, Tumor has higher TcR diversity compare to NAT,
49
==PAGE:50==
which align with current study on the diversity between PBMC and other tissues [48,
49].
The shared pattern of SDV of starting at low SDV and increase with age till age of
60−70 can be explained by the immune ageing theory, as the immune system will
become less diverse with age [50]. The pattern start decrease at oldest-old age group
can be explained by the Elite immunity theory which indicates human with extreme
longevity may still remain highly functional immune system at extreme old age [51].

6 Conclusion
This study demonstrates the feasibility of using profile Hidden Markov Models
(pHMMs), to analyse and compress T-cell immune repertoire data from cancer
patients. By using the pHMMs, we were able to identify significant tissue-level sepa-
rations between filtered PBMC and other tissue types using two filters, one focus on
individual immune profile while another focus on common immune profile, as reflected
in both PCA and UMAP analyses. The strong statistical support (Adonis p = 0.001)
for these findings and the successful projection using rotation matrix to project the HC
dataset back to PCA result reinforce the potential of pHMMs in capturing immune
repertoire differences between PBMC and other Tissues. Borderline results observed
in other tissues like NAT and Control indicates the models have the ability to distin-
guish other tissues besides PBMC, however, the current workflow is not sensitive to
express significant difference (p > 0.05).
However, the current workflow showed limitations in distinguishing finer clinical vari-
ables, such as age, sex, cancer stages and types within PCA spaces (p > 0.099). These
results show that while pHMMs are effective in identifying broader patterns between
filtered PBMC and other tissues, their mathematical structure may limit their abil-
ity to detect separation between clinical variations. Future enhancements with more
advanced models, such as dynamic Bayesian network[52], or train the model on a
homogenous large dataset may enhance the capability of this approach.
Furthermore, the exploration of simulated datasets revealed a direct correlation
between forward score distributions and sequence diversity, suggesting that the for-
ward score can be considered as a proxy for immune diversity analysis. The standard
deviation of forward scores approach (SDV) offered a new perspective, which requires
future verification but in the Reuben dataset the results aligned with existing theories
on immune ageing and diversity dynamics.
In conclusion, this study proves it is feasible to use pHMMs to compress immune reper-
toire and demonstrates the ability of pHMMs in capturing immune repertoire diversity
and tissue-level separation in cancer patients. However, this is only a feasibility Study,
further optimization and validation are needed to fully understand the potential of
pHMMs in immune repertoire analysis. The current workflow is not suitable for real
world application due to the limitation of the model or data processing method, but
it is a good start for exploring the potential of pHMMs in immune repertoire analysis.

7 Future Perspectives
In this project, there are several future perspectives that can be considered:
Dynamic Bayesian network (DBN) is a generalization of a hidden Markov model
(HMM) that can model dynamic systems. It is a Bayesian network that relates vari-
ables to each other over adjacent time steps. Unlike pHMMs, the DBN can capture
the relationship between different HMM modules which may increase the performance
of the model.
In this thesis project, The pHMMs model, Rotation matrix projection and SDV
approach can be further verified by more datasets using both filters, especially the
datasets with more samples inside each tissue type. This will help to understand the
clinical variable separation in the PCA space.
The current workflow is sufficient to distinguish filtered PBMC from other tissues
but is insufficient for differentiating other clinical variables such as age, sex, or cancer
stage. Further optimization, including more sequences inside the sample and better
filtering or other data processing techniques, could enhance its ability to capture
differences within various tissues. While trends of separation among other tissues are
evident, the current workflow fails to fully capture these distinctions.
