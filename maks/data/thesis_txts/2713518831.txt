Abstract  
English 
This thesis is a sub-project of a project with the purpose of removing the need for emulsifying salts 
in the production of cheese powder and thereby making cheese powder a potential clean label 
alternative to cheese powders with artificial emulsifiers and texture modifiers. 
In this sub-project the adjustment of pH effect on a dispersion of cheese powder was studied. The 
dispersion of cheese powder was used as a simulant for the cheese feed used to produce cheese 
powder. Five pH levels (7.0, 6.01 (no adjustment), 5.5, 5.0, and 4.5) were evaluated in regard to their 
stability, particle size distribution, rheological properties, distribution of dry-matter, protein, fats, 
and calcium in the dispersion. CLSM was used to confirm the particle size distribution and 
interactions between the fat and protein of the dispersions. 
It was confirmed that the dispersions’ stability, particle size distribution, rheological properties, 
distribution of dry-matter, protein, fats, and calcium could be modified by adjusting the pH of the 
dispersion. In addition, it was confirmed that there is a correlation between the dispersion’s 
stability, the size of the protein aggregates, and the state of calcium in the dispersion.  
pH 5.0 was regarded as an outlier because it had unexpected extreme results across all analyses. It 
was proposed that this was caused by still active rennet in the cheese powder.

Introduction 
Clean label is a trend which has been introduced during the twenty-tens, where consumer 
preferences have shifted away from some commonly used food ingredients. Especially those which 
have been artificially produced such as artificial colors, flavorings, sweeteners, and emulsifier, and 
ingredients with chemical sounding names have been described as unwanted[1]. In this study the 
consumers preferences towards four commonly used stabilizers (pectin, corn starch, Carrageenan, 
and milk protein concentrate) have been evaluated in the form of plain yogurt. The results show that 
consumers were willing to pay 3.13 $ - 4.35 $1 for a clean label plain yogurt (32oz.) and clean label 
also increased the consumers’ willingness to accept textural defects. The price for a non-clean label 
yogurt (32 oz.)  is 2.36$ (Walmart). This shows that consumers are willing to pay more for a clean 
label product (up to 84%). This is also supported by a survey (n=1300) commissioned by the PR 
agency Ingredients Communications[2]. The survey showed that 52% of the participants said they 
were willing to pay 10% for more extra for a clean label product, 18% said they would pay 75% or 
more for a clean label product.  
To accommodate the clean label trend, the food industries are trying to reformulate their products 
to become clean label by removing and replacing food additives. Cheese powder can used to replace 
some of these additives because the composition of cheese powder ensure texture-modifying and 
emulsifying properties [3]. Cheese powder is produced from a mixture of ground cheeses, water and 
emulsifying salts which are heated and homogenized into a uniform dispersion called cheese feed, 
which is then spray dried into the cheese powder.  The emulsifying salts are added to ensure that 
the cheese feed will not separate before spray drying. But emulsifying salt cannot be categorized as 
clean label which hinders the use of cheese powder as a replacement for the unwanted additives. 
Cheese powders potential as a replacement for the unwanted additives establish incentive to 
removal emulsifying salts from cheese production, and to do this knowledge of how cheese feeds 
physico-chemical properties are affected by different influences.  
In this project, we are trying to determine how cheese feed would be affected by change in pH level 
This is done with a reverse engineering approach, where a suspension of cheese powder in 
demineralized water is prepared to simulate the behavior of cheese feed to an extent were the 
results from this study can be used to establish some possible guidelines for where the next steps of 
research should be focused.  
 
Hypotheses  
To conduct this study 3 hypotheses have been proposed: 
I. 
Stability of the cheese powder dispersions depends on the protein aggregation size 
distribution. 
II. 
Protein aggregates’ size distribution is affected by calcium’s status in cheese powder 
dispersions. 
III. 
Modification of stability, rheology, and microstructure of hetero-aggregated cheese 
powder dispersion is possible by pH adjustment.

Theory  
Milk proteins and their structure 
The proteins in milk are divided into two groups based on the solubility at pH 4,6 at 20℃. The 
soluble proteins are called whey proteins because they stay dissolved in the whey during the 
coagulation step of cheese making. The protein which precipitates at pH 4,6 (the isoelectric point) 
are caseins which make up the protein network of cheese.  
Bovine caseins are consisting of 4 different proteins,  𝜅-casein Β-casein and the two 𝛼- casein S1 and 
S2. In milk, the proteins are bound into a spherical structure called micelles[4].  
According to the Dalgiesh Model[5]  the 𝛼-caseins and the Β-casein are bound to calcium phosphate 
clusters (can be replaced by calcium ions) creating a network which populate the insides of the 
micelles. The surface of the micelle is loosely covered by Κ-casein which gives the micelles stability 
towards aggregation.  The stability of the micelles are caused by two factors: First is the micelle 
having a low negative charges (ζ-potential ~−20 mV at pH 6,7) which presumably is not enough to 
avoid aggregation, the other and most likely dominating factor for the stability of the micelles are 
the steric stabilization created by the tail of the Κ-casein pointing away from the micelle which is 
composed of enough hydrophilic amino acids that it can interact with a layer of water around the 
micelles (5-10 nm).

The stepwise production of cheese 
Cheese is produced [4] from milk which traditionally have come from a range of mammals. The most 
common are sheep, goat, buffalo, and bovine (cow). The production of most cheese is done in large 
industrial dairies, where the process has been standardized. 
Standardization of milk 
Raw milk is received and then pasteurized to eliminate possible spoiling by the microflora naturally 
present in the raw milk. Next the milk is standardized in regard to its content of fat, protein and 
calcium content. This is done by either skimming the milk or adding cream, the protein content is 
often increased by adding a milk concentrate made by ultrafiltration, and the calcium content is 
increased by addition of calcium chloride (𝐶𝑎𝐶𝑙ଶ).  
Acidification of the milk 
The now standardized milk moves into the start of the cheesemaking process. 
The first step is called acidification, and can be done in two ways: 
First is direct acidification where acid (lactic, citric or 𝐻𝐶𝑙 are some of the acids used) is directly 
mixed into the milk reducing the pH to 4,6. the iso electric point of caseins will then start to 
aggregate and form into a gel called coagulum. Sometimes direct acidification is done in 
combination with heat treatment up to 80℃. The method of direct acidification is mostly used to 
make fresh cheeses like cottage cheese, cream cheese, ricotta, paneer, and mascarpone[6].  
The second method of acidification is indirect acidification were a lactic acid producing culture is 
mixed into the milk and it is left to ferment until the desired pH is reached (5,2 for rennet 
coagulation). The method of indirect acidification is mostly used on cheese where rennet will be 
used for the coagulation of cheeses which must ripen. This is because the cultures added are also 
producing flavor compounds which can be developed further during ripening. Cheese in this group is 
cheddar, camembert, brie, stilton, dana blue, comté.  
The acidification step also has an effect on the final rheological properties of the cheese because the 
reduction of the pH in the milk increases the solubility of calcium phosphate (fully soluble at pH 5,2). 
The effect of calcium on the rheological properties were studied by Ayala-Bribiesca E (2016)[7] and it 
was determined that with increasing calcium content the cheese would become harder (due to 
moisture loss) and that the cheese would become slightly more springy at ultra-high levels of 
calcium.  
Coagulation of the casein  
After the acidification the most characteristic step of cheese making is reached. This is the 
coagulation step where caseins aggregate and are structured into a gel state called the coagulum 
where fat globules and whey are entrapped. The first method direct acidification is described 
above[8].  
The other method is a two-step process where the surface structure of the micelles are broken. In 
more detail the selected proteinases called rennet are mixed into the acidified milk. The proteinases 
are able to hydrolyze the hydrophilic end of the Κ- casein resulting in that small chain of peptide, 
which dissolves into the whey, are cut off the protein and thereby are the steric stabilization provide 
by the Κ −𝑐𝑎𝑠𝑒𝑖𝑛 broken. The Micelles will now start to aggregate and binds in to a three 
dimensional network which are the coagulum. But this formation is dependent on the presence of at 
critical amount of 𝐶𝑎ଶା, as it works as a tether between the hydrolyzed micelles. 𝐶𝑎ଶା can be 
replaced by colloidal calcium phosphate[9]. 
After the milk has been turned in to coagulum it is cut in to smaller pieces called curds. This starts a 
process called syneresis[10] where the whey, entrapped during the coagulation, is expressed out of

The final steps of cheese production[4] 
The final steps of cheese making are the salting and ripening, where the curds are mixed with salt. 
This is done to control moisture content and to preserve the cheese. The last step of cheese 
production is the ripening period which can range from 3 weeks to above 2 years. Here the cheese 
can go through significant changes due to moisture loss and growth of microflora in and on the 
cheese[4]. These steps are not described further because it is not within the scope of this project.

Cheese powder productions[12] 
Cheese powder is produced by spray drying a mixture of cheese, water, and emulsifying salts (ES) A 
flowchart of the process is shown in Figure 4. This mixture is called the cheese feed and the majority 
of the challenges faced in the production of cheese powder is during the production and handling of 
the cheese feed.   
 
 
Figure 4 simplified flow chart of the production of cheese powder[12]. 
Cheese feed is normally produced by grinding cheese and mixing it with water and emulsifying salt, 
where after the mixture are heated to 60℃ and held for up to an hour before further heat 
treatment, homogenization and finally spray drying.  Emulsifying salts are added to stabilize the 
cheese feed during the holding time. Otherwise the cheese feed will start to separate into layers of 
oil, water, and protein. The stabilization is done by emulsifying salts’ ability to bind calcium and 
thereby promoting the emulsification of water and the cheese fat by the caseins dissociated from 
the micelle. In more detail, the 𝐶𝑎ଶା, linking the casein together in the cheese matrix, exchanged by 
𝑁𝑎ା form the ES. This ion exchange breaks the casein network, and a more open and reactive 
configuration of the casein is created.  
This new configuration of the casein has improved water binding and emulsifying abilities because 
this configuration is more open and thereby the more hydrophilic and lipophilic end of the casein is 
exposed.  
This thesis is part of a project where the overall focus is to remove the ES from the production of the 
cheese powder and thereby creating a clean label cheese powder. 
Emulsions and how to measure the stability.   
In the food industry are oil in water(O/W) and water in oil (W/O) emulsions/dispersions some of the 
most common food mediums. They are prepared by inducing very high shear on a mixture of water 
and fats, forcing the dispersed phase into small droplets surrounded by the continuous phase.  But 
due to the different polarity of oil and water, the emulsion will quickly start to separate and it is 
therefore unstable.  
To combat this instability stabilizing/emulsifying agents are added to the oil and water mixture. 
 
Some of the most used emulsifiers are polysaccharides and proteins.  
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
Most of the polysaccharides stabilize the continuous phase by gelation or by increasing the viscosity 
and thereby immobilize the droplets of the dispersed phase[13].  
The stabilizing effect of protein comes from it having ends with different functionalities[14].  
The lipophilic tail is soluble in oil and the hydrophilic head is soluble in water, and therefore  the 
droplets will be covered by proteins interacting with both the continuous phase and the dispersed 
phase, keeping the emulsion stable as showed in Figure 5.  
 
Figure 5 Schematic representation of droplets presented in water-on-oil (A) and oil-on-water (B) microemulsion stabilized 
with protein[15].  
In this project the stability of the dispersions was measured with the Turbiscan Tower made by 
Formulaction, which is based on Static multiple light scattering technology[16].  
A NIR light source (880 nm) sends light into the sample where the light is scattered. The light then 
emerges from the sample and is detected by two detectors; one at 0° measuring the amount of light 
going through the sample and another detector at 135° measuring the light being cast back by the 
scattering. These two detectors measures the light output as a percentage of the light input which 
give the desired results. An example of the delta back scatter stability curve shown below in Figure 6.

The size of cheese particles and how to measure them   
Cheese is a protein gel where fat globules have been entrapped, but in camembert cheese the fat 
and protein only contribute with 44,8 % of the cheese mass the main component of camembert 
cheese is water (50,7%). The last 4,5% consist of salt, calcium, and ash.  
All these components of the cheese have a size, and some of the components occur in different 
forms with different sizes depending on the type and production method of the cheese.  
Figure 7 shows the size range of these components.

D [3,2] is the Surface Weighted Mean, also known as the Surface Area Moment Mean 
Diameter or Sauter mean. 
- 
The Span is the measurement of the width of the distribution. The narrower the distribution, 
the smaller the span becomes 
- 
Uniformity is a measure of the absolute deviation from the median. 
 
Method and materials 
Experimental design 
Five dispersions of cheese powder in water are prepared. Four of the dispersions will get their pH 
adjusted to one of the following pH levels: pH 7.0, pH 5.5, pH 5.0, and pH 4.5. The remaining 
dispersion will be left untreated (pH 6,1). 
The samples are then homogenized and analyzed for these parameters: stability, particle size, and 
viscosity. Hereafter the dispersions are separated in to three phases (top layer, water phase and 
sediment) by centrifugation. Each of the phases are then weighed and freeze dried. The now dry 
phases are then analyzed for the following:  
- 
Total fat content 
- 
Total protein content  
- 
Total calcium content  
Figure 9 shows a flow chart of the experimental design. It also shows which analysis have been 
conducted of which phase, and the results of the other phases have then been calculated based on 
the measured results and the knowledge of the total content in the cheese powder.

Methods 
Physical stability 
Stability of the dispersion was measured with the Turbiscan Tower from Formulaction (France, 
Toulouse). The measurement was conducted by loading a sample vial with 20mL of the dispersion. 
which was placed in the equipment. Scans were conducted every 2 minutes for 3 hours at 30℃  
Particle measurement 
The particle size of the samples was measured with the Mastersizer 2000 from Malvern Panalytical 
(Great Britain, Malvern). The samples were measured by injection of sample to a laser obstruction 
level between 10% to 20% after which the samples were measured. A refractive index of 1.469 
(sunflower oil) was used. Sample values for number-based was created using the Mastersizer 2000 
software.    
Viscosity measurement  
The dispersion viscosity was measured with the Discovery HR-2 form TA-instruments (USA, New 
Castle). The analysis was conducted as a flow sweep with a shear rate range of 200 1/s - 0.1 1/s 
The total protein contents 
The crude protein content was determined by measurement of total nitrogen content of a sample 
with the Dumas combustion method, where a sample is combusted at 800℃ and the nitrogen is 
quantitatively converted in to 𝑁2 gas which is then measured by a thermal conductivity detector. 
The total nitrogen content can then be converted into the crude protein content with a Nitrogen-
protein conversion factor for this project the general factor for dairy 6.38 was used [19]. 
Homogenization with piston homogenizer  
The dispersion was poured into the sample funnel and the homogenizer was started and set to the 
required pressure by turning a valve handle. This was then repeated this was repeated twice for the 
final dispersion.  
Between samples the homogenizer was rinsed by running demineralized water through it. This was 
not done between repetitions of the same sample.  
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
The piston homogenizer used is the Rannie: High pressure laboratory homogenizer model: Lab-type 
8.30 H from APV Rannie A/S (Denmark, Albertslund). The company was closed first of May 1988.   
 
Phase separation 
The dispersion was separated into phases using the 4-16Ks centrifuge from Sigma Laborzentrifugen 
(Germany, Osterode am Harz). The dispersion was centrifugated at 5000G for 1 hour at 40℃.  
Fat measurement 
Fat content of the samples were determined with the combined Smart 6 microwave and Infrared 
moisture & solids analyzer, and the ORACLE Rapid NMR Fat Analyzer setup form CEM Cooperation 
(Charlotte, North Carolina, US). 
Calcium measurement 
Calcium was measured with ICPMS This method was created internally at DTU, a description of the 
method is attached to this report together with equipment SOPs (see appendix A). 
Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy 
The dispersions microstructure was evaluated with CLSM which was conducted using the same 
method as described by [20] 
Development of sample preparation method 
To be able to conduct the analyses a sample need to be prepared. The dispersion is comprised of a 
mixture of cheese powder and water combined into a dispersion. To ensure that the analysis of the 
sample was representative, some requirements for the dispersion was needed, the requirements are 
the following:  
- 
The untreated sample needs to be stable for 15 minutes, to be able to handle the sample 
and to ensure the representative measurement of viscosity.  
- 
The particle size of the sample should be small to verify the proper dispersion of the cheese 
powder into the water and that the sample has been properly homogenized.  
Seven experiments were set up to find the most optimal dispersion preparation method. They are 
the following:  
- 
Hydration time.  
- 
Premixing of dispersion. 
- 
Type of homogenizer. 
- 
Preheating of the dispersion. 
- 
Concentration of cheese powder in the dispersion.  
- 
Homogenization pressure. 
- 
Homogenization repetitions.  
All analysis done for the development of the sample preparation method were done on duplicates. 
Notice that the scale of the X-axis (Δ𝐵𝑆%) changes between the different experiments, this was 
done to make the figures readable.

Premixing method 
Three different methods of mixing the cheese powder into water were tested.   
No premixing: The water and cheese powder were combined and shortly stirred with a spoon to mix 
the two.  
Magnetic stirrer: The cheese powder was combined with the water gradually, while being stirred at 
600-800 RPM on a magnetic stirring plate. After initial mixing the dispersion was left for 2 hours on 
the magnetic stirring plate to ensure complete dispersion of the cheese powder.   
Stand mixer: The cheese powder water was combined and mixed for 2 times, 5 minutes with a 2-
minute break in between, at max speed.

The sample preparation method 
A dispersion of demineralized water and cheese powder was mixed to a concentration of 17.5%. the 
Dispersion were stirred for 2 hours at 600-800 RPM on a magnetic stirring plate, and then the 
dispersion was left to hydrate overnight.  
Before homogenization the dispersion was stirred again, while it was heated up to 60℃ and held for 
10 minutes. Thereafter the dispersion was homogenized twice at 400 bars of pressure, and then the 
dispersion was ready for analysis.  
The pH adjustments were done just before the homogenization.

Results of the rheology analysis  
In Figure 33 the viscosity of all dispersions are shown (figure 6 B shows a close up of pH 7.0, pH 6.1 
and 5.5). The highest viscosity is measured for pH 5.0 (23.0 cP at highest shear rate) followed by pH 
4.5 (5.4 cP at highest shear rate). 
For the pH groups, showed in Figure 34, the differences in viscosity are not as pronounced. pH 5.5 
has a viscosity of 2.9 cP with a SD of 0.59 cP, pH 6.1 has a viscosity of 2.5 cP SD:0,2 and pH 7.0 
viscosity is 2.7 cP : 0.2. This small increase in viscosity of the pH 5.5 and the relatively large SD, 
points to the calcium starting to dissolve into the aqueous phase which then releases casein from 
the network they were bound in. This is also confirmed by [24] where it was concluded that in the 
pH range of 5,3 to 5,6 the largest percentage of the total casein were dissociated form the micelles 
(the dissociation were temperature dependent 4℃: 56%, 20℃: 31%, 30℃ :9%). These dissociated 
caseins are soluble in the dispersion where they can function as emulsifiers, which results in the 
increase in viscosity. At pH 4.5 the caseins are close to their isoelectric point where casein does not 
have any charge, resulting in the casein starting to aggregate creating a gel structure which has an 
increased viscosity. This presence of gel structure is also confirmed by the viscosity measurement 
because it shows shear thinning behavior which is also seen in casein gels such as yogurt or cream 
cheese. For pH 5.0 the increased viscosity could be caused by the still active rennet[23] in the cheese 
powder which creates a strong network of protein like the one found in rennet coagulated cheese, 
which result in the higher viscosity.

Discussion of the hypotheses 
In several of the analyses, the pH 5.0 dispersion had unexpected results which were often in the 
extreme end of the result measured: It was the most unstable of the dispersions, it had the highest 
viscosity, the largest particle size, it had a decrease in the amount of calcium in the water phase. It 
was proposed based on this, that it was caused by the presence of still active rennet in the cheese 
powder[23], which is indirectly supported by several of the results above.   
For the calcium measurement a decrease of 45% of the total calcium present in the water phase was 
seen when compared with PH 6.1. This resembles what is seen during the coagulation step of 
cheesemaking, where the protein is bonded together with calcium into a gel structure entrapping 
fat.  
The entrapment of fat into the protein gel is supported by the results presented in Table 19 which 
show that 93.8% of the fat in the dispersion is present in the sediment together with the majority of 
the protein (68.8%). The connection between protein and fat is also confirmed by the CLSM images. 
The stability data of pH 5.0 points toward the formation of a semi-stable gel. This gel is seen a semi 
stable zone from 19 mm to 38mm. Over time a slight increase in ΔBS(%) is seen, which is caused by 
the gel becoming denser, due to the expulsion of water from the gel, a process also known from 
cheesemaking where it is called syneresis.  
Based on this the results from pH 5.0 are regarded as outliers.  
Hypothesis I 
The first hypothesis states: 
Stability of the cheese powder dispersion depends on the protein aggregation size 
distribution. 
The first hypothesis can be confirmed based on the comparison of the stability curves and the 
measured particle size distribution. The stability of the dispersion increases when the pH level is 
decreased to 5.5. This is not directly seen in the size distribution results (median of the percentiles), 
but the changes can be seen on the standard deviation of pH 5.5, which increases across all 
percentiles when compared to pH 6.1, d(0.1) increase of 650%, d(0.5) increase of 571%,  and 
d(0.9)increase of 238%. This increase in standard deviation points towards that a tiny part of the 
proteins in the dispersion are starting to aggregate. This is not supported by the CLSM results, where 
no apparent differences are seen between pH 5.5 and pH 6.1 most likely due the small area of the 
dispersions depicted in Figure 35.  
When the pH level is decreased further the stability of the dispersion sharply drops and the particle 
size increases, but the lowest stability and highest particle size distribution are not obtained by pH 
4.5 as could be assumed, as it is very close to the isoelectric point of casein where the casein should 
aggregate and precipitate. The lowest stability is seen for the pH 5.0 together with the largest 
particle size distribution for all percentiles, (for d(0.9), the medians of pH 4.5 and 5.0 are overlapping 
when taking standard deviation in to account). It is theorized that this is due to still active rennet 
present is in the cheese powder, which results in the increased size distribution of the protein 
aggregates[23].   
 
 
==PAGE:46==
38 
 
Hypothesis II 
The second hypothesis states: 
Protein aggregates’ size distribution is affected by calcium’s status in cheese powder 
dispersions. 
 
The result of the calcium measurements shows how calcium become increasingly soluble with the 
decrease of pH. For pH 5.5 this is shown by the increase in the standard deviation from 1.4% for pH 
6.1 to 16.34% for pH 5.5.. A similar trend is also seen for the results of the particle size distribution, 
and based on calcium’s role in binding caseins together[28] in cheese. It is proposed that by 
changing the solubility of calcium by adjusting pH, casein inclination to aggregate is affected and that 
the average size of these aggregates will increase with the percentage of total calcium dissolving in 
the water phase. And based on this the second hypothesis is confirmed, under the assumption that 
pH 5.0 is disregarded due to it being affected by still active rennet.  
Hypothesis III 
The third hypothesis states  
Modification of stability, rheology, and microstructure of hetero-aggregated cheese 
powder dispersion is possible by pH adjustment 
Based on the results presented above it has been shown that by adjusting pH of a dispersion of 
cheese powder, the stability of the dispersion can be both increased and decreased. Under 
hypothesis I it has been confirmed that the stability of a dispersion is correlated with the particle size 
distribution in dispersion. Under hypothesis II the particle size distribution in have been correlated 
with the state of calcium solubilization.  
It has also been proven that the viscosity of the dispersion can be increased by reducing pH, and that 
the distribution of dry-matter, protein and fat can be modified by adjusting the pH of the dispersion. 
And based on these results hypothesis III can be confirmed. 
Combined discussion of the hypotheses results and how it can be utilized in the 
production of cheese powder 
In the production of clean label cheese powder, there is a need for the cheese feed functioning as a 
stable emulsion, to ensure the production of a high-quality cheese powder with the desired 
functionality.  
The results presented in this thesis have shown that the stability of a cheese powder dispersion can 
be increased by reducing the pH of the dispersion to 5.5, and that if the pH is reduced any further 
the dispersions stability will be reduced to a level lower than a dispersion with no pH adjustment. 
Further experimental work have then been conducted to determine the main causes for this change 
in stability. 
 
It was discussed that the increased stability of the pH 5.5 dispersion was caused by a series of 
factors. The decrease of the pH resulted in the increased solubilization of the calcium present in the 
dispersion. This was, to a limited extent, confirmed by our results which showed a large variation in 
the possible content of calcium in the water phase of the pH 5.5 dispersion. The solubilization of the 
calcium, the casein micelle started to break down due to its role in the structure of the casein 
micelle[28], and an increased amount of casein disassociated from the micelle were present in the 
dispersion, which is in agreement with what was concluded by [24]. The disassociated casein could 
then start to function as emulsifiers in the dispersion, which is indirectly confirmed by the small 
increase of viscosity, and that the fat was rearranged from the top layer of the dispersion to the 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
sediment, together with the majority of the dispersion’s protein. The stability of the pH 5.5 also 
remained high because the casein did not to aggregate, which was confirmed by the CLSM images. 
 
Conclusion 
This thesis has shown that by adjusting the pH of a cheese powder dispersion the following 
characteristics of the dispersion can be modified:  
- 
Stability of the dispersion. 
- 
The particle distribution.  
- 
Rheology. 
- 
The microstructure of the dispersion. 
- 
The distribution of dry matter, protein, and fat between the phase layers. 
- 
The solubility of calcium in the dispersion.  
It has also been proven that there is a correlation between the dispersion’s stability, the particle size 
distribution and the percentage of calcium dissolved in the water phase of the dispersion.   
 
Decreasing pH level of the dispersion will result in the stability increasing until pH 5.5 is reached. If 
the pH is further decreased the stability will go down drastically, due to aggregation of casein and 
possibly due to active rennet in the pH 5.0 dispersion. The aggregation increases the particle size of 
the dispersion and is increased by 4756% for the d(0.1), 284% for the d(0.5) and 76.3%  for the d(0,9) 
when comparing volume-based percentiles of pH 6.1 and pH 4.5. 
The percentage of dry matter in the water phase were reduced by 48% (comparing pH 6.1 and pH 
4.5) due to the aggregation of casein which is confirmed by the increase of 176% for the percentage 
of protein in the sediment. The fat of the dispersion was also rearranged between the phases 
because the fat had started to bind to protein, which was confirmed by the reduction of 94% of the 
percentage of fat in the top layer, from pH 6.1 to pH 4.5. This was further confirmed by the increase 
of fat in the sediment (increased by 520% from pH 6.1 to pH 4.5).  
This connection between the fat and the protein was proposed to be caused by the solubilization of 
calcium, which is an integrated part of the casein micelle structure, and when it became soluble it 
would increase the amount of casein disassociated from the micelle, where the casein can function 
as emulsifiers. The solubilization was confirmed by an increase of 15 % of the percentage of calcium 
in the water phase.  
The connection between fat and protein was finally confirmed by CLSM of the dispersion, these 
images show increased interaction between protein and fat in the dispersion. 
Further work 
 As mentioned above there are challenges which must be addressed before a high quality clean-label 
cheese powder can be produced, but in addition to these challenges the possible presence and 
effects of rennet in the cheese powder should be studied. 
A life cycle analysis of the production of cheese powder with and without emulsifying salt should 
also be conducted and compared to ensure the sustainability of the production of clean label cheese 
powder.  
==PAGE:48==
40 
 
In the production of cheese powder these results can be used to point towards the main research 
challenges that have to be solved before a high-quality cheese powder can be produced.  
The challenges are the following: 
 
Calcium must be solubilized to ensure the increased presence of disassociated casein in the 
cheese feed.  
 
This solubilization of calcium may not affect the charge of the casein to an extent where 
they start to aggregate. 
 
The effect of rennet in cheese feed should be characterized. 
 
A life cycle analysis of the production of cheese powder with and without emulsifying salt 
should be conducted and compared to ensure the sustainability of the production of clean 
label cheese powder.
