Abstract 
The recognition of the immune system as an important player in the regulation of tumor 
outgrowth has led to the development of various anti-cancer immunotherapies. Most 
research within this area has been carried out in mouse models, but these are often bad 
predictors of the clinical outcome of immunotherapies in human. Due to the close 
resemblance of the porcine immunome to its human counterpart, the present study 
exploits the potential of the pig as a large animal model for adoptive T cell therapy 
(ACT), an immunotherapy showing promising results for the treatment of melanoma. 
The use of the pig as a model for ACT requires in vitro expansion porcine T cells, which 
are preferentially specific for tumor antigen and carry a young memory phenotype. 
Hence, this study set out to investigate if it was possible to polyclonally expand porcine 
T cells, and further if antigen-specific expansion could be obtained by co-culture with 
antigen and various cytokine combinations. Detection of porcine memory T cells by flow 
cytometry was enabled through the design of a suitable antibody panel. 
Using the polyclonal stimulant Con A, 6-fold expansion of porcine T cells during a 
culture period of 2 weeks was achieved. Compared to human T cells, which can be 
expanded up to 200-fold in the same time frame, the observed fold change was very low. 
Hence, the results suggest that in vitro culture of porcine T cells requires specific growth 
factors, which were not supplied by the human or murine T cell medium used in the 
present study. 
 
Antigen-specific expansion of porcine memory T cells was investigated using PBMCs 
from mini-pigs vaccinated with tetanus toxoid. Co-culture of these cells with tetanus 
toxoid and IL-2 or IL-2 + IL-15 led to expansion of a CD4-CD8αhiCD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ 
subset, which is thought to represent human CD8+ TEMRA cells. The results of the study 
implicated that CD8αhiCD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ T cells are not terminally differentiated, 
questioning whether this subset actually corresponds to TEMRA cells.  
 
Currently, porcine central and effector memory CD4+ T cells are thought to be 
CD8α+CD27+ and CD8α+CD27-, respectively. In this study, stimulation of porcine T cells 
with Con A shifted the phenotype of porcine T helper cells toward a central memory 
phenotype. As Con A is a strong T cell stimulant, this finding strongly questions the use 
of CD27 as an identification marker of porcine memory T helper subsets.  
Overall, the results from the present study demonstrates that there are still major 
obstacles to be overcome before the potential of the pig as a model for ACT can be fully 
exploited. These include the development of a complete immunological tool box, further 
elucidation of the phenotypical characteristics of different porcine T cell subsets, but 
most importantly the establishment of optimal culture conditions for porcine T cells.

1 Introduction 
Adoptive T cell therapy (ACT) is a personalized cancer treatment which harnesses the 
patient’s own T cell response to destroy cancer cells. So far, ACT has shown to promote 
complete relapse-free regression in cases of metastatic melanoma [1], and the use of the 
therapy for treatment of other cancer types is further being investigated [2]–[5]. Most of 
the research within ACT and other cancer immunotherapies have been carried out using 
mouse models. However, mouse models are bad predictors of successful clinical 
outcomes of immunotherapies in humans [6]–[8]. In order to optimize and exploit the 
full potential of ACT, there is a need for a relevant supplementary animal model, and 
since the porcine immunome closely resembles its human counterpart [9], [10], the aim 
of this study was to investigate the potential of the pig as a large animal model for ACT. 
To use the pig as an animal model for ACT, in vitro expansion of porcine T cells is 
necessary. The expanded T cells should preferentially be specific for tumor antigen and 
carry a memory phenotype. For human T cells, it is possible to carry out a 200-fold T cell 
expansion after polyclonal stimulation and two weeks culture [11]. Furthermore, human 
T cell culture with different cytokines such as IL-7, IL-12 and IL-21 has shown to direct 
differentiation of T cells into memory phenotypes in vitro [12]–[14]. Based on these 
results, I set out to test if similar results could be obtained for porcine T cells.  
From December 2015 to March 2016, I carried out a pre-project entitled “Non-Specific 
Growth Factors for Proliferation of Swine T Cells” [15], which sought to establish 
optimal culture conditions for polyclonal expansion of porcine T cells. From the 
beginning, in vitro culture of porcine T cells proved extremely challenging [15]. Initial 
experiments were set up with cryopreserved porcine peripheral blood mononuclear cells 
(PBMCs), and culture of these with various polyclonal stimulants such as concanavalin 
A (Con A) and phytohaemagglutinin (PHA) repeatedly resulted in poor cell viability 
and no overall T cell expansion. Different parameters were changed in an attempt to 
improve cell survival, including the concentration of polyclonal stimulant, the use of 
porcine serum instead of fetal bovine serum (FBS) and the cell seeding density. 
However, none of these changes contributed significantly to better cell survival, and I 
therefore speculated if the freezing/thawing step was causing the problems. Hence, I 
started to use freshly purified PBMCs instead of cryopreserved PBMCs. Although this 
improved cell viability slightly, immense cell death was still observed. Around this time, 
our group realized that the FBS we had been using for all our experiments was expired. 
The present study is a continuation of my pre-project. The aim of the study was to 
exploit the potential of the pig as a large animal model for ACT. For this purpose, 
PBMCs from mini-pigs immunized with a cancer peptide vaccine as well as tetanus 
toxoid were used. Due to relatively weak responses mounted by the cancer vaccine [16], 
==PAGE:16==

antigen-specific expansion of porcine T cells is here being investigated using tetanus 
toxoid as a stimulant for in vitro culture. Based on results found for human T cell 
culturing, I hypothesized that using a fresh FBS batch it was possible to: 
1. Polyclonally expand porcine T cells up to 200-fold in vitro during a culturing period 
of two weeks  
2. Skew the phenotype of tetanus toxoid specific porcine T cells toward a memory 
phenotype in vitro  
3. Expand tetanus toxoid specific porcine memory T cell populations in vitro

1.1 The Immune System 
The immune system is a complex network of effector molecules, immune cells and 
organs that collaborate to protect the body against invading pathogens and non-
infectious foreign particles. Generally, the immune system can be divided into two major 
parts: The innate and the adaptive immune system [17].  
The innate immune response is the body’s first line of defense. It mediates non-specific 
protection through physical and chemical barriers such as epithelial surfaces and 
antimicrobial agents produced at these surfaces, as well as phagocytes (neutrophils, 
monocytes and macrophages), natural killer (NK) cells and cells that secrete mediators of 
inflammation (basophils, mast cells and eosinophils). This arm of the immune defense 
also involves molecular components such as the complement system, acute-phase 
proteins and cytokines [17], [18].  
The innate immune system recognizes highly conserved pathogen-associated molecular 
patterns (PAMPs) which are molecular structures shared by a wide variety of 
microorganisms. Examples of PAMPs are bacterial lipopolysaccharides, peptidoglycans 
and doublestranded RNA. Recognition of PAMPs by the innate immune system initiates 
a rapid, but highly effective response which, in most cases, is sufficient to eradicate 
pathogenic microorganisms. However, if invading pathogens manage to escape the 
innate response, the body’s second line of defense, the adaptive immune system, takes 
over [19].  
In contrast to the innate immune system, the adaptive immune system shows exquisite 
antigen specificity and leads to long-lasting protection against the invading pathogen 
[18]. Immune protection by the adaptive immune system is primarily carried out by two 
cell types: B lymphocytes and T lymphocytes. The immune response mediated by B 
lymphocytes is referred to as humoral immunity. B cells originate from hematopoietic 
stem cells in the bone marrow and have the ability to differentiate into antibody 
secreting plasma cells. Antibodies play an important role in elimination of extracellular 
microorganisms through neutralization, opsonisation and activation of the complement 
cascade. In contrast to B lymphocytes, T lymphocytes are responsible for the adaptive 
cellular immune response [17], [18], [20], and since T lymphocytes are the main focus of 
this project, the function and activation pathway of this cell type is elaborated below.    
1.1.1 T lymphocytes  
T lymphocytes derive from hematopoietic stem cells continuously migrating from the 
bone marrow to the thymus, where  T cell maturation takes place [19], [20]. They are 
divided into two main classes based on their expression of the CD4 and CD8 surface 
proteins: T helper  (Th) cells and cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs), respectively [18].  
==PAGE:18==
4 
 
Th cells and CTLs recognize antigen-derived peptides presented by major-
histocompatibility-complex (MHC) molecules on the surface of other cells. Whereas Th 
cells recognize peptides presented by MHC class II molecules, CTLs recognize peptides 
presented on MHC class I molecules. MHC class II molecules are generally found on the 
surface of professional antigen presenting cells (APCs) such as dendritic cells, 
monocytes, macrophages and B cells. The peptides presented by MHC class II primarily 
originate from extracellular antigens that have been internalized and processed by the 
APC. In contrast, MHC class I molecules are found on all nucleated cells and associate 
with peptides derived from cytosolic antigens, for example virus antigens or antigens 
from intracellular bacteria [18], [19]. 
 The T cell receptor 
1.1.1.1
Antigen recognition by T cells is mediated through their T cell receptors (TCRs). Each 
TCR is composed of two polypeptide chains: An α- and β-chain or a γ- and δ-chain. In 
human, most T cells display α:β TCRs, whereas γ:δ TCRs are only expressed by a minor 
proportion of all human T cells [18]. Since the present study focuses on α:β T cells, the 
properties of the α:β TCR  is described in the following.  
 
Figure 1.1 The T cell receptor 
The TCR constitutes an α- and a β-chain, each composed of a constant region (closest to the membrane) and 
a variable region (furthest from the membrane). The variable region is responsible for recognition of the 
peptide:MHC complex displayed by other cells. In the cell membrane, the TCR associates with the CD3 
complex (a CD3γ chain, a CD3δ chain and two CD3ε chains) and a homodimer of ζ chains. Upon T cell 
activation, ITAMs of the CD3γ, CD3δ, CD3ε and ζ chains are phosphorylated by Lck, which initiates a 
phosphorylation cascade eventually leading to T cell differentiation and proliferation.  Figure from [21]. 
 
The α- and β-chain constituting the α:β TCR each contains a constant region and an N-
terminal variable region (Figure 1.1), of which the latter determines the TCR specificity 
[18], [19]. The TCR α:β heterodimer is dependent on help from other molecules to initiate 
==PAGE:19==
5 
 
T cell activation upon antigen encounter. In the T cell membrane, the TCR associates 
with a homodimer of ζ chains as well as the so-called CD3 complex comprising one 
CD3γ chain, one CD3δ chain and two CD3ε chains. These molecules are not involved in 
antigen recognition, but they are important for signal transduction through the TCR. The 
intracellular domains of the CD3γ, CD3δ, CD3ε and ζ chains contain immuno-receptor 
tyrosine-based activation motifs (ITAMs) (Figure 1.6). Upon T cell activation, the Scr 
family kinase Lck (lymphocyte-specific protein tyrosine kinase) phosphorylates the TCR 
associated ITAMs, which further initiates a phosphorylation cascade, eventually leading 
to the activation of the transcription factors NFкB, NFAT and AP-1 driving T cell 
differentiation and proliferation [18].       
 T cell activation 
1.1.1.2
T cell activation is highly dependent on dendritic cells (DCs) which constitute an 
important link between the innate and the adaptive immune defense, as they are the 
only cell type capable of activating naïve T cells. DCs constantly engulf extracellular 
antigens in local tissues, process them into peptide fragments and load them onto MHC 
class I and MHC class II molecules. To develop into highly potent APCs, DCs must be 
activated through danger signals such as PAMPs or heat shock proteins secreted by 
injured host cells. Upon activation, dendritic cells migrate from the tissue to the local 
draining lymph nodes, begin to upregulate co-stimulatory molecules such as B7 and 
initiate the secretion of cytokines involved in T cell polarization [17], [19]. In the lymph 
node, activated DCs present peptide antigen bound on MHC molecules to naïve T cells 
[18]. Upon recognition of their cognate antigen, naïve T cells will differentiate into 
antigen-specific effector cells,  a majority of which travel to peripheral tissues to help 
eliminate infected target cells [22]. 
 Effector T cells 
1.1.1.3
Naïve CD4+ T cells can differentiate into any of five main Th subsets depending on the 
cytokine and co-stimulatory signal they receive during activation: T-helper 1 (Th1), T-
helper 2 (Th2), T-helper 17 (Th17), follicular T helper cells (Tfh) and regulatory T cells 
(Treg). The different classes of Th cells are distinguished by the cytokines they produce, 
and together they carry out a plethora of different functions [18], [19], [23]. Th1 cells 
secrete IFN-γ and TNF-α and their main function is to activate the microbicidal activity 
of macrophages infected with intravesicular bacteria, thereby helping macrophages to 
clear off resident pathogens. In contrast, the Th2 subset produces cytokines (IL-4, IL-5 
and IL-13) that recruit and activate eosinophils and mast cells as well as promote 
antibody class switching to IgE, all important in the defense against parasites. The main 
role of Th17 is to activate neutrophils through secretion of IL-17, thereby mediating 
protection against extracellular bacteria and fungi. The Tfh cells reside in B-cell follicles 
where they provide help to B cells for high-affinity antibody production [18]. Finally, the 
role of Tregs is to protect against autoimmune responses. This subset recognize self-
==PAGE:20==
6 
 
antigens and suppress expansion of autoreactive naive T cells, partly through secretion 
of IL-10 and transforming growth factor-β (TNF-β), and partly through competition with 
naïve T cells for co-stimulatory molecules [18], [19]. 
In contrast to naïve CD4+ cells, naïve CD8+ T cells can only differentiate into one effector 
cell type, namely CTLs  [19]. The main function of this subset is to kill host cells infected 
with intracellular pathogens, but they are also able to kill off malignant cells. Killing by 
CTLs can be mediated through direct cell-cell contact or by cytokine secretion [18]. 
 Immunological memory 
1.1.1.4
After clearance of an infection, more than 95% of the effector cells die, while a small 
population differentiates into long-lived memory cells [24]. Memory T cells constitute a 
source of CD4+ and CD8+ antigen-experienced T lymphocytes which have a low 
activation threshold compared to naïve T cells and confer life-long protective immunity 
against previously encountered antigen [25]. 
The human memory T cell pool is highly heterogeneous and is currently believed to 
constitute five major subsets: Stem cell memory T cells (TSCM), central memory T cells 
(TCM), transitional memory T cells (TTM), effector memory T cells (TEM) and terminal 
effector memory T cells (TTE or TEMRA). After clearance of an infection, the small 
population of surviving T cells will differentiate into any one of these memory subsets 
[26], but the mechanisms behind memory fate decision are not fully understood [27]. 
Differentiation of memory T cells is thought to follow a linear progression through TSCM, 
TCM, TTM, TEM and TTE, where less differentiated subsets can differentiate into more 
differentiated phenotypes upon antigen re-stimulation or homeostatic signaling. The 
various subsets can be distinguished from each other by their differential expression of 
CD45RO, C-C chemokine receptor 7 (CCR7), CD28 and CD95 (Figure 1.2) [22].  
The first human memory T cell subsets described were TCM and TEM cells [28]. These 
subsets are CD45RO+CCR7+CD28+CD95+ and CD45RO+CCR7-CD28-CD95+, respectively. 
Upon development of naïve CD4+ and CD8+ T cells into effector or memory T cells, they 
lose expression of CD45RA, and upregulate CD45RO [29]. The CD45 molecule is 
important for TCR signaling, and the shift from the CD45RA to the CD45RO isoform in 
effector and memory T cells is thought to facilitate both TCR signaling and antigen 
recognition [18], [29].  
Whereas both human TCM and TEM subsets are positive for CD45RO, they differ in their 
expression of CCR7 and CD62L. TCM cells express CCR7 and CD62L, which interact with 
chemokines (CCL19 and CCL21) and carbohydrates, respectively, expressed by the high 
endothelial venules (HEV) of the lymph nodes. Thus, the TCM subset primarily home to 
lymph nodes, whereas TEM cells which lack CCR7 and CD62L expression mainly reside 
==PAGE:21==
7 
 
in non-lymphoid tissues [30]. Besides from exhibiting different homing potentials, the 
TCM and TEM subsets also differ with respect to their functional characteristics.  
 
Figure 1.2 The linear differentiation pathway of human memory T cells  
The six different subsets of the human memory T cell pool can be classified based on their expression of 
CD45RO, CCR7, CD28 and CD95. Markers changing upon differentiation from one subset to the next are 
indicated in red. Upon antigen re-encounter, quiescent memory T cells differentiate into effector T cells 
displaying highly unpredictable effector functions. After pathogen clearance, surviving effector cells fall 
back into a quiescent memory state of a more differentiated state than the memory subset they originated 
from. The signals responsible for memory fate decision are largely unknown. During differentiation from the 
TSCM phenotype to terminally differentiated TEMRA cells, the memory cells lose and acquire different functions 
(lower panel). Figure modified from [22]. 
 
CD4+ and CD8+ TCM cells display little or no immediate effector functions [31],  but upon 
antigen re-stimulation, TCM cells initiate IL-2 production,  expand vigorously [28] and 
differentiate into effector cells [25]. Compared to TCM cells, TEM cells exhibit rapid effector 
functions upon antigen re-encounter [32]. CD8+ TEM cells carry large amounts of perforin, 
and both CD4+ and CD8+ TEM begin to secrete IL-4, IL-5 and IFN-γ after re-activation [25]. 
Although the  CD4+ and CD8+ TEM subset have the ability to react quickly to danger, 
these cells have shorter life-spans and mount lower proliferative responses than the TCM 
subset [33]. Thus, the TCM subset which is able to self-renew and simultaneously generate 
more differentiated progeny, function as a repository of effector T cells in secondary 
responses [22], [32]. 
Both TCM and TEM cells are thought to derive from the same progenitor subset, namely 
the TSCM subset. TSCM cells have stem-cell like properties and can differentiate into any 
one of the other memory phenotypes (Figure 1.2). They also have a high proliferative 
==PAGE:22==
8 
 
potential and self-renewal capacity, which are typical stem cell features [34]. Transitional 
memory T cells, TTM, have been placed between TCM and TEM. They carry a less 
differentiated phenotype than TEM, and display a higher responsiveness to IL-15 in vivo 
[22]. The TTE (also called TEMRA) subset is thought to represent a terminally differentiated 
memory T subset, and has limited effector functions and proliferative capability [35], 
[36]. Finally, a memory subset referred to as tissue-resident memory T (TRM) cells has 
been described in humans. The main function of this subset is protection against 
pathogen invasion at mucosal surfaces and epithelia [37].

1.2 Cancer Immunoediting 
Besides from its role in eliminating invasive pathogens, the immune system is also 
thought to play a pivotal role in cancer prevention [38]. Cancer is one of the leading 
causes of death worldwide [39]. The disease is driven by accumulative genetic mutations 
eventually leading to aberrant regulation of cell proliferation and homeostasis [40]. 
Cancer cells carry antigens which discriminate them from their non-transformed 
counterparts. These include mutated, overexpressed and viral antigens as well as cancer-
testis antigens which are normally only expressed by germ cells of testis and ovary [38]. 
The ability of the immune system to recognize tumor antigens can lead to tumor 
elimination, but it also plays a role in moulding the immunogenic phenotype of the 
tumor cells [41]. This process is called cancer immunoediting and consists of three 
consecutive phases, namely the elimination, equilibrium and escape phase (Figure 1.3) 
[38].  
The elimination phase is initiated by the detection of a developing tumor by the immune 
system, which will attempt to destroy the emerging tumor. The mechanism behind 
tumor recognition is not fully understood, but cancer cells are thought to secrete danger 
signal such as Type I IFNs [42] and express different stress ligands [43], both of which 
can activate the innate immune system. In addition, tumor antigens are responsible for 
the activation of the adaptive immune system, which is required for effective cancer 
immune surveillance. If the elimination phase leads to complete tumor destruction, the 
immunoediting process ends at this stage. However, in some cases, rare tumor cell 
variants manage to escape the elimination phase and enter the equilibrium phase [38]. 
In the equilibrium phase, outgrowth of tumor cells is prevented by the adaptive immune 
system which keeps the cancer cells in a dormant state. The cancer cells are kept in check 
by the effects of IL-12 and IFN-γ as well as the effector functions of CD4+ and CD8+ T 
cells. The equilibrium phase can last for a life-time. However, the adaptive immune 
response subjects the tumor cells to a constant selection pressure, facilitating the survival 
of tumor cells with the ability to circumvent detection and/or elimination by the immune 
system [38].  
These tumor cells may enter the escape phase which is characterized by formation of 
visible tumors [38]. Tumor cells can escape the immune system using various strategies. 
One strategy is to reduce immunogenicity, for example by downregulating tumor 
antigens or by constitutively express anti-apoptotic molecules. Another strategy is to 
induce an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. This can be accomplished by 
secretion of immunosuppressive components such as TGF-β, galectin and indoleamine 
2,3-dioxygenase (IDO), or by attracting regulatory immune cells such as Tregs and 
myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs). Tregs inhibit the effector functions of 
conventional T lymphocytes through the production of immunosuppressive cytokines 
==PAGE:24==
10 
 
(IL-10 and TGF-β) and the expression of negative co-stimulatory molecules (CTLA-4, PD-
1 and PDL-1), whereas MDSCs primarily serve to activate Tregs [44].   
 
Figure 1.3 Cancer immunoediting 
When intrinsic tumor suppression mechanisms fail, malignant cells may arise. These are subjected to cancer 
immunoediting, encompassing three sequential phases referred to as the elimination, equilibrium and 
escape phase. Tumor cells secrete danger signals and express tumor antigens, which enable detection of 
emerging tumors by the innate and adaptive immune system in the elimination phase. If the elimination 
phase does not promote complete tumor destruction, rare tumor cells might enter the equilibrium phase 
where tumor outgrowth is kept in check by the adaptive immune system. The immunomodulatory effect of 
the equilibrium phase can lead to the escape and outgrowth of tumor cells which have acquired the ability to 
escape immune detection and destruction. Escape from the immune system by tumors can be accomplished 
via secretion of immunosuppressive components ( TGF-β, galectin and IDO), and recruitment of regulatory 
T cells such as Tregs and MDSCs. Tregs inhibit conventional T lymphocytes by production of 
immunosuppressive cytokines (IL-10 and TGF-β) and the expression of negative co-stimulatory molecules 
(CTLA-4, PD-1 and PDL-1), whereas MDSCs activate Tregs [38].   
It is important to note that not all tumor cells follow a unidirectional flow through the 
immunoediting phases. Some enters directly into the elimination or escape phase, 
whereas others may enter a preceding phase [38].

1.3 Cancer Immunotherapy 
Knowledge of the interplay between tumor development and the effects of the immune 
system has led to a search for immune based therapies that use immunological means to 
treat cancer [38]. Today, several different immunotherapies have been approved by FDA 
for treatment of different cancers including the check point inhibitors Ipilimumab [45], 
Nivolumab [46] and Pembrolizumab [47] for treatment of advanced melanoma and lung 
cancer, therapeutic antibodies such as Rituximab [48] and Herceptin [49]  for treatment 
of lymphoma and breast cancer, respectively, as well as the cancer vaccine Sipuleucel-T 
for treatment of prostate cancer [50]. In addition, a cell based immune therapy called 
adoptive T cell transfer (ACT) has been developed, and its use for treatment of 
metastatic melanoma is currently being investigated in a phase III trial [51]. As ACT is 
the primary focus of the present study, it is further described below. 
1.3.1 Adoptive T Cell Transfer 
The aim of ACT is to increase the frequency of anti-tumor T cells already present in 
cancer patient and at the same time circumvent anti-cancer immune suppression 
mediated by e.g. Tregs. The main advantage of ACT compared to conventional cancer 
treatments is its ability to mediate not only anti-tumor reactivity, but also long-term 
protective immunity against cancer [52]. 
ACT is a personalized cancer treatment in which immune cells with antitumor activity 
are isolated from the patient, expanded ex vivo and then transferred back into the patient 
upon administration of a lympho-depleting regiment. After re-infusion, the cancer 
specific T cells can traffic to the site of the tumor and lead to tumor destruction [53]. The 
fact that ACT could mediate dramatic cancer regression was first showed in melanoma 
patients in 1988 [54]. Today, different clinical trials have demonstrated that the use of 
autologous tumor infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) for ACT is the most effective way to 
obtain complete relapse-free regression in cases of metastatic melanoma [1].  
Human TILs for the treatment of melanoma are generally prepared as shown in Figure 
1.4. Tumor isolated from the patient is either digested into single-cell suspensions or 
divided into multiple fragments. When using the latter approach, each tumor fragment 
is individually grown in the presence of IL-2, and tumor cells are gradually destroyed as 
the TILs begin to overgrow. Pure lymphocyte cultures are obtained within 2-3 weeks and 
if a suitable co-culture assay exists, each culture is tested for anti-tumor activity. Rapid 
expansion of cancer specific T cell cultures is then carried out in a medium containing 
excess feeder cells, an antibody targeting the TCR CD3 complex and high doses (~6000 
IU/mL) of a T cell growth-factor, commonly IL-2. 5 to 6 weeks after the tumor was 
resected from the patient, up to 1011 tumor-specific lymphocytes are ready for re-infusion 
into the lympho-depleted patient along with an IL-2 regiment [1], [52]. Lymphodepletion 
of the patient prior to reinfusion of TILs has been shown to improve the clinical response 
==PAGE:26==
12 
 
to ACT treatment significantly [55]. The reason for this is still not completely 
understood, but lymphodepletion is thought to facilitate ACT treatment by generating 
homeostatic space for the adoptively transferred T cells [56] and inducing circulation of 
IL-15 which preferably stimulates the transferred T cells to proliferate in the absence of 
endogenous lymphocytes [57].

The fact that TILs are able to mediate complete cancer remission in cases of melanoma 
has raised the question of whether ACT can be used for the treatment of other types of 
cancer. However, melanoma is the only type of cancer that has shown to reproducibly 
give rise to anti-tumor TILs [1]. An attempt to widen the use of ACT for the treatment of 
other cancers has therefore led to the development of T cells that carry genetically 
engineered anti-tumor receptors [53]. By inserting different genes encoding either 
conventional alpha-beta TCRs or so-called chimeric antigen receptors (CARs), T cells can 
be engineered to recognize tumor derived antigens [1]. Using these methods, ACT has 
==PAGE:27==
13 
 
proved to mediate regression in several types of cancer histologies including 
neuroblastoma [5], leukemia and lymphoma [2]–[4].          
1.3.2  Cytokines for improvement of ACT 
Although CD4+ T cells have been shown to promote tumor eradication by their ability to 
recruit and activate tumor-specific CD8+ T cells in vivo [58], [59], most research within 
ACT has focused on the CD8+ subset, because of its inherent ability to mediate direct 
killing of tumor cells [59].   
A successful clinical outcome of ACT has been shown to be is strongly correlated to the 
ability of transfused CD8+ T cells to persist in vivo, self-renew and differentiate into 
tumor-reactive effector T cells [52]. Studies in mice have shown that whereas naïve (TN) 
and memory CD8+ T cells have a high proliferative and self-renewal potential and are 
able to differentiate into anti-tumor effector cells, terminally differentiated T cells 
primarily function in immediate anti-tumor reactivity and have short life-spans [52]. 
Mice studies further demonstrate that adoptive transfer of CD8+ T cell subsets carrying 
less differentiated phenotypes (TN, TSCM and TCM) show better in vivo persistence, tumor 
eradication and expansion compared to the more exhausted TEM and effector subsets 
[60]. These findings have led to a search for culture conditions that favor the expansion 
of poorly differentiated long-lived CD8+ memory phenotypes. In general, expansion of T 
cells for ACT is achieved by addition of a mitogenic stimulus (such as cognate antigen, 
lectins or anti-CD3/CD28 beads) along with one or more of the cytokines belonging to 
the common γ-chain family (IL-2, IL-7, IL-15 and IL-21). IL-2 was the first cytokine used 
for ACT and has been used extensively over the past decades [52]. However, T cell 
culture with this cytokine often includes high doses (3000-6000 IU/mL) of IL-2 over a 
period of 4-6 weeks, which leads to the formation of highly differentiated effector T cells 
[61]. In addition, culture with IL-2 drives collateral expansion of regulatory T cells, as 
these cells express the IL-2Rα chain constitutively [62]. Due to these problems associated 
with the use of IL-2 in ACT, the potential of other cytokines including IL-7, IL-12, IL-15 
and IL-21 has been investigated, and results relevant to this study are summarized 
below.      
 IL-7 
1.3.2.1
Together with IL-15, IL-7 supports the development and homeostasis of naïve and 
memory T cells [63] . This cytokine signals through the IL-7 receptor (IL-7R), which is 
composed of a common γ chain shared with the receptors for IL-2, IL-15 and IL-21, and 
an IL-7Rα subunit specific for IL-7 [64]. Naïve and memory T cells express high levels of 
IL-7Rα whereas this subunit is expressed at low levels or is absent on the surface of 
effector T cells and Tregs. Consequently, IL-7 has the ability to drive homeostatic 
proliferation of T memory cells without collateral expansion of regulatory T cells [65]. 
The potential of IL-7 in ACT has therefore been exploited. IL-7 has been shown to 
==PAGE:28==
14 
 
promote proliferation of in vivo primed tumor-reactive CD4+ T cells without the need for 
additional antigen stimulation in vitro. The CD4+ T cells were expanded several fold and 
maintained a memory-like phenotype. The fact that IL-7 can drive expansion of CD4+ 
tumor-specific memory T cells without the need for antigen clearly suggests the 
usefulness of IL-7 for ACT, especially in cases where the target tumor antigen is not 
known [12].   
 IL-15 
1.3.2.2
IL-15 signals through a receptor composed of a β and a γ chain shared with the IL-2 
receptor, and an α chain unique for IL-15 [63]. Consequently, IL-15 and IL-2 have many 
features in common, including stimulation of conventional T cell proliferation and 
survival as well as the synthesis of immunoglobulins. In contrast to IL-2, IL-15 is 
furthermore implicated in NK T cell differentiation, survival and activation [66]. 
Together with IL-7, IL-15 also governs in vivo homeostatic proliferation and survival of 
CD4+ and CD8+ central memory T cells [63].  
When using comparable concentrations of IL-2 and IL-15 for culture of PBMCs isolated 
from metastatic melanoma patients, both cytokines were found to be equally effective in 
significant expansion of tumor-reactive CTLs and in promoting cytokine mediated anti-
tumor responses [67]. In another study, it was shown that IL-15 were superior in 
promoting T cell viability and proliferation in vitro compared to IL-2 [68]. Altogether, 
these studies imply that the use of IL-15 for culture of porcine T cells might contribute to 
higher viability and greater proliferation compared to IL-2. In addition, as IL-15 is 
involved in central memory cell homeostasis, this cytokine might further have the ability 
to skew porcine T cell differentiation toward a memory phenotype. 
 IL-21 
1.3.2.3
IL-21 is a pleiotropic cytokine which is primarily produced by NK cells, Tfh cells and 
Th17 cells [69], [70], but also to a minor extend by CD8+ T cells [71], [72]. The cytokine is 
involved in the regulation of a variety of different immune activities including B cell 
differentiation [73], IgG class switching [74] and inhibition of Treg mediated 
immunosuppression [75]. IL-21 is not essential for CD8+ T cell development, but it has 
been shown to enhance CD8+ T cell proliferation and effector functions. In mice, IL-21 
synergize with IL-7 and IL-15, repectively, to improve proliferation of both naïve and 
memory CD8+ T cells and increase T cell IFN-γ production [76].     
In both human and mice, IL-21 is involved in retaining the longevity of the CD8+ 
memory T cell repertoire, and furthermore in preventing exhaustion of this T cell subset 
[52]. When provided by artificial antigen presenting cells (aAPCs) along with anti-CD3 
antibody, IL-21 proved superior to IL-2 for expansion of human memory CD8+ T cells 
exhibiting high cytotoxicity without collateral expansion of Tregs [77]. Furthermore, in 
vitro priming of tumor-specific T cells with IL-21 has shown to selectively expand tumor-
==PAGE:29==
15 
 
reactive CD8+ T cells with high affinity for tumor antigen and an effector memory like 
phenotype [13]. Altogether, this clearly suggests the usefulness of IL-21 for in vitro 
generation of porcine T cells harboring a memory phenotype. 
 IL-12 
1.3.2.4
Besides from the common γc cytokines described above, the potential of pro-
inflammatory cytokines such as IL-12 for T cell expansion has also been exploited. The 
primary role of IL-12 in vivo is to induce IFN-γ production by NK and T cells, and this 
effect has shown to be synergistically enhanced by simultaneous stimulation with IL-18 
[78]. However, co-culture of murine T cells with IL-12 has also proved to favor 
differentiation into memory T cell subsets [79], [80]. Furthermore, combining IL-12 with 
IL-7 or IL-21 followed by withdrawal of IL-12 yielded less differentiated T cell 
phenotypes, led to upregulation of genes associated with stem cells (e.g. SOX2, NANOG 
and OCT4) and even had the ability to revert the differentiation state of TILs isolated 
from patients with melanoma [14].   
 Cytokine combinations 
1.3.2.5
Results from many studies have pointed to the fact that using combinations of cytokines 
for T cell culture more efficiently generates less differentiated memory-like subsets 
compared to using individual cytokines [76], [81], [82]. For instance, IL-7 and IL-15 
synergistically enhance expansion of human TCM cells [82], and can furthermore instruct 
the generation of an even less differentiated phenotype, the TSCM phenotype [81]. The 
combination of IL-7 and IL-15 for T cell culture have proven so effective in generating 
human TCM and TSCM cells that a protocol for T cell expansion using IL-7 in combination 
with IL-15 instead of IL-2 alone is currently being tested in a phase I/IIa clinical trial with 
advanced stage melanoma patients [83].  
The combination of IL-15 and IL-21 has also shown to skew human T cell differentiation 
towards a central memory phenotype, and to generate a cell population with a higher 
frequency of tumor-reactive cytotoxic CD8+ T cells and NK cells [84]. In addition, the 
culture of CD8+ melanoma TILs in media containing IL-15 and IL-21 greatly increased 
the ability of the TILs to persist in vivo compared to IL-2 [85].

1.4 The Pig as a Large Animal Model 
During recent decades, murine models have been widely used to develop and test novel 
drug candidates before they enter clinical trials. However, results from murine 
experiments often translate poorly to the clinic. The correlation between the murine and 
human response is least consistent in trials within the field of immunology, which plays 
an important part in various human diseases including cancer [6], [7]. In order to 
improve current ACT of cancer patients, the development of a relevant supplementary 
animal model is therefore necessary. In contrast to mice, the anatomy, genetics and 
physiology of pigs closely resembles that of humans [9], [10]. The porcine genome is 
comparable with the human genome in terms of both size and composition. 
Furthermore, the majority of the proteins constituting the porcine immunome have both 
structural and functional features in common with their human counterparts. The 
human immune system has been compared to the porcine and murine immune system, 
respectively, by evaluating different parameters with respect to similarities between 
human and mouse or pig. It was found that for >80% of the analyzed parameters, the 
porcine immune system resembled the human immune system more closely than the 
murine immune system, and mice were only more similar to humans in <10% of the 
cases [9], [10]. The porcine immune system is well characterized and the availability of 
porcine reagents and tools has increased markedly over the last decade [86]. In the 
context of cancer, genetic transformation of porcine and human cells both requires 
accumulation of six mutations ([6], [7]), whereas the conversion of mouse cells to 
neoplastic cells is only caused by two genetic changes [89]. Altogether, the similarities 
between human and pig with regard to both immunology and cancer development 
clearly supports the usefulness of the pig as a large animal model for ACT treatment of 
humans [9].  
1.4.1 Porcine T cells 
Porcine T cells can be divided into two different subsets based on the type of TCR they 
carry: αβ T cells and γδ T cells. In human and mice, γδ T cells only constitute a minor 
proportion (1-5% in blood) of the total T cell population. However, along with other 
ungulates and chicken, swine belong to the so-called “γδ-high” species, which display 
high frequencies of γδ T cells in blood and spleen [90], [91]. In fact, the γδ population can 
constitute up to 85% of all peripheral blood lymphocytes in swine [91]–[95]. γδ T cells 
play a role in both innate and adaptive immunity, while TCR-αβ T cells, which is the 
focus of this project, constitute the key cellular component of the adoptive immune 
system together with B cells [20], [96].  
Porcine αβ T cells are generated in the thymus, where their development follows a route 
from immature CD4-CD8- T cells to mature CD4+ or CD8+ T cells via a double positive 
CD4+CD8+ T cell stage [97]. The majority of porcine αβ T cells are CD4+CD8α-, 
CD4+CD8α+ or CD4-CD8αhi, but a small subpopulation of CD4-CD8α- TCR-αβ T cells has 
==PAGE:31==
17 
 
also been identified. One of the peculiarities of the porcine immune system is the high 
abundancy of extrathymic CD4+CD8α+ αβ T cells. The size of this subpopulation varies 
considerably, but can constitute up to 80% of the total CD4+ population in adult swine 
[93].  
Porcine αβ T cells can further be divided into at least two subpopulations based on their 
CD8β-expression; a CD8β positive and a CD8β negative population. The size of the 
CD8β positive subpopulation as well as the CD8β expression profile vary between 
animals of the same age, and almost all CD8β positive αβ T cells are found within the 
population of CD4-CD8αhi T cells [93]. Currently, the CD3+TCR-αβ+CD4+CD8α-/+ 
phenotype is believed to represent porcine CD4+ Th cells, whereas porcine CD8+ CTLs 
are assumed to be CD3+TCR-αβ+CD4-CD8αhiCD8β+ [90]. The phenotypic characteristics of 
these two subpopulations are further described below.

thought to be related to antigen-recognition, and many studies support the idea that 
CD8α is expressed on the surface of activated and memory porcine CD4+ T cells. Porcine

1.4.3 Porcine CD8+ effector and memory T cells 
The majority of porcine CTLs carry the phenotype CD3+TCR-αβ+CD4-CD8αhiCD8β+ 
[101]–[103], hereafter referred to as CD8β+ T cells. Porcine CD8β+ T cells have been shown 
to play an essential role in a variety of different viral infections, but apart from that little 
information on alterations in phenotypic and functional characteristics during activation 
and memory formation of this T cell subset exists. 
In a recent study by Moreno et al., two cytotoxic T cell subpopulations were identified: A 
CD8βloCD11ahi and CD8βhiCD11alo T cell population. These subpopulations were 
furthermore shown to differ in their expression of CD45RA and CCR7. The 
CD8βhiCD11alo subpopulation was mainly positive for these two markers and did not 
express perforin. Thus, this subset was thought to represent naïve CD8+ T cells. This was 
in agreement with previous findings by Reutner et al. [99] who demonstrated that CD8βhi 
porcine T cells were also positive for CD27, a marker present on naïve T cells in human. 
In contrast to the CD8βhiCD11alo subset, most CD8βloCD11ahi T cells were CD45RA-
CCR7-, expressed higher levels of perforin and produced more IFN-γ, which indicated 
that this subset might correspond to effector or memory cytotoxic T cells. A small 
fraction of the CD8βloCD11ahi T cells were CCR7-CD45RA+. This subset was thought to 
correspond to terminally differentiated memory T cells re-expressing CD45RA, so-called 
TEMRA cells [25], [104]. In addition, a minor population of CD8βloCD11ahi T cells was 
CD45RA-CCR7+. Based on the fact that human memory T cells carry the lymph node 
homing CCR7 receptor, the authors speculated that the porcine CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA-
CCR7+ subpopulation might represent central memory cells with the inherent ability to 
recirculate through secondary lymphoid tissue [104].

1.5 T cell culturing 
Today, no protocols on porcine T cell expansion are available in the literature. This 
project is therefore mainly based on what is known about human T cell culturing. A brief 
overview of existing methods for human T cell culture is provided below. 
1.5.1 Polyclonal T cell stimulation 
Human T cells can be expanded polyclonally by the use of mitogens such as the bacterial 
super-antigen Staphylococcal enterotoxin B (SEB) [105], PMA (phorbol 12-myristate 13-
acetate)/ionomycin [106], [107], PHA or Con A [108]–[110]. The latter, which is used in 
this study, is a plant derived lectin which binds to glycosylated side chains of the TCR 
complex [111], thereby driving T cell proliferation [108]–[110]. Polyclonal expansion of 
human T cells can also be achieved using anti-CD3/CD28 polystyrene beads, which 
activate T cells through simultaneous binding to the CD3 complex and the co-
stimulatory molecule CD28 [112]. 
1.5.2 Culture of antigen-specific T cells 
Antigen-specific human T cells can be expanded through stimulation with different 
systems including peptide pulsed autologous DCs, cell-based aAPCs and bead-based 
aAPCs [113]–[115].  
Autologous DCs are frequently used for human T cell culturing. Briefly, isolated DCs are 
pulsed with the peptide antigen of interest and used to stimulate naïve T cells. Using this 
approach, it is possible to obtain 2-5×107 T cells of which 70-80% are antigen-specific 
during a period of 3-4 weeks. One drawback of this method is that it is time-intensive 
and it is often not possible to obtain sufficient numbers of autologous DCs for the entire 
culture period. Hence, the final T cell expansion is frequently carried out in a non-
specific manner, meaning that the antigenic specificity of the expanded cells drops from 
70-80% to 5-20% [114]. 
Anti-CD3/CD28 beads are an example of bead-based aAPCs. T cell proliferation can be 
induced independent of cognate antigen presentation by stimulation with different 
antibodies (e.g. anti-CD3, -CD2, -CD28 or -CD45) coupled to solid surfaces or 
polystyrene beads [115], [112]. Bead-based aAPCs can be used in combination with a 
preceding peptide-MHC multimer T cell enrichment step to mediate antigen-specific 
expansion of human T cells [113]. Alternatively, antigen-specific expansion can be 
achieved by the use of aAPCs coated with anti-CD28 antibody and HLA-
immunoglobulin fusion proteins, which can be loaded with the peptide antigen of 
interest [116]. One major limitation to the last mentioned aAPCs is that the exact peptide 
sequence of the target antigen which is responsible for activating the T cells must be 
known [115]. Sometimes, cell-based aAPCs are therefore preferred. 
==PAGE:34==
20 
 
Cellular aAPCs are derived from human or xenogeneic cells that have been manipulated 
to express molecules involved in TCR, co-stimulatory and adhesion events required for T 
cell activation. One well-known aAPC cell line is K562, which is derived from a chronic 
myelogenous leukemia (CML) patient [115]. Parental K562 cells do not express MHC-I or 
MHC-II molecules on their surface, but can be transduced with HLA genes and different 
co-stimulatory molecules, allowing for presentation of peptide antigen on their cell 
surface [117]. One major advantage of cell-based aAPCs is that they can be banked, and 
thereby constitute a long-term source of aAPCs to be used for T cell expansion. 
Furthermore, it is not required to know the peptide sequence of the target antigen which 
is responsible for mounting the T cell response [118].   
1.5.3 Cell culture medium 
A wide variety of different cell culture media optimized for different purposes exist. So 
far, no medium optimized for porcine PBMC culture has been developed. In this project, 
RPMI Medium 1640 (ThermoFisher) which was originally developed for human cell 
culture is therefore used. RPMI Medium 1640 contains a bicarbonate buffer maintaining 
a physiological pH at ~7.4 and the pH indicator phenol red [119]–[121]. To facilitate cell 
growth, RPMI 1640 is further supplemented with a range of different nutrients such as 
amino acids, glucose, vitamins and inorganic salts [119]. Rapidly growing lymphocytes 
also requires supplementation of the non-essential amino acid L-glutamine to sustain 
nucleotide synthesis [120]. Some laboratories prefer to use GlutaMAX™ supplement 
instead of L-glutamine, because GlutaMAX™ contains a stabilized form of L-glutamine 
preventing the build-up of the toxic waste product ammonia [122]. 
Many cell culture medias are supplemented with fetal calf serum (FCS) or fetal bovine 
serum (FBS) [120]. In some studies, especially those involving adoptive transfer of 
cultured cells into humans, autologous rather than animal-derived serum is used to 
prevent adverse immunologic effects from proteins present in the FBS or FCS [123]. 
Serum is a rich nutrition source containing important components for cell culture such as 
protein, lipids, hormones, growth factors, vitamins and minerals [120]. In addition to 
serum, a mixture of the antibiotics penicillin and streptomycin sulfate or gentamycin is 
often added to cell cultures to avoid bacterial growth [120]. Finally, many laboratories 
add feeder cells to support T cell growth [124], which often constitute PBMCs irradiated 
with (10-50 Gy) [125]. These contribute to growth by providing various extracellular 
matrix components and an undefined mixture of growth factors [126].

1.6 Flow Cytometry 
In this study, porcine T cells are studied by means of flow cytometry. Flow cytometry is 
a high-throughput technology which enables simultaneous determination of several 
characteristics of single particles (often cells) as they flow through a laser beam. These 
characteristics include relative size, granularity and fluorescence intensity of the particle. 
Flow cytometry can be used to analyze any suspension of cells or particles with 
diameters ranging from 0.2-150 um [127]. As particles flow through the laser beam(s) of 
the flow cytometer, they will scatter and emit light dependent of their physical 
characteristics. The light scattered or emitted by each particle can be detected by the flow 
cytometer and is stored for later analysis. Some particles are auto-fluorescent and 
naturally emit light following. However, in most cases, cell suspensions are prepared for 
flow cytometry by staining with flourescent dyes, enabling visualization of non-
flourescent cell components. Fluorescent dyes can be linked to antibodies with specificity 
for different antigens or proteins. In this case, the fluorescence of a cell will be a measure 
of the amount of antigen or protein present on the cell surface or in the cell cytoplasm 
[128]. In contrast, flourescent dyes such as CellTrace™ CFSE Cell Proliferation dye and 
Cell Proliferation Dye eFluor® 450 bind to primary amines of cellular proteins. As the 
cell divides, the dye is equally distributed to daughter cells, and the number of cell 
divisions can therefore be determined by the intensity of the flourescent signal emitted 
by each daughter population [129], [130].  
1.6.1 The fluidic system 
The flow cytometer consists of three main components: The fluidic system, the optical 
system and the electronics [127]. The purpose of the fluidic system is to transport the 
cells to the laser interrogation point and position them in the center of the laser beam to 
optimize illumination. This is accomplished by injecting the sample into a stream of 
sheath fluid within the so-called flow cell (see Figure 1.6). The flow of the sheath fluid 
accelerates the sample and ensures that cells are confined to the center of the sample 
core, a process referred to a hydrodynamic focusing [127]. Hydrodynamic focusing 
furthermore causes the cells to separate from each other at greater distances, minimizing 
the risk that two cells will flow past the laser beam at the same time [128]. 
1.6.2 The optical system 
In some cytometers, the interrogation point with the laser beam, also called the analysis 
point, is located within an optical clear region of the flow cell (see Figure 1.6), whereas in 
other cytometers, the sample intersects with the laser beam after emerging from the flow 
cell through a nozzle.  Most flow cytometers have more than one laser, which makes it 
possible to use a broader range of fluorochromes with different excitation wave lengths. 
The lasers are aligned along the stream of cell sample, and the cells pass through one 
laser beam at a time [128].  
==PAGE:36==
22 
 
 
Figure 1.6 The fluidic system of a flow cytometer 
Air pressure is pushing both sheath fluid and cell sample into the flow cell, where the sheath fluid serves to 
hydrodynamically focus the sample stream, thereby facilitating optimal illumination conditions for the cells 
at the analysis point. The hydrodynamically focused sample intersects with the laser beam at an optically 
clear region of the flow cell. Each cell flowing past the laser scatters SSC and FSC laser light, and if 
fluorescent molecules present in the cytoplasm or on the surface of the cells are excitated, these will emit 
light. Figure modified from [131].  
 
At each analysis point, light scattered or emitted from cells as a consequence of laser 
illumination is collected by different lenses. In flow cytometers with multiple lasers, the 
blue laser which generates light at 488 nm is usually the primary laser. At the 
interrogation point of the primary laser, there are typically two lenses: one located along 
the path of the laser beam and one orthogonal to the path (Figure 1.7, A) [128]. The first 
mentioned lens directs light onto a photodiode, which detects forward-scattered light 
(FSC). FSC is laser light that has been slightly bent by the cell, and the intensity of this 
light is proportional to area or size of the cell [127]. The lens located at right angles to the 
laser beam collects side scattered (SSC) and fluorescent light. SSC is laser light bent at 
wide angles to the direction of the laser beam, and this light is proportional to the 
granularity and internal complexity of the cell [127], [128]. Flourescent light is detected 
in a way similar to SSC, except for the fact that flourescent light is passed through a 
series of dichroic mirrors and optical filters that separate the light according to 
wavelengths and directs light of same color onto separate detectors called 
photomultiplier tubes (PMTs). In some flow cytometers, the PMTs are organized in a 
linear fashion (Figure 1.7, A), whereas in others they are organized in an octagon (Figure 
1.7, B). In the latter system, light emitted from cells is directed to the PMT octagon via 
optical fibers and are then passed through various mirrors and filters to separate the 
light according to color (Figure 1.7, B). Each laser in a flow cytometer has its own set of 
photodetectors, but only the primary laser detects FSC and SSC light [128].

Figure 1.7 Different optical systems 
(A) Dichroic mirrors, shortpass (SP), longpass (LP) and bandpass (BP) filters are used to split light emitted 
from cells into different photodiodes and PMTs according to wavelength. The given system allows for 
detection of FSC, SSC, red, orange and green light. Figure from [128]. (B) In some flow cytometers, the light

1.6.3 The electronics 
The light photons detected by photodiodes and PMTs in a flow cytometer are converted 
into voltage pulses, which can be amplified to create a stronger signal and finally 
digitized. The signal amplification can either be linear or logarithmic [127]. Linear 
amplification creates a digitized value which is proportional to the light intensity of the 
signal, and is normally reported on a linear scale from 0-1024 (Figure 1.8, upper panel). 
When logarithmic amplification is used, which is the case in this study, the final 
digitized value is proportional to the logarithm of the light intensity of the signal. This 
type of amplification involves the conversion of the values into relative intensity units 
==PAGE:38==
24 
 
which are displayed on a logarithmic scale. The advantage of logarithmic amplification 
is that it allows for the display of a large range of intensities compared to linear 
amplification (Figure 1.8) [128]. 
 
Figure 1.8 Differences between linear and logarithmic amplification 
The graphs show flow cytometric signals obtained from beads of 5 different intensities using linear and 
logarithmic amplification, respectively. In the upper graph, signals obtained with linear amplification are 
plotted directly on a 0-1024 scale. Linear amplification causes the two brightest beads to be off-scale. In 
contrast, when using logarithmic amplification (lower graph), all signals are on scale. By convention, signal 
intensities are plotted as relative intensity values when using logarithmic amplification.  
Having passed through all the lasers of the flow cytometer, each cell will now have a 
series of different numbers assigned to it, which describe its size, granularity and 
fluorescence. Using different software, this data can be displayed in histograms and dot 
plots, and analyzed with the aim of determining various physical characteristics of the 
cell populations present in the sample [128].  
1.6.4  Spectral Overlap & Compensation 
When using multicolor flow cytometry, a phenomenon called spillover often arises. 
Spillover is caused by overlap between emission spectrums of different fluorochromes, 
and occurs when light emitted from one fluorochrome spills into the detector which is 
designed to measure light emitted from another fluorochrome [133]. An example is the 
spectral overlap observed between the fluorochromes fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) 
and R-phycoerythrin (R-PE) (Figure 1.9). As illustrated in Figure 1.9, approximately 15% 
of the light emitted by FITC spills into the R-PE detector, whereas around 2% of the light 
emitted by R-PE is collected by the FITC detector [134]. 
==PAGE:39==
25 
 
 
Figure 1.9 Spectral overlap between FITC and R-PE 
Excitation and emission spectra of FITC and R-PE are shown. Emitted light from FITC and PE are detected 
by PMTs equipped with a 530/30 and 585/42 BP filter, respectively. The figure shows that ~15% of the light 
emitted by FITC spills into the R-PE detector. In contrast, ~2% of the light emitted by R-PE is detected by the 
FITC detector. Figure modified from [134]. 
Spillover is corrected by a process called compensation. The purpose of compensation is 
to correct for spillover of any fluorochrome into neighboring channels. To compensate 
for spectral overlap, the spillover of each fluorochrome in a given antibody panel into 
other detectors is measured using single-color controls. Based on these measurements, a 
compensation matrix is generated, which is then used to correct contributions from 
fluorochromes that overlap into a given detector [133].    
1.6.5  FMO controls 
Performing compensations as described above can result in a visual spread of 
populations, making it difficult to define the boundaries between negative and positive 
populations [135], [136]. As the position of a positive and negative population in a given 
plot is affected by the compensation applied to the experiment, the position of the 
negative population cannot be defined using an unstained control. Instead, fluorescence-
minus-one controls (FMOs) are performed. In FMOs, cells are stained with all 
fluorochromes except one, and this sample is then used to define the boundary between 
positive and negative cell populations [137].

2.2 General methods 
All washing steps were followed by 5 min. centrifugation at 1500 rpm and subsequent 
removal of the supernatant. Centrifugation steps were carried out in a SL 40R centrifuge 
from ThermoFisher. Solutions and tubes used for this study were sterile except for 
DMSO (dimethyl sulfoxide), FACS washing buffer (PBS/0.2% Bovine Serum Albumin 
(Sigma Aldrich)/0.1% Sodium azide), FACS tubes, antibody stock solutions, 
compensation beads and counting beads. FBS was heat-inactivated at 56°C for 30 min. 
before use. Cell concentrations of unstained cells were determined using a 
NucleoCounter NC-200 (Chemometec), and cells stained with Cell Proliferation Dye 
eFluor® 450 (eBioscience) or CellTrace™ CFSE Cell Proliferation dye (ThermoFisher) 
were counted with an Olympus CH-2 microscope using a Bright-line (0.100 mm, 
0.0025m2) hemocytometer. Microscopic images of cell cultures were obtained using a 
Leica DMIL microscope connected to a Leica DF290 camera. All flow cytometric data 
was acquired on a BD LSRFortessa™ cell analyzer. Data was analyzed using FACSDiva 
software version 8.0.1 and FlowJo software version 10.1. 
2.2.1  PBMC purification 
PBMCs were isolated by Lymphoprep gradient separation in SepMate tubes (both from 
Stemcell Technologies). Briefly, heparinized blood was diluted 2x in PBS/2% (v/v) FBS, 
transferred to SepMate tubes containing 15 mL Lymphoprep and centrifuged at 1200g 
for 20 min. For each SepMate tube, the supernatant was transferred to a 50 mL Falcon 
tube and washed in PBS/2% (v/v) FBS. If red blood cell contamination of the pellet was 
observed, the pellet was re-suspended, treated with 5 mL red blood cell lysis buffer for 2 
min. and washed in PBS/2% (v/v) FBS.    
2.2.2 PBMC cryopreservation  
PBMCs were purified as in section 2.2.2 and the cell pellet was reconstituted in 90% 
FBS/10% DMSO to make a concentration of 10 mio. cells/mL. The cell suspension was 
quickly aliquoted to freezing ampoules (app. 1 mL/ampoule) which were transferred to a 
CoolCell® Cell Freezing Container (BioCision) and left at -80 °C overnight. The 
following day, ampoules were transferred to liquid nitrogen for long-term storage.  
2.2.3  PBMC defrosting 
Just before use, ampoules with cryopreserved PBMCs were removed from the liquid 
nitrogen container and kept on dry ice. For each ampoule, 10 mL of RPMI Medium 1640 
– GlutaMAX-I (ThermoFisher Scientific) + 10% FBS (Lot. No. 1774921, ThermoFisher 
Scientific) + 50 U/mL Penicillin-Streptomycin (PenStrep) (ThermoFisher Scientific), was 
transferred to a Sarstedt tube and pre-warmed to 37°C. Each ampoule was quickly 
thawed by alternately adding pre-warmed media to the ampoule, alternately 
transferring thawed cell suspension to the tube with pre-warmed media.

2.2.4  Cell staining: Old protocol 
PBMCs were washed and reconstituted in serum-free RPMI Medium 1640 – GlutaMAX-I 
(ThermoFisher) to a final concentration of 10 mio. cells/mL. 5 mM CellTrace™ CFSE Cell 
Proliferation stock solution (ThermoFisher Scientific), was added to a final concentration

by adding FBS to make up 20% of the total volume followed by 2 min. incubation at 
room temperature. The cell suspension was then washed in the same media in which the 
cells were to be cultured. 
2.2.5  Cell staining: New protocol 
PBMCs were washed 2x in PBS (5°C). PBS was transferred to Falcon tubes and left to 
pre-warm in a 37°C water bath. Cell pellets were reconstituted in pre-warmed PBS to a 
final concentration of 106 cells/mL.  Cell Proliferation Dye eFluor® 450 (eBioscience) or 
CellTrace™ CFSE Cell Proliferation dye (ThermoFisher) was reconstituted in DMSO to a 
stock concentration of 10 mM and stored in 10 uL aliquots at -20 °C. 10-15 min. before 
use, an aliquot was removed from the freezer and placed in a 37°C incubator to pre-
warm. 1 mL of pre-warmed PBS was added to 10 uL pre-warmed cell dye stock. 500 uL 
of this solution was further diluted into 50 mL warm PBS to make a final concentration 
of 1uM. An equal volume of the 1uM dye solution was added to the cell suspension to 
make a final labelling concentration of 0.5 uM, and the cells were incubated at 37°C/5% 
CO2 for 10-15 min. Surplus dye was quenched by addition of FBS to make a 
concentration of 10%, the suspension was left on ice for 5 min., and subsequently 
washed in the same media in which the cells were to be cultured.

3 Results 
The results from the present study are presented in the sections below. 
3.1  Optimization of growth conditions 
In a previous study by R. Andersen [15], culture of porcine PBMCs proved challenging 
and consistently resulted in extensive cell death and no T cell expansion. Thus, this 
study was initiated by experiments aiming at improving cell culture conditions for 
porcine PBMCs. In three parallel experiments, I set out to test the influence on media 
composition, IL-2 concentration and culture well plates on T cell viability and 
proliferation. 
3.1.1  New FBS batch improved T cell proliferation and survival 
The FBS used in prior experiments was a generous gift from Section for Diagnostics and 
Scientific Advice, DTU Vet. However, in May 2016 I realized that the batch was expired. 
Although Section for Diagnostics and Scientific Advice had tested and approved its 
performance, other groups experienced problems when using the batch for T cell 
culturing. I therefore speculated that the expired FBS batch did not provide optimal 
conditions for T cell growth. Furthermore, another group working with culture of 
murine T cells found that the use of RPMI Medium 1640 GlutaMAX-I (ThermoFisher), 
which contains a stabilized form of L-glutamine, led to poor T cell growth compared to 
RPMI Medium 1640 (ThermoFisher) supplemented with non-stabilized L-glutamine. 
Based on the information above, I set out to test the effect on porcine T cell viability and 
growth of culture in three different media: 1) RPMI Medium 1640 GlutaMAX-I plus fresh 
FBS, 2) RPMI Medium 1640 plus fresh FBS and non-stabilized glutamine (murine T cell 
culture medium), and 3) RPMI Medium 1640 GlutaMAX-I plus the expired FBS batch 
(see method section 2.3.1). 
PBMCs from each of two domestic pigs (#475 and #476) were stimulated with Con A (1 
ug/mL) and IL-2 (100 IU/mL), and cultured for six days in one of the three above 
mentioned media using 24-well plates. On day six, the PBMCs were harvested and 
analyzed by flow cytometry, and the viability and proliferative response of the 
differentially treated cells were investigated using the gating strategy described in 
Figure 3.1. I found that RPMI Medium 1640 GlutaMAX-I plus fresh FBS was superior in 
maintaining cell viability after 6 days culture (Figure 3.2, A), whereas the media 
optimized for murine T cell culture gave the highest proliferative response (Figure 3.2, 
B). The low T cell viability observed after growth in murine T cell culture medium might 
be caused by the high proliferation rate, possibly leading to T cell exhaustion. Based on 
the results, RPMI Medium 1640 GlutaMAX-I plus fresh FBS seemed to give the best 
conditions for T cell survival. However, PBMC culturing in this medium did still not 
==PAGE:52==
38 
 
facilitate overall T cell expansion (Figure 3.2, C), which is a requirement for the use of the 
pig as a large animal model for ACT.

3.1.3  Round bottom 96-well plates promote T cell expansion 
Parallel to the experiments testing the effect of media composition and IL-2 
concentration on porcine T cell culture, I also investigated whether replacing the hitherto 
used flat bottom 24-well culture plates with round or flat bottom 96-well plates 
improved the growth of porcine T cells in vitro. For the same reasons as described for the 
IL-2 titration experiment above, the media optimized for murine T cell culture was used 
in this experiment. PBMCs from pig #475 and #476 were treated with Con A (1 ug/mL) 
and IL-2 (100 IU/mL), and set up in round bottom 96-well plates (50.000 PBMCs/well), 
flat bottom 96-well plates (200.000 cells/well) or flat bottom 24-well plates (106 
PBMCs/well). The cells were cultured for 20 days, and the cultures were supplemented 
with new media plus IL-2 and split when necessary (see method section 2.3.3).  
To assess the effect of different culture plates on T cell expansion, the total number of 
cells obtained for each condition was counted by hemocytometer on day 0, 14 and 20. 
For PBMCs cultured in round bottom 96-well plates, an average expansion of 6.3 fold 
was observed for both pigs on day 14, whereas culture in flat bottom 96-well plates led 
to an average expansion of 1.4 fold and culture in 24-well plates either resulted in cell 
death (pig #475) or a constant cell number (pig #476) after 14 days culture (Figure 3.4). 
The fact that round bottom 96-well plates facilitated more cell growth than the other well 
plates is also suggested by representative microscopic images of cell cultures from pig 
#475 taken on day 2, 6 and 14 (Figure 3.5). These images show that the cell density of 
porcine PBMCs increased faster in round bottom 96-well plates compared to flat bottom 
96- or 24-well plates, which is particularly evident from the images obtained on day 6. 
Around day 15, cell growth in all well plates ceased and the cultures were re-stimulated 
with Con A (1 ug/mL) to facilitate further growth. Despite Con A re-stimulation, cell 
death was observed for all conditions from day 14 to day 20, and the extent of cell death 
was more pronounced for the PBMCs in round bottom 96-well plates, probably due to 
exhaustion following 6-fold expansion (Figure 3.4). Based on the results obtained from 
the optimization experiments, I decided to carry out future experiments using RPMI

3.1.4 Poor in vitro survival of cryopreserved porcine PBMCs  
To use the pig as a large animal model for ACT, expansion of porcine T cells, which are 
preferentially specific for tumor antigen, is needed. Furthermore, it is advantageous to 
be able to culture PBMCs which have been cryopreserved, as working with 
cryopreserved cell material is time efficient and reduce practical limitations associated 
with keeping large research animals. 
Having shown that porcine PBMCs was expanded polyclonally by stimulation with Con 
A (1 ug/mL) and IL-2 (100 IU/mL) followed by 14 days culture in round bottom 96-well 
plates, antigen-specific expansion of porcine T cells using round bottom 96-well plates 
was also investigated (see method section 2.3.4). As previously mentioned, antigen-
specific expansion of porcine T cells is here being investigated using tetanus toxoid as a 
stimulant for in vitro culture, because PBMCs from the mini-pigs showed weak 
responses to cancer peptides in vitro. Purified PBMCs from two mini-pigs (#4045 and 
==PAGE:58==

#4544) vaccinated with tetanus toxoid were therefore used. ELISPOT results measuring 
IFN-γ release of PBMCs isolated from these pigs at different time points after 
immunization are shown in Figure 3.6 [16]. The results are given as the ratio between the 
IFN-γ response obtained after stimulation with tetanus toxoid and the response obtained 
after stimulation with media only (negative control). The cut-off defining a biologically

weeks post 9th immunization, the tetanus response on this day was assumed to be similar

been better to use two other pigs showing better tetanus toxoid responses.

3.2  Antibody panel for memory cell detection 
Based on the studies described above, a new experiment investigating the expansion of 
tetanus specific T cells were set up using fresh porcine PBMCs and leaving out the 4 
hour incubation step. As the purpose of this experiment was further to skew the T cell 
populations toward a memory phenotype by using different cytokines, a new antibody 
panel enabling the identification of CD4+ and CD8+ memory T cells was designed. 
Recently purchased antibodies included in the panel were titrated as described in 
Appendix A . The panel contained four new primary antibodies: anti-CD27, -CD11a, -
CD45RA and -CCR7 antibodies (see method section 2.4.2) The first three antibodies are 
anti-pig, whereas the latter is anti-human, but has previously shown to cross-react to pig 
[100]. The reactivity of these antibodies was tested on freshly isolated PBMCs from mini-
pig #4049 by comparing an FMO control for each antibody to a sample stained with the 
complete antibody panel (see method section 2.4.2.2). The samples were analyzed by 
flow cytometry, T cells were identified as shown in Figure 3.1, and the T cell population 
was further gated for CD27(+/-), CD11a(+/-), CD45RA(+/-) and CCR7(+/-) subpopulations (see 
Figure 3.8). 
The direct ex vivo staining of porcine PBMCs showed that 38.8% of all T cells were 
positive for CD45RA (Figure 3.8), and that 43.1% of all PBMCs were positive for 
CD45RA (see  Appendix B, Figure 8.9). These percentages are rather low compared to 
what has been found for other anti-CD45RA antibody clones (the clone used in this 
project was MIL13). Percentages ranging from 69%-98.2% CD45RA+ porcine 
lymphocytes was found for clone K252 1E4 [141], and 60% of porcine PBMCs were found 
to be CD45RA+ when using  two other antibody clones (6E3/7 and 3C3/9) [142]. It is likely 
that this difference is either due to differential CD45RA+ expression in domestic pigs 
compared to mini-pigs, or due to different reactivity of the various antibody clones used.  
CD11a is a molecule constituting the α-chain of the integrin LFA-1, which is involved in 
leukocyte extravasation into the tissue during infection [18]. In human, CD11a is 
expressed on the surface of all T cells [18], and it can be expressed at high or low levels 
[143]. In agreement with this, I found that almost all porcine T cells were CD11a+ (~94%), 
and that they were either CD11alo or CD11ahi (Figure 3.8). The reason that I only found 
94% of the T cells to be CD11a+ is probably due to the concentration of antibody used. 
However, due to limited resources, the antibody concentration was not increased in 
future experiments.  
The anti-CD27 antibody gave rise to a CD27dim and a CD27hi population as expected 
(Figure 3.8) [144]. Furthermore, when gating for CD27 positive cells within the PBMC 
population, ~37% of the PBMCs stained positive for CD27 ( Appendix B, Figure 8.10). A 
similar result was found by the supplier [145], indicating that the antibody worked.

3.3.1  Stimulation with tetanus toxoid compared to Con A 
First, an attempt to reproduce the PBMC expansion results found in Figure 3.4 was 
made. For the PBMCs treated with Con A and IL-2 as in the previous experiment, I 
found either a decrease in the total number of PBMCs (pig #4049) or only a slight PBMC 
expansion (pig #4542) after 7 days culture (Figure 3.11, A). As the PBMCs were cultured 
for 7 days instead of 14 days, I did not expect a 6-fold expansion as previously found, 
but I expected to see a more pronounced increase in the total cell number, as human T 
cells can be expanded up to 200 fold within 12 days [11]. I did therefore not succeed in 
reproducing the previously obtained results. 
The difference between the total number of alive PBMCs found after 7 days culture with 
Con A + IL-2 versus tetanus toxoid + IL-2 was further assessed. Since tetanus toxoid is a 
much weaker stimulant than Con A, it was expected to give rise to a lower number of 
PBMCs on day 7, which was also observed (Figure 3.11, A). However, the difference 
between the total number of PBMCs obtained after stimulation with Con A and tetanus 
toxoid, respectively, was small (Figure 3.11, A), which is surprising as Con A is a 
vigorous T cell stimulant and would be expected to lead to extensive T cell expansion 
compared to tetanus toxoid. 
It was investigated whether the poor overall T cell expansion following Con A 
stimulation was caused by poor T cell proliferation. First, it was tested whether our 
experimental design allowed for identification of proliferated T cells. A comparison of 
the proliferative response of a control to a cell culture stimulated with Con A and IL-2 
clearly demonstrated that T cell proliferation could be detected by our assay (Figure 3.11, 
B), and I found that Con A mediated vigorous T cell proliferation independent of the 
addition of cytokines (Figure 3.11, C, upper panel). Considering this immense 
proliferative response following Con A stimulation, it is surprising that no or little 
overall increase in PBMC number was seen after stimulation with Con A and 7 days 
incubation (Figure 3.11, A). This suggests that PBMCs cultured with Con A proliferate 
vigorously, but also undergo extensive cell death.

3.3.3  Expansion of CD8+ memory T cells 
As mentioned in section 1.3.2, a successful clinical outcome of ACT is highly dependent 
on the longevity of the adoptively transferred T cells. Thus, it was next assessed whether 
stimulation with different cytokine combinations and tetanus toxoid enabled expansion 
of memory CD8+ T cells. Based on recent findings by Moreno et al. [104], porcine memory 
cytotoxic T cells were assumed to be CD4-CD8αhiCD8βloCD11ahi, hereafter referred to as 
CD8βloCD11ahi T cells or CD8+ memory T cells. This population was shown to be 
primarily CD45RA-CCR7-, but it contained a small CD45RA-CCR7+ subset which was 
thought to represent central memory CD8+ T cells and a small population of 
CD45RA+CCR7- cells, which were thought to correspond to TEMRA cells. Since the anti-
CCR7 antibody used in this study did not cross-react to pig, I were not able to identify 
central memory CD8+ T cells, and the CD8βloCD11ahi memory pool was therefore studied 
as a whole. Considering that the majority of CD8βloCD11ahi cells were CCR7-, I assumed 
that it was possible to identify TEMRA cells based on a CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ phenotype.  
I found that stimulation with tetanus toxoid in combination with IL-2 alone or co-
administered with IL-12, IL-15 or IL-21 led to better CD8βloCD11ahi T cell proliferation 
than the control, tetanus toxoid alone or tetanus toxoid together with IL-18 (Figure 3.14, 
A). Although the proliferative responses mounted by IL-2, IL-2+IL-12, IL-2+IL-15 and IL-
2+IL-21 were similar in size, only treatment with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 or IL-2 plus IL-
15 led to overall expansion of the CD8βloCD11ahi memory population (Figure 3.14, B). 
The difference in memory cell expansion between the different cytokine treatments was 
also obvious from the raw data (Figure 3.14, C). However, memory cell expansion 
achieved by stimulation with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 or IL-2 plus IL-15 was more 
pronounced for pig #4049 than for pig #4542, probably due to the fact that pig #4049 is 
more responsive to tetanus toxoid than pig #4542 as previously suggested.  
It was further found that the majority of CD8 memory cells were CD45RA+ after 7 days 
culture (Figure 3.14, D). For pig #4049, CD45RA expression was independent on 
treatment with cytokines and/or tetanus toxoid, whereas the results obtained for pig 
#4542 indicated that treatment with tetanus toxoid plus cytokines drives further 
CD45RA expression compared to the control and treatment with tetanus toxoid alone. 
Due to an analysis mistake, the anti-CD45RA antibody was first thought not to work and 
was therefore not included in the antibody panel for the samples stained on day 0. 
Consequently, it was not possible to compare CD45RA expression on day 0 to that found 
on day 7.

3.3.5 Expansion of CD4+ memory T cells 
Similar to the analysis of the CD8+ memory pool above, I investigated if CD4+ memory T 
cells were expanded after treatment with tetanus toxoid and different cytokines. In a 
study from 2013, Reutner et al. [100] suggested that activated CD4+CD8α+ T cells separate 
into two different subsets that are either CD27+ or CD27-, representing porcine CD4+ TCM 
cells and TEM cells, respectively.  
I found that treatment with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 or IL-2 + IL-15 tended to maintain a 
constant number of CD4 TCM cells after 7 days culture, whereas the other treatments 
facilitated TCM survival to a smaller extent (Figure 3.17, A). Although tetanus toxoid with 
IL-2 or IL-2+IL-15 improved TCM survival compared to the other treatments, the total 
number of CD4 TCM population was not increased after 7 days culture (Figure 3.17, A). 
When analyzing the development of the CD4+ TEM population, I saw a trend toward 
more cell death after treatment with tetanus toxoid in combination with cytokines 
compared to the control and cultures receiving tetanus toxoid alone (Figure 3.17, B). This 
was expected, as CD4 TEM cells already exist in a late differentiation state, and cytokine 
stimulation therefore may cause further exhaustion. Surprisingly, culture of PBMCs 
from pig #4049 after stimulation with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 or IL-2+IL-15 led to 
expansion of CD4 TEM cells. The reason for this is not known. 
Next, the distribution of TCM and TEM cells within the joined TCM and TEM population 
following stimulation with tetanus toxoid and different cytokine treatments was 
assessed (Figure 3.17, C). On day 0, the TCM subpopulation was predominant, whereas 7 
days culture of the control and the culture treated with tetanus toxoid alone increased 
the percentage of TEM cells slightly. For pig #4049, the distribution of the two memory 
populations after treatment with tetanus toxoid and various cytokines was not changed 
significantly compared to day 0, except for the cultures which were treated with tetanus 
toxoid plus IL-2 or IL-2 and IL-15. For these treatments, a larger percentage of TEM cells 
was observed. For pig #4542, cytokine treatments lead to a higher distribution of TCM 
cells, but when compared to the results in Figure 3.17 (A&B), this seemed to be due to 
poor TEM survival rather than expansion of the TCM subpopulation. 
Finally, the distribution of CD4+ TCM and TEM populations in cultures treated with tetanus 
toxoid was compared to those treated with Con A. Surprisingly, it was found that for all 
cytokine conditions, Con A was able to skew the phenotype of CD4+ T cells towards a 
central memory phenotype much more efficiently than tetanus toxoid (Figure 3.17, D). 
Since Con A is a very strong polyclonal T cell activator which would be expected to 
drive CD4+ T cell differentiation towards a late differentiation state, this result questions 
whether CD4+ TEM and TCM cells can actually be identified based on CD27 expression, as 
suggested by Reutner et al. (2013) [100]. This could also help explain why I saw an 
unexpected expansion of TEM cells following treatment with IL-2 and IL-2 plus IL-15.

4 Discussion 
Current mouse models are bad predictors of the clinical outcome of human 
immunotherapies, including ACT [6]–[8]. Due to its close resemblance to humans with 
regard to both genetics and immunology [9], [10], the present study aimed at exploiting 
the potential of the pig as a large animal model for ACT. The use of the pig as a model 
for ACT requires expansion of porcine T cells, which has previously proved difficult [15]. 
Furthermore, expansion of antigen-specific memory T cells is also needed. In this study, 
attempts to improve in vitro culture conditions for porcine T cells were made, and the 
ability to expand porcine antigen-specific memory T cells was investigated. 
4.1  Porcine T cells requires specific survival factors 
In the present study I demonstrated that exchanging expired FBS with a new batch 
significantly improved the proliferative capacity and viability of porcine T cells 
stimulated with Con A, but did not lead to an increase in the total number of T cells after 
6 days culture (Figure 3.2). Furthermore, I showed that the survival of T cells treated 
with Con A was independent of IL-2 addition to the medium (Figure 3.3), probably 
because Con A is able to drive IL-2 production by T cells.   
Culture in round bottom 96-well plates enabled 6-fold expansion of porcine PBMCs after 
14 days culture (Figure 3.4). Since the PBMC cultures were stimulated with Con A and 
IL-2, of which the latter favors T cell survival and growth [146], it was assumed that the 
expanded cells were primarily T cells. I did, however, not succeed in reproducing the 
results in a second experiment (Figure 3.11, A), which could be caused by too early 
transfer of the cell cultures from round bottom 96-well plates to flat bottom 48-well 
plates. Another explanation could be that the cells were cultured in RPMI medium 1640 
with non-stabilized glutamine and fresh FBS in the first experiment, whereas in the 
latter, the cells were grown in RPMI medium 1640 GlutaMAX-I plus fresh FBS.  
Polyclonal stimulation of human T cells with anti-CD3/CD28 beads and IL-2 followed by 
12 days culture has been shown to facilitate up to 200 fold T cell expansion [11]. My 
results show that stimulation with the polyclonal stimulant Con A plus IL-2 drives a 
strong proliferative T cell response (Figure 3.11, C, upper panel), but only leads to a 6-
fold (Figure 3.4), little or no overall increase of the total number of porcine T cells (Figure 
3.11, A). This clearly implies that the culture conditions used in the present study are not 
providing an optimal in vitro environment for porcine T cell survival. Since the media 
used was optimized for either human or murine T cell culture, it is likely that it lacks one 
or more growth or survival factors needed for ex vivo survival of porcine T cells. 
Supplementing the media with autologous or porcine serum instead of FBS and adding 
feeder cells might provide the essential components and right cytokine milieu necessary 
for in vitro culture of porcine T cells. Furthermore, it is possible that expansion of 
porcine T cells from PBMC cultures might lead to collateral cell death of other cell types 
==PAGE:82==
68 
 
such as B cells, which are not provided with the right growth factors in a medium 
optimized for T cell culture. As necrotic cells have leaky cell membranes [147], they 
might leak intracellular constituents such as reactive oxygen species into the media, 
which could hamper T cell survival. I therefore speculate that T cell enrichment prior to 
culture could provide better conditions for T cell survival.  
Nevertheless, our results give various indications that porcine T cells are more fragile ex 
vivo than human T cells. This is both implied by major PBMC loss following 
cryopreservation and by the poor growth observed after a four hour incubation step at 
room temperature (Figure 3.7). Introducing another purification step such as T cell 
enrichment could therefore lead to a further decrease in survival. The lack of persistence 
following incubation and cryopreservation could, however, also be another indication 
that porcine T cells require other culture and cryopreservation conditions than human T 
cells. In general, more experiments aiming at optimizing culture conditions for porcine T 
cells are required to draw further conclusions. 
4.2  Indications of CD8+ T cell expansion by tetanus toxoid 
Moving on to antigen-specific stimulation of porcine T cells, my results showed 
indications of a proliferative T cell response induced by in vitro stimulation with tetanus 
toxoid of PBMCs from pigs vaccinated 5 times with tetanus toxoid (Figure 3.11, C&D). 
However, no overall T cell expansion following stimulation with tetanus toxoid and 
various cytokine combinations was observed (Appendix D, Figure 8.12). The 
proliferative response mounted after stimulation with tetanus toxoid was low compared 
to that found for stimulation with Con A (Figure 3.11, C). This was expected since Con A 
is a mitogen which can drive proliferation of more than 20% of the lymphocyte 
population [105], [148]. In contrast, tetanus toxoid responsive T cells in the pigs used in 
this experiment only constituted ~0.03% of all PBMCs [16]. Therefore, stimulation of in 
vivo primed porcine PBMCs with tetanus toxoid was not expected to drive vigorous T 
cell proliferation or expansion. 
Treatment with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 alone or IL-2 plus IL-15 gave rise to a higher 
proliferative T cell response than treatment with tetanus toxoid in combination with IL-2 
plus IL-12, IL-18 or IL-21 (Figure 3.11, C, lower panel). This is probably due to the 
inherent ability of IL-2 and IL-15 to drive proliferation of conventional T cells [66], 
whereas the other cytokines are mostly involved in sustaining memory and naïve T cell 
populations [52], and in induction of IFN-γ production by T cells [78], [138]. 
Interestingly, I found that T cells treated with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 alone or IL-2 plus 
IL-15 mounted a proliferative response similar to that found for T cells treated with 
tetanus toxoid alone (Figure 3.11, C, lower panel). Whereas the first mentioned 
treatments preferably expanded cytotoxic T cells, the latter did not drive proliferation of 
T helper cells, nor cytotoxic T cells (Figure 3.13). The proliferative response observed for 
==PAGE:83==
69 
 
the T cells cultured without any cytokines (Figure 3.11, C, lower panel) must therefore be 
mounted by other T cell subsets, for example γδ T cells or NK T cells. However, this 
requires further investigation. 
It is peculiar that treatment with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 or IL-2 plus IL-15 preferably 
promotes proliferation of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, because tetanus toxoid specific T cells 
has been demonstrated to be mainly CD4+ in humans [149]. The activation of porcine 
CD8+ T cells might be driven by cross-presentation of the exogenous tetanus toxoid 
protein on MHC class I molecules presented by APCs. Another explanation for the 
preferential proliferation of cytotoxic T cells could be that the proliferative response 
observed is not antigen-specific. Since I have not performed a parallel IFN-γ release 
assay to verify antigen-specific activation, I cannot be completely confident that the 
observed proliferation is a response to stimulation with tetanus toxoid. This theory is 
further supported by the fact that treatment with tetanus toxoid and different cytokines 
led to up to 2-fold higher T cell proliferation compared to the control (Figure 3.11, C, 
lower panel). This number is quite high considering that only ~0.03% of the porcine 
PBMCs were specific for tetanus toxoid and that each cell did not undergo several 
rounds of proliferation (Figure 3.11, D). It is, however, very likely that some T cell 
proliferation is mounted by antigen stimulation, because the PBMCs used were derived 
from pigs which have previously shown strong responses to tetanus toxoid in an IFN-γ 
ELISPOT assay (Figure 3.9).  
4.3  Effects of cytokines on the CD8+ T cell phenotype 
Assuming that CD8βloCD11ahi T cells represent a pool of memory CD8+ T cells and not 
activated T cells, I found that tetanus toxoid in combination with IL-2 alone or together 
with IL-2 plus IL-12, IL-15 or IL-21 mounted similar proliferative responses of CD8+ 
memory T cells (Figure 3.14, A). Nevertheless, only tetanus toxoid and IL-2 alone or 
together with IL-2 plus IL-15 promoted overall CD8+ memory T cell expansion (Figure 
3.14, B), indicating that although IL-12, IL-15 and IL-21 drive proliferation, they do not 
sustain CD8+ memory T cell survival.  
The fact that tetanus toxoid and IL-2 alone favoured expansion of memory T cells was 
surprising, since the use of IL-2 for ACT has proved problematic due to its ability to 
drive T cell differentiation [52]. However, studies have shown that the use of IL-2 
dosages at 50-600 IU/mL together with short culturing periods (10-14 days) can promote 
expansion of human T cells with memory-like phenotypes [150], [151], which might 
explain my results.  
IL-21 mounted a significant proliferative response of the CD8βloCD11ahi subset 
compared to the control, but did not promote in vitro survival and expansion of this 
population (Figure 3.14, A&B). This was surprising, as IL-21 is involved in sustaining the 
longevity of CD8+ memory T cells in both human and mice [52]. The lack of porcine 
==PAGE:84==
70 
 
memory T cell survival following co-culture with IL-21 might be caused by differences 
between species. It is also possible, that co-culture of porcine T cells with IL-7 or IL-15 
would augment the effect of IL-21 on CD8+ memory expansion and survival, as these 
cytokines synergize with IL-21 to promote T cell proliferation in mice [76]. This should 
be further investigated.[52].  
Similar to IL-21, stimulation with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 plus IL-12 mounted a 
significant proliferative response (Figure 3.14, A), but did not favour CD8+ memory T cell 
survival (Figure 3.14, B). As previously mentioned, the primary in vivo function of IL-12 
in human is to induce IFN-γ production by T cells, but it also drives proliferation and 
differentiation of CTLs [78]. Consequently, stimulation of porcine memory CD8+ T cells 
with IL-12 might lead to T cell differentiation and ultimately exhaustion. This contradicts 
previous findings showing that IL-12 favours the generation of CD8+ memory T cells in 
mice [79], [80]. However, as these studies were carried out in mouse models, it is likely 
that IL-12 affects porcine T cells differently. Combining IL-12 with IL-7 or IL-21 would 
possibly drive porcine memory T cells expansion more efficiently, since these 
combinations led to less differentiated T cell memory phenotypes in a study on 
melanoma patients [14].  
The test of IL-18 for memory T cell expansion clearly demonstrated that this cytokine did 
not lead to porcine CD8+ memory T cell proliferation,  nor survival (Figure 3.14, A&B). 
Although there are indications that IL-18 accelerate memory CD8+ T cell proliferation in 
mice [152], it has been shown to promote production of IFN-γ by porcine T cells [138], 
and it is therefore not surprising that stimulation of porcine T cells with this cytokine 
does not favor CD8+ memory T cell survival, but probably rather leads to T cell 
activation, differentiation and exhaustion.  
Finally, there is the question of whether porcine CD8βloCD11ahi T cells actually represent 
CD8+ memory T cells and not activated T cells, as both has been suggested by Moreno et 
al. [104]. Assuming that the CD8βloCD11ahi phenotype corresponds to activated T cells 
and not memory T cells could explain why cytokines such IL-2 and IL-15 which are 
implicated in conventional T cell proliferation give rise to the highest expansion rates of 
CD8βloCD11ahi T cells. However, further investigations on the physical characteristics of 
the porcine CD8βloCD11ahi T cell subset is required to fully elucidate its immunological 
role in the porcine immune system. 
4.4  CD45RA upregulation by CD8+ memory T cells 
Porcine CD8βloCD11ahiCCR7-CD45RA+ cells are thought to correspond to human TEMRA 
cells [104], which are generally believed to be terminally differentiated, have poor 
proliferative capability and to express high levels of perforin [35], [36]. Based on a 
previous study by Moreno et al. [104], I assumed that all CD8βloCD11ahi T cells were 
==PAGE:85==
71 
 
CCR7-, and that the CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ phenotype therefore corresponded to TEMRA 
cells.  
One peculiarity of the results of the present study is the high expression of CD45RA by 
CD8βloCD11ahi CD8+ memory T cells, which is largely independent of how much the 
subset has proliferated (3.14, A&D). This implies that porcine CD45RA is not a marker 
for terminal differentiation. In a study by Carrasco et al. [153], they propose a model for 
CD45RA expression by human T cells in which CD45RA is thought to be an indicator of 
the time elapsed since the last antigen encounter, rather than a marker for terminal 
differentiation. They showed that CD45RA expression by human anti-tumor cytotoxic T 
cell clones was lost upon antigen stimulation. 2 weeks after antigen encounter, a longer 
phase of gradual re-expression of CD45RA was initiated and this re-expression phase 
was finalized around 8 weeks after stimulation [153]. According to this model, the high 
expression of CD45RA observed on the porcine CD8βloCD11ahi T cells is an indication 
that the cells have not met their specific antigen during the past 8 weeks. However, as up 
to 40% of the CD8βloCD11ahi T cells proliferated following tetanus stimulation (Figure 3.9 
A), the high expression of CD45RA on porcine CD8βloCD11ahi T cells cannot be due to 
lack of recent antigen encounter, and my results therefore conflicts with the model 
proposed by Carrasco et al. [153].   
An explanation for the observed results for CD45RA expression by CD8βloCD11ahi T cells 
could be that porcine CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ cells do not correspond to human TEMRA 
cells, and therefore behave differently. First of all, I have assumed that all porcine 
CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ cells are CCR7- and therefore carry a TEMRA-like phenotype, but 
because the anti-CCR7 antibody did not work, I cannot be sure that this is the case. 
Secondly, in contrast to human TEMRA cells which express high levels of perforin, the 
porcine CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ subset was not observed to express higher levels of 
perforin than the CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA- subset [104], further questioning whether 
porcine CD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ corresponds to the human TEMRA subset. 
4.5  Naive T cells and CD8+ memory T cell precursors 
In addition to the CD8βloCD11ahi T cell population, I identified two additional subsets: 
one which was CD8βhiCD11alo and one which was CD8βhiCD11ahi. Moreno et al. [104] 
have previously demonstrated that the majority of the CD8βhiCD11alo population was 
CD27+ and CD45RA+, and porcine CD8βhiCD11alo T cells were therefore thought to 
represent naïve CD8+ T cells. In line with the study by Moreno et al. I found that 
CD8βhiCD11alo expressed CD27 (Figure 3.15, B), but in contrast to their findings, I 
showed that freshly isolated CD8βhiCD11alo T cells were primarily CD45RA-, but 
upregulated CD45RA in culture (Figure 3.15, C), suggesting that CD8βhiCD11alo subset 
does not correspond to naïve T cells.  
==PAGE:86==
72 
 
Compared to Moreno et al. I further identified a CD8βhiCD11ahi population (Figure 3.16, 
A) which was expanded in culture no matter the treatment received (Figure 3.16 B). Due 
to a CD45RA expression profile similar to that of the CD8βloCD11ahi subset (Figure 3.16, 
C), I speculate that the CD8βhiCD11ahi population might represent a precursor to the 
CD8βloCD11ahi subset, which has started expressing high levels of CD11a, but have not 
yet undergone CD8β downregulation. However, this requires further investigation. 
4.6  The porcine CD8+ memory phenotype – a mystery 
In general, our results on porcine CD8+ memory T cells shows that there are still many 
unanswered questions regarding the phenotypic characterization of this subset. 
Assuming that CD8βhiCD11alo cells represents porcine CD8+ memory T cells, I here show 
that these are expanded less than 2-fold following antigenic stimulation with tetanus 
toxoid in combination with IL-2 or IL-2 plus IL-15. To obtain a successful clinical 
response in ACT, a larger expansion of the memory population would probably be 
required. Furthermore, a more profound elucidation of the phenotypic characteristics 
and properties of porcine CD8+ memory T cells is required before further investigations 
on phenotype skewing for ACT can be performed.  
4.7  CD4+ memory T cells 
Having looked into expansion of CD8+ memory T cells, I also investigated if it was 
possible to expand CD4+ memory T cells. I found that treatment with tetanus toxoid and 
IL-2 or IL-2 plus IL-15 promoted survival of CD4+ TCM cells, whereas tetanus toxoid and 
IL-2 in combination with IL-12, IL-18 or IL-21 did not sustain the CD4+ TCM subset to the 
same extent (Figure 3.17, A). For the CD4+ TEM population, treatment with tetanus toxoid 
and various cytokine combinations generally led to cell death, probably due to terminal 
differentiation and exhaustion (Figure 3.17, B).  
Interestingly, I demonstrated that treatment with Con A led to an upregulation of both 
CD27 and CD8α by CD4+ T cells, thereby skewing the CD4+ T cell population toward a 
central memory phenotype (Figure 3.17, D). In fact, more than 62% of CD4+ T cells 
carried a central memory phenotype after 7 days culture with Con A. This result was 
independent on co-stimulation with cytokines. 
As previously mentioned, CD27 is a co-stimulatory molecule, which is expressed on 
thymocytes and naïve T cells (both CD4+ and CD8+), and promotes T cell proliferation 
and survival. In human, CD27 is gradually downregulated after antigen-specific clonal 
expansion and during T cell differentiation into effector cells [144]. It is therefore 
surprising that Con A, which is a strong inducer of T cell proliferation and would be 
expected to drive T cell differentiation, leads to upregulation of CD27 by porcine CD4+ T 
cells. A similar tendency has been observed in two studies by Reutner et al. [99], [100]. In 
their most recent study [100], no downregulation of CD27 was observed for the CD4+ 
==PAGE:87==
73 
 
naïve, TCM or TEM population after 4 days incubation following stimulation with anti-CD3 
antibody and PMA/ionomycin. In the other study [99], T helper and CTL subpopulations 
showed even slightly elevated levels of CD27 expression after stimulation with anti-
CD3/CD27 antibodies and four days incubation. In both studies, the authors explain the 
lack of CD27 downregulation following in vitro stimulation as a consequence of 
differences between in vitro and in vivo cytokine environments. They hypothesize that 
the cytokine milieu needed for appropriate differentiation into effector T cells is not 
provided in vitro which causes the lack of CD27 loss [99], [100]. CD27 upregulation after 
in vitro stimulation of T cells with plate-bound anti-CD3 or CD3/CD2 antibodies has also 
been demonstrated for human T cells [154]. However, in another study where human T 
cells were stimulated with LPS pulsed monocytes and superantigen, CD27 
downregulation was observed [155]. This might suggest that T cell differentiation into 
effector cells and the collateral CD27 loss depends on the culture conditions in vitro, and 
could explain the lack of CD27 downregulation observed after in vitro culture of porcine 
CD4+ T cells. 
However, a more likely explanation for the lack of CD27 loss in porcine T cells could lie 
in the identity of the CD27 molecule. The porcine orthologue to human CD27 was first 
identified in 2012 [99]. Until then, the molecule was designated SWC2 (Swine Workshop 
Cluster 2). Based on its moderate homology to amino acid sequences of CD27 from other 
mammals including human, cattle and monkey as well as functional similarities to 
human CD27, SWC2 was identified as porcine CD27 [99]. It is therefore possible that 
SWC2 does not correspond to the porcine orthologue of human CD27, and might explain 
the unexpected upregulation of this molecule on porcine T cells following Con A 
stimulation.    
No matter what caused the unexpected CD27 upregulation by porcine T helper cells, it is 
unlikely that a vigorous T cell stimulant such as Con A should be able to drive an 
immense shift toward a CD4+ central memory phenotype as observed in this study. My 
results strongly question the use of CD27 as a single discrimination marker for porcine 
TCM cells, and suggest that a further phenotypical analysis including identification of 
markers such as CD62L and CCR7 is required for the identification of porcine T helper 
memory subsets. I am therefore not confident that the T helper populations investigated 
in this study are actually representing porcine memory T cells. 
4.8  Antibody obstacles 
As suggested by the discussion above, phenotypic characterization of porcine T cells still 
requires a lot of work. This is complicated by a less developed porcine immunologic tool 
box. In my study, the anti-CCR7 antibody used did not cross-react to pig although cross-
reactivity had previously been verified [100]. Furthermore, the anti-pig CD45RA (clone 
MIL13) did not give results comparable to those found for other anti-CD45RA clones in 
==PAGE:88==
74 
 
the literature (see section 3.2). Differential staining by the individual anti-CD45RA clones 
might be caused by recognition of different splice variants, or by genetic differences 
between domestic pigs and mini-pigs as well as variations in expression between 
animals of different age. The reason for lack of anti-human CCR7 cross-reactivity might 
also be caused by differences between pigs of different breeds. No matter the reason, 
limited availability of pig specific antibodies impedes the development of the pig as a 
large animal model, and a more complete immunological tool box is hence needed.

5  Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to exploit the pig as a large animal model for ACT. This 
requires the ability to expand porcine T cells in vitro, preferentially with a young 
memory phenotype. Based on results for human T cells, it was hypothesized that 
polyclonal expansion porcine T cells of up to 200-fold could be performed in vitro. In 
addition, it was hypothesized that antigen-specific porcine T cells could be skewed 
toward a memory phenotype and that these memory subsets could be expanded in vitro. 
Using the polyclonal stimulant Con A and round bottom 96-well culture plates, 6-fold 
expansion of porcine T cells was achieved. However, the result was not reproducible, 
and the fold change was very low compared to what has been observed for human T 
cells. In general, the results from the present study suggest that in vitro culture of porcine 
T cells require specific growth factors, which are not present in media optimized for 
human or murine T cell media. Hence, further studies on optimization of culture 
conditions for porcine T cells are required.  
 
Based on studies performed by another laboratory, it was assumed that porcine CD8+ 
memory T cells carried the phenotype CD4-CD8αhiCD8βloCD11ahi, and that a subset of 
these which were positive for CD45RA corresponded to the porcine counterpart of 
terminally differentiated human TEMRA cells. Ex vivo stimulation of in vivo primed porcine 
PBMCs with tetanus toxoid and IL-2 or IL-2 plus IL-15 led to up to 2-fold expansion of 
CD8+ memory T cells after 7 days culture. However, the majority of the expanded 
memory cells expressed CD45RA+, and this expression was independent of how much 
the cells had proliferated, suggesting that they were not terminally differentiated and 
hence did not represent human TEMRA cells. Thus, we succeeded in expanding porcine 
CD4-CD8αhiCD8βloCD11ahiCD45RA+ T cells, but whether this population represents 
porcine TEMRA cells remains unclear.  
 
Porcine central and effector memory T helper cells have been suggested to be 
characterized by a CD4+CD8α+CD27+ and CD4+CD8α+CD27- phenotype, respectively. In 
the present study, it was demonstrated that stimulation of porcine PBMCs with Con A 
shifted the phenotype of T helper cells toward central memory phenotype. As it is 
unlikely that a strong T cell stimulant such as Con A should be able to drive expansion 
of central memory T cells, my results question the use of CD27 as a single discrimination 
marker for porcine memory T helper subsets.  
In general, the present study shows that there are still major obstacles to be overcome 
before the potential of the pig as a model for ACT can be exploited. These include the 
establishment of optimal culture conditions for porcine T cells and the development of a 
complete immunological tool box. Finally, a throughout elucidation of the phenotypical 
characteristics of different porcine T cell subsets is also needed.

6 Future Studies 
Future studies should first and foremost focus on establishing optimal culture conditions 
for porcine T cells. These studies could include a test of media containing porcine serum, 
both autologous and allogeneic, as well as different nutritional components. 
Furthermore, the effect of using different combinations and concentrations of the 
cytokines already tested in this study could be investigated. It is also possible that 
another polyclonal stimulant than Con A would facilitate more T cell expansion. As 
already mentioned, performing a T cell enrichment step before T cell culturing might 
also provide better conditions for T cell survival, and this should be assessed. 
Our laboratory has six different in-house SLA class I molecules available. These 
represent some of the most common SLA alleles found in swine. Once optimal culture 
conditions for porcine T cells are found, the next obvious step would therefore be to test 
if SLA tetramers bound to cancer relevant peptides can drive expansion of tumor specific 
porcine T cells. Finally, if optimal culture conditions enable sufficient expansion of 
porcine T cells, an experiment testing ACT on a porcine cancer model should be carried 
out.

8.1  Appendix A  
When purchasing a new antibody, a titration is normally performed to find the optimal 
antibody concentration. It can, however, be difficult to decide on the best antibody 
concentration by visual inspection of the data only. One way to compare the signals of 
different antibody concentrations is by determining a so-called Stain Index (SI) for each 
concentration. SI is given by the ratio between the separation of the mean fluorescence 
intensity (MFI) of the positive and negative population, respectively, divided by two 
times the standard deviation of the negative population (see Figure 8.1). Hence, the 
higher the SI value of a given antibody concentration, the better is the separation 
between the positive and the negative peak [156]. 
 
Figure 8.1 The Stain Index 
The stain index is a measure of how good a given fluorophore (at a certain concentration) is to separate the 
positive and the negative population. It is calculated as shown in the equation, where MFI1=Mean 
fluorescence intensity of the positive population, MFI2=Mean fluorescence intensity of the negative 
population and SD=standard deviation of the negative population. Figure modified from [156]. 
Newly purchased antibodies for the antibody panel shown in Table 2.5 was titrated as 
described in section 2.4.2.1. The data was gated for singlet lymphocytes and separation 
of the positive and negative population after staining with each antibody concentration 
was inspected in a histogram. If relevant, an SI for each antibody concentration was also 
determined as described above.
