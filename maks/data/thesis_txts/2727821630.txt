Abstract
This thesis delves into the idea of automating a drone to follow a river, with the goal of
reducing the amount of human intervention when using drones to perform hydrological
measurements. There was a focus on deriving a solution operating with a minimal
amount of sensors and processing hardware, so as to allow more payload to be used for
hydrological sensors. A system was proposed based on using a convolutional neural
network to detect the drones position over a river, and provide control commands
based on this information. The system was tested below and above of the vegetation
line (low and high altitude), with two different control schemes, using velocity and
position commands.
In the end, the best combination of altitude (high, above the vegetation line) and
control scheme (position) achieved, on average, flights of up to 65 meters, and an
average autonomous percentage of 92.34 %. The proposed solution thus serves as a
building block to a neural network based approach for autonomous river following,
based on lightweight components, which decreases the amount of human intervention
needed for a data collection flight.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since the dawn of mankind, water has been a paramount resource, allowing for the
flourishing of society as we know it today. Early communities of the human species
first settled around the Fertile Crescent, consisting of the Tigris, Euphrates and Nile
rivers. The presence of these water bodies allowed humanity to transition to a seden-
tary lifestyle, heavily supported on agriculture and trade (both activities facilitated
by abundant access to water) [1].
The nickname Blue Planet certainly has its scientific backing: around 71% of
Earth’s surface is water, with about 96.5% of that being composed of oceans, making
the blue seas the most distinguishable feature of planet Earth when observed from
space. The remaining 3.5% of water in the globe consists mainly of water in the air
as water vapour, in rivers, lakes, icecaps and glaciers [2].
Rivers in particular consist of 0.00002% of the global water reserves, and 0.006%
of global fresh water reserves.
While the reader might find these numbers rather
small, rivers nowadays are still as important as they were millenniums ago. Human
economical activities and societal development are still heavily reliant on access to
water resources, even in countries which are not located in arid regions [3].
According to the United Nations1, the ongoing climate crisis has brought lower
availability and quality of water, for both drinking and agriculture, particularly in
poor countries. In light of the importance in water resource management, the field of
Hydrology is partly concerned with understanding the impact climate change has on
the natural water cycle, and therefore, on human activities [3].
1.1
Project Motivation
In the past few decades, water monitoring in both rivers and lakes has been supported
mainly by ground based stations (static in-situ), field measurements (non-static in-
situ) and space borne platforms. Due to the decreasing number of available ground
stations, the time-consuming efforts of field measurement campaigns, and the diﬀi-
culties in using solely space borne systems (due to their limited spacial and temporal
resolution), Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have emerged as a flexible alternative
to provide researchers with needed data for monitoring water bodies [4].

The interest in using UAVs for hydrological measurement tasks relies in a few
factors. UAVs provide a versatile approach, in the fact that payloads can be easily
changed for specific measurement tasks.
UAVs have been used for measurements
such as water level (orthometric height) [4], bathymetry ([5], [6]), and Water Surface
Elevation (WSE) [5]. A wide variety of sensors are used for these tasks, such as sonar,
camera-based laser distance sensor, and Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR). Some of
these sensors may even require contact with the water surface (see figure 1.1), while
others may be operated at altitudes up to 60 m above the water surface [4].

Furthermore, UAVs are capable of providing researchers with a higher temporal

of satellite data.
Despite all of its advantages, the use of UAVs is still not optimal, particularly
in river environments, as there are still burdens associated with the use of this tech-
nology. For example, there is a requirement to meticulously prepare flight plans to
ensure the UAV follows a relevant flight path to perform measurements. Often, the
aforementioned measurements require the drone to perform cross-sections at constant
distance intervals in the river. This means that the drone must essentially follow the
river whilst moving between cross-section locations.
Mission planning software for drone missions heavily relies on Global Navigation
Satellite System (GNSS) waypoints. While this is not a concern in large rivers, in

1 Introduction
In light of the aforementioned obstacles nowadays present in the deployment of
UAVs for hydrological measurements in riverine environments, the problem at hand
can be summarised: due to the unreliability of GNSS coordinates, and the large
human burden for the pilot, operation of UAVs in this environment is still challenging,
and requires extensive preparation.
We thus arrive at the motivation for the work developed in this master thesis.
Whilst automation cannot fully circumvent all the previously listed issues, this thesis
aims at studying how vision-based algorithms can be used to help alleviate the human
burden currently associated with the use of UAVs for hydrological measurements, and
proposing a solution which enables a UAV to autonomously follow a river without
relying on the use of GNSS coordinates.
1.2
Challenges
Riverine environments present several challenges for UAV autonomous flight, as they
are highly unstructured natural environments. Water surface can drastically change
its appearance, based on factors such as reflections, presence of foreign objects, and
changing lighting and weather conditions. These factors make water diﬀicult to de-
tect using conventional vision-based algorithms. Furthermore, riverine environments
present other challenges, such as the presence of overhanging branches, and dense
forest canopies, which can be a flight obstacle. Some of these aspects are illustrated
in figure 1.3.
Figure 1.3. Example of sun reflection on a river course. Coupled with the presence of

navigation of UAVs in riverine environments.

Furthermore, as it has been previously reported, drone flights for certain measure-
ments may require proximity to the water surface, while others may be performed
at higher altitude. Particularly at low altitudes in narrow rivers, the potential for

1.3
Research Question
The previous paragraphs roughly outline the major challenges encountered for au-

From a computer vision per-

ensuring a stable and safe flight. In light of this, a broad research question can there-

the literature review process:
How can an Unmanned Aerial Vehicle autonomously follow a river?

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
At the time of writing of this document, UAVs are more popular than ever. The
flexibility of these machines allows for the implementation of new solutions in a variety
of fields, as UAVs provide users with an unique level of accessibility to varied locations,
and can perform otherwise dangerous tasks for humans.
The science community has been a major player when it comes to the proposal
of new applications for drone platforms. From 2001 to 2021 roughly 80,000 papers
indexed with the keywords ”UAV” or ”drone”1 have been published, and there has
been in particular an exponential growth in publications related to UAVs since 2015
[7]. These numbers clearly illustrate that there is a large and still growing interest in
UAV applications, covering a plethora of fields.
2.1
General Applications
Over the last decade, UAVs have become a landmark of what robotics and automation
can achieve, in both societal and economical terms. Much of the interest in the use
of UAVs comes from the fact that a variety of sensors can easily be incorporated as
payload, thus expanding the tasks that can be performed by UAVs, in particular in
the field of Remote Sensing. As covered in the Introduction, drones present many
advantages when compared with other measuring approaches and platforms, which
includes higher temporal resolution and higher detail of the area covered.
A good example of the use of UAVs for remote sensing tasks includes agricultural
applications [8]. Within this domain, drones have been extensively used, in particular
fitted with sensors such as hyperspectral cameras ([9], [10], [11], [12], [13]). Beyond
this, drones have also proved to be powerful in the monitoring and management of
natural hazards [14], particularly in flood monitoring ([15], [16]), landslide monitoring
and management ([17], [18], [19]), post-earthquake damage assessment ([20], [21]), and
wildfire monitoring and mapping ([22], [23]).
The aforementioned tasks are usually performed from high altitudes, typically
with the required sensor mounted in a top-down view configuration. When it comes
to tasks beyond Remote Sensing, drones are faced with new challenges, such as flight
in indoor environments or in unstructured natural environments. A good example of
drone applications in diﬀicult and confined environments are those within the mining

Outside of indoor environments, outdoor environments can also prove to be a

problem which is of considerable interest for this project, which is the automated fol-

between the task of following a trail and following a river, the relevant subject matter
for this thesis. Between the two, the problem comes down to enabling a vision system
to recognise the ”path” that it must follow.
Giusti et al. [28] proposed the use of a deep neural network to automate a quadro-
tor to navigate through a forest trail. The collected dataset consisted of images of
three classes: looking left from the center of the trail, looking towards the center of
the trail, and looking right from the center of the trail. Therefore, the Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN), with the network architecture proposed in [29] was trained
for image classification, providing a probability of each of the aforementioned classes
(left, center, right) from images obtained from a frontwards facing camera mounted
on the UAV. The drone (see figure 2.1) was controlled with yaw (or heading) steering
commands derived from the output of the neural network. In wide trails, with even
lighting conditions, the drone was able to autonomously follow the trail for up to a
few hundred meters, but it encountered challenges in narrow trails, often crashing.

2.2
Drones in Riverine Environments
Having explored a variety of general applications for UAVs, the following section focus
on the relevant use case for this project: drone navigation in riverine environments.
Depending on the altitude of the flight, several challenges arise for drones navigating
in these environments. At low altitudes (below any existing tree canopies), the major
challenge consists of the existence of branches and other natural objects that can
behave as obstacles for the drone flight. Furthermore, it is also challenging to detect
the desired water regions with a generalised algorithm - changes in lighting, foreign
objects in the water, and unexpected reflections can be detrimental to the success of
some computer vision approaches.
==PAGE:22==
10

When it comes to high altitude flights (above any existing tree canopies), flying
will often rely on visual information to detect the river, and derive waypoints for
the UAV to follow. This can be diﬀicult to obtain in scenarios where, once again,
there are unstructured natural objects (trees) occluding the view of the river from a
downward facing camera at a high altitude. Similarly to low altitude flights, it can
also be a challenge to derive a general algorithm that is able to provide robust river
detection in all scenarios.
Despite the adversities and challenges of drone flight in these environments, suc-
cessful approaches have been proposed over the last 15 years. Therefore, the following
section will provide the reader with a chronological account of the implemented ap-
proaches within the field of drone flight in riverine environments.
The seminal work in this field consists of the implementation proposed by Rathi-
nam et al. [31] in 2007. The aim in this work was to enable a UAV, in this case a fixed
wing UAV, to perform autonomous surveillance and monitoring operations of river
boundaries, bridges and coastlines. The motivation behind this is that automation
can reduce operational costs, and account for scenarios where manned inspection is
not a viable solution. Using an algorithm based on a pyramid-linking image segmen-
tation and a Bayesian likelihood classifier, the system was capable of extracting river
image waypoints acquired from a downward facing near-infrared camera, and convert
these waypoints to GNSS coordinates. Thus, the UAV, flying at an altitude of 135
meters, was able to follow a river, with the average cross track error of the estimated
waypoints with respect to the centerline of approximately 9 meters.
Following this, in 2011, Yang et al. [32] proposed a new method for estimating
the range and bearing of landmarks to solve the SLAM problem for MAVs flying in
GNSS-denied riverine environments. To segment a river from images obtained from
a monocular camera in the MAV, a watershed algorithm followed by the Shi-Tomasi
Corner Detector allowed for the extraction of landmarks around the river surface for

3.1
Digital Images
Moving at a speed of about 300, 000, 000 m/s, electromagnetic waves color the world
around us. More generally known as light, these electromagnetic waves are composed
by a massless entity, the photon, that can be described by its energy, frequency
and wavelength. Energy sources emit light, travelling at different wavelengths and
frequencies. The human eye is capable of visualising electromagnetic waves emitted
between the wavelength range between 400 −700 mm - this represents only a small
portion of the electromagnetic spectrum, which ranges in wavelength between 100 m
and 0.1 nm [44].
For computer applications, the acquisition of light in a format readable by ma-
chines is the foundation of many disciplines within the fields of Robotics and Au-
tomation. Therefore, this section delves into the process of obtaining a color digital
image.

3.1.1
Optical System
As it has been described, light is a key component to capture images. In an outdoor
setting and during the day, a prime source of energy is the Sun. As light is emitted by
the sun, as it hits objects in the scene, that energy is reflected and can be captured by
a camera. But it is not as straight forward as one might assume - in order to acquire a
digital image, one must measure the amount of energy of the relevant electromagnetic
waves, but focusing the desired energy onto a measuring sensor requires that the light
reflected by the object or scene of interest converges to the sensor. This is where an
optical system comes in [44].
The main component of an optical system is a lens, a piece of shaped glass whose
task is to focus the incoming light onto the imaging sensor of a camera [44].
As
light leaves the initial medium (air) and enters a new medium (glass), the process of
refraction bends the light as it passes through the glass. A lens is usually a round
disk, protruding at its center on both sides, and smoothly converges on the edge of
the disk, forming a convex shape.

Upon being reflected by an object, parallel rays of light
intersect each other, both before and after passing through the lens, at F, the Focal
Point. The line where in which F lies, the optical axis, passes through O, a point in
the middle of the lens, and denominated optical center. The distance between O and
F is called focal length, f [44].
3.1.2
Imaging Sensor
Having focused the light rays reflected by the object of interest, it is now possible
to utilize an image sensor to record the image. Imaging sensors are made of a two
dimensional array of individual cells that measure and convert the incident light into
==PAGE:29==
3.2 Deep Learning
17
a voltage. This voltage can then be transformed into a digital number. There is a
direct relationship between the incident light and the obtained digital number, which
increases or decreases based on the amount of incident light. The sensors resolution,
meaning the amount of cells over the 2D grid is what determines the resolution of the
images obtained, as more cells results in higher resolution images [44].
To obtain a digital image, the sensor readings are converted into a pixel value
that ranges between the interval 0 −255. In the case of a greyscale image, 0 denotes
black, 255 denotes white, and the values between consist of a gradient of different
shades of grey. Thus, the final obtained greyscale digital image consists of a 2D array
of values between 0 and 255, and of dimensions equal to the resolution of the imaging
sensor [44].
In the case of color images, the imaging sensor works in a slightly different way.
Similarly to how humans perceive color, based on the intensity of red, green and
blue light (RGB), a color image is obtained by also measuring the intensity of the
incoming primary colors. To do this, three different sensors, each sensitive to only
one of the primary colors, can be utilized to measure the RGB intensities. Instead
of having three sensors, a much cheaper and common alternative is to have only one
sensor, where each cell is made sensitive to only one of the three colors. Using a Bayer
Pattern, half of the sensors cells are made sensitive to green, and the remaining cells
are evenly divided between red and blue. Using a demosaicing algorithm, the RGB
values for each sensor can be estimated [44].
A color digital image is therefore represented by three 2D matrices, where each
matrix describes the intensity of the pixel values for each color. Each pixel value
represents one of the 256 measurable different shades of each color, and the final
RGB image results in an image where each pixel value is a vector containing three
pixel values for RGB [44].

The structure of a CNN is usually composed of three main stages [48].

3.2.3
Transfer Learning
One of the biggest burdens associated with working with CNNs is related with the
training time. Even when using GPUs, training a CNN can be a rather long process,
and access to needed hardware is not always widely (and freely) available. Therefore,
aspects such as weight initialization are of prime importance, as this process has a
direct impact on the convergence speed of a network. As Glorot and Bengio [50]
reported in 2010, the use of random weight initialization can drive the layers into
saturation when using activation functions such as logistic sigmoid, and proposed
the use of non-linear activation function and a new initialization scheme to increase
convergence speed.
The poor initialization of weights can also lead to other problems, such as vanish-
ing gradients - a situation where the calculated derivatives in the network are small
and close to zero to such an extent that the weight changes have a negligible effect on
the network [45]. Moreover, the problem of exploding gradients also exists, behaving,
as the name indicates, in an opposite fashion related to vanishing gradients - the
derivative values are so large, that weight updates are essentially meaningless.
==PAGE:37==
3.2 Deep Learning
25
One method that allows to avoid all these issues, and improve training time con-
sists on the use of transfer learning. As the name indicates, this technique consists in
utilising the knowledge of a previously trained model as a starting point to train a new
model. Similarly to how knowledge is transferred in the human mind - a person who
can drive a car can more easily learn how to drive a bus over a person that has never
driven before - the use of transfer learning can be beneficial for similar classification
tasks [45].
Using the weights of previously trained models means that there is no concern
with weight initialization, and also presents an advantage when it comes to dataset
availability. Neural networks need considerable amounts of data to be able to gen-
eralize, but large amounts of data are not often widely available for certain tasks.
Therefore, due to the availability of a wide range of pretrained models, trained on
a wide variety of data, one can most likely find a model that has performed a task
somewhat similar to the one entailed by the desired dataset. By freezing the initial
layers of a pretrained model, these will act as feature detectors, whilst the unfrozen
layers can be adjust to fit the specific problem, and altered to the number of desired
classes [45]. All in all, transfer learning can be a powerful tool in allowing to train
models faster with small datasets, whilst keeping high levels of accuracy.
3.2.4
Network Training
Dataset sizes and architectures vary greatly. Therefore, there is no general set of
parameters for training a CNN. Hyperparameters consist of several parameters that
can be adjusted on a neural network, and are key to improve their performance. The
diﬀiculty of training a neural network arises from the fact that there is no analytical
formula that can be used to determine the right combination of hyperparameters for
a dataset [51]. There are several hyperparameters one can tune to improve model
performance, such as convolution kernel width, dropout, weight decay, learning rate
and batch size, to name a few [48].

Nowadays, society takes for granted the ability to precisely determine positions in the

of spaceborn platforms to enable precise localization makes up one of the many gears
that keep humanities’s machine running.
Global Navigation Satellite Systems consist of a constellation of satellite systems
orbiting the earth, which send signals that can be interpreted by GNSS receivers on
the earth’s surface. There are several prominent navigational satellite systems, oper-
ated by several governments: GPS, the first of its kind, and operated by the United
States government; GLONASS, developed in the late 1970’s by the former Soviet
Union, and nowadays operated by the Russian Federation; Gallileo, with the first
satellite launched in 2005, and developed and operated by the European Union; and

COMPASS, initially a regional satellite navigation system, nowadays encompassing
the whole globe, and managed by China [52].
The following section delves into a brief description of the functioning of GNSS,
covering topics such as World Geodetic System 1984 and error sources.
3.3.1
World Geodetic System 1984
Measuring position on a 2D plane is relatively simple. One must only fix an origin and
two perpendicular axis meeting at the origin to be able to measure the relative position
of other points in the space. When moving onto 3D, a new axis perpendicular to the
existing axis and passing through the origin is added. Whilst this seems relatively
simple, when attempting to develop a worldwide reference system, one is faced with
challenging obstacles.
As Newton first noted, Earth is not a perfect sphere - the poles are slightly flat-
tened, and the globe bulges at the equator. Furthermore, one would logically assume
the planet’s axis of rotation as one of the axis of a terrestrial reference system. But,
due to polar motion, the phenomena which describes the movement of the earth’s
rotation axis about the poles, this undertaking becomes apparently less trivial [52].
Nowadays, the standard for most mapping and navigation is the World Geodetic
System 1984 (WGS84).
This consists of a Earth Centered, Earth Fixed (ECEF)
Cartesian coordinate frame, which takes into account models such as the planet’s
geoid (extension of the idealized mean sea level over earth’s land surface), gravity
field, and fundamental constants, such as earth’s angular velocity and gravitational
constant [52].
Position in WGS84 can be measured with latitude, longitude and
altitude. These geodetic coordinates can be described as ([52]):
• Latitude - the angle measured between the perpendicular line to the surface
passing through an interest point and the equatorial plane. Values in the north-
ern hemisphere are positive, and in the southern are negative.
• Longitude - the angle measured in the equatorial plane between the meridian
where the point of interest lies and the reference meridian.
• Altitude (or height) - distance measured along the line perpendicular to the
surface between the point of interest and the geoid.
An illustration of the measurement of latitude, longitude and altitude can be seen
in figure 3.8. This coordinate system and coordinates consist of the positions where
in which GNSS estimate position on.

emitted by multiple spaceborn platforms reach the receiver. As satellites operate at

As any other electromagnetic wave, signals travelling from a satellite can be re-

4.3.1.2
AlexNet
AlexNet [55] is, by today’s standards, a quite simple neural network for image classi-
fication. The architecture consists of merely eight layers, and the models activation
function is ReLU. Originally published in the early 2010s, AlexNet helped shape
the deep learning landscape, as the approaches nowadays considered standard were
initially proposed in the original AlexNet paper.
The authors described the use of ReLU in a deep neural network for the first time,
as well as the use of artificial data augmentation to improve neural network learning.
The paper also describe the use of multiple GPUs to train DNNs.
The network won the ILSVRC 2012 classification task, and has since become a
benchmark. Therefore, the AlexNet architecture is also selected as one of the models
to be trained for the 3-class river classification problem. Due to its shallow layers,
it should provide acceptable inference speeds, and it can serve as a benchmark of
traditional feedforward networks for the classification task.
4.3.1.3
SqueezeNet
Having become the benchmark of deep neural network performance, a desire arose to
try to match the performance of AlexNet with smaller and more eﬀicient networks. [56]
thus proposed the SqueezeNet architecture: a smaller CNN which matchs the accuracy
of AlexNet on the ImageNet dataset, while using 50 times fewer parameters.
This model was designed with smaller filters, and has a smaller number of input
channels. The model employs the use of a building block named Fire Module, consist-
ing of a 1 × 1 squeeze convolutional layer, followed by an expand layer, with a mix of
1×1 and 3×3 convolutional filters. Furthermore, the architecture employs a strategy
of down-sampling late in the model, so as to produce larger activation maps, under
the assumption that larger activation maps equal higher classification accuracy. The
overall architecture consists of using the ReLU activation function, and one convolu-
tional layer followed by 8 Fire Modules, and terminating with a convolutional layer.
Dropout and Max Pooling are also employed.
This network achieved AlexNet-level of performance upon testing on ImageNet,
whilst the number of bytes needed to store the parameters of the model (model size)
was 50x smaller than AlexNet with no compression (240MB vs. 4.8MB). Due to the

then also selected to be trained for the 3-class river classification problem.
4.3.1.4
MobileNet
Neural networks can be created with several applications in mind. Therefore, it is
not surprising that networks have been created for the purpose of being implemented
for mobile and embedded vision applications. This is the case of MobileNets [57],
specifically designed to be eﬀicient and streamlined architectures.
MobileNet distinguishes itself from other architectures by the fact that it utilizes
depth-wise separable convolutions. A depth-wise convolution is applied as a single
filter to each input channel, followed by a point-wise convolution, which combines the
outputs of the depth-wise convolution.
By applying this factorization, both the computation time and model size are
drastically reduced, therefore making this network adequate for real time applications.
As such, due to its enhanced computational performance, the MobileNet architecture
is selected for the classification problem at hand.

4.3.4
Training
When training neural networks, optimizing hyperparameters is a challenging and
lengthy task. This stems from the fact that there is not a ”perfect” combination of
hyperparameters, as subtle changes to the dataset, architecture, optimizer, etc., can
completely alter the training outcome. Furthermore, finding the ”best” combination
takes its time: multiple combinations of hyperparameters must be tried, and the
training time for each neural network can range from a few minutes to days.
Therefore, hyperparameter sweeping pipeline was implemented in PyTorch, taking
advantage of the Weights & Biases (wandb) platform5. This platform in conjunction
with its Python library allow to perform hyperparameter sweeps - this allows a user
to define a range or a list of hyperparameter values that can be used to train net-
works in a sequential automated way, thus saving time and avoid having to manually
change hyperparameters after every network is trained. In this case, the alteration
of hyperparameters employs the use of random search [61].
The hyperparameter sweeping strategy is as follows (the described process was
exactly the same for all the four selected networks). Each networks’ hyperparameter
sweep was performed on the DTU Computing Center’s 6 High Performance Comput-
ing (HPC) cluster, which allows for the use of high performance GPUs to accelerate
training times for DTU students and faculty.
The networks were interchangably
trained on Tesla V100-PCIE-32GB, NVIDIA A100 80GB PCIe and NVIDIA A100-
PCIE-40GB GPUs, depending on the availability of these in the computing cluster.

sweep and their respective values or ranges were:

An extra technique was also employed to improve training performance.
The Re-

network, it is often observed that the training loss stabilizes, which can hint at the
fact that the model training is stuck at some local minima. This effectively means
the model is no longer learning. This can be due to the fact that the learning rate is
too high - therefore, reducing the learning rate once a plateau is observed in the loss
can help the model ”escape” the aforementioned local minima, and continue learning.
Upon the end of each sweep, a new sweep automatically begins with a new ran-

momentum. In order to optimize computing resources, the training pipeline was also
altered so as to terminate a sweep in the event of a non-reduction of the training loss
after an interval of 50 epochs.

Software Architecture
To achieve autonomous flight, the software is built upon the Robot Operating System
framework, using the melodic distribution release. This enables for the creation of
several nodes, essentially executable programs that can subscribe to/and publish data
to topics. This results in a modular approach to the software, where tasks can be
assigned individual nodes that can be altered to communicate with other nodes.
To establish a connection between the companion computer (running Ubuntu 18.04
LTS) and the flight computer (running PX4-Autopilot 1.13.3), the MAVLink14 mes-
saging protocol is utilized. As the intent is to run the application on ROS, the mavros
package was utilized, which enables ROS to use the MAVLink protocol.
By using the mavros package, the main node of the system is created, which
establishes the communication between the companion and flight computer. Several
14https://mavlink.io/en/, Accessed on: 17-05-2023
==PAGE:58==

4 Implementation
topics are thus available from the drone, including current GNSS location, IMU data,
arm/disarm status, etc.
In order to reach the goal of autonomous river following, several nodes where im-
plemented in ROS. The inference node was responsible with the real time inference
of the convolutional neural network. This node’s script(inference.py) starts by load-
ing the selected model through PyTorch. In order to ensure faster inference times
and lower memory load, the model is loaded with FP16, which means the model uti-
lizes 16 bit floating point numbers instead of 32 bit. The node accesses the webcam
connected to the companion computer using OpenCV to obtain frames in real time,
and each frame is fed to the neural network, which outputs a softmax prediction of
the probability of the input frame corresponding to the left, center and right class.
These probabilities are then published in individual topics.
The mavros package offers the user a plethora of topics related to the autonomous
control of a drone, upon the system being in offboard mode. With this in mind, two
control nodes were developed based on velocity and position commands.
4.5.1
Velocity Commands
Velocity commands can be published on the setpoint_velocity/cmd_vel topic of mavros
as TwistStamped messages. As the name indicates, these commands publish velocity
values on the xyz-axis and orientation of the /map coordinate frame in mavros, which
is relative to a fixed point in the world. Due to this, two nodes are necessary to enable
the use of velocity commands.
The transform node is responsible with providing the system with a coordi-
nate frame corresponding to the drones body frame.
This is done by calculating
the coordinate system transformation between the pose of the drone (/mavros/lo-
cal_position/pose, with format PoseStamped) and the fixed /map coordinate system.
Using the ROS tf package, a new coordinate frame, body, is published in the tf tree.
In order to provide the drone with actual velocity commands, the velcontrol
node is used.
An overview of this node can be seen in figure 4.14.
This node
subscribes to the individual probability nodes, published by the inference node, and
to the tf tree where the body frame is published. The node takes the probabilities,
and publishes TwistStamped velocity commands to the setpoint_velocity/cmd_vel
topic. The velocity command published depends on the probabilities received. If the
probability of left and right are below a threshold, t, the node sends a linear forward
velocity command with velocity v to the drone, making it fly forward.
If either the left or right probabilities are higher than the assigned threshold, the
node sends commands with an angular velocity input, or rotation speed, which alters
the yaw attitude of the drone, and sets the forward linear velocity to zero.
The
rotation speed (rot) is calculated based on the left (l) and right (r) probabilities, and
tuned by a value, α:
rot = −α(l −r)
(4.1)
==PAGE:59==

Figure 4.14. Flowchart of the velcontrol node. Legend: Yellow - decision if statement;
Blue - process. A more detailed version of this flowchart can be found in Appendix C.
The expected overall behaviour of the drone is that if the left and right probabili-
ties are low, the drone continues flying forward. If the drone is not pointing towards
the center, it is expected that the rotation speed will achieve its highest value at
the beginning of the rotation procedure, and decrease throughout the rotation, as the
right and left probabilities should decrease and become somewhat similar as the drone
rotates towards the center direction. Once the necessary threshold is met again, the
drone should move forward.
Furthermore, in order to ensure a constant altitude upon flying, the velcontrol
node also contains vertical velocity commands, in both instances of the drone flying
forward or turning. To do this, the node subscribes to the mavros/state topic, which
contains information on the currently used control mode. Moreover, the node also
subscribes to the mavros/local_position/pose topic, which contains the current pose
of the drone in the local frame.
In order to hold altitude, the node saves the last z pose of the local_position/pose
topic when the system is in manual mode, which becomes the target altitude, and,
upon switching to autonomous mode, the node constantly checks the current altitude,
and calculates vertical velocity commands based on the altitude error, meaning the
distance between the current and the target altitude.
4.5.2
Position Commands
Another option for sending control commands to a drone in offboard mode with mavros
consists of sending position commands. Instead of providing the drone with velocities
to define the movement in its axis, with this approach, setpoints in the drones local

frame are given for the drone to follow. The intent here is to have a node, poscontrol,
that makes the system follow a point that is updated with every inference instance
of the neural network, at a fixed distance and changing offset ahead of the drone. An
overview of this node can be seen in the flowchart in figure 4.15.
Figure 4.15. Flowchart of the poscontrol node. Legend: Yellow - decision if statement;
Blue - process. A more detailed version of this flowchart can be found in Appendix C.
In the case of position commands, the strategy for river following consists in
providing setpoints directly ahead of the drone, and offset by an angle. What the
inference probabilities yield for control purposes in this control scheme is also different
- in velocity commands, the rot variable consists of a changing yaw velocity, constantly
updated by the neural networks outputs.
In the case of position commands, the probability of the left and right class are
similarly calculated as in rot, but instead provide the needed offset angle to rotate
the target point to be followed, as well as define the heading the drone should be
facing. The offset angle, angleoff is calculated as follow, and scaled by a variable, α,
and with l being the left probability, and r the right probability:
angleoff = −α(l −r)
(4.2)
This node makes use of the /mavros/setpoint_raw/local topic, and shares some
similarities with the velcontrol node. The aforementioned topic publishes messages
of the type PositionTarget, which can receive a pose target (xyz), fixed velocities for
each axis, and a yaw value and rate. To calculate the desired point, a distance, d,
ahead of the drone is calculated based on the desired velocity, v:
d = v ∗0.01
(4.3)
The values for the xgoal and ygoal positions of the drone are thus calculated based
on d, angleoff, and the current yaw heading. To the current pose, the distance d is
added, and the offset is calculated using trigonometry based on xcurrent, ycurrent,
yawcurrent (the last 3 obtained from the /mavros/local_position/pose topic) and
angleoff:

xgoal = xcurrent + d ∗cos(yawcurrent + angleoff)
ygoal = ycurrent + d ∗sin(yawcurrent + angleoff)
(4.4)

velocity commands - with the main difference that no altitude error is calculated -
instead, the last measured altitude in manual mode (target altitude) is set as zgoal.
In terms of the velocities, these are calculated based on the desired velocity, v.
Finally, yaw heading is defined as the sum of current yaw heading and angleoff.

observed here, resulting in pilot intervention to correct a veering towards the
left of the river. Only the modified V2 version was used. Flight distances and
autonomy were generally higher when compared with low altitude flights.
• Position Control at High Altitude - the same right bias was also observed,
with pilot intervention also being necessary to correct left veering. However, this
control scheme resulted in the longest recorded flight, and one 100% autonomous
test flight.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
Having implemented and tested a solution, an analysis of the results is warranted.
The following chapter focuses on discussing several aspects related to the overall
system performance, such as the causes for behaviours observed at low and high
altitude, and determining which control scheme is more adequate for each altitude.
Beyond this, the chapter also details some of the hardware limitations observed on the
experimental prototype, and how this had an impact on testing. Finally, the chapter
concludes with a section detailing future work and improvements to ameliorate the
system performance.
6.1
System Performance
Operation of autonomous systems in unstructured natural environments is a chal-
lenging task. The testing of the proposed river following system for UAVs occurred
over the month of June 2023. Naturally, as testing occurred in different days, flight
conditions varied.
6.1.1
Weather Conditions
It is diﬀicult to exactly pin point how weather conditions affected the neural network’s
performance. Upon testing, no tools or specific hardware were used to record the
weather conditions. Furthermore, as testing did not always occur at the same time
of the day, the sun’s position varied over test flights. This then results in several
variables which can not be accurately described via quantitative data. Despite this,
it is of note that the undesired behaviour observed in the pendulum movement, as
well as the networks right side bias were observed throughout various testing days,
with wildly different weather and lighting conditions. Therefore, at this moment, no
correlation can be drawn between the weather during testing and these behaviours,
which therefore hints at something more inherent to the trained model itself.
6.1.2
AlexNet
From the four trained and tested CNNs to be utilized as the onboard classifier,
AlexNet provided the highest performance metrics, therefore being selected for imple-
==PAGE:86==
74
6 Discussion
mentation on the experimental prototype. Although having a rather simple architec-
ture, it is possible that the pooling layers of AlexNet are one of the main reasons that
this network performs better in this dataset. In order to properly classify each class, it
is expected that the spatial location of features on the river banks and water to be the
prime indicators of each class. From the selected architectures, AlexNet is the only
one that only uses Max Pooling layers in its architecture, which enhances the spatial
location of features. The remaining networks use Average Pooling, which essentially
smooths out features in the kernel neighbourhood.
This might be a contributing
factor to AlexNet’s higher performance for the classification problem.
In a general sense, the network performed as expected - it was able to output
predictions for the three classes in a timely fashion, as the network averaged an
inference rate of approximately 7 Hz over all the recorded test flights, providing a
robust enough inference loop for the real time system.
The network was trained
with a plethora of images captured in February 2023, from a dataset containing
images captured over an afternoon with varying lighting conditions (see figure 4.8 in
subsection 4.3.3).

As previously detailed in Chapter 5, the probability outputs provided by the neural

that the altitude from the water surface is irrelevant - for the solution at hand, a far
more relevant altitude is the one between the drone and the vegetation line.
In low altitude, in most flights, the pendulum behaviour invariably led the drone to
the left river bank, were pilot intervention often occurred to avoid crashing. However,

Stampedam. This had an effect on the ability to fly within line of sight, as the drone
remained visible to the pilot at longer distances. On the contrary, the pilot location
at Stampedam was much closer to the water surface, and surrounded by overhanging
vegetation, that complicated safe flying, due to more limited line of sight and radio
and video transmission limitations (to be further discussed in section 6.2).
6.1.5
Velocity and Position Control
At the early stages of this thesis, the initial control strategy used in testing was solely
velocity control (V1). When it became clear how this control strategy resulted in
unstable flights, and after attempting to alter the control algorithm to provide forward
velocity while yawing (V2), which also resulted in problems of its own (pendulum
behaviour), a new control strategy was theorised.
As such, the position control scheme was developed and implemented. The in-
tuition of developing this control scheme is that by utilising target positions and
the entirety of the output probabilities, this would result in a more responsive and
smoother flight, as all probabilities would be used to help center the drone during the
river following process.
This however was not the case - this control strategy helped highlight that the
low certainty probabilities are somewhat noisy, with a slight bias towards the right
probability. This resulted in the observed flights at low altitude with the P1 control
scheme abruptly yawing left, not allowing the system to perform a representative
flight, regardless of the different attempted combinations of flight parameters.
Due to time limitations, only one test was performed with P2 at low altitude,
which yielded comparable results with V1/V2. However, due to the limited number
of flights, it is not correct to state that the P2 method is the superior method at
low altitude, although it is strongly suspected that that is the case. Even though V2
presented lower average flight distance when compared with V1 (36.753 m vs. 40.980
m), it did display a more stable flight, and higher autonomous percentage (85.86
% vs. 81.43 %). V2 also proved to require less pilot intervention with 3.5 average
interventions per flight versus 4.33 for V1.
Therefore, the optimum flight control
scheme for low altitude from the data collected in this thesis is control scheme V2.
At high altitudes however, the conclusion is different.
For these flights, only
control policy V1 was used for velocity control - this was the case since that the
amount of yaw corrections at high altitude was so small that the difference between
V1 and V2 would be relatively minimal. In the case of position control, the difference
between the performance of P1 and P2 is glaring. The P1 control scheme resulted in
==PAGE:90==
78
6 Discussion
average flight distances approximately half the distance of P2 (33.467 m vs. 65.621
m) and a significantly lower autonomous percentage (78.61 % vs. 92.34 %). This
again highlights the negative impact of utilising the whole range of neural network
prediction probabilities to perform flight corrections.
Finally, between V1 and P2 at high altitude, the difference is rather smaller. P2
presents a slightly higher average flight distance over V1 (65.621 m vs. 61.070 m) and
a marginally smaller autonomous percentage (92.34 % vs. 92.50 %). Adding in the
fact that the average pilot interactions per flight for both V1 and P2 was 1.8, and
keeping in mind that P2 provided the only 100 % autonomous recorded test flight,
the P2 control scheme is narrowly considered to be the optimal approach for high
altitude flights.
6.2
Hardware
The experimental prototype presented in subsection 4.4 consisted of an initial starting
platform, which suffered several iterations over the course of the testing phase of this
project.
Namely, the initial FPV feed proved to be lacklustre in its performance,
causing issues as the drone flew further away from the pilot. Furthermore, the lack
of accurate altitude measurements, although not being crucial for testing, failed to
provide to additional relevant data on the drone’s operational altitude. The final
experimental prototype weighed 2.03 kg without batteries, with the extra components
needed for autonomous flight, the Jetson Nano and the webcam, weighing 176 and
75 grams, respectively.
6.2.1
Live Video Issues
The initial FPV setup of the prototype consisted of using a GoPro Hero 8 connected
via a micro HDMI cable to the Herelink Airunit onboard the drone. The expected
would be to receive the FPV imagery on the handheld Herelink remote controller. Un-
fortunately, this did not happen, as upon taking off, the video feed became distorted
with interference to the extent that the video was uninterpretable. This proved to be
a large setback for the testing phase, as onboard imagery is crucial for the monitoring
of the test flights.
It was speculated that the cause for this could be the proximity of the GNSS
antenna to the Herelink antennas, as these were mounted on the same fixture on
the drone, with the GNSS antenna above the Herelink antennas. A new fixture to
place the GNSS antenna further away from the antennas did not solve the issue.
After attempting a plethora of different micro HDMI cables, which did not solve the
problem, a new FPV system was implemented. Independently from the Herelink, a
digital FPV system was implemented in the drone, making use of a Runcam FPV
camera and a FrSky SkyVision HD receiver/monitor, with a HDzero tx5m.1 video

6.3
Future Work
As it has been discussed in this chapter, there are several improvements that can be
made to enhance the operational range of the proposed solution. Time constraints
prevented the implementation of some of the improvements henceforth proposed, and
are thus described in this section as future work.
6.3.1
Hardware
The final experimental prototype was not optimal - the FPV setup proved burdensome
during testing, and the failure in altitude measurements did not allow to gather extra
data to further define the operational altitude beyond below/above the vegetation line.
Regarding the live camera feed, it is clear that a more robust solution is necessary,
so as to allow the drone to operate at longer distances. As the area surrounding river
environments will always most likely consist of densely vegetated areas, populated by
obstacles such as trees, this presents some challenges to conventional FPV systems.

Regarding the altitude measurements, it is indubitably necessary to circumvent

towards the right probability in a variety of scenarios, in different lighting conditions
and flight locations.

surrounding areas had very particular features, it would be of interest to further

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Water bodies such as rivers can cover great distances, and performing hydrological
measurements in these locations can result in lengthy data collection campaigns. The
work developed in this thesis has demonstrated how Unmanned Aerial Vehicles can
autonomously follow a river, based on a low weight hardware approach. Taking into
account the solution’s performance, the work developed serves as a valuable building
block towards a solution capable of covering longer distances.
While the deployed CNN was trained for only one river in Denmark, the system
was still able to function in unvisited locations of the Mølleåen river, where no data
had been previous collected for training purposes. Although some shortcomings in
regards to inference were observed, these however still allowed the system to perform
as desired. It can certainly be stated that the proposed solution is limited by the fact
that it was trained for only one river, and that it is likely that the system would not
function in a different river location. There is, however, still an interest in being able
to enable UAVs to autonomously follow specific rivers. The collection of hydrological
data is not a one time event - the continuous collection and analysis of this data,
over a long period of time, allows to derive the needed conclusions on the evolving
characteristics of a water body.
The proposed solution was able to demonstrate the potential of using CNNs for
the purpose of river following.
Neural networks can be trained to solve a 3-class
problem based on real river collected data, and use output probabilities to input
steering commands to an autonomous UAV platform. From the proposed and tested
control schemes, collected data suggests that velocity control presents the best results
at low altitude, while position control performs better at high altitude.
All in all, the work developed for this masters’ thesis aimed at enabling a UAV
to follow a river autonomously, so as to reduce pilot intervention.
After several
rounds of test flights, which included different control strategies and flight altitudes,
it can be concluded that a drone, equipped with the proposed solution, is capable of
consistently following a straight river section for an average distance of up to approx-
imately 65 meters, with minimal human intervention. Furthermore, the proposed
solution complies with the low hardware weight objective, thus allowing for extra
payload weight to be utilized for relevant hydrological measuring sensors.
