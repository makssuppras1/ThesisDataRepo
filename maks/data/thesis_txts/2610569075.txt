Abstract
Countries like Denmark or Switzerland have set ambitious targets to reduce Carbon
Dioxide (CO2) emissions in near future. Conventional coal or gas power plants ought
to be replaced by an increasing share of Renewable Energy Sources (RESs). Instead
of containing primarily controllable generators, future Transmission Grids (TGs) and
especially Distribution Grids (DGs) will consist of a large number of inverter based
distributed generators. Intermittent electricity production stemming from Photovoltaic
(PV) generators or wind turbines will increase the loading in DGs. On the one hand,
overvoltages caused by high RES penetrations can have detrimental effects on grid
stability or security of supply. On the other hand, increased loading of networks leads to
costly power losses, which, for the Danish system alone, are worth over one billion DKK
per year. This master project is a part of the Indo-Danish ID-EDGe project aiming to
find new solutions for loss minimization in DGs.
In this thesis, a distributionally robust chance-constrained AC optimal power flow
(DRCC-OPF) operational planning tool for radial three-phase DGs is presented. The
method is based on an iterative scheme using uncertainty margins to reduce the feasibility
space of the Optimal Power Flow (OPF) problem in the planning stage.
This way,
stochastic power infeed originating from RESs is incorporated in the schedule which
reduces the need for real-time control actions. The model includes PV installations,
batteries, flexible loads, and an On Load Tap Changer (OLTC) transformer to increase
flexibility. The output of the program contains optimal set points for inverters, power
curtailment, battery operation, load shifting, and OLTC tap position.
The code is
written in the Python programming language and will be published on GitHub for
further collaboration. A case study based on the European CIGRE Low Voltage (LV)
grid showed that ignoring uncertain PV production in operational planning leads to
high violation probabilities of bus voltage constraints once the uncertainty is resolved.
Furthermore, a parameter variation for PV penetration was performed to show its effects
on power losses and demonstrate the modularity of the tool. Finally, an own power flow
calculation based on the Backward and Forward Sweep (BFS) method was implemented
and validated with the 141 bus MATPOWER benchmark case. The main contribution of
this project is the analytical reformulation of Distributionally Robust Chance-Constraint
(DRCC) which was developed for balanced TGs and had to be adopted for DGs using
a BFS-OPF scheme to replace the non-linear AC power flow equations. Further, the
OPF problem is designed to be solved by any off-the-shelf solver. Complex numbers
are consequently split into real and imaginary part to avoid dependency on commercial
software, which decomposes them internally.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In 2019 the Danish government set the target to reduce Denmark’s greenhouse gas
emissions by 70% in 2030 compared to 1990 [1] and being independent of fossil fuels by
2050 [2]. In the same year the Swiss government set its target to reach climate neutrality
by 2050 and halve its CO2 emissions by 2030 compared to 1990 [3]. These goals are in
line with the Paris Agreement stating that special efforts should be undertaken to keep
the global temperature increase below 1.5°C compared to pre-industrial times [4]. 40%
of the world’s CO2 emissions stem from the electricity and heat production sector [5].
The European Union has set ambitious targets to replace fossil fuel fired power plants
and switch to RESs. By 2030, 27% of its electricity should be generated by RESs [6].
Countries like Denmark already exceeded its obligations and it is expected that by 2030
the share of RESs will be 54% [7].
While from a climate’s perspective this development is highly desirable, it imposes
challenges to grid stability and power losses. In 2018 the total power losses for Denmark
were approximately 5% which results in high electricity costs for the end consumer
[8]. Furthermore, the intermittent electricity generation of wind turbine generators and
PV solar panels induce large imbalances to the grid. On cloudy days, solar irradiance
changes up to 60% per second are observed [9] which stresses the importance of new
strategies for grid operation. High shares of PV in LV DGs cause overvoltages that
inhibit an increased penetration [10]. In order to ensure safe and efficient grid operation
these uncertainties should be included for operational planning. Different approaches
including robust optimization ([11],[12]), chance-constrained optimization ([13],[14],[15]),
Distributionally Robust Optimization (DRO) ([16],[17],[18]) or scenario based methods
([19],[20]) were studied and improved in the last years to consider the stochastic nature of
RESs in OPF problems. OPF is broadly used for scheduling problems and aims to find
the optimal generation dispatch for a given time horizon including power flow constraints.
This project is part of the ID-EDGe project (Indo-Danish collaboration for data-driven
control and optimization for a highly efficient distribution grid). It aims to find solutions
to minimize losses in DGs due to unpredictable behavior of rooftop PV, small wind
turbines, and electric vehicles [21]. In the past, DGs were mainly designed according to
the fit-and-forget approach and upgraded by grid reinforcements [22]. With widespread
installation of smart meters the state of DGs can be observed in real-time, which
offers great opportunities.
As a part of this project, this master thesis develops a
standalone scheduling tool for distribution grids with DRCCs. The goal is to design
a modular program that can be used by industry and academia to perform studies

for loss minimization considering the stochastic power injection from inverter based
Distribution Energy Resources (DERs). The methodology used in this tool is applicable
for radial DGs for both single-phase and three-phase representations. Flexible units,
such as batteries, flexible loads, or OLTC transformers are included and parameter
variations for PV penetration, peak load, seasonal effects and asymmetric loading can
be performed. The case studies were performed based on real PV measurement data
from the DTU Risø campus. The tool is written in the Python programming language
which offers the possibility to test the algorithm in hardware-in-a-loop systems, such as
SYSLAB [23].
The main contribution of this project consists of the reformulation of DRCCs. The
method was originally developed for balanced TGs [24] and calculates uncertainty margins
shrinking the feasibility space for the OPF problem.
It was then used to include
Chance-Constraints (CCs) in unbalanced DGs by obtaining the uncertainty margins
from Monte-Carlo simulations in [25], based on the iterative BFS-OPF power flow
method presented in [26]. This project combines the analytical reformulation of CCs
from [24] and the BFS-OPF power flow method from [26]. Moreover, the OPF problem
is formulated in a way that to be solved by any off-the-shelf solver. To be independent
of commercial software that internally decomposes real and imaginary part of complex
numbers, they were consequently split into their real and imaginary part for all constraints

2.1.1
Power Grids
The electric power system is considered as the largest man made system and is split
into TG and DG. The European TG ranges approximately 500’000 km on voltage levels
between 110 and 400 kV [27]. The voltage level is the key distinction between TGs
and DGs and decreases from 400 kV to 0.4 kV in LV DGs. In addition, the X/R ratio
indicating the ratio between reactance and resistance is highest for TGs and becomes
smaller with decreasing voltage level. Finally, TGs are heavily meshed to ensure a stable
grid even if single lines or generators trip. DGs, on the other hand, finally connect
households to the grid and are often radial or weakly meshed.
As mentioned before, the electric power system comprises several different voltage
levels. The TG is operated at the highest voltage level, in Europe between 150 and
400 kV, and serves as international connection. Transmission System Operators (TSOs)
monitor and control the state of the TG and maintain balanced operation. DGs are
operated on a lower level and are further split into the High Voltage (HV), Medium
Voltage (MV) and LV grid ranging from 0.4 - 60 kV [28].
They are operated by
Distribution System Operators (DSOs) and are historically less actively controlled. Transformers
connect the different voltage levels and create a synchronous area.
The X/R ratio is defined by the reactance divided by the resistance of a line. In TGs
the reactance is usually much larger than the resistance to avoid active transmission
==PAGE:20==
4
2 Literature Review
losses. This is of importance since line lengths are significantly longer than in DGs.
With decreasing voltage levels the resistance of the lines or cables increase and can
exceed the reactance in LV grids. Due to the high X/R ratio in TGs the influence of
active and reactive power to the voltage magnitude and angle can be decoupled. Thus,
active power primarily influences the voltage angle, while reactive power impacts the
voltage magnitude. However, as the voltage level and X/R ratio decreases in DGs this
strict separation is not valid anymore and active power gains influence on the voltage
magnitude. As a result, injecting reactive power in LV grids has a smaller effect on
voltage support than in high voltage grids [29]. Other than the TGs, DGs are often radial
or weakly meshed. The different topology has implications on power flow calculation
methods, which will be explained in the following section.

Transmission Grids
For TGs a variety of solution algorithms are used to solve the non-linear AC power flow
equations. Examples are the (Gauss)-Seidel iterative algorithm, or the Newton-Raphson
method. Since Equation (2.2) is non-linear, there is no analytical solution. Therefore,
iterative solution algorithms are used to solve them on computers. with the Newton-Raphson
algorithm being predominately used. It is based on the Taylor expansion and is a method
to find the solution of a generic non-linear function f(x) = 0. It uses the Jacobian matrix
to linearize the non-linear function around the point of interest until convergence is
reached. One disadvantage of this well-established algorithm is that for each step the
Jacobian matrix has to be re-computed which is computationally expensive.
To overcome the computational burden linear approximations to Equation (2.2) have
been developed.
A popular way to linearize the full non-linear equations is the DC
approximation. Assuming small angles, nominal voltages at all buses and neglecting
resistance in the lines, the nodal power balance becomes linear and only dependent
on the reactance and voltage angle difference. However, reactive power and losses are
neglected which disqualifies this approximation for this project.
Distribution Grids
While the aforementioned solution algorithms are efficient and robust for balanced TGs,
they have shortcomings when it comes to ”ill-conditioned” DGs [31]. As described in
Section 2.1.1, DGs are inherently unbalanced and contain a wide range of X/R ratios.
Therefore, different algorithms have to be used for studies in DGs.
One popular approximation used in [17] is the LinDist Flow.

approximation neglects losses and is, therefore, not suited for this thesis.

2.1.3
Three-Phase Power Flow
For balanced conditions, especially present in TGs, single-phase representations are
often used to reduce complexity and computational effort. However, DGs are inherently
unbalanced, thus requiring to model all three phases and their influence on each other.
In Europe, DGs are usually four wire three-phase networks consisting of three phases
and a neutral conductor [36]. In a fully symmetrical four wire network there is no current
flowing in the neutral conductor. In reality, asymmetric changes in loads or production
lead to unbalances across the phases which are compensated by the neutral conductor
[37]. Hence, the impedance of the neutral wire has to be taken into account for power
flow calculations. It is common practice to reduce the primitive 4 × 4 impedance matrix
with the Kron reduction [38] to obtain a 3 × 3 representation of the grid that can be used
by standard power flow algorithms, such as the BFS scheme presented in Section 2.1.2
[35]. Implications of the Kron reduction on power losses and special considerations will
be explained in Section 2.1.5.
In contrast to single-phase representations, there is a need to quantify voltage unbalances
in three-phase systems and include it in the OPF problem formulation. According to
EN50160 the Voltage Unbalance Factor (VUF) has to be smaller than 2% for 95% of
the week [39]. In literature there is a variety of definitions of the VUF. [40] lists three
different definitions of the VUF:
(a) VUF = max voltage deviation from average line voltage
average line voltage
(b) VUF = max voltage deviation from average phase voltage
average phase voltage
(c) VUF = negative sequence voltage component
positive sequence voltage component
The positive- and negative sequence voltage components are obtained by the symmetrical
components analysis, which is widely used to perform fault analyses [41]. The positive
sequence system describes balanced three-phase operation. In this condition there is no
current flowing through the neutral point. Therefore, the negative- and zero sequence
components are zero for this ideal condition. If the system is unbalanced the negative
sequence component becomes non-zero. Hence, it is a measure to describe the deviation
from normal operation.
Depending on the magnitude of the VUF the deviation of definitions (a) and (b) are
very small. Since this work does not include fault analysis nor symmetrical components,
the approximation (a) is used to quantify the unbalance at the nodes. Further details
on the calculations the VUF are shown in Section 4.4.2.

flow. In this case, single values for branch current and bus voltage become 3 × 1 column

2.1.4
Optimal Power Flow
OPF is a powerful extension of the Economic Dispatch which determines the optimal
dispatch of units connected to the power grid. While the Economic Dispatch neglects
power flow limits and purely minimizes total costs, OPF extends the optimization
problem with a model of the grid. A generic optimization problem is given by:

with the decision variables x and state variables u. The function f(x, u) is called the
objective function. Equality constraints are aggregated in h(x, u), while g(x, u) collects
the inequality constraints of the optimization problem (2.4). The OPF problem can
either be single- or multi-period.
The decision variables are optimized by the solver to minimize the objective function.
Usually, they contain the power output of generators, inverter set points, or BESS
charging/discharging power, if present. As their name suggests, state variables define
the state of the system. Depending on the application, this could include voltage and
branch current magnitude, phase angles, or state-of-charge of BESSs.
The objective function plays a key role in OPF because it determines the goal of
the optimization. Usually, it contains the cost function of production, but it can be
extended with environmental costs, security of supply, or component degradation costs.
The function h(x, u) contains all equality constraints of the model.

demand. If the network model further contains for example BESSs, their power output
is included in the nodal power balance, too.
The inequality constraints g(x, u) contain the limits of the decision and state variables.
This is of special interest because, as we will see in Section 2.2, these inequality constraints
can be formulated as CCs. Chapters 4 and 5 introduce and explain all constraints needed
for this project.
2.1.5
Power Losses
As stated in Chapter 1, the goal of this project is to create a tool that allows for different
studies to minimize distribution losses in DGs. While it is straightforward to calculate
active power losses in a single-phase representation, special considerations apply for
three-phase systems.
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Chance Constraints
9
The key drivers for power losses in DGs are overloaded feeders and unbalanced loads
[42]. Losses can be calculated either by multiplying the impedance of a line with the
square of the branch current, or by calculating the difference between the power injected
at both ends of a line. Using the first method for a 4 wire three-phase system, the line
current in the neutral conductor has to be calculated explicitly to obtain the total power
loss. However, if the impedance of the reduced 3 × 3 matrix obtained by Kron reduction
is used, multiplying the impedance with the square of the three branch currents leads
to erroneous results [42]. The reason is that the reduced 3 × 3 matrix is created by a
mathematical reformulation and does not directly represent the full 4 × 4 matrix. In
contrast of using the primitive 4 × 4 matrix, the power losses in each individual phase
of the reduced 3 × 3 system are not correct. However, the sum of losses of all three
phases delivers the correct result [43].
For this project the power flow calculations are done with the reduced 3 × 3 matrix.
Therefore, multiplying the square of branch current magnitudes with the impedance
would lead to erroneous results. To obtain the correct result, the losses are defined as
the difference of power input and output of each branch: sLoss = i∗
Br · (vfbus −vtbus),
with the subscripts fbus and tbus reading as from-bus and to-bus. The active power losses
are then given by the real part of the apparent power losses.
2.2
Chance Constraints
This section provides an overview over the current state-of-the-art of chance constrained
optimization. First, the motivation and theoretical background is explained in Section 2.2.1.
Second, CC reformulations assuming Gaussian distribution are presented in Section 2.2.2.
Section 2.2.3 lists sample based methods. Finally, Section 2.2.4 presents an important
extension of traditional CCs - DRCCs.
2.2.1
Theory
The methods presented in Sections 2.1.2 and 2.1.4 assume perfect information about
production and consumption of electricity. Obviously, this is never the case in reality
and both supply and demand are random variables.
As described in Section 2.1.4,
OPF calculates optimal decisions over a given time horizon and objective function. In
the past, the majority of power plants connected to the grid were dispatchable. Thus,
uncertainty of production was significantly lower than today and the future, where DERs
will increase their share. Therefore, TSOs and DSOs must account for intermittency of
RESs in operational planning.
The concept of Chance-Constrained programming was developed as extension of
stochastic programming and aims to optimize a function containing random variables
while maintaining inequality constraints at a prescribed level of probability [13]. Recently,
CCs were applied for power systems, specifically for OPF problems [44]. Following the
notation of [15] and generalizing the problem to a random variable X the standard CC
is given by Equation (2.5):

The main advantage of taking assumptions of distributions is that the reformulation
results in a closed-form expression. Hence, the number of constraints remains constant
and is independent of the number of data available [45].

2.2.3
Sample Based Methods
As stated in [19], sampling based methods substituting the CCs with a finite number of
constraints obtained by a large number of samples leads to a high number of constraints
with increasing decision variables which is prohibitive for large systems. Instead, the
authors define a minimum number of samples to be drawn in order to enforce low
violation probabilities, based on a hybrid approach proposed in [20]. The advantage
is that no assumption on the distribution of the forecasting error has to be taken.
Furthermore, the number or samples required is independent of the number of decision
variables, but on the dimension of the uncertainty vector, or size of the network. For
large networks, this still leads to a high number of constraints, which is computationally
expensive.

2.2.4
Distributionally Robust Chance-Constraints
DRO aims to eliminate shortcomings of assuming known distributions due to their
dependence on single probabilistic models [52]. The authors describe DRO as a game
against nature. Here, ambiguity sets A are used which include a wide range of possible
distributions of realizations of random variables. The game is a two-stage process, where
the modeler first optimizes its objective function according to the expected value. Then,
nature chooses the distribution from the ambiguity set that harms the modeler most.
Therefore, the modeler has to choose the worst-case distribution from the ambiguity set
to enforce performance guarantees [52].
As their name suggests, DRCCs allow for robust optimization independent of the
distribution of the forecasting error.
The authors of [14] extend their reformulation
for known distributions to ambiguous sets of distributions. [12] further develops the
reformulation method proposed in [14] and presents a case study comparing it to deterministic
OPF and CCOPF.
An interesting reformulation for DRCCs in DGs is presented in [49]. There, a convex
approximation of CCs using the Chebyshev generating function is presented. It uses a
linear approximation of the power flow equations from [53], assuming lossless networks,
however. Finally, the reformulation approach results in a convex problem that can be
solved efficiently in real-time. Similar to other reformulations, the CCs are represented
by standard SOC constraints. The authors state that the CCs are guaranteed to be
satisfied for exactly known distributions, but show that even for estimated first and
second moments (mean and variance) drawn from samples the CCs are satisfied with
high accuracy. An important feature is that solving time is independent of the amount
of measurements since the first and second moment are computed offline. However, the
amount of measured data significantly influences the result.
A modular distributionally robust reformulation method is presented in [50]. The
authors introduce an analytical CC reformulation which is valid for a large range of
(known) distributions. A prerequisite is that the first and second moments of the random
variable of interest exist and can be estimated from historical data. The general form,
adjusted to the conventions of this thesis, is given by:

[24]. Depending on the choice of A the CC reformulation is applicable to Gaussian

Φ. As stated in [50], Equation (2.7) can be interpreted as a nominal constraint that

Finally, data-driven approaches became popular recently and are used in [18]. According

They outline the dependence on limited data and indicate that sampling errors from

lead to intractable problems. The majority of methods above lead to conic programs
which can be solved by standard solvers.

Implementation
The tool is written in the Python programming language and was developed and tested

The Python programming language offers a wide variety of packages and is used
broadly in both industry and academia. There are several reasons for choosing Python as
the programming language. First, as an open-source high-level programming language, it
allows for efficient programming and offers a wide variety of extensions. Second, Python

GUROBI solver.
GUROBI is used as the solver to solve the OPF problem.
While Python is an
open-source project, GUROBI is a commercial solver, but is widely available at least in
academia. Furthermore, GUROBI can be used in different programming languages, such
as C++, MATLAB, or Java. Additionally, it offers a powerful interface for Python that
allows for efficient definition of OPF problems in a human readable way. Nevertheless,
the equations derived for the OPF problem are independent of the solver. Therefore,
any other open-source solver can be used to solve the OPF problem.

3.4
BFS-OPF Scheme
As described in Section 3.3 the BFS-OPF scheme is implemented as a sub-routine. It
imports the uncertainty margins of the current iteration m to perform the calculations.
By linearizing the power flow equations around the current operating point, the resulting
OPF problem is convex. Hence, it can be solved efficiently and obtains a global optimum.
However, this does not imply that this operating point lies in the feasibility space. To
enforce a feasible solution the optimal values are passed to an exact BFS run to project
the solution into the feasible space. The resulting bus voltages are then handed back to
the OPF solver to re-calculate the global optimum according to the updated voltages.
This iterative scheme has converged as soon as the deviation of the solutions from the
OPF and BFS lies below a predefined threshold. Since voltage angles in LV DGs are
small [33] the convergence criterion solely contains deviations of the voltage magnitude.

3.5
Monte-Carlo Validation
Due to the intermittent power generation of PV fields the optimal schedule obtained by
the DRCC-OPF might lead to constraint violations in real-time operation. To validate
the results a Monte-Carlo simulation was implemented. It receives the optimal schedule
from the DRCC-OPF scheme and runs power flow calculations for a specified number of
samples. The samples can either be artificially generated or imported from real data. It
was decided to use the BFS method for power flow calculations because of its efficiency
and robustness for radial DGs. Furthermore, the same sub-routine as for the BFS-OPF
scheme can be used, thus reducing the complexity of the program.
As described above, the first step is to import the optimal schedule for the given time
horizon. The optimal schedule contains set points for the inverters, battery charging/discharging
state, the tap position of the OLTC transformer, and the effective load for each node.
The load at each bus depends on control actions, such as load shifting or shedding and
is thus also included in the schedule.
For the Monte-Carlo simulation it is assumed that the set points are fixed and no
real-time control actions are implemented. This means that the stochastic PV generation
influences not only the active power, but also the reactive power output of the inverters.
With the fixed set points and the sample data the actual net nodal apparent power is
calculated and passed to the BFS sub-routine. This is repeated for all time steps and
samples and finally exported to the result file. With this result the number of violations
of the branch current and bus voltage magnitude are calculated and compared with
the predefined violation probability. The number of constraint violations can also be
interpreted as the possibility that the DSO has to perform real-time control actions.
3.6
Forecast
The optimal schedule is generated according to the forecasted PV power output. Forecasting
techniques are a separate research field, thus the forecasts used for this project are either
based on historical data or created randomly. The forecast script forecast version to be
generated as input parameter.
Real data from the DTU Risø campus is available for this project. There, three
PV installations can be used for laboratory experiments.
At SYSLAB a 400 V LV
grid is operated with, among others, the three aforementioned PV installations. The
total installed capacity is 24 kWp with 8 kWp for each house [23]. The measurements
at SYSLAB are taken in 1s resolution and for this project data from the year 2019 is
used. To investigate seasonal effects two forecasts are calculated, one based on data in
summer and the other representing winter solar production. The two seasonal forecasts
are obtained with data from 100 days around the 23rd of June for summer and the first
100 days in the year for winter. For the forecast, measurement data is imported, cleaned
and finally averaged over the specified time horizon. The cleaning step removes possible
”nan”s or negative values in the measurement data. The measurements from the two
data sets are normalized and then multiplied with the installed capacity of each node.

such as coal or nuclear, are connected to the grid by synchronous machines delivering
three-phase AC power. PV panels, on the other hand, generate a Direct Current (DC)

the only power source are PV installations.

PV and BESS:
RESs such as PV or wind share the characteristics that their Marginal

5.1
General Form
A generic chance-constraint for vector x according to is defined in Equation (5.1)
[47],[45].
P{x ≤xmax} ≥1 −ε,
(5.1)
with (1 −ε) the confidence level that the constraint is fulfilled with high probability.
Typically, ε is 0.05 which means that Equation (5.1) is met with 95% probability. Vector
x is dependent on stochastic PV production and could be represented by current or
voltage magnitudes. In this form, constraint (5.1) is not tractable, since it leads to a
semi-infinite problem. Therefore, it has to be reformulated to be incorporated into the
OPF problem. The approach presented in [45] uses a linear approximation of the random
variable around its forecasted operating point. By introducing uncertainty margins, the
feasibility space for the OPF is reduced and the solution is robust against the assumed
distribution of vector x. Note that the distribution of x is of interest, not the distribution
of the forecasting error ω. For linear relationships, the distribution of x will follow the
distribution of ω, which is not the case if non-linearities are present. The iterative scheme
offers the advantage of linearly dependent uncertainty margins in the OPF formulation
which are re-calculated in an outer loop according to the updated operating point. In
the following, the linearization and calculation of the uncertainty margin will be derived.

value. Since the only source of uncertainty in this project is the PV production pPV, the

7.1.1
Power Flow Validation
The results of the power flow validation showed that the results have a high accuracy
at low computational cost.
Nevertheless, this BFS scheme has limitations and it is
important to know its limits of applicability. On the one hand, it is only applicable
for radial or weakly meshed DGs. Moreover, in this tool only radial networks can be
investigated, but it could be extended to weakly meshed grids [35]. This is of interest in
densely populated areas where rings are more present than in sparsely populated rural
areas. It is worth noticing that the extension for weakly meshed grids only requires a
change in the BIBC and BCBV topology matrices. Hence, the applicability of this tool is
not limited to radial grids. On the other hand, one has to be aware that depending on the
number of wires in the network the results do not directly correlate with real values. For
three-phase three lines networks this method calculates the correct individual branch
currents. However, for three-phase four wire networks, the Kron reduction has to be
performed, which results in a 3 × 3 reduced matrix. If the branch currents or power
losses per phase are of interest, this method cannot directly be used. As explained in
Section 2.1.5, the sum of the power losses for all phases is correct, but the losses for
the individual phases are not. For the scope of this project the total power losses are of
interest. Therefore, the three-phase BFS scheme is well suited for the studies that can
be performed with this tool.

7.1.3
Chance-Constraint Reformulation
The core part of the project is the CC reformulation and its integration in operational
planning. In Section 6.3.3 the results of the case study described in Section 6.2.3 were
presented. Here, they are further explained and discussed. The results showed that
without accounting for stochastic PV production the probability of overvoltages exceeds
the acceptable range (see Figure 6.18). Assuming Gaussian distribution for the voltage
profile reduces the violation probability close to the predefined value. However, only
if the profile were perfectly Gaussian, the CC reformulation would be tight [50]. The
distributionally robust reformulation shows empirical violations below the predefined
threshold, which implies overly conservative decisions obtained by the OPF.
There are multiple reasons why the voltage magnitude profile is not perfectly Gaussian
and, therefore, exceeds the predefined violation probability.
First, only for a linear
relationship between Gaussian forecasting error and current or voltage magnitude the
realizations would be Gaussian distributed, too. As described in Chapter 4, the approximate
BFS method is used to replace the non-linear AC power flow equations. However, the
relationship is still not fully linear due to the square root to calculate the magnitude
of currents and voltages. Only if imaginary parts were discarded throughout the OPF
formulation, the relationship would be linear. As seen in Figure 7.1, the voltage angle
is lowest for bus 14. Therefore, non-linearities from the square root are largest.
Second, and closely linked to the first point above, to obtain a convex constraint for
the lower voltage bound, the voltage phasors are rotated to lie in the same feasibility
space (see Equation (4.20)). Assuming small angles the imaginary part is discarded and
the lower bound of the voltage magnitude is represented by its real part. This introduces
an additional modeling error which is largest for bus 14.

Third, as shown in Figure 6.22 the branch current profile is evidently not Gaussian

Fourth, the power ratio used to calculate the uncertainty margin (see Equations (5.9b)

As a result, the actual power ratio
might deviate from the anticipated value and, therefore, the sensitivity of fluctuating
PV production on the bus voltage is different than calculated. Theoretically, the actual
power ratio can be used to re-calculate the uncertainty margins, but this intensifies
convergence problems and affects the performance. Therefore, it was decided to use the
predefined power ratio γ. From Figure 6.23 it becomes clear that this simplification
might be too strong, where the actual power ratio is zero. The absence of the power
ratio in the OPF problem is a shortcoming of this method, which was also listed in [45].
By externally calculating the uncertainty margin a convex problem is obtained, but no
global optimality can be guaranteed. Note that including the power ratio into the OPF
problem leads to a non-convex optimization problem which has no global optimality
guarantees, either.
Finally, an out-of-sample analysis is performed.
This means that not the same
samples are used for the Monte-Carlo simulation than for the OPF calculation. This
phenomenon is also known as the optimizer’s curse, where the out-of-sample performance
is often disappointing [55].
Lower violation probabilities than anticipated for distributionally robust reformulations
are also observed in e.g. [50] and shown in Figure 6.21. The most general reformulation
solely requires the existence of a mean and variance which can be estimated based on
historical data. As its name suggests, the distributionally robust case is robust against
the worst-case distribution in this family. The Wasserstein metric is a popular tool used
for DRO to define the family of distributions [55]. As explained in Section 2.2.4, DRO
is a game against nature, where nature tries to harm the optimizer as much as possible.
Following a robust approach, the optimizer should take the worst-case distribution in
an ambiguity set sharing the same properties. This ambiguity set can be imagined as
a sphere. The worst-case distribution of this ambiguity set will lie on the surface of
the sphere. The more general the assumption of the distribution, the larger the sphere
gets. For the present case, the ambiguity set includes all distributions with existing
mean and variance. If the empirical distribution lies closer to the more strict Gaussian
distribution, it will lie closer to the center of the sphere.
Since the optimization is
performed assuming the worst-case distribution on the surface of the sphere, this results
in an overly conservative solution. The choice of the ambiguity set is an important step
which heavily influences the performance of the reformulation. The ambiguity set should
be large enough to contain the actual distribution with high confidence. However, if it
includes too general distributions, this leads to overly conservative decisions [55], which
was observed in Figure 6.21.

7.2
Numerics and Applicability
This section discusses the methodology and implementation and lists limitations of the
applicability of the tool. First, numerical problems will be explained. Second, comments
on modeling accuracy and assumptions provide an overview over the applicability of the
tool. Finally, the scaling properties for larger systems will be discussed.
As explained in Section 3.2, the program contains an iterative scheme calculating
and updating the uncertainty margins. The absolute value of the margins is not limited
by design and can theoretically exceed the physical limits. For the branch current, this
could result in larger uncertainty margins than the upper bound of this specific branch.
The bus voltage is constrained by a lower and upper bound. If the symmetrical margins
exceeds half of the feasibility space, the bounds are overlapping. Obviously, these cases
lead to infeasible problems for the OPF.
It was observed that for highly correlated
measurement data the bounds exceed these physical limits. This is one of the reasons
why for the case study the historical data from SYSLAB is assumed to be independent.
The computational effort depends, among others, on the number of iterations for
the uncertainty margins. To avoid jumping between two solutions a smooth update
was implemented with the following logic.
Instead of directly applying the updated
margin to the BFS-OPF problem, a weighted sum of the old and updated margin is
passed to the solver. This way, the change in boundary conditions is less drastic and
convergence should be enforced. However, this scheme comes at cost of more iterations.

In this project a reasonable balance of accuracy and computational efficiency was sought.
As explained above, the correlation of the measurement data is assumed to be zero.
Additionally, generic samples were generated according to historical data. Experiments

the tool is applicable.
Finally, computational efficiency is important when it comes to large scale problems.
For this thesis a time step of 15 minutes was chosen. This coincides with the standard
time resolution for the European intraday market. However, more countries are adopting
shorter time steps, e.g. 5 minutes, or even continuous intraday trading. As mentioned in
[9], solar irradiance can change up to 60% per second. This indicates that the traditional
operational planning might not be suited to react to the increasing share of fluctuating
DERs. In this project, the schedule contains optimal set points for the inverters and
curtailment factors. Given the highly fluctuating solar irradiance, it is questionable how
meaningful a fixed inverter set point for a 15 minutes time interval is. One reason for
the 15 minutes interval is the computational burden. In the current implementation the
model building takes a high share of the total runtime. Table 7.5 lists the total runtime
and percentage of each part of the OPF for the three-phase 18 bus test network with
96 time steps. Here, the formulation of the constraints has by far the biggest share. A
further breakdown into the individual constraints showed that the constraints for the bus
voltage and branch current use roughly 90% of the time used to formulate the constraints.
As shown in Equations (4.14) and (4.17), the topology matrices are multiplied with
the nodal injected current. While the BIBC matrix is sparse, the matrices R & X as
real and imaginary part of the multiplication of BIBC and BCBV matrices, do not
contain any zeros. Leveraging the sparsity of the BIBC matrix and only executing the
multiplication for non-zero entries the constraint building time was improved by 40%.

This chapter concludes the thesis by summing up the key findings and functions of the
tool in Section 8.1 and providing an outlook for future research in Section 8.2.
8.1
Summary
The goal of this master project was to develop a flexible optimization tool for loss
minimization using distributionally robust chance constraints to consider uncertain power
injection of distributed generators for operational planning. The tool described in this
thesis is capable of calculating an optimal schedule for radial distribution grids including
batteries, flexible loads, PV installations, and an OLTC transformer at the reference
node. The topology, installed capacities, variable limits, or load profiles are imported
from external files and internally processed to a standard form.
Both single-phase
and mutually coupled three-phase networks can be studied, provided the availability
of the respective data (e.g. mutual impedance matrices for three-phase grids). Real
data for PV production can be imported and used for operational planning. However,
better performance of the CC reformulation was observed by endogenously generating
samples based on historical data. Policies for reactive power injection of inverter, the
planning horizon and temporal resolution, or solver settings are defined in a single file
which is imported by the main file.
All equations and methods are independent of
commercial software, however, the implementation for this thesis was done with the
GUROBI interface for Python.
An own power flow calculation program was implemented and validated with a
MATPOWER reference case. Further, a parameter variation for PV penetration was
presented. It was shown that increasing PV penetration coupled with flexibility stemming
from batteries or flexible loads can help to reduce losses in the distribution grid. The
chance-constraints reformulation was tested and demonstrated for a simplified network.
The results showed that neglecting stochastic power infeed from RESs leads to overvoltages
in real-time operation. Assuming Gaussian distribution for the voltage magnitude is
too strong an assumption and leads to slightly higher empirical constraints violations
than anticipated. By expanding the family of distributions, the distributionally robust
case showed that the predefined violation probabilities are met, but tend to be too
conservative. This phenomenon was identified and discussed.
A literature review was presented in Chapter 2 to provide the theoretical background
used for this project. Then, the structure and implementation of the tool were explained
in detail in Chapter 3. In Chapter 4 all equations used to formulate the OPF problem
==PAGE:92==

were derived. Afterwards, Chapter 5 showed the analytical reformulation of the CCs.
Chapter 6 and Section 6.3 defined the case study and showed its results. Finally, the
discussion and limitations of the results were presented in Chapter 7.
8.2
Outlook
The increasing share of RESs pose a big challenge for stable and efficient grid operation.
Incorporating uncertainty into the operational planning and actively manage DGs are
actual research topics. In this project some problems were not fully resolved and further
work could be done in the following directions:
Computational efficiency:
To use the tool for larger systems efforts in computational
efficiency should be taken. The model building takes a large share of the total runtime.
Furthermore, linear approximations of branch current and bus voltages, and replacing
the inverter capacity with piecewise linear functions would reduce complexity of the
optimization problem and decrease solving time.
Chance-Constraint reformulation:
The CC reformulation does not include compensation
actions by controllable devices to balance fluctuating solar energy. Participation factors
are often used in TGs to balance the intermittent renewable energy production with
conventional generators [47]. The share of conventional generators in DGs is smaller,
and other balancing methods should be found to minimize power curtailment. Ideally,
the procurement of reserve capacities is included in the OPF problem. Furthermore, the
uncertainty margins are not bound by design. To avoid infeasible problems caused by
overlapping boundaries, a method should be found to prohibit the margins to become
too large. This is especially urgent if heavily correlated networks should be studied.
Convergence issues:
The uncertainty margins for branch currents do not converge
properly and jump between solutions. It is assumed that the cause is changing power
flows in times when the local demand and production are similar.
Optimality guarantees of the DRCC-OPF scheme:
Due to the iterative method
the current scheme does not include global optimality guarantees. While the convex
OPF problem obtains globally optimal set points according to the uncertainty margins
calculated at each iteration, the uncertainty margins are not guaranteed to lead to a
global optimal solution. If the uncertainty margins are included in the OPF, the resulting
problem is not convex. Finding a convex approximation of the respective constraints
could lead to globally optimal decisions.
Time resolution:
In this project a time resolution of 15 minutes is used. For real
world application a higher resolution would be preferable for several reasons. On the one
hand, fluctuations in supply and demand happen in orders of seconds instead of minutes.
==PAGE:93==
8.2 Outlook
77
Therefore, fixed inverter set points for long time intervals are not meaningful. On the
other hand, the forecast accuracy drops significantly the further the realization is in
future. Continuously updating the forecast and adjusting the schedule accordingly would
significantly improve the real-time performance and computation time [54]. Obviously,
this requires a computationally more efficient implementation.
The source code of the tool will be uploaded to a public repository on GitHub for
further collaboration. It is hoped that the tool will be used by different stakeholders to
further improve it and extend its applicability.
