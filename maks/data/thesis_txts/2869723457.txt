Abstract
This thesis analyses the changes in sea level and sea ice at the port of Thule from 2016
to 2024. It uses tidal measurements, GNSS interferometric reflectometry (GNSS┬¡IR), and
satellite┬¡based microwave sensors for sea ice. The aim is to assess whether GNSS┬¡IR
can be used to monitor sea level and ice dynamics in coastal areas.
The results show that GNSS┬¡IR measurements for sea level change of 1.45 mm/year,
while tide gauge (TG) measurements show a sea level change of 3.76 mm/year. The dif┬¡
ference may be because some calculations do not include the bedrock uplift. In addition,
the GNSS┬¡IR data show a clear agreement between GNSS┬¡IR sea level and TG sea level.
A more detailed analysis of 2023 shows that GNSS┬¡IR measurements may well be used to
estimate the ice freeboard. A trend analysis indicates a growth in freeboard height of 1.5
mm/day during winter. These results show that GNSS┬¡IR can contribute to improved sea
ice monitoring near coastal areas, thereby strengthening climate research and predictions
of sea level rise and sea ice dynamics.

Introduction
When seawater freezes in the worldÔÇÖs oceans, sea ice forms, significantly impacting polar
regions and local and global climate conditions [1]. Sea ice the most important role in
regulating the EarthÔÇÖs temperature by reflecting sunlight and influencing ocean currents
and atmospheric patterns [2].
Different measurement methods are used to measure sea ice extent and sea level, and
they complement each other to ensure accurate and reliable data. Three primary mea┬¡
surement systems are used: satellite┬¡based, airborne, and ground┬¡based as the Global
Navigation Satellite System (GNSS). GNSS measurements are an important reference,
as they help verify and calibrate airborne and satellite┬¡based data. Through airborne mea┬¡
surements, sea ice dynamics and their impact on climate can be better modeled, allowing
a better understanding of their impact [3].
Combining these technologies ensures a more accurate understanding of how sea ice
evolves, which is crucial for climate research and predictions of future sea level changes.
1.1
The Arctic climate
Changes in Arctic ecosystems caused by the loss of sea ice impact humans and animals
in the Arctic region and beyond [4].
The Arctic climate is cold. This is caused by the fact that less solar energy can reach
the polar areas. Ice and snow cover the poles, which give the Arctic a high albedo effect
that keeps it cool. The climate at the poles is where the temperature rises the most.
In the Arctic, the temperature increases three times the global average. Due to snow
and ice melting, more solar energy is absorbed due to the albedo effect. Sea ice losses,
glacier melting, and the melting of the Greenland ice cap are caused by significant regional
warming [5].

Ice┬¡albedo feedback is one of the most critical mechanisms in the Arctic climate sys┬¡

energy absorption and further warming. This positive feedback loop contributes to the
accelerated warming in the Arctic. Here, a temperature increase is three times faster

The climate systemÔÇÖs sensitivity is important to this development, and the Arctic reacts
more strongly to these changes. Arctic sea ice is also affected by population density, and
shipping in the Arctic is more significant than in the Antarctic. This makes sea ice in the
Arctic more vulnerable [7].
Arctic ice reflects the sunÔÇÖs energy back into space, so the warmer the Arctic Ocean be┬¡
comes, the more ice is lost. This could raise sea levels and melt Arctic permafrost, which
in turn would amplify global warming. However, the effects of Arctic changes are not
confined to the region [8]. Scientists have claimed that there is a link between the winter
storms and colder winters in the middle northern hemisphere and the warming at the pole,
which results in warmer weather in the Arctic. Areas in the polar region are home to a
whole ecosystem that relies on ice for food and survival, which is changing because of
melting ice [9].

1.2
Sea ice
Sea ice is frozen seawater. Because salty water makes up sea ice, it has a lower freez┬¡
ing point than freshwater. When the seawater temperature reaches ÔêÆ1.8 ÔùªCelsius, ice
forms, and this sea ice will act as an insulating layer between the seawater and the atmo┬¡
sphere [13]. In addition, salinity influences the porosity of the ice, its mechanical strength,
and thermal properties. The sea ice reaches a maximum in March and a minimum in
September. As sea ice forms, it is driven by both thermodynamics and wind and ocean
currents, causing its movement [8]. Ahead of sea ice formation, the water goes through
several freezing processes. First, the seawater cools, but with colder seawater, the wa┬¡
terÔÇÖs density increases═¥ thus, the cooled water sinks and the warmer water flows with the
sea surface [13].
Before the satellite era began around 1970, the understanding of this sea ice was limited
to expeditions that crossed the ice or to submarine measurements initiated in 1958. Sub┬¡
marines collected upward┬¡looking sonar profiles, which were then converted to determine

the thickness of the sea ice. These measurements were primarily taken for navigation and
defense purposes. By measuring the height above the water surface of sea ice, satellites

1.2.1
Sea ice thickness
Sea ice thickness can be measured and estimated using several methods. By drilling,
airborne sensors, submarines, and satellites [13]. Before seawater freezes and forms
sea ice, the water must cool down. When this happens, it increases the waterÔÇÖs density
and thus becomes heavier. This layer of cold, heavy water will sink and be replaced
by warmer water. This process will continue until the top (and warmest) layer of water
reaches the freezing point of seawater. When the freezing point is reached, sea ice will
form. For the water to continue to cool, it must lose its heat. This heat exchange occurs
through the ice═¥ more sea ice will form as the water gets colder. As the ice gets thicker,
this heat exchange will become more challenging. The ice will stop growing when the
heat loss stops between the water and the atmosphere. The thickness of the ice at this
point is the maximum thickness of the sea ice and is called the thermodynamic equilibrium
thickness [13]. To assess the future implications of further reduction in ice cover, long┬¡
term observations of the thickness of Arctic sea ice are needed [15]. The sea ice in the
Arctic Ocean generally ranges in thickness from 0 to 5 m during the winter seasons [8].
1.2.2
Sea ice concentration
Sea ice concentration is the amount of ice that covers a specific area, usually expressed
as a percentage. Sea ice concentration describes the proportion of an area of the ocean
that is covered by sea ice, referred to as the total area, and this is stated as a percentage.
When the concentration of sea ice is 100%, the entire area is covered by sea ice. The
passive microwave method is a good way to monitor sea ice concentration, which is why
satellites are often used. As a result of these satellite observations, it is possible to monitor
the annual variations and trends in sea ice. To determine whether an area is covered by
ice, it is usually divided into grid cells, and the total area covered by ice is determined
by summing the areas of each cell times their fractional concentrations. There is often
a threshold of 15┬¡20% when looking at area and concentration, which means that if the
selected grid cell contains less than 15┬¡20% ice, it will be assumed that there is no ice
[16].
Sea ice concentration represents the amount of ice covered by a given area, while sea
ice extent represents the total area covered by sea ice. Concentration looks at smaller
areas. Sea ice extent is just a number (given as a percentage = 15%) where if the sea
ice concentration is at least 15%, then the total area is defined as ice, and this is for both
thick ice, densely packed ice, and areas where there is more dispersion of the sea ice.
1.2.3
Sea ice dynamic
As sea ice grows, melts, and moves constantly, it changes dynamically. It is affected by
the tides and ocean currents, which determine where and how the ice is located. It is
common for sea ice to form near the poles and therefore makes in┬¡situ measurements
more difficult [17].

1.3
The purpose of the project
This project aims to prove that GNSS┬¡IR can be used to tell something about sea ice.
It is especially in coastal areas that there is interest in this regard. This thesis focuses
on the sea ice in Thule Port. Thule is an interesting place because a GNSS receiver is
set up with a view of the sea at the Port in Thule. The Port is also the location for a tide
gauge, which makes the location suitable, as this tide gauge will be used to validate the
GNSS┬¡IR measurements by comparing the water level variations with the reflected signals
from the sea ice. This provides a good opportunity to investigate the relationship between
GNSS┬¡IR data and changes in the thickness and extent of the sea ice. This analysis aims
to assess whether GNSS┬¡IR can tell about the sea ice dynamics. If this analysis works
and the signals show sea ice, it can help improve sea ice monitoring near coastal areas.
Data from 2016┬¡2024 will be included in the analysis to cover a sufficiently long period.
Potential long┬¡term trends and seasonal variations can be observed with an appropriate
time interval.

2
Theroy
This chapter presents the concepts related to GNSS┬¡IR, tide gauges, space┬¡based mea┬¡
surements, and the necessary elevation and correction techniques.
GNSS┬¡IR is a technique that utilizes reflections of GNSS signals from the EarthÔÇÖs surface
to determine parameters such as sea level and sea ice extent [18]. GNSS┬¡IR comple┬¡
ments traditional tide gauges that measure sea level variations using pressure sensors
in coastal areas. By combining these methods, sea level changes can be studied more
comprehensively locally and globally.
Satellite measurements contribute to monitoring sea ice and sea levels as well. The Ad┬¡
vanced Microwave Scanning Radiometer 2 (AMSR┬¡2) sensor tracks the concentration of
sea ice and is used to confirm its presence. Validating GNSS┬¡IR and tide gauge mea┬¡
surements can more accurately observe trends.
2.1
GNSS interferometric reflectometry
GNSS┬¡IR is used for environment sensing. When measuring the GNSS antenna phase
center position, reflected GNSS signals (multipath) are one of the significant sources of
error. In addition to allowing accurate measurement of environmental signals, multipath
effects can also be measured with geodetic quality GNSS instruments. Unlike carrier
phase data for positioning, these multipath studies use signal┬¡to┬¡noise ratio (SNR) data.
Due to the distance between the receiver and the satellite and the antenna gain pattern,
SNR data are dominated by long┬¡period variations. When these long┬¡period variations are
removed with a low┬¡order polynomial, the multipath effect is all that remains. As GNSS
multipath measures the distance between the antenna and the reflecting surface, higher
water levels produce the minor reflector heights and lowest modulation frequencies [19].
The GNSS and Global Positioning System (GPS) satellites transmit radio waves in the
L┬¡band at approximately 20 cm wavelength. Each satellite transmits multiple carriers in
slightly different frequencies, L1, L2, and L5, all in the same band, so the wavelength is
about 20/24/25 cm. There are two reflections when getting a reflection. There will be
one in the same polarization (right┬¡handed) and another in the opposite polarization (left┬¡
handed). The proportion between the two reflected signals will depend on the surfaceÔÇÖs
elevation angle and material properties. The GNSS┬¡IR is designed with two antennas.
One is looking up to get the direct signal, and one is looking downward or sideways to
get the reflection signal. This method requires an additional antenna and receiver. This
method applies to both space┬¡ and air┬¡borne platforms. The GNSS┬¡IR method tracks a
single channel per satellite. The direct and reflected radio waves are combined to track
the resulting multipath [20].
GNSS satellites are visible in the sky longer than other satellites. Geostationary satellites
that stay in the same place in the sky can not be used for GNSS┬¡IR═¥ moving satellites
are required. The orbital plane inclination is approximately 55 degrees concerning the
equatorial plane, leaving a gap at the northern and southern poles. The azimuth angle
goes from 0 to 360 degrees═¥ the elevation angle is zero in the horizontal direction and 90
degrees at the zenith [21]. Figure 2.1 illustrates the principle and relation between the
elevation, azimuth, and zenith angles.

GNSS┬¡IR uses signals from GNSS and GPS to make reflection┬¡based measurements of
sea level. This is done by using the GNSS signals reflecting off a surface and thus an┬¡

signals. These signals must be precise measurements to analyze which surface the re┬¡

as in classic GNSS use. The interference that occurs comes from the reflected and direct
signals. As the reflected signal has a delay, it creates an interference pattern when the
signals are combined. This interference pattern is called the signal┬¡to┬¡noise ratio (SNR)
[22].
The reflected signal observed by the GNSS system has a more significant effect on low
satellite elevation angles. The direct signal multipath creates interference patterns over
time by interfering with the reflected signal (red). The Reflector Height (RH) is estimated
based on these changes (constructive and destructive) in the interference pattern [23][22].

reason: the antenna gain pattern near the horizon cannot properly distinguish directly

any quadrants. The ground┬¡based antennaÔÇÖs height affects the frequency of the power
signal but not the magnitude. It is best to place the antenna at a high point for the logistics
of installing the antenna and the signal processing benefits [25]. It is important to have
coherence for GNSS┬¡IR because waves on a water surface will scatter the radio wave
in all directions, making it impossible to detect coherent signals. Mirror┬¡like reflections or
specular reflections are reflections of smooth surfaces. There will be a different interfer┬¡

3.3.1
Algorithm for AMSR┬¡2
This updated method utilizes numerical weather predictions (NWP) and radiative transfer
models (RTM) to simulate the microwave radiation intensities a satellite observes. Simu┬¡
lations such as these determine how atmospheric influences affect satellite sensor data,
which is crucial for accurately determining sea ice concentration (SIC).
Before RTM can simulate brightness temperatures (TB), meteorological data must be
adjusted in time and space. A central time for the satellite scan is identified, and NWP
data matches the closest observation time. Interpolation is then applied using Gaussian
weighting of NWP data. RTM is then used to calculate simulated TB for each pixel in the
satellite data.
Figure 3.2 illustrates the flowchart for processing Level 2 and Level 3 SIC for AMSR┬¡2
and SSMIS data. In this thesis, AMSR┬¡2 data is used. The input consists of Level 1 SIC
data from AMSR┬¡2 and SSMIS, supplemented with NWP data, which helps determine
how much atmospheric influence is present in the TB measurements. RTM simulates
microwave TB for the frequencies selected for SIC algorithms.
Figure 3.2: Sea ice concentration processing flow chart [36].
The processing starts with correcting for land influence near coastlines, where land radi┬¡
ation can interfere with sea ice observations. A first guess (FG) of SIC is calculated using
the NASA TEAM method and static reference points. Observed TB is then corrected for
atmospheric influence using NWP data. The final SIC calculation is based on the cor┬¡
rected TB values and FG SIC, followed by error estimations. This results in a Level 2
product representing SIC for a single satellite swath file.

To obtain a Level 3 product, tie points are refined, and statistics are computed for all
daily swath files. Tie points represent the microwave emission signatures of open water
(SIC=0) and entirely ice┬¡covered areas (SIC=100). Instead of static tie points, which may
be affected by sensor operation, atmospheric changes, or climate variations, the OSI SAF
algorithm utilizes dynamic tie points. These adjust for seasonal variations and minimize
errors due to sensor drift and atmospheric fluctuations, using daily observations and a
30┬¡day moving average.
The tie┬¡point calculation consists of multiple steps. The FG SIC is estimated using the
NASA Team algorithm as a starting value. NWP data and RTM are then used to remove
atmospheric influence from TB measurements. The corrected data is clustered into two
groups: SIC=0 and SIC=100. The final SIC is calculated using a 30┬¡day moving average
of tie┬¡points and OSI SAF SIC values derived from specific geographic areas to deter┬¡
mine daily means and standard deviations. Data availability is tracked through automatic
alerts: a drop of 40 points triggers a warning, while a drop of 80 points signals a more
serious issue. These alerts help detect data supply problems that could affect the quality
or coverage of the SIC product (L3).
The RTM process begins by obtaining meteorological parameters such as air tempera┬¡
ture, wind speed, and total atmospheric water vapor from ECMWFÔÇÖs high┬¡resolution model
(0.5┬░ x 0.5┬░). The new method uses RTTOV, which requires additional parameters, in┬¡
cluding vertical temperature profiles. It operates at a higher spatial resolution of 0.25┬░ x
0.25┬░ with 3┬¡hour updates. RTTOV has two configurations: ÔÇÖclear skyÔÇÖ (no clouds) and
RTTOV┬¡SCATT (all┬¡sky, with clouds).

Spaceborn measurement

contain an incredible number of variables. The sea ice concentrations, longitude/latitude,
time, and uncertainties are extracted from these variables. The sea ice concentrations
are given in percent, and the time is in seconds.

5.1
Sea level from GNSS┬¡IR
This thesis attempts to show how GNSS┬¡IR is a helpful complement to the traditional way

the freeboard of sea ice, which is crucial for estimating its overall thickness and volume.
Sea ice plays a vital role in maintaining ocean health and regulating global sea levels,
making it important to understand the long┬¡term variability of sea ice. As sea ice extent
and thickness change, sea level is directly impacted by melting and indirectly through
changes in oceanic and atmospheric dynamics [52]. Over time, this method may aid
climate analyses, as there are far more GNSS receivers than tide gauges, allowing for a
denser spatial coverage of sea level variations. By using the GNSS┬¡IR method, the height
above the ellipsoid is determined for the same nine┬¡year period. As global warming accel┬¡
erates ice melt and contributes to rising sea levels, GNSS┬¡IR could become an essential
tool for monitoring the impact of climate change on the cryosphere and ocean systems.

The entire 9┬¡year period is illustrated in Figure 5.3, where the primary signals are domi┬¡
nated by the lunisolar tidal components M2 and S2. These harmonic components cause
semidiurnal oscillations in the data due to gravitational attraction between the Moon and
the Sun. The M2 and S2 constituents constitute the primary harmonic components of the
tidal dynamics and represent the lunar and solar┬¡induced semidiurnal tidal variation, re┬¡
spectively. The M2 constituent, which is the most dominant, is generated by the MoonÔÇÖs
gravitational influence on the EarthÔÇÖs oceans and has a characteristic period of 12.42
hours. Every day, there are two high tides and two low tides caused by this component.
The S2 constituent is derived from the SunÔÇÖs gravitational influence. Tidal dynamics are
significantly affected by S2 despite its generally weaker nature than M2. The interference
between these two harmonic signals results in tidal modulation, which manifests in spring
and neap tides, among other things. These arise due to the periodic phase difference
between the tidal forces of the Moon and the Sun, where maximum constructive inter┬¡
ference leads to amplified tidal oscillations during spring tides. In contrast, destructive
interference reduces the amplitude oscillations during neap tides. M2 and S2 form the
foundation for semidiurnal tidal patterns and are crucial in describing global tidal dynam┬¡
ics [54]. On the nine plots that have been made, it is possible to see the variation from
the tide. The sea on Earth is affected by the forces of the Moon that pull in with its gravity.

5.6
Uplift
This section is primarily based on data and information about the method from D. Berg,
2023 [55].
As GreenlandÔÇÖs ice sheet loses mass, these enormous amounts of melting ice are causing
the pressure on the bedrock to decrease. Land uplifts are well┬¡known since they occurred
during the last ice age. GNSS stations measure bedrock movements, viscoelastic defor┬¡
mation (past ice variability), and elastic deformation (ongoing ice variability + ocean tides
+ solid Earth tides). There is no longer just the last ice age to blame for these land uplifts
in Greenland, a large part of the Greenland ice sheet is melting. This lack of pressure
on the bedrock causes the bedrock to rise to equalize the general pressure since this
deformation is isostatic, and the Earth will, therefore, return to a state of equilibrium. The
rebound from the last ice age is called glacial isostatic adjustment (GIA). The deforma┬¡
tion of the Earth has several different timescale phenomena for different kinds of trends
in the observed signal. The relevant part of this thesis is the vertical bedrock formation,
measured with GNSS. GIA is the EarthÔÇÖs reaction to mass redistribution caused by glacial
changes. This occurs as viscous flow, with an elastic instantaneous lift during melting ice.
In areas with a lot of ice, such as Greenland, the bedrock movement is more significant
and, therefore, more complex to model since, in these areas, it is a combination of both the
elastic and viscoelastic (GIA) movements. Consequently, there is significant uncertainty
in the modeled vertical movements. By measuring sea level with GNSS┬¡IR, the advan┬¡
tage is that the vertical movement of the bedrock at that exact location is measured with
high accuracy, making it possible to measure the local sea level. On the other hand, the
uncertainty of the individual measurements with GNSS┬¡IR is more significant than when
using TG. This is because several fluctuations can give other signals since the reflected
signals are converted to sea level. To model the Greenland uplift, it is crucial to have some
fixed points, and the GNSS stations are good as they are installed on the bedrock[48].
A trend analysis of the GNSS measurements observed at the GNSS station in Thule
(THU2) has been made. This trend analysis shows an average uplift rate of 6.88 mm/year
from 2002 to 2024. This information is crucial for determining reflection height with GNSS┬¡
IR. The uplift from the bedrock must be taken into account here. It also makes it possible
to compare TG and GNSS┬¡IR methods.

Discussion
6.1
Tide gauge, GNSS┬¡IR and AMSR┬¡2
In this study, the AMSR┬¡2 sensor was chosen as a data source to provide information
about sea ice concentration in a given area. One of the main advantages of AMSR┬¡2 is
its higher spatial resolution compared to Special Sensor Microwave Imager/Sounder (SS┬¡
MIS). AMSR┬¡2 operates at multiple microwave frequencies and has a resolution of down
to 3 ÔêÆ5km at the highest frequencies, significantly better than SSMIS, which typically has
a 15┬¡25km resolution. This difference makes AMSR┬¡2 particularly suitable for capturing
small┬¡scale changes in sea ice, especially along the ice edge and in areas with dynamic
sea ice cover. Compared to SSMIS, AMSR┬¡2 data provide higher spatial resolution, more
frequency bands, and better sea ice sensitivity, which is an important factor here. It is not
easy to look at sea ice concentration near the coast (max 500 m) with satellite data. So,
the most important is to have a low resolution. Furthermore, sea ice concentration is only
used to determine in which periods sea ice is present.
The GNSS┬¡IR data from 2016┬¡2017 shows significant gaps, evident when the analysis by
the gnssrefl software works with the data. These gaps are primarily because the GNSS
receiver, in the first years, only received GPS signals, and therefore, these time gaps
were not covered by GLONASS or Galileo frequencies. gnssrefl software has difficulty
handling these data gaps. In mid┬¡2019, this changed to the GNSS receiver, which also
started to receive GLONASS and Galileo frequencies. This improves data continuity,
and the number of observations also increases significantly. At the same time as more
observations, the number of gaps in the data also decreases to only a few per year or less.
This can also be seen in Table 5.1, which shows that from 2016 to 2020, the number of
observations tripled.
The gnssrefl software is used to analyze GNSS signals so that it can be used to look at
reflection heights. This software can do many things but requires different input parame┬¡
ters to function optimally. Some default values will be used if the user does not set these
parameters. These default values will not be able to cover all GNSS┬¡IR locations. This
can cause further uncertainties in the processing process if these parameters are not set
correctly. Some of these inputs will be specific to each GNSS┬¡IR location.
When analyzing the TG data, it is important to remember that linear regression assumes
a constant rate of change, which may not reflect reality. External factors such as climate
change, changes in ocean currents, and ice melting can affect future water levels non┬¡
linearly.

6.2
Limitations and Uncertainty
In the Port of Thule, the sea surface undergoes seasonal variations, with sea ice in winter
and open water in summer. Therefore, there are deviations between TG and GNSS┬¡IR
measurements, as a mixture of ice cover, tides, and wave activity can impact estimated
reflection heights from the GNSS┬¡IR method.
The prerequisite for using GNSS┬¡IR in the perfect system is that the reflected surface is
flat. In this way, the angular relationship between the direct and the reflected signal will re┬¡
main constant. However, the existing dynamic conditions will cause significant variations
in the observed data. These effects introduce a new sea┬¡level uncertainty not considered
in this thesis.
6.2.1
RINEX2 to RINEX3
In the selected data period, the file type changed significantly from RINEX2 to RINEX3
in 2020. Raw GNSS data is stored and exchanged in RINEX (receiver┬¡independent ex┬¡
change format). The format was developed to make data independent of the GNSS re┬¡
ceiver manufacturer and enable standard data processing across different GNSS receiver
manufacturers. There is information in these files regarding observation data, which is the
raw measurements and navigation data, as well as meteorological data, which includes
atmospheric measurements like temperature and pressure. GPS is the only GNSS sys┬¡
tem used by RINEX2, but RINEX3 also uses GNSS systems GLONASS, Galileo, and
BeiDou.
6.3
Future studies
The results of this thesis evolve into a possible new method for monitoring sea levels and
sea ice. Since satellite data often has a large spatial resolution, it is not easy to have good
observations near the coast, as this is often a resolution below 1 km is needed. Therefore,
the method using GNSS┬¡IR to measure sea level has been investigated. With further
studies and the knowledge that this gnssrefl software has unique input values for each
site, it is important to investigate several areas where other reliable sources can validate
the sea level measured with GNSS┬¡IR. Investigating several areas will also provide insight
into how consistent the bias observed in Figure 5.16 is, where there is an expectation that
the tidal signals should be close to the same.
Another way to improve coastal observations in areas that are not easily accessible could
be upward┬¡looking sonars (ULS). This method is not new, but with fixed points underwater,
coastal areas may be a good option. Near Daneborg in Greenland, a test ULS has been
set up, with the first test data in 2023. Such a ULS determines the sea ice keel, that is,
the thickness of the sea ice below the sea surface, whereas satellites such as CroySat
measure the sea ice freeboard, which is the thickness of the ice above the sea surface.
This test instrument near Daneborg has a small uncertainty compared to the satellite data
from CroySat.

Conclusion
This study provides an understanding of sea level change through the nine years of 2016┬¡
2024. It does this by combining sea ice concentrations measured by microwave sensors
borne on satellites, tide gauges using a pressor sensor to measure the water level, and
GNSS interferometric reflectometry to measure the reflector height. In order to compare
the two methods of measuring sea level, the heights must be in the same system. There┬¡
fore, all heights are given as height above the ellipsoid to have the same reference point.
From the GNSS┬¡IR analysis, Figure 5.2 and the given slope at the trend illustrates that
the sea level is rising 1.45 mm/year. Here, it does not consider that the bedrock is mov┬¡
ing upwards. That is also why the sea level rise for the GNSS┬¡IR is smaller than the sea
level rise measured by the TG. Figure 5.1 and 5.2 shows a clear pattern in the tidal sig┬¡
nal. That represents the tides caused by the moon and the sunÔÇÖs gravitational influence.
These results are also compared with those of the TG. Then, it is knowledge that the TG
measurement is much more precise. From Figure 5.3, the tidal signal is clear for all nine
years. The most clear signal is the moonÔÇÖs gravitational influence, which is also the most
common. In Figure 5.4 and the given slope at the trend show that the sea level is rising
3.76 mm/year. The sea ice concentration was used to divide the data into the correct
seasonal periods. In Figure 5.5, a plot is given for a 7┬¡year period where a winter period
from January to May is set where the sea ice concentration is close to 100% and a sum┬¡
mer period from July to October where there is a sea ice concentration close to 0%. This
is to work with these periods where there is an expectation of whether sea ice is present.
The data from AMSR┬¡2 comes in a grid with a size of 10x10 km, as shown in Figure 5.6.
It can be not easy to get precise concentrations in coastal areas. Here, two grids have
been chosen because the grid where Thule is located has very little data, as it covers too
much land. So, to get a better overview of this exact area, the neighboring grid was also
chosen.
In section 5.5, the year 2023 is chosen to be analyzed further. The sea ice concentration
was examined that year to validate a small winter and summer period. After the GNSS┬¡IR,
the chosen period was 21 days in March and 21 days in September. The tidal signals are
illustrated in Figure 5.9 and 5.10. The size of the errors is shown in Figure A.15 and A.16
in the appendix. The same is done for the TG method═¥ the same periods are illustrated
in Figure 5.12 and 5.13. It is the same two periods, which are both converted into height
above the ellipsoid. Now, it is possible to compare the two methods. First, it can be seen
that there is no shift in sea level during the winter period. The tidal signals are incredibly
close to each other (Figure 5.15), but this is not what is expected. When looking at the
summer period, there is a shift in the heights (Figure 5.16). During the summer, there is
no sea ice present═¥ therefore, there is an expectation that there will be no shift in the tidal
signals. This bias is not yet known. If this bias is corrected, the period in March will be
as expected (Figure 5.17). Here, it is seen that GNSS┬¡IR has a higher height above the
ellipsoid than TG, as GNSS┬¡IR has a reflection height on top of the ice, i.e., on the tops
of the sea ice freeboard that is present, and therefore, it is also expected to see a higher
height for GNSS┬¡IR than for the TG heights. This difference in the trend in Figure 5.17
indicates that the ice freeboard will grow 1.5 mm daily.
