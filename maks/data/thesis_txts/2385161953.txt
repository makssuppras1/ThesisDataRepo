ABSTRACT 
The two microalgae species Nannochloropsis gaditana and Polytomella caeca were grown to provide fatty acids 
that have double bonds on delta Δ5, Δ7 and Δ8. Fatty acids of this type are useful for the “aquaculturing 
biochemicals for future” project, as they have potential to be converted into hydroxy fatty acids via 
hydroxylation. Approximately 20L of Nannochloropsis gaditana was cultivated for this project.  
An experimental setup was made for Nannochloropsis gaditana to test the effect of light intensity and nitrate 
concentration on fatty acid composition, lipid content and growth parameters. The setup consisted of two 
irradiance levels (200 PAR and 1000 PAR) and 3 nitrate concentrations (0.22 g/l, 0.11g/l and ~0.025g/l). 
Comparisons of biomass productivity, lipid content and fatty acids profile was done to estimate the most 
effective treatment to produce high yield (mg/l) and productivity (mg/l/day) of the fatty acids C20:4 n-6 and 
C20:5 n-3 (both containing double bonds at Δ5 and Δ8). A secondary treatment of sudden high irradiance was 
tested on 3 of the cultures, to test if lipid content could be raised through light stress. The idea was to 
approximate two stage growth where a primary treatment promoted high growth rate, and a secondary 
applied stress to increase lipid content. 
The experiment showed that the highest lipid content (12.15% of dry weight) occurred under highest nitrate 
concentration (0.22 g/l) and highest irradiance (1000 PAR). Highest C20:5 n-3 content (21.8% of total fatty 
acids) was found under high nitrate concentration (0.22 g/l) and low irradiance (200 PAR). Highest productivity 
of biomass (0.064 g/l/day) along with highest productivity of C20:4 n-6 + C20:5 n-3 (0.0194 mg/l/day) was 
achieved under the lowest nitrate concentration (~0.025g/l). The secondary treatment proved to have no 
effect.  
An attempt to cultivate Polytomella caeca was made. Here Polytomella caeca was cultivated in Acetate, 
Pyruvate and soil water medium. In all instances, the culture was discarded due to strong contamination.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Motivation - Algae as a means of food security and 
environmental sustainability. 
From the authors perspective, the most interesting aspects of microalgae is its potential to be a competitor to 
traditional agriculture, to be a driver towards enhancing food security in third world nations and to increase 
environmental sustainability globally. Interesting aspects of algae in this regard are: 
• 
Microalgae may be grown in areas that are not traditionally used for agriculture (Walsh et al., 
2015)  i.e. deserts, open oceans and urban areas. In this way food production may be increased, 
or human encroachment on rich natural habitats may be reduced.  
• 
Many species can be grown in sea or brackish water, or water can be effectively recycled within 
the cultivation system, reducing the pressure on freshwater resources (Walsh et al., 2015). 
• 
Selected species of microalgae have high nutrient value making them useful in combating 
malnutrition as done in e.g. Chad and Bangladesh (FAO. Fisheries, 2008; UN, 2010; Rich, 2015).  
• 
Algae may be produced to have a higher biomass yield per unit area, when compared to terrestrial 
plant (Clarens et al., 2010), providing potential to be more cost effective. 
While there are potentials, there are also challenges to overcome. For one, the aspects of economy is currently 
less attractive than traditional agriculture (Clarens et al., 2010). Additionally, the legal assistance and support 
structure, usually awarded to traditional agriculture, is not available to the same degree, and thus the industry 
is likely to forego benefits i.e. subsidies, loan programs, disaster assistance etc. when it comes to growing algae. 
None the less, the tradition of cultivating microalgae is young compared to terrestrial agriculture and thus may 
be improved to compete or exceed. One way of achieving this is to advance the industry that is already in place. 
This industry, in large parts, focus on the production of high value products, and as it matures it may be a 
catalyst to get the technology, the legal structures and social acceptance in place for microalgae to be a crop 
of the future. It is wort mentioning that up until recently a large focus has been on using algae for biodiesel. 
This has provided ample research from which this thesis benefits greatly. 
1.2 Microalgae for high value products 
Microalgae are used in production of high value compounds such as fatty acids, pigments, proteins, and thus 
has applications in the medical, food and cosmetics industry (Guerin, Huntley and Olaizola, 2003; Kirby and 
Keasling, 2009; Cuellar-Bermudez et al., 2015).  
Another highly valuable product is hydroxy fatty acid found at a value ranging from 50 Euro/g (2-
Hydroxyhexanoic acid 98%) to 25,000 Euro/g ((±)-3-Hydroxyoctanoic acid ≥97%) (Sigmaaldrich, 2017).  
Hydroxy fatty acids may be used in production of lubricants, emulsifiers and stabilizers, and are used in 
chemical, cosmetic and food industry. They have been shown to have anti-inflammatory, anti-cancer, anti-
fungal, anti-diabetic and antibiotic properties (Kim and Oh, 2013). 
==PAGE:9==
pg. 2 
 
A common source of hydroxy fatty acids is the castor bean which naturally contains ricinoleic acid (12-hydroxy-
9-cis-octadecenoic acid) at 85-90% of the seed oil (Smith, Moon and Kunst, 2000).  Regarding microalgae 
recent research has shown, that the hydroxy fatty acids may either be found directly in some species of algae 
(see section 2.1.3), or be produced indirectly by a method of hydroxylating specific fatty acids by use of 
enzymes. It is the method of hydroxylation that is of interest to this thesis.  
Microalgae are interesting regarding the production of hydroxy fatty acids, as there is variety between species 
in terms of content of lipid, fatty acids profile, growth rates etc. Thus, if seeking to have a high yield of specific 
fatty acids (so that these can be converted to hydroxy fatty acids), it may be valuable to choose a species that 
has potential of high lipid yield, high growth or the right fatty acids profile. 
Another benefit to speak for the candidature of microalgae is, that growth, lipid content, fatty acids profile etc. 
of the algae may be adjusted by manipulating cultivation conditions. These cultivation conditions cover 
physical, chemical and biological factors (explained more in depth in section 2.3), which may be manipulated 
to be ideal for growth, lipid content or a specific fatty acids profile. As will be shown in section 2.5, it is often 
the case that there is a tradeoff where one set of conditions is ideal for e.g. growth, but does not produce a 
high lipid content. Such a tradeoff may be addressed by the strategy of two stage growth (see section 2.5.7) to 
initially favor one aspect (growth) of the algae and sequentially another aspect (lipid content or ideal fatty acid 
profile). 
Regarding the production of hydroxy fatty acids by hydroxylation of fatty acids, a collaborative project between 
Aarhus University and Danish Technical University (DTU) with assistance from Kyoto university, seeks to use 
algae to produce fatty acids. These are then turned (through hydroxylation) to hydroxy fatty acids (see 
Appendix I). In this regard, the associate professor at the University of Aarhus, Zheng Guo, will test the use of 
a mutated Δ9 hydratase on fatty acids of varied carbon chain length and with cis double bonds on Δ5, Δ7, Δ8 
and Δ10. 
As part of this project, DTU Food was tasked with cultivating algae that has a fatty acids profile that match the 
fatty acid profile mentioned. This is predominantly external to this thesis and by research assistant Anita Ljubić. 
This thesis is concerned with selection and cultivation of 2 species of algae. The species are Polytomella caeca 
and Nannochloropsis gaditana, and they are useful to the “aquaculturing biochemicals for future” project as 
the former, possibly, has fatty acids containing cis double bonds on Δ7, and the later fatty acid containing cis 
double bonds on Δ5 and Δ8 (see section 2.7 and 0). Referring to Polytomella caeca, the Δ7 double bonds is 
found in the fatty acid 16:4 (z4,z7,z10,z11) (which will be referred to as C16:4 n-3). Referring to 
Nannochloropsis gaditana, the Δ5 and Δ8 double bounds is found in Eicosapentaenoic acid (C20:5 n-3), 
Arachidonic acid (C20:4 n-6) and Dihomo-gamma-linolenic acid (C20:3 n-6), all containing both double bonds 
(Δ5 og Δ8). 
It must be noted that the assumed C16:4 n-3 fatty acids of Polytomella caeca is reported as 17:1(9Z)/16:4 
(4Z,7Z,11Z,13Z) and may be 17:1(9Z) rather than C16:4 n-3.

The value of the above-mentioned algae regarding the “aquaculturing biochemicals for future” project, relies 
not only on them containing the desired fatty acids, but also that there is a potential high yield of these fatty 
acids. This high yield will be a function of high growth, high lipid content and a fatty acid profile that favors the 
fatty acids of interest (C16:4 n-3, C20:3 n-6, C20:4 n-6 and C20:5 n-3). This again is affected by the species of 
algae and the conditions under which it is cultivated (as mentioned above).  
The aim of this thesis was to select and cultivate 2 species of algae that will be useful to the “aquaculturing 
biochemicals for the future” project. The selection criteria for the algae are, that they have the right fatty acid 
profile (containing fatty acids with double bond on Δ5, Δ7, Δ8 or Δ10) and high lipid content.  
In cultivating the selected algae, one aim was to supply biomass for the “aquaculturing biochemicals for the 
future”. Another aim was to investigate how to best cultivate the algae to optimize the yield of the fatty acids 
(C16:4 n-3, C20:3 n-6, C20:4 n-6 and C20:5 n-3) by variation in cultivation conditions (light and nitrate 
concentration) and by two stage growth. 
As part of the cultivation experiment, another aim was to test the effect of a secondary treatment of sudden 
exposure to high irradiance. The intent here was to simulate two stage growth were the primary treatment 
promoted high growth and the secondary promoted high lipid content. 
An additional aim was to analyze pigment composition (only for Nannochloropsis gaditana). This is interesting 
as many pigments are high value compounds. 
The aims, mentioned above, are based on the hypothesis, that the ideal candidate for the “aquaculturing 
biochemicals for the future” project could be found by cross referencing data on algae and their growth rate, 
lipid content and fatty acids profile from a multitude of scientific reports. That the growth conditions 
(specifically irradiance and nitrate concentration) could be manipulated to improve the yield of the correct 
fatty acids. 
The aims and hypothesis as per above are superficial and are elaborated in section 2.7, where they can be 
understood in the context of the theory.

2 THEORY 
2.1 Lipids and fatty acids 
2.1.1 LIPID 
A definition of lipids may be, that lipids contain long carbohydrate chains, are insoluble in water and soluble in 
chloroform, ether or other solvents (Kates, 1986). This definition has been criticized and excludes certain 
compounds considered lipids; it is however suitable in the context of this thesis.  
Their biological usage is that of energy storage, signaling, and as the main constituent of the cell membrane 
(Berg, Tymoczko and Stryer, 2002). Lipids, as per Fahy, et al. (2009), are classified as: fatty acyls, glycerolipids, 
glycerophospholipids, sphingolipids, sterol lipids, prenol lipids, saccharolipids and saccharolipids. Of these 
glycerolipids, glycerophospholipids, sphingolipids and saccharolipids have fatty acids as subunits (Voet and 
Voet, 1995).  
Lipids may alternatively be grouped in terms of phospholipids, glycolipids and triglycerides. Phospholipids is 
the main building block of the cell membrane, and generally phospholipids consists of a polar (and hydrophilic) 
head with a phosphate group and 2 nonpolar tails consisting of fatty acids (see Figure 2.1) (Voet and Voet, 
1995). Glycolipids are found on the outer surface of cell membranes, where they help to stabilize the cell 
membrane and facilitate cellular recognition (Voet and Voet, 1995). Glycolipids further helps to optimize 
efficiency of photosynthesis (Kalisch, Dörmann and Hölzl, 2016). Triglycerides consists of glycerol and 3 tails of 
fatty acids, and functions as energy storage in form of fats.  
 
Figure 2.1 An example of a lipid (Phospholipid) consisting of a polar head and a non-polar tail of fatty acids from 
Timberlake, (2011). 
2.1.2 FATTY ACID 
A Fatty acid is a long chain of hydrocarbons with a carboxylic acid at the end (Voet and Voet, 1995). The bonds 
between the hydrocarbons may either be single or double, which is denoted as saturated and unsaturated 
respectively. The double bond (unsaturated bond) may either be cis or trans (see Figure 2.2). Cis indicates that 
==PAGE:12==
pg. 5 
 
hydrogen atoms protrude to the same side of the double bond, and therefore cause a bend in the hydrocarbon 
chain. Trans indicate the hydrogen atom protrude to opposite side of the bond (Voet and Voet, 1995).  
 
Figure 2.2 Cis and trans bond to the left and right respectively. 
The name on the fatty acids are based on length of the hydrocarbon chain, the number of double bonds, and 
the position of the first or all double bonds counting from either the carboxylic acid end or the terminal methyl 
carbon end (see Figure 2.3). These conventions are referred to as Δx nomenclature and n-3/ω−x nomenclature 
(Chauhan, 2009). In this way linoleic acid is referred to as 18:2, cis,cis-Δ9,Δ12 (Δx nomenclature) or 18:2, n-6 
(ω−x nomenclature). As seen Δx nomenclature indicates the position off all double bonds, and whether they 
are trans or cis, whereas ω−x nomenclature only indicates the first double bond, and we can usually assume, 
that any further double bonds happen 2 carbon atoms down the carbon chain from the previous.  
 
Figure 2.3 A diagram of linoleic acid where blue numbers indicate Δ x nomenclature and red ω−x nomenclature. 
2.1.3 HYDROXY FATTY ACIDS 
Hydroxy fatty acids (see figure Figure 2.4) are similar to fatty acids but have one or more hydroxyl group along 
the carbon chain (Kim and Oh, 2013).  
 
Figure 2.4 A diagram of the hydroxy fatty acid 12-hydroxy-octadec-cis-9-enoic (ricinoleic) acid 
Hydroxy fatty acids can be found in plants, animals and microorganisms (Kim and Oh, 2013). They  are mainly 
derived from terrestrial plants (Joo et al., 2012), but variety and quantities does not meet industrial demand  
(Chua, Xu and Guo, 2012). 
Specific to algae, superficial literature studies shows, that small quantities of monohydroxy fatty acids along 
with 15,16-dihydroxydotriacontanoic and 16,17-dihydroxytritriacontanoic acids has been found in

Nannochloropsis sp. (Gelin et al., 1997). 3-hydroxy fatty acids has been found in 24 out of 28 strains of aquatic 
cyanobacteria (Li et al., 1998). 4 monohydroxy polyunsaturated fatty acids together with  3(S)-hydroxy-
6(Z),9(Z),11(E),15(Z)-octadecatetraenoic acid has been found in the macroalgae Laminaria sinclairii (Proteau

Cells synthesize hydroxy fatty acids from fatty acids through fatty acid-hydroxylation (see Figure 2.5) by using 
enzymes such as P450 hydroxylase (in yeast) or diol synthase (in yeast and bacteria) as catalyst (Kim and Oh, 
2013). This hydroxylation is possibly a mechanism for detoxification (Joo et al., 2012), and can be used to

A large variety of fatty acid-hydroxylating enzymes are present across the organism as per above (Kim and Oh, 
2013). These enzymes seem to have an affinity to transform one fatty acid or fatty acids with specific 
characteristics into specific hydroxy fatty acids. The enzymes in question will in some cases require a co-factor 
(e.g. NADH) to perform catalysis.  
2.2 Lipids and fatty acids in algae 
Algae contain a large variety of lipids both common and unique (Domínguez, 2013), but the typical distribution 
of phospholipids, glycolipids and triglyceride are:  
● 
10-20% Phospholipids (Deniaud and Rozentsvet, 1990; Dembitsky and Rozentsvet, 1996) , but up to 
95% have been recorded (Mooney et al., 2007). 
● 
>50% Glycolipids (Domínguez, 2013). 
● 
1%-97% Triglyceride (Domínguez, 2013). 
All the lipids above contain subunits of fatty acids as a non-polar “tail”, indicating that algae with a high lipid 
content is likely to have a high content of fatty acids.  
==PAGE:14==
pg. 7 
 
In regards to fatty acids it should be noted, that algae have a tendency of longer carbon chains than other 
organism (Domínguez, 2013).  According to Lang et al., (2011) algae with can be found to have a carbon chain 
length of 28. They generally have more double bonds than seen in other organisms, and they have a high 
affinity for producing n-3 and n-6 fatty acids (Domínguez, 2013). Furthermore, algae may produce uneven 
length carbon chains, something which is uncommon in other organisms (Lang et al., 2011; Domínguez, 2013) 
2.3 Cultivation of algae: 
In order to cultivate algae, the following is required: a source of energy (e.g. light or organic carbon), a carbon 
source (e.g. CO2, or organic carbon), macronutrients (in form of nitrogen, phosphate, magnesium and silicates) 
and micronutrients such as iron or manganese (Bux and Chisti, 2016). Successful cultivation further requires 
that environmental parameters such as pH, temperature and salinity are tolerable to the algae. 
Energy (specifically chemical energy) is used to fuel the various activities within the algae and may come from 
either photosynthesis or from breaking down organic compounds (Richmond and Hu, 2013). Algae acquiring 
energy by the former case is considered phototrophic and in the later heterotrophic.  
Carbon is the mayor building block of all aspects of the algae, and may be acquired either by fixing either 
organic or inorganic carbon. Algae converting inorganic carbon to organic carbon using energy from 
photosynthesis are considered photoautotrophic, and the carbon source is carbon dioxide or carbon dioxide in 
a dissolved form (Richmond and Hu, 2013).  
Of Macronutrients, the two most prominent are Nitrogen and Phosphorus. Nitrogen is essential to biomass 
production, and a building block for amino acids (which are the building blocks of proteins), nucleic acids (which 
form DNA and RNA) and adenosine triphosphate (an energy transferring molecule commonly known as ATP). 
Nitrogen is most commonly obtained as nitrate (NO3-), but is readily obtained from ammonia and urea, when 
this is available (Richmond and Hu, 2013). Phosphorus is part of adenosine triphosphate, nucleic acids and the 
phospholipids (which are the mayor component of the cell membrane), and is obtained on the form of 
phosphate (Richmond and Hu, 2013). 
Micronutrients are needed in small amount, but none the less are important to various metabolic functions 
e.g. as cofactors in enzyme reaction or an essential component of e.g. chlorophyll (as is the case with 
manganese)(Hopkins and Hüner, 2009). Micronutrient include, but are not limited to: iron, copper, zink etc 
(Richmond and Hu, 2013). 
Requirements to the parameters as per above vary between trophic method and species. Each species has a 
minimum (or limiting) requirement, and often a maximum tolerance above which the algae is stressed. The 
parameters themselves may be affected by growth of the algae, as e.g. carbon sources (biological or CO2) and 
==PAGE:15==
pg. 8 
 
nutrients may be depleted. Algae increasingly shade other algae from light as concentration increase (self-
shading), and pH changes through respiration and photosynthesis of the algae (pH decreases during 
respiration, which is due to release of CO2, and increases during photosynthesis, due to uptake of CO2; the 
reason being that the bicarbonate-carbonate buffer system is pushed to release H+ and OH-respectively). 
Stress applied to algae may change in concentration of e.g. lipids (Singh et al., 2016), or affect the lifecycle of 
the algae causing it to go into cyst stage (Lewis et al., 1974). 
The cultivation of the algae may be divided into different growth phases namely; lag phase, exponential 
growth phase, linear growth phase, stationary phase and death phase (see Figure 2.6).  Lag phase is a period 
of very slow or no growth, where the algae adapts to the cultivation environment (Richmond, 2003). 
Exponential phase happens when the algae is not limited by any of the variables mentioned above, and 
therefore can grow exponentially at the specific growth rate. Linear growth phase occurs when a variable is 
supplied at a constant rate and used at the same rate thus growth occurs at the rate of supply and linearly. For 
a photosynthetic cultivation setup, such variables could be CO2 or light, which can be depleted at the rate they 
are supplied, when the concentration of algae is sufficiently large. The stationary phase happens when one or 
several factors becomes limiting. At this point death rate and growth is equal so that no growth will be observed 
(Richmond, 2008).

The type of trophic growth effects choice of media, cultivation setup etc., and will impact production cost 
(production cost of algae biomass has a tendency to increase with heterotrophic growth (Richmond, 2008)). 
Further there are some interesting aspects associated with the trophic attributes among others: 
● 
Concentration in heterotrophic cultures may become markedly higher than phototrophic cultures 
(Richmond, 2008), which may for instance reduce prices downstream e.g. cost of harvesting. 
● 
Changing the tropic growth method of one species may increase lipid content (Marudhupandi, 
Sathishkumar and Kumar, 2016) and change fatty acid profile (Hulanicka, Erwin and Bloch, 1964). 
● 
During phototrophic growth, the algae takes up CO2, which in turn cause an increase of pH. During 
heterotrophic growth pH may move in either direction depending on the organic carbon source (as 
shown for Polytomella caeca in Wise, (1959)).

2.4.2 
CULTIVATION 
Nannochloropsis. gaditana tolerates large variation in light intensity showing growth in the range of 15 to 1200 
µmol photons/m2/s (Simionato et al., 2011). It will photosynthesize under light of various wavelength as shown 
in Kim et al., (2014), where it is grown under white, blue and red light. 
Nannochloropsis gaditana has successfully been cultivated in various media containing various nitrogen 
sources (Urea Nitrate etc.) at very low and high concentration (Lubián et al., 2000; Rocha, Garcia and 
Henriques, 2003; Simionato et al., 2013) (Simionato et al., 2011). Further the algae may be grown at 
temperatures from 20oC to 35oC, but specific growth rate decrease at temperatures towards 35oC (Figueroa et 
al., 1997).  The aspect of growth conditions is examined more thoroughly in section 2.5 and a compiled data 
set of cultivation methods and the output response is available in Table 2.4 section 2.4.4. 
2.4.3 APPLICATIONS OF THE CANDIDATE SPECIES 
As mentioned Nannochloropsis gaditana contains carotenoid (see section 2.4.1), and can be a source of 
commercially valuable pigment that are of use in: the cosmetics industry (Guerin, Huntley and Olaizola, 2003), 
animal feed and dietary supplements (Kirby and Keasling, 2009) and as food additives (Britton and Frederick, 
2009).  
Nannochloropsis gaditana has a high nutritional value (high content of protein and unsaturated fatty acids), 
and may be used in as a nutritional supplement (Cuellar-Bermudez et al., 2015) or in aquaculture as feed for: 
rotifers  (Rocha, Garcia and Henriques, 2003); larvae of fish, mollusks and crustaceans (Camacho-Rodríguez et 
al., 2013).  
Due to high lipid content Nannochloropsis gaditana is used as a model species in evaluating the viability of 
algae based biodiesel (Jinkerson, Radakovits and Posewitz, 2013).

2.5 Strategies of optimizing yield of C20:3 n-6, C20:4 n-6 and 
C20:5 n-3 in Nannochloropsis gaditana 
Optimizing yield of C20:3 n-6, C20:4 n-6 and C20:5 n-3 may be done through adjustment of 3 factors: 1. yield 
of the algae, 2. the lipid content of the algae and 3. the proportion of fatty acids of interest. In calculating the 
yield of Δ5 fatty acids simple equation for yield may then be: 
𝑌𝑖𝑒𝑙𝑑 𝑜𝑓 𝛥5 𝐹𝑎𝑡𝑡𝑡𝑦 𝑎𝑐𝑖𝑑 (𝑔/𝑙)
=  𝑆𝑢𝑚 𝑓𝑟𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 𝑜𝑓 𝛥5 𝐹𝐴 (% 𝑜𝑓 𝑡𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 𝑓𝑎𝑡𝑡𝑦) ⋅𝑓𝑎𝑡𝑡𝑦𝑎𝑐𝑖𝑑 (% 𝑜𝑓 𝑙𝑖𝑝𝑖𝑑) 
⋅𝑙𝑖𝑝𝑖𝑑 𝑐𝑜𝑛𝑡𝑒𝑛𝑡 (% 𝑜𝑓 𝑏𝑖𝑜𝑚𝑎𝑠) ⋅𝑦𝑖𝑒𝑙𝑑 𝑜𝑓 𝑏𝑖𝑜𝑚𝑎𝑠𝑠 (𝑔/𝑙)  
Yield is of course more interesting as a function of time (as g/l/day), and so the specific growth rate of the algae 
is very important. These factors vary with cultivation conditions, and usually ideal conditions for one factor is 
less than ideal for another (Sharma, Schuhmann and Schenk, 2012). As an example: under growth conditions 
that are not ideal (when stress is applied) algae tends to increase content of lipids (specifically neutral lipids) 
and polysaccharides (Singh, et al., 2016), but growth rate is reduced. The conflict of growth conditions can 
potentially be addressed by two stage growth (see section 2.5.7). 
The following is a description of how different parameters of culture conditions may be adjusted to affect the 
factors as per above. Effects are, as much as possible, specific to Nannochloropsis gaditana, or if that is not the 
case, it is stated. It is worth noting that the list is unexhausted, and that emphasis has been put to the effect of 
illumination (as this matches the experimental setup).  
2.5.1 CO2 
CO2 concentration in a culture medium may be adjusted by aeration, thus moving the concentration in the 
medium towards one in equilibrium with the inflowing air. By adding concentrated CO2 in the air stream, the 
concentration is pushed towards an equilibrium higher than atmospheric air providing more carbon for the 
algae to use for biomass. Specifically, addition of CO2 up to 5% of an airstream of 1l/l/min has been shown to 
markedly increase growth rate, lipid content and final concentration of biomatter (as seen in Ren, Ogden and 
Lian (2013)), or in-between cultures of same conditions as can be seen in data collected on Nannochloropsis 
gaditana from the literature (see 0). Data is not available to ascertain the effect on ∆5 and ∆8 fatty acids (see 
C20:5 n-3) when increasing CO2.  
2.5.2 PH 
While Nannochloropsis gaditana has been shown to tolerate pH range from 6 to 10 (Rocha, Garcia and 
Henriques, 2003), highest growth rate was found when pH was stabilized around 8 (Rocha, Garcia and 
Henriques, 2003; Ren, Ogden and Lian, 2013).  In Rocha, Garcia and Henriques, (2003; Ren, Ogden and Lian, 
(2013) pH was stabilized using a tris buffer. No indication on pH’s effect on lipid content fatty acid profile was 
found for Nannochloropsis gaditana specifically, but it is found for Nannochloropsis sp., that lipid content is 
highest at around 8 (Khatoon et al., 2014) and that the content of ∆5 and ∆8  fatty acids is highest for pH 7-8, 
though slightly higher at 7 (Bartley et al., 2013). 
==PAGE:26==
pg. 19 
 
2.5.3 NITRATE 
Nitrate limitation is a commonly used strategy to induce stress and cause an increase in lipid content. This 
strategy is valid for Nannochloropsis gaditana (Simionato et al., 2013). It is further found for Nannochloropsis 
gaditana, that nitrate limitation offsets a change in the composition of lipids, seen as an increase in 
triacylglycerols and a reduction of monogalactosyl-diacylglycerol, digalactosyl-diacylglycerol, diacylglycerol, 
sulfoquinovosyl-diacylglycerol, and phosphatidylcholine (Simionato et al., 2013). Further there is an increase 
in free fatty acids  (Simionato et al., 2013).  
A change in the lipids contained in Nannochloropsis gaditana must be associated with a change fatty acid 
profile, and it may be seen, that the fatty acid profile of Nannochloropsis gaditana under nitrate limited 
conditions change to contain less C20:4 n-6 and C20:5 n-3 and more C16:0 and C16:1 (Simionato et al., 2013). 
Additionally nitrate limitation is seen to cause a reduction in growth rate (Simionato et al., 2013), and thus 
there are tradeoff in nitrate limitation, that are not beneficial to increasing the yield fatty acids containing Δ5 
and Δ8 double bonds.    
2.5.4 HETEROTROPIC GROWTH  
It has been shown for Nannochloropsis salina that when comparing heterotrophic and phototropic growth 
under same conditions, except light exposure and carbon source, that there is an increase of the final 
concentration of biomass and lipid content under heterotrophic growth (Marudhupandi, Sathishkumar and 
Kumar, 2016). The growth rate is markedly lower under heterotrophic growth, and further there is a change in 
fatty acid profile, decreasing the content of monounsaturated fatty acids and increasing the content of 
polyunsaturated fatty acids.  (Marudhupandi, Sathishkumar and Kumar, 2016).  
The increase in lipids content may be, that heterotic growth acts as a stressor in which reactive oxygen is 
released when degrading the organic carbon source (Marudhupandi, Sathishkumar and Kumar, 2016). The 
reactive oxygen may damage the cell, and the increase in unsaturated fatty acids may be a survival mechanism, 
due to their ability to scavenge free radicals (Richard et al., 2008).  
The effect of heterotrophic growth may likely be similar in Nannochloropsis gaditana, but in the literature the 
only information is on mixotrophic growth, and here lipid content is decreased (Ren, Ogden and Lian, 2013). 
Insufficient information is available to ascertain the effect on fatty acids profile. 
Finally, while heterotrophic growth may provide possibilities regarding increasing yield of Δ5 and Δ8 fatty acids, 
it adds risk of contamination by e.g. bacteria or fungus.  
2.5.5 IRRADIANCE 
Irradiance can be adjusted in terms of intensity (quantity) and wavelength (quality) and has an impact on 
growth rate, lipid content and fatty acid profile (Kim et al, 2014; Camacho-Rodríguez et al, 2015b). The range, 
which may be considered ideal, vary between species, and most often the species adapt to different light 
regimes by adjusting chlorophyll content and accessory pigments. This means that the reaction to a change in 
==PAGE:27==
pg. 20 
 
light regime depends on the change itself (e.g. an irradiance of 200 PAR vs 1500 PAR) and the adaption period 
(Figueroa et al., 1997; Simionato et al., 2011). 
For Nannochloropsis gaditana specifically there is no significant change in growth rate in between light 
intensities of 15 PAR to 2,100 PAR, and only at 5 PAR is a clear reduction seen (Simionato et al, 2011; Camacho-
Rodríguez et al, 2015b). The same pattern is seen for final lipid concentration, which is similar under irradiance 
of 15 PAR to 1,600 PAR (Simionato et al., 2011; Camacho-Rodríguez et al., 2015b). That said, the maximum 
lipid concentration is reached earlier at irradiance of 1,200 PAR and above (Simionato et al., 2011). The effect 
of light intensity on lipid content, as per above, is all associated with continuous  exposure over an extended 
period, meaning the algae has ample time to adapt. None the less Simionato et al., (2011) indicates that sudden 
change to high light intensity in stationary phase may provide stress to increase lipid accumulation. This though 
seem to require a cofactor of nutrient limitation. 
Increase in light intensity seems to change the fatty acid profile, and it has been shown for Nannochloropsis 
sp. That, and increase in light intensity from 55 to 800 PAR causes a decrease in the proportion of C20:4 n-6 
and C20:5 n-3 and an increase in C16:0 (Sukenik, Carmeli and Berner, 1989). Most affected was C20:5 n-3 which 
was shown to be ~40% of fatty acids at 35 PAR and ~10% at 550 PAR photon/m2. For C20:4 n-6 the decrease 
was only ~5% in the same range (Sukenik, Carmeli and Berner, 1989). This though may not be the case for 
Nannochloropsis gaditana as shown by (Camacho-Rodríguez et al>, 2015b), where change in C20:5 n-3 was 
negligible at light intensity from 200 – 1800 PAR and under different temperatures. 
Finally, it has been shown, that a change in wavelength of incoming light impacts fatty acid profile, meaning 
that white light corresponds with high content of C20:5 n-3, which is reduced under blue and even more so 
under red light. The reverse is the case for C16:0 (Kim et al., 2014).

2.5.6 OTHER METHODS 
A multitude of culture parameter may be modified to induce stress, and through that increase accumulation 
of lipids. These variables were not addressed during the experimental design, and therefore only mentioned in 
brief. Some of the more prominent parameters are: 
• 
Change in salinity which, regarding Nannochloropsis gaditana, has been shown to increase lipid 
content under hypotonic salinity (Matos et al., 2016). Within Matos et al., (2016) it was found that 
lipid content was highest at salinity of 26ppt. 
• 
Limitation in phosphor, which seems to induce increase in saturated fatty acids (Singh et al., 2016). 
• 
Chemical additives that induces stress, such as azide and brefeldin. In case of the aforementioned lipid 
accumulation may be increased without compromising growth (Singh et al., 2016). 
==PAGE:28==
pg. 21 
 
Alternatively, to changing the cell composition through stress, yield of Δ5 and Δ8, may be increased by 
advancing certain metabolic pathways or nutrient availability of the cell. Examples of the former is 
phytohormones, which has been shown to increase both growth rate and proportion of PUFA and MUFA of 
total fatty acids (Salama et al., 2014). Examples of the former are EDTA and Surfactants which may increase 
lipid accumulation by changing permeability of the cell membrane, and thereby increase nutrient availability 
in the cell (Singh et al., 2016). 
Co-cultivation is another interesting way to address yield optimization. Here a culture is cultivated in the 
presence of another species of algae, bacteria or fungus. In case of bacteria, and depending on the bacteria, 
an increase in biomass and lipid accumulation may be induced as seen for the algae Rhizobium sp. and 
Dunaliella sp. (Do Nascimento et al., 2013; Le Chevanton et al., 2013). In case of fungus, an increase in biomass 
and lipid content may be seen, as has been shown for e.g. Scenedesmus obliquus (Yen, Chen and Chen, 2015). 
In case of mixed culture of species it has been seen for a polyculture of Nannochloropsis gaditana and 
Dunaliella salina, that both growth rate and lipid content increases in a mixed culture compared to not mixed 
(Liu et al., 2013).   
2.5.7 TWO STAGE GROWTH 
In two stage growth algae are initially cultivated under a method that promotes high growth rate. When 
sufficient biomass has accumulated (e.g. at stationary phase), the method is changed to induce lipid 
accumulation through stress.  The consequence of this is a lipid yield that is higher than any method applied 
separately (Su et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2014).  
From an industrial perspective irradiance seems like an interesting parameter, as it may be readily adjusted 
without adding or subtracting substance from the media. This provides the possibility of changing growth 
parameters throughout cultivation, without adding difficulties of recycling media. Change in irradiance may be 
adjusted throughout the experiment e.g.  by adding a stronger artificial light source or by using one phase of 
growth (the stressed phase) to protect the other from high radiation (see Figure 2.9).  
It is valuable to note, that it has been found for Nannochloropsis sp., that the content of C20:5 n-3 is reduced 
in favor of c16:0 and c16:1 as light intensity increases (Sukenik, Carmeli and Berner, 1989). If this is also the 
case for Nannochloropsis gaditana, this will likely impede the yield, that could be achieved by stressed 
conditions, both as standalone and under two stage growth.

2.6 Pigments and the effect of nitrate and light stress on pigment 
composition of Nannochloropsis gaditana 
Pigments in microalgae are used in photosynthesis either in regard to light harvesting, expansion of the 
wavelength range in which light energy can be captured, or photoprotection from high irradiance (Andersen, 
2005).   
Within the literature Nannochloropsis gaditana is found to have the pigments, chlorophyll-a, violaxanthin, 
antheraxanthin, zeaxanthin, astaxanthin, vaucheriaxanthin, canthaxanthin and beta-carotene (see section 
2.4.1). Of these chlorophyll-a is the only photosynthetic active pigment, and the remaining are carotenoids, 
either acting as accessory light harvesting pigments, or protection against damage from high irradiance.  
Violaxanthin, antheraxanthin and zeaxanthin forms the violaxanthin cycle, which helps algae or plant to adapt 
to different levels of irradiation (Jahns, Latowski and Strzalka, 2009). Under high irradiance violaxanthin is 
converted to zeaxanthin by de-epoxidation reaction with antheraxanthin as an intermediate, and under low 
light the reaction is reversed (Jahns, Latowski and Strzalka, 2009). Zeaxanthin helps to dissipate excess energy 
from photosynthesis, and thus acts as photoprotection, whereas violaxanthin assist in light harvesting (Jahns, 
Latowski and Strzalka, 2009).  
Literature shows, that the content of violaxanthin in Nannochloropsis gaditana decreases when exposed to 
high irradiance (Figueroa et al., 1997). In contrast to violaxanthin, zeaxanthin content increases under high 
irradiance. For all pigments concentrations (violaxanthin, antheraxanthin and zeaxanthin) the literature shows 
an increase under nitrate limitation (Simionato et al., 2013). 
Astaxanthin is a carotenoid, which scavenges for reactive oxygen and thus helps the cell deal with stressful 
environments. In that respect astaxanthin accumulated under high irradiance, nutrient limitation etc. (Han, Li 
and Hu, 2013). Apart from a report showing that astaxanthin may accumulate in Nannochloropsis gaditana 
(Lubián et al., 2000) no data is available on the effect of environmental conditions.    
==PAGE:30==
pg. 23 
 
Vaucheriaxanthin is part of the light harvesting complex, and is thought to increase the light harvesting band 
(Frank et al., 2006). The peak absorption of this pigment seems to be around 490nm (Frank et al., 2006), which 
indicates that this is the wavelength for which it promotes light harvesting. The literature indicates, that the 
concentration of vaucheriaxanthin in Nannochloropsis gaditana increases under nitrate limitation, otherwise no 
information is available for the species.  
Beta-carotene and  Canthaxanthin  are scavengers of free radicals and thus help prevent damage from high 
radiation (Filomena de Jesus ’raoso, Maria Miranda Bernardo de Morais and Manuel santos costa de Morais, 
2015). Both increases in concentration in Nannochloropsis gaditana under nitrate limitation (Simionato et al., 
2013).

3 AIMS AND HYPOTHESIS 
3.1 Aims 
The aims may be divided into 3 major aims, being: 
1. To investigate the effect of growth parameters on fatty acid composition of Nannochloropsis gaditana 
and Polytomella caeca. 
2. Use the above along with experimentally obtained values on yield, productivity and lipid content to 
estimate condition, that produce highest yield and productivity of fatty acids of interest for the 
“aquaculturing biochemical for future” project.  
3. To produce biomatter of Nannochloropsis gaditana and Polytomella caeca (only in so far as it contains 
the correct fatty acids) to be used in the “aquaculturing biochemicals for future”. 
Regarding cultivation of Nannochloropsis gaditana the aims are:  
• 
To estimate how to maximize the yield of the fatty acids C20:3 n-6, C20:4 n-6 and C20:5 n-3 in 
Nannochloropsis gaditana trough an experimental setup. In the experimental setup, the aim is to 
compare different growth methods and how they impact the lipid content, growth rate and fatty acid 
profile of Nannochloropsis gaditana. These growth methods being: 
o 
Close to Ideal conditions (as estimated by literature) 
o 
Nitrate limited 
o 
Light stressed (under high radiation) 
o 
Nitrate limited and light stressed simultaneously  
o 
Two stage growth, where the first stage is close to ideal growth, and the second stage is light 
stressed, or light stressed and nitrate stressed simultaneously.  
An added aim, that is outside the scope of the title, is to do a pigment profile of Nannochloropsis gaditana 
under the different growth conditions as mentioned above.  
In regard to Polytomella caeca the aims are:  
• 
To establish a more precise fatty acid profile of Polytomella caeca as the literature is inconclusive as 
to whether Polytomella caeca contains C16:4 n-3 and therefore a fatty acid with cis double bonds on 
Δ7.

3.2 Hypothesis 
The aims, as given in section 1.3, are based on a set of hypotheses, which again are based on literature. The 
hypotheses are as follows: 
• 
That Polytomella caeca contains either C16:4 n-3 or 17:1(9Z), but not both, and that either constitute 
up to 100% of total fatty acids.  
• 
That lipid content of Nannochloropsis gaditana can be increased through light stress. That this stress 
can be induced to a small degree by high irradiance alone, and to a larger degree by sudden change 
from low irradiance to high (see section Irradiance2.5.5).  
• 
That the fatty acid profile of Nannochloropsis gaditana vary depending on irradiance, and that it will 
react in one of two ways: 
1. Increased irradiance will, like shown for Nannochloropsis sp., affect an increase in total fatty 
acids of C16:0 and C16:1 and decrease in C20:5 n-3 (see section 2.5.5). 
2. Increased irradiance will mean an increase in C20:5 n-3 of total fatty acids as has been shown 
for Nannochloropsis gaditana under continuous cultivation (see section 2.5.5). 
• 
That the effect of irradiance, as per above, is more pronounced under nutrient limitation. 
• 
That lipid content will increase when Nannochloropsis gaditana experience stress induced by limited 
access to Nitrate.   
• 
That any of the forms of stress as mentioned above will impede growth rate, though light stresses less 
than Nitrate stress. 
• 
That the content of various pigments in Nannochloropsis gaditana is affected in terms of mol 
pigment/100mol chlorophyll-a by different level of irradiance and nitrate concentration. In this way 
Violaxanthins content increases with higher irradiance, while zeaxanthin decreases, and content of all 
pigments increases when nitrate is limited.

4 METHOD 
4.1 Selection of species 
A database was compiled containing 2014 profiles of algae (see Appendix V). Each profile included data on fatty 
acids, lipid content or both. Among the profiles 1133 unique species were found, and these where cross-
referenced to provide profiles containing both lipid content and fatty acid profile. The total number of such 
profiles were 130. 
Considering constraints of the laboratory space and resources available, along with a set of selection criteria 
as described below, the two species Nannochloropsis gaditana and Polytomella caeca were considered. 
The criteria for selection of these algae were: 
• 
They had a lipid profile, that matched the needs of the project “Aquaculturing biochemicals for future”.  
• 
In comparison between the 130 algae species (see Appendix V), they were found to have both high 
lipid content and high proportion of the appropriate fatty acids. More specifically, when multiplying 
fatty acid of interest with lipid content these ranked highest.  
• 
The likelihood of cross contamination between the two species was very low as one was a 
heterotrophic (Wise, 1959) freshwater species (Guiry and Guiry, 2017) and the other mixotrophic 
(Rocha, Garcia, and Henriques, 2003)  saline species (Guiry and Guiry, 2017).  
• 
Few scientific publications had been found about each species, so any findings might contribute to 
furthering the knowledge of microalgae.  
• 
They represented two tropic methods each of which had implications for cultivation (see section 2.3), 
and, in extension, the economics of using algae as a source for hydroxy fatty acids. 
4.2 Cultivation of Nannochloropsis gaditana 
The goal of this cultivation experiment was to establish how Nannochloropsis gaditana reacted in term of fatty 
acid profile, lipid content, growth rate and pigment composition when cultivated under combinations of 2 
irradiation levels and 3 different nitrate concentrations.  
More specially the experimental setup exposed Nannochloropsis gaditana to high irradiance (1,000 PAR from 
cool white LED) and low irradiance, that seemed ideal for growth in accordance with the literature (200 PAR 
from cool white LED). Additionally, an attempt at two-stage growth was made where the algae, that had been 
cultivated under low irradiance, as per above, was exposed to a secondary treatment of sudden high irradiance.  
A blind of low irradiance exposure was made for the secondary treatment.  
The 2 light regimes were done under 3 different concentrations of nitrate (limited, ~1,764 µM and ~3,528µM) 
and in triplicates providing the setups as indicated in Figure 4.1. 
A detailed explanation of the experimental cultures is found in section 4.2.3. The inoculation and preculturing 
is detailed in 4.2.1 and 4.2.2. Growth diary is contained in Appendix II.

4.3 Cultivation of Polytomella caeca 
Strains of Polytomella caeca was acquired from SAG culture collection at Goettingen University. This was not 
axenic containing bacteria or other contaminants (SAG, 2003). The strain was acquired on 2 separate occasions 
representing 2 separate growth experiments. A diary was kept during cultivation (see Appendix II). 
4.3.1 CULTIVATION EXPERIMENT 1 
A setup was made to grow up Polytomella caeca to a point, where a preliminary fatty acid profile could be 
attained and to work as mother culture for additional experimental setups.  As the culture from SAG was 
contaminated, another aspect of this setup was to gauge, if choice in carbon source and adjusting of pH would 
help prevent the contaminants from dominating the culture. The choice of carbon source, temperature and pH 
was based on what was successfully used in the literature as per 2.7.2. 
Polytomella caeca was inoculated in 170ml  Polytomella medium (UTEX, 2017) in a 500 ml DURAN bottle. The 
bottle was kept in darkness at ~22oC and with the lid loosened to allow for air exchange. Media was added 
continuously over 21 days to around 500mL 
The preculture was transferred into 2 reactors (see detailed description of reactor in section 4.3) each 
containing 400 ml medium (as per Table 2.6 section 2.7.2) with a carbon source of 1g/l acetate in one and 1g/l 
pyruvate in the other. A pH meter was left in the reactor running continuously and with the option to control 
==PAGE:40==
pg. 33 
 
input of CO2. Temperature was kept steadily around 22oC. Air was supplied at 0.1-07l/min coming to 0.3-
1.7l/l/min (this broad range is due to the nature of the setup, and difficulties in measuring exact flow). The 
reactor with acetate was setup to be dosed with CO2 when pH increased to above 6.  
For the first batch pH and optical density at 750 nm was measured regularly. Daily visual control of the cultures 
was done along with occasional inspection through an Olympus BX50 microscope with 40x objective.  
After 4 days 250 ml from each reactor was extracted, and a fatty acid profile was established. Cultivation 
continued for an additional 14 days, after which the cultures were abandoned due to heavy contamination. 
During cultivation of this first batch, an attempt was made to isolate Polytomella caeca. Here, what was 
recognized as cells in cyst stage were moved to Polytomella medium by pipette and left in a veil under the 
same conditions as during initial inoculation of Polytomella caeca.   
4.3.2 CULTIVATION EXPERIMENT 2 
A second culture was inoculated a little over a month later than the first. The purpose here was to cultivate the 
culture from SAG under conditions that were less ideal for contaminants. This was achieved by growing in a 
less rich media and under lower temperature specifically: in soil water medium (see Appendix VI) and at around 
17oC and otherwise as above.  
The culture was moved to 3 DURAN bottles of 200, 500 1,000ml containing 1l soil water between them. The 
bottles were places in a cooler box at 16-.17oC, and the lids of the bottles were left loose to allow air exchange. 
The bottles where left undisturbed over the growth period with no monitoring except visual and occasional 
microscopy. 
At the end of cultivation fatty acid profile was established.

4.4 Reactor setup 
All reactors consisted of 500ml, 1L, 2L or 5L DURAN bottles plugged with a rubber stopper or closed off with a 
screw on cap (as seen Figure 4.2). 2 tubes and a glass pipette were used to provide a sample/injection port, 
and air supply and a pressure vent. The air supply was equipped with a 20 µm syringe filter, which was 
connected to an air supply of atmospheric air. The air inlet was placed in the bottom of the reactor to provide 
agitation. The sample port was clammed, when not in use, and samples were taken by a syringe. 
Light was supplied (to Nannochloropsis gaditana only) by cool white fluorescent light for cultures at 200 PAR 
and cool white LED light at 1,000 PAR.  Irradiance was controlled by adjusting the distance between bottle and 
light source.  
Cultures were agitated by aeration and manual stirring of the bottles daily. 
4.5 Irradiance, pH, temperature and nitrate concentration 
Irradiance was measured using a WALZ US-SQS/L Submersible Spherical Micro Quantum Sensor. Results are 
collected in PAR, which is an expression of µMol photon m−2s−1, measured within the photosynthetically active 
radiation band of 400 – 700nm. The values are collected on the edge of the reactor, where it was perpendicular 
to the light source. 
pH for Polytomella caeca cultures was measured with a Milwauke MC122 pH controller. The probe of the 
controller was left in the culture throughout cultivation and connected to control CO2. pH for Nannochloropsis 
gaditana was measured with a HANNA instruments 991001 pH meter on a weekly basis. HANNA instruments 
991001 was equally used to measure temperature. 
4.6 Fatty acid profile, Lipid content and Pigment composition 
500ml-1,000ml samples of Nannochloropsis gaditana to be used for Pigment analysis, lipid content and fatty 
acid profile was collected and centrifuged at 6,000rpm / 6340 RCF(g). Supernatant was discarded, and algae 
dry matter was mixed with 350ml deionized water to remove salts. The mix was centrifuged again, supernatant 
was discarded, and algae dry matter was collected and frozen. Before use for analysis the frozen dry matter 
was freeze-dried. The amount of dry matter left was at times insufficient for the analysis as per above, in which 
case fatty acid profile was prioritized. 
Two-step direct transesterification as per Safafar, Møller and Jacobsen, (2017)  was used to establish a fatty 
acid profile. The results are given in % of total fatty acids, and the steps are as follows: 
1. Between 0.05 to 0.1 mg Freeze dried sample is placed in a DURAN GL 18 beaker and weighed to 4 
decimal precision.  
2. 1ml Well mixed Methanolic sodium hydroxide(1M) (98% methanol) and 1 ml pure Toluene is added to 
the beaker and mixed on a vortex. 
3. The mixture was put to sonication for 10 min in a 2510 Branson sonicator. 
4. The mixture was boiled for 5 minutes in a water bath at 100oC. 
==PAGE:42==
pg. 35 
 
5. The mixture was cooled in a water bath until it had room temperature. 
6. 2ml BF3-methanol (14% w/w) and 25 µl internal GC FAME 2% C23 in heptane was added.  
7. The mixture was boiled for 2 minutes in a water bath at 100oC. 
8. The mixture was cooled in a water bath until it had room temperature. 
9. 1 ml heptane and 2 ml saturated salt solution was added. 
10. The upper phase was transferred to a GC vial (between 0.6 to 1ml per vial)  for Chromatographic 
analysis (GLC-FID).  
11. Gas chromatography was done using Agilent Technologies 7890A GC System Gas Chromatograph. 
12. A run of know fatty acid standards was used for identification by comparison of retention times. 
Lipid content was measured by Bligh and Dyer by the following steps:  
1. Prior to analysis glass cups were placed in an oven at 105oC for two hours and left to cool in a 
desiccator. When cool, the cups were weighed to 4 decimal precision.  
2. Freeze dried algae (~5mg) were placed in a centrifuge glass and weighed to a precision of 2 decimals. 
3. 10 ml deionized water, 30 ml methanol and 15 ml chloroform was added in that order and stirred for 
30 seconds (by an ULTRA turrax mixer at 15,000 rpm), at which point 15 ml Chloroform was added, 
and the mixture was stirred for additional 30 second.  
4. 15 ml deionized water was added, and the mixture was shaken by hand.  
5. Samples were centrifuged at 2,800 rpm at 18oC for 10 minutes in a IEC centra-GP8R centrifuge.  
6. The upper face of the mixture is removed by vacuum suction, and the extract is filtered through phase 
separation filter. 
7. The filtered content is placed in the pre-weighed glass cups (see step 1), and the entire content is 
weighed at 4 decimals precision.  
8. The cups were left overnight under a fume hood to let chloroform evaporate, and the cup was 
weighed to 4 decimal precision.  
9. Lipid content was calculated as:  
%lipid = g(lipid) * 41g *100/(g(extract)-g(lipid)*g(sample)) 
Pigment analysis was performed by high performance liquid chromatography by a method modified from (Van 
Heukelem and Thomas, 2001) (see protocol in 0). 
4.7 Biomass, optical density and nitrate 
Biomass was measured as ash free dry weight (ASFW) by a method slightly adapted from (Moheimani et al., 
2013) and as follows: 
1. Filters were flushed and placed in a ceramic crucible and dried at 105oC for at least 2 hours. 
2. 30 ml sample was passed though Ø7mm Whatman GF/F glass fiber filter paper under suction.  
3. The filters and biomass were placed in an oven and dried at 105oC for 24 hours. 
==PAGE:43==
pg. 36 
 
4. The filters were placed in a desiccator to cool after which they were weighted to a precision of 1 
milligram 
5. The filters were placed in an oven at 450oC for 24 hours., left overnight and weighed the following day. 
6. The filters were placed in a desiccator to cool after which they were weighted to a precision of 1 
milligram. 
7. AFDW was calculated by dry (𝑑𝑟𝑦𝑤𝑒𝑖𝑔ℎ𝑡(𝑔) −𝑎𝑠ℎ𝑤𝑒𝑖𝑔ℎ𝑡(𝑔))/30𝑚𝑙∙1,000𝑚𝑙/𝑙. 
8. The steps per above was repeated in triplicates for each reactor on a regular basis, and when samples 
were taken for Lipid content etc.  
Note here, that the method above omits rinsing after filtration of the algae. This has been strongly 
recommended by Zhu and Lee (1997), as certain components of the media, e.g.  N, Fe and Pb, has been shown 
to evaporate at temperatures toward 540oC. This issue is further enhanced as the medium contains a tris buffer 
((HOCH₂)₃CNH₂), which will remain in the liquid that stays in the filter after step 2 and will evaporate in step 
5 thus adding to AFDW. To address this issue the steps 1 to 6 as per above has been performed on pure medium 
to produce a blind value (𝑏𝑙𝑖𝑛𝑑= 𝑑𝑟𝑦𝑤𝑒𝑖𝑔ℎ𝑡−𝑎𝑠ℎ), with the blind AFDW calculated as (𝑑𝑟𝑦𝑤𝑒𝑖𝑔ℎ𝑡(𝑔) −

Optical density (OD) was measured with a shimadzu UV-1800 spectrophotometer daily to estimate growth. 
For Polytomella caeca the absorption was measured at 750nm. For precultures of Nannochloropsis gaditana 
absorption was measured at 540nm in accordance with (Rocha, Garcia and Henriques, 2003). For the 
experimental cultures absorption was measured as full spectra from 300-800nm, with and interval of 2nm. 
540nm was still used to estimate growth. 
A correlation between biomass and optical densities at 540nm was made for Experimental cultures of 
Nannochloropsis using data as per above. Here OD density was correlated with biomass measured on the same 
day by averaging all instances of OD/AFDW and by linear least square regression. 
Nitrate was estimated based on Redfield ratio and biomass accumulation. Specifically as 4-9% of ∆AFDW and 
in accordance with (Ketchum, Lillick and Redfield, 1949). AFDW was based on a conversion of OD540. Maximum 
and minimum estimates of nitrate usage were plotted against growth curves to ascertain if the cultures 
experienced nitrate limitation.

4.9 Air flow 
Airflow was established by placing the air tube of a reactor in a clear container at the same level of the reactor 
and containing water to the Hight of what was in the reactor. The airflow was not switched off or changed 
while doing this, and the syringe filter was kept in place, meaning the flow should be the same as in the reactor.  
A clear cup full of water was placed bottom up above the bobble column, and water was displaced with air (see 
Figure 2.1). The time it took for 100ml water to be displaced was noted and used to calculate flow in L/min 
(data and calculations are contained in Appendix X).

pg. 38 
 
 
Figure 4.3 Illustration of water being displaced by air to measure flow 
To avoid contamination of the Polytomella cultures, the method as per above was used on an alternative 
airflow which had a bobble column comparable to the one of the reactor.  
Air flow for L4xN and H4xN cultures have not been measured, but are estimated based on averages from the 
other cultures.  
4.10 Statistical methods 
Data is generally presented as means of experimental triplicates and with ± standard deviation when relevant.  
A correlation between OOD540 and AFDW was found using a linear regression using the Microsoft excel add-
on xlstat.  
The statistical software Primer 7 with the PERMANOVA add-on was used to perform permutational ANOVA to 
test the effect of light intensity and nitrate concentration on the response parameters: biomass productivity 
(g/l/day), yield (g/l), concentration of various pigments, lipid content and content of the fatty acids C20:5 n-3. 
Response variables were tested for homogeneity of variance using PERMDISP, and was followed by 
permutation analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) using Euclidian distances. Upon significant difference between 
treatment methods or interaction between method (p <0.5) a paired comparison was done to examine 
influence as the response variables.

5 RESULTS 
The two species of algae Nannochloropsis gaditana was successfully cultivated, both in regard to biomass 
production for the “aquaculturing biochemicals for future” and the experimental setup. Cultivation of 
Polytomella caeca was unsuccessful on all accounts. 
Overall the cultivation period of Nannochloropsis gaditana was longer than it was seen in the literature (see 
section 5.2). Lipid content was in the low end of what was reported (see section2.44), content of C20:5 n-3 and 
C20:4 n-6 was in the range of what was reported, though far below value proposed by Lang et al., (2011). Some 
pigments matched the literature, though many exceeding by 1 order of magnitude (see section5.5). 
About 20 liters of algae at ~1g/l was produced for the “aquaculturing biochemicals for future” project.

5.2.4 DISCUSSION OF GROWTH 
As highlighted above, the Nx2 and Nx4 cultures seemed to be governed by linear growth. That is to say, increase 
in biomass was governed by an influx of a limiting parameter. These limiting influx parameters was either light 
or CO2. It was worth noting, that OD540 (or biomass) increased slightly when the culture was placed in 
secondary treatment. In that case both CO2 and light influx increased (see Table 4.2 section 4.2.3).  
Light undoubtedly was a limiting factor, which may be inferred by the increase in chlorophyll-a (OD680) 
compared to biomass (OD540).  
It was interesting to note, that the culture with the lowest nitrate concentration (0xN) had the highest biomass 
productivity and didn’t seem to display linear growth.  A possible reasoning of this (but it is outside the scope 
of this thesis ) is a strategy of optimizing proliferation as per (Geiderl, Maclntyre and Kana, 1998). An example 
could be, that the algae under nutrient replete conditions increased investment in other factors than growth 
(e.g. pigments) to increase competitiveness, while under nutrient limiting conditions investment would be 
diverted towards growth.   
Another possibility is that the treatment in question (0xN) was performed latest (2 month after 4xN and 1 
month after 2xN), thus have had time for adaptation.

5.3 Nannochloropsis gaditana lipid content  
As seen 2.4.4 the lipid content was in the range of 6-12%. This was low compared to the literature, which 
averages around 20% and peaks 45% (see section 2.4.4). Highest lipid content was found in H4xN and Stress4xN.  
For lipid content of the 6 cultures L4xN, H4xN, L2xN, H2xN, L0xN and H0xN there was found no significant difference 
in between high and low irradiance (p>0.05), which corresponds to the literature see Simionato et al., (2011; 
Camacho-Rodríguez et al., (2015). A significant difference due to nitrate concentration, specifically, 4xN 
cultures, were higher than the other two cultures. 
It was interesting to note, that the increase in lipids under high light and nitrate limitation as proposed by 
(Simionato et al., 2011) was not found to be statistically significant. A reason for this may likely be, that 1000 
PAR irradiance did not produce significant stress to express this relationship.  
No significant change was found as a result of secondary treatment (p>0.05). 
As the method for measuring lipid content required a substantial amount of biomass, compared to what was 
harvested, it was not possible to ascertain lipid content in during lag and exponential phases for any of the 
cultures.

5.7 Nannochloropsis gaditana collapsed cultures 
An initial attempt at LNx2 was made where agitation was performed by a magnetic stirrer at 100rpm. After one 
day, this culture had turned from light green to yellow, and large amount of yellow coagulate had settled at 
the bottom. This culture was left for about a week with the magnetic stirrer turned off, but showed little signs 
of recovery.  
It must be assumed that the amount of sheer stress applied by the magnetic stirring, exeeded the tolerances 
of the algae.  
The culture G4xN (see 5.17), an attempt at mixotrophic cultivation, started showing signs of contamination in 
form of whitish mucus after a few days of cultivation. Under microscope what looked like fungus spores was 
revealed (see Figure 5.16). Contamination increased rapidly over the next couple of days and the contaminant 
turned from whitish goo to whitish goo with reddish areas with a sponge like texture. 
An attempt to inhibit the contaminants by placing the culture under UV light was made. and may have had 
some effect as media showed some indication of clearing (though very little). Eventually the culture was 
abandoned. A sample was taken for fatty acids analysis but was lost during centrifugation.

5.9 Polytomella caeca 
The initial cultures of Polytomella caeca started showing signs of contamination early after inoculation.  The 
contamination was seen as yellowish mucus and produces an unpleasant odor.  
During cultivation, it was found difficult to establish if the algae was growing or indeed present. Only at one 
point was, what looked like, a cell in vegetative stage recognized through a microscope.  
Attempts to control the contamination by adjusting pH to extremes both through adding CO2 and by titration 
with HCl. The literature had indicated that the algae could tolerate a broad pH range and the hope was that 
going to the extremes of this range would suppress the contaminant in favor of the algae. The attempts were 
unsuccessful and finally the culture was abandoned.  
A fatty acid profile was made of the two setups (acetate and pyruvate) and showed high content of C16:0 and 
an undetermined fatty acid or artefact. 
After communication with SAG a second attempt was made at cultivation Polytomella caeca. Here the 
conditions were changes to be much less favorable to bacteria and fungus. None the less the culture developed 
contamination. Again, a fatty acid profile was made and this time a high content of 18:1 n-9 was found.

6 SOURCES OF ERROR 
Agitation of Nannochloropsis gaditana performed by aeriation and daily manual stirring. This produced some 
unevenness in the OD540 measurement which can be seen in growth curves. AFDW was estimated on OD540 
so any error here would be compounded on these results.  
As mentioned in section 4.7 the tris buffer contained in the media caused an increase in the AFDW. This was 
not considered before late in work on this thesis. An attempt to correct this was made by adding a bind.  While 
this should give an estimate of the amount of tris buffer caught in the filters used for AFDW, it is likely that 
more would be caught when algae clog the filter. This would mean that AFDW is possibly an overestimation.  
Originally it was planned that measurement of Nitrate concentration would be made throughout the 
experiment. Unfortunate it proved difficult to do this due to the tris buffer. Instead nitrate concentration was 
estimated by AFDW, which was again estimated by OD540. As seen above both values are subject to error 
which will be compounded on to nitrate concentration. For this reason, it has been difficult to estimate if the 
algae were nitrate limited or not.  
During centrifugation of samples used for estimate of lipid content, fatty acids profile and pigment composition 
a reddish hue was seen in the supernatant.  This color may stem form pigment or lipid meaning that there is a 
chance that these has been underestimated.

7 CONCLUSION 
Fatty acids profile of Nannochloropsis gaditana was affected by the different cultivation methods. 
Specifically regarding C20:5 
n-3, 
which 
was 
interesting 
to 
the 
“aquaculturing biochemical 
for future“ project, it was found that; higher irradiance was associated with a decrease of the fatty acid 
and increase in nitrate concentration was associated with an increase of the fatty acid. 
Highest lipid content was found under nitrate concentration of 0.22 g/l and irradiance of 200 PAR in the 
L4xN culture. Highest yield (g/l) was found under 0.22 g/l nitrate and irradiance of 1,000 PAR in the H4xN 
culture. Highest biomass productivity (g/l/day) was found under low nitrate consecration (0.025 g/l) in the 
L0xN culture.  This last point is surprising as it was expected that low nitrate concentration would produce 
nitrate limitation and thus impede growth.   
Estimated of yields (mg/l) of the sum of C20:5 n/3 and C20:4 n-6 was calculated as a function of the above 
factors. Here it was reviled that most effective cultivation method was 1,000 PAR and 0.22 g/l 
nitrate (H4xN). Highest productivity (mg/l/day) was found under 200 PAR and ~0.025 g/l nitrate.  
Approximately 20l of Nannochloropsis gaditana was cultivated “aquaculturing biochemicals for future under 
conditions similar to the ones producing highest yield as per above. The Fatty acid profile, lipid content and 
yield is expected to be similar to the L4xN culture though this was not tested.   
In regards to two stage cultivation the secondary treatment was found to produce no significant changes in 
any aspect as per above. It is estimated that sudden change in irradiance is an ineffective stressor and thus do 
not produce the expected increase in lipids.   
I addition to the above pigment composition was done for Nannochloropsis gaditana here it was found that 
increase in nitrate concentration was associated with an increase in total carotenoids and chlorophyll-a in 
relation to dry weight. The inverse was the case for total carotenoids/chl-a. Under nitrate concentration below 
0.11 g/l increase in irradiance was associated with increase in total carotenoids/chl-a.  
An increase in Violaxanthin and decrease in zeaxanthin under high irradiance was expected but this tendency 
was only notices for violaxanthin but not zeaxanthin.    
No significant effect was produced by the secondary treatment.   
Cultivation of Polytomella caeca failed on all accounts it was found that that the contaminants (either those of 
the non-axenic culture or external) came to dominate the cultures. In this regard attempts of establishing a 
more conclusive fatty acids profile and determine if Polytomella caeca contained C16:0 n-3 was unsuccessful.

Appendix VI. 
Soil water medium 
The steps of creating the soil water medium are as follows: 
1. Soil was collected from next to a tree at a small patch of trees at DTU. The soil was loamy with a twigs 
and root material within it.  
2. Twigs, leaves and large stones were removed from the soil and it was left to dry overnight at around 
35oC. The soil went from dark blackish to greyish. 
3. The soil was ground by mortar and pestle and run through a sieve and stored in cold storage at 5oC. 
4. A 2-liter bottle was filled with soil to 300ml and deionized water to 2l. 10 garden peas were squeeze 
and added. 
5. The bottle was autoclaved for 2 hours at 115oC and left overnight in cold storage to settle. 
6. The clear water was passed through consecutively lower pore filters until Whatman quality 1. The 
extract has no visual particulate and color of beer with foam on the top.  
7. The filtered medium was autoclaved at 115oC for 15 minutes.

Appendix VII. 
Modified f/2 medium 
The medium is based on modified f/2 medium and mixed in artificial seawater with addition of a 40mM tris 
buffer. The steps in producing the medium is as follow: 
1. Autoclave desired amount of deionized water in bottle that will be used to contain the medium. 
2. Add compounds of artificial seawater as per table 1 under continuous mixing from magnetic stirrer. 
No attention has been paid to the order of adding the compounds. 
3. Add 40mM tris base under continuous mixing from magnetic stirrer., 
4. Add the compounds of f/2 medium as per table 2 under continuous mixing from magnetic stirrer. 
5. The medium is titrated to pH 8 with HCl.
