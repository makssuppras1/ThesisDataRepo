Abstract
Abstract
This thesis deals with the development of a mathematical model to solve the problem of routing
trains at the Copenhagen Central Station during the morning rush hour (6am to 9am). Furthermore,
it also presents a solution visualization approach to visually aid the analysis of the obtained solutions.
The motivation behind this project comes from the real life context of solving the same problem at
the Danish State Railways (DSB). DSB provided the used datasets and supported this project with
operational information about the Copenhagen Central Station.
The developed model is based on the generalized set-partitioning problem (GSPP) model and was
able to solve the problem under given generalizations of constraints. Total enumeration of columns
was used considering the relatively small problem size. The designed model aims to maximize the
shortest headways (time interval between two trains) at the station platforms while avoiding collisions
at the station tracks. The results show that when using the commercial solver CPLEX the model
can be used to solve the problem in a matter of seconds. However, if the intention is not to use such
a solver further development of new solution approaches are to be expected to solve the problem in
a practical time interval.

INTRODUCTION

Introduction
The railway network plays an important role in the daily life of a signiﬁcant part of the Danish
population and is an essential part of the infrastructure in a modern and well-functioning society.
In the Danish context, it is extensively used in everything from daily commute to trips around the
country. However, the more passengers need to be transported using the public train system, the
more important and challenging it is to create eﬃcient and robust schedules to meet the demand.
In the development process of train schedules, eﬀectively routing trains into, out of, and through
stations is an essential part of ensuring the feasibility of the proposed schedules. This is due to
the many diﬀerent paths trains can take through (bigger) stations, the frequency at which trains
arrive and leave the station, the complexity of rules regarding trains crossing each other to avoid
collisions, and many other factors that make the problem tedious and time-consuming to solve by
hand.
The routing of trains at a station can take place using diﬀerent planning horizons for diﬀerent
reasons, hence railway planning problems can be classiﬁed according to their planning horizon.
A common approach is to classify them as either strategic, tactical, operational, or short-term.
Strategic problems deal with long term decisions of 2-20 years. Tactical problems deal with a shorter
horizon of about 1 year. Operational problems deal with the horizon of a month to a year and short-
term problems look into days and even problems in real-time. At a tactical level, the routing of
trains can be used to assess the feasibility of a timetable proposal, while at an operational and
short-term level it can be used to adjust a given plan according to unexpected change requirements.
Thus the development of tools to support the routing of trains at a station proves to be potentially
helpful at many levels of the planning process.
This thesis focuses on the problem of routing trains at the Copenhagen Central Station during
the morning rush hours1. Among others things, the main considerations taken into account while
routing trains at the station are related to the station capacity, the avoidance of train collisions on
their way in and out of the station and the robustness of the resultant routing solution in terms of
delays and other problems that might occur during daily operation. The more robust the plan the
more it is unaﬀected by these problems. The goal is to develop a mathematical model framework
that not only has the ability to deliver feasible and robust solutions in appropriate time, but that
also produces intuitive and relevant visualizations of the solutions to properly aid the planning
process. Furthermore the ambition is to design the model in such a way that it can be used on a
personal computer using open-source software to solve the problem in reasonable time.
Luckily, the ﬁeld of Operations Research oﬀers a variety of tools to deal with a broad variety of
problems including the train routing problem. In this thesis the potential of using the so-called
Generalized Set-Partitioning Problem (GSPP) model to optimize train routing will be investigated.
The GSPP has previously shown great results in solving the Berth Allocation Problem (BAP) which
has similarities to this problem, hence is believed to be applicable. The performance of the model
will be examined given the actual train station infrastructure of Copenhagen Central Station and

2.1
Generalized Set-Partitioning Problem
The set partitioning problem (SPP) and set packing (SP) are close related and well-known problems
that have been studied over decades. They deal with the problem of ﬁnding an optimal combination
of subsets of elements of a set. One of the early works with a overall introduction to them is given by
Balas and Padberg [1]. The generalized set partitionig problem (GSPP) model structure can be seen
in the section 2.1.1 where its implementation to the so-called berth allocation problem is presented.
The model proposed on this thesis builds upon it. The GSPP can be seen as a combination of SPP
constraints (1b) and SP constraints (1c) and is in general NP-hard. [2] [8]
2.1.1
Berth Allocation Problem modelled as a GSPP
The berth allocation problem (BAP) deals with the problem of assigning ships to berth positions in
a container terminal. It is a well studied problem and a diverse range of models have been applied to
it. It was considered in this thesis as it can be closely compared to the problem of assigning trains
to platforms of a railway station, as described in section section 6. Burkhal et. al. [2] reviewed
diﬀerent BAP models and compared them from a computational perspective. Their results indicate
that the generalized set-partitioning model proposed by Christensen and Holst [3] outperforms all
other models. The model can be described as:

2.2
Breadth-ﬁrst search (BFS) algorithm
A graph is a combinatorial structure extensively used in discrete mathematics. It consists of a
collection of nodes and edges, each "connecting" a pair of nodes. An edge of a graph can be classiﬁed
as a directed edge if it forms an asymmetrical relationship between its end nodes. [5] An example
of this are edges representing a unidirectional ﬂow between nodes A and B, where movement can
only occur from node A to B, but not the other way around. Graphs formed by directed edges can
be classiﬁed as directed graphs. This thesis uses directed graphs to model the tracks network of the
given railway station.
A common operation on graphs is that of traversing its nodes to build paths formed by a group
of nodes and the edges connecting them. Among many others, the so-called breadth-ﬁrst search
(BFS) is one of the simplest graph traversal algorithms. Starting from a given source node s the
BFS explores the graph in all possible directions from s visiting all nodes connected to s at each
iteration until its designed ending is triggered. [5] Together with the use of a queue to store the
nodes being visited by the algorithm, the BFS can be used to collect all possible paths in a directed
graph.

PREVIOUS WORK
3
Previous Work
Railway transportation has been an area of focus in Operations Research for a long time. This
section is therefore not intended to provide an extensive review of the literature, but to serve as a
pointer to the works that were considered most relevant and inﬂuential during the development of
this project.
Huisman et al. [4] reviewed Operations Research models and techniques used in passenger railway
transportation. Their problem classiﬁcation approach use both the planning horizon and physical
location, mainly related to how they are organized at the Nederlandse Spoorwegen Reizigers (NSR),
part of the Dutch National Railway Company (Nederlandse Spoorwegen/NS). The review covers a
wide range of problems, from strategic to short-term planning levels.
Lentink et al. [6] discussed a model-based algorithm approach for supporting the creation of shunting
plans for passenger trains. In their work they proposed a modiﬁcation of the tracks network model
to solve the problem of detecting conﬂict in cross sections of the tracks network. Their ﬁnal proposed
solution consists of a four step approach, which was validated with planners at NSR.
Christensen and Holst [3] presented a formulation of the BAP problem as a generalized set-
partitioning problem (GSPP). The BAP shares similarities with the problem of assigning trains
to railway platforms in which both deals with the assignment of a vehicles to physical locations
(berths and platforms) to be occupied by them for a given amount of time. The columns in the
model represent the assignment of a ship to a berth at an interval of time. Set packing constraints
ensure the a berth is occupied by at most one ship at each point in time. The authors conclude that
with column generation and brach and price approaches it was possible to obtain optimal solutions.
Lusby et al. [7] described a set packing inspired formulation to the problem of routing trains at
major railway junctions. The described formulation consists of a mixed integer program with a
resource based constraint system. In combination with discrete time intervals (15 seconds) track
sections form what they called tints. Tints resources are then given a capacity of one. A path
can then be represented in a column in the model consisting of a representation of occupied and
unoccupied tints. It also presented a way to handle banned track sections2. The path generation
process takes into account potential variations of train speed while using the paths. The solution is
based on a branch-and-price approach which attempts to dynamically expand the problem to ﬁnd
feasible solutions. The authors state that the model performs well in ﬁnding solutions within a few
percent of optimally in reasonable time based on results taken on real life test instance.

PROBLEM
4
Problem
The problem consists of routing regional and long distance trains (F&R) at the Copenhagen Central
Station given a speciﬁc timetable. A part of the station network is reserved exclusively for commuter
trains (S-Tog), hence these trains and corresponding tracks will not be considered in this project.
The station infrastructure with its platforms and tracks is shown in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Copenhagen Central Station platforms and tracks. The white platforms used for F&R trains and
their respective numbers are the ones under consideration in this project. The grey tracks and platforms are
used for S-Tog trains and out of this project’s scope.
The station consists of 12 platforms whereof 9 are reserved for F&R and the others for S-Tog.
Platform 26 has its particular property of being a track endpoint. Its use in this project is simplied
to not be used by trains travelling to or from the north direction (Nørreport). Given their origin,
destination, arrival and departure times, the trains must be assigned a platform and the complete
path of tracks it’s using on its way in and/or out of the station. The main constraints to be
considered when generating a solution are:
• Train collisions must be avoided at the tracks to and from the platforms;
• Coupling and decoupling of trains into diﬀerent formations;
• A platform can only be occupied by one train at any point in time.
These constraints are not exhaustive as there are more of them to be taken care of in a real life
context. These, together with proposed directions on how to include them in the model are discussed
in section 9.

Objective
In order to make the solution more robust longer headways are desired, allowing for the ﬁnal plan
to better mitigate potential train delays during operation. Therefore it is desired to optimize:
• the trains headways
• the assignment of platforms according to the the trains’ destinations.
Beyond ﬁnding solutions for the routing of the trains at the station, another problem was raised
during the development of this project: displaying a given solution in a clear and intuitive manner.
One of the intentions of this project is for such a mathematical model to potentially become an
actual tool in the planning process taking place at DSB, which is why having an intuitive way of
looking at a given solution is considered of signiﬁcant importance. Therefore, it was decided to
include it in the scope of this project.

6.2
Modelling the tracks network
The next step towards the development of the model was to ﬁnd a way to process the tracks network,
which will be used to ensure that a given solution avoids train collisions on their way in and out
of the station. The main goal of it, is to process the network to build feasible paths for the given
trains in the timetable. Due to this requirement, the network was modelled using directed graphs,
which have well known pathﬁnding algorithms related to them.
In order to keep it simple to process, it was decided to use two distinct graphs: one with inward-
directed edges, i.e from outside the station to the platforms, and one with the outwards-directed
edges, i.e from the platforms and away from the station. The crossing points, platforms, and end-
points were modelled as network nodes and the tracks connecting them as edges. Both graphs share
the same node structure and node numeration, so that their underlying structure is identical. An
illustration of the two graphs applied to the sample instance is shown in Figure 5.

6.5
Optimizing headways
The next step in the model development is to include the optimization of the trains headways. As
mentioned before, longer headways are desired as it makes the ﬁnal solution more robust, allowing
for the ﬁnal plan to better mitigate potential train delays during operation. The implemented
solution builds upon two main points:
1. Calculate the headways among all trains;
2. Detect which train are assigned to the same platform;
These points allow us to detect which is the lowest headway in a given solution and, consequently,
try to optimize it in the model’s objective function.
The calculation of the headways among all trains is stored in a square matrix of size equal to the
amount of trains, i.e one row and one column for each train. As the amount of trains is not expected
to be much higher than 100 trains for a rush hour period of 3 hours the size of this matrix is not
expected to be an issue. Every position in the matrix stores the headways in minutes between the
trains related to the respective row and column. Given arr(i) as the arrival time of train in row
i and dep(j) as the departure time of train in column j, each position of the matrix contains the
resultant headway between train i and j (headwayij) in the following way:

6.5.2
Further development of headways optimization
The model (4) was tested and the time it took to obtain solutions for the desired rush hour period
was around 1 to 2 hours. Even though this running time is acceptable, it does not facilitate a quick
iteration of potential plans. Thus more time was spent in an attempt to improve the running time
by further developing the model, more speciﬁcally, how the headways are handled.
The model (4) considers every possible pair of trains and their headway and tries to ﬁnd the absolute
best solution with the shortest headway being as long as possible. However, as it is later discussed
in section 9 "Discussions", it is not necessarily the best solution overall, because there are extra
constraints related to diverse factors that are not taken into consideration in the current state of
the model. The model’s value in its current state is considered to be in giving the planner a quick
starting point, which can then be further worked on to obtain the ﬁnal solution that meets these
extra requirements in the planning process. With this in mind, a new step was included in the
processing of the headways matrix. A new parameter γ was introduced to ﬁlter which headways are
going to be taken into consideration when solving the problem. This parameter is used to build a
list of train pairs (L) with headways of length up to the value of γ. An illustration of this concept
is given in Figure 13.
Figure 13: Building process of list of trains pairs L with headways up to γ = 2. As the train paris [3,1],
[3,2], [3,5] and [4,1] have headways bigger than γ they are not included in list L.
Furthermore a new variable δpij and a new parameter f (degree of freedom) were introduced.
Together they allow the solution to have f headways shorter or equal to γ without aﬀecting the
model objective value.

DISCUSSION
9
Discussion
The more realistic the solutions, the higher the chance of the model to be used in the planning
process. For this reason, it is important to further analyse the model assumptions. The assumption
that one minute is enough for a train to arrive at a platform independently of its origin, or leave the
station area independently of its destination was done based on the experience of the planners, as
this information was not found documented. It serves as a starting point for the development of the
model. More detailed calculations of travel time within the station area and the intricacies of speciﬁc
track segments are considered necessary towards generating more realistic solutions. furthermore,
the assumption that any platform is suitable for any train does not hold true in reality as the
platforms vary in length and it is possible that a platform is too short for a certain train length.
However, the inclusion of it in the calculations should be trivial, as it can be handled solely during
the generation of columns with no expected modiﬁcation in the mathematical model itself.
Speciﬁc safety regulations may apply at speciﬁc points of the tracks and must be fulﬁlled. They
specify a minimum time window between trains crossing these points and can be handled when
building the columns.
This project did not take into consideration the cargo trains and other special types of trains that
are not part of the provided timetable, but do need to be considered in the actual planning process
as they may compete for resources with the passenger trains. The inclusion of these trains will also
be a step forward towards more realistic solutions.
In the early stages of the train plan development process, a globally optimal solution according to
the headways is not very important. This is due to the fact that many other constraints need to
be taken into consideration until the ﬁnal plan is created. They are not only constraints related
to the trains, but also related to the diﬀerent activities and the necessary resources (e.g people
and materials) required to perform the planned activities for the trains at the station. Therefore,
at this stage of development, obtaining a fast solution with shorter headways is considered more
interesting than waiting longer for a solution with longer headways.
For a period of time of 3 hours, the model is small enough that solutions were obtained very fast
(1s) with CPLEX. The results obtained with the open source solver GLPK were not as good, being
potentially impractical to be used. In cases of periods with a high density of trains the development
of more advanced solution approaches (e.g column generation, branch-and-price etc.) are potentially
necessary if the intention is to not use advanced commercial solvers.
Perhaps one of the biggest advantages of the proposed model is its potential to handle complex
constraints in a fairly easy way. These constraints can be for example speciﬁc requirements for each
train line according to its characteristics or a more advanced path occupation approach taking into
account a potential variable train speed. These constraints can be handled in the process of building
the model matrices, which oﬀers a lot of ﬂexibility in regards to how the columns and tracks are set
to be occupied in a given column without the necessity to modify the mathematical model itself.

9.1
Future Work
9
DISCUSSION
9.1
Future Work
Development of a function to save and load generated columns for a given timetable may help saving
time. From a tactical perspective (around 1 year time horizon) timetables do not change often and
it is mostly like the case that for given a timetable the planner will try to ﬁnd diverse solutions and
make adjustments to them in order to come up with a ﬁnal solution. In this way, the planner would
potentially solve the problem for the same timetable multiple times. If the parameters aﬀecting the
generation of the columns do not change, then it is not necessary to generate them again.
Further improvements on the data processing to expand the amount of trains, including non-
standard trains with particular characteristics, are also necessary to make the model more applicable
in real life.
It is important to notice the limitation of the proposed model with regards to the size of the problem.
The bigger the station infrastructure and the amount trains travelling through it the harder it get
computationally. The amount of columns to be created get proportionally bigger with the amount
of possible combinations of trains, platforms and paths. Therefore, if it is desirable to extend the
period of time, and consequently, the number of trains under analysis then the more important it
will become to develop more advanced solution approaches.
At its current state the model does not try to minimize the amount of shortest headways. In other
words, a solution with one headway of one minute is considered as good as a solution with two
or more headways of one minute each. Developing the model to minimize the number of shortest
headways will potentially lead to better solutions from the planner perspective.

10
CONCLUSION
10
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to develop a mathematical model to solve the problem of routing
trains at the Copenhagen Central Station and provide an intuitive way of visualizing the solutions
obtained with it. The developed model builds upon the general set partitioning model (GSPP) and
was capable of optimize the shortest trains headways while avoiding collisions. Given the size of
the Copenhagen Central Station the problems was small enough so that total enumeration was a
viable option. The developed solution viewer consisted of two visuals. Combined they provide both
a general overview of the assignment of trains to the station platforms over time and a minute to
minute overview of the station tracks occupation.
The model was tested using a dataset provided by the Danish State Railways (DSB) and two
solvers: the open source solver GNU Linear Programming Kit (GLPK) and the commercial solver
CPLEX. A big diﬀerence in performance was seen when solving the same instances with diﬀerent
solvers, as expected. The CPLEX solver was able to solve the problem in about one second while
the GLPK solver took from 4 seconds to over one hour depending on the complexity of the instance
under analysis. The model was further developed in an attempt to obtain less optimal solutions but
faster with GLPK. This approach (using GLPK) was successful only to a certain extent, where the
complexity of the instance is somehow limited. Thus, further more advanced solution approaches
are considered necessary in this case.
