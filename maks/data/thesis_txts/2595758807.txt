Abstract
Currently, more than half of world population is living in cities. The change of land use
will lead to land surface temperature (LST) change. The resulted urban heat island (UHI) ef-
fects have multiple impacts on energy consumption, air quality and human health. The better
understanding of the effects of the impervious surfaces and green infrastructure on LST is nec-
essary for mitigating adverse impacts, and supporting urban planning and public health man-
agement. This study performed distance-based analysis, grid-based analysis and point-based
analysis to explore the inﬂuence of impervious surface, green area and water bodies on land
surface temperature.
The spatial variation of LST displayed a similar pattern in Paris for the four seasons. Differ-
ent patterns were observed in Geneva from Spring to Winter. From the distance-based analysis,
the highest LST was observed in the city center for all the seasons both in Paris and Geneva.
The dominant driving factor of LST variation was in the presence of a water body. With differ-
ent spatial distribution of impervious surface, a signiﬁcantly positive correlation was observed
between mean LST and impervious surface density in the two cities. The inverse correlation
between mean LST and green area density was observed in Paris for all the seasons. Generally,
mean LST decrease with the increase of green area density in Spring, Summer and Winter. A
Spatial Lag Model was used to explore the spatial correlation amongst LST and three indices
(i.e. the normalized difference built-up index (NDBI), the normalized difference vegetation in-
dex (NDVI) and the modiﬁed normalized difference water index (MNDWI), and density of
three land cover features, respectively. The inverse correlations between LST and NDVI and
MNDWI, respectively, were observed from Spring to Winter both in Paris and Geneva. The
same pattern was observed for the densities of green area and water body with LST in Geneva.
Spatial variation of LST is inﬂuenced by combined effects of various driving factors. The in-
crease of evenly distributed green area and water body is suggested to lower LST and mitigate
UHI effects.
Keywords Green urban infrastructure; Impervious surface; Land surface temperature; Ur-
ban heat islands; Urban water bodies

Introduction
1.1
Context
The global urban population will increase from 4.2 billion in 2018 to 5 billion by 2030. More than
half (55%) of the global population lives in urban areas currently. The ratio of urban-to-rural
population is expected to increase to two-thirds (66.4%) by 2050, according to United Nations
agencies [Nations, 2018]. Built-up areas are the nexus of more than 50% of the population and
80% of the economic activity on earth. The global urban land cover is expected to increase by
1,527,000 km2 by 2030 [Song et al., 2020]. This will lead to the Land surface temperature (LST)
being signiﬁcantly impacted by land use change caused with the population increase. LST are
generally higher in urban areas compared to rural green spaces. This is due to the urban heat
island effect, particularly during the summer time, leading to an impact on energy consumption,
air quality and human health [Mauree et al., 2019]. Therefore, a better understanding of the
effects of the impervious surfaces and green infrastructure on LST is necessary for mitigating
adverse impacts, and supporting urban planning and public health management [Norton et al.,
2015, Langemeyer et al., 2020].
Remote sensing technologies are currently a core technology for Earth observation [Fu et al.,
2020]. There are various remote sensing platforms and types of Earth observation sensors. They
are used to obtain data on the global scale with high precision and high resolution, expanding
the understanding of the environment from spatial and temporal perspectives [Guo et al., 2015].
Thermal remote sensing is one of the branches, capturing the radiation emitted from the ground in
the 3-5 µm and 8-14 µm wavelengths [Yu et al., 2014]. Land surface temperature can be obtained
from thermal remote sensing data. In addition, various indices, like the normalized difference
built-up index (NDBI), the normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) and the modiﬁed nor-
malized difference water index (MNDWI), can be obtained from the remotely sensed images for
monitoring the spatial distribution of land cover.
Landsat 5, Landsat 7 Enhanced Thematic Mapper Plus (ETM+) and Landsat 8 images are fre-
quently used in previous studies to derive LST. Kumar and Shekhar [2015] used Landsat 5 and
the Radiative Transfer Equation (RTE) method to derive LST without atmospheric corrections.
Weng and Fu [2014] characterized the annual and seasonal LST behaviors based on Landsat 5
images with less than 30% of cloud cover, using RTE method with NDVI Thresholds Method to
obtain the emissivity values. Bokaie et al. [2016] used the Dark Object Subtraction method to
correct satellite images atmospherically. LSTs were derived from Landsat 7 in Zhou et al. [2011]
and Zhang et al. [2009b]’s studies with different atmospheric correction methods. Yu et al. [2014]
compared three different approaches for LST derivation using Landsat 8 satellite images, includ-
ing the Radiative Transfer Equation-based method, the split-window algorithm and the single
channel method. Empirical emissivity values were used in most of the studies. To avoid the at-
mospheric correction, clear sky images were frequently selected [Zhou et al., 2011, Bokaie et al.,
2016]. Landsat 8, carrying the newest thermal infrared sensor (TIRS), was rarely used in seasonal

INTRODUCTION
LST estimation and analysis of LST and land cover features.
Land cover classiﬁcation maps were created using various methods in previous studies. Weng
et al. [2004] used the hybrid classiﬁcation procedure combined maximum likelihood and decision
tree algorithm for land cover classiﬁcation. Bokaie et al. [2016] classiﬁed various types of land
cover using supervised classiﬁcation method and maximum likelihood algorithm. Zhou et al.
[2011] derived the land cover classiﬁcation map from aerial imagery using an object-based clas-
siﬁcation approach. Generally, object based approaches and supervised classiﬁcation methods
allow to achieve better results. The spatial correlation of LST with dependent variables is signif-
icantly impacted by the accuracy of the chosen classiﬁcation methods. The modiﬁcation of land
cover classiﬁcation using situ-measurement or orthophoto method were not performed in those
studies.
A lot of studies have investigated the correlation between LST and land cover features. Es-
toque et al. [2016] applied urban-rural gradient analysis to determine the spatial variability of
LST and the spatial distribution of impervious surface and green space across the urban-rural
gradients. Multi-resolution grid-based analysis was used to examine the inﬂuence of impervious
surface and green space on LST based on various size of polygon grids. Zhang et al. [2017] exam-
ines the spatial and temporal patterns of LST in the context of the urban heat island (UHI) phe-
nomenon. The relationship of LST with six social-ecological variables, i.e., land use/land cover,
vegetation index, impervious surface index, water index, population density, and fossil-fuel CO2
emission, were also investigated. A total of 5000 random points were generated across the study
area in their study to explore the relationship of LST with NDVI, NDBI and NDWI. The bivariate
correlation analysis and scatter plots were frequently used to determine the relationships between
LST and variables. Due to various limitations, those studies focused on conducting analysis with
1-day land surface temperature data. There is however a lack of studies looking at how land cover
features inﬂuence both the spatial and annual evolution of land surface temperature and urban
heat islands effects.
1.2
Objectives
The aim of this study is to investigate the seasonal effects of land cover features on LST in
Paris and Geneva. The speciﬁc objectives of this study are: (1) to derive the LST, NDBI, NDVI
and MNDWI, and analyze their spatial variations using Landsat 8 satellite images; (2) to apply
distance-based analysis to explore the temporal and spatial pattern of LST along transects in dif-
ferent study areas; (3) to apply grid-based analysis to analyze the relationship between LST and
the density of green area, and between LST and the density of impervious surfaces; and (4) to
apply the Spatial Lag Model (SLM) to investigate the spatial correlation of dependent variables
with LST as point-based analysis. This study is expected to enhance the understanding of how
LST varies with land cover features across different seasons and in two different settings.

2.4
Grid-based analysis
This analysis examines the inﬂuence of different land cover on LST based on 100 * 100 m and
400 * 400 m polygon grids, which are typical of the standard sizes of a city block. After these
sets of grids have been prepared, the mean LST and the density of land cover for each grid were
computed. 100 * 100 m grid cells were created to map the distribution of different land cover. 400
* 400 m grid cells were used to create density map of impervious surfaces and green area.
For each grid cell, the density of impervious surfaces over the surface of the grid cell [ha/km2]
and density of green area over the surface of the grid cell [ha/km2] were computed, respectively.

2.5
Point-based analysis
In order to further explore the spatial correlation between land cover features and LST and the
combined effects of land cover features on LST, a spatially regressive model was created. In
this part, a total of 10000 random points were generated across Paris and 25000 generated across
Geneva, and used to investigate the spatial correlation of LST with NDVI, NDBI, and MNDWI
[Zhang et al., 2017]. The Spatial Lag Model was selected, which is a kind of classic spatial regres-
sive models and recommended in [Qiao and Liu, 2020]’s study with comparison to Spatial Error
Model and Ordinary Least Squares model. The estimated SLM model with exogenous explana-
tory variables of NDBI, NDVI MNDWI is as follows:
LST = α + ρWLST + β1NDV I + β2NDBI + β3MNDWI + ε
(11)
where:
α is the model intercept; ρ is the spatial autoregressive coefﬁcient, which is scale parame-
ter indicating the effect of the dependent variable in the neighbors on the dependent variable in
the focal area [Qiao and Liu, 2020]; W is the spatial weight matrix; WLST is an N×1 vector of
spatial lags for the dependent variables; β1, β2, and β3 are the K×1 vector of regression coefﬁ-
cients; β1NDVI, β2NDVBI and β3MNDWI are an N×K matrix of observations on the exogenous
explanatory variable multiplied by a K×1 vector of regression coefﬁcients β for NDVI, NDBI and
MNDWI, respectively; ε here is a N×1 vector of normally distributed random error terms. SLM
mainly considers the spatial autocorrelation of dependent variables, reﬂecting that the inﬂuenc-
ing elements of LSTs will act on critical regions by some kind of spatial mechanism.

2.6
Statistical analysis
Various statistical analysis methods were applied to identify the correlation between LST and
land cover features. The box plot was used in this study to depict the groups of LST through their
quartiles. The boxplot analysis was used to visually present the dispersion of LST and compare
the variation of LST amongst different land cover features. The signiﬁcance t-test was applied to
compare the difference of impervious surfaces and green area. The bivariate correlation analysis
and scatter plots were used to determine the relationships between mean LST and the density
of impervious surfaces and green area. Pearson’s correlation coefﬁcient was used to measure
linear correlation between LST and the densities. Statistical signiﬁcance test was used for testing
the hypothesis of whethere there are any relationship between the LST and the density. If the
probability is lower than the conventional 5% or 1% (p <0.05 or p <0.01), the correlation coefﬁcient
is called statistically signiﬁcant. The 95% conﬁdence interval (CI) was used to create error bars
for scatter plots.

Satellite imagery

area, vegetation cover, Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), Modiﬁed Normalized

the newest thermal infrared sensor for the Landsat project, providing two adjacent thermal bands,
i.e. band 10 TIRS 1 (10.6 - 11.19 µm) and band 11 TIRS 2 (11.5 - 12.51 µm). The resolution was 100
m and then resampled to 30 m. The land cloud cover and scene cloud cover of selected data sets
were less than 10%, respectively. No further atmospheric corrections were performed, as it was
assumed that the atmospheric conditions were homogeneous within the study area. Based on

4.5
Distance-based analysis
The similar patterns of LST were observed along the four transects with angle of 0, 45, 90 and 135◦
in Geneva, and the four transects with angle of 0, 45, 90 and 135◦in Paris, respectively. For the
two cities only the results along the transect of 90◦from North to South crossing the city center
are presented here.
Both in Geneva (Figure 16) and Paris (Figure 17), a symmetric pattern along the transects were
observed: LST shifting from low to high and low again. Peaks were always observed in the city
center. From the city center outward (both to the south and the north), a signiﬁcant temperature
gradient, as a result of the UHI effect can be seen. Similar structures have also been observed
in other cities [Weng et al., 2004, Xiao et al., 2007]. There are two exceptions observed in Paris
and Geneva, respectively. Deep valleys are always observed around the city center of Geneva
amongst all the seasons, as the city center is situated along Lake Geneva. Similarly, deep valleys
are observed around the city center of Paris in the four transects amongst all the seasons, affected
by the river Seine within the Paris Basin.

Figure 15: Transect of 90◦in Geneva
The LST of water body is usually lower than other kinds of land cover features [Pal and Ziaul,
2017]. The transect in Geneva crosses grids with different proportion of water body. As presented
in the Figure 15, the transect crosses a small size of water body in the northern part, leading to a
"valley" in the LST gradient. The variation of LST in the northern part is smaller than that around
the city center, which is affected by the large lake. The inﬂuence of water body size has been
explored in previous studies [Pal and Ziaul, 2017]. A negative correlation was observed between
the proportion of water body and LST, meaning that in a ﬁxed region, the lower the ratio of water
body, the higher the LST. As proved in [Huang et al., 2008] ’s study, the average size of the water
body, the proportion of the total landscape composed of the largest water area, the isolation and
fragmentation of the water body are all negatively correlated with the average LST. Except for
the inﬂuencing factors of the surrounding, the total size and amount of core water have a greater
impact on LST, indicating that the interior water body has small external inﬂuence. The decrease
of LST in Geneva is more signiﬁcant than the decrease in Paris. The area of Lake Geneva is far
larger than the River Seine. The decrease of LST around city center is around 10◦C in Geneva
from Spring to Autumn (2◦C). In Paris, however, the decrease is around 3.5◦C in Spring and
Autumn, 6◦C in Autumn and 2◦C in Winter. The decreasing pattern is similar in Winter for Paris
and Geneva, which can be explained by the heat capacity of water body. Water body retains more
temperature than surrounding non water body in Winter, and therefore maintains a minimum
temperature differential.

The results of Paris showed that green area is negatively correlated with LST with statistical
signiﬁcance. It is consistent with numerous previous studies, which identiﬁed negative corre-

density and patch density [Maimaitiyiming et al., 2014], and fraction of vegetation [Naeem et al.,
2018]. The main reasons for the decrease of LST within green area, compared to impervious sur-
faces, may be the low heat storage capacity and heat losses through evaporation and transpiration
processes in the green areas. Green areas can lower LST by providing shade to prevent land sur-
faces from direct heating during daytime, which was conﬁrmed in [Zhou et al., 2011]’s study.

The overall patterns of green area with LST in Geneva are different from Paris. LST increases

impervious surfaces densities. The results indicate that when more than half of areas in Geneva
are covered by impervious surfaces, LST increases with impervious surfaces density. Exceptions
are observed in Autumn and Winter. LST increases with green area density, from 40 - 60 to 80 -
100 ha/km2. The pattern is different from Paris and some previous studies as aforementioned. It
may be explained by the patch density of green space. [Li et al., 2012] found that patch density
of green space had a signiﬁcant positive relationship with LST. The increase of patch density will
increase LST. The more fragmented the green space, the higher LST, which was found in [Zhang
et al., 2009a]’s study. Given the same amount of green area, patch density increase would lead
to an increase in total patch edges and a decrease in mean patch size. A continuous and green
space may produce stronger cool island effects than that of several pieces of green area with
the same area in total. An evenly distributed, rather than clustered, pattern of green area can
further reduce LST [Zhou et al., 2011]. The increase of total patch edges may provide more shade
for the surrounding and enhance the energy exchange between green area and the surrounding,
contributing to the decrease of LST [Li et al., 2012, Zhang et al., 2009a]. However, the effect may
not play a signiﬁcant role in controlling LST. LST difference between 20 - 40 and 80 - 100 ha/km2
density groups is around 0.3◦C.

5
Conclusion
This study used land cover data in 2017 for Paris and the data in 2020 for Geneva to study the
relationship between land cover features and LST. Previous studies [Youneszadeh et al., 2015,
Kumar et al., 2018, Pal and Ziaul, 2017] have shown that LST changed over time due to change in
different land cover categories. The 14.14% decrease of barren land may introduce 9◦C increase of
LST. Seasonal LST was averaged based on Landsat 8 satellite images from 2013 to 2020. Land use
change in the study areas, in terms of growth of urban agglomerations, transforming of wetland
into artiﬁcial land, decrease of agricultural land, and etc., would contribute to the uncertainty
of seasonal LST. The estimation of LST from thermal images can be more complicated than the
method used in this study. The images were acquired with land cloud cover and scene cloud
cover less than 10%, respectively. The effects of the atmosphere and surface roughness on LST
were hence not taken into account. Although atmospheric correction (which may introduce an
error of 4–7 ◦C for the mid-latitude summer atmosphere [Weng et al., 2004]) was not performed
here, we are nonetheless conﬁdent that the selection of the data with little or no cloud cover
limited the impact of the correction. Empirical NDVI values of soil and water, and shape factor
were used to estimate the proportion of vegetation and emissivity. However, snow usually show
very low NDVI values (e.g. 0.1 or less), which is lower than the empirical value of 0.2 in this
study, contributing to the uncertainty of estimated LST and the interpretation of LST patterns
[USGS, 2020]. In addition, different climatic conditions may signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the amplitude
of daytime LST [Zhou et al., 2011].
In this study, the remotely sensed data from 2013 to 2020 in Paris and Geneva were divided
into Spring, Summer, Autumn and Winter. The correlation between spatial variability of LST
and land cover features (i.e. impervious surface, green area and water body) were investigated
using distance-based analysis, grid-based analysis and point-based analysis. The seasonal LST
variation from city center to rural area were explored by transects across the cities. Both in Paris
and Geneva, the highest LST was observed in the city center for all the seasons. The dominant
driving factor of LST variation was water body. With different spatial distribution of impervi-
ous surface, a signiﬁcantly positive correlation was observed between mean LST and impervious
surface density in the two cities. The inverse correlation between mean LST and green area den-
sity was observed in Paris for all the seasons. It was however not possible to fully conclude on
the impact on the mean LST and green area density in Geneva, as it is a large city with complex
mixture of land cover features. Generally, mean LST decrease with the increase of green area
density in Spring, Summer and Winter. SLM was used to explore the spatial correlation amongst
LST and three indices (i.e. NDBI, NDVI and MNDWI), and density of three land cover features,
respectively. The inverse correlations between LST and NDVI and MNDWI, respectively, were
observed from Spring to Winter both in Paris and Geneva. The same pattern was observed for
the densities of green area and water body with LST in Geneva. It was difﬁcult to establish a
general rule for the correlation between NDBI and LST, and impervious surface and LST due to
the fact that they are affected by an ensemble of factors, e.g. climate condition, topography and
land cover classiﬁcation. Spatial variation of LST is inﬂuenced by combined effects of various

more deeply the inﬂuence of built-up areas on LST. We can however conclude that future urban
