Abstract
Local pulse wave velocity estimations can be used to asses arterial stiffness, which is
further associated with the progression of atherosclerosis. Local pulse wave velocity
estimations are of great clinical interest but it has turned out to be difficult to both
ensure the precision within individuals, as well as to evaluate the accuracy. The aim of
this thesis is to develop an estimation scheme for local pulse wave velocity estimations
in the common carotid artery. The developed estimation scheme relies on fast imaging
i.e. 2250 frames/s, which is acquired using synthetic aperture imaging ultrasound.
The pulse wave velocity is calculated using the transit time and the travel distance
of the pulse wave between two nearby measuring sites. Due to pulse wave reflections
the determination of the transit time is achieved by means of time reference points
(the systolic foot and the dicrotic notch). The time reference points are found in
diameter distention waveforms acquired simultaneously at the two measuring sites.
The methods used in the estimation scheme is tested in a phantom study, a simulation
study and finally in ten healthy volunteers.
The results of the phantom study showed that it was possible to obtain repeatable
diameter distention waveforms across multiple pulse cycles. The diameter change in
the phantom setup was found to be 0.80 mm. However, due to potential limitations
in the phantom setup it was not possible to acquire repeatable PWVs as intended.
Therefore, a simulation study was conducted. The results of the simulation study
confirmed that the estimation scheme was capable of detecting the correct tissue
displacement with a relative percentage error of 5.4%. Furthermore, the estimation
scheme was capable of calculating accurate PWVs, when omitting potential pulse
wave reflections. Finally, the estimation scheme was tested on data acquired from the
right common carotid artery of ten healthy volunteers. The results showed that the
average intra-individual variation was lower when using the dicrotic notch as a time
reference point (SDdn of 0.62 m/s, CVdn of 11.94%) compared to the systolic foot
(SDsf of 1.53 m/s, CVsf of 26.89% ). The PWV acquired using the dicrotic notch
showed a moderate positive correlation to the PWVs acquired from the Bramwell-Hill
equation (r = 0.50). To gain clinical acceptance refinements are still needed in both
the estimation scheme and in the reference method.

Resumé
Lokale pulsbølgehastigheder kan bruges til at vurdere halspulsårens stivhed og dermed
graden af åreforkalkning. Dette har stor klinisk interesse, men det har vist sig både at
være svært at sikre den intra individuelle præcision samt at evaluere nøjagtigheden.
Formålet med dette master projekt er at udvikle en algoritme der kan udregne lokale
pulsbølgehastigheder i halspulsåren.
Algoritmen benytter sig af syntetisk apertur
billedannelse som ved hjælp af ultralyd kan opnå en billedhastighed på 2250 billeder
i sekundet. Pulsbølgehastigheden beregnes ud fra transittiden mellem to nærtliggende
målesteder, samt den korresponderende tilbagelagte distance. Grundet pulsbølge re-
fleksioner bestemmes transittiden ved hjælp af tidsreference punkter (Den systoliske
fod og det dikrotiske hak). Tidsreferencepunkterne defineres i kurver som beskriver
blodkarets diameter ændring som funktion af tid. De udviklede metoder som benyttes
i algoritmen testes i et phantom studie, et simulerings studie og til sidst i et klinisk
studie som involverer ti raske frivillige personer.
Resultatet af phantom studiet viste, at det var muligt at detektere diameter æn-
dringerne som funktion af tid. Resultatet viste en diameter ændring på 0.80 mm.
Grundet potentielle begrænsninger i forsøgsopsætningen var det imidlertid ikke muligt
at opnå konsistente pulsbølgehastigheder. Derfor blev der udført et simulerings studie.
Resultatet af simuleringsstudiet bekræftede, at algoritmen, med kun en lille afvigelse
på 5.4%, er i stand til at udregne den korrekte vævsbevægelse og dermed diame-
ter ændring. Desuden kunne simulerings studiet også udregne nøjagtige pulsbølge
hastigheder, dette var dog uden at tage pulsbølge refleksioner i betragtning. Endeligt
blev algoritmen testet på data optaget fra den højre halspulsåre på ti raske frivillige
personer. Resultatet viste at den gennemsnitlige intra-individuelle variation var lavest
ved anvendelse af det dikrotiske hak som reference punkt (SDdn = 0.62 m/s, CVdn
= 11.94) sammenlignet med den systoliske fod (SDsf = 1.53 m/s, CVdn = 26.89).
Pulsbølgehastighederne opnået fra det dikrotiske hak viste en moderat positiv korre-
lation til pulsbølgehastighederne opnået fra Bramwell-Hill ligningen (r = 0.50). For
at opnå klinisk accept er der stadig behov for forbedringer både i algoritmen og i
reference metoden.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Pulse wave velocity (PWV) estimations are commonly used to asses arterial stiff-
ness, which is further associated with the progression of atherosclerosis. Section 1.1
contains the motivation for acquiring local PWV estimations along with a descrip-
tion of the underlying pathology of atherosclerosis. Section 1.2 presents the relevant
anatomy used throughout the thesis and Section 1.3 provides a short description of
local PWV estimations. Section 1.4 presents the state of the art including the rela-
tion between local PWV and arterial stiffness, which is used to asses atherosclerosis.
Finally, Section 1.5 presents the hypothesis of this thesis.
1.1
Atherosclerosis
Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) are globally the number one cause of death. In 2016 it
was estimated that more than 17.9 million people died due to CVDs [32]. Atheroscle-
rosis is the pathology that underlies CVD and refers to the narrowing of arteries,
which is caused by plaque deposit. Arteriosclerosis refers to the arterial stiffening
and hardening of blood vessels. People with atherosclerosis always have arteriosclero-
sis, however it is not necessarily the other way around. Atherosclerosis is defined as a
focal inflammatory fibro-proliferative response to an injury as postulated in 1977 by
Ross et al [36]. The injury can be caused by many risk factors such as smoking, dia-
betes melitus, hypertension, high levels of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) in the blood,
and a genetic predisposition [33]. Most risk factors induce injury to the protective
endothelial cells covering the innermost layer of the blood vessel called the intima.
The injury causes the endothelial cells to secrete adhesion molecules, chemokines and
chemoattractants, which then attract monocytes and other white blood cells to the
intima. Monocytes that are attached to the intima will begin to migrate into the
intima. It is upon entry that the monocytes transform into macrophages and be-
come activated [33]. Macrophages are phagocytes and they engulf substances such as
LDL present in the intima. This is initially a protective process. However, activated
macrophages release toxic oxygen, which oxidizes LDL. The oxidized LDL is then ex-
cessively engulfed by nearby macrophages. This causes the macrophages to become
foam cells. Foam cells are considered to be the primary cause of atherosclerosis and
the primary component in plaque development. Furthermore, activated macrophages
also produce growth factors that both increase the proliferation of smooth muscle
cells and increase the presence of collagen. This causes the lumen of the blood vessel

2

to narrow and increase the arterial stiffness. Fig. 1.1 shows an atherosclerotic blood
vessel.
Figure 1.1: This figure shows how the plaque development takes place inside the
vessel wall [41].
The initial signs of atherosclerosis are seen in persons in their teens and early 20s
[16]. It starts with fatty streaks. Fatty streaks alone do not cause any symptoms
but they can progress into fibroatheroma. Fibroatheroma is characterized by further
accumulation of macrophages, fatty materials, connective tissue, and smooth muscle
cells, which again causes the plaque to increase in size and eventually occlude the
blood vessel. Finally, plaque structures can become unstable and rupture, this is
typical seen in individuals older than 55 years. Unstable plaque is characterized by a
thick lipid core and a thin fibrous cap. This increases the risk of rupture, which can
cause thrombosis distal to the rupture site. Symptoms of atherosclerosis are typically
not seen before 70% of the blood vessel is occluded or rupture causes stroke.
It is therefore of great clinical interest to diagnose atherosclerosis before symptoms
occur. Here, local PWV estimations in the common carotid artery (CCA) are inves-
tigated as a diagnostic tool to obtain local information about the state of the blood
vessels.
1.2
Anatomy
All arteries in the human body originate directly or indirectly from the aorta [41]. The
aorta thus forms a cardiovascular connection between the heart and the rest of the
body. The aorta can be divided into three segments: The aortic arch, the descending
aorta and the ascending aorta [41]. In this thesis only the aortic arch is of interest.
The aortic arch branches into three major arteries, which carries blood to the head
and to the upper limps. These arteries are called: The brachiocephalic artery, the
left CCA and the left subclavian artery. The brachiocephalic artery branches into
the right CCA, which supplies the right side of the head and neck with blood. Thus

The close proximity of the IJV to the CCA should be considered when making PWV
estimations on the CCA. This is because the pulse wave, which is travelling in the
blood vessel wall, is expected to vary in wave dynamics depending on the vessel wall

CCA is often used for local PWV estimations. This is because the CCA is located
superficial and offers a straight and a relatively branch free measuring site compared
to other blood vessels [27].

to-femoral PWV is also referred as the regional PWV and is considered to be the
gold standard measurement [27]. However, as a consequence of the long measuring
distance it will not give valuable information about the local arterial wall condition
[15]. In addition, the measuring distance (∆d) is often based on the surface distance
between the two measuring sites [27], which is clearly an approximation that will
influence the PWV estimations. It is therefore of great clinical interest to measure
the local PWV, but it has turned out to be difficult, to both ensure the precision
within individuals, as well as to evaluate the accuracy, as the ground truth for local
PWVs remains unknown.
In 1991 Benthin et al. [1] used ultrasound to measure the distension waveform (DWF)
at two locations, separated by 3.6 cm, along the CCA. The DWF depicts the displace-
ment in the blood vessel wall caused by the propagating pulse wave. The transit time
(∆t) was calculated using the cross-correlation between the two DWFs and was fur-
ther improved by employing polynomial interpolation. Early results showed some
unexpected artifacts, which were found to be caused by pulse wave reflections. Pulse
wave reflections arise from anatomical obstacles like arterial bifurcations. The pulse
wave reflections were found to affect the shape of the DWF and obscure the PWV
estimations. Benthin et al. concluded, that it was possible to obtain results, that
were less effected by reflections, by using short parts of the recordings around the
systolic foot. Here the systolic foot refers to the beginning of the systole.
In 2004 Rabben et al. [35] used another approach and investigated an area-flow (QA)
method for local PWV estimations on five anaestethetized dogs and 21 humans.

aortic valve and represents the end of the systole and the start of the diastole [27].
The dicrotic notch is determined as the time point after the waveform peak, at which
the acceleration waveform reaches a maximum. The systolic foot is determined in
the same way but before the waveform peak. In the study conducted by Hermeling
et al. they recorded the propagation of 14 acceleration waveforms along a 16.4 mm
blood vessel segment. Linear regression was then performed on the position of the
measurements and the corresponding arrival time of the reference points. Finally, the
PWV was estimated from the reciprocal of the regression slope. They found that the
PWV in young individuals is 5.00±0.70 m/s and that the diameter distention of the
blood vessel in young individuals is 0.69±0.17 mm. Hermeling et al. also used the
Bramwell-Hill equation (1.4) as a reference method to evaluate the accuracy of the
method. However, instead of using ¯A they used Ad, which denotes the cross-sectional
area in diastole. They concluded that the local PWV could be measured with a rela-
tively good intra-individual reproducibility, i.e. a coefficient of variation of 10% and
that they where able to discriminate between young and older individuals. However,
the PWV was only poorly correlated to the evaluation method with a correlation of
determination of r2 = 0.54. This was done using the dicrotic notch as a time-reference
point.
In 2016 Huang et al. [15] compared PWVs obtained from the diameter DWF, the
anterior wall DWF and the posterior wall DWF, respectively. For each method they
compared the PWV obtained from the systolic foot and the dicrotic notch. They
concluded that the precision is higher when using the diameter DWF, possibly be-
cause it is less susceptible to motion caused by the patient (movement, breathing or
swallowing). They found that in the healthy subjects the PWV was 5.21 ± 0.95 m/s.
This was obtained using the diameter DWF and the dicrotic notch as a time reference
point. Finally, the dicrotic notch appeared more reliable in discriminating between
healthy and hypertensive groups.
The majority of studies have used conventional ultrasound. In conventional ultra-
sound each frame is constructed from a series of imaging lines. This creates a trade-off
between spatial resolution and frame rate. In local PWV estimations fast image ac-
quisition is necessary. This is because the pulse wave is expected to travel at velocities
as high as 10 m/s over a short measuring distance 1-2 cm [40][6]. Recently, this has
been solved by using synthetic aperture imaging (SAI), which can provide a frame
rate as high as the pulse repetition frequency [28]. In 2011 M. Couade et al. [6] used

SAI to calculate the PWV of the waves generated by the systolic foot and the waves
generated by the dicrotic notch. The best precision was obtained using the pulse
wave generated by the dicrotic notch, with a coefficient of variation of 13%. However,

From the research described above and from the review article [27], it becomes clear
that there are many research directions, which employ different methods for local
PWV measurements.
This means that there is not a single method that can be
considered as a reference standard.
Therefore, evaluating the performance of the
methods with respect to accuracy has shown to be difficult. For that reason math-
ematical derived relations, such as the Bramwell-Hill equation have often been used
to evaluate the accuracy [12][35]. A major drawback to the Bramwell-Hill equation
is that the local PP is difficult to access non-invasively [12] and for that reason it is
often substituted with the brachial PP [35]. Another thing that complicates PWV
estimates in all studies is the pulse wave reflection. The measured pulse wave is not
composed of a single wave but a superposition of multiple waves. These waves are
a result of multiple physiological reactions such as the opening and closure of the
aortic valve, as well as reflections caused by obstacles such as the bifurcation in the
arterial tree. For that reason certain time reference points are used as they are less
affected by the reflections. Finally, SAI enables high frame rate, which is desired
when measuring high velocities over short distances.
1.5
Thesis Hypothesis
The research results described in Section 1.4 show that there are many different
approaches regarding PWV estimations using ultrasound. None of the mentioned
studies have succeeded in estimating the local PWV, while evaluating the method’s
accuracy to a satisfactorily extent. To implement local PWV estimations using ul-
trasound in clinical settings, the method has to be both precise and accurate. This is
why it is still important to conduct new research on this topic. In this master thesis
it is hypothesized that it is possible to calculate the local PWV in the CCA of ten
healthy volunteers, by means of SAI. Both the systolic foot and the dicrotic notch
are investigated with the purpose of determining, which time reference point is best
suited for PWV estimations. The estimation scheme is tested and evaluated on phan-
tom data and on simulated data. In addition it is hypothesized that the estimation
scheme is precise i.e. a coefficient of variation less than 10% within individuals and
accurate with a correlation coefficient of r ≥0.80 to the reference method when tested
on clinical data. Here, the Bramwell-Hill equation is used as a reference method.

2.1
Synthetic Aperture

Ultrasound systems consist of a transducer, which functions both as a transmitter
and as a receiver of ultrasounds. It is the piezoelectric crystals on the transducer
that convert electrical signals into acoustic waves. The acoustic waves propagating
through the tissue are then being reflected and scattered when interacting with tissue
boundaries, which gives rise to echoes. These echoes are intercepted by the transducer
and converted into electrical signals by the piezoelectric crystals. The signals are then
used to create images of tissue structures. Ultrasound is a non-invasive and non-
ionizing image modality and is therefore considered safe. In conventional ultrasound
the frame-rate (fr) is generally low, as each image is constructed from a series of

spherical wave with a larger amplitude each element must be delayed appropriately.

behind the transducer it is possible to emulate a diverging spherical wave by means of
multiple elements in transmit [29]. The appropriate timing applied on each transmit
element is calculated using the time of flight from the position of the virtual source
to the receiving elements. The results are superimposed waves with a larger ampli-
tude and thereby an increased signal to noise ratio and penetration depth. Typically
multiple virtual sources are used to ensure that the entire region of interest is being
insonified.

2.4
Tissue Displacement
The tissue displacement is calculated using the line segments. Each line segment has
a spatial extend of 0.6 cm and must cover a region, in which the blood vessel wall

user in a B-mode reference image. The acquired line segments are then beamformed
using the flow sequence to obtain a higher temporal resolution. The result is four
line segments each containing frames of RF flow data, which describe the temporal
behavior of the vessel wall in that exact location.

2.5
Pulse Wave Reflections
Intuitively, it should be possible to calculate the PWV by simply comparing the time
difference between the two diameter DWFs. This can be done using the cross correla-
tion. Nevertheless, it has been shown that pulse wave reflections can alter the profile
of the DWF and thus prevent precise PWV measurement [1]. Pulse wave reflections
occur as the pulse wave encounters obstacles such as blood vessel bifurcations. The
CCA, which is investigated in this study, splits into the external carotid artery and
the internal carotid artery and, thus, creates a reflection site. The pulse wave reflec-
tions will then travel back and affect the distal measuring site first followed by the
proximal measuring site. The effect of the pulse wave reflections can be seen on the
diameter DWFs, see Fig. 2.8.
Figure 2.8: Figure showing how the proximal diameter DWF is affected by tis-
sue displacement first, as expected. However, the reflecting pulse wave
causes the distal diameter DWF to reach the waveform peak before the
proximal diameter DWF.
As expected the proximal diameter DWF is affected by tissue displacement first. This
is because the pulse wave reaches the proximal measuring site first followed by the
distal measuring site. However, in some cases the reflecting pulse wave causes the
distal diameter DWF to reach the waveform peak before the proximal diameter DWF.
This means that the time difference between the two curves is being distorted. In

reflections. The systolic foot is defined as the maximum acceleration before the wave-

2.6
Segmentation of Heartbeats
This estimation scheme calculates PWVs from both the systolic foot and the dicrotic
notch. The estimation scheme thus calculates two PWV from each heartbeat. As
heartbeats are not perfectly periodic, a robust segmentation method is needed. To
do this the user selects an interval in the proximal diameter DWF see Fig. 2.11.
Figure 2.11: Simple schematic showing the reference points and the selected points.
This allows the user to deselect failed diameter DWF. The chosen interval is used
to obtain the duration of an average heartbeat period together with the number of
heartbeats. The duration of an average heartbeat period is calculated by performing
autocorrelation of the proximal diameter DWF within the chosen interval. The dura-
tion between the maximum peak and the following peak in the autocorrelation is used
to get the duration of an average heartbeat period. The number of heartbeats are
then calculated by dividing the duration of the average period with the duration of
the interval. The result is then rounded to the nearest integer. Moreover, the average
duration of one period is used to divide the diameter DWF into roughly its heartbeat
periods. Depending on the periodicity of the heartbeats the signal may drift. This
means that in some cases the systolic foot is not included in a given period, causing
faulty measurements. To increase the robustness of the method, the waveform peak
in each heartbeat period is used for fine adjustments. In this method, the waveform
peak is defined as the position of the maximum value within the first half of a given
heartbeat period. By considering data within half a period before the waveform peak
==PAGE:41==
2.6 Segmentation of Heartbeats
25
and half a period after the waveform peak, it is possible to obtain two subsegments
which contain the systolic foot and the dicrotic notch, respectively. Fig. 2.12 shows
the subsegments in the proximal diameter DWF.
Figure 2.12: Figure showing the segmentation of the proximal diameter DWF.
The corresponding subsegments in the distal diameter DWF are acquired using the
same parameters obtained from the proximal diameter DWF. This ensures that the
relative displacement of the time reference points between the proximal and distal
diameter DWF remains unchanged. Having segmented both the proximal and the
distal diameter DWF into its subsegments it is possible to make pulse wave estima-
tions. The systolic foot is used as a time reference point in the subsegment acquired
before the waveform peak and the dicrotic notch is used as a time reference point in
the subsegment acquired after the waveform peak. Each time reference point is then
determined as the maximum acceleration of the corresponding subsegment.

3.1
Experimental Setup for The Phantom Study
The purpose of the phantom setup is to imitate the real cardiovascular system as
much as possible and provide conditions that enable consistence and repeatable mea-
surements. To do this, a specialized flow pump is used to imitate the heart. It consist
of a piston, a valve, an inlet and an outlet. The piston controls the flow motion and
the valve provides the unidirectional flow. Blood mimicking fluid and tissue mim-
icking material are used to imitate the properties of blood and tissue. The tubing
is made out of plastic and is embedded into to the phantom. The blood mimicking
fluid is pumped through the tubing into the phantom and is fed back into the flow
pump. The phantom is molded around a 3D printed core, see Fig. 3.1. The geometry
of the core is created from MR data and is based on subject_002 in the pre-clinical
study [31]. The geometry of the core includes the CCA, the bifurcation, the internal
and the external carotid artery. The core is then removed from the phantom and the
resulting void forms the blood vessel lumen.

CHAPTER 4
Simulation
As the phantom model was unable to validate the estimation scheme with respect to
PWV estimations, a simulation study is conducted. Again, the aim is to see if the
estimation scheme is capable of providing accurate PWVs, this time in a simulated
environment. This is done by comparing the PWV found by the estimation scheme
to the simulated PWV input. The simulation tool is developed within Center for Fast
Ultrasound (CFU) and the data is constructed using Field-II [19].
4.1
Experimental Setup for The Simulation Study
The simulation constructs image data of a blood vessel, in which a pulse wave prop-

CHAPTER 5
In-vivo study
This chapter presents the results, when applying the estimation scheme on in-vivo
clinical data. Section 5.1 provides a short description of the data acquisition con-
ducted on each subject. Section 5.2 describes the tissue displacement of subject_001
and Section 5.3 provides an evaluation of each of the subjects corresponding B-mode
images and diameter DWFs. Section 5.4 contains the results when using the discrotic
notch and the systolic foot as time reference points. The two reference points preci-
sion is evaluated using a lower tailed paired t-test. Finally, the accuracy is evaluated
using the Bramwell-Hill equation as a reference method.
5.1
Measurements
The data is recorded using SARUS and is obtained from a longitudinal scan of the
right CCA in ten healthy volunteers. The measurements are conducted 3 cm before
the bifurcation. Just as the phantom data, the data acquisition was performed in
2016 [31].
Each ultrasound examination is started by letting the subject rest for
10-15 minutes prior to the examinations. The blood pressure (BP) of the subject is
then measured using a cuff. Since, the real-time images from SARUS are of poor
quality, a conventional ultrasound scanner is used to position the transducer over
the region of interest. The transducer is then disconnected from the conventional
ultrasound scanner and connected to SARUS for data acquisition. The procedures
are conducted while holding the transducer by hand and not in a fixed setup as with
the phantom.
This includes two obvious sources of errors, as the medical doctor
holding the transducer as well as the patient can cause disturbances by moving in
between the two acquisition procedures. The scans are performed by an assigned
medical doctor, while an engineer is controlling SARUS [31].

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
PWV estimations are of great clinical interest as they can be used to evaluate the
elastic properties of the blood vessel, which is correlated to atherosclerosis and age.
In regional PWV estimations, 10 m/s is considered the cut-off value when predicting
cardiovascular events in clinical settings. However, regional PWV estimations do not
provide local information about the blood vessel and the credibility of the results
are affected by the approximated measuring distance (∆d). As a solution to this
problem, local PWV estimations are conducted over a short measuring distance in
the CCA. In this master thesis local PWVs are estimated based on time-reference
points in tissue displacement curves. The data is acquired using SAI with a frame
rate of 2250 frames/s. Three studies are conducted: A phantom study, a simulation
study and an in-vivo study. In both the in-vivo study and in the simulation study
a rectangular window was used instead of a Hanning window. The Hanning window
was used in the phantom study to remove the contribution of air bubbles in the blood
mimicking fluid. The rectangular window was used to remove the contribution from
the stationary tissue in the in-vivo data. However, it should be emphasised that the
same estimation scheme is applied in the simulation study and in the in-vivo study.
Moreover the same filter characteristic is used in all three studies.
6.1
Phantom Study
The purpose of the phantom study was to imitate the real cardiovascular system
and provide conditions that would enable consistence and repeatable measurements.
The tissue displacement captured in the diameter DWFs was indeed repeatable and
comparable to what was seen in the movie-clip. However, the second derivative of
the diameter DWF was obscured by noise, which made the localization of the time
reference points difficult. The noise occurred even though a Savitzky-Golay digital
differentiator with a smoothing filter with the length of 0.028 s was used. The noise
came from air bubbles trapped in the blood mimicking fluid. The air bubbles were
seen as RF-lines of locally increased intensity, which created small lack shifts in the
diameter DWF estimation. The small disturbances, caused by passing air bubbles,
became highly visible in the acceleration waveform. It was possible to reduce the
amount of noise, by masking out the air bubbles. This was done using a Hanning
window, which followed the tissue movement by means of a previously estimated
diameter DWF. Furthermore, a cross correlation artifact was observed, causing a
==PAGE:84==
68
6 Discussion
linear trend in the diameter DWF. This cross correlation artifact is only observed in
the phantom setup. One explanation is that the homogeneity of the tissue mimicking
material confuses the cross-correlation, whereas the more heterogeneous tissue seen
in the clinical data is more robust against this kind of cross correlation artifact.
Although these challenges were resolved the resulting PWVs were still inconsistent
and unreliable. This indicates that the estimation scheme is unable to capture the
PWVs in the phantom setup. One explanation is that reflections caused by the plastic
box surrounding the phantom, are obscuring the localization of the time reference
points. Another explanation is that the plastic tubing causes the measurements to be
conducted too closely to the region in which the pulse wave is formed and therefore
the actual propagation is not yet seen. This could help explain the unreliable PWV
estimations seen in the phantom study.
Despite the failed PWV estimations the
phantom study was capable of detecting the tissue movement. The resulting diameter
DWF showed a tissue diameter displacement of 0.80 mm, which is as expected when
compared to a movie-clip of the same phantom.
6.2
Simulation Study
As the phantom model was unable to validate the estimation scheme with respect to
PWV estimations a simulation study was conducted. The purpose of the simulation
study was to see if the estimation scheme was capable of providing accurate PWVs,
this time in a simulated environment. The simulation constructs image data of a
blood vessel, in which a pulse wave with a prior known velocity propagates.
By
applying the estimation scheme to the simulated data it was possible to calculate the
PWV. This PWV was then compared to the input PWV. To make the simulation as
real as possible a physiological pulse wave was used as an input. The physiological
pulse wave was derived from the diameter DWF obtained from subject_001. Two
simulations were performed using the same physiological pulse wave.
In the first
simulation the pulse wave was set to propagate with a velocity of 2 m/s and in the
second simulation the pulse wave was set to propagate with a velocity of 4 m/s. The
results showed that the estimation scheme was able to calculate the PWV, with a
reasonable accuracy i.e. 2.5%. There was no difference in accuracy when comparing
the systolic foot to the dicrotic notch. This is because the simulation tool is currently
unable to simulate the pulse wave reflections. Thus the non-linear effect from wave
reflections on the time reference points is not included in the simulation. This is
obviously a major drawback regarding the simulation, which under perfect conditions
should mimic the true environment. Furthermore, the simulation showed that the
estimation scheme underestimates the diameter distention with 5.4%. This confirms
that the estimation scheme is able to calculate the tissue motion with a relatively
small error.
==PAGE:85==
6.3 In-vivo Study

6.3
In-vivo Study
The estimation scheme was applied on data from ten healthy volunteers. The data
was recorded from longitudinal scans of the right CCA and the tissue movement was
calculated using the estimation scheme. From the tissue movement it was seen that
a trend could occur. This trend was found to be caused by movement between pa-
tient and transducer. The movement was found to be fairly constant throughout the
acquisition. It was therefore possible to reduce its influence by subtracting the best
straight-linear fit to the wall movement. The resulting offset created from subtracting
the linear trend was removed. Finally, diameter DWFs were calculated by subtract-
ing the posterior wall movement from the anterior wall movement. The diameter
DWFs obtained from subject_001 showed a total diameter displacement of 0.37 mm
and 0.42 mm for the proximal and distal measuring site, respectively. This was less
than the 0.69±0.17 mm reported in [14]. A possible explanation could be that the
transducer has been moved slightly during the localization procedure and the acqui-
sition procedure. This could result in a cross-section that does not depict the blood
vessel at its widest point. Another explanation may be that the pressure exerted by
the transducer causes an unintentional compression of the blood vessel. This could
also explain the smaller detected distention. However, both the systolic foot and the
dicrotic notch were depicted in the diameter DWF and its corresponding acceleration
waveform, making PWV estimations possible.
Before calculating the PWVs for each subject, the quality of the available data was
evaluated. The evaluation showed that the imaged blood vessel in subject_009 was
in fact the IJV and not the CCA as intended. This was determined by looking at the
direction of blood flow, which was opposite to that of the CCA. Subject_007 showed
a relatively poor blood vessel wall contrast, which was believed to be a result of the
transducer being slightly off the desired imaging axis. This was further confirmed by
the diameter DWF, which showed a reduced diameter distention. In addition, it was
difficult to identify the systolic foot and the dicrotic notch in the diameter DWF. As a
result of this both subject_007 and subject_009 were removed and thus not included
for further evaluation. In the B-mode image of both subject_005 and subject_010
the CCA and IJV were visible. The CCA was located beneath and parallel to the
IJV. As a consequence of this, the selected line segment extends into both types of
blood vessels. This means that the estimation scheme captures a combination of the
movement seen in both types of blood vessels. Finally, it should be mentioned that
the blood vessel of subject_008 shows the largest angle to horizontal. The recorded
tissue movement is therefore a combination of not only the axial tissue motion but
also the lateral tissue motion, which could influence PWVs.
The subsequent results obtained using the dicrotic notch as a time reference point
showed that subject_001, -_002, -_003, -_004 and -_006 have PWVs that are fairly
stable around 4.5 m/s. The PWVs seen in subject_005 are higher and lie around
7.82 m/s. It is important to recall that subject_005 was 52 years old at the time
==PAGE:86==
70
6 Discussion
of the data acquisition and was therefore older than the other subjects. Aging is
associated with a decrease in elasticity of the blood vessel.
This can explain the
higher PWV seen in subject_005. Furthermore, both subject_008 and subject_010
have highly fluctuating PWVs and subject_010 contains even negative PWVs. The
negative PWVs occur because the dicrotic notch is not being depicted in the acceler-
ation waveform. The estimation scheme relies on peak detection and if the dicrotic
notch does not constitute a peak in both measuring sites the estimation scheme will
fail to calculate the true PWV. It is suspected that the close proximity of the IJV
influences the acceleration waveform, thus suppressing the dicrotic notch as a time
reference point. The reasoning for this is based on the fact that the pulse wave in
the CCA and in IJV is travelling in opposite direction. Furthermore, as described in
Section 1.2, the tissue composition in a vein is different from an artery. Hence, the
pulse waveform in the IJV will be different from the pulse waveform seen in CCA. It
is therefore possible that disturbances in the diameter DWFs and its derivatives in
subject_010 occur as a result of the close proximity of the IJV.
The PWVs obtained using the systolic foot as a time reference point showed a higher
variation both across and within individuals compared to the dicrotic notch. These
differences can be explained from the reflective waves. The reflective waves are seen
as inflection points in the acceleration waveform. In subject_004, the inflection point
subsequent the systolic foot is more clearly depicted compared to the possible inflec-
tion point subsequent the dicrotic notch. This complies with [12], which showed that
the systolic foot is not entirely free from wave reflections. These unwanted reflections
can influence the localization of the time reference points if they are included in the
filtered smoothing. This however, requires that they are located close to the time ref-
erence point. The arrival time of the inflection points depends on the distance to the
reflection site and the stiffness of the blood vessel. The measurement should therefore
be conducted as far away from any possible reflection site e.g. the bifurcation. In
this thesis the measurements are conducted 3 cm from to the bifurcation.
Finally, the result obtained from the systolic foot showed that both subject_008
and subject_010 experienced negative PWVs. This indicated that the estimation
scheme was unable to detect the correct time reference points and subject_008 and
subject_010 were therefore excluded for further evaluation.
6.4
Precision and Accuracy
CV values were calculated within each subject and for each of the time reference
points. The average CV values were found to be significantly lower when using the
dicrotic notch as a time reference point (SDdn of 0.62 m/s, CVdn of 11.94%) compared
to the systolic foot (SDsf of 1.53 m/s, CVsf of 26.89% ) when tested on a 5% level of
significance. Moreover, large variation across subjects were observed when using the
systolic foot as a time reference point. As the cut-off value is 10 m/s [2], the systolic
foot could not be used to distinguish between healthy and potential sick patients. The
==PAGE:87==

71
systolic foot was therefore not included in the accuracy evaluation. The Bramwell-
Hill equation (1.4) was used as a reference method for accuracy evaluation.
The
local CCA PP was approximated by subtracting 29% from the brachial PP [26]. The
resulting PWVs found using the dicrotic notch and the reference method experienced
a reasonable low bias of 0.53 m/s. However, the interdependence between the two
methods showed only a moderate positive relationship with a correlation coefficient of
r = 0.50, which is much less than the hypothesized r ≥0.80. A reason for this is that
the Bramwell-Hill equation rely on a correct determination of the difference between
the systolic and diastolic areas. This means that the less prominent diameter DWF
will affect the estimated PWVs in the Bramwell-Hill equation to be slightly higher
than expected.
This shows how important it is for the transducer to be located
correctly over the widest point of the blood vessel and without compressing the blood
vessel. Finally, it should be mentioned that the statistical strength is strongly limited
by the low sample size.
6.5
Recommendation
This thesis investigates the PWVs in the right CCA. As described in Section 1.2 the
right CCA is a secondary branch of the aortic arch, whereas the left CCA is a direct
branch of the aortic arch. This could effect the propagation of the pulse wave. It
is therefore recommended that future work conduct measurements on both the left
and right CCA to see if there are a difference between the PWVs in the two arteries.
Moreover, it is recommended to avoid the presence of the IJV in the measured data.
This is because the IJV alters the dynamics of the recorded CCA pulse wave, which
makes the determination of the time reference point difficult. Finally, the smaller
detected diameter DWFs are believed to be a result of the errors associated to the
positioning of the transducer over the blood vessel as described in Fig. 5.7.a. One
solution is to use cross-sectional scans of the blood vessel instead of longitudinal scans.
This would make it possible to track the entire perimeter of the blood wall vessel as
a function of time. This would also make it possible to investigate whether the cross
section of the blood vessel is cylindrical or elliptic, which is important to know when
calculating the systolic and diastolic areas in the the Bramwell-Hill equation.

proposed solution, which does not take cost into account, is illustrated in Fig. 6.1.

Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to develop an estimation scheme for local pulse wave
velocity estimations in the CCA. It was hypothesized that the estimation scheme
would be precise i.e lower than 10% of intra-individual variation and accurate with a
correlation coefficient of r ≥0.80 to a reference method.
The phantom study showed that it was possible to obtain repeatable diameter DWFs
across multiple pulse cycles. The change in the diameter of the blood vessel wall
was found to be 0.80 mm. However, due to potential limitations in the phantom
setup it was not possible to acquire repeatable PWVs as intended. Instead a sim-
ulation study was conducted. The simulation study confirmed that the estimation
scheme is capable of detecting the tissue movement with a relative percentage error
of 5.4%. Furthermore, the simulation showed that, when omitting the influence of
potential reflections, the estimation scheme is capable of estimating accurate pulse
wave velocities within 2.5% of the predefined velocity. The estimation scheme was
then tested on the right CCA of ten healthy volunteers. It was not possible to achieve
a precision lower than 10%. However, the intra-individual variation was found to be
significantly lower when using the dicrotic notch as a time reference point (SDdn of
0.62 m/s, CVdn of 11.94%) compared to the systolic foot (SDsf of 1.53 m/s, CVsf of
26.89%) when tested on a 5% level of significance. In addition, large variations across
subjects are observed when using the systolic foot. Therefore, only the dicrotic notch
is evaluated with respect to accuracy. The mean of the PWVs acquired using the
dicrotic notch shows a reasonable low bias i.e. 0.53 m/s to the PWVs acquired from
the reference method. Unfortunately the agreement between the dicrotic notch and
the Bramwell-Hill equation only shows a moderate positive correlation i.e. r = 0.50.
This is much lower than the hypothesized correlation coefficient of r ≥0.80.
To gain clinical acceptance refinements are needed in both the estimation scheme
and in the reference method. The largest source of errors in the estimation scheme
is the reflective waves, which can obscure the localization of the time reference point.
This is however not the case in the reference method, which instead relies on the
correct determination of the local PP and the geometry of the artery. Future work
should therefore (1) refine or develop new methods that minimize the influence of
the reflective wave, (2) develop a method that can determine the local PP in the
CCA by means of ultrasound, and (3) improve the acquisition procedure to ensure
the correct positioning of the transducer. The latter two are important when using

E.1

The purpose of this project plan is to outline the objective and justification of the

Three supervisors are involved in the MSc.project: Marie Sand Traberg, Matthias Bo

E.1.1
Hypothesis
This project includes the development of an imaging scheme for estimation of pulse
wave velocity (PWV) in the carotid artery.
Both axial and lateral tissue motion
will be investigated using synthetic aperture imaging data. The PWV is commonly
used to asses arterial stiffness, which is further associated with the progression of
atherosclerosis. It is hypothesized that the lateral blood-tissue motion can be used
to detect the PWV with higher accuracy and precision and thus be more sufficient in
detecting the progression of arterial stiffness.

E.2.1
Data familiarization
The available data are acquired from 10 healthy volunteers both in-vivo and from
carotid phantom models. A beamforming script in MATLAB is used for data beam-
forming. The data contains 350 frame-index’s each of which contain 384 interleaved
flow-sequences and bmode-sequences. By combining the flow sequences, it is possible
to obtain 64 high resolution images (HRI) per frame index and thus 22400 HRI frames
in total. The result is a frame-rate equal to 2250 frames a second, which can be used
for pulse wave velocity estimations.
E.2.2
Develop and implement imaging scheme
This is considered to be the core task of the thesis. The main objective is to develop
and implement the estimation algorithm for the pulse wave velocity in the carotid
artery and apply it on ten healthy volunteers. MATLAB will be used to solve this
task. Typical pulse wave velocity estimations is based on the propagation of a pulse
wave between two measuring sites, using the axial tissue motion. This project will
investigate the usage of the lateral blood-tissue motion for pulse wave velocity esti-
mations. The estimation algoritm will be applied on both in-vivo data and on carotid
phantom models.

The result will be analysed and compared to other similar studies.

E.2.5
Verification and validation
Verification is done to ensure the quality of the estimation algorithm and validation
is done to ensure that the estimation algorithm fulfills its intended purpose, which is
to asses arterial stiffness based on the pulse wave velocity estimations. The precision
of the algorithm will be evaluated on the available data and the accuracy will be
evaluated through field-II simulation.
E.2.6

E.6
Evaluation of The Project Process
Both physiological and technical knowledge as well as a thorough understanding of
their mutual interactions have been used to acquire the result presented in this thesis.
In the initial project plan it was written that the lateral tissue movement could be
used for PWV estimations. This was however not the case and it is therefore not
used in the final project. The following presents the tasks, which were successfully
accomplished, along with task that could have been improved.
Accomplished tasks:
• The halfway report allowed me to be confronted with challenges well in advance
of submitting the thesis.
==PAGE:125==
E.6 Evaluation of The Project Process
109
• The unwanted contribution from stationary tissue and air bubbles were reduced
by means of masks, which were added to the rf-data.
• It was possible to track the movement of the blood vessel wall in all three
studies.
• It was possible to calculate PWVs in the simulation study and in the in-vivo
study as well as in the reference method (Bramwell-Hill).
What could have been done differently and what could be improved:
• I could have structured the project better. This could have been done by start-
ing with the simulation study, then the phantom study and finally the in-vivo
study.
• To gain clinical acceptance, both the precision and accuracy should be improved.
One solution is to ensure the correct positioning of the transducer along the
blood vessel.
• It was not possible to acquire consistent PWVs in the phantom study. Improve-
ments in the experimental setup seem necessary.
