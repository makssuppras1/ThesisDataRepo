1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 IAQ and THERMAL COMFORT 
Nowadays, in industrialized societies people spend most of their time indoors (Klepeis et al. 2001). People 
are seated during substantial part of the time spend indoors (offices, transportation, etc.) Therefore, it is 
important to establish a healthy, comfortable and productive living environment. The productivity level 
of the workers decreases, at insufficient indoor environment (Wargocki, 2014). This results in higher 
expenses for the employers. For example, in United States the economic loss due to impairment of 
occupants’ productivity and healthcare was estimated up to $160 billion (Fisk, 2000). Two major factors 
should be considered considerations are thermal comfort and indoor air quality (IAQ). 
An occupant feels thermally comfortable when he or she evaluates the environment as thermally 
satisfactory (ANSI/ASHRAE Standard 55). Thermal comfort is defined o based on personal factors 
(metabolic rate and clothing insulation) and environmental factors (humidity, air velocity, air temperature 
and mean radiant temperature). Often used model for the assessment of thermal comfort is PMV/PPD 
method (Predicted Mean Vote/Predicted Percentage of Dissatisfied) described in ISO 7730: 2006 (EN ISO 
7730, 2006). The method, which is based on empirical studies defines a heat balance equation including 
all of the above mentioned factors to estimate if a person is in thermal balance with the surrounding.  
The major pollution sources affecting the IAQ are the occupants and the building itself. Standards, such 
as The European Standard EN 15251:2007 specifies different categories of criteria for thermal comfort 
and indoor air quality including required ventilation rates for diluting emissions from people and building 
components. The ventilation rates depend mostly on the age, size and type of the building, the building’s 
equipment, HVAC systems and number of occupants. According to the EN 15251:2007 the recently built 
structures have lower demand for the ventilation rates, as they are more carefully designed. The building 
materials became in recent years more controlled. The smoking in public places is often prohibited. 
Therefore, the remaining sources of pollution are becoming the occupants. In order to establish a healthy 
indoor environment, it is important to investigate and control their emissions.  
Poor IAQ may result in sick building syndrome (SBS) symptoms. The SBS are symptoms directly related to 
the time spent by occupants in buildings (Joshi, 2008). SBS symptoms are for example, headache, 
tiredness, lack of concentration, sleepiness, uneasiness etc. As a result occupant’s productivity and 
performance decrease. By increasing the ventilation rates both cognitive performance, health and 
comfort can be improved (Wargocki et al. 2016 and Wargocki et al. 2014).

1.2 Human body 
Due to metabolic process in the human body, the body releases heat, moisture and pollution into the 
space. In this way it affects the air movement and the indoor air quality. Thus, building occupants are 
exposed to the pollution emitted from their body and to the pollution coming from the surrounding, 
including other occupants. 
1.2.1 Bioeffluents and particles released from human body 
The human body is an indoor pollutant. It emits both, gasses (bioeffluents) and particles. The airborne 
particles from the human body (bio-aerosols) are mostly emitted from the respiratory system and by 
shedding of skin and hair. The bio-aerosols might consist of viruses, bacteria, fungi, microbial toxins, 
viruses, etc. (Douwes et al., 2003). Through breathing, talking, sneezing and coughing the human body 
exhales infectious particles (viruses, bacteria etc.). Such diseases might be tuberculosis, influenza, 
chickenpox etc. (Morawska, 2005). The bio-aerosols are then dispersed along the space according to the 
airflows in the room. If they are not inhaled, they either stay on the room surfaces or they are removed 
through the ventilated exhaust.  
The human body sheds between 6 and 13 g of skin flakes per day. For particles greater or equal to 0.5 μm, 
this is from 100 000 per minute when sitting and up to 10 000 000, when moving (Ramstorp, 2000). 
Additionally, the production of skin flakes is increased with the friction between the clothing and the skin 
(Charnley and Eftekhar, 1969). The skin flakes are then released through the openings in the clothing 
textile or through the ends of loose clothing. This skin flakes contain a wide variety of microbiota. These 
are contagious pathogens (average size dp 13 μm), such as Acinetobacter, Staphylococcus aureus or 
Clostridium etc. (Beggs et al., 2008; Spendlove et al., 1983). If the flakes are lifted from the surfaces or 
inhaled directly, they might infect the occupant. It has been reported that the particles greater than dp 
50 μm can be transferred upwards into the breathing zone by the free convection flow around the body 
(Clark and Cox, 1973). 
The bioeffluents are volatile and non-volatile organic compounds (VOCs and NVOCs), that may be 
detected by our olfactory system as odours. The most commonly known bioeffluent is CO2, emitted 
indoors as a consequence of respiration (Committee of European Communities, 1992). The body odours 
depend on gender, health status, age, activity and culture (Herberger et al., 2010 and Yamazaki et al., 
2010). As well, some of the studies confirmed, that their production may depend on the season (Zang et 
al., 2005). The most intensive body sites where the bioeffluents are generated are the oral cavity, armpits, 
groins, head and feet (Dormont et al., 2013, Yamazaki et al., 2010 and Pandey et al., 2011).  
The smell coming from the armpits is caused by human perspiration system or sweating. Sweating is a 
consequence of the body’s thermal regulation, caused by the changes from the environment (Robinson, 
1945). The human skin has three different gland types, namely eccrine, sebaceous and apocrine. Eccrine 
glands emit odourless compounds and are distributed all over our body. Apocrine gland release lipids, 
steroids and proteins and last, sebaceous glands release lipids and sebum (Dormont et al., 2013).  
In the area of armpits, all three types of skin glands are denser. Some of the sweat is released into the air 
instantly, and some of it stays on the skin. The sweat compounds collected from the skin are derivative 
esters, aldehydes, alkanes, carboxylic acids, short chain alcohols, and ketones etc. (Dormont et al. 2013 
and Stefaniak and Harvey, 2006).

The sweat from the skin is accumulated by the skin microbial and further metabolized into volatile and 
non-volatile odours compounds. The number of skin microbiota on the skin area of the armpits was 
determined to be 2.41 x 106 /cm2, which was the highest number of examined areas in the study by 
Yamazaki et al. (2010). Peterson et al. (2016) examined the groups of compounds released from the 
armpits and corresponding bacteria, as shown in Figure 1.1.

The odour from human groins area is partially coming from the skin and partially from human wastes 
(faces and urine). The compounds coming from the urine have stronger smell, as there is a higher amount 
of microbial microorganisms. The compounds found in human wastes are acids, ammonia, sulphur and 
nitrogen compounds. There were other volatile metabolites found, such as butanone, 4-heptanone, 2-
pentanone, cyclohexanone, pyrazines, lower aliphatic alcohols and octanols, ethanol, n-propanol, etc. 
(Pandey and Kim, 2011). 
The air contains ozone (O3). The ozone is highly reactive and react with human body products. The 
products of these reactions present even bigger harm for the exposure compared to ozone itself. There 
are 2 types of reactions with ozone. The first type is on the gas phase level. The reaction compounds with 
ozone are often a result of human activity (alkenes, NO2) and the products are mainly radicals. The other 
type of reactions occurs on the surface level. These are reactions with human skin oil. There is a high 
variety of the reactions because the products are primary and then react further forming secondary 
products (Wisthaler and Weschler, 2010; Tamas et al., 2006). 
1.2.2 The Free Convection Flow 
As a consequence of thermal regulation, the human body emits heat and moisture into the space. The 
production rate depends on the activity level, clothing insulation and thermal environment. Part of the 
heat loss released is sensible and part latent, which is emitted in the space by convection in the air and 
radiation to the solid objects. The rate of the latent and sensible heat loss changes depending on relative 
humidity and room operative temperature (EN CR 1752). Generally speaking, the body dissipates the heat 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
from the skin surface by conduction, convection, radiation and evaporation through the skin. Additionally, 
the respiration system (through the mucous layer) emits the heat by means of convection and moisture 
evaporation (Bogdan et al., 2016). The proportion of total amount of heat exchanged by radiation, 
convection and conduction and evaporation depends on the environmental parameters such as 
temperature, relative humidity, velocity, etc. (Melikov, 2015). Human body exchanges heat and mass with 
the surrounding environment. The environment close to the human body is called a human body micro-
environment (Lewis et al., 1969). Melikov (2015) separated the human body micro-environment into four 
parts. First part is the layer of air separating the skin and inner surface of the clothing, second part is the 
clothing layer. Third part is the air layer from the skin to the outer layer of the clothing which is in contact 
with the room air. The fourth part is the transient respiration flow.  
The individual’s body skin temperature, depends on the body part, personal factors (metabolic rate and 
clothing) and on the surrounding (air and mean radiant temperature, air velocities, humidity etc.). An 
average body skin temperature in comfort state is around 34°C (Melikov, 2004a). According to EN 
15251:2007 in category II, the suggested operative temperatures in the office spaces for sedentary activity 
(1.2 met) are 20°C in winter (1 clo) and 26°C in summer (0.5 clo) (EN 15251, 2007). In such environment 
the body skin temperature (bare or covered with clothing) is higher compared to the surrounding 
temperature. As a result of the temperature difference the air in contact with the skin (and clothing) 
becomes warmer from the surrounding. The layer of air formed in the vicinity of human body is called 
free convection flow (Melikov, 2015). 
The free convective flow (FCF) around the human body forms a convective boundary layer (CBL) around 
the human body. When the FCF rises above the head it becomes a thermal plume (Melikov, 2015). The 
study by Ličina et al. (2015b) showed that the results of the CBL and breathing flows obtained from 
measurements with the thermal manikin resembling human body were in accordance to the ones 
obtained from a real human subject. 
The characteristics of CBL depend on many parameters. The flow is in the lower parts of the body laminar, 
slower and thinner compared to the upper parts, where it becomes faster, turbulent and thicker (Melikov, 
2015). 
The peak velocities of the CBL could reach values up to 0.3 m/s (Ličina et al., 2014a). With distancing from 
the surface (skin or clothing) the velocities increase from zero to the peak and decrease when they reach 
the surrounding air (Lewis, 1969), as shown in Figure 1.2.  
==PAGE:12==

Figure 1.2: The development of the velocity profiles in CBL in front of the leg (Lewis, 1969) 
The shape of the CBL is influenced by the location of the body, body’s posture and clothing. The 
temperature difference causes changes in thickness and velocities of the CBL. CBL at higher temperature 
differences becomes thinner and faster (Ličina et al., 2014a, Homma et al., 1988; Voelker et al., 2014). 
The standing manikin has a clear transition of the CBL in thermal plume at the upper head height (Figure 
1.3). Meanwhile, the transition of the CBL of sitting occupant starts at the chest height of 0.95 m. If the 
body leans backwards the peak velocities in CBL increase for 40% (Ličina et al., 2014a). 
 
Figure 1.3: The pseudo colour visualization (PCV) of the CBL (Licina et al., 2014a) 
The CBL of the sitting person has the lowest thickness at the feet (0.5 cm) and the highest at the head 
height at 1.1 m (up to 15 cm) (Melikov and Zhou, 1996; Melikov, 2005 and Ličina et al., 2014a).  
The temperature in the CBL decreases from the temperature of the body surface to the room air 
temperature (Ličina et al., 2014a).In case of the nude person, the outer layer presents the skin. When a 
person is dressed, the outer layer is the clothing. The clothing acts as a thermal insulation for our body 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
and compared to the skin, has lower outer surface temperature. The cooler outer surface of the clothing 
reduces the strength and the thickness of the CBL (Melikov, 2015).  
A study by Ličina et al. (2014a) showed that loose clothing affects the shape and turbulence intensity of 
the CBL. Between the clothing and the skin an additional layer of upward flow is established. The clothing 
structure and distribution along the body is not uniform and thus additionally changes the shape of the 
CBL. The clothing surface temperature can be decreased below the surrounding’s air temperature (with 
radiant cooling) and therefore, it may create a downward flow (Melikov, 2015). 
Ličina et al. (2014a) investigated the effect of the position of the table and the chair insulation. The results 
showed that the presence of the table and distance from the abdomen is important. The velocities at the 
mouth of the seated manikin were the highest (0.17 m/s) when the table was absent, and gradually 
reduced, when table was pushed closer and touching the abdomen. Additionally, it was shown that the 
chair insulation in contact with the body stops the formation of the CBL. 
Several studies (Zukowska et al., 2010 and Ličina et al., 2014) showed that, the seated manikin due to its 
posture creates an asymmetrical thermal plume formed already at the chest caused by the heat from the 
tights, (Figure 1.4). The figure shows, the importance of the surrounding temperature on the shape and 
wideness on the CBL. 
 
Figure 1.4: PCV of the CBL at 0.5 s interval: The top figures show the CBL at 20°C and the bottom at 26°C room air temperature 
(Ličina et al., 2014). 
The thermal plume affects the flow in the room and thus may pollute the air. Compared to the velocities 
and thickness of the CBL, the thermal plume is wider and faster. The vertical air velocity in the thermal 
plume above a seated person can be up to 0.25 m/s (Zukowska et al., 2011).

The CBL transports pollution generated in the vicinity of the human body or by the human body to the 
breathing zone (Licina et al. 2015a, Rim and Novoselac, 2009). In this way the CBL is an important for 
human exposure to own bio-effluents and pollution generated in the surroundings. This will be discussed 
further in the next sections of this chapter. 
The convection heat transfer coefficient over the body has a value in the range of 3.5-3.7 W/(m2.K) and 
differs locally due to body part, shape and posture. The radiant heat transfer coefficient is in the range of 
4.5 and 5.0 W/(m2.K) (Melikov, 2015; Clark, 1975; Mayer and Schwab, 1988 and Mayer 1992). 
1.2.3 Breathing 
The breathing is in nature a transient flow. The breathing cycle normally consists of inhalation, exhalation 
and a brake. In reality, the duration of each phase depends on the type of activity, psychological state of 
emotions and the age of the subject (Kociuba-Usciłko et al., 2003). When the activity level is increased or 
level of emotion reinforced, the cycle becomes more frequent and the phase durations shorter. Human 
subject studies showed that the duration of inhalation and exhalation can be from 0.7 to 1.7 s and the 
brake from 0.5 s to 1.5 s with diverse breathing rate of 16 to 24 breaths/min (Bogdan, 2009). Olmedo et 
al. (2010) at their study of three ventilation cases (no ventilation, mixing ventilation and displacement 
ventilation) used the exaltation rate of 11 l/min (0.75 l/exhalation). Kociuba-Usciłko et al. (2003) indicated 
that the inhaled air per minute differ for men and women in range of 8-11 L/min and 6-8 L/min, 
respectively. Melikov and Kaczmaryk (2007) measured exposure with a thermal manikin having 
pulmonary ventilation of 6 l/ min and frequency of 10 times per minute, with the breathing cycle of 2.5 s 
inhalation, 2.5 s exhalation and 1.0 s pause (according to Hoppe, 1981).  
The inhalation flows don’t differ much in shape of the air movement whether they come from the mouth 
or the nose because they are located very close. The air is sucked inwards with high velocities. However, 
the suction affects the air only 2-3 cm away from the openings (Melikov, 2015). 
The characteristics of the exhaled air are in average 34°C and almost 100% humidity (Olemdo et al., 2010). 
The air can be exhaled either from the mouth or the nose. The characteristics of the jet differ in case of 
breathing, talking, sneezing or coughing. The exhalation jet is a free non-isothermal symmetric jet 
(Bogdan, 2016). A study by Nielsen at al. (2009a) showed the importance of simulating the exhalation 
flows with jets of higher (realistic) temperatures. When the jet from the mouth was isothermal (the same 
air temperature as the surrounding air) the direction of its flow was horizontal and when it was non-
isothermal (warmer from the surrounding), the jet’s ending was curved slightly upwards before expelled 
into the surrounding air. The isothermal exhalation jet from the nose was shaped straight and directed 
downwards, however the non-isothermal flow had an ending curved slightly upwards. Both jets have high 
momentum and penetrate the FCF. This is in accordance to the study by Özcan et al. (2005), where the 
velocities of continuous exhalation jet from the nose were studied. The results showed that the end of 
the non-isothermal exhalation jet was curved upwards. 
The direction of exhalation jets is visible from the Figure 1.5, where Melikov and Kaczmaryk (2007) 
performed a smoke visualisation of the manikin.

1.3 The total volume ventilation (TVV) 
The total volume ventilation (TVV) principle is commonly used for distributing the air in the indoor spaces 
today. It controls the temperature and air quality level in the whole room. The most commonly used 
principles are mixing air distribution (referred as mixing ventilation (MV)) and displacement air 
distribution (referred as displacement ventilation (DV)). 
Mixing ventilation supplies the air through air terminal devices (ATD) creating mixing with momentum 
driven flows (Figure 1.7 left). The supplied air is clean and mixed along the whole room and reaches the 
occupied zone with lower velocities. The aim of the mixing ventilation is to supply the clean air and mixes 
it with polluted air evenly in the occupied zone. The air is then removed through the exhaust (Muller et 
al. 2013). 
Displacement ventilation (DV) works in the way that the polluted air is replaced with the clean air (Figure 
1.7 right). The air is supplied from ATDs located at the wall near to the floor. The clean supplied air has 
low velocities and low temperatures. When the clean cool air comes in contact with the warm objects 
such as occupants, equipment etc. the vertical temperature difference (stratification) creates an upward 
flow movement. The warm and polluted air is then lifted towards the ceiling and from there exhausted by 
the outlet in the ceiling. The aim of DV is to supply the clean completely unpolluted air to the occupied 
zone Muller et al. (2013). 
 
Figure 1.8: The mixing (left) and displacement ventilation principle (right) (Price Engineers, 2016) 
Many studies have showen that the total volume ventilation (TVV) is not efficient for the pollution 
removal. Melikov (2016) suggested that TVV should be replaced with decentralized ventilation principles. 
The TVV is inefficient air distribution. The clean air is supplied all over the room and far from the occupants 
(Nielsen, 2009b). The flow rates excess 100 times the flow rate needed for breathing (0.2-0.3 l/s) 
(Hyldgaard, 1994). The air distribution with TVV is difficult to control. The TVV aims to establish the 
uniform temperature and velocity conditions in the occupied zone. However, the personal preference 
differ among the occupants and thus, an average occupant does not exist. The TVV are slow in response. 
The TVV have high energy consumption due to the need to conditioned usually high amount of air that it 
is supplied to the entire space for cooling and heating. Additionally, the polluted air is recirculated and 
mixed with outdoor air. The TVV systems are permanently built in the space and therefore do not allow 
the flexibility in the use of space. 
The development over the last decade showed an importance of the localized ventilation systems. The 
local ventilation system affects the humans’ micro-environment directly. The main advantages are the 
users control over the characteristic of the personalized flow, control of the airflow interaction around 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
the human body, efficient cleaning of room air, improved cleanness of inhaled air and preferred 
environment, quick response, energy savings potential and increased flexibility in space use (Melikov, 
2016).Another major advantage of the local ventilation principles is, that they are more convenient and 
cheaper for buildings’ renovation. For example, public buildings with heritage protection, where invasive 
construction interventions are forbidden could finally operate with the mechanical ventilation. 
1.4 Advanced ventilation principles 
Advanced ventilation principles or advanced air distribution (AAD) can be used alone or as a supplement 
to the TVV as a background ventilation. AAD can be based on supply of clean air closed to occupant’s 
breathing zone or local exhaust of pollution at the source or both. Melikov (2016) reviewed the 
importance of AAD and showed that their performance is better compared to TVV. The main advantage 
of the local air supply methods is that they supply the air locally and thus directly affect the human micro-
environment. The local air exhaust methods remove the pollution locally at the source (occupants) and 
thus prevent a further pollution spread into the breathing zone of the occupant or in the room. 
The formation of FCF is easily influenced by the room temperature, air velocities of the surrounding, 
clothing, body’s posture, movement and other above mentioned parameters (Melikov, 2015). Therefore, 
they need to be taken under a careful consideration when studying either the exposure or flow 
interaction. The FCF interacts with other flows, such as exhalation or momentum driven ventilation flows. 
The flow interaction has a substantial impact on the exposure to the pollutants generated from the human 
body, as CBL carries pollution upwards into the breathing zone (Licina et al., 2015a). 
As previously discussed, the FCF is an upward flow. Thus, the downward flow acts as an opposing flow, 
horizontal flow as transverse flow and upward flow as assisting flow. In this way, by the movement of air 
flows the FCF can be influenced. The flow interaction was investigated by several studies discussed in the 
following.

1.4.2 Local air exhaust methods 
Removal of pollution at the location of the source is another method for improving indoor air quality and 
reduction of the needed ventilation rate. The bed with ventilated mattress shown in Figure 1.20 applies 
this principle. It locally removes the body-bioeffluents before they are diluted into the air, mixed and 
brought in the breathing zone, where they are inhaled. Thus, less air needs to be supplied by the total 
volume ventilation (TVV) and needed air flow is reduced from 4-6 ACH to 1.6 ACH (air changes per hour). 
The method is called the Ventilated Mattress (VM) and it exhausts the body-bioeffluents using an exhaust 
opening located at groins and feet (Figure 1.20).

1.5 Justification of this study 
The key to understanding the perceived air quality (PAQ) are the investigations of bioeffluents. In the past 
century the CO2 concentrations were used as the indicator of air quality. Additionally, many studies 
assessed the ventilation efficiency based on the CO2 levels. Zhang et al., (2016a) studied if the exposure 
of CO2 at 5000 ppm would reduce the cognitive performance, arise the acute health symptoms, establish 
sensory discomfort and evoke the changes in physiological responses. The subjects were exposed for 2.5 
hours to the reference case with CO2 of 500 ppm and 2.5 hour to CO2 at 5000 ppm. The results showed 
that, the exposure to increased amounts of CO2 didn’t cause any increase in health symptoms, there was 
no effect reported on either the PAQ or the physiological state. Additionally, there was no change in 
simple and moderate cognitive performance tasks.  
A related study by Zhang et al., (2016b) investigated the effect of occupants’ exposure to CO2 and 
bioeffluents. Three exposure types had an air flow rate high enough to keep the CO2 at 500 ppm and to 
remove the pollution from the bioeffluents. In one of those cases pure CO2 was added to the level of 1000 
ppm or 3000 ppm. In remaining two cases, the outdoor ventilation rate was decreased, so the occupants 
produced the natural (bioeffluent) CO2 to the level of 1000 ppm or 3000 ppm. The same 25 subjects stayed 
in the room 225 minutes. The measured entities were physiological responses, cognitive performance and 
subjective ratings. When chemically pure CO2 was added, no statistically significant changes were 
observed. In contrast, when the bioeffluent CO2 was produced, the sleepiness, headache and difficulty in 
thinking clearly and other symptoms occurred. The study suggests that in fact, the moderate amounts of 
bioeffluents and not pure CO2 have harmful effect on the occupants’ exposure. Does not suggest that 
body released bio-effluent are the reason. Can be exhaled CO2? 
The above mentioned studies reveal the importance of removing human body bio-effluents as one of the 
major indoor air pollutant. Therefore, it is highly important to study how the pollution from the human 
body is generated and transported to be inhaled and spread in the room. Additionally, it is important to 
critically evaluate the existing ventilation systems in the buildings and their performance with regard to 
the transportation and exposure to human body generated bio-effluents.  
The transport and exposure of the bio-effluents depends on the airflow interaction close to human body, 
i.e. the airflow interaction in the human body micro-environment. With this respect the interaction 
between the free convection flow generated around human body, the flow of breathing, especially the 
exhalation flow and the flow generated by ventilation systems is important. Another important factor for 
the exposure to the human body generated bio-effluents is the source control.  
In order to be able to carefully investigate the interaction of the flows, free convection flow has to be 
studied further. The studies confirmed that the flow is highly depended on the external parameters. 
The airflow interaction in the vicinity of human body has been studied. Bolashikov (2010) used a breathing 
thermal manikin to study the importance of interaction between free convection flow and personalised 
flow on exposure the polluted room air. The personalised flow was supplied either from front against the 
face or upward from the edge of a desk toward the face of closely seated manikin (10 cm from apart the 
front of the desk). The strength of the free convection flow at the breathing zone was reduced by use of 
a passive control method (braking the CBL by a movable plate gently pressing against the lower chest) or 
by an active method (sucking the CBL at the level of the desk edge). As a result the personalised flow was 
able to penetrate the free convection flow at the face flow and to provide more clean air to inhalation. 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
The control of the interaction of the free convection flow with a personalised flow supplied from two 
diffusers installed on the headrest of a seat was also studied. The diffusers installed on the left and right 
side of the head of a seated breathing thermal manikin supplied the clean air tangentially to the face 
beneath the CBL and directly to the nose/mouth. The portion of clean air in the inhaled air increased up 
to 90% by supplying part of the personalised air downward against the free convection flow. In this study 
exposure to body generated bio-effluents was not studied neither the importance of pollution source 
control because the pollution was well mixed with the surrounding air. Additionally, the interaction of the 
FCF, exhalation flows (both nose and mouth) and the PV flow when the pollution was generated at the 
armpits and at the groins separately. 
Cermak (2004) used two breathing manikins, one polluting and one exposed, to study the importance if 
the interaction between the free convection flow, personalised flow and flow of exhalation around the 
body of the polluting manikin for transport of pollution and its portion in the air inhaled by the exposed 
manikin. Two different tracer gasses were used to simulate body generated pollution: N2O was used to 
simulate bio-effluents generated at the armpits and the groins and SF6 was mixed with the exhaled air to 
simulate pathogens. Experiments were performed at different background air distribution modes – 
mixing, displacement and underfloor air distribution. The study revealed the importance of the airflow 
interaction for cross-infection between occupants. However it did not show the importance of the airflow 
interaction for the exposure of a person to his/her own bio-effluents neither the importance of the 
location of generating bio-effluents (the same tracer gas was released at the groins and the armpits) and 
the source control).  
Melikov and Kaczmaryk (2007) concluded that the temperature, humidity and pollution level in inhaled 
air can be measured accurately without simulating the breathing when it is measured at the upper lip at 
distance smaller than 0.01 m from the face. However, using the breathing manikin is important for the 
studying the transport of the pollution between the occupants or cross-infection. As well method for 
prediction for the prediction of the air acceptability was proposed. Nevertheless, the manikins’ 
characteristics regarding face and body shape should be standardised in order to compare the results 
easier. 
Regarding the breathing parameters obtained close to the breathing zone Ličina et al. (2014b) reported 
on the opposite. The temperature boundary layer around the human body in a quiescent environment 
was studied. A breathing thermal manikin was used to examine air temperature, mean in time and 
standard deviation of the temperature fluctuations in relation to the ambient temperature, body posture 
and the human respiratory flow. The results of the manikin tests were crosschecked with a more realistic 
scenario with a real human subject in order to evaluate the degree of compatibility between experiments 
with thermal manikin and human. It was observed, that at lower temperatures (20°C) the fluctuations of 
air temperatures increase in the vicinity of the body. A large standard deviation of air temperature 
fluctuations was recorded in the chest region, which increased even more in the case of applied 
exhalation. The air temperature in the breathing zone was found to increase in a backwards leaning 
manikin, while the opposite was examined for a forward leaning one. Exhalation through the mouth did 
not disturb the chest region, while creating a steady air temperature drop with increased distance from 
the mouth. Due to physics of the jets flow, the exhalation through the nose had no effect on the air 
temperature in the chest region. Tracer gas analysis showed that exhaled air from the nose has the ability 
to penetrate the region below the chest. The comparison between the thermal manikin and the human 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
subject revealed minor inconsistencies, which led to the conclusion that manikins can be used for accurate 
measurements of occupant’s thermal microenvironment.  
The goal of a study (Ličina et al., 2014a) was to analyze and define the human convective boundary layer 
in a quiescent environment depending on the parameters such as clothing, furniture and body posture. 
Initially, the variables of the analysis were two different ambient temperatures and body postures in order 
to define the changes of the velocity field around the thermal manikin. Further analysis involved the 
changing of clothing insulation, design of the surroundings and inclination of the manikin with the goal of 
examining the effect of these changes on the airflow characteristics of the breathing zone. The results of 
the increase of temperature from 20 to 26°C report that while the CBL flow is widened in front of the 
sitting manikin, there is no difference on its shape in front of a standing one. Furthermore, such an 
increase reduces the CBL mean peak velocity in front of a sitting manikin. The same peak velocity 
reduction in the breathing zone can be reported in the case of implementing clothing on the manikin. 
Thin-tight clothing resulted in the reduction of peak velocity by 17 while thick-loose clothing ensued in a 
40% reduction. It was observed, that the clothing and chair design have a minor influence on the velocity 
profile beyond the immediate vicinity of the body. In the case of body posture analysis, it has been 
observed that a forward leaning manikin induces a 45% lower peak velocity than a backwards leaning one. 
In addition to pseudo color visualization, PIV measurements provided a good synergy between 
quantitative and qualitative airflow characteristics and were deemed adequate for CBL investigations. 
The studied parameters obtained in the study are important because the human body the ability to 
transfer the pollution. The ability of the human CBL to transport pollution in a quiescent indoor 
environment is investigated in this study (Licina et al., 2015a). The importance of the source of the 
pollution in the vicinity of the human body is examined in relation to the distribution of pollution in the 
breathing zone and the thickness of the pollution boundary layer. Additionally, the study examines the 
influence of the air temperature, table positioning and the body inclination. The distribution of pollution 
in the breathing zone and the thickness of the pollution boundary layer are found to be considerably 
influenced by the source location of pollution. When located around the chest, the pollution source causes 
the highest breathing zone concentrations, as opposed to the source being at the lower back or behind 
the chair, which result in a negligible exposure. Personal exposure was found to increase with an 
intensified transport of pollution resulting from a reduced ambient temperature or a backwards inclined 
body. Furthermore, the position of the table has the ability to reduce the pollution transport to the 
breathing zone when placed at zero distance from the human body. Alternatively, if positioned 10cm away 
from the body, an increase in the transport of clean air is achieved from beneath. 
Another study investigated the interaction between the human CBL and uniform airflow with a variable 
velocity and direction (Licina et al., 2015b). A human body is represented by a thermal manikin with a 
complex shape and surface temperature distribution. The flow around the manikin’s body is defined using 
PIV and PCV. The magnitude and direction of the surrounding airflows is found to considerably influence 
the airflow distribution around the human body. The results report that a downward flow of 0.3m/s is 
required to create a collision zone with the CBL at the nose level which reduces the peak velocity. The 
same test with a flow velocity of 0.175m/s had no effect on the convective flow in the breathing zone. On 
the other hand, it has been proven that a transverse horizontal flow of even 0.175m/s disturbs the CBL at 
the breathing zone. Furthermore, an upwards flow effecting a sitting manikin was found to induce a 
reduction of the peak velocity in the breathing zone and change the flow pattern around the body. 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
Studies show that ozone in the indoor air reacts with skin oil (Wisthaler and Weschler, 2010 and Tamas et 
al., 2006). The secondary product of the reaction is the squalene which when inhaled increases the risk of 
cancer. The CBL transports and increase the exposure to squalene. Ličina et al. (2014a, 2014b, 2015a) and 
Rim and Novoselac (2009) studied the importance of the CBL for transport and exposure to pollution 
generated near or at different sites of the body. However they did not study the importance of the 
interaction of the free convection flow with the flow of breathing and locally applied ventilation flow (e.g. 
personalised ventilation) aiming at providing clean air for breathing.  
Limited research on the impact of source control on exposure to bio-effluents generated by seated human 
body has been reported. Bivolarova et al. (2016) reported on substantial reduction of exposure to bio-
effluents released at the armpits and the groins of seated human body by use of ventilated cushion. The 
working principle of the method was already discussed (Figure 1.18). The removal of pollution generated 
at the armpits and the groins increased and thus the exposure decreased when the flow rate of the air 
exhausted through the seat cushion increased. However, the study was limited to study the only the 
impact of the free convection flow. The impact of the interaction of the free convection flow with the flow 
of breathing as well as local ventilation flow, such as personalised ventilation has not been studied. The 
study didn’t simulate breathing and the room temperature of 23C wasn’t investigated as well the studies 
presenting the concentrations weren’t investigated with the instruments with high frequency response 
(fast gas meters), which are able a careful selection of the breathing periods. 
The review of the research on the importance of the airflow interaction at the vicinity of human body on 
the exposure to pollution, especially human body generated bio-effluents reveal that the research is 
insufficient and limited. The combined effect of interaction between the free convection flow, the flow of 
breathing and locally applied ventilation flow combined with pollution source control has not been 
studied. The missing knowledge is important for understanding the mechanisms of exposure as well as 
developing effective exposure reduction strategies. 
All studies on exposure reported so far in the literature have been based on use and measurement of 
tracer gas as replacement of body generated pollution. All studies have employed gas analysers with long 
response time, 1 min or more. However the use of such instruments for studying the exposure is 
inappropriate because the airflow interaction at the vicinity, especially the breathing zone, of human body 
is dynamic and highly transient. In this case gas concentration measurements with much faster 
instruments must be performed in order to assess the exposure correctly based not only on mean 
concentration but also on its standard deviation and maximum levels. Another drawback of the reported 
studies is the incorrect analyses of the measured data, restricted mainly to mean concentration measured 
for long time periods including several breathing cycles. However as already discussed previously in this 
chapter each breathing cycle typically comprises inhalation, exhalation and brake. The tracer gas 
concentration measured during the whole breathing cycle is different than the concentration measured 
only during inhalation and thus the exposure could be different. 
The present study was designed and performed to provide some of the missing knowledge on the airflow 
interaction at the vicinity of human body and its impact on exposure to body generated bio-effluents. 
More advanced measuring instruments and data analysing methods that those used and reported so far 
were used.

3. METHODOLOGY 
3.1 Experimental facilities 
3.1.1 Test chamber 
The experiment took place in controlled a climate chamber. The dimensions of the chamber were 4.7 m 
x 6 m x 2.5 m (W x L x H). The chamber was air conditioned by an upward piston flow supplied from the 
floor through a porous fabric. On the top of the fabric there was a metal grid used to allow walking inside 
the chamber. From inside, the chamber had double walls which consisted of thin textile layers forming a 
curtain. The distance between the curtain and the solid mother wall was 1.6 cm. This construction allowed 
part of the supplied room air to flow behind the curtain and to ensure a mean radiant temperature equal 
to the room air temperature. In this way the radiant temperature asymmetry was negligible. Also, the 
heat transfer between the outer walls’ surfaces and the surrounding air in the chamber was minimized. 
The air was exhausted through an opening (with a free-flow area of 2.4 x 2.4 m2) in the centre of the 
chamber’s ceiling. The supplied air flow was uniform and did not reach velocities higher than 0.05 m/s. 
The supply air in the chamber was 100% clean outdoor air. 
The air temperature in the chamber could be controlled within the range of 5 to 50°C, with an accuracy 
of ±0.2 °C (Kjerulf-Jensenet al., 1975). 
In the middle of the chamber, there were a table and a chair placed on a wooden board. The table with 
the manikin seated on the office chair was placed on the wooden board in order to avoid any flows coming 
from the background ventilation and to establish a quiescent environment. 
3.1.2 Ventilated cushion  
The ventilated cushion (VC) is an advanced air removal method (Figure 3.1). Its performance and 
optimization were studied by Bivolarova et al. (2016) and Rezgals (2015). The VC was manufactured by 
Ultimate Speed and originally it was designed to supply the air in order to provide cooling of the body in 
contact with the cushion. In the study by Rezgals (2015) its design was modified. It worked as a local 
exhaust system with air suction for the removal of the (polluted) air. The suction of the VC was realized 
through 8 rows of openings (Figure 3.1).

The flow in the ducts was regulated by the fan and both dampers. Their settings differed based on the

surrounding in order to reduce the flow in Branch 1. Therefore the sum of flows from Branch 1 and Branch

by the micromanomenter and duct areas, the flow was calculated. The difference between the total flow 
and the flow from Branch 2, gave the result of the flow in Branch 1 or the flow of the ventilated cushion 
(1.5 L/s, 3 L/s and 5 L/s).

Prior to the measurements, the velocity profile of the RMP was examined by using previously described

from the diffuser’s discharge surface with a single anemometer, in order to avoid the measuring error due

The results of the measured velocity profile are presented in Appendix A. 
The average velocity of the RMP was determined based on the average of the velocities (i.e. the measured 
mean air speed at each point) captured at the central point and four neighboring points in all four 
directions. The velocities chosen for the experiment were 0.2 m/s and 0.4 m/s. 0.2 m/s is the 
recommended velocity in the occupied zone for the comfortable thermal environment in the office spaces 
by the standard EN ISO 15251:2011 (ISO, 2011). 0.4 m/s is the velocity that was expected to penetrate the 
free convection flow around the human body (Kaczmaryk, 2002; Bolashikov et al., 2003).

pressure difference (dP) of 0.6 Pa, the set point for air pressure at chamber’s control was 15 Pa and the 
damper in the PV system was 65 % closed. The average velocity in the center of the RMP of 0.4 m/s was 
reached, when the chamber’s micro-manometer showed pressure difference of 2.9 Pa, with the set point 
of the pressure at 26 Pa and the damper 0 % closed (fully opened).

3.2.1 Thermal manikin 
During the experiments a breathing, non-sweating thermal manikin was used to simulate an occupant.

skin surface temperature (about 34°C) of an average person under the state of thermal comfort. This was

3.2.4.2 Fast CO2 and N2O concentration meters 
Fast CO2 and N2O meters were used in this study to measure with much higher sampling frequency the 
concentration of the two gases compared to the Innova Gas Analyzer. The fast meters use the method of 
non-dispersive infrared absorption (NDIR) to analyse gases. In total, three N2O and three CO2 fast meters 
were used during the experiments to sample the gases concentrations in three points in the chamber. 
Each meter had an inlet and an outlet. The meters’ inlets sampled the gases through polyamide nylon 
tubes with diameter of 4 mm. The pump sucks the sampled air through the measuring chamber of the 
meters, where the gas is analysed. The pump was connected to the instrument outlet. It is important to 
note that smaller than 2 mm sampling tubes may result in negative pressure difference causing 
inconsistent air tightness in the sensor’s measuring chamber. The chamber analyzer consists of cone 
shaped space between the emitter and gas detector, with sensors for pressure difference and 
temperature compensation (Figure 3.13) (Kierat and Popiolek, 2017). More details on the construction of 
the fast meters used the current study can be found in Kierat and Popiolek (2017). 
The gas sampling flow rate, sampling tube length and the level of gas concentration had an impact on the 
final results (measured gas concentration) obtained with such fast meter (Kierat and Popiolek, 2017). 
Therefore it is important, to know all three parameters in order to apply the dynamic correction method 
==PAGE:49==
49 
 
suggested by Kierat and Popiolek (2017). This method is needed to restore the waveform of the gas 
concentration changes at the sampling input of the meter on the basis of the recorded measurement 
results, having taken into account the pre-defined dynamic properties of the meter. The correction 
method is based on the Fourier transformation. When using the proposed dynamic correction method it 
is possible to increase the upper frequency limit of the meter about 2.6 times (Kierat and Popiolek, 2017). 
The frequency of the meters during the measurements presented in the current study was 4Hz which 
means that the sampling rate was 4 samples/sec. The accuracy of the instruments was similar to the 
Innova gas analyzers.  After averaging over 2 seconds, the method used NDIR allows to measure with 
expanded uncertainty of (95%) approx. ±20ppm and 2% of readings (K).

3.5 Measurement procedure 
Firstly, the measuring equipment was turned on, in order to warm up the instruments and measure more 
accurately. In the experiment three CO2 and three N2O gas meters were used, measuring at three points 
in the room. Each Innova set had four channels activated. In the morning and in the evening, inter-
calibration of the Innovas and fast gas meters was performed. Description of the inter-calibration is given 
in Appendix C of this report. The set points in the chamber control system remained constant over the 
whole experiment. 
Before every case, all of the computers were synchronised in order to keep the exact timing for further 
data analysis. The computers were synchronised with online server: 0.dk.pool.ntp.org, by the Internet 
Time in the computer (NTP Pool Project, 2016). The computer time works separately from the central 
process unit (CPU), so the time of the samples collected was uniform and the data could be compared. 
Depending on the study case the manikin’s lungs were turned on or kept OFF. Afterwards the dosing of 
the N2O and CO2 gas was started. If needed, the VC and the PV were adjusted and set to the required flow 
rate. Steady state gas concentration was determined that it was reached by monitoring on two computer 
screens (connected to each Innova) the concentration measured with the Innovas in the mouth of the 
manikin. After the steady state was reached all other sensors (fast gas meters, anemometer and 
temperature sensor) were started to log the data. The manikin was logged for 6 – 10 minutes per case (6 
samples/min). The average duration of one case was 50 minutes in order to collect around 12000 samples 
with fast gas meters and 10 – 15 samples with Innova, depending on the model of the gas analyser and 
the number of the channels. In order to collect more samples with Innova, several cases were measure 
==PAGE:56==
56 
 
up to 3 or even 4 hours. However, for the purpose of this experiment the measurements with Innova were 
to control the fast gas meters.

5. DISCUSSION 
In order to explain the results obtained in this study the dynamics of the jets generated during exhalation 
nose and exhalation mouth, the physics of their interaction with the FCF and its effect on the transport of 
pollution released at the groins and the armpits is discussed. Some of the results included in Appendix D, 
E, F and G are referred. It is important to note, that due to easier understanding of the text the breathing 
mode is in some parts of the text referred as Inhalation nose and means the same as Inhalation 
nose/exhalation mouth. Additionally, the Inhalation mouth means the same as Inhalation 
mouth/exhalation nose. 
5.1 The impact of BREATHING 
The importance of the location of the pollution source for its removal was discussed in many studies 
presented in the following chapters. 
Dilution of the gasses in the FCF 
The present study confirmed previous findings by Licina et al. (2015c) that the pollution source location 
has a significant impact on the exposure. Average concentrations measured without breathing, without 
ventilated cushion (VC) in operation and in the absence of the personalized ventilation (PV), showed that 
the pollution (tracer gas) generated at the groins and the armpits is carried by the free convection flow 
(FCF). The resulting natural path of the pollution from the body carried by the FCF has been described by 
Ličina et al (2015c), Ličina et al (2015b); Rim and Novoselac, (2009).  
The pollution from the groins (simulated by N2O), was carried by the free convective flow (FCF) upwards 
to the chest and then to the mouth and nose, and above the head. In the breathing zone, the pollution 
from the groins was carried mostly from the front part of FCF (see Figure 5.1 (left)). The mean 
concentrations measured at the chest were more than 2 times higher compared to the concentrations 
measured in the breathing zone (at the case “VC_0L/s_No_Br_PV_0_m/s” the N2O concentrations 
measured at the mouth were 274 ppm and at the chest 885 ppm). Thus, the concentrations of pollution 
coming from the groins gradually decreased when they reached the breathing zone. In Appendix D the 
additional N2O concentrations measured with Innova around the manikin were performed. They showed 
that, the pollution from the groins was mostly diluted behind the mankin’s back.  
The results of the present study were in accordance with previously obtained results reported by Ličina 
et al. (2015c) that at an ambient temperature of 23°C, the FCF brings the gaseous pollution upwards. Rim 
and Novoselac (2009) also reported that FCF at low mixing flows surrounding human body brings pollution 
from the lower parts of the body (feet) upwards. 
==PAGE:85==
85 
 
 
Figure 5.1: Illustration of the dilution of the pollution generated at the groins (N2O) (left) and armpits (CO2) (right) 
The pollution from the armpits (simulated by CO2), was released at the chest height. Part of it was 
entrained into the FCF from the side/back and part from the front of the manikin as illustrated in Figure 
5.1 (right). The pollution was then taken upwards into the breathing zone. The rest of the pollution left 
the FCF and was diluted with the surrounding air in the vicinity of the human body. The air in the vicinity 
had its own movement. The surrounding air could dilute the tracer gas and moved it upwards or around 
the body. The concentrations released into the surrounding air might be re-entrained in other parts of 
FCF. 
When there was no breathing the mean concentrations from the armpits measured at the chest were less 
than 2 times higher than the concentrations measured in the breathing zone. These results showed that 
the pollution from the armpits did not decrease as much as the pollution from the groins (more than 3 
times lower at the mouth than at the chest) as it moved upwards from the chest to the breathing zone. 
These findings suggested that the exposure to the pollution from the armpits was higher compared to the 
pollution from the groins. Similar results were found by Ličina et al (2015c) who showed that the personal 
exposure increased with decreasing the distance between the body and the pollution source. Also an 
important finding of the study by Ličina et al. (2015c) was that pollution emitted from the armpits affects 
the breathing zone more compared to the pollution coming from the groins. It should be noted that as 
the natural convection flow rises it becomes more turbulent and thus it entrains more pollution from the 
surrounding air. This is especially important for the entrainment of the pollution at the head height 
because it is one of the highest parts of the body, where the already turbulent FCF encourages mixing. 
 
 
==PAGE:86==
86 
 
Exposure to the pollution in the FCF 
The present study showed that simulating breathing is important for the exposure and flow interaction 
when conducting the experiments with thermal manikin. At the breathing mode exhalation 
mouth/inhalation nose and exhalation nose/inhalation mouth the pollution from the armpits showed 
elevated concentrations during the inhalation periods compared to no breathing. However, the pollution 
from the groins showed that concentrations of inhalation periods remained similar to no breathing. 
In the study by Melikov and Kazmaryk (2007) with the breathing thermal manikin the same two breathing 
modes were tested (Inhalation nose/Exhalation mouth and Inhalation mouth/Exhalation nose). The 
pollution concentrations in the inhaled air were studied. Firstly, the amount of air inhaled from the 
personalized ventilation and secondly, the amount of air re-inhaled from the exhalation. 
The study found that the pollution concentration in the inhaled air can be measured accurately with a 
thermal manikin without breathing simulation, if they are measured at the distance lower than 1 cm at 
the upper lip. The obtained conclusion was in accordance to the results for the exposure to the pollution 
from the groins. The inhalation periods for both breathing modes showed similar results to no breathing. 
However, the exposure to the pollution from the armpits was during the inhalation periods at both 
breathing modes increased. The difference in the results was probably, due to the fact that the present 
study had the possibility to separately investigate the inhalation periods because the concentrations were 
measured by instruments with high frequency response. Thus, the selection of the inhalation periods was 
possible and the presented results were more accurate compared to the results from the study by Melikov 
and Kazmaryk (2007).  
The results from the study reported as well, that the percentage of re-inhaled air measured over the whole 
breathing cycles of the breathing mode exhalation nose/inhalation mouth was higher compared to the 
exhalation mouth/inhalation nose. 
In contrast to discussed in the study above, the pollution from the groins showed that the breathing 
modes didn’t present difference of concentrations in the inhaled air. As well, the exposure to the pollution 
from the armpits showed higher values of concentration at exhalation nose/inhalation mouth compared 
to exhalation mouth/inhalation nose. The exhalation jets in present study had clean air and only inhalation 
periods were analysed. In order to confirm the findings of the study above, the concentration inhaled over 
the exhalation nose/inhalation mouth should be lower compared to exhalation mouth/inhalation nose. 
The actually re-inhaled air in case of exhalation nose/inhalation mouth was brought by FCF. 
The study by Melikov and Kaczmaryk (2007) reported that re-inhaled concentrations were 19% lower 
when there was break simulated in the breathing cycle compared to the concentrations in the absence of 
the break. The present study revealed that the break was important for the exposure to the pollution 
from the groins because it allowed the FCF to recover over the break. However, the brake didn’t have a 
strong impact on the pollution from the armpits since the FCF bringing the pollution over the brake periods 
didn’t change the concentrations. The concentrations increased only during the inhalation periods (when 
the inhalation jet sucked the pollution into the nose or mouth). 
Exhalation mouth/Inhalation nose 
The dynamics of the air jets is discussed in detail in Appendix E. The dynamics of the jet from the mouth 
is different than dynamics of the jets from the nose. The jet from the mouth, pushed the air to the sides 
==PAGE:87==

(towards the nose, towards the chin and to the sides of the manikin’s lips). At the sides the clean jet was 
mixed with the surrounding polluted air. The exhalation jet was faster and stronger than the FCF and thus 
it penetrated it. The clean jet entrained some of the pollutions from the breathing zone and pushed it 
upwards towards the nose with a slight angle (Figure 5.2). The non-isothermal jet was at the end slightly 
curved upwards because it was warmer than the surrounding air. This is in accordance with the study by 
Nielsen et al. (2009a), Olmedo et al. (2010) and is presented in chapter 1.2.3 of this report.

coming from the groins (further away from the breathing zone) was mixed (and then only slightly 
increased compared to the case without breathing. This is probably due to pollution being pushed and 
mixed at the sides of the exhalation jet from the mouth. As previously mentioned, at the sides of the free 
exhalation jet, the pollution is entrained and mixed. The transient exhalation jets cause turbulent flows in 
the breathing zones of the manikin. 
For the pollution generated at the armpits the measurements during the exhalation periods show 
decrease of the CO2 concentration at both locations, mouth and nose (Appendix F, Figure F.2). The break 
between the exhalation and inhalation had less impact on the FCF bringing the pollution to the nose from 
the armpits compared to the pollution from the groins. The CO2 concentration over the brake either 
increased or remained the same. Thus the part of the FCF flow bringing the pollution was unstable – 
turbulent and only the inhalation flow increased the concentration. 
At the chest, the CO2 concentration values measured in the breathing mode inhalation nose/exhalation 
mouth were slightly lower compared to No breathing case. This is due to the fact that the armpits are 
located close to the breathing zone. The exhaled air entrains the pollution generated at the armpits. The 
pollution diluted over the breathing zone to the chest is mixed with the clean upward exhalation jet from 
the mouth and thus slightly decreased at both locations-breathing zone and chest. 
Exhalation nose/Inhalation mouth 
When the jets come from the nose, the jets are directed downwards towards the chest (Figure 5.4). Thus, 
they break the FCF and pushed the clean jets into the chest region. The jets from the nose during the 
exhalation periods do not affect the concentrations at the mouth. The non-isothermal jets were at the 
end slightly curved upwards because they were warmer than the surrounding air, as described in the 
Chapter 1.2.3 of this report. Each exhalation jets from the nose was a free clean jet and coming in equal 
amount from the two nostrils. The jets were transient and they pushed the surrounding air away each 
time the exhalation period in the breathing cycle started. The pollution from the surrounding was 
influenced by the exhalation jets. The jets mixed with the pollution at their sides in the shear layer 
entraining the pollution and carrying it in their direction, where the pollution was afterwards mixed and 
diluted. The jets interacted with the FCF as opposing flows and the result was increased turbulence and 
mixing.  The high standard deviations of the concentrations showed that the flows in the vicinity of the 
body were very turbulent and thus difficult to identify.

5.2 The impact of BREATNING and VC 
The source control applied with the VC first removed the pollution due to suction and secondly, it affected 
the direction, velocity and shape of the FCF. 
The measured N2O and CO2 tracer gas concentrations at the chest showed that the pollution decreased 
with the VC in use, regardless from the breathing mode. The higher the suction flow rate the lower was 
the pollution concentration. The same results were observed for the exposure to the body-bioeffluents. 
These results are in agreement with those of previous studies showing the ability of the VC to decrease 
exposure to the pollutants emitted from the armpits and the groins (Bivolarova et al., 2016b; Bivolarova 
et al., 2016c; Rezgals, 2015). Rezgals (2015) showed that the VC sucking only 1.5 L/s can respectively 
decrease the exposure to pollutants from groins and armpits with 85% and 64% in a still indoor 
environment with 26 °C room air temperature. The study also showed that although the VC was still able 
to reduce the exposure to both pollutants, the efficiency of the VC decreased with decreasing the room 
temperature to 20°C. In the present study, surprisingly in the cases when the VC was sucking 1.5 L/s and 
the manikin was breathing either with the mode exhalation mouth/inhalation nose or exhalation 
nose/inhalation mouth, the exposure to the pollution from the armpits was the same as in the case 
without VC working. The explanation of this result follows below. Before that, it should be noted that the 
study by Rezgals (2015) was conducted with non-breathing manikin and also the clothing insulation and 
type of clothing were different than in the present study. 
Firstly, the pollution was removed already at the source and thus, the FCF brought only some of the 
pollution upwards into the breathing zone. Secondly, the suction flow locally changed the direction of the 
FCF because the VC was placed only at the back side of the manikin. Thus it created two parts of the FCF. 
One part was the FCF in the front of the manikin. The front part of the FCF remained almost unaffected 
with a free upward direction. The other part is the FCF weakened mostly at the back and the pelvis. The 
local upward FCF flow might be partially or in total redirected towards the sucking surface of the VC. At 
the sides (from the hips and to the shoulders of the manikin) the velocities of the flow might decreased 
to zero velocity and the flows might have become stagnant in certain zones. The locations of the stagnant 
zones depend on the flow rate from the VC, as shown in Figure 5.6. In the stagnant zones the dilution of 
the gases might strongly increase and afterwards be mixed or entrained by another (third) type of flow, 
==PAGE:91==
91 
 
such as flow .of exhalation. Additionally, the VC with its suction redirected natural path of the flows and 
thus created unpredicted vortices in the vicinity of the human body (Figure 5.7). This may explain the 
results why the VC at suction flow rate of 1.5 L/s was not efficient to reduce the exposure to pollution 
generated at the armpits (Figure 4.10 and Figure 4.23 in the results chapter). In order to understand clear 
the flow dynamics detail measurements are needed. 
Such formation of the stagnant zones was observed in the study by Ličina et al., (2015d). The study 
investigated the interaction of the FCF with room ventilation flows and studied its impact on the transport 
of the pollution into the breathing zone. In the breathing zone, the opposing ventilation flow to the FCF 
at 0.3 m/s stopped the FCF upward movement and increased the concentrations and thus the exposure 
to the pollution. 
With VC at 1.5 L/s the pollution from the groins was brought into the breathing zone mostly by the front 
part of the FCF (Figure 5.6 right, shown in yellow). The dilution of the pollution from the armpits (CO2) 
was slightly more complex and difficult to predict since the locations of the stagnant zones couldn’t be 
precisely determined. It is assumed that the stagnant locations were at the sides of the manikin in height 
shoulders. The first part of the CO2 might be diluted into the front FCF (yellow). The second part might be 
diluted into the region of the armpits and concentrated there (light green). The third part might be diluted 
towards the back in the direction of the suction flow from the VC and so removed (green). The complex 
formation of the flows above the manikin’s shoulder when VC operated at 1.5 L/s is shown in Figure 5.7, 
where the development of vortex is visible.

5.3 The impact of PV on exposure 
The results show that the applied source control, i.e. the VC, alone could not completely remove the 
pollution from the groins. However, the personalized ventilation (PV) reduced the exposure to the 
pollution from the armpits and from the groins obtained during the inhalation periods at the breathing 
mode Inhalation nose/exhalation mouth.  
At the opening of the ATD, in the first region of the jet (potential core region) the temperatures and 
velocities are constant, the turbulence intensity low (laminar jet) and the air is unpolluted. However, the 
natural entrainment and mixing of the air might occur further in the jet. Such phenomena is caused by 
the velocity or density difference in polluted surrounding air and supply air from the PV. In this way, the 
length of the potential core can be decreased (Melikov, 2004). 
In the case with no breathing the inhaled concentrations for the pollution generated at the groins and the 
armpits decreased with increasing flow rates of the PV (Figure 5.10). The PV with centre line velocity at 
the face (target) of 0.2 m/s didn’t completely penetrate the FCF, as the flow from the PV (blue) bounded 
upwards along with FCF (yellow). The PV at 0.4 m/s penetrated the FCF and supplied the air into the 
breathing zone and removed the polluted air. This is in accordance with the study by Cermak et al. (2002) 
showing that in order to improve IAQ the air from the PV has to be clean (unmixed with the surrounding 
air) and strong enough to penetrate the FCF. 
The clean jet from the PV (indicated with blue on Figure 5.10) with uniform initial velocities and low 
turbulence intensity at its core was transverse to the front FCF (yellow). The long core of clean and 
unmixed air at higher target velocities of 0.4 m/s reached the manikin’s face and penetrated the front FCF 
(Figure 5.10 - yellow). The jet was blowing in the pointed direction because it was isothermal. The FCF 
from the sides and the back might be mixed and thus some of the pollution could be entrained back into 
the breathing zone.

into the surrounding

the released body pollution. The PV at 0.2 m/s combined with breathing mode Inhalation nose/exhalation 
mouth removed the pollution from the groins better compared to the pollution from the armpits. The 
reason was that the pollution from the groins was brought by the part of FCF from the front. Compared

The PV at 0.4 m/s combined with breathing mode Inhalation nose/exhalation mouth penetrated the FCF

The study by Melikov et al. (2003), already presented in Chapter 1.4 investigated the combined effect of 
the total volume ventilation and PV, namely round movable panel (RMP) and vertical desk grill (VDG).

with regard to improved air quality. Additionally, the study confirmed that the when the PV is directed

The disadvantage of suppling the fresh air into the occupants breathing zone is that the pollution is 
distributed behind the occupant and thus the pollution might be spread to the other occupants in the 
same room (cross-infection). Studies (Melikov et al. 2003; Cermak and Melikov 2007; Halvonava and

such as the VC is important for the reduction of the pollution in the room. The elevated air velocities in

Kaczmaryk 2012). Supplying the flow at higher velocities improved the perceived air quality (PAQ) and 
decrease the negative impact of temperature, relative humidity and pollution. However, the PV might 
cause discomfortable local cooling to the body segments affected by the flow. The performance of PV 
regarding the exposure reduction at lower velocities can be improved by the application of the suction 
box or movable panel described in the chapter 1.4.1 of this report (Bolashikov et al., 2009  
Studies have shown, that PV could improve the thermal comfort (Kaczmaryk et al., 2002, 2004). It has 
been reported that the PV improved the acceptability of the thermal environment at room temperatures 
above 23⁰C. It has been shown that the PV didn´t increase the eye blinking intervals in human subject 
study combining the air temperature between 23-29⁰C and PV temperatures of 23-26⁰C (Melikov, 2004b).

5.5 HEAT LOSS FROM THE MANIKIN 
The heat loss, especially the local heat loss of the body segments in contact with the VC, increased with 
the increase of the flow rate in the cushion, i.e. the cooling effect of the VC increased. With the VC suction, 
the heat loss due to increased convection around the human body is increased. This is especially, from 
the parts where ventilated cushion is in contact with the body. From all body segments, the manikin is 
most affected at the back and thighs. This may result in thermal asymmetry (defined in ISO 7730). 
The cooling effect of the VC might be pleasant when our body needs to be cooled, e.g. hot climates, or 
stressful jobs, when people sweat a lot. However, there are some improvements that could be done 
towards the unwanted cooling effect, especially at lower range of comfortable temperatures 
recommended in the standards. Since the cooling is caused by forced convection, convective heating on 
the back and pelvis might be applied. If the air for heating wouldn’t affect the breathing zone, it could be 
recirculated from the room. Thus, while the VC removes the warm air from the body, it simultaneously 
supplies it back from separate openings. Additionally, the control system with the sensor for bioeffluents 
may be attached to the exhaust duct of the VC. The VC turns on the suction when the sensor recognizes 
an increased level of the bioeffluents from the body. 
The result obtained from the manikin were in accordance to the result measured by Rezgals (2015). 
Rezgals investigated the cooling effect of the VC to the body segments of the manikin and showed it with 
regard to the Equivalent temperature. At 23°C the manikins clothing insulation was 1 Clo, which is higher 
compared to the clothing insulation in this study (0.39 Clo). In accordance, the results obtained from all 
segments (All) and the Back of the manikin showed similar results as the results in the current study 
obtained for the VC cooling effect. In both studies the equivalent temperature was the highest at VC 5.0 
L/s and among the body segments at the Back of the manikin.  
Riis (2017) tested the effect of active (integrated grid of electrically heated wires) and passive heating 
(thermal insulation from 20 mm thick Armaflex) on the reduction of the cooling effect from the VC. It was 
concluded that VC with passive strategy wasn’t efficient for prevention of the cooling of the segments and 
provide thermal comfort in the range of 20-24°C. The VC with active strategy compensate the cooling 
effect well and provides thermal comfort in cold environments. However, due to the electricity supplied 
for the heating the energy consumption increased. It was recommended that the VC is tested with real 
human subjects. 
The comparison of the whole body heat loss from the manikin shows that the cooling effect of the PV at 
target velocity of 0.4 m/ is 64% lower compared to the PV at 0.2 m/s. The reason is that the PV cools 
smaller area of manikin’s body. The heat loss from the right side of the face was slightly higher compared 
to the left side when PV was working. This most probably happened because the PV didn’t supply the air 
from the center but slightly from the side (Figure 5.15 and in C.1 appendix C).

Besides, the potential to reduce exposure the use of PV has been shown to have potential for saving 
energy. Already in study by Niu (2003), the potential to save the energy with usage of advanced local 
ventilation systems was introduced. It was further studied and suggested by (Schiavon and Melikov, 2009; 
Schiavon et al., 2010; Niu et al., 2007). 
It has been suggested that the VC has potential for energy savings since the background ventilation 
combined with the VC can decrease the required flow rates of the total volume ventilation by the pollution 
removal of the VC (Bivolarova et al., 2016c). Bivolarova et al. (2016c, Rezgals, 2015) showed that the VC 
at 1.5 L/s combined with 1 air change per hour reduced the bioeffluents at the breathing zone. The 
combination was 65% more efficient than supplying 1.5 ACH (in IAQ category I for low pollution buildings). 
A recent study which performed energy simulations for a call-center in Copenhagen found that the VC 
with reduced background ventilation combined with MV and chilled ceiling (CC) reduces the energy use 
compared to only MV supplying the required ventilation rate per person (Nielsen, 2017). Although the 
study found as well that applying the VC with reduced MV and CC lead to the same energy saving as 
reduced MV combined with CC, the implementation of the VC would still be beneficial. The studies by 
Rezgals (2015), Riis (2017) as well as the current study show that the VC also provide cooling to the body 
parts in contact with its surface. The VC cooling effect might promote  energy savings, especially in warm 
environments, by providing local body cooling and at the same to keep rather high indoor temperatures 
(instead of using additional energy to provide cooling to the whole space from the background ventilation 
system). This needs to be studied further. 
However, the present study had some limitations and the impact of complex flow interaction on the 
exposure to the pollution has to be further examined. Some of the parameters were kept the same during 
the whole  
The study was obtained only under one room temperature (23°C). The results of concentrations may differ 
at lower and higher room air temperatures tested, such as 20°C or 26°C, studied by Rezgals (2015) and 
Ličina et al. (2015a, b, c).  
==PAGE:102==
102 
 
The breathing was tested without humidification. Based on the results obtained by Melikov and 
Kaczmaryk (2007) the humidity might have an impact on the re-inhaled amount of the exhaled air.  
Regardless from breathing other factors may affect the exposure. The changes of the characteristics of 
the FCF could have an impact on the pollution transportation and respectively on the exposure. 
Additionally, the manikin’s posture, clothing (insulation and tightness) and position of the table (10 cm 
from abdomen) were kept the same during the whole experiment. Thus other variations, such as absence 
of the table or table touching the abdomen and blocking the FCF might be investigated. Ličina et al. (2015c) 
reported decreased transport of pollution in such posture compared to the case without the presence of 
table. With light tight clothing and manikin’s body inclined backwards, the thickness of FCF increases. The 
thickness of FCF was lower at the breathing zone compared to the chest (Ličina et al, 2015b).  
Additionally, only one type of personalized ventilation was studied – the RMP. For example, the vertical 
desk grill (VDG) combined with VC could be investigated. The performance might be improved since the 
VC locally changes the formation of FCF. Additionally, the impact of breathing in such combination with 
assisting flow can be examined. 
A flow rate of the VC at 1.5 L/s wasn’t sufficient for the removal of the pollution from the armpits or it 
even promoted the mixing and the exposure. Thus the flow rates between 1.5 L/s and 3.0 L/s should be 
investigated.  
The complex changes in formation of FCF when VC is in operation needs to be examined with more 
measuring points of velocity, temperature and concentration along the whole manikin’s body in the FCF.  
The dilution of the gasses in the FCF should be examined carefully, by measuring the concentrations of 
the pollution with more measuring points. Furthermore, the study investigated only two pollution sources 
on the human body. Thus, different locations of the pollution source may be tested, such as pollution 
from the feet.  
Both VC and PV could be applied for the renovation of the old existing buildings or spaces with only little 
space available, as they require less space for implementation and allow flexibility of the usage (Melikov, 
2016). 
The optimized strategies with energy saving potential can be joined for the usage in the electrical vehicles 
or hospitals, however the level of noise due to the operation of the VC or PV has to be considered.

6. CONCLUSIONS 
This study investigated the effect of the interaction of the FCF, the breathing flow and the personalized 
flow from front on the exposure to body bio-effluents generated at the groins and the armpits in the case 
without source control and with source control by ventilated cushion. Additionally, the local cooling effect 
on the human body caused by the VC and PV combined with breathing at 23°C room air temperature was 
examined by use of thermal manikin. Based on the results it was concluded: 
 
The location of the pollution source is important for the exposure; 
 
The exposure to the pollution is affected by both the inhalation and exhalation flows. 
 
The simulation of the breathing mode is important for the accurate assessment of occupant 
exposure; 
 
The breathing mode Inhalation mouth/exhalation nose increases the exposure to the pollution 
from the armpits compared to No breathing; 
 
The breathing mode Inhalation nose/exhalation mouth only slightly increases the exposure to 
the pollution from the armpits compared to No breathing; 
 
The FCF bringing the pollution from the armpits didn’t have a strong impact on the inhaled 
concentrations however, the inhalation periods (inhalation flow) increased the exposure; 
 
The breathing modes Inhalation nose/exhalation mouth and Inhalation mouth/exhalation nose 
don’t have a strong impact on the pollution from the groins. Over the break the FCF bringing the 
pollution from the groins recovered and increased the exposure at the nose and at the mouth; 
 
At the chest the exhalation jets from the nose interact and remove or weaken the front FCF. The 
exhalation jets from the mouth promote the mixing in the breathing zone and thus the air is 
pushed slightly downwards and effect are visible at the chest.  
VC 
 
The VC reduced the exposure to the pollution from the groins and armpits with increasing the 
flow rates of the VC  from 1.5 to 3.0 and 5.0 L/s; 
 
The type of the breathing mode had an impact on the exposure to the pollution from the 
armpits when the VC works at 1.5 L/s. The inhaled pollution is higher at Inhalation mouth 
compared to Inhalation nose. At the groins the type of the breathing mode didn’t have an 
impact on the exposure. 
 
When the VC was combined with breathing it didn’t reduce the exposure at VC 1.5 L/s. 
However, when there was no breathing, the pollution from the armpits was slightly reduced. 
 
The VC affects the pollution spread from the armpits in the FCF below the armpits. Thus, the 
pollution from the armpits is removed at the chest; 
 
The break during the breathing cycle didn’t have a strong impact on the inhaled pollution from 
the armpits because the inhalation and exhalation jets promoted a very high mixing of the parts 
of FCF bringing the pollution into the breathing zone. However, the break had an impact on the 
pollution coming from the groins; 
PV 
==PAGE:104==
104 
 
 
The personalized ventilation (PV) reduced the exposure to the pollution from the armpits and 
from the groins obtained during the inhalation periods at the breathing mode: Inhalation nose 
at PV at 0.2 m/s and almost completely reduced at PV 0.4 m/s; 
 
PV at 0.2 m/s didn’t completely penetrate the FCF. However, the PV at 0.4 m/s did; 
 
The highest concentration reduction was obtained with PV at 0.4 m/s (without breathing and 
source control – VC). It could reduce the exposure to the pollution from both pollution sources 
completely (up to 98%). 
PV+VC 
 
Exposure to the pollution from the groins and the armpits during inhalation nose with PV at 0.2 
m/s combined with VC decreased with increasing flow rates of the VC.  
 
Compared to the VC and PV working alone the pollution is reduced more efficiently when the 
advanced ventilation strategies are combined in case of No breathing. However, when the 
breathing is present, the pollution from the armpits increases at VC 1.5 L/s+PV0.2 m/s compared 
to PV at 0.2 m/s alone.

back of the manikin;  
 
When the PV worked at 0.4 m/s it removed more heat from the face compared to PV at 0.2 m/s;

APPENDIX B: THE INTER-CALIBRATION 
The gas was dosed in the beginning of Branch 1 and measured in Branch 3, 5 cm after the fan. For each 
gas (N2O and CO2) the inter-calibration was carried out at two concentration levels – at outdoor 
concentration when the gas wasn’t dosed ‘no dosing period’ (0 ppm for N2O and 450 ppm for CO2) and 
when the gas was dosed at constant flow rate ‘dosing period’(260 ±10 ppm for N2O and 2500±50 ppm for 
CO2). 
Inter-calibration procedure: 
1. By using Micatron flow sensor, Damper 1 and a fan the flow in the ventilation system was 
adjusted. Flow rates for N2O and CO2 were 33.3 L/s and 33.3 L/s, respectively. 
2. When determined based on measurements with Innova, that the concentrations of N2O and CO2 
in outdoor supply air to the test rig was about 0 ppm and about 450 ppm, respectively , the 
measurement simultaneously started with all three fast gas meters and Innova for no dosing 
period. When Innova collected 6 samples, the measuring period stopped. 
3. The dosing started. Inter-calibration of measuring equipment for N2O and CO2 had different 
dosing rates and different flows in the ventilation system. The dosing rate for N2O was 0.5 L/min 
and for CO2, 2.0 L/min. The dosing rate and the flow were determined based on the sensitivity 
(measuring range) of the instruments calibrated.  
4. After steady state was reached, the measurements were repeated in the same way for the dosing 
period. 
5. After the inter-calibration, the data were collected from both the fast gas meters and the two 
Innovas. 
6. The data from each inter-calibration were fit in a linear regression line in Microsoft Excel. 
Afterwards, the mean value of the slopes of the regression lines was calculated. The mean value 
was used as a correction coefficient for the measured with the fast meters N2O and CO2 
concentrations measured during the experiments.

APPENDIX E: Dynamics of air jets 
EXHALATION: The exhalation jet was an incompressible free jet with round inlet. When the jet was formed 
it pushed the surrounding air away with a round kicker. The jet had an axisymmetric potential core (Figure 
E.1 and E.2). At the inlet the flow in the jet was laminar and later on, it was transformed into a turbulent 
flow. First, the flow became turbulent at the sides of its spread – in the shear layer. Afterwards, the whole 
flow became turbulent. 
 
Figure E.1: Incompressible free jet with round inlet (Del Rio et al., 2014) 
The free exhalation jet was faster from the surrounding air, although it had a similar density. The 
differences in the velocity created the shear layer. In the shear layer, the vortices were developed  
 
Figure E.2: Round Free jet (from the video: Andersen B.O. and Hjorth Jensen M., 2012, Örlü, R. & Alfredsson, 2011) 
 
Figure E.3: Vortex ring and instantaneously turbulent jet (Olmedo et al., 2010) 
==PAGE:128==
128 
 
The formation of these side vortices was a consequence of Kelvin Helmholtz instability (Figure E.4).  
 
Figure E.4: The principle of Kelvin Helmholtz instability (Ref) 
 
As the waves and later on vortices were formed, the jet entrained the surrounding air and carried it along. 
The surrounding air may contain the pollution. The pollution might be transferred from one side of the jet 
and then be mixed in the turbulent part.
