Abstract
This Master’s thesis aims to investigate the changes in the microstructure and the me­
chanical properties of nickel­based superalloys under different heat treatment conditions.
The specific heat­treated conditions mimic the real­time operating conditions of internal
combustion engines in MAN company’s vehicles.
Those Nickel alloys attract interest because of their particular features and properties. On
the contrary to the nimonic alloys, turned out that even very small changes to the thermal
aging process can easily affect the material’s properties, and that just high temperature
does not necessarily soften the material. Precise control is needed, and these materials’
hardening results from different factors.

1.1
Background
MAN Energy Solutions has great experience in constructing Internal Combustion Engines
(ICE) that provide efficiency, sustainability, and profitability to the marine industries and
beyond. Currently, they are developing new dual­fuel and two­stock engines that meet
with high demand. The selection of the appropriate materials is crucial for implementing
new components that meet those high demands.
Nickel superalloys are frequently employed in metallic valves for automotive, maritime,
and internal combustion engine applications up to 760°C [1]. More specifically, the ser­
vice temperature of exhaust valves in low­speed engines (fig. 1.1) can reach up to 600°C
with high pressures, making them the most vulnerable and demanding components of
the engine [2]. Nickel alloys like UNS N07080 (commonly called Nimonic), are the most
commonly used in these applications due to their ability to withstand high temperatures,
corrosion resistance, and creep resistance are systematically used even for the highly de­
manding exhaust valves [fig. 1.2] that control the hotter gasses in the combustion cham­
bers that pass through and create a high­temperature environment [2]. Engine valves in
internal combustion engines are subjected to cyclic loading, high temperatures, and cor­
rosive environments during operation. Expansion and contraction of the crystal structure
can evoke dimensional changes in the valves that lead to the concentration of the local­
ized residual compressive stresses. Taking into consideration all of the above, cracks can
happen over time and result in leakage of combustion gases. The engine performance is
reduced due to fatigue, and embrittlement leads untimely to mechanical failure [3].

Problem description
The scope of this MSc thesis is inherent in understanding the evolution of the microstruc­

decreased times. This increased temperature is there to accelerate the otherwise sub­
stantially long­term operational effects. In this way, it will be possible to understand the
behavior after long­term high temperature exploitation.
The main purpose is positioned around the investigation of finding the stability of the given
materials. To characterise their microstructural evolution under the given temperatures
and correlate it with the changes in the mechanical properties. As a result, this would
give insight into the performance of these materials in the MAN engine components as
exhaust valves. Furthermore, it would validate the selection of those materials over com­
monly used Nimonic 80A. It will help to form a basis for identifying possible risks and
failure modes of the MAN components and after the comparison to find the most suitable
material.
These alloys emerged in vivid interest because of their intricate nature and behavior. The
characterization of the microstructure, hardening measurements, present phases, phase
compositions, and changes in mechanical properties will be the main concerns of this
thesis research.
The materials of interest received by MAN are the Ni38.6Cr3.5Al and a variation of it,

Theory
2.1
Superalloys
The drive for high­temperature alloys was initially fueled by the need for steam turbines
and internal combustion engines in the early 20th century. Superalloys were introduced in
1940 by the demand for further improvement in technological aspects with piston engines,
gas turbines, and the aviation industry [5]. Fe­rich alloys have been used to develop a
variety of stainless steels, while Ni­rich alloys have been advanced to manufacture super­
alloys, which can be seen as the evolution of the first [1]. Superalloys can be defined as
materials with the ability to maintain their mechanical characteristics even at homologous
temperatures TH = T/Tm above 0.6,[6] even though the usual operation temperatures
that they used for are around 0.8Tm or higher. Feature multi­phase microstructures where
the identity, size, shape, and distribution of secondary phases are meticulously controlled
for the best performance.[5] Superalloys are categorized into three main groups based on
their primary ingredients: iron­based, nickel­based, and cobalt­based alloys. These can
be further divided into cast and wrought structures, and Ni­based superalloys can be both.
Nickel is the only one with an FCC structure at all temperatures, and this characteristic
makes it more widespread than Fe­based and Co­based alloys. Nickel superalloys get
strengthened by the precipitation of the coherent γ’ phase, [1] are highly adaptable and can
be optimized by adjusting the composition of alloying elements such as chromium (Cr),
tungsten (W), molybdenum (Mo), tantalum (Ta), niobium (Nb), titanium (Ti), zirconium (Zr),
iron (Fe), and aluminium (Al) to enhance their metallurgical properties. Chromium im­
proves oxidation resistance, while cobalt reduces stacking fault energy,[6] and so makes
it more difficult for dislocations to cross slip and gives high hardness. Accordingly, pro­
cessing methods, like directional solidification, single­crystal technology, powder metal­
lurgy (PM), and hot isostatic pressing., as well as coating systems were developed [5].
Alternatives such as intermetallic compounds (e.g. nickel aluminides and titanium alu­
minides), refractory materials, ceramics, and carbon composites have been also investi­
gated to achieve a lower cost, and despite preferable economical and thermal properties,
these materials introduce a major drawback of embrittlement.[1]. Nimonic80 was the first
precipitation hardenable nickel superalloy and the optimized version later used was the
Nimonic80A, in which precipitation of γ’ Ni3Al and γ’ Ni3Ti along with MC­type primary
carbides and Cr­rich grain boundary carbides takes place. In (??) is the cross­section of
a Ni80A alloy with the illustration of the different precipitates. [5].

Nickel­based alloys are well known for their exceptional properties, making them indis­
pensable in various demanding applications. Their high corrosion resistance makes them
suitable for use in aggressive environments such as chemical processing, marine, and
aerospace industries. Additionally, these alloys maintain their strength and integrity at
elevated temperatures, rendering them ideal for applications in high­temperature envi­
ronments like ICE, gas turbines, jet engines, and nuclear reactors. Another significant
property of nickel­based alloys is their resistance to creep; they can withstand prolonged
exposure to high temperatures without significant deformation, making them especially
valuable in the aerospace and power generation industries [8].
While the alloy performs well under high temperatures and resists degradation from cor­
rosion, it may be more brittle and less capable of deforming plastically without breaking
when subjected to mechanical stress at lower temperatures. In principle, more than 22w%
Cr can evoke brittleness due to bcc nature [9]. That’s why the combination of PM tech­
niques, additional elements for compositional changes, and appropriate HT parameters
are used to give the most optimal performances and provide good overall mechanical
properties [9].
2.2
Metallurgy of Ni­based superalloys
2.2.1
Mechanical Properties
Mechanical properties are directly affected by temperature. Primarily properties such as
yield strength and ductility are influenced by the movement of dislocations. At lower tem­
peratures, the slip systems are more restricted and there are no recovery processes;
instead strain­hardening happens [1]. Dislocation density increases, which means higher
forces are needed to deform the material. This often leads to a brittle nature, which is why
the balance between strength and ductility is what needs to be achieved to have a tough
material.

With increasing temperature, there is a reduction in dislocation density and area, the mo­
bility increases because of the diffusion mechanisms, and defects like vacancies appear
or multiply. The material is more stress­relieved in this stage and becomes weaker but
gains ductility.
As the temperature rises further softening is happening. Diffusion of lattice and creep
mechanism starts to dominate. At these elevated temperatures, the strength of the ma­
terial reduces further through processes such as dislocation climb, recrystallization, and
grain growth [1].

2.2.3
Ni Intermetallics
Ni aluminides are compact, they have low densities, high melting points, high­temperature
strength properties, and creep resistance. In most aluminides, the critical temperature is
the same as the melting point (Tc=Tm). The drawback is their brittle nature and limited
machinability due to low polycrystalline ductility at ambient temperature.
Increase strength by increasing ductility with the cohesion of the grain boundaries, so
more stresses can allow the easy movement of dislocations through the interfaces and
the structure. This results in resistance to coarsening.
The γ’ Ni3Al ( L12­ fcc) aluminide is the most significant ordered phase responsible for
the primary strengthening of nickel superalloys [1]. It differs in lattice parameter with the
γ Ni, but It is coherent with it and precipitates homogeneously throughout the matrix,
which means low interfacial energy and resistance to coarsening. Shows stability up to
its solution temperature, around 1400°C., and remains ordered after the addition of other
elements in the solution [1]. The ordered L12 structure of Ni3Al precipitate is shown in
[fig. 2.5], it gets stronger as the temperature increases. The Kear­Wilsdorf (K­W) locks
mechanism is responsible for this remarkable strengthening anomaly behaviour of Ni3Al.
This is happening because of the rapid work hardening during ordering, by the cross slip
of screw dislocations from primarily fcc {111} planes to {100} and their interaction with pre­
cipitates [1], [12]. This is a thermally activated process and that’s why it is temperature
dependent, and arose with increasing temperature. However, in even higher tempera­
tures yield strength finally declines due to dislocation glide [1].
Figure 2.5: Ordered structure L12 of Ni3Al [13]
NiAl ( L20­ bcc) with a higher melting point than Ni3Al(1638°C), gives an high oxida­
tion resistance at high temperatures. It is also brittle at ambient temperatures even in
the case of fine­grain production, and that is because of bcc type of crystal structure lacks
sufficient slip systems. For all compositions yield strength decreases with increasing tem­
peratures.[11]
The phase γ’(Ni2Cr ­ bco) is another ordered phase that occurs after long exposures at
low temperatures. It gives great strength to the material but also (under 500°C), exhibits

2.2.4
Discontinuous Precipitation
Discontinuous precipitation (cellular) does not occur uniformly in the material. Is a ther­
mally activated process, that starts from the grain boundaries. Precipitates attached and
elongated along with the boundary migration. In this solid­solid transformation, the pre­
cipitates are taking place in certain shapes, places, and orientations [7]. The degree of
supersaturation of the matrix can affect the mechanical properties, and it becomes obvi­
ous that the prior plastic deformation to a material affects the process and the properties.
When prior plastic deformation accelerates the continuous precipitation, it might reduce
and retarding the discontinuous precipitation depending on the alloy composition. The
presence of a high dislocation density enhances the nucleation of the continuous precipi­
tates. The driving force arose from the supersaturation of the matrix or phase boundaries
with low free energy. When the solubility of Cr to Ni drops from the solution temperature,
during ageing leading to the supersaturation of the matrix with Cr and the precipitation is
happening [16], [7].
2.2.5
NiCrAl alloys
It is known that Kurdjumov­Sachs (K­S) applied between γ and α phases [4]. In binary
Ni­Cr alloys with high Cr concentration (<30wt%), a lamellar structure is formed by the
cellular DP reaction of α­Cr. In ternary Ni­Cr­Al the lamellar becomes more versatile con­
sisting not only of γ­Ni and α­Cr but of the precipitation of γ’­Ni3Al (L12) structure too.
With the Ni engaged in the formation of the aluminide, the matrix becomes depleted of
nickel, and Cr increases because it exceeds its equilibrium solubility in the matrix [4]. In
lower temperatures, the volume fraction of γ’ is higher and so the supersaturation of α­Cr
increases more, leading to an even finer lamellar. In (fig. 2.6 (a)) the composition of the
nano­lamella is shown. In this way, it has been shown that the Al addition leads to this
very fine lamellar structure with significantly high strength due to the DP of Cr, [17] even
though it doesn’t contribute to creep resistance in the same way. This structure seems
to be stable up to 600°C, depleted at 700°C and collapsed at 800°C. This is happening
because nickel dissolves more chromium with increasing temperature and the lamella
declines (fig. 2.6 (b)), [4].

To quantify the combined effect of temperature and time of the heat treatment process on

Jaffe tempering parameter was used. Different C values were used to determine the
most suitable ones among 15, 19.5, 20, and 40, for each alloy. As a rule, is that 16­20
used for Ni­base alloys prior to solution treatment processing and further precipitation

category fits this case, the wider range of values mentioned above has been selected
for better processing. The values that gave a higher R2 (based on the method of least
squares) and a strong Pearson’s (r) value (close to 1 or ­1) will indicate a better fit and
a stronger linear relationship, respectively helping optimize the heat treatment process
effectively. This is how the model works by indicating how much of the variance of the de­
pendent variable (Hp), can be explained by the independent variables (HV). The numbers
of fitting parameter C have been derived from regression models, and in order to derive
the linearity, the single linear regression model has been used.

This indicates how much of the variance of the dependent variable (Hp), can be explained

Results and discussion
4.1
About Materials
A delve into the insights acquired from the various processes that took place is going to
be given. The results and observations will be presented by method and by material, in
order. First for DSA760 and then for COS.
Comparisons will be made between each other as well as between the Ni38Cr3.8Al su­
peralloy in an attempt to understand the behaviour of the materials under these specific
conditions. Results of Ni80A are about also to be used for further comparison.
Starting by pointing out the differences related to the processes done by the manufacturer
and to the compositions, in comparison with the aforementioned Ni38Cr3.8Al alloy ([4]).
In this case, the initial HT conditions as well as the chemical composition of the NiCrAl al­
loys differ. An initial forging was done in the selected solution temperature of the material.
This process held to a double­phase region (Ni­Cr), with a fairly high amount of Cr, ap­
proximately 23wt pct and 24wt pct for DSA760 and COS respectively. This means a more
extensive precipitation of α­Cr is expected [9]. Moreover, the precipitation of γ’ acceler­
ates even more the DP reaction [17]. The chemical compositions differ comparatively to
the Ni38Cr3.8Al alloy, as well as to the Nimonic80A, and they are richer. The essential
distinct difference in compositions between the two NiCrAl alloys (DSA and COS), is the
addition of 0.1 wt% of Ce in COS.
4.1.1
DSA760
The initial annealing treatment condition (1050°C) and further ageing (800°C), don’t pro­
mote the formation of lamellar structure. As mentioned above, this is the temperature of
the lamellar destruction [4]. The lamellar still is not expected after the heat treatments
that follow for 99h, 643h, and 1553h at 1023K (750°C), and 973K (700°C) respectively.
The microstructure and the mechanical properties were affected in a different way, com­
pared to the one with lamellar structure. There are changes in strength and ductility are
expected, as well as possibly bigger creep resistance. Furthermore, the α­Cr precipitates
are not distributed homogeneously in the matrix instead, bands of precipitates are ob­
served along the surface of the samples. Those formed stripes were visible even with the
naked eye in most cases. This inhomogeneous structure gives out different mechanical
properties. Lastly, dispersed voids are detectable throughout the surface, for all the HT
conditions. This porosity is because of the manufacturing process. In addition, the whole
structure of the material (matrix along with the precipitates), seems elongated unidirec­
tional and is assumed to be a result of the initial forging and pressing.
4.1.2
COS
On the other hand, the initial annealing condition of COS was at 1043K (770°C ­ for 16h).
In this case, the precipitation leads to a nano­lamellar formation, with lamella depletion
occurring in the late stages of the HT conditions.

Conclusions
The investigation of NiCrAl alloys turned out to be rather complex. The phases con­
tinuously interact with each other to reach equilibrium and this conceals challenges.
The focusing materials of interest were the Ni38.6Cr3.5Al (DSA760) and Ni39.7Cr3.4Al
(COS), and their comparison with the Ni38Cr3.8Al and Nimonic. Both of the alloys were
forged in high temperature, in a two­phase area. Albeit the compositions are almost
identical between them, turned out that they have microstructural differences that in
turn, affects the mechanical properties.
However, the microstructural differences between the two might lay in the different man­
ufacturing processes rather than the composition.
Nimonic is strongly temperature­dependent showing smoother changes, ensuring linear
changes in temperature over time.
The DP of α­Cr and the specific manufacturing processes were used, making DSA the
strongest alloy out of the three, that maintain high hardness with time, especially at
700°C.
DSA shows more inhomogeneous morphology. This is created by the α­Cr precipitates
and gives a big difference hardness in the regions. Therefore, the discontinuous precip­
itation of α­Cr can be considered as the basic strengthening mechanism for the DSA.
For DSA, at 750°C precipitates seems coarser, but doesn’t seems the same at 700C.
Additionally, for both temperatures the α­Cr precipitates appear to become more dis­
persed as time progresses. This dispersion is a mechanism aimed at reducing the
overall energy within the system. These changes in the microstructure indicate a corre­
sponding reduction in the DSA hardness. This aligns with the results of chromium mass
fraction exhibits an increasing trend over time.
One the other hand, is contradictory with the more supersaturated γ matrix, and with the
reduction of volume fraction in Cr with temperature.
For COS, also dispersion of precipitates with time is observed.
DSA and especially COS are more prone to changes. Adapted better and easier to
environmental changes, and can be easier predict.
COS shows a fine lamella structure despite the different (solution treatment) forging
temperature and annealing than Ni38Cr3.8Al alloy.
Since the reduction in hardness is connected with lamellar destruction over time, can
conclude that COS might have less extensive α­Cr precipitation, due to the lower forging
48

temperature, and the main strengthening mechanism is the lamellar structure. Whith the
lamela destruction hardness drops abruptly, especially at 750°C.
COS exhibit a more stable behaviour in hardness resistance.
CeCo5 can contribute to the homogenous nucleation of α­Cr in COS, since is stable, by
promoting additional nucleation sites for precipitation.

further research and more methods would be beneficial to apply in those new materials.
First and foremost, XRD and SEM should be used for all the HT conditions to

Microhardness can be used to determine the local changes and precisely identify
the changes in phases.
