Abstract
The use of mucins in aqueous lubrication comes from their amphiphilic properties. How-
ever the behaviour of such lubricants is strongly dependent of their environment. The inﬂu-
ence of the composition of the buﬀer solution on the complexation between porcine gastric 
mucin (PGM) and polyallylamine hydrochloride (PAH) was reviewed by using phosphate-
based, sulphate-based and zwitterion-based buﬀers with diﬀerent ionic strength. The 
eﬀect of temperature on the lubrication properties of bovine submaxillary mucin (BSM) 
and Pluronic F-127 was also studied. The solutions were analysed by means of dynamic light 
scattering, zeta potential, pin-on-disk tribometry, circular dichroism and optical waveguide 
spectroscopy. The complexation of PGM with PAH brought better lubrication in a 
zwitterion environment compared to bivalent anions-containing solutions, with which PAH 
tended to aggregate. The addition of NaCl however improved the case of sulphate based 
solutions. The increasing temperature went along with an increase in the coeﬃcient of 
friction. This was ascribed to F-127 forming micelles and BSM changing conformation at 
high temperature.

Chapter 1
General Introduction
1.1
Aqueous Lubrication
Lubrication is used to reduce the friction eﬀect occurring between two rubbing surfaces.
The role of the lubricant is to create an intermediate layer that will diminish or remove
asperity contacts between the surfaces. The most used type of lubricant in engineering is
oil, most of the time combined with additives to give it speciﬁc properties. However, it is
a limited resource and not environmental-friendly. Therefore an alternative is investigated
in aqueous lubrication. The advantage of such water-based lubricants is that they are
safe (non-toxic and inﬂammable), cheap and available, not harmful for the environment,
biocompatible and have a high speciﬁc heat capacity, which make them handle friction-
induced heat. Yet their application is limited to temperatures between 0℃and 100 ℃
and to non-ferrous metal systems to prevent corrosion eﬀects.
Aqueous lubrication is
nonetheless well designed for biomedical application.
The main issue remaining is that water lubricants have a poor pressure response com-
pared to that of oil, so the lubricating ﬁlm diﬃcultly withstands the pressure between the
surface and is ﬂushed out of the contact area. It is thus necessary to keep the contact-
ing surfaces hydrophilic in order to maintain the intermediate layer. A solution is to use
brush-like macromolecules, that will exhibit an amphiphilic behaviour: the hydrophobic
part will then stick to the surface while the hydrophilic part will foster the ﬁlm creation.
1.2
Mucin-based solutions as lubricants
Among such amphiphilic molecules are mucins. Mucin is the main component of mucus,
a biological hydrogel that covers some of animals’ tissues to protect and lubricate them.
The mucus layer provides the epithelial tissues with protection from mechanical stress,
dehydration, and bacterial infections.
Mucus also allows the diﬀusion of nutrients or
drugs through the epithelial to the circulatory vessels. The backbone of the mucous gel is
made by mucins which are therefore responsible for most of the mucus properties. Mucin
can be used as coatings to provide anti-fouling protection for biomedical devices or drug
delivery systems. One of the interest for mucin in biotribology as a lubricant comes from
the amphiphilicity it can develop.
More sustainable alternatives to mucins are also developed, as commercially available
mucin is extracted from animals.
An example of such artiﬁcial materials are copoly-
mers that contain both hydrophilic and hydrophobic blocks. However, both mucin and
“mucin-like” materials are strongly dependent on the environment of their origin, thus
their composition and structure may vary from one medium or environmental condition
to the other, and consequently diﬀerent interfacial properties can be expected.

1.3
Goal of the master thesis
This master thesis aimed to study the eﬀect of the environmental parameters on the
lubricating properties of aqueous solutions. It was divided into two separate projects.
The ﬁrst one focused on porcine gastric mucin and its mixing with PAH. The eﬃ-
ciency of this combination regarding lubrication is strongly inﬂuenced by the nature of
the medium the two molecules are diluted into. More speciﬁcally a focus was made on the
eﬀect of the salts contained in the buﬀer solutions. Consequently various buﬀer solutions
were investigated and the interaction between PGM and PAH was analysed under diﬀerent
angles, including both bulk and surface properties. This project will be the object of a
journal publication.
The second project put an emphasis on the inﬂuence of temperature on the lubrication,
surface adsorption and structure of bovine submaxillary mucin and “mucin-like polymer”
Pluronic F-127. The range of temperature studied spanned below and above room tem-
perature.
This report will ﬁrst introduce some of the key elements of the project in order to have
a better understanding of the arguments discussed. A word on lubrication will be followed
by a deﬁnition of the materials analysed, and ﬁnally the principle behind the devices will
enlighten the meaning of the acquired data. The projects will then be exposed one at
a time in a similar fashion. After being ﬁrst introduced in their context, the followed
procedure will be described before exposing and discussing the results. A conclusion will
ﬁnally close the report as a sum up of the outcome of both studies.

Chapter 2
Theory
2.1
Lubrication
Two solid surfaces in contact are actually only in contact at localised points. When one of
the bodies is moving from the other, friction forces will appear at the contact points and
resist to the movement [1]. Two types of friction can be distinguished depending on the
nature of the relative movement, sliding a rolling, but this thesis will only focus on the
sliding movement. A coeﬃcient of friction (µ) is deﬁned as the ratio of the friction forces
over the load applied by one body onto the other.
Dry friction usually involves high coeﬃcients of friction (mostly higher than 0.5) and
results in high friction forces and energy loss. The role of lubricants is to overcome this
issue by acting as a intermediate layer between the surfaces (or tribopair) [2]. Even though
the two surfaces are separated in some cases, that does not necessarily prevent localised
contacts to happen, but the lubricant can weaken them. A distinction is made between
diﬀerent regimes of lubrication (Figure 2.1):
• Hydrodynamic lubrication: the surfaces are separated by a thick ﬁlm, compared
to the surfaces’ asperities, of lubricant.
The surfaces can be considered as non-
deforming as very few asperities contacts are involved.
• Elastohydrodynamic lubrication: the thinner ﬁlm and high contact pressures at the
asperities make that the deformation of the surfaces has to be considered.
• Boundary lubrication: only a thin molecular ﬁlm separates the surfaces.
Figure 2.1: The Stribeck-plot showing the diﬀerent regimes of lubrication: hydrodynamic
(HD), elasto-hydrodynamic (EHD), mixed and boundary lubrication [3].

2.2
Materials
2.2.1
Mucins
Mucin is the molecule constituting the mucus gel present in animals bodies. Its role is to
protect the epithelial tissues of the organs that are exposed to the external environment, as
the respiratory tract, the gastrointestinal tract, the reproductive tract, or the oculo-rhino-
otolaryngeal tracts [4]. It therefore acts as a protective barrier against foreign bodies, but
can also help in the diﬀusion of nutrients or drugs into the vessels. Mucins are glycoproteins
with a high molecular weight and polydispersity in size. They are composed of ca. 80%
of carbohydrates and ca. 20% of a protein core. Both are highly glycosylated regions,
which means that many hydroxyl or other functional groups are attached to them. This
structure can give mucins amphiphilic properties, and also characterises their negative
charge.
The pH and ionic strength of the medium inﬂuences the conformation of mucins as
well as it propensity to form gels for high concentrations. The interesting characteristic of
mucins is its adhesive property or mucoadhesion which is due to electrostatic, hydropho-
bic, and H-bonding interactions.
There are two main commercially available types of
mucin: porcine gastric mucin (PGM) and bovine submaxillary mucin (BSM). Their bio-
compatibility as well as their sticky and slippery behaviour makes them of high interest
as lubricant for biological systems [5].
2.2.2
Polymers
Two kinds of polymer were used in the project: the ﬁrst one was to be used as complexation
agents, which means that was expected to interact with the PGM in some ways. The other
one is a so-called “mucin-like polymer” as it shows good lubrication properties by its own
and a similar structure to that of mucins.
Complexation agents
Two polymers were chosen as potential complexation agents with PGM as they are pos-
itively charged: an electrochemical binding can thus be expected. Indeed both branched
polyethylenimine (b-PEI) and polyallylamine hydrochloride (PAH) contain amine groups
in their monomer: PAH posseds one primary amine while b-PEI has a mixture of primary,
secondary and tertiary amines as a result o fthe branching happening on the amonia. Be-
sides, their low molecular weight are expected to give a small size of these polymers which
may facilitate their interaction with mucin.

Mucin-like polymer: Pluronic F-127
F-127 is a triblock copolymer, composed by a central poly(propylene oxide) (PPO) block
(length 69) coupled with two poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) extremities (length 100) (Figure
2.3). The particularity of this copolymer is its amphiphilicity: PEO is hydrophilic while
PPO is hydrophobic.
Figure 2.3: Chemical structure of Pluronic F-127.
Concentrated aqueous solutions of F-127 are liquid at low temperature and forms a gel
above 20 ℃in a reversible process [6]. The amphiphilicity and self-aggregation properties
allows F-127 unimers to form micelles in speciﬁc conditions. These micelles consist in
aggregates that can take diﬀerent shapes, but always with the PPO component being
contained in a PEO shell. A critical micelle concentration (CMC) and critical micelle
temperature (CMT) are deﬁned to give the frame of conditions for micellisation. CMC
of F-127 in an aqueous solution has been reviewed to be from 0.26 wt% to 0.8 wt% [7].
Figure 2.4 gives the CMT as a function of the concentration of F-127 [8].
Figure 2.4: Values of the CMT as a function of F-127 concentration, as determined by
various approaches.
2.3
Experimental devices
2.3.1
Dynamic Light Scattering
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) relies on the Brownian movement of particles to measure
their size [9]. Indeed, particles in solution randomly move consequently to their collision
with the solvent molecules. The speed of the particles is actually related to their size,
with the biggest particles moving slower than the smaller ones. The size we talk about in
this case is called the hydrodynamic diameter (Dh) and corresponds to the diameter of a
sphere that would diﬀuse the same way the particles do in the same environment.
5
==PAGE:17==
To measure the speed of the particles, a light beam is emitted through the solution
and a detector analyses the scattered light. An illuminated particle scatters the light in all
directions, therefore a medium that contains thousands of particles will result in multiple
light beams falling on the detector. A speckle pattern with black and white spots is then
detected as the scattered light beams interfere with one another (Figure 2.5a). Finally,
with the particles moving, this pattern will change over time, with the white and black
spots growing or diminishing in intensity. The devices measures the rate of the variation
in intensity over time and relates it to the motion of the particles, and eventually their size.
(a) Speckle pattern
(b) DLS Setup
Figure 2.5: Principle of DLS.
DLS therefore plots a size distribution by intensity where the X axis shows the Dh
distribution of the solution and the Y axis shows the relative intensity of the scattered
light. From there, it is possible to access a size distribution by volume and then even by
number. These are calculated as intensity is proportional to the 6th power of the diameter
and the volume proportional to the 3rd power of the radius (Figure 2.6). Recommendations
from the provider say that the intensity plot is realistic enough if it presents one peak,
however if more than one are present, a volume plot will give a more accurate view of
the importance of the second peak. The number plot should be avoided as small errors in
data can dramatically aﬀect that kind of plot [10]. A Z-average size can also be calculated
by the software, but is reliable only for a monodispersed distribution (i.e. centred and
narrow distribution).
Figure 2.6: Illustration of the diﬀerent size distribution plots in the case of a solution
containing two sizes of particles in equal number.

2.3.2
Zeta Potential
The zeta potential is deﬁned as the overall charge a particle acquires in a medium. Any
particle in suspension in an aqueous environment exhibits a surface charge that can exist
for diﬀerent reasons. The main ones are the ionisation of acidic of basic groups at the
surface of the particle, the diﬀerential dissolution of ions present at the surface, or the
adsorption on the particle’s surface of charged species. The surface charge of a particle
deﬁnes the distribution of the surrounding ions and results in an electrical double layer.
The ﬁrst layer is composed of strongly bound ions close to the surface of the particle and
called the Stern layer. The second and outer one is composed by more diﬀused ions and
contains a boundary beneath which the ions are stable and stay with the particle when it
moves. Th zeta potential is the potential at this boundary [11].
The zeta potential of a colloidal system therefore indicates its stability as a low value
(positive or negative) would result in particles ﬂocculating, whereas a high zeta potential
will involve particles that tend to repel each other. The main factors aﬀecting the zeta
potential are the pH and the conductivity of the solution [12].
Figure 2.7: Illustration of the deﬁnition of the zeta potential.
One of the methods to measure the zeta potential of particles in a solution is elec-
trophoresis [13]: the movement of the charged particle is measured as a voltage is applied
accross a pair of electrodes in between which the solution is located. The zeta potential
is then related to the so-called electrophoretic mobility measured by laser Doppler eﬀect.
2.3.3
Circular Dichroism
Circular Dichroism (CD) Spectroscopy is based on the diﬀerence of absorption between
the left and right circularly polarised components of a linearly polarised light beam [14].
These components can then be separated using a quartz-based modulator. After the beam
passes through the sample, each component can be more or less absorbed: if they both
have the same amplitude (i.e. were equally absorbed or not at all) then their combina-
tion will give back the original plain-polarised light. However if one component is more
absorbed than the other, the combination will result in a elliptically polarised radiation
(Figure 2.8). A Circular Dichroism spectroscopy measures for each wavelength either the
ellipticity (in degrees) of the ellipse, or the diﬀerence in absorption of both components;
these two results are actually linked with a numerical relationship.

Figure 2.8: Illustration of the principle of CD. In (I) the combination of the left (L) and
right (R) components gives a linearly polarised radiation, whereas in (II) the diﬀerence of
amplitude between L and R components results in an elliptically polarised radiation.
The part of the molecule that causes the absorption of either component of the radi-
ation is called the chromophore. This chromophore can be optically active, or chiral, if
it is part of an assymetric molecule or given its own structure. In the case of proteins, if
several chromophores are in the same surroundings on the polypeptide backbone, inter-

2.3.4
Pin-on-disk tribometry
A tribometre is used to calculate the coeﬃcient of friction at the interface of two objects
that can be of diﬀerent shapes and materials. One of the most common one is the pin-
on-disk system, which consists of a rotating disk in contact with a pin. A load is applied
on the pin to set the sliding contact pressure, and the tribopair can be immersed into a
lubricant that may or may not change the coeﬃcient of friction. A strain gauge linked
to the pin’s arm then measures the friction force.
The coeﬃcient of friction is ﬁnally
calculated as the ratio between the friction force and the applied load. The evolution of
the coeﬃcient of friction is measured over time and usually ﬂuctuates cyclically, due to
slightly oﬀalignment between the disk and the pin, or other artefacts (Figure 2.9). That
is the reason why an average of the coeﬃcient of friction over a long period of time is
taken as the global value. The time frame should therefore be big enough to encompass a
relatively stable value for at least 90% of the time. The pin is also positioned to a certain
distance from the centre of the disk in order to set the sliding length over the time frame.
In this study, pin-on-disk tribometry has been used with two diﬀerent devices: Part
1 used a pin-on-disk only tribometre where the load and alignment of the sample were
adjusted manually whereas Part 2 used a so-called Micro Traction Machine (MTM). This
machine can be used in a ball-on-disk mode that gives information on the rolling friction;
for the matter of this study, we stuck with the pin-on-disk mode and a sliding contact
conﬁguration. The MTM involves a closed system that can be set to work at diﬀerent
temperatures, and all the alignments and loads are monitored with a computer. It has
to be noted that while MTM allows automation and temperature controlled tribology
measurements, it is not as reliable at low speeds (10 mm/s and below) as the manually
set tribometre.

Optical Waveguide Lightmode Spectroscopy
Optical Waveguide Lightmode Spectroscopy (OWLS) is an optical based technology that
measures the adsorbed mass of particles of a colloidal solution onto a waveguide. To do
so, the device emits a plane-polarised He-Ne laser beam of wavelength 633 nm at diﬀerent
incidence angles through the waveguide until reaching total refraction, where the detector
will receive the biggest signal amplitude (Figure 2.10) [16]. The incidence angle changes
upon the adsorption of macromolecules on and at the vicinities of the waveguide surface
in a liquid medium. The waveguide is made of a 1 mm thick AF 45 glass with a 200-
nm thick Si0.25Ti0.75O2 waveguiding outer layer, that presents nano-features that allow
macromolecules to amass on the surface. The value of the adsorbed mass of molecules per
unit area of the surface of the waveguide is estimated based on de Feijter’s equation [17]:

Chapter 3
Introduction
3.1
Lubricity of PGM
Previous studies have reported the slippery nature of mucins at tribological interfaces
composed of engineering materials, especially those of elastic and hydrophobic surfaces in
aqueous environment [5, 18–27]. This is mainly because mucins are amphiphilic that they
can spontaneously adsorb onto hydrophobic surfaces from water, and form hydrophilic
layer, which is essential for aqueous lubrication. In this sense, mucins behave similarly
with macromolecular surfactants. The two types of commercially available mucins, namely
BSM and PGM, are known to adsorb eﬀectively on hydrophobic surfaces. Interestingly
though, only BSM reveals an eﬀective lubricating property. Despite comparable or higher
amount of adsorption compared to BSM, PGM displays nearly ignorable lubricating eﬀects
at neutral pH condition [23]. Even though PGM shows an improved lubricity at acidic pH
[18], the scope of application would be signiﬁcantly broadened if PGM is rendered slippery
at neutral pH too. Additionally, commercially available BSM is much more expensive
(nearly 800 times) than PGM. Thus, it is highly desirable to ﬁnd means to overcome the
poor lubricity of PGM for a wide range of bioengineering applications.
3.2
Previous studies
As PGM adsorbs onto hydrophobic surfaces in a high amount, yet displays poor lubricity,
it can be proposed that a main problem is insuﬃcient binding strength of PGM with the
underlying substrates. In turn, this also means that an eﬀective way to overcome the
problem is to enhance the binding strength of PGM with the underlying substrates. In
order to maintain the capabilities to supply the lubricating ﬁlms under cyclic tribostress,
it is important to achieve it via “non-covalent” ways.
In covalent bonding, the ﬁlms
rubbed away from a tribointerface cannot be regenerated until covalent bonding reactions
startover [19]. A ﬁrst successful attempt was to mix PGM with chitosan at an acidic
pH (ca. 3), which dramatically improved the lubricating properties of PGM (a reduction
in the coeﬃcient of friction by ca.
two orders of magnitude), with an ideal ratio of
50% mw. This was attributed to that chitosan, which is positively charged at pH 3, is
electrostatically attracted to PGM, which is still negatively charged at the same pH, and
a formation of complexation between them leads to stabilization of the lubricating ﬁlms
[25]. Nevertheless, this synergistic lubricating eﬀect was observed at acidic environment
because of the poor solubility of chitosan at neutral pH. Based on the same principles,
it was expected that other amine-based polymers may act similarly with chitosan even
at neutral pH. A number of other polycations were tested in this context [26], but only
b-PEI behaved as expected, whereas many others, including PAH and poly(L-lysine), did
not reveal suﬃcient synergistic lubricating eﬀects by mixing with PGM. Among them,
12
==PAGE:24==
it was noted that the hydrodynamic size of PAH (ca. 500 nm) was too high compared
to that expected from its molecular weight (ca. 15 kDa), even before mixing with PGM
solution. A control experiment of measuring the hydrodynamic size of PAH in distilled
water, namely < 10 nm, indicated that this extraordinary large size of PAH was related
to the fact that a phosphate based saline (PBS) was used in the experiments mentioned
above. Thus, it is reasonable to suspect that phosphate ions in PBS may interact and
form complexes with PAH and hinder the associative interaction with PGM afterwards.
However, no systematic studies on the role of salts in buﬀer solutions in complexation
between PGM and polycations and synergistic lubrication properties have been conducted
to date.
3.3
Purpose and plan of the study
Thus, the major interest of this study is to investigate the inﬂuence of diﬀerent buﬀers
in the associative interaction and synergistic lubrication between PAH and PGM. The
selected buﬀers were PBS, sulphate based saline (SBS) and HEPES, a zwitterion-based
solution. SBS was chosen to investigate if complex formation between PAH and divalent
phosphate ions is a general behavior observed from other divalent ions too. In addition
to PAH, b-PEI was also studied in these three types of buﬀers, as previous studies on the
interaction between PGM and b-PEI have been carried out in PBS buﬀer only. For each of
buﬀers, the concentration of NaCl was varied to investigate the role of ionic strength. The
analytical tools in this study included dynamic light scattering and zeta potentials to trace
the change in the hydrodynamic diameter of the particles and surface charge characteristics
in diﬀerent buﬀers as well as upon mixing with PGM. The lubricating properties of the
PGM:polymer mixtures were assessed using pin-on-disk tribometry with a PDMS/PDMS
tribopair. Finally, optical waveguide lightmode spectroscopy was used to correlate surface
adsorption properties with the lubricating properties at the PDMS/PDMS interface.

Methods
4.1
Buﬀer solution
Three diﬀerent saline buﬀer solutions groups were prepared. Firstly, a series of phosphate
buﬀered saline (PBS) was prepared by varying the source of phosphate ions and total ionic
strength. For instance, PBS was prepared by dissolving PBS tablets purchased from VWR
(Lutterworth, UK) in milli-Q grade water, and was denoted to as “PBS 1”. According to
the manufacturer, PBS 1 contains 10mM Na2HPO4, 1.8 mM KH2PO4, 137 mM NaCl,
and 2.7 mM KCl. 500 mM of NaCl was further added to PBS 1 to prepare a PBS with a
higher ionic strength, and it was denoted as “PBS 1+1”. Alternatively, PBS was prepared
also by dissolving constituent salts in milliQ-grade water according to a standard recipe
(10mM Na2HPO4 and 1.8 mM KH2PO4, except for 2.7 mM KCl), and it was denoted as
“PBS 0”. 140 mM of NaCl was added to PBS 0 to prepare “PBS 0+1”, that is comparable
to PBS 1 in the ionic strength. 500 mM NaCl was added to PBS 0 to prepare another
PBS with a high ionic strength, and it was denoted as “PBS 0+2”. Secondly, two types
of HEPES buﬀers were prepared too. “HEPES 0” was prepared by dissolving 10 mM
of 4-(2-hydroxyethyl)piperazine-1-ethanesulfonic acid (VWR, Lutterworth, UK). HEPES
0+1 was prepared by addition of 140 mM of NaCl to HEPES 0. Lastly, a series of sulphate
based buﬀer solutions was prepared by dissolving 10 mM Na2SO4 in Milli-Q grade water
(denoted as “SBS 0”), and additionally adding 140 mM or 500 mM NaCl to SBS 0, and
denoted as SBS 0+1 or SBS 0+2, respectively. All chemicals were from Sigma-Aldrich
(Denmark Aps, Brøndby, Denmark) unless otherwise mentioned. The list of compositions
of all buﬀers are shown in Table 1. The pH of all buﬀers was adjusted to 7.0 to 7.4 by
adding HCl or NaOH.
4.2
Mucins, polymers and solutions
PGM, PAH and PEI were also purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Denmark Aps, Brøndby,
Denmark). The PGM was of Type III: partially puriﬁed, bound sialic acid 0.5 - 1.5%.
The molecular weight (Mw) of PAH was ca. 15 kDa (product code: 1001791626) and
denoted as PAH. The molecular weight (Mn) of b-PEI was ca. 10 kDa (product code:
101685708). All materials were used without any further processing after receiving from
the manufacturer.
All polymer and PGM solutions were prepared by dispersing in respective buﬀer solu-
tions overnight at the concentration of 1 mg/mL. PGM solutions were then centrifuged at
6000 RPM for 30 minutes. The non-dissolving ﬁbrous precipitates were excluded and only
the solution parts were used for further experiments. The PGM:Polymer mixture solutions
were prepared by thoroughly mixing the two stock solutions at a 1:1 v/v ratio (w/w ratio
too) prior to each experiment, hence yielding a total concentration of 1 mg/mL.

Zeta potential
The zeta potentials of PGM, PAH and PGM:PAH solutions in diﬀerent buﬀers were char-

4.5
Pin-on-disk Tribometry
Tribological tests were run on a pin-on-disk tribometre (CSM, Peseux, Switzerland). A
loaded pin and a rotating disk were immerged in 2 mL of solution in a tribocup and the
sliding force was measured at controlled rotation speeds. The radius of sliding track was
3 mm from the centre of the disk. The external load was applied with a dead weight and
was 1 N, equivalent to a contact pressure of 0.35 MPa for a PDMS/PDMS tribopair (see
below). The coeﬃcient of friction was then calculated as the ratio between the friction
force and the load, i.e. µ = friction force/load. The sliding speed was varied between 0.25
and 100 mm/s with at least 20 rotations at each speed. The data acquisition frequency
was set to 1 Hz.

lubricity did not improve in PBS 0 suggests that NaCl did not play a signiﬁcant role (at
least within the ionic strength range of 10 to 150 mM) in the aggregation between PGM
and phosphates, and consequently the lubricating properties. In contrast, the mixtures
in HEPES (with or without 140 mM NaCl) showed a great improvement in the lubricity
over the entire speed range. This supports the assumption that an aggregation between
PAH and phosphate ions in PBS is chieﬂy responsible for the poor lubricity in that con-
dition. The DLS data shown in Figures 5.4-5.5 above also support that the contrasting
lubricating properties of PGM:PAH mixtures in PBS vs. HEPES are mainly due to the
presence/absence of pre-aggregation with salts in respective buﬀer.

[26] have shown that despite a large amount of adsorbed mass of PAH (or PAH:phosphate
aggregates) in PBS, the lubricating eﬀect is ignorable, which implies the weak interaction
forces with PDMS surface.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The eﬀect of mixing PGM and PAH on the lubricating properties of the solution have
been studied over diﬀerent media. The change in particle size distribution, zeta potential,
friction coeﬃcient and surface adsorption were analysed. Following previous studies that
proposed a complexation between PGM and b-PEI in PBS based on electrostatic attrac-
tion, this phenomenon seemed to be present in HEPES solutions as well given the similar
size distributions. Consequently, a good lubrication of PGM:b-PEI mixtures was observed
in all buﬀer solutions. PAH behaved diﬀerently depending on the solution: a high Dh in
PBS was opposed to the smaller one in HEPES, and ascribed to the interaction and ag-
gregation between PAH and the phosphates of the solution. By varying the ionic strength
of PBS it was shown that the size distribution of PAH tended to a smaller and monodis-
persed value with increasing concentration of NaCl, notably because of its screening eﬀect
that delay the aggregation of PAH with the phosphates. Similar observations were made
for SBS solutions, indicating a broader role of bivalent anions in the aggregation of PAH.
The zeta potential of the mixtures were, excepted HEPES 0, contained between the
potential of their components, and the zeta potential of PAH was smaller in HEPES than
in PBS or SBS, even though the potential measured in the latter two may actually be that
of PAH:phosphates aggregates.
As expected from the previous study, the smaller size of PAH allowed an eﬀective
complexation with PGM and thus a good lubrication to take place in HEPES compared
to PBS. The addition of NaCl did not change the lubrication of the mixture for HEPES
and PBS solutions. However, the lubricity of the mixture in SBS increased dramatically
with the addition of NaCl.
Finally two characteristics of an eﬃcient adsorbed ﬁlm on a PDMS surface were iden-
tiﬁed. Indeed such a ﬁlm should show both thickness and strength in order to minimise
the negative eﬀect of the boundary lubrication of two PDMS surfaces as well as sustain
the shear stress in the sliding contact. PAH had a higher adsorption on PDMS surfaces
in PBS 1 than in HEOES 0+1, however this may again be the eﬀect of PAH:phosphate
aggregates in PBS 1, whereas PAH monomers alone adsorbed on the surface in HEPES
0+1. After mixing, a high adsorption was observed in HEPES 0+1, which was linked to
the strong complexation of PAH with PGM, resulting in a strong lubricating ﬁlm. The
low adsorption in PBS 1 was also consistent with the lubricating properties of the mixture
in this medium.

Introduction
7.1
Eﬀect of temperature on lubrication
Temperature is one of the most important environmental parameters that aﬀect ﬂuid
lubricating properties. For instance, as temperature increases, the viscosity of most ﬂuids
decreases following an Ahrrenius model, therefore the eﬃcacy of elasto-hydrodynamic or
hydrodynamic lubrication is expected to be deteriorated.

where η and η0 are viscosities at elevated and ambient temperatures.
This is practically important issue in real tribosystems, as most bearing systems op-
erate at higher temperatures than room temperature. Moreover, even if the average or
overall temperature of a system remains low, local temperature at the tribological contact
region can be much higher due to local heating arising from friction.
The importance of temperature for ﬂuid lubrication is thus general for all ﬂuid lu-
bricants. Because of relatively short history of aqueous lubrication, however, the role of
temperature on aqueous lubrication has been much less studied to date [6, 28–30]. As well
known, aqueous lubrication is typically achieved via boundary lubrication because waters
capabilities to increase its viscosity under pressure, i.e. pressure coeﬃcient of viscosity, is
too low. In other words, that most aqueous lubrication functions involve surface-active
additives that are adsorbing onto tribopair surfaces and lower shear strength. Thus, the
inﬂuence of temperature on the eﬃcacy of aqueous lubrication is more complex because
not only the change of viscosity of ﬂuid (water), but also the changes of surface adsorption
and conformation of additives at the surfaces as a function of temperature should be taken
into account.
A list of possible parameters that aﬀect aqueous lubricating properties as a function
of temperature is shown below. With increasing temperature,
• the viscosity drops, thus the formed ﬁlm will be easily washed away.
• the adsorption onto the tribopair surface can increase, leading to a thicker molecular
ﬁlm, or diminish, resulting in a weak lubrication ﬁlm.
• the conformation of the particles in solution changes and may be more or less
favourable to lubrication, depending on each additives.
• the formation of micelles is facilitated
26
==PAGE:38==
Thus, it is necessary to investigate not only surface properties but also bulk conforma-
tional properties of additives to understand the inﬂuence of temperature on the lubricating
properties.
7.2
Goal and plan of the study
In this study, we have selected two types of macromolecules that are known to be good
aqueous lubricant additives, namely a poly(ethylene oxide)-poly(propylene oxide)-block-
poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO-PPO-PEO) and BSM, both of which show amphiphilic char-
acteristics. Pluronic F-127 was selected as a triblock copolymer. The comparative study
on these two amphiphilic macromolecules in response to temperature change is interesting
because water solubility of the two with increasing temperature is expected to be diﬀerent.
For example, BSM is expected to be more dissolvable in water at high temperature, but
F-127 is expected to be less dissolvable in water at high temperature. This is because,
ﬁrstly, the portion of hydrophobic patches is more signiﬁcant for F-127 compared to BSM,
and secondly, even hydrophilic moieties in F-127, i.e.
PEO blocks, are known to lose
water solubility with increasing temperature. This is indebted from the formation of a
unique cage-like structure of EO units with a few water molecules in its solubility, and
this structure is collapsing at high temperature.

Mucins, polymers and solutions

nm using a path length of 1 mm, to minimise scattering. These records had a step size
and bandwidth of 1 nm. 5 repeats were made for each set and the average graph was
calculated. The CD signal of the buﬀer background was also measured and substracted
from the average data.
The temperature of the solution in the cuvette was controlled by a CD 250 temperature-
controlled cuvette holder (Quantum Northwest, Inc., Liberty Lake, WA) coupled with a
AWC100 Recirculating Cooler (JULABO GmbH, Seelbach, Germany). The acquisition
were started after the stabilisation of the temperature of the cuvette indicated by a led
on the device and an equilibration time of 5 minutes. The CD spectroscopy was run for
temperatures of 1, 10, 20, 37 and 60 ℃.

Conclusion
The inﬂuence of temperature on the bulk (particle size distribution and structural con-
formation) and surface (coeﬃcient of friction and adsorption) properties of BSM and two
diﬀerent concentrations of PEO-PPO-PEO copolymer F-127 was reviewed in the tem-
perature range of 1 ℃to 60 ℃. The two macromolecules responded diﬀerently to the
evolution of temperature. The friction coeﬃcient of F-127 solutions was increased by the
formation of micelles above ca. 26 ℃characterised by a bigger size of the particles in solu-
tion. These micelles adsorbed less to PDMS surfaces than F-127 monomers, and therefore
prevented the creation of a strong hydrophilic ﬁlm on the surfaces. The worse lubrication
of BSM at high temperature was identiﬁed to be ascribed to the structure change of the
macromolecules. Indeed, their structure tended to a disordered state with increasing tem-
perature that may have aﬀected the aqueous lubrication ﬁlm formed at the PDMS surface.
However this ﬁlm thickness was steady over temperature, as the adsorption of BSM was
similar from 20 ℃to 60 ℃. Finally, the viscosity of water played an underlining role in
the increase of friction over temperature for all solutions.

Chapter 11
General Conclusion
This master thesis highlighted some of the eﬀects of environmental parameters on the
lubrication of aqueous solutions.
The mixing of PGM and PAH showed to be more eﬃcient in zwitterionic solutions
(HEPES) rather than bivalent anions-containing ones. Indeed PAH tended to form ag-
gregates with such anions and this resulted in a poorer lubrication, which could also be
linked to the worse surface adsorption of the mixture. The addition of NaCl in the buﬀer
solution however managed to prevent the aggregation of PAH in PBS and especially in
SBS, in which the lubrication dramatically improved.
The temperature had two diﬀerent inﬂuences on the lubrication of BSM and F-127: on
the one hand F-127 formed micelles at higher temperature. This resulted in a worse sur-
face adsorption and lubrication, and therefore less ﬁlm forming at the PDMS surfaces. On
the other hand, BSM conformation shifted from a well structured network to a disordered
one, which constituted a weaker lubrication ﬁlm. Finally, the temperature also modiﬁed
the solution’s viscosity, which had a role to play in the increase of friction over temperature.
Investigations regarding the environment’s inﬂuence on the lubrication of mucins are
still to be run. Particularly, the complexation of PGM with PAH can be studied over
other buﬀer solutions in order to understand better the complexation conditions of the
two elements. Similarly, investigating in other polycations or other macromolecules can
also give more information on why PGM is able to form a complex or not with one entity
and in speciﬁc environments. The temperature inﬂuence study should be improved with
a more reliable OWLS procedure and with other solution concentrations. Indeed, it could
be interesting to see if the change of structure of BSM still occurs for higher or lower
concentrations.
