Abstract
Psoriasis is a chronic inflammatory disease that can progress to Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA), causing
joint inflammation in addition to skin symptoms [1]. Diagnosing PsA is challenging due to over-
lapping symptoms with other rheumatic diseases and the lack of well-defined diagnostic criteria
and biomarkers [2]. This thesis explores the use of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data
and supervised machine learning to predict subject phenotypes and diagnose PsA. The aim is
to investigate existing biomarkers, process scRNA-seq data, explore feature selection techniques,
and evaluate the feasibility of using the super learner framework for phenotype prediction. The
methods involve quality control, preprocessing, and cell type annotation. Additionally, various
techniques, such as cell type expression values, differentially expressed genes (DEG), feature se-
lection, and dimensionality reduction by PCA, are applied to address high dimensionality while
retaining biological information content. Using The super learner framework an ensemble of
algorithms is created and used for prediction, and the biological relevance of selected features is
assessed [3]. The results demonstrate the potential of scRNA-seq data to predict subject pheno-
types for PsA, cutaneous psoriasis (PsO), and healthy individuals. The best model based on cell
type-specific DEGs had an accuracy of 0.901 in predicting the disease phenotypes. The model
achieved an AUC of 0.99 in distinguishing PsA from healthy subjects. all models had an AUC ≥
0.778, for differentiating between PsA and healthy subjects. Furthermore, potential biomarkers
and gene clusters associated with PsA are identified, including a set of 14 genes with potential
diagnostic relevance. However, further validation and replication studies are required to confirm
these findings. The study contributes to exploring the disease pattern of PsA and highlights
the potential of scRNA-seq data for diagnosis and biomarker identification, facilitating future
advancements in single-cell analysis.

Introduction
Psoriasis is a chronic inflammatory disease, and the most dominant type is cutaneous psoriasis
(PsO), characterized by red, scaly patches on the skin, [4]. In approximately 30% of psoriasis
cases, the disease progresses into Psoriasis Arthritis (PsA), which not only affects the skin but
also leads to arthritis symptoms and joint involvement. However, diagnosing PsA can be chal-
lenging due to its diverse and overlapping symptoms with other rheumatic diseases [5]. Early
diagnosis is important for improving treatment outcomes and preventing further disease pro-
gression. The lack of well-defined diagnostic criteria and biomarkers further complicates the
diagnosis of PsA [6]. To enhance diagnostic accuracy and gain a deeper understanding of the
disease, gene expression data can be utilized to explore the genes involved. One specific approach
is using Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data, which provides a high-resolution view of
cellular heterogeneity [7].
One method to utilize scRNA-seq data for diagnostic purposes is supervised machine learning,
where clinical outcomes can be predicted based on selected features.
Ensemble learning can
be used to overcome the issue of identifying the optimal model without prior knowledge. For
example, the super learner framework can be utilized, which builds an ensemble based on a broad
selection of algorithms by cross-validation [3].
This project aims to explore how scRNA-seq data can be used for subject phenotype predic-
tion, specifically focusing on diagnosing PsA using a scRNA-seq dataset of Peripheral Blood
Mononuclear Cells (PBMC)s from healthy controls, Cutaneous Psoriasis (PsO) patients, and
PsA patients. To accomplish that, the following sub-aims are established:
• Investigate existing biomarkers for PsA and PsO and the existing machine learning method-
ology for scRNA-seq.
• Examine the steps included in processing scRNA-seq data to obtain a high-quality dataset.
• Explore different feature selection and dimensionality reduction techniques to identify the
most informative genes and cell populations for discriminating between PsA, PsO, and
healthy individuals.
• Evaluate the feasibility of predicting the phenotype using the super learner framework
and assess the biological relevance of the features contributing to the prediction and their
potential as novel biomarkers.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Psoriasis
Psoriasis is a chronic immune-mediated inflammatory disease that affects 2-4% of the population
worldwide [4]. It is mainly characterized by patients having scaly patches of skin, which can
be red, inflamed, and itchy.
The disease can occur at any age but typically begins in early
adulthood, equally distributed between men and women [1].
The symptoms of psoriasis, such as itching, pain, and scaling, can cause discomfort and negatively
affect the quality of life for patients. In addition, psoriasis is associated with an increased risk of
other health conditions, such as cardiovascular disease and depression, which can further reduce
the patient’s quality of life [8]. The most common diagnostic criteria include the presence of
characteristic skin lesions, such as plaques, and the absence of other skin conditions that have
symptoms similar to psoriasis [4, 1]. There is currently no cure for psoriasis. The treatment
involves a reduction of symptoms and slowing the disease progression. The medication given to
patients is a combination of topical and systemic treatments, which reduce inflammation and
symptoms and help prevent disease progression [1, 9].
Different psoriatic disease phenotypes exist. One definition of subtypes is; a synovial predominant
type, an entheaeal, and a cutaneous. These are defined by an interplay of individual genotypes
and triggers [10]. The most common is the cutaneous type which is seen in 80-90% of psoriasis
cases, affecting the skin with plaque. This type is the focus type of this thesis. PsO can develop
into the more severe disease PsA [1].
2.1.1
Psoriasis Arthritis
Up to 30% of psoriasis patients develops PsA over time [11, 5]. PsA involves joint inflammation
and can lead to joint damage over time. In some cases, PsA develops without signs of psoriasis
[2].
PsA typically affects the small joints of the hands and feet but can also affect larger joints such
as knees, hips, and shoulders. it is characterized by bone erosion and new bone formation around
the affected joints. The symptoms of PsA include joint pain, stiffness, swelling, fatigue, and nail
change. The severity of PsA can vary widely from person to person, as well as the symptoms,
making it difficult to make a precise diagnosis [2]. Diagnosis of PsA requires an evaluation by
a rheumatologist to avoid misdiagnosis and unnecessary treatment. It may involve blood tests,
X-rays, or scans of the joints, and evaluation of the patient’s symptoms and medical history.
Further other types of arthritis, such as rheumatoid arthritis and osteoarthritis have to be ruled
out [1]. Early diagnosis of PsA is important to reduce the risk of developing complications and
start effective treatment [6].

2.1.2
Pathogenesis
The pathogenesis of psoriasis is not yet fully understood, but it is believed to be driven by
a combination of genetic and environmental factors that result in a self-sustained and chronic
immune response [13]. Psoriasis is known to be T-cell mediated, and several theories regarding
its underlying cause exist. For instance, studies have found that the antimicrobial peptide LL37,
which serves as an antimicrobial defense mechanism in damaged skin, can become an autoantigen
in psoriasis and lead to T cell activation, known as the Koebner’s phenomenon. Other triggering
events, such as trauma and medication, can also contribute to developing psoriasis [13, 8].
The immune cells and processes involved in the inflammation are shown in Figure 2.1, where
the interplay between the skin and joint inflammation associated with PsO and PsA are shown.

Figure 2.1: Immune cells and processes involved in PsO and PsA. Genetic factors are displayed in the

The immune system is activated by, for example, the autoantigen LL37, which is digested and
displayed on the MHC complex of an antigen-presenting cell. A T-cell’s T- cell receptor binds
to the complex and recognizes the LL37 as being foreign. This activates an immune cascade,

Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells
PPBMCs are identified as any blood cell with a round nucleus, and include a mixture of cells
composed of lymphocytes (T cells, B cells, and NK cells), monocytes, and dendritic cells obtained
from human blood. Lymphocytes are the most abundant, constituting 70–90%. Upon recognition
of antigens, some PBMCs differentiate and develop effector functions [17, 18]. Monocytes are
phagocytic cells that digest foreign substances and recruit immune cells to sites of infection.
Dendritic cells are antigen-presenting cells and play a crucial role in initiating the adaptive
immune system by activating the T cells. T cells identify and destroy infected cells. B cells
produce antibodies to neutralize pathogens and provide long-term immune memory [19]. Each
mentioned cell type contains several subcategories, representing different cell states within the
cell type. Throughout this thesis, the term ’cell types’ will be used generally unless referring to
a specific cell state.
2.3
Single Cell RNA Sequencing
Throughout the last decade, bulk RNA sequencing has contributed to exploring the genome, and
biological interplay across a broad range of fields, including transcript annotation, finding and
annotating new genes, gene isoforms, and other transcripts. Bulk RNA sequencing data represent
an average gene expression across cells and cannot distinguish between individual cells [20].
Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq), which was introduced in 2009, enables gene expression
analysis at the single-cell level, allowing insights into cellular heterogeneity and the underlying
molecular mechanisms [7, 21]. It provides a snapshot of the transcriptome of each individual
cell, providing a high-resolution view of cellular gene expression patterns. The technology can
identify cell types and states, define developmental trajectories and investigate the molecular
mechanisms underlying diseases [21].
scRNA-seq follows the same basic principles as bulk RNA sequencing, which involves the isolation
of mRNA from a sample, conversion of RNA to cDNA, sequencing of the cDNA, and bioinformatic
analysis of the sequencing data [20]. However, scRNA-seq requires modifications to the standard
protocol to account for the small amount of starting material per cell and the technical challenges
of sequencing single cells. One modification is the labeling of each cell by Unique Molecular
Identifier (UMI). UMIs are short nucleotide sequences added to cDNA during library preparation
to distinguish between transcripts originating from the same gene in the same cell. Another
modification is the capturing of each cell using plate or droplet-based methods, allowing for the
parallel processing of thousands of cells [7]. A flowchart of the five steps involved in cell capturing
using droplets and sequencing is shown in Figure 2.2.

The first step describes the cell capturing. A dissociated cell mixture is fed into a microfluidic
device, together with primer-coated gel beads. Gel Bead-in-EMulsion (GEM)s are then formed
by capturing cells and beads. Each GEM contains a single cell and a barcode used as UMI. In the
next step, mRNA is captured, reverse-transcribed to cDNA, and amplified. After amplification,
the droplets are broken, and the cDNA is collected and processed to generate a sequencing
library. This includes fragmentation, end repair, adapter ligation, and size selection. The final
step includes sequencing using, for example, Novaseq 6000. The barcodes present in each read are
used to assign the read to its originating cell, allowing for the reconstruction of the transcriptional
profile of each individual cell in the sample [7, 23].
Once sequencing is complete, the raw reads are processed where low-quality reads are removed,
and quantification is carried out by using a reference genome or transcriptome [23]. The output
data is count matrices with the dimensions of cells × number of transcripts. The data is sparse
because there is a low amount of starting material per cell, where most of the genes remain
undetected. The zero can either be due to the gene not being expressed in the cell (real zero),
or the gene was expressed but not detected (dropout). This issue leads to a cell-to-cell variation
that is not biological but arises because of a technical issue [23].
The standard protocol for preprocessing scRNA-seq data involves quality control of the data,
removal of droplets, normalization, and clustering to identify subpopulations of cells and analyze
gene expression patterns [23].

2.4
Super Learner
Machine learning is the study of developing algorithms that can learn patterns and relationships
from data by capturing the underlying distribution in the data. Machine learning is categorized
into two main types: supervised learning, where an outcome variable is predicted based on
features given in the data, and unsupervised learning, which can be used for clustering and
identifying patterns in data [25].
Several different algorithms have been developed to achieve accurate predictions of an outcome
variable using a set of features. However, one major challenge in machine learning is the bias-
variance tradeoff. A model with high variance may perform well on the training data but fail to
generalize to new data, while a model with high bias may not capture the underlying relationships
in the data and miss important patterns in the data. A model with high variance results in
overfitting, which is more likely in highly flexible models, where the model is complex and might
fit too closely to the training data [26].
Ensemble learning is an approach in machine learning to overcome the limitations of a single
model. It is impossible to know which learner will perform best for a given prediction problem
and dataset. In ensembled learning, multiple models are combined to obtain a better perfor-
mance. Super learner is an ensemble learning method that combines multiple algorithms by
cross-validation to obtain a more accurate prediction model. An overview of the steps in the
algorithm is illustrated in Figure 2.3. The steps within super learner involve creating a li-
brary of selected algorithms. First, each algorithm is trained on the whole dataset. Second,
cross-validation is used to obtain predictions for subsets of the data and create a weight for
each algorithm within the library. Lastly, the weights and predictions on the whole dataset are
combined into a final super learner[3].

2.5.1
Machine Learning in scRNA-seq
Machine learning has been applied in various ways to process scRNA-seq data. For example, di-
mensionality reduction techniques like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and Uniform Man-
ifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) have been used to capture the biological variability
of the data while reducing its complexity. Additionally, variational autoencoders have been used
to model the complex non-linear relationships between genes [32]. Machine learning has also been
used to remove technical variation within the data, such as batch effects, amplification bias, and
dropout, while still retaining the biological variation, using unsupervised clustering techniques
like hierarchical clustering, k-means, and t-SNE [32, 33]. However, accurately clustering cells in
high-dimensional and sparse scRNA-seq data remains challenging [32]. Assigning the sequenced
cells to the correct cell type is another important task, and several approaches have been inves-
tigated, including several clustering algorithms, support vector machines, random forests, and
neural networks [23]. Due to the high heterogeneity of scRNA-seq data, identifying cell types
remains difficult. To address this challenge, several methods, such as marker gene-based ap-
proaches and cell type-specific gene expression analysis, have been proposed. Machine learning
has also been used to identify new biomarkers and diagnostic genes [34].
Regarding machine learning algorithms, that can predict subject-based diagnostic outcomes from
scRNA-seq data, only a few papers have investigated this area. One reason for this is the data
structure, where the observations are cells rather than individuals, which makes it difficult to
apply existing machine learning frameworks while preserving the cell variability within each
subject. Bryan and Thomson et al. [35] suggest an algorithm called CloudPred for predicting
disease phenotypes. The algorithm estimates the abundance of each cell population in a patient
by modeling each patient’s data as a mixture of Gaussians. The abundance serves as a bag-of-
features and is used as the predictive feature for the patient’s phenotype [35]. Another phenotype
prediction method, called ScRAT, has been developed by using attention-based neural networks
[36]. The model uses a mixup module to increase the number of training samples. Then the most
informative cells for each phenotype are found without relying on cell type annotations. [36]. In
addition, Zeng Feng et al. [37] developed a model where disease phenotypes are predicted based
on a deep generative probabilistic model, and the distribution is used as the predictive feature
for SVM for phenotype prediction [37]. Other studies have predicted phenotypes by aggregating
the counts to obtain data similar to bulk RNA, sequencing data, and then applying machine
learning frameworks [38].
Overall, while machine learning has been applied to various aspects of scRNA-seq data analy-
sis, further studies are needed to develop effective predictive algorithms for diagnosis based on
scRNA-seq data.

The process of transforming the initial PMBC scRNA-seq data into a final processed dataset
involved several steps. Firstly, quality control (QC) was performed to filter out cells with low
sequencing quality.
Secondly, preprocessing steps, including scaling and normalization, were
applied. Thirdly, the identification of doublets within subjects was performed. Lastly, the cell
type of each cell in the dataset was determined. These steps significantly influenced the quality
of the input data for the modeling process and are crucial in achieving a high-quality dataset.
The primary objective of the modeling phase was to predict the disease phenotype of the subjects
based on the scRNA-seq dataset. To accomplish this, it was decided to create aggregated counts
for each subject to obtain a dataset with subjects as rows and genes as features instead of the
dimensions cells × genes. The aggregated dataset consisted of 81 subjects and more than 100,000
features, yielding a high-dimensional dataset (p»n) that would lead to overfitting if used directly
for modeling.
To address the challenge of high dimensionality while retaining informative features, various
techniques were applied. These included cell type expression values, identification of Cell Type

4.1
Data Processing
The data were obtained from GEO, and a Python script was created to obtain the desired file
structure and filenames necessary for loading the data into R using the Seurat package. Seurat,
is an R package specifically designed for processing scRNA-seq data and offers a range of functions
that enables easy data processing [46].
Each of the 28 sequenced samples was loaded into R as Seurat Objects, using the corresponding
count matrix, barcode file, and feature file, and merged with the corresponding cell metadata file.
The Seurat Object consists of multiple slots, including a count matrix slot containing gene counts
per cell and a slot for metadata stored as a data frame. Throughout the processing, additional
data is saved in the Seurat Object, keeping track of the data processing. The 28 samples were
distributed in seven batches, each containing four runs. Each of the 28 samples will be handled
separately in all data processing steps. Each sample is a mixture of n subjects from the data.
4.1.1
Quality Control
The processing of the data followed the guidelines outlined in Luecken et.al [23]. Initially, cell QC
was performed to ensure that all cellular barcode data corresponded to viable cells. Barcharts,
scatter plots, and violin plots were used to set qualified thresholds for the removal of low-quality
cells. Cells with fewer than 200 measured genes, more than 15% mitochondrial genes, and a
novelty score of less than 0.8 were removed.
The novelty score is a measure for the complexity within each cell and is calculated as
log10(number_of_genes)
log10(number_of_transcripts). It was observed that all cells with fewer than 500 detected transcripts
were already removed from the dataset. Visualizations were examined before and after the QC to
ensure removal of outlier cells while retaining high-quality cells. Additionally, cells not assigned
to a subject and cells belonging to an undefined subject X01 were also removed from the dataset.

Preprocessing
After removing low-quality cells from the dataset, the Seurat function SCTransform was applied
with default settings. This step involved normalizing the data, identifying highly variable genes,
and regressing out technical variation. Subsequently, a linear dimensional reduction PCA was
performed, followed by clustering of the data using the built-in Seurat functions FindNeighbors

4.1.3
Doublet Removal
To eliminate doublets (two cells in one droplet), DoubletFinder was applied. The DoubletFinder
algorithm works by creating artificial doublets from the data (25% of cells) and subsequently
performs PCA to describe the similarity between real and artificial cells in a low-dimensional
space. Real doublets are identified as the top-n cells with the highest number of artificial doublets
as their k-nearest neighbors, where n represents the expected number of real doublets [47].
It was not possible to obtain the expected number of real doublets from the clinical study, and the
n rate was therefore determined manually, based on the demuxlet identified doublets, for each
sample. The rate was calculated by dividing the fraction of doublets identified by demuxlet by
the number of subjects within the sample minus 1. Additionally, the k parameter, which defines
the neighborhood size, was also manually determined for each sample using the ground truth
demuxlet data. A range of k values and proportions of artificial doublets were examined, and
the final k value was chosen based on visualizations of the mean-variance normalized bimodality
coefficient (BCmetric) and mean AUC for a range of k values. The peak value indicated by mean
AUC was used when possible (Figure 4.2a), otherwise, the BCmetric was used (Figure 4.2b).
The charts used for k estimation for the remaining samples can be found in Appendix A.

4.1.4
Cell Type Annotation
The cell type of each cell was determined using two tools: the R packages Azimuth and SingleR.
Both tools rely on mapping to annotated PBMC cell datasets, with the reference dataset Hao
and Hao et al. [48]. The annotations from both tools were compared, and cells with matching
annotations were labeled accordingly.
For the remaining cells, the cell type was determined using the results from Azimuth, which
performs best in comparative studies [49]. The confidence scores from Azimuth were used to
calculate an upper threshold for each cell type by calculating the Median Absolute Deviation
(MAD). Cells with scores below the cell type-specific MAD threshold were removed from the
dataset. However, if the MAD threshold for a cell type exceeded 0.75, an upper threshold of 0.75
was used instead to prevent the exclusion of cells Azimuth had assigned with high confidence.
Cell types categorized as platelets and erythrocytes and Cell types with fewer than 100 cell counts,
including AXL+ dendritic cell, CD8 Proliferating, and innate lymphoid cell, were removed.
Furthermore, all cells belonging to subjects denoted as PSX were excluded. The steps outlined
in Section 4.1.2 were carried out again for the final dataset.

4.2.1
Cell Type Expression Dataset
One approach to overcome the number of features was to use the average expression of each cell
type across subjects as features. The workflow to accomplish that is illustrated in Figure 4.4.
The processed data were split into train and test sets, and the UMI counts were aggregated based
on cell type and subject, for each set respectively. This resulted in a dataset with genes as rows
and celltype_subject as columns. The average expression was then calculated across all genes
for each cell type within each subject, followed by scaling, resulting in a dataset with subjects
as rows, and cell types as columns.

4.2.2
PCA Input Dataset
Another method used to reduce the high feature dimensionality was a linear dimensional reduc-
tion by PCA. The steps involved in generating the model input data are illustrated in Figure 4.5.
The data were first split into train and test sets and then aggregated by subject using the UMI
corrected counts. PCA was performed for each classification contrast (PSA_HC, PSA_PSO,
PSO_HC), creating a total of 3 different PCAs. The test data were projected onto the Principal
Component (PC) space, and PCs that accounted for 90% of the cumulative variance based on
the training data were included as features.

Discussion
In this thesis, the aim was to explore the utilization of scRNA-seq data for subject phenotype
prediction, focusing on the diagnosis of PsA using a scRNA-seq dataset of PBMCs from healthy
controls, PsO patients, and PsA patients. Four sub-aims were set to achieve this overall aim.
These were; investigating existing biomarkers for PsA and PsO, examining the steps involved
in the processing of scRNA-seq data, exploring feature selection and dimensionality reduction
techniques, and evaluating the feasibility of predicting the phenotype using the super learner
framework. The analysis yielded several results that require further validation, and it is important
to consider various aspects when interpreting these findings, as discussed in the following.
6.1
scRNA-seq Data Quality
The scRNA-seq data showed that cells from each subject were spread across multiple samples.
The number of sequenced cells within each sample ranged from under 5000 (PBMC-02-2) to over
30,000 (PBMC-05-4), with a similar pattern observed for cell count in each subject (Figure
5.1, Appendix B 9). The manufacturer’s instructions suggest sequencing 500-10,000 cells per
sample, which is lower than the number of cells sequenced in most samples [51]. Cells with less
than 500 genes detected were already removed from the data, suggesting that additional cells
originally were present in the samples (Figure 5.3a). Sequencing a higher number of cells than
recommended could affect the quality of cells in each sample, including the sequencing depth and
technical noise, thereby affecting downstream analyses. Moreover, the variation in cell counts
between individuals introduces bias in the results of the model and the biological findings, as
some subjects would have more cells available to determine their clinical status. However, this
data reflects real-world scenarios where the number of sequenced cells for a given subject can
vary across hospitals and laboratories.
The presence of batch effects, which result from technical variations among samples, can affect
the results. However, batch corrections were not applied for this dataset because the subjects
were split into multiple batches. Furthermore, the best approach to address the batch effect is
through experimental design [23].
To identify doublets in the scRNA-seq data, DoubletFinder was used. However, the doublet
formation rate for DoubletFinder was determined using an alternative approach, as the stan-
dard method failed to capture a realistic proportion of doublets. One possible reason could be
the higher number of cells sequenced per sample exceeding the recommended guidelines. This
raises concerns about the accurate classification of true doublets, potentially causing misleading
results in downstream analysis. To address this, a potential solution is to compare the doublet
classification obtained from DoubletFinder with a different doublet identification tool, such as
the R package DoubletDecon, which was used for the same dataset in Liu et al.’s study [38].

6.2.
DATA PREPERATION METHODS
Cell type annotation was performed using the Azimuth and SingleR R packages. The two tools
showed overall agreement assigning the same label to 73% of the cells. It was chosen to assign the
remaining cells based on scores from Azimuth. The annotation results showed the presence of 25
distinct cell types, with CD14 Mono being the most dominant cell state, accounting for nearly
70,000 cells. On the other hand, CD4 Proliferating represented the least frequent cell state,
comprising 213 cells. Each of the cell types was found across all clinical statuses. The variations
in cell counts for each cell type reflected the general pattern observed in the distribution of cell
types within PBMC samples (Figure 5.6) [17]. The cells that Azimuth and SinlgeR predicted
differently may have been assigned incorrectly, which could hinder the identification of cell type-
specific biological patterns. One potential solution is manually annotating the cell types using
well-defined cell type-specific marker genes for the cells where Azimuth and SingleR disagree
[23]. Additionally, it is crucial to ensure that the fraction of cell counts per cell type reflects
real-world proportions to avoid introducing bias and potentially drawing incorrect biological
conclusions regarding the importance of specific cell types in diagnosing PsA.
The high number of sequenced cells per sample, exceeding the recommended range, may affect
data quality. Lower sequencing quality may introduce noise and bias and affect the identification
of meaningful patterns in the data. Doublets and misclassified cells in cell type annotation can
further confound downstream analyses, including identifying differentially expressed genes and
cell type assignments. Further, in this study, PBMC cells are used. This does not represent the
complete lymphoid system because only recirculating lymphocytes can be isolated from blood
[19]. These limitations are considered when interpreting the results of phenotype prediction and
drawing biological conclusions based on the data.
6.2
Data Preperation Methods
Three different methods were used to generate various model input datasets, each containing
different features and illustrating various approaches for utilizing scRNA-seq data for phenotype
prediction of psoriasis.
Using cell types as features provided insights into the overall differences between the cell types
within the data. The averaged expression within each cell type showed differences in expressions
across the clinical statuses (Figure 5.9). PCA on the complete dataset allows for dimensionality
reduction while retaining variance. Limitations include the potential loss of biological information
leading to increased difficulty in interpreting the PCs in a meaningful way, as they represent linear
combinations of multiple genes and may not directly correspond to specific biological functions.
For this model, one PCA was created for each contrast within the data, which limits its usability
for new data. A Higher usability for new data would have been achieved using only one PC
space, which also was tried for this data, but with no remarkable results.
CTS DEGs offer
the advantage of selecting genes related to the clinical statuses, reducing noise, and potentially
capturing expression patterns within individual cell types.

6.3.
MODEL CONSTRUCTION
The findings from each dataset provide insights into the pathogenesis of PsA. The expression pat-
terns between cell types showed increased expression levels within Treg, CD4 TCM, CD4 Naive,
and B Naive cells for subjects having PsA compared to PsO and healthy subjects. These findings
were also supported by literature and the fact that psoriasis is a T cell-driven disease (Figure
5.6) [50]. The correlations between cell types illustrates potential biological interactions. For
instance, a positive correlation was observed between cDC2 and CD14 Mono cells. Similarly, a
positive correlation was identified between B memory and B intermediate cells (Appendix C
Figure 10).
The PCA method showed semi-clustering of the data according to clinical status (Figure 5.11,
5.14 ). This shows the potential of undiscovered disease stages or early symptoms in PsO patients
that may lead to the development of PsA, which may not have been detected by the CASPAR
criteria in Table 2.1 [2, 12]. This could be explored further by creating loading plots and iden-
tifying which genes in the PCs influence the data clustering. The PCA based on DEGs shows
improved separation between clinical statuses, particularly between PsA and healthy subjects
and PsO and healthy subjects, compared to the PcA on the complete dataset.
One limitation of the generated datasets is the feature reduction and selection during the data
preparation processes. The reduction was necessary to address the issue of having more than
10,000 features and only 81 observations, which can lead to challenges in statistical analyses
(p » n problem). Further, the selected features were aggregated across cells, such that each
observation represented a subject, resulting in a loss of cell-cell variation within each subject.
The limitations of each data preparation method should be considered. The approaches provide
biological insights, but they may not capture the full complexity of the disease. The findings
should be further confirmed and validated through experimental studies and comparison with
datasets from other studies. Integrating additional omics data, such as antibody-derived tags as
done in Liu et. al [38], could provide further validation and additional insights.
6.3
Model Construction
The model was constructed using nested cross-validation, comprising an outer loop with five
folds for data preparation and model testing and an inner loop with ten folds for constructing
the super learner. The same train-test split was applied to each dataset in the cross-validation
process to compare the different input datasets. Data preparation steps were performed within
the five folds of the outer cross-validation loop, contributing to the fact that each fold represented
an independent dataset. However, considerations are needed when selecting the steps to include
in the nested cross-validation to avoid introducing misleading information. For example, steps
like doublet identification and cell type annotation, which rely on information from the complete
sample, were not included. Although their inclusion would have enhanced the independence
of each fold, it could also introduce potentially misclassified cells.
An alternative approach
could involve creating the train-test split using unsupervised clustering methods like hierarchical

The library for the super learner was built following the guidelines stated in Mark J. van der Laan

small dataset size, constructing an ensemble based on several algorithms may increase the risk
of overfitting. One approach to overcome this could be constructing a final super learner by
combining a few of the best-performing algorithms.
6.4
Model Performance and Results
The datasets generated through different data preparation methods resulted in six final Super-
Learner frameworks, each comparing the contrasts (PSA_HC, PSA_PSO, PSO_HC). Overall,
the models showed better performance in distinguishing PsA subjects from healthy subjects
compared to distinguishing PsA from PsO subjects.
When comparing the model performance, Mlfc_pca achieved the highest accuracy of 0.901 and
distinguished PsA and PsO subjects from Healthy subjects with AUC values of 0.989 and 0.875,
respectively. The High AUC of 0.989 suggests that the model captures the underlying disease
pattern but could also indicate potential overfitting. Looking at the performance of the algo-
rithms included in the super learner, it was observed that several algorithms were included in
4-5 of the cross-validation folds, indicating capturing of an underlying distribution and possible
overfitting (Appendix D Figure 17). Five genes were found biologically relevant by exploring
the feature importance, but only THRA was found relevant in distinguishing PsA from healthy
subjects. Three other genes were found relevant for distinguishing PsA and PsO subjects and
were related to SD (Table 5.3). This finding conflicts with the performance of the Mlfc_pca
phenotype predictions of PsA and PsO subjects, achieving the lowest AUC of 0.63. To further
assess whether Mlfc_pca overfits or captures a true pattern, AUC calculations on both training
and test data, bootstrapping, and validation on new unseen data could be performed.
According to distinguishing PsA and PsO subjects, Mpca was the best-performing model with an
AUC of 0.798. However, by examining the loading plots containing the most important features,
the subjects were not well separated according to their clinical status. In comparison, the best
models of Liu. et al. [38], which used the same dataset for phenotype prediction, achieved an
AUC >0.87 when distinguishing PsA from PsO subjects. One limitation of their study is that
did not address overfitting.

6.5.
POTENTIAL BIOMARKERS
Differences between subjects enrolled in the study could also affect the model results. A propor-
tion of the subjects took different medications, and the degree of symptoms varied from subject
to subject. For example, some PsO subjects experienced joint pain. Therefore, the phenotype
prediction of the subjects should be investigated further, especially in cases where models dis-
agree regarding the subject phenotype. This could be done by inspecting the labeled subjects in
Appendix G.
The number of algorithms included in the final super learner varied between 20-30 across each
model input dataset. Generally, more models were included for distinguishing PsA from Healthy
subjects compared to distinguishing PsA and PsO subjects. For each final super learner, there
were also included algorithms that diverged during the fitting process, resulting in a risk of
infinity. Interestingly, these diverging algorithms were still included in the final super learner,
and it would be relevant to explore the performance of the super learner without these algorithms.
Additionally, it was observed that some of the included algorithms performed better than the
overall super learner based on their average AUC.
6.5
Potential Biomarkers
None of the biomarkers identified in the literature survey (Table 2.2) were found within this
dataset. The differential gene expression analysis revealed the potential for new biomarkers, with
a total of 122 genes showing enrichment in related diseases and an absolute log2foldchange >
0.5. Furthermore, 14 genes were identified as relevant for phenotype prediction based on their
feature importance.
These genes should be further explored, and their biological function should be investigated to
validate their potential as biomarkers. It is important to consider that measuring a gene does
not guarantee its desired biological function because of alternative splicing [24]. Additionally,
exploring the expression of these genes in a larger population of subjects is needed, as a potential
biomarker should be present in a significant proportion of the subjects. Furthermore, the genes
are cell type-specific, which should be explored further since the databases used for this study are
based on bulk-RNA sequencing data and, therefore, don’t provide information regarding what
cell types the genes are found within. The identified genes could also serve as input features for
a further model, for phenotype prediction.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrated the potential of scRNA-seq data of PBMCs to predict
subject phenotypes for PsA, PsO, and healthy subjects. Additionally, the results provide insights
into the genetics and cell types signatures associated with PsA and potential biomarkers.
A literature survey on the topic revealed that no diagnostic biomarkers for PsA and PsO exist,
but several candidate biomarkers has potential to serve as clinical biomarkers. Additionally,
the literature related to phenotype prediction using scRNA-seq data indicated limited research
within the area, and only a few models, have been developed, but need further validation.
The analysis of PBMC scRNA-seq data for subject phenotype prediction in PsA provided insight
into the genetics of the disease. Different approaches were applied to select informative features
from the vast amount of data, including cell types, PCA, and DEGs. These generated datasets
were used as inputs for the super learner models.
The constructed super learner models demonstrated an overall high accuracy ≥0.74. The results
indicate that both the cell type and PCA data can be utilized for subject phenotype prediction.
The constructed super learner models showed better performance in distinguishing PsA subjects
from healthy subjects with an AUC ≥0.778 compared to distinguishing PsA from PsO subjects,
where the best performing model was Mpca with an AUC of 0.798. Mlfc_pca was the overall best-
performing model, with an absolute log2 fold change > 0.5. The model achieved an accuracy of
0.901 and demonstrated promising results in distinguishing PsA subjects from healthy subjects.
However, it is crucial to validate the model’s performance further and assess the possibility of
overfitting.
During the analysis, potential new diagnostic biomarkers were explored. The cell types CD4
TCM, Tregs, and B naive, were differentially expressed for PsA subjects. Further, the PCA
revealed a clear separation of subjects based on the first two principal components, indicating
potential gene clusters.
Through DEG analysis and feature importance measured by mean
decrease Gini scores obtained from the random forest model, a total of 14 genes were identified as
biologically relevant according to feature importance. These genes were associated with diseases
similar to PsA and PsO. Notably, IFNGR1, which is involved in antimicrobial immune response,
was among the identified genes.
The findings contribute to the potential of using PBMC scRNA-seq data for the diagnosis of
PsA subjects, and the identification of potential biomarkers. Nevertheless, further validation
and replication studies are necessary to confirm these findings. With ongoing research in scRNA-
seq technology and analysis techniques, it is expected that continued progress will be made in
uncovering the complexity of PsA and other diseases at the single-cell level in the future.

Further Perspective
Several topics can be explored to further supplement the research and overcome this project’s
limitations.
Firstly, improvement of the data quality, to avoid bias and technical noise affecting the results.
Addressing issues such as the high number of sequenced cells, batch effects, and potential misla-
beled cells (doublets and cell types) could be explored further to create a higher-quality dataset.
Furthermore, examining variations of specific cell type clusters within PsA subjects could provide
insights into the heterogeneity and distinct cellular signatures of PsA. PCA analysis showed well-
separated clusters of subjects and subclusters within their clinical status. Further investigation
of these clusters could reveal sub-stages of PsA and early signs of PsA development.
More sophisticated methods can be considered to address the limitations of averaging gene ex-
pression within a cell type. A possible approach is exploring the potential of calculating interac-
tions between cell populations using the Python package CellPhoneDB [52] to include cell types
as features in the data. An alternative model input dataset could be based on the identified
disease-related genes as features. Further genes identified as relevant by literature could also be
used as features for phenotype prediction.
Machine learning can be used for disease phenotype prediction, but using multiple algorithms
in a super learner may lead to overfitting. Therefore the next step is constructing a final super
learner based on a few selected algorithms. Furthermore, improving model performance can be
achieved by addressing multicollinearity among input features.
Validating the generated models on unseen data from different laboratory studies is needed
to assess their generalizability and robustness across diverse datasets and experimental setups.
Additionally, exploring the identified genes as potential diagnostic biomarkers and examining
their associations with specific cell types in scRNA-seq databases can provide a broader context
for their relevance. Clinical validation of the findings is also essential. Examining the subjects
that the models find challenging to predict can provide insights into potential explanations, such
as clinical measurements or medication usage.
This project focuses on selecting relevant features within scRNA-seq data and does not consider
cell-cell variation within each subject. One way to overcome this could be by handling each
subject as a data frame, with its measured genes and cells. Machine learning techniques used for
image classification could be utilized for phenotype prediction and provide insight into disease-
related cell-cell variations.
By addressing these topics, the research can be further validated and used to improve the results
and provide valuable insights into the prediction of subject phenotypes using PBMC scRNA-seq
data for PsA, PsO, and healthy subjects.
