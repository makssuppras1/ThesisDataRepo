ABSTRACT
With the increasing demand of the power and recent advancements in the power electronics
components have completely changed the structure of the power system. The potential of
renewable energy (RE) sources like wind and solar are being harnessed to contribute to the
power share of the modern world. Distributed generation (DG) units which are small-scale
units are being installed close to the consumption sites in remote locations usually known as
microgrid. High penetration of converter based DG results in reduced short circuit power,
frequency ﬂuctuations and reduced inertia in the system. Novel stability challenges have emerged
with increasing penetration of converter based generation in the microgrid. For instance, the
phase locked loop (PLL) which is utilized for synchronization of the grid voltage phase and
frequency has been known to cause instability in a weak grid[1][2].
In this report, small signal stability (SSS) of 4 components based microgrid is investigated using
DIgSILENT PowerFactory software. Components of microgrid include synchronous generator,
synchronous condenser, type-4 wind turbine generator and a load. The electro-magnetic transient
(EMT) simulation coupled with the modal analysis is utilised to study the SSS of the microgrid.
The impact of the PLL design parameters (phase margin and bandwidth) on the SSS of the
system when connected to the weak grids like microgrid is explored in this report. Further, the
impact of parameters of the PLL of converter on the oscillatory stability of microgrid is analysed.
Moreover, the impact of penetration of wind power in the microgrid and its aﬀect on the PLL
parameters and oscillatory stability is also studied. A synchronous condenser is employed in the
microgrid to see its eﬀects on the SSS of the of the system. Role of synchronous condenser in
oscillatory stability of microgrid is analysed by connecting it at two diﬀerent locations in the
microgrid. Finally, an interaction between network impedance and oscillatory stability of the
microgrid is also investigated.

INTRODUCTION
Electrical power system has matured to a very complex system and it has become very eﬃcient in
providing energy from generators to end users. With increasing power demand, the power system
is growing rapidly. Renewable energy (RE) like, solar and wind, are also being utilized to fulﬁl
the energy demands of the current era. Development in the power electronic components makes
it easier to harness the potential of the RE sources. There is rapid increase being noticed in the
installations of the distributed generators (DGs). They are installed close to the consumption
sites to make them more eﬃcient and to minimize the infrastructure cost. A few DGs are installed
together to meet the load demand of the small area which becomes a small power system usually
known as a microgrid.
1.1
Introduction to Microgrid
"A microgrid is a group of interconnected distributed energy resources and loads within clearly
deﬁned electrical boundaries that acts as a single controllable entity with respect to the grid"
[3]. Distributed energy sources in microgrids are consist of distributed generations as well
as distribution storage units and loads. The generating units may consist of renewable and
conventional power sources. These generation units can be diesel/steam generator, wind, photo-
voltaic, bio-energy and fuel cell based generations while storage units may consist of batteries
and ﬂywheels.[4] A graphical view of the microgrid is shown in the Figure 1.1 which contain
diﬀerent DGs, storage unit and a load.
Figure 1.1: Microgrid Graphical Overview.[5]

==PAGE:11==
1. Introduction
There are many beneﬁts of the microgrids, few of them are listed below [6][7];
• Provide eﬃcient and low cost energy
• Improved operation and reliability
• Local energy supply to remote communities
• Economical supply of energy
• Reduced grid congestion during peak hours
• Diversiﬁed risk of operation
• Flexible installation sites
• Fewer distribution/transmission resources
Microgrids can be either connected or disconnected from the main grid system, thus operating in
grid-connected or islanded mode of operation. The main grid and microgrid can be connected
through a point of connection called point of common coupling (PCC). The power can be
exchanged through PCC and thus increasing the reliability of the overall system.[6] The mode
of operation of the microgrid (grid-connected or islanded) introduces many challenges in the
operation, control and protection. Each mode of operation demands robust control and protection
schemes for successful operation. The challenges and issues imposed by the microgrid can be;
bidirectional power ﬂows in grid-connected mode, limited short circuit capacity, stability problems,
low inertia system and intermittent power output.[8]
Islanded mode of operation of the microgrid, have very speciﬁc attributes that are diﬀerent as
compared to the conventional power system due to which special control methods are require
for the microgrid system. First of all, the rotating inertia of the system which is quite less in
microgrids. When a power imbalance occurs in the conventional power system, the diﬀerence is
fulﬁlled by other power sources (rotating inertia) but the microgrids mainly consisting of power
electronic based generations, severely aﬀected by less inertia problem which may lead to the
stability issues. Power/frequency (P/f) control in microgrids is not inherently available. Secondly,
the microgrids are low voltage distribution systems, the power ﬂow only depends on the voltage
magnitude unlike the transmission grids where power ﬂow is dependent on the voltage angle.
Third, the intermittent nature of the RE sources also need to be taken into account during the
designing of the microgrid control.[9] Keeping in view above issues, very ﬁne control techniques
need to be developed and applied to the microgrids to maintain the system strength and stable
operation.
1.2
Introduction to Power System Stability
"Power system stability may be broadly deﬁned as that property of a power system that enables
it to remain in a state of operating equilibrium under normal operating conditions and to regain
an acceptable state of equilibrium after being subjected to a disturbance".[10] Although power
system stability is a single phenomenon, but it has been divided into three main types. The
basis of this categorisation is due to the physical nature, size and time span of stability issue.
Stability classiﬁcation is represented in Figure 1.2 and investigated instability in this report is
also highlighted in the red box.

1. Introduction
Figure 1.2: Power system stability classiﬁcation.[11]
The purpose of the classiﬁcation is to develop an ease in identifying the instability cause and
apply relevant tools and analysis to mitigate its eﬀect. However, it is possible that in a speciﬁc
condition, instability does not occur in its pure form. This statement is speciﬁcally true in stressed
power system and in case of cascading events. It means that system could not cope with one
type of instability and which lead the system to other type of instability.[11] In small-disturbance
angle stability, two types of instabilities are studied which are non-oscillatory instability and
oscillatory instability and they depend on the type of control used for generator.
1.2.1
Non-Oscillatory Instability
This instability occurs due to the lacking of synchronising torque in the system and steady
increase in the rotor angle is observed. Lacking of synchronising torque is due to the absence of
the automatic voltage regulator (AVR) i.e. constant ﬁeld voltage condition. This instability is
illustrated in the Figure 1.3.

1.2.2
Oscillatory Instability
Oscillatory instability is due to the lacking of suﬃcient damping torque and the rotor oscillations
of growing amplitudes are the result of this instability. When system is equipped with AVR
(excitation control), this kind of generator response can be noticed. The Figure 1.4 shows the
behaviour of generator in this kind of instability.
Figure 1.4: Oscillatory Instability with Excitation Control[10]
In this thesis report, the focus is placed on the oscillatory instability of the system. Inherited
electro-mechanical oscillatory behaviour of the power system is studied in this instability type.
1.3
Small Signal Stability in Power System
In small signal stability (SSS) problems, the system oscillates with undamped or growing
oscillations phenomenon due to lack of damping or synchronising torque in the system. The
unstable oscillations could appear on one generator or a small part against the rest of the power
system or the whole interconnected power system.
Electromechanical oscillation in power system is studied in the area of rotor angle stability
problem, these oscillations exist because of the swing equation which governs the the synchronous
generators’ power outputs in relation with the variation of rotor angles. At steady state, the
generators’ electromagnetic torque is constant and equal to the mechanical power, a sudden
change of electromagnetic torque caused by the disturbance such as a fault on the line will
result in an unbalance in net power which promotes or weaken the rotor’s motion of generators.
When considering the interconnected generators, some of the synchronous generators can lose
synchronisation during a disturbance. The disturbance can result in two kinds of unstable
oscillation, one is the insuﬃcient synchronisation which relates to the rotor angle deviation, the
other one is insuﬃcient damping which relates to the rotor speed deviation. Lack of damping
torque is the main problem that exists in small signal stability and results in undamped oscillation
in power system[11].

1. Introduction
Power system oscillations are typically divided into three groups/modes depending on its global
or local scale, which are listed here;
1. Inter-area oscillations: a group of generators in a area swings against the generators in
another area and oscillation frequency is between 0.1 - 0.3 Hz
2. Intra-area oscillations: a group of generators swings against the generators in the same
area and frequency of oscillation ranges from 0.4 - 0.7 Hz
3. local area/inter-machine: Generators oscillates against the closely located generators with
oscillation frequency of 0.7 - 2.0 Hz and this is the adverse interaction between the control
system of the machines.
1.4
Microgrid SSS and Problem Formulation
The rapid development in power electronic converters has changed the structure of the conventional
power system which has mainly synchronous generators as the power sources. Voltage source
converters (VSCs) in wind turbines, solar PV and HVDC links and induction generator in wind
turbines are more integrated into the power system [12] [13]. With this advancement in the
system, the stability challenges which need to be addressed have also changed.
Although microgrids allow the integration of the renewable energy sources in the power system
while reducing the losses and enhancing reliability of electric supply but they create novel
challenges for power network operation. Traditionally, synchronous generators are responsible for
providing damping and synchronizing torque to eradicate the oscillation problems in the power
system. An increasing penetration of microgrids, may have eﬀect on the stability performance of
the system as both conventional system and microgrids have diﬀerent operational dynamics. It
has been observed that dominant modes of oscillations in microgrids are found to be in higher
frequency range as compared to the conventional power system oscillation modes.[14]
Furthermore, dominance of RE sources in microgrids leads to the less inertial characteristics of
the system which may change the damping trajectories of the low frequency oscillations in the
system.[15] In [16],the frequency of oscillatory modes for the DGs penetrated distribution system
is found to be around 4Hz. A system with 2MW type-4 wind turbine was analysed in [17] which
demonstrates that with decreasing the grid strength (high impedance of the grid network), the
system is suﬀering from the oscillatory stability issue at around 10Hz.
Voltage source converters (VSCs) synchronised with phase locked loop (PLL) control, despite
having fast power control ability are found to be oscillating, specially when coupled with weak
AC grid. The PLL design parameters (phase margin and bandwidth) has direct connection
with the stability of the system. A small increase in bandwidth of PLL can lead the system
into instability.[1] The converter synchronisation instability and low frequency oscillations issues
have been noticed by the industries. It has also been observed that a large penetration of the
DGs, a case of weak grid i.e. high impedance network, is also causing instabilities in the system
frequency.[2] The literature indicates that PLL and converter control have an important role
in the oscillatory stability of the system, also, when the oscillation frequencies are not in the
conventional range of SSS and going higher. It is then become inevitable to investigate it more in
the microgrid perspective and see the eﬀects of the PLL design parameters. it will be interesting

1. Introduction
RE sources in microgrids, cannot provide inertial support to the system in case of any disturbance
and imbalance. The synchronous condenser (SC) which has inherit inertial response can resist
with the frequency changes in case of imbalance in the system. SC is proposed as potential
solution to provide inertia along short circuit power and voltage support.[18]
1.5
Research Motivation and Goals
The microgrids have the issues of less rotating inertia due to the penetration of RE sources
which are converter driven machines. SC having natural inertial characteristics may help in the
system stability. The objective of this masters thesis is to analyse the SSS of the microgrid while
studying an interaction between converter and synchronous condenser. The eﬀect of converter
PLL control parameters in the SSS is also investigated. The following questions are answered in
this report;
1. How the PLL parameters can impact the small signal behaviour of the microgrid?
2. How the penetration of the wind power into microgrid can aﬀect the PLL parameters?
3. How the SSS of the microgrid is aﬀected when SC is connected at diﬀerent locations in
microgrid?
4. How network impedance aﬀects the microgrid small signal behaviour?
1.6
Approach to Research
The steps followed to achieve the research goals are listed below;
1. A reference, islanded one bus microgrid system is developed which has synchronous
generator, Synchronous condenser, type-4 wind turbine and a load.

analysis tools in PowerFactory. The main focus is placed on the EMT analysis due to some
limitations observed in PowerFactory Modal analysis.
3. A suitable PLL design is investigated for SSS of the microgrid. The eﬀect of diﬀerent wind
power penetration levels on the PLL design is analysed using the EMT analysis.
4. At the end, a suitable SC location is investigated for SSS of the microgrid by connecting
the SC at two diﬀerent locations. An optimal operation location for SC is then proposed
based on the PLL design.

2.3
Power System Stability Analysis Techniques
The study of the power system stability analyses the behaviour of the power network under
certain conditions and situations like before and after the disturbance. There can be many types
of the disturbances which may include; sudden loss of load or generation in the system, outage of
an element or component and faults. The system is considered rugged if it, under perturbed and
normal conditions, can continue its stable operation. Therefore, it is required that power system
should be designed in such a way that it can withstand the contingency conditions without losing
the synchronism with rest of the network.[19] There are mainly two types of analysis techniques
utilised in power industry to study the system behaviour under contingencies, which are;
• Steady State/Transient Stability (RMS) Analysis
• Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) Analysis
RMS analysis technique is the mostly used in the power system analysis due to its eﬀective
computational time while EMT simulation technique is less common to be utilized on the large
power systems due to the practical considerations.[20]
2.3.1
RMS Analysis
The RMS analysis uses the simpliﬁed models of the generator neglecting the stator transients,
passive network elements are considered as steady state representation and AC network is consid-
ered as constant impedance or admittance model. Having these simpliﬁcations, computational
time for RMS analysis is really short and eﬃcient. The disadvantage of this analysis technique is
that as neglecting stator transients, AC network transients and steady state representation of the
passive elements, only transients up to frequency 5Hz can be observed.[21] Only the fundamental
frequency values of voltage and current are considered in RMS analysis.[20]
2.3.2
EMT Analysis
The EMT analysis considers the detailed models of the machines, dynamic behaviour of passive
network elements and converter driven machines which leads to the more accurate results as
compared to RMS analysis. Even, the fast transients can also be captured with this technique.
The major drawback in using this technique is the computational time due to the small step size
during simulation, due to which, this technique is more suitable for small power systems.[21]
In EMT analysis, instantaneous current and voltage values are taken into account. This means
that very high frequencies like surges and lightning can also be modeled with EMT analysis
technique.[20]

3.1
Phase Locked Loop
The PLL is a feedback control system which is used to synchronise the output signal in frequency
and phase with its reference or input signal. PLLs are, now-a-days, most widely utilised in the
power electronic converters for the purpose of synchronisation of frequency as well as phase of
the grid voltage.[22]
Penetration of the distributed generators (DGs) have been increased into the power system
after the advancements of the power electronic components. DGs are being installed very closed
to consumption sites in low to medium voltage distribution grids. Power electronic converter
interface decouples the rotating generators like in type-4 WTG. Hence, the operation of the power
electronic converters has diﬀerent characteristics as compared to the conventional synchronous
generators and it imposes various impacts on the static and dynamics performance of the grid.
The PLL is one of the most important aspect of the converters when it comes to the stability of
the modern DGs connected grid. The concept of PLL was ﬁrst introduced by Appleton in 1923
and it was used for synchronous reception of the radio signals. Now-a-days, PLL techniques are
being widely used for voltage and frequency synchronisation of power electronic converters and
grid network.
3.1.1
Principle of PLL
A typical PLL system mainly consists of three sub-systems or components and those are; a phase
detector, a loop ﬁlter and a voltage controlled oscillator. Simpliﬁed block diagram of the PLL is
shown Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Simpliﬁed Block Diagram of PLL
The phase detector measures the phase diﬀerence between reference input signal and feedback
output signal and then produces a voltage according to the phase diﬀerence of the two signals.
The loop ﬁlter is a low pass ﬁlter which blocks the higher frequency signals and stabilizes the

loop operation. The output of the loop ﬁlter is then ampliﬁed and passed through the oscillator
to produce the output signal frequency. This out frequency is then fed to a integrator to generate
the phase of the output signal. When frequency of the output signal is locked with the frequency
of the input signal, the phase diﬀerence detected by phase detector becomes zero and out signal
gets locked with the input signal.[23]

3.2
Synchronous Condenser
RE sources like wind and solar are variable and intermittent in nature. They signiﬁcantly lack
the ability to handle the network faults and disturbances, due to which they cannot provide grid
support. Consequently, the increasing penetration of these intermittent energy sources reducing
the stability and resilience of the power systems. Also, the increasing installation of RE based
power plants, resulting into the decommissioning of the conventional power plants. The large
synchronous generators which were used to provide short circuit power and rotating inertia to
the power system are being taken out of the system which is leading towards a less inertia and
less stable system. [25] In this scenario, SC is an interesting solution which can provide grid
support in form of reactive power, short circuit power and inertia.
3.2.1
SC Basics and Beneﬁts
SCs are rotating machines which resemble with synchronous generators in their electrical design.
They are neither motors or generators as they do not drive mechanical loads or driven by a
turbine or engine.[25]
15
==PAGE:25==
3. Critical Components in Microgrid
SCs were being used from the very beginning of the electrical grid development for voltage
regulation. With the development of low cost power electronic based devices like Static Var
Compensators and STATCOMs, SCs were replaced by them due to their better controllability,
speed and ﬂexibility. Also, SCs were used to widely applied in the power networks to provide the
reactive power support to the system before development of power electronic based compensation
devices. The beneﬁts of installing SCs in power system are quite understood. A synchronous
generator or a motor can be applied as a SC but without prime mover or mechanical load and
ﬁeld voltages are regulated such that SC can absorb (under-excited) or supply (overexcited)
reactive power. It is basically a rotating reactive power absorbing or generating machine.[26] An
interesting feature to note that SC operates very close to zero active power, however, very small
amount of active power is needed from the system for its operation. The active power demand
by SC is utilized for compensating the losses in ventilation, bearings and electrical losses.[27]
SC can provide promising voltage support and in case of voltage dips on the system, the SC can
provide reactive power, thus improving the voltage proﬁle of the system and quickly restoring
the system power.[28] Moreover, SC can also support the power network in the form of providing
short circuit capacity which can assist the protection of the system. SC being a rotational
machine, it is inherited with the feature of rotating inertia which can assist in the stability of the
closely installed generator. The stored kinetic energy in the rotating mass of SC is available for
injection in or absorb from the system for the frequency regulation during frequency imbalance
events. For example, when a generation source is suddenly lost, a decreasing trend in the system
frequency is seen but SC can provide additional rotating energy to balance the system frequency.
In case of sudden load loss or reduction, the system frequency may go high as compared to
nominal frequency of the system and SC can absorb extra power from the system to maintain
the frequency, thus stabilizing the system frequency. It can be concluded that SC can help in
providing voltage support, short circuit power, inertia and frequency stability.[18]
3.3
Excitation System for Synchronous Condenser
The basic purpose of the excitation system is to feed or provide direct current to the ﬁeld windings
of the synchronous machine. Also, excitation system plays an important role in controlling the
machine voltage and reactive power ﬂows by control of ﬁeld current. The excitation system
can be contemplated from the synchronous machine prospective and power system perspective.
From the generator point of view, the excitation ﬁeld current should be adjusted such that it
can maintain the terminal voltage of generator while from system prospective, excitation system
contribute to the control of the voltage and improve the system stability. It should have the
capability to to respond to the disturbances to avoid instability in the system.[10]
3.3.1
Types of Excitation Systems
The excitation system may be classiﬁed into following categories based on the power sources
used for excitation;[10]
1. DC excitation systems
2. AC excitation systems
3. Static excitation systems

3.3.1.1
DC Excitation Systems
In this excitation systems, DC generators used to provide ﬁeld current to the rotor of synchronous
machine through slip-rings. The exciter could be motor driven or by the generator shaft and,
either self-excited or separately excited. When separately excited used, the exciter ﬁeld is
provided by pilot exciter which was a permanent magnet generator. However, DC exciters were
last favourable in mid 1960s and were replaced by the AC exciters.
3.3.1.2
AC Excitation Systems
This kind of exciters uses the AC machines (alternators) as the power source of the generator
excitation. Normally, the exciter and the generator are mounted on the same shaft. The output
of the AC exciter is converted to the DC by using controlled or un-controlled rectiﬁers to feed it
to the rotor of the synchronous generator. The rectiﬁers may be stationary (the DC output of
rectiﬁers is given to the ﬁeld windings of the synchronous generator using slip rings) or rotating
(slip rings and brushes are not required to feed the ﬁeld windings of the synchronous generators).
3.3.1.3
Static Excitation System
All the components used in, static excitation system, are stationary. The rectiﬁers can be
controlled or uncontrolled, to feed dc current to the ﬁeld windings of the generator through slip
rings. The power supplied to the rectiﬁer circuit is provided by the main generator through an
auxiliary bus utilizing the step-down transformer. The following three types of static excitation
systems are wisely used;
• Potential-source controlled rectiﬁer systems
• Compound source rectiﬁer systems
• Compound-controlled rectiﬁer excitation systems
3.3.2

This excitation system was deﬁned by IEEE committee in 1968 and it is generally referred as
IEEET1 excitation model. This model has been widely utilised to represents the systems with

4.3
Modal Analysis in PowerFactory
The objective of the thesis is to study the SSS or oscillatory stability of the microgrid. It will be
a good idea to have a brief detail that how Modal analysis tool is being used in PowerFactory
and what kind of results can be obtained.
The computation of the eigenvalues is very powerful tool for studying the oscillatory instability
of the power system. The Modal analysis tool in PowerFactory calculates the eigenvalues of all
the power plant and controllers modes. The computation of eigenvalues obtained after beginning
the transient simulation (RMS) of the system. The eigenvalues are calculated in the steady
state condition and such a state is achieved with a load-ﬂow calculation and followed by the
initial conditions. The calculation of initial conditions means that all the time-derivatives of
the system state variables are zero i.e. system is in steady state condition. The modal analysis
tool calculates the system A-matrix utilising the numerical algorithms. The representation of
the network model is same as the RMS simulation network model. It means that ﬁrst RMS
calculations need to be performed to get the Modal analysis results and PowerFactory does not
have the option to simulate Modal analysis after calculating the EMT analysis.[19]
The studied system depicts the oscillatory stability if all the calculated eigenvalues lie on the left
side of the complex plane, which means that eigenvalues must have negative real parts. It is
then evident that electro-mechanical oscillations have damping behaviour and system is stable.
If the eigenvalues lie in the right of the complex plane, it shows that system is unstable. The
stable eigenvalues are shown in green colour while unstable eigenvalues are shown in red colour
when eigenvalue plot is obtained in PowerFactory after performing Modal analysis. An example
of the stable and unstable eigenvalues obtained in PowerFactory is given in Figure 4.10.

5.1
RMS Vs EMT Analysis in PowerFactory
DIgSILENT PowerFactory simulation tool can easily analyze the dynamic behaviour of the
power system due to transients in time domain. It is very convenient to model and analyse the
simpler or complex power systems such as industrial system or huge transmission network while
considering the mechanical and electrical parameters. PowerFactory categorises the transients in
the power systems into three main types; which include short-term or electro-magnetic transients,
mid-term or electro-mechanical transients and long-term transients. PowerFactory is well suited
to analyse these three categories of the transients. Consequently, there are three main simulation
options are available for the analysis of the system, which include; [19]
• RMS basic simulation function which uses the symmetrical steady state model for mid and
long term transients under balanced conditions.
• 3 phase RMS simulation function to study mid and long term transients under balanced
and un-balanced conditions, i.e. unsymmetrical faults can also be analysed.
• An EMT simulation function which utilises the dynamic network model for electro-
mechanical and electro-magnetic transients under balanced and un-balanced conditions of
the network.
The main focus here is the comparison between basic RMS simulation function which is a
prerequisite to perform Modal analysis and EMT simulation function. The balanced RMS
simulation operation considers the dynamic behaviour of electro-mechanical, thermal and control
devices. The passive network elements are considered by steady state representation. The
following studies can be performed, depending upon the models of generators, control devices and
power plants; mid-term stability, oscillatory stability, transient stability and motor startup.[19]
To take into account the dynamic behaviour of passive electrical elements, instantaneous values
of voltage and current are considered in EMT analysis and it is well suited for studying the
exact dynamic behaviour of converter driven machines like wind turbines, HVDC systems and
harmonic components of the voltage or current.[19]
During the analysis of the microgrid model and performing the simulations on the model, it was
observed that simulation results for RMS analysis are stable while the results for EMT analysis
do not show the stable operation for a speciﬁc scenario. A comparison through Figure 5.1 and
Figure 5.2 is provided here to understand the diﬀerence between the results of both analysis
techniques. Both ﬁgures show the terminal voltage Vs time curve of synchronous generator for a
speciﬁc scenario. Details of scenario are not mentioned here as they will come in later sections of

5.2.1
Impact of PLL Design Parameters on Overall Microgrid Stability
The studied design parameters of PLL are phase margin (PM) and bandwidth (BW). To analyse
the parameters characteristics of the PLL, simulations are performed on the base case scenario.
PLL being an integral part of the wind turbine control system, have a huge impact on the stability
of the overall system. The phase margin and the bandwidth are the main design parameters of
the PLL which decide the stable operation of the system. The higher bandwidths of PLL depicts
a fast response while lower bandwidth values show a slow response of PLL. The EMT analysis is
performed on the system to see that how phase margin and bandwidth of the PLL may aﬀect
the stability of the system. It has been observed that there is a direct connection between the
phase margin and bandwidth of the PLL and stability of the system. PLL having high phase
margin can operate at higher bandwidths as compared to PLL having low phase margin.
The analysis is performed on the microgrid model and the boundary bandwidths for the PLL
are observed at diﬀerent phase margins. The boundary bandwidth means that at speciﬁc phase
margin, there is one highest value of the bandwidth, at which the system can maintain its
stable operation and above that bandwidth, system stability is compromised. The system starts
showing oscillatory instability when boundary bandwidth is crossed, and system is always stable
if the boundary bandwidth is not crossed. The simulations are performed at diﬀerent phase
margins starting from 1◦to 90◦. The system can not be simulated at 0◦of phase margin and it
has been found that above 80◦of phase margin, very high value of bandwidth can be achieved
which seems quite ideal. So, the range of phase margin from 1◦to 80◦is taken into account
for analysis purpose. Each combination of the phase margin and bandwidth of PLL are linked
to the diﬀerent proportional and integral gains of PLL PI controller. These gains values are
entered into the PI controller of the PLL and PowerFactory simulations are performed. The
phase margins in degrees and boundary bandwidths in rad/s are presented in Table 5.1 and this

Eﬀect of Load Change and Inertia

applying any disturbance to the microgrid system and all the values of phase margin and
bandwidths are in steady state condition. To investigate the PLL design parameters dynamics,
the load change event as small disturbance is applied to the microgrid system. It is observed
that in each scenario of wind penetration level presented in previous sections, the parameters of
PLL stay the same, i.e. system can withstand the 10% load change event. There is no oscillatory
instability observed due to the small disturbance.
Change of Synchronous Generator Inertia):
The inertia of the rotating machines in
the power system plays an important role in the frequency stability. The impact of inertia is
investigated on the small signal behaviour of the studied microgrid. It is noticed that the inertia
does not improve the SSS of the microgrid, i.e. the PLL bandwidths remain same and no increase
in bandwidth can be achieved by increasing the inertia of the synchronous generator.

6
CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
6.1
Conclusion
This thesis report presents the comparison between RMS and EMT analysis functions in
DIgSILENT PowerFactory. The RMS simulation takes into account the dynamic behaviour of
the electro-mechanical and control device but passive network elements are considered by steady
state representation. RMS analysis is not a suitable tool to study the dynamics of the converter
based machines like wind turbines or HVDC system while EMT analysis takes into account
the exact behaviour of the converter driven machines. Also, the network impedance aﬀect is
not observable in the RMS analysis while EMT analysis precise takes it into account while
simulating the system. That is why, EMT analysis is utilized to study the stability behaviour of
the microgrid.
The PLL design guidelines (phase margin and bandwidth) for RE equipped VSC control systems
under weak grid conditions, such as a microgrid, are presented. The impact of changing the
PLL phase margin on the boundary bandwidth for stability is observed. High bandwidth or
fast response of the PLL can be achieved at higher phase margins, but there is certain limit of
the bandwidth for a speciﬁc phase margin at which the PLL can operate normally. Increasing
bandwidth too high is not a suitable choice and could lead to the oscillatory instability in the
system.
The penetration of wind power into the microgrid also aﬀects the PLL parameters. The bandwidth
of PLL has to be reduced at same value of phase margin with increasing the share of wind power
in microgrid. Higher the penetration of wind power in microgrid, lesser will be the bandwidth
at which PLL can function without creating instability issues. Increasing penetration of wind
power also restricts the PLL bandwidth for maintaining the stability.
For the studied system, the synchronous condenser (SC) location shows an impact on the PLL
parameters. This can be explained by the variation of impedance seen from PCC due to the
location of SC and its interaction with the PLL. At low network impedance in the system, the
PLL can be fast and can operate at high bandwidths while high impedance network greatly
aﬀect the PLL performance and results into the slow PLL response. It is more prone to the
oscillatory instability in high impedance grid networks.
For the system under study, the inertia of the synchronous machines stabilises the frequency of
the the microgrid in case of power imbalance but it does not aﬀect the small signal instability
caused due to power behaviour of the microgrid. The inertia of the machines and PLL parameters
do not interact to reduce the oscillatory instability of the microgrid.

6. Conclusion and Future Work
6.2
Future Work
The study conducted here focuses more on the PLL parameter characteristics with respect
to wind power penetration, SC location in the network and network impedance. The current
conclusions where drawn based on the system under study, however, in order to generalize the
conclusion of the study, a more detailed analysis involving analytical models and wider test
cases could be carried out in future. It will be interesting to optimise the size of the SC which
is suitable to provide grid support in disturbances and imbalances. The reactive power and
inertia provided by the SC which is needed to maintain the stable operation of the microgrid
during disturbances and faults can be analysed. Also, a storage system can be connected to the
microgrid and its impact on the SSS of the system will be an exciting aspect to investigate.
