Abstract
This project considers the replication of aerodynamic behaviour of real ice with
artificial ice on a circular cylinder. Photometric scanning was adopted to scan the
real ice in order to produce artificial 3D-printed ice. Both the real ice tests and the
artificial ice tests were tested for aerodynamic performance for angle of attack between
0◦and 90◦and later compared in terms of lift and drag coefficients and the Den Hartog
criterion to identify instability regions. By comparing the results between the real and
the artificial ice, sufficient accuracy would have to be documented in order to assess
whether artificial ice can in fact replicate the aerodynamic behaviour of real ice. If
correlation is proven to be sufficiently high, using artificial ice instead of real ice would
provide a less economically demanding option for aerodynamic testing of circular
cylinders with ice. In addition, the approach of conducting aerodynamic performance
tests for circular cylinders with ice would be more accessible for a broader range of
wind tunnels, as the approach does not require a cooling unit.
The main experiments were conducted at the Climatic Wind Tunnel at FORCE
Technology, where tests of rime ice and degraded ice were considered. Photometric
scanning and aerodynamic performance tests were executed for the first rime ice
test, although the test proved unsuccessful during the performance tests due to the
cooling unit malfunctioning, resulting in the ice melting and the termination of the
experiment. A second rime ice test was carried out, where the photometric scanning
and the aerodynamic performance tests were conducted successfully. Proceedingly,
photometric scanning and performance tests were carried out for the residual ice, after
being degraded by extensive aerodynamic testing. The 2nd rime ice and the degraded
rime ice was 3D-printed to construct the artificial ice, and later tested for aerodynamic
behaviour, similarily to the real ice.
Here, three modifications of the artificial ice
were considered: artificial rime ice, artificial rime ice with additional roughness, and
artificial degraded rime ice.
The correlation between the real ice and the modifications of artificial ice was
investigated by means of lift and drag coefficients and instability regions. Satisfyingly
high correlation was found in the comparison of lift and drag coefficients between
the 2nd rime ice test and the artificial rime ice and the artificial degraded rime ice,
and similarly between the degraded ice and the artificial degraded ice. Although, the
correlation between the 2nd rime ice test and the artificial rime ice with additional
roughness was insufficient. In the comparisons of instability regions, it was found
that there were correlation between the 2nd rime ice test and the artificial ice tests,
but only for a limited region. Based on these observations, it was found that artificial
ice can in fact reproduce the aerodynamic behaviour of real ice in terms of lift and
drag coefficients, but not in terms of the Den Hartog criterion. Therefore, further
investigation of the correlation of instability regions between real ice and artificial ice
is necessary to fully extend the application area of using artifical ice.

1. Introduction
Technical University of Denmark
1
Introduction
The study of aerodynamic loads on structures by means of experimental testing
in wind tunnels (WT) have existed since the late 1800’s and remains as relevant
today.
In addition to aerodynamic loads, the phenomenon of ice accretion in
relation to aerodynamic loading has been of interest. Several studies have previously
been conducted on this particular topic, both by researchers and students. While
aerodynamic forces prove to play a significant role in terms of dynamic loading, ice
accretion itself constitute a greater importance for surface features and roughness as
well as the change in volume and shape around cylinders.
While experimental testing of cylinders exposed to ice accretion might provide
accurate results, it is not economical.
In addition, every test is a destructive test
where newly formed ice is removed by the applied aerodynamic loads. Therefore,
one might consider other alternative approaches.
Earlier studies have proved the
feasibility of applying artificial ice onto cylinders and subjected these to tests of
aerodynamic loading, though it must be noted that the approach is not accurate
enough to substitute real ice extensively.
The objective of this project is to investigate the accuracy of conducting aerodynamic
testing with replicated 3D-ice applied to a circular cylinder in comparison to real
ice. The evaluation of the accuracy is based on the aerodynamic force coefficients
which are recorded during aerodynamic testing, and the Den Hartog criterion, which
will consider instability regions.
If a sufficient accuracy of this approach can be
documented, proving that artificial ice can substitute real ice, this particular method
would provide several beneficial outcomes. Firstly, it would be a less computational
heavy method, as well as less economically demanding than by using real ice.
Furthermore, aerodynamic testing with the addition of artificial ice accretion can
be conducted in any WT, as this approach would not require the testing facility to
be a climatic wind tunnel (CWT). Lastly, future tests would not be conducted in a
destructive manner, as artificial ice is more robust than real ice.
This project is structured in the following manner: firstly, test objects and instruments
will be installed in the wind tunnel.
Then, a measurement of load coefficients at
different rotation angels and air speeds will be conducted. Afterwards, atmospheric
in-cloud icing simulations will be conducted, followed by measurements of the
aerodynamic performance, and photometric scanning of the ice layer. Subsequently,
the ice layer will be replicated as a 3D model, which will be printed in segments and
applied to the circular cylinder, and similar aerodynamic testing will be run as before.
Results obtained from the aerodynamic performance of the real and artificial ice will
then be compared and eventually adjusted.

2. Methodology
Technical University of Denmark
2
Methodology
Several methods of acquiring data might be required to fully analyze circular cylinders
for aerodynamic performance, while subjected to ice accretion. The data of interest
can be divided into secondary data and primary data. Secondary data refer to data
gathered by someone else that have been collected for a specific purpose, and might
have some relevance for the project at hand. In other words: data gathered by other
students and researchers in reports, researching papers and other relevant literature.
Primary data, on the other hand, refer to data collected by the author for this specific
project, and can be divided into quantitative data and qualitative data. Quantitative
data consist of data acquired by means of experiments as well as photometric scanning
of ice, while qualitative data account for observations that are made during the project.
Discussions and reflections carried out during the project’s life cycle, and acquired
insight in the studies of aerodynamic excitations and ice accretion, provided through
supervision are considered as qualitative data.
In order to verify the validity of the data and draw conclusions, triangulation might be
required. By triangulating, one considers the validation of data through cross verifying
several sources. For some cases, it might be sufficient to compare primary data with
secondary data, while for other cases, secondary data might not be present. Hence, a
more thorough quantitative data acquisition might be required where an experiment
is carried out several times with the intent of indicating consistency, and validating
a result’s feasibility.
Comparing different experiments might also be necessary to
identify potential commonalities and differences. Relevant theoretical models for both
aerodynamic performance of circular cylinders and ice accretion will be considered in
order to fully grasp the study in this particular project, see Chapter 3.
2.1
Used literature
For the most part, the literature that have been used to carry out this project have been
developed at the Technical University of Denmark. Firstly, the preceding work during
the Bachelor thesis of Rebekka Dinesen [2] has served as a substantial resource in
terms of grasping the topic of ice accretion and replication of aerodynamic behaviour
of ice on circular cylinders, where the approach to replicating ice was defined. Also,
the Master theses by Mia Lund [1] and Jens Frederiksen & Mads Høst Gandil [5] have
provided necessary insight about the topics of aerodynamics of circular cylinders with
ice accretion. The papers of Holger Koss, see [3] and [4], have served as great assets for
the theoretical understatement of aerodynamics around circular cylinders.

3.1
Aerodynamics of circular cylinders
Aerodynamics consider dynamics of fluids, in other words the substances gases and
liquids, and is the state of a fluid in motion, also referred to as a flow. Moreover, a
flow can be quantified by its location and time that are described by the system of
a flow’s density ρ, pressure p, temperature T and the velocity U, the so called Euler
approach [3, p. 18]. A flow can be further characterized as a stationary flow, where the
values describing the flow are not affected by time, or as a instationary flow, where
the values vary with time. In this particular project, the flow will be considered a
stationary flow, as neither density, pressure, temperature nor velocity vary with time
during the experiments. The flow state can be described by dimensionless parameters.

Surface roughness and flow régimes
The flow and its separation around a body is affected by the body’s geometrical
shape and its surface features, and by the surface roughness.
When considering
the geometrical shape, different bodies fall into the following categories:
semi-
aerodynamic, and non-aerodynamic bodies [4, p. 41].
Aerodynamic bodies, also
referred to as streamlined bodies, are characterized by the flow following the body’s
surface, where the separation points are near the rear edge.
With semi-aerodynamic bodies the point of separation might vary depending on the
body’s shape and surface roughness, and accounts for quasi-streamlined bodies like
cylinders. Here, the separation bubble can be considered to be larger than for the
streamlined bodies.
Non-aerodynamic bodies refer to bodies with fixed points of
separations of the flow, and account for bodies with sharp edges. The separation
bubble for non-aerodynamic bodies might account for the whole effective body [3,

Aerodynamic performance of a body can be presented by a function of the force
coefficients over the Reynolds number. The drag coefficient, especially, is of interest
when considering the aerodynamic performance, and the curve of the function for the
drag coefficient can be characterized by four different ranges, as shown for a circular
cylinder in Figure 3.2.

The curve starts in the subcritical flow régime where the flow is not affected by the
surface roughness of the body. It should be noted that the drag coefficient is to be
expected to remain at 1.2 in this specific régime for circular cylinders [6, p. 329].
Subsequently, the curve moves into the critical flow régime as the Reynolds number
increases, where the drag coefficient is expected to drop as it is affected by the surface
roughness. This drop is referred to as the drag crisis. A further increas in the Reynolds
number results in the drag coefficient increasing, which is the range denoted as the
supercritical flow régime. After the supercritical range follows the transcritical flow
régime, where the drag coefficient remains constant [4, p. 41].

3.2
Cable vibrations
An additional phenomena that occur with aerodynamic loading on bridge cables is the
effect of cable vibrations. Cable vibrations may be present for different reasons, either
by induced loading from rain, wind, and/or ice. Over the years, span lengths of cable-
stayed bridges have increased, naturally resulting in longer cable lengths as well. As
a product of the increasing cable length, the cables are more susceptible to vibrations
due to low structural damping[12, p. 12]. Cable vibrations can be categorized by the
cause of the vibration, although this project will only consider galloping.
3.2.1
Galloping instability
Galloping instability is a dynamic motion of a single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF) sys-
tem that occur with flow turbulence or from flow separation, and acts perpendicular
to the flow direction. In terms of flow separation, asymmetrical flow separation stems
from asymmetrical roughness and features on circular cylinders. Thus, it can be ex-

instability. The instability takes the form of a supporting force rather than a resisting
force, where the vibrations in both directions generate alternating lift forces that are
constantly adding energy into the vibrational action [7, p. 49]. Total damping ζ of a

3.3
Ice accretion
Ice accretion is the build up of ice on objects, and all ice accretion is caused by
atmospheric icing. Atmospheric icing is defined by the procedure of any droplet, rain,
or wet snow that falls from the atmosphere and freezes and sets onto any object [8,
p. 5]. Further on, the formation of ice on objects is dependant on the characteristics
of the particles that hit the object. These particles can take the shape of super-cooled
droplets, solid ice or a combination of the two. Also, their velocity they have when they
hit the object and their trajectory are significant as well. The trajectory depends on
the particles density. Smaller particles may deflect off their path while they follow the
flow towards the object, while larger particles are more inclined to stick to the objects
surface. Figure 3.3 shows how the droplets may behave when drifting with the flow
towards a circular cylinder.
Figure 3.3. Droplet trajectory around circular cylinders [8, p. 40]
Atmospheric icing can be divided into two main types of icing: precipitation icing
and in-cloud icing. Precipitation icing accounts for glazed ice from freezing rain or
drizzle and wet snow. Glazed ice forms from freezing rain or wet in-cloud icing, where
large droplets partly freezes on impact with an object, and is characterised by smooth
distributed ice accretion with a transparent color. As glaze forms at temperatures near
freezing point, the droplets may flow around objects due to wind or gravity, ultimately
forming icicles.
Wet snow sticks to objects as melted snow crystals, and forms onto objects when the
air temperature is slightly above freezing point. In case the temperature decreases,
the wet snow will freeze and result in a growth of varying strength and white color.
In-cloud icing accounts for rime ice, and as pointed out earlier: for glaze ice too.
Rime ice completely freezes on impact with an object and its shape is characterised
by eccentrically formed rime vanes that are pointing windward. Depending on the
accretion rate, rime ice can be defined as either hard rime or soft rime. Hard rime is
defined by its higher density than soft rime and its opaque color, while soft rime has

4.1
Earlier work
The preceding work conducted by Dinesen considers the feasibility of photometric
scanning and reproduction of ice accretion on circular cylinders. While the approach
proved a certain feasibility in the sense of recreating the ice accretion, Dinesen con-
cluded that the method was not accurate enough to replace real ice for aerodynamic
testing. Several challenges were met during Dinesen’s project, which will be addressed
in this section and further integrated in following sections. Firstly, the camera that
was used to conduct the photometric scanning was initially equipped with a zoomable
lens, which resulted in photos with different focal lengths. The zoomable lens allowed
for slightly spontaneous changes of the focal length during the photo session, and was
therefore exchanged by a fixed lens to ensure equal focal length throughout the pho-
tometric scanning and guarantee a continuous level of precision.
Aerial photographing with a handheld camera was considered when the length of a
tripod did not allow the camera to access the motive properly. An aerial photography
would introduce unwanted noise to the photos, the approach was therefore discarded,
and the current tripod was exchanged with a tripod that could provide a sufficient
height. Additionally, having the ISO at the lowest level and the exposure time adjusted
accordingly proved favorably for the quality of the photos as these settings minimized
noise.
Before the ice accretion process began, white spray paint was added to the dry cable on
the side where the ice accretion would not be as prominent as it would be for the front
part of the dry cable. The paint would act as a map for the modelling software, easing
the process of piecing together the photos and ensuring that the bridge cable would
be modelled correctly. The lighting conditions were considered as well. The Climatic
Wind Tunnel at FORCE has built-in spotlights in the ceiling serving as the only light

4.4
Assessment of approach for photometric scanning
Photometric scanning is a scanning method where a large dataset, consisting of photos
taken of a subject, is gathered to reconstruct a digital 3D model. Here, the term subject
refers to the photographic object of interest. Before conducting photometric scanning
of any subject, it is essential to define how the scanning should be conducted, or
specifically which tool and which settings will be used. These factors are decisive
as they might lead to either a poor or satisfying end result. The modelling software
that has been used for processing the images is Agisoft Metashape, and guidelines
on how to proceed with photometric scanning are provided by Agisoft [11], and in
this section the method will be described and adopted according to this particular
project’s requirements. Furthermore, an assessment of the method’s viability will be
conducted by recreating a digital 3D model of the bridge cable with the artificial ice
made by Dinesen.
4.4.1
Camera settings
The camera selected for the photometric scanning was a Canon EOS 700D with a
Sigma 28mm F1.4 DG HSM lens. The lens is a fixed lens with focal length of 28mm.
In case a zoom lens was used, it is recommended that the focal length should be set
to either minimum or maximum during the photo session to ensure satisfying results
[11, p. 5]. As elaborated in section 4.1, a fixed lens would in most cases be preferred
over a zoom lens as a zoom lens could introduce the possibility of the focal length
spontaneously changing. Therefore, using a zoom lens was not considered for the
photometric scanning in this project.
In order to ensure that each photo would produce the same result, the camera was set
to manual mode, meaning that its settings would not change during the photo session
unless the user changed them. Furthermore, the images would be captured in the
RAW format, as RAW contains the most amount of information compared to any other
format, as the images taken would be uncompressed. When adjusting the settings of
the camera, it is important to keep in mind that the most significant settings are the
ISO value, the shutter speed and the aperture. These settings are described below.
• ISO - a camera’s sensitivity to light, Low ISO is related to low sensitivity to light,
while high ISO is related to high sensitivity to light. Low ISO often results in a
higher quality photo as it limits the amount of noise and graininess that might
appear. Therefore, the ISO should be kept at the lowest possible value to ensure
that the camera captures high quality photos.
26
==PAGE:44==
4. Preliminary considerations
Technical University of Denmark
• Shutter speed - the length of time measured in seconds that the shutter of the
camera stays open until the photo is taken, exposing the camera sensor to light.
A longer shutter speed, also referred to as slow shutter speed, means that more
light is absorbed by the camera sensor, resulting in a higher quality image, but
also introduces the possibility of motion blur to the image if the subject were to
move. It is recommended that the shutter speed is not too slow, but not too fast
either, depending on the photographic scene.
• Aperture - the size of a lens’ opening where light can pass through, measured in
f/stops, also called f-numbers that are written as fractions. Lower f-numbers are
related to higher aperture and higher exposure to light, and higher f-numbers are
related to lower aperture and lower exposure to light. For instance, a f-number of
1.4 results in higher aperture, while a f-number of 5.6 results in lower aperture.
Additionally, aperture adjusts the depth of field where small aperture leads to
greater depth of field, and high aperture leads to smaller depth of field.
When adjusting these camera settings, it is also important to consider how they might
influence each other as they adjust the final exposure. Large aperture results in more
light passing through the lens’ opening. This can be compensated by a faster shutter
speed which limits the amount of light that the camera is exposed to, and finally the
ISO level adjusts how sensitive the camera lens should be to light. Depending on the
setting of the photographic scene, these settings need to be adjusted accordingly. The
settings that were selected to conduct the photometric scanning in DTU building 119,
to ensure the highest possible quality, are provided in Table 4.7.

Procedure of capturing images
Any photographic session should be planned before starting capturing images, to
avoid unwanted fore-or backgrounds from being included in the image. Additionally,
completely flat surfaces should be avoided as a subject, since Metashape has
difficulties recognising depths for such surfaces [11, p. 6]. Also, most of the image
frame should be taken up by the subject, but not the whole frame as that might result
in parts of the subject being missed. In order to ensure that the subject is reconstructed
satisfactorily when being processed in Metashape, it is essential to capture a sufficient
amount of images of the subject, which oftentimes involves taking more photos than
what is required where the images should all be captured at different angles and
heights.

4.4.3
Lighting conditions
When conducting photometric scanning of an object with shiny or transparent
properties, the light conditions should simulate those of a cloudy day [11, p. 5]. This
due to the fact that bright light, or bright spots caused by light reflections, lead to
difficulties regarding reconstruction of the model, as bright light seldom contains any
information that a modelling software can process. Therefore, the camera flash should
be turned off as well. The photographic scanning was carried out at DTU building 119
for the assessment of the method with the already printed artificial ice, and in the CWT
at Force for the main experiment with real ice. Both locations raise different problems
regarding lighting conditions.
At DTU building 119, the photographic scene was in a large assembly hall with
spotlights in the ceiling that emitted strong, bright light which could not be turned
off. Therefore, the images had to be taken without adjustments of the lights. However,
since the ceiling height is rather tall, there was no risk of capturing the spotlights
in the image frame. Other conditions applied at the CWT at Force. The tunnel has
integrated spotlights that are meant to function as working lights, and additional light
sources on the outside. In this instance, the integrated light sources in the CWT were
turned off, and two spotlights with softboxes, which emit a softer light with equal light
distribution, were placed on either side of the bridge cable, serving as the only light
source, as discussed in section 4.1.
4.4.4
Modelling of artificial ice
After the photometric scanning of the artificial ice at DTU in building 119, the
photos were losslessly converted from RAW to TIFF (tag image file format) in Adobe
Photoshop. Through losslessly conversion, there is no loss of information taking place
during the conversion, as file compression is prevented and the resolution is retained.
Also, the conversion is necessary as a RAW file is a flat image, while the TIFF format is
a pixelated variation of the RAW format that is more suitable for digital processing.
In order to run Metashape effectively, it is recommended by Agisoft to have a computer
with at least 16GB of RAM, a CPU processor that can run at least at 2GHz and a GPU
with at least 1024 unified shaders. A virtual computer was provided to run Metashape,

with the following specifications: 16GB of RAM and a CPU that could run at 3.2GHz

The TIFF images were first loaded into Metashape where unwanted images with noise
were removed.
An alignment of the images was then carried out by the software
where the images were positioned according to the camera’s focal length and the
image’s coordinates - at which angle they were shot. Also, Metashape considers the
commonalities between the images, ensuring that they are aligned correctly.
The
preciseness of the alignment can be adjusted in terms of accuracy.
A higher accuracy results in a more accurate alignment of the images, though with the
disadvantage of increased computational time. Lower accuracy, on the other hand,
gives a less accurate alignment where the result might not be of satisfactory quality,
but the computational time is reduced. Furthermore, at High accuracy the images
are processed in their original size, at Medium accuracy the images are downscaled,
and at Low accuracy the images are even further downscaled. On the contrary, at
the Highest accuracy images are upscaled, so that commonalities between images are
accurately identified, though this level of accuracy might not be reasonable to apply as
it drastically increases the computational time. Therefore, the accuracy was set to High
to save some computational time, but still managed to obtain a result of satisfactory
quality. The alignment can be adjusted by other settings, too, though they have been
set as default due to not enhancing the alignment further.

After the alignment is finished, a dense point cloud can be created from the aligned
images. The generation of the dense point cloud is computed by each image’s depth,
where all the images are finally merged together, including their mapped depth. The
quality of the dense point cloud can be adjusted similarly to the accuracy for the
alignment. The quality of the dense point cloud was set to High, again to ensure a
satisfactory result for the model without an immense increase in computational time.
During the generation of the dense point cloud, a depth filter is applied in the
overlapping areas between the aligned images to reduce the effect of images with
noise or poor focus have on the end result. The depth filter can be adjusted to Mild,
Aggressive, and Moderate. The Mild filter mode is useful for models that contain small,
important features that still should be included in the model, while the Aggressive filter
mode is a rougher filter, and is useful for models that do not contain small significant
features. The Moderate filter mode falls between Mild and Aggressive filtering. Also,
the depth filter can be disabled, though it is not recommended [11, p. 23]. The Mild
filter was selected for this project as the model contains small features that should not
be excluded. Other adjustments to the dense point cloud could be made, though they
were set to default as they were considered to not affect the end result.
As a final step, a mesh can be generated. The source data that will be used to generate
the mesh can be selected. For this project, the dense point cloud is used as the source
data since it generates a high quality output, though with the disadvantage of increased
computational time. The surface type that was selected for the modelling of the mesh
was an arbitrary surface type, which is suitable for any kind of surface, though with
an increased cost of memory consumption. Still, the arbitrary surface type is ideal
for modelling ice as its surface more often than not is irregular. The final adjustment is
made for the face count which determines the optimal amount of polygons that should
be generated in the mesh. The level of detail is given by the levels of High, Medium,
and Low. The face count was set to High for this project in order to generate a model
with a satisfyingly high level of detail. Other options for the mesh could be applied as
well, but has not been considered in this project. The final mesh of the artificial ice

middle large enough for the bridge cable to fit through, were temporarily mounted
onto the wall and later adjusted so that the bridge cable was positioned exactly
in the centre of the holes in the splitter plates.
By introducing splitter plates to
the experimental setup, the air flow distortion at the ends of the bridge cable was
minimised, which made sure that the flow around the whole cable was uniformly
distributed [12, p. 142]. Making sure that the bridge cable was placed in the centre
of the holes in the splitter plates mitigated the potential risk of forces being transferred
from the cable to the splitter plates. The inner tube with the outer tube tightly fixed
onto it was then fit onto the flexible joints.
Four 190mm aluminium tubes for each splitter plate were then placed in between the
walls of the CWT and the plates fixed onto the walls, providing the necessary distance
for the later application of a cover that would shield the flexible joints from the wind.
The transducers that were connected to the testing rig were further adjusted, so that
the bridge cable was located in the centre, before finally fixing them onto the rig. Both
the cover, how they are fitted around the flexible joint, and how the splitter plates are

5.3
Aerodynamic performance of dry bridge cable (T2) in CWT
An aerodynamic performance test of the dry bridge cable was conducted to establish
an overview of its performance before any ice accretion had taken place. Additionally,
the tests would make it evident whether the experimental setup was sufficiently robust
to withstand the desired wind speeds. The generated forces from the wind were logged
and recorded in LabView where the sample time was increased to 30 seconds, while
the sampling frequency was left untouched at 2048Hz. The bridge cable was tested for
angles of attack of 0◦, 90◦, and 180◦where the screw keeping the inner tube in place
was loosened in order to rotate the bridge cable. After the bridge cable was rotated to
the next angle of attack, the screws were tightened again.
The wind speeds were adjusted by the CWT’s measure of rounds per minute(rpm).
For each angle of attack, the bridge cable was sequentially subjected to winds
of 100rpm, 300rpm, 500rpm, 750rpm, 1000rpm, 1200rpm, 1400rpm, and, finally,
1500rpm—ultimately producing an image of the flow around the bridge cable. The
following workflow for the aerodynamic performance tests was adopted:

2. Set wind speed to 100rpm.
3. Log data when pressure is stabilized.
4. Repeat for wind speeds until 750rpm is logged.
5. Set wind speed to 0rpm.
6. Log data for another zero measurement when pressure is stabilized.
7. Set wind speed to 1000rpm.
8. Log data when pressure is stabilized.

After conducting the aerodynamic performance tests, the obtained forces were

recorded forces are disproportionately larger than the differential pressure, which is a
result of the transducers’ difficulty of recording forces at a low wind speed. Hence, it is
not reasonable to include these values in the plots. Therefore, it was determined that
lift and drag coefficients of Reynolds numbers lower than 0.18·105 would be excluded
from the plots. The aerodynamic performance of the dry cable is shown in Figure 5.11

5.10
Modelling
In the following, the process of post-processing and preparing the model for 3D-
printing will be thoroughly described, where the used software is Autodesk Meshmixer.
Here, a section from the photometrically scanned rime ice R2 will be used as the
basis for the approach. It should be noted that every model is different, and therefore
different approaches to post-processing might be necessary. In other words, not every
approach works similarly for every model. Thus it is essential to figure out how a model
should be post-processed on forehand. The mesh was firstly imported from Agisoft as
an stl-file. Since the mesh did not have a defined thickness, it was necessary to convert
the mesh into a solid file, with a specified thickness, before the model was ready to be
3D-printed.
After importing the mesh, it was scaled according to the bridge cables true size, where
the bare bridge cable that was not covered with any rime ice was discarded with
the plane cut tool. Thereafter, the plane cut tool was used to cut the mesh into 9
sections, 8 of about 18.5cm and one of 11.3 cm, where the sections were processed as
individual files. This due to Meshmixer’s limitations of handling large data files. It was
observed that processing the whole mesh as one file often resulted in either models of
poor quality with missing sections, which Meshmixer decided to discard, or that the
software terminated leading to loss of progress.
Another observation was that Meshmixer was not capable of immediately transform-
ing the sections into solids as they did not have enclosing boundaries. It was there-

ensured an enclosed shape that Meshmixer could transform into a solid. Firstly, an
inspection of the mesh was conducted to find any holes or flaws in the mesh that re-

For enclosed holes, the selection tool was used to select the boundary edge of the hole,

6
Results
In this chapter the results from the main experiments, consisting of the tests with real
ice and artificial ice, will be presented. The results will be presented in a chronological
order, from test R1 to test ADR. Each test follows similar approach as described
in Chapter 5, besides the tests of the artificial ice where only the aerodynamic
performance was of interest. Lift and drag coefficients will be computed for all the
tests, and the Den Hartog criterion will be carried out for tests R2, AR, ARR, and ADR.
Spline interpolation was applied to determine the variation of CL, CD, and H over
smaller changes of α in order to sufficiently identify possible instability regions.
This, because the measured values would only provide a limited picture of where
the instability regions might occur. The interpolation was carried out for steps of
∆α = 1◦by the bessel spline interpolation method, with the add-in for Excel “Data
Curve Fit Creator Add-in” provided by SRS1 Software. Cubic spline interpolation could
have been applied instead, although bessel spline provides a parabolic fit with less
oscillations than cubic spline[13], and was therefore selected for the interpolation.
Contour plots of the instability regions for the Den Hartog criterion will be provided,
where the software OriginPro 2023 has been used to generate the plots. It should
be mentioned that OriginPro interpolates between the imported data points when
generating contour plots, and so the plots might not be exact, but still provide
necessary information about the instability regions. Comparisons of the results will
be made in Chapter 7.

Comparative Analysis
This chapter will address the comparison of the results that were presented in Chapter
6. Primarily, the comparisons should consider the similarity or dissimilarity between
real ice, considering test R2 and test DR, and artificial ice.
Here, by means of
investigating the variations of CL and CD over Reynolds numbers, and additionally
galloping through the Den Hartog criterion for the comparisons of R2 with the different
modifications of artificial ice. The comparison by means of the Den Hartog criterion is
carried out by overlaying one contour plot over another, and identifying whether their
instability regions are matching or not. As some of the plots might convey the same
essence of each comparison, a selection of plots within each test series have been
made. All the comparisons can be found in Appendix F. It is worth noting that due
to the difference in viscosity of air as a result of the difference in temperature during
the test with real ice and the artificial ice, there is a difference in Reynolds numbers,
even though the tests were conducted with the same airspeed. See Appendix B for the
relation between airspeeds and Reynolds numbers for the different tests.

8. Discussion
Technical University of Denmark
8
Discussion
This chapter will discuss the observations and comparisons that were made from the
previous chapters, where the discussion will be used to finally draw a conclusion for
the project in Chapter 8. The key findings of this project can be boiled down to the
following:
• Highest quality mesh model achieved with the light condition of only spotlights
at level 25, with softboxes.
• High correlation in aerodynamic behaviour at Re ≥1×105 between the 2nd rime
ice test (R2) and the tests with artificial rime ice (AR) and artificial degraded rime
ice (ADR).
• High correlation in aerodynamic behaviour between the test with degraded rime
ice (DR) and the artificial degraded rime ice at Re ≥1×105.
• Dissimilarities in aerodynamic behaviour between the 2nd rime ice test and the
artificial rime ice with additional roughness (ARR).
• Correlation for instability regions between the 2nd rime ice test and the tests with
the different modifications of artificial rime ice and degraded rime ice for limited
regions.
The photometric scanning of the 1st rime ice test, the 2nd rime ice test and the test
with degraded rime ice was conducted with different light configurations in order to
determine which light configuration resulted in the highest quality mesh model, as
elaborated in Section 5.8. After inspecting the mesh models from the photometric
scans of each test, it was apparent that the spotlights, at level 25 with softboxes was
the optimal light configuration. This, because the mesh model for the 2nd rime ice test
was generated without any holes in the surface of the bare bridge cable. Additionally,
the light configuration provided sufficient light for both the lower-and upper side of
the bridge cable.
On the contrary, the light configuration applied for the photometric scanning of the
1st rime ice test with only using the built-in working lights in the CWT proved that the
lighting of the lower side of the bridge cable was poor. For the photometric scanning
of the degraded rime ice, the spotlights with softboxes were again used, but with light
level 20. Here, it was expected that the lower light would provide higher contrast
between the ice and the bare bridge cable, minimising potential reflection of light.
The generated mesh model of the degraded rime ice was of sufficient quality, while
the parts with the bare bridge cable had holes.
This suggested that the provided
light was not enough, resulting in parts of the bare bridge cable not having any
depth information. As discussed in Section 6.3.2, the holes in the mesh model of the
degraded rime ice was not a major hindrance as they could be repaired.

9. Conclusion
Technical University of Denmark
9
Conclusion
Preliminary investigations of the testing equipment were carried out at DTU building
119 to assess the accuracy, especially for the force transducers, and to verify if the
equipment was suitable for further testing in the CWT. Firstly, calibration tests were
conducted for the transdcuers, that were isolated, then a several series of calibration
tests were carried out for the transducers with an aluminum tube in between, and
finally aerodynamic performance tests were conducted for the aluminum tube. As
the results were satisfying, it was concluded with that the testing equipment could be
used for aerodynamic performance tests. The experimental setup in the CWT was then
assembled, with a bridge cable as the testing object. Aerodynamics performance tests
were carried out for the bare bridge cable, to verify the experimental setups robustness,
which proved to be satisfying. Thereafter, the first main experiment, the 1st rime ice
test, could commence. Ice accretion was conducted on the bridge cable, and the
ice was photometrically scanned and thereafter tested for aerodynamic performance.
Unfortunately, due to the cooling unit malfunctioning, the testing of the 1st rime ice
had to terminate due to the cooling unit not being able to maintain a low enough
temperature, resulting in the ice melting.
A 2nd rime ice test was conducted. Again, the ice accretion on the bridge cable was
executed, and the ice was photometrically scanned. Aerodynamic performance tests
were then carried out for the bridge cable with the 2nd rime ice for angles of attack
from 0◦to 90◦. The former rime ice was degraded after the aerodynamic performance
tests were complete. It was also of interest to investigate the aerodynamic behaviour
of the degraded rime ice, and to produce a 3D-print of it as well. The degraded rime
ice was photometrically scanned, and then tested for aerodynamic performance for
angles of attack of 180◦and 0◦. Different light conditions were applied for the different
tests in order to determine what level of light resulted in the highest quality mesh
model. The images of both the 2nd rime ice and the degraded rime ice were imported
to Metashape, where a mesh model was generated.
By comparing the generated
mesh models, it was apparent that the mesh model of the 2nd rime ice provided the
highest quality mesh model with the used light condition of spotlights, at level 25, with
softboxes. The mesh models were then further post-processed in Meshmixer before
the files were ready to be 3D-printed.
Three aerodynamic performance tests were conducted, here of the artificial rime ice,
the artificial rime ice with additional roughness, and the artificial degraded rime ice,
and the tests were compared with the results of the lift-and drag coefficients for the
2nd rime ice and the degraded rime ice. Additionally, instability regions determined
by the Den Hartog criterion were compared with those of the 2nd rime ice.
In
general high correlation was found between the 2nd rime ice test compare to the
artificial rime ice and the degraded rime ice, for both the lift-and drag coefficient.
The correlation of the aerodynamic behaviour was identified for Reynolds numbers
higher than 1×105, whereas at lower Reynolds numbers, the drag and lift coefficients

10. Further Investigations
Technical University of Denmark
10
Further Investigations
As elaborated in Chapter 8, the correlation of instability regions between the 2nd rime
ice test and the other tests with artificial ice were not satisfying to the extent that it
proved that artificial ice could replicate real ice. Therefore, a further investigation of
the Den Hartog criterion and the comparison of instability regions between real and
artificial ice should be conducted. Further on, as the amount of data and models of the
artificial ice is increasing, developing a database for all the ice models, for future use,
and for conservation-and sharing purposes, should be carried out.
The replication of aerodynamic behaviour with artificial ice proved successful for lift
and drag, and therefore it might be of interest to investigate the application area.
A reasonable assumption is that one should acquire similar results independently
of bridge cable and applied artificial ices scale. Therefore, an investigation of the
aerodynamic behaviour of artificial ice should be conducted for different scaled sizes,
and the results should be compared with that of the true scale. Also, as the method
of replicating ice involves 3D-printing, the effect the filament size of the print has on
the aerodynamic behaviour should be looked into. Lastly, the degradation of ice after
several performance tests should be considered as a further investigation as well.
