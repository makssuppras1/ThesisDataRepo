Abstract
Stricter and stricter emission regulations from International Maritime Organization
(IMO), such as Energy E’¼āciency Design Index (EEDI) and Emission Control Areas
(ECAS) have provided big challenges to the engine designing work nowadays. One simple
example is that a global SO2 emission control of 0.5% will be established by the year 2020.
But the sulphur content in heavy fuel oil is 3.5%. However, Dim-ethyl ether (DME) is a
fuel with the properties of non-sulphur, soot-free and high cetane number (around 60).
These makes DME a perfect future fuel for diesel engine to compromise with the coming
regulations and make the engine more environmentally-friendly.
DME is also a fuel with low lower heating value (LHV), low viscosity and high volatil-
ity, which resemble lique’¼üed petroleum gas (LPG). These also make DME a much di’¼Ćerent
fuel than normal diesel. So, before heading to application of DME engine, it is rational
to predict the DME spray structure in engines. Thus, the model in present thesis is
created to provide the mixture pro’¼üle for fuel spray. The present model could be roughly
divided into three functional sections: liquid length (LL) calculation, stoichiometric mix-
ing line penetration (Ls) calculation and varying Full-width spreading angle (FW angle)
approaching methods. The liquid length calculation function is modi’¼üed and integrated
from SiebersŌĆÖs[55] model while stoichiometric mixing line penetration calculation function
is modi’¼üed and integrated from Musculus and KattkeŌĆÖs[60] model. Combined studies from
Siebers[55] and Richards[26] with researches from Engine Combustion Network (ECN), FW
spreading angle approaching function comes out. The author exerted the database from
ECN to mathematically ’¼üt, interpolate and ’¼ünd the approaching methods and expres-
sions for varying FW angle throughout the whole spray. These expressions and methods
could give precise descriptions for the varying FW angle with respect to di’¼Ćerent devel-
oping phases of the fuel spray, namely initial region, transition region and self-similar
region. Thus, compared to SiebersŌĆÖ and Musculus and KattkeŌĆÖs model with constant FW
angle, some improvements for the results from present model could be implemented. To
con’¼ürm the model working capabilities, various validations for present model have been
implemented. The validations include: normal vehicle engine, large marine engine, at-
mospheric propane gas jets and DME engine applications. Among these validations, the
whole mixture pro’¼üles have been validated for normal vehicle engine; and stoichiometric
mixing line have been checked for large marine engine, atmospheric propane gas jets and
DME engine applications.
The validations show favorable aligning.
Thus, combining with present model, a
method which is modi’¼üed from geometrical similarity theory is utilized for designing
the DME spray in target engine. The target engine is a small size marine engine for
small or medium size ’¼üshing boats. It comes from Weichai 226B-3 series with 4 strokes.
The results shows that due to the very di’¼Ćerent physical and thermal properties of DME
compared to normal diesel, the designing parameters for injector and operation conditions
for the target engine also greatly change.

1.1
Context
The advantages of treating DME as an alternative fuel are presented in this section,
especially in subsection (1.1.3) and subsection (1.1.4). And, throughout time line, the
more and more rigorous regulations issued out by IMO are analyzed in subsection (1.1.1)
and subsection (1.1.2). Regarding to these regulations, the more environmental-friendly
DME fuel will diminish the soot, NOX and SOX emission. Regardless of these environ-
mental progressive elements, the commercial merits of DME fuel will also be studied in
subsection (1.1.4), which have strong connection with enforcing the DME into reality
utilization.
1.1.1
Regulation for pollution gases
The exhausting gases from the ship have the possibility to form chemical substances
in the air which will have negatively in’¼éuence to global climate by reinforcing global
warming, acid rain and etc.
To supervise the ships reducing harmful emission, with the assistance of Annex VI
of IMOŌĆÖs pollution prevention treaty (MARPOL), IMO states that the international
shipping ought to have series mandatory-e’¼āciency measures. These measures could be
sorted into three parts: making new ship better; making existing ship better; and driving
ship better.
The measurements requirements would tend to be tougher and tougher
over the years. By the year 2025, all new ships are required to be 30% more e’¼āciency
compared to the average e’¼āciency for ships built between 2000 and 2010. And these
measurements are rooted in the regulations for legally inspecting. So, all these measures
and requirements are not an option, but necessities.
Among all those pollution gases, IMO mainly concernings are sulphur oxide (SOX) and
nitrogen oxide (NOX). Addressing these two emission problems as well as atmospheric
particulate matter (PM) which have connection with the soot emission, IMO have utilized
and de’¼üned several global Emission Control Areas (ECAS) for supervision[2]
ŌĆó Baltic Sea area de’¼üned in Annex I of MARPOL (SOX only);
ŌĆó North Sea area de’¼üned in Annex V of MARPOL (SOX only);
ŌĆó North American area (came into e’¼Ćect on 1 August 2012) de’¼üned in Appendix VII
of Annex VI of MARPOL (SOX , NOX and PM); and
ŌĆó United States Caribbean Sea area (came into e’¼Ćect on 1 January 2014) de’¼üned in
Appendix VII of Annex VI of MARPOL (SOX, NOX and PM).

Along with cavitation, there are another three mechanisms that may contribute to the

the distance from the nozzle tip to the location where the ’¼ürst droplet is formed.
As mentioned before, the shear velocity (velocity di’¼Ćerence between the injecting
fuel and ambient gases) on the interface imposes aerodynamic force which result in the
initialization of surface waves.
The surface waves grow in magnitude with time and
become more and more unsteady. At some point, the surface waves separate from the
jet.

Only

Nonetheless, in fuel spray,

Conclusions and summaries
(1) It is found that self-similar region of fuel spray can not be reached when the den-
sity variation is too large. Only after the spray have reached a momentum domain
region where the density ratio (Žülocal/Žüamb) is near one and the density variation is
small, the self-similar region is achievable. And from the place of reaching momen-
tum domain region, the Full-width spreading angle (FW angle) is only related to
momentum ’¼éux. For fuel spray, it is found when the density ratio is approximately
1.138 at the center-line, the requirement for "near one density ratio and the density
variation is small" is ful’¼ülled.
(2) Two expressions for converting Half-maximum spreading angle (HM angle) to FW
angle are obtained for velocity pro’¼üle and mass fraction pro’¼üle respectively: ╬Ė =
2.0514Žåv + 1.2890, and ╬Ė = 2.1520Žåm + 0.6619. Žåv and Žåm are separately represent
HM velocity angle and HM mass fraction angle. These two expressions are used in
present thesis when researching and ’¼ütting FW angle in self-similar region.
(3) Density ratio and momentum ’¼éux are detected as two elements that a’¼Ćect the FW
angle the most.
(4) It is found that a constant FW angle throughout the whole spray is only true after
reaching the self-similar region. Before that, the density ratio variation is large
and will markedly in’¼éuence FW angle. Thus, it is believed that the more rational
way for approaching FW angle is to vary the FW angle with respect to di’¼Ćerent
developing phase of the fuel spray, namely initial region, transition region and self-
similar region.

Future works
(1) The present model is a model for quasi-steady spray. For further studying and
more elaborate information regarding to spray developing, a more complex transient
model may need to be developed. This will extend the function and applying range
of present model.
(2) Although this non-vaporized model could provide fair prediction for vaporizing
spray, it shows that when the vaporization process is very ’¼üerce the prediction for
the liquid length is still obviously in’¼éuenced. Future study for minimize or cover the
in’¼éuence from vaporization may need to be done. This could enhance the accuracy
of prediction results for present model.
(3) The narrower representative FW angle in initial region should also be further stud-
ied, in order to remove the e’¼Ćects of it.
(4) Due to limitation of data, the validations for present model are still a little bit
con’¼üned and narrow. Every di’¼Ćerent conditions only have one or two cases for
validating. More validations for speci’¼üc and important conditions, such as large
marine engine applications and DME applications, may need to be implemented
for present model. This will further approve and guarantee the working abilities of
present model.
