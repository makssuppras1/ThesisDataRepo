Abstract
Modeling human mobility patterns is crucial for applications in urban planning, public
health monitoring, and recommendation systems.
Recent advancements in data ac­
cessibility and artificial intelligence have enabled more accurate modeling of these pat­
terns. This study leverages a global smartphone dataset tracking the movements of nearly
60,000 individuals to explore general mobility patterns and the factors influencing human
movement. Using deep learning models, including Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) re­
current neural networks and transformer architectures, we focus on the next semantic
prediction, which forecasts the type of location a user will visit next rather than the ex­
act geographic spot. Our approach offers a more reliable account of individuals’ routines
than previous studies that relied on voluntary data from Location­Based Social Networks.
By combining transformer architecture with time, user ID, and location type encodings,
we managed to link the context with the prediction performance and identify the informa­
tion these notions provide to the model. Our research uncovers significant patterns and
insights into human mobility behavior, enhancing our understanding of the underlying fac­
tors driving these movements.

Introduction
Modeling human mobility patterns has a wide range of applications within domains such
as urban planning [1, 2], public health monitoring [3], and recommendation systems [4].
Scientists have recognized the importance of this field for centuries [5], but recent ad­
vancements in data accessibility and Artificial Intelligence (AI) have paved the way for
more accurate models. The widespread adoption of smartphones and other electronic
devices enabling location tracking has generated a vast amount of data that can be uti­
lized for analyzing human movement patterns. In the last two decades, researchers have
explored various methods as documented in over 30 different publications [6], reflecting
the increasing importance and complexity of understanding human mobility dynamics.
Deep learning (DL), a subset of artificial intelligence (AI), has proven to be a transforma­
tive tool for analyzing and predicting complex patterns from vast datasets. Deep learning
involves training neural networks with multiple layers, allowing the model to learn and ex­
tract features from the data at various levels of abstraction. Unlike traditional machine
learning approaches, deep learning models such as Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM)
recurrent neural networks (RNNs) [7] and relatively new, yet powerful transformer archi­
tectures [8], excel at capturing temporal dependencies and non­linear relationships within
sequential data [9]. In mobility prediction, these capabilities are essential in discover­
ing underlying trends and behavioural patterns that influence how individuals move and
interact with their surroundings.
Despite the popularity of human mobility modelling among scientists, the problem remains
partially unresolved. One of the most commonly mentioned challenges [6], that makes
human mobility predictions inaccurate, is the random nature of human decisions, which
tend to alter their regular patterns. Even though the problem with the variability of hu­
man choices may seem difficult to overcome, understanding these random behaviours
presents an opportunity to delve deeper into the similarities and differences in mobility
patterns across individuals in society.
In this study, we aim to discover the general mobility patterns and the factors that human
mobility depends on. Our research relies on a subset of a global smartphone dataset,
encompassing the movements of almost 60,000 people [10]. It offers a more dependable
account of individuals’ routines compared to Location­Based Social Networks [11, 12],
which have been widely used in research on human mobility but are limited to sequences
of visits users choose to share on social media. We use this data to train multiple deep­
learning classification models based on LSTM and transformer architectures.
We seek to understand the patterns among users by focusing on predicting the semantics
of their potential subsequent location. Next semantic prediction, as this task is known,
aims to forecast the type or category of the next place a user will visit rather than its exact
geographic location. For instance, predicting that the next visit will be to a restaurant or
grocery store rather than predicting a specific restaurant or store. This approach differs
from the more challenging task of next location prediction, which attempts to pinpoint an
exact geographic spot [13].
Lastly, we evaluate and compare our deep learning modelling techniques against various
state­of­the­art baselines, using the results to uncover significant patterns and insights
into human mobility behaviour. This analysis allows us to assess the effectiveness of our
models and to better understand the underlying factors influencing human movement.

Literature Review
Human mobility patterns have fascinated researchers for decades. Thus, it comes with­
out surprise that the next location prediction challenge quickly gained popularity, as it
ultimately evaluates the quality of modelling human mobility.
The first significant publication touching upon next location prediction [14] leveraged ev­
idence theory to overcome limited data accessibility (cold start). The cold start problem
was revisited many times in recent decades, but the evidence theory did not gain popu­
larity in next location prediction. Between 2009 and 2015 there were several papers [15,
16, 17] that attempted to use pattern recognition to find similarities in human trajectories,
and further utilize it for location prediction. Simultaneously numerous attempts [18, 19,
20, 21] extracted contextual features for human trajectories and computed the prediction
under the probabilistic frameworks.
In the last decade, almost every next­location prediction paper has proposed a solution
that uses either Markov chain or neural network architecture. The Markov­based ap­
proaches [22, 23, 24, 25, 26, 27] outperform pattern recognition and probabilistic frame­
works in exploring unvisited locations, at the same time offering explainability of the hidden
layers and results. However, the greatest improvement in performance in next­location
prediction comes from leveraging deep neural network architectures. However, more ac­
curate predictions come at the cost of losing explainability.
Most neural network approaches in next­location prediction [28, 29, 30, 7, 31, 32, 33,
34, 35] use Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and the more advanced variants like Hi­
erarchical RNNs and Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM). The motivation for using RNNs
is to capture complex, non­linear relations in the sequence of visited places by the user.
However, the groundbreaking paper ”Attention is all you need” from 2017 [8] switched the
focus of the neural network attempts to the Transformer architectures [36, 37, 38]. The
research from 2017 shows that the encoder­decoder transformer architecture keeps the
sequential analysis accuracy of RNNs at a significantly lower training time.
The papers mentioned above used different datasets for their research, therefore they
approach the problem from a slightly different angle. The most popular, and easiest ac­
cessible, dataset type used for this purpose gathers information about social behaviours
from Location­Based Social Networks (LBSN), where people register their activity and
location in their social media profiles. Despite its popularity, this approach may be con­
sidered biased towards the activities that people share on social media. Alternatively,
numerous papers use Global Positioning System (GPS) Traces, where a group of users
is tracked with a GPS device that records the time and location of the user. Historically,
the GPS Trace datasets were collected from taxi drivers and public transport vehicles, but
with the increase in the popularity of smartphones, the GPS measurements usually come
from our pockets. GPS trace datasets are more difficult to collect, but have an advantage
over LBSNs, as they are more similar to real­life human traces.
In our research, we are focusing on the most recent methods for the next location predic­
tion, namely the LSTM and Transformer. However, we also implement a simple Markov­
based model, to benchmark the results of our deep­learning approaches. Additionally,
we develop our solutions using a GPS trace dataset.

Dataset
3.1
Data description
Our dataset was collected globally by a leading smartphone and electronics company
from 2015 to early 2019, involving users who consented to share their information for
research purposes. It comprises data from 7.1 million anonymised users, totalling 2.7
billion stops after GPS trajectories were spatially clustered using the Infostop algorithm
[39, 10]. Various subsets of this data have been used in previous studies dedicated to
the human mobility area [40, 41]. All data analysis complies with the EU’s General Data
Protection Regulation 2016/679 (GDPR).
Each stop in the dataset is associated with an anonymised user ID, location ID, latitude,
longitude, and the respective arrival and departure times. In our study, we utilize an al­
ready preprocessed dataset prepared by Peter Edsberg Møllgaard during his PhD. [10],
focused on the next semantic prediction. The methodology for attaching semantic infor­
mation to user locations involved a detailed process divided into two main subproblems:
estimating Home and Work locations from individual temporal trajectories, and attaching
spatial semantics from OpenStreetMap (OSM).
• Estimating Home and Work locations.To estimate home and work locations, the
algorithm used aims to detect changes in residential and occupational patterns by
splitting the user’s trajectory into daily sequences. In these sequences, a location
is chosen for each hour, prioritizing stops with the longest durations. For home esti­
mation, the frequency of visits during night hours (11 pm to 5 am) is computed over
42 days, averaging the hourly frequencies across all days with data. The location
with the highest frequency is assigned as Home. Similarly, work locations are es­
timated based on the frequency of visits during business hours (9 am to 5 pm) on
weekdays, using hourly and daily frequencies (the number of days the location was
visited across the time window). The location with the highest daily frequency, while
not being home, is labelled as Work. The rest of the locations receive Other label.
• Attaching spatial semantics. For attaching spatial semantics from OpenStreetMap,
the Python package Pyrosm [42] is used to retrieve points of interest (POI) and area
polygons from OpenStreetMap. For each POI or area, spatial coordinates, key, cat­
egory, and value tags are saved. This data is then integrated with user trajectory
data through a two­step process: checking if a user location lies within any area
polygon and assigning that area’s information, and then performing a spatial join to
match the closest POI within 50 meters.
Lastly, the dataset is filtered to include only the trajectories of users estimated to reside
in Sweden, considering only sequences entirely within the country. Travel trajectories
passing through Sweden are excluded unless the user has a permanent residence there.
After the mentioned processing, the dataset structure looks as follows:
• user ­ anonymised user ID
• loc ­ location ID (Each user’s location IDs are unique, signifying that the same ID
may not correspond to the same physical location across different users.)
• start, end ­ arrival and departure time

3.2
Preprocessing
Primarily due to computational constraints, only changes in location are taken into ac­
count. Consequently, if a user leaves a certain location and returns there without any in­
termediate stops, it is disregarded. Consecutive stops at the same place are thus merged
into a single stop, with the arrival time recorded from the first stop and the departure time
from the last stop, ensuring temporal coherence within a trajectory. This implies that small
circular walks are potentially excluded from the data if no stops are recorded along the
route.
Additionally, all stops without associated semantics were filtered out as our goal is to
predict these semantics. This ensures that only meaningful stops are included in our final
dataset.

aggregation, the dataset was reduced to 61.3% of its original size, resulting in 40.3 million
stops. Further filtering for missing semantics led to the removal of 21.1% of the remaining

For each user, we have one sequence that documents their visited locations throughout
the data collection process for that individual. Figure 3.1 demonstrates the wide range
of these sequences. On average, one sequence covers 410 days, but they can span
hours, days, months, or even years, sometimes with extended breaks in between. These
differences present a challenge in determining an appropriate modelling approach for
such diverse temporal patterns. We elaborate on our methodology for handling these
variations in Section 3.5.

4.1
Markov chain
As a baseline approach to our problem, we have chosen a Markov chain. Markov chains
provide a powerful framework for modelling sequential data. A Markov chain is a stochas­
tic process that satisfies the Markov property. This term refers to the memoryless property,
which asserts that the future state of the process depends only on the present state and
not on the sequence of events that preceded it [43].
Formally, a Markov chain consists of a set of states S = s0, s1, s2, ..., sn and a transition
matrix P. The transition matrix P is an nxn matrix where each element pij represents
the probability of transitioning from state si to state sj. The sum of the probabilities in
each row of the transition matrix is 1, ensuring that ∑
j pij = 1 for all i. The sequence
of states X0, X1, X2, ... forms the Markov chain, where Xt represents the state at time t.
The Markov property is mathematically expressed as:
P(Xt+1 = sn+1 | Xt = sn) = P(Xt+1 = sn+1 | Xt = sn, Xt−1 = sn−1, . . . , X0 = s0) (4.1)

key hyperparameters, the number of stacked LSTM layers and the size of layers, where

5.1
Baselines
Establishing baseline models is a fundamental step in machine learning and deep learning
research. Baselines provide a benchmark for evaluating the performance of new mod­
els, enabling researchers to measure improvements and confirm the effectiveness of their
methods. They also ensure the relevance and validity of research by showing that new ap­
proaches outperform simpler, well­known techniques. Their simplicity and interpretability
provide a clear reference point, making complex results easier to contextualize and un­
derstand.
In our study, we present several baseline models, including predicting the most frequent

5.4
Time encoding
To improve the Transformer150 model and make it more ”context­aware”, we embed time
information as an additional input feature. It is motivated to explore any additional recur­
rent relations in human mobility. According to Kazemi et al [46], the standard, the non­
periodic notion of time can be improved with the Time2Vec representation they propose
in the paper.
We explore the potential of this time encoding method, using the transformer architecture
with 4 attention heads and location embedding size 64, and time2vec with a kernel size
of 16. Surprisingly, this approach does not improve the prediction, reaching only a 0.552
F1 score and 0.627 accuracy. To get more insights into time2vec encoding, we plot its
hidden layer in Figure 5.5.
The idea of Time2Vec could be described as an algorithm that automatically detects and
extracts the features from time. As highlighted in the paper, Time2Vec was designed to
be a universal approach for different time­related problems. In our case, t2v detected
1­month and 1­week periodicity, which may have many confirmations in human mobility
behaviours. However, none of the embeddings manage to identify daily relations, which
is understandable considering most users do not have more than 3 locations recorded
during a day.
To capture the time features that time2vec couldn’t extract from our data, we manually
encode hour, day of the week, day of the month, and month number information as an
alternative way for time embedding. The comparison of the models that consider time
information is presented in Figure 5.6.

and analyse them to gain a better understanding of human mobility. This section com­

data analysis point of view.

the hyperparameters if we are searching for the most efficient solution.

not affect significantly neither F1 score nor accuracy. However, with the multiclass clas­

6
Discussion and Conclusion
In this research, we analyse the human mobility patterns throughout the next semantic
location prediction where we try to predict the next category of the location that the user
is going to visit. This approach is less accurate compared to the next location prediction
(forecasting the (x, y) coordinates of the user on the map), but its outputs are easier
for interpretation, and therefore the models are often more explainable. Our goal is to
use the next semantic prediction for a better understanding of human mobility, so we
focus this discussion on how well we managed to interpret the results of the model and
find interesting dependencies in mobility patterns, rather than on the model performance
evaluation. Nonetheless, as the model itself plays a pivotal role in this research, we
analyse its results as well, placing it among other next semantic location models that
have been developed in the past.
Throughout the testing of different modelling approaches, we trained over 20 models and
the most important ones are gathered in Table 5.10. The first significant performance
improvement comes from the architecture selection as the Transformer approach outper­
forms the LSTM. This observation indicates that the future of the next location prediction
lies within attention­based models rather than in recurrent neural network areas. Similar
observations have been made in other fields, which brings the question of how close we
are to having a common architecture choice for all problems within Machine Learning.
Another major model improvement is achieved by different processing of the input se­
quences. For the Transformer150 the input sequences group users’ stop locations so
that every trajectory vector contains one month of data. This approach turns out to be
superior compared to the previous attempt which does not take into account the time
factor in creating user sequences. We show in periodicity analysis 3.4 that time factor
is an important contextual information for semantic trajectories, therefore it comes with­
out surprise that standardizing the input sequence time length is beneficial for the model
performance.
The best­performing model is built with Transformer architecture, uses input sequences
grouped monthly, and the location type encoding that enriches every location with either
Home, Work, or Other label. The F1 score for this model is 0.557, which is over 75% higher
than the baseline model results. Additionally, the accuracy of 0.636 is over twice as good
as the one for baseline, meaning that the best models’ predictions are twice more often
correct than the ones from baseline. These comparisons with baseline may question the
adequacy of the Markov Chain approach we used to compare our models, but in reality,
our first attempts with LSTM were barely better than Markov, which highlights how far we
managed to push our modelling quality.
Despite the impressive progress our models made throughout the process, they eventu­
ally faced some limitations that were difficult to overcome. One of them is the random
nature of human mobility described by Chaoming Song et al. [54], which occurs when
people alter their paths from their regular cycles. One of the most common regular pat­
terns in human mobility is the work­home cycle which our model predicts correctly. In
Table 5.9 we observe that the Home class is correctly predicted over 97% of the time, and
the Work class 45% of the time, which is still a good result for the multi­class classifica­
tion problem with 737 classes. However, the performance of 0.233 F1 score for the Other
Understanding the Human Mobility Patterns Based on Next Semantic Prediction
37
==PAGE:46==
compartment shows that our model is not capable of predicting less frequent locations
correctly.
One of the features that could help predict the less common classes is the time that was
proven influential in the periodicity analysis 3.4. We tested two approaches for encoding
this information to the model, but none has significantly improved the prediction. It is diffi­
cult to point to a single reason for the inability of the model to capture the time periodicity,
as the deep learning solutions are more of a black­box architecture. We may suspect,
that the big range of frequencies of visits for different users could make the time patterns
more difficult to spot for the model, as some monthly trajectories had only single locations,
and others could have up to 150 locations.
During the work on the project, some ideas must have been abandoned due to the limited
duration of the study and delayed data access. In future work, several solutions could
be worth trying to extend and improve our research. Firstly, incorporating additional con­
text data ­ users’ age and gender ­ could provide deeper insights into users’ behaviour
across demographic groups. This information might help by identifying age or gender­
specific mobility patterns that are not captured solely by location semantics. Secondly,
employing a weighted cross­entropy loss function could improve model performance by
addressing class imbalances within the dataset, particularly for less frequent semantic
categories. Lastly, aggregating similar key tags into broader but meaningful categories
could reduce model complexity while preserving predictive accuracy. Within our dataset
of over 700 unique locations, numerous entries share similarities, such as swimming,
swimming area, swimming pool, which could be effectively grouped into a single category
without compromising their semantic meaning.
