Abstract
Machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) such as MACE offer near DFT accuracy at a
fraction of the computational cost. However, a limiting factor is the cost of generating highôÙ
quality training data. This thesis investigates the development of a systematic and costôÙefficient
approach to training robust MACE models. It is therefore investigated to what extent models
trained on the cheapest available structures (e.g., dimers and trimers) can extrapolate to larger
structures such as nanoparticles and vice versa.
To test this, an active learning workflow was implemented, iteratively adding structures with inôÙ
creasing numbers of atoms. The results show that widely used training strategies, including
standard active learning workflows, fail to reliably extrapolate to outôÙofôÙdistribution structures.
It was found that extrapolation performance deteriorates when extending structurally homoôÙ
geneous datasets. Tests on the influence of correlation order demonstrated that excessive
degrees of freedom can hinder generalization when not properly constrained. For example, reôÙ
ducing the correlation order from three to one for dimers significantly improved extrapolation to
nanoparticle test structures while maintaining accuracy for dimers themselves. This highlights
the importance of structural diversity and weight restriction from the outset.
The results suggest that the design of a structurally diverse initial dataset is of critical imporôÙ
tance. Therefore, an attempt was made to construct such a dataset, which successfully comôÙ
bined low computational cost with high accuracy across both small and large systems. At the
same time, this approach opens up important questions about how to define and quantify strucôÙ
tural diversity, and how to systematically design datasets that ensure the generalization needed
for atomistic simulations.

Introduction
Atomistic simulations are essential for understanding processes at atomic scale. Many microôÙ
scopic phenomena, e.g. adsorption and diffusion of molecules on surfaces, occur on time and
length scales that make direct measurements difficult. Atomistic simulations address this chalôÙ
lenge by providing detailed insight into reaction mechanisms, transition states, and structureôÙ
property relationships. In doing so, they help accelerate the discovery of new materials, such
as catalysts and batteries, for clean energy applications [1].
A central component of atomistic simulations is the potential energy surface (PES), a mathematôÙ
ical function that represents the total energy of a system as a function of the nuclear coordinates.
The PES thus determines which configurations are thermodynamically stable, how atoms move,
via forces as negative energy gradients, and how reactions proceed along a reaction coordinate.
Historically, the PES has been constructed either through analytical models (e.g. classical force
fields) or via expensive pointôÙbyôÙpoint calculations using quantum chemical methods [2]. Today,
the stateôÙofôÙtheôÙart quantum mechanical modeling method is Density Functional Theory (DFT),
which offers reliable accuracy for many systems. However, the computational cost of most DFT
codes approximately scales O(N 3), where N represents the number of electrons [3]. This limôÙ
its the practical applicability of DFT for very large systems, such as realistic catalytic models
like platinum nanoparticles containing hundreds of atoms. An approach to modeling PES at
lower computational cost is to use machine learning (ML) models [4]. These models learn the
relationship between the atomic structure and the corresponding energies and forces from a
limited set of calculations to accurately predict the energies and forces of new structures.
Semiempirical methods have long been used to bridge this gap by introducing physically moôÙ
tivated approximations and fitting to reference data, trading some generality and accuracy for
a reduction of the computational cost. In this sense, the machine learning potentials (also reôÙ
ferred to as machine learning force fields) can be regarded as a modern, dataôÙdriven version
of semiempirical methods, as they rely on reference data from electronic structure calculations
rather than solving the SchrûÑdinger equation explicitly. Among the existing machine learning
interatomic potentials (MLIPs), such as PaiNN [5], NequIP [6], SchNet [7], and GAP [8], the
MACE model [9] is a widely used model due to its accuracy. It currently achieves stateôÙofôÙtheôÙ
art accuracy on several established benchmarks, including rMD17, 3BPA, and AcAc [9]. This
demonstrates the MACE modelãs capability to match the performance of traditional methods
while being orders of magnitude faster. Figure 1.1 illustrates the central goal of MLIPs, which
is to combine near ab initio accuracy with the efficiency of classical force fields.

Computational cost can be defined as the resources required to perform a calculation and it is
measured in terms of the time and memory usage [10]. Reducing computational cost is therefore
desirable to minimize resource consumption and to make research practices more sustainable,
while also reducing the human effort required to set up DFT calculations and waiting for results.
For machine learning models, training is the most computationally expensive step. However,
once trained, a ML model has a fixed architecture and weights, and each prediction requires
only a small number of operations, such as message passing or propagation steps through the
network. In contrast to DFT, there is no need for an iterative selfôÙconsistent field loop, and the
computational cost grows much more favorably with system size. For MACE, the computaôÙ
tional cost depends on the systemãs local structure (e.g., average number of neighbors) and the
modelãs hyperparameter choices.
However, this cost consideration ignores the cost of generating the datasets, which distorts the
whole picture. The performance of MLIPs depends strongly on the quality and the amount of
training data [11]. Therefore, a good MLIP ultimately comes at the cost of expensive ab initio
data. This is especially relevant, given that there is currently no universal strategy for training
MLIPs, and designing datasets that yield high accuracy MLIPs is still largely guided by trial and
error.
A straightforward and intuitive approach to designing the datasets is to start with a set of strucôÙ
tures similar to the target system and iteratively refine it based on the modelãs performance
on a test set. While effective, this process relies heavily on expert judgment, introduces huôÙ
man bias, and limits reproducibility. To address these issues, more systematic strategies have
been explored. The most prominent ones being automated active learning workflows, that aim
to improve model performance and reproducibility, while reducing computational cost, human
bias, and manual effort [12, 13]. However, they generally do not take the computational cost of
creating the initial data set into account.

Since there is a scarcity of highôÙfidelity datasets, future applications will require generating trainôÙ
ing datasets from scratch. This creates a strong need for a costôÙefficient and systematic apôÙ

The goal of this thesis is to investigate what information can be extracted from the cheapôÙ

dataset. Specifically, it explores whether there is a systematic and costôÙefficient way to
train MACE potentials. A central question is: How well does extrapolation work in both
ways, meaning, to what extend can models trained on small structures such as dimers
and trimer extrapolate to large structures such as nanoparticles and vice versa.
In the context of organic molecules, experiments have suggested that MACE models are capaôÙ
ble of extrapolating with no loss of accuracy to larger fragments with more complex interactions,
which supports the premise of this thesis [14]. As a test system for this thesis platinum nanoparôÙ
ticles with adsorbed carbon monoxide were chosen, since it is a widely used model system in
heterogeneous catalysis studies, due to their relevance in industrial processes [15].

2.1.1
Graphs as Data Structure
A graph is formally defined as G = (V, E) [18]. It consists of a set of nodes V and a set of edges
E that connect pairs of nodes. Graphs provide a general language for describing and analyzing
entities with relations or interactions.
Many types of data can be represented as graphs. Examples include social networks, databases
and disease pathways. In the context of physical sciences and materials modeling, atomistic
systems are a particularly relevant class of graphôÙrepresentable data. Atoms can be interpreted
as nodes, and the bonds or interactions between them as edges [19]. Figure 2.1 illustrates this
mapping using a small molecule as an example.

MACE

2.2.4
MACEôÙFoundation Models
The MACEôÙFoundation models, such as MACEôÙMPôÙ0 [30], were developed with the aim of elimôÙ
inating the significant computational and human effort that is required for development and valôÙ
idation of potentials for each particular system of interest and the general lack of transferability
from one chemical system to the next. The motivation is to develop a single generalôÙpurpose ML
model. The MACEôÙMPôÙ0 model was trained on the Materials Project data [31], which consists
primarily of inorganic crystals and is heavily biased towards oxides. MACEôÙMPôÙ0 was found
to be capable of accurately simulating a surprising range of chemical properties in solid, liquid,
and gaseous phases. Also other foundation models showed great generalization performance.
Such foundation models, preôÙtrained on large structural and property databases, carry the hope
of running nearôÙDFTôÙaccuracy simulations at a fraction of the cost [30].
There are still reasons, why foundation models are not always the best choice for a given task.
Foundation models are typically trained on DFT data using a specific exchangeôÙcorrelation funcôÙ
tional (e.g., PBE for Materials Project). This hardwires functionalôÙspecific biases into the model.
If a different level of theory is more appropriate for the target system, the predictions from the
foundation model may be systematically off.
Second, artifacts in the training data will always be inherited by the model and the architecture
setting, such as the number of interactions or degree of correlation, can not be changed when
finetuning. Therefore one can only have full control over the training data, the level of theory, and
the inductive biases built into the model architecture when training the models from scratch. This
leads to better interpretability. Still, one of the key advantages of foundation models remains:
they can be fineôÙtuned with relatively little systemôÙspecific data, which in many cases offers a
good tradeôÙoff between generality and task accuracy.

3.2.1
Dimers
For all six combinations of the elements of the test system (Pt, C and O), the interatomic distance
between dimers was systematically varied. To capture both short and long range interactions
of the pair potential, the maximum interatomic distance rmax was set to 10 û for all pairs and
the minimum distance rmin was determined individually for each pair. This was done by starting
from the sum of the covalent radii (stored in ASE) and progressively reducing the distance by

Baseline Models
To provide a reference for comparison, baseline models were trained to reflect the ãtraditionalã
approach to training MACE potentials. While there is currently no well established or standardôÙ
ized training protocol, similarities in training strategies can be identified in the literature about
catalytic systems and solidôÙstate modeling with MACE.
The following approaches are commonly encountered in the literature when using MACE:
ãÂ A foundation model is used directly, without previous fineôÙtuning, usually as a component
in a larger workflow. It is used for example to generate initial structures for geometry
optimizations [43, 44].
ãÂ MACE models are trained using data generated from molecular dynamics (MD) simuôÙ
lations. The MD generated structures are then either picked randomly, or in an active
learning approach, picked by uncertainty, and used to finetune the model. Here, three
different approaches to generating the MD data appear in the literature:
ã Running MD with a classical interatomic potential known to yield reliable dynamics
(e.g., the StillingerôÙWeber potential for MoS2) [45].
ã Running MD directly with a foundation model (without previous finetuning) [11].
ã Creating an initial dataset, to fineôÙtune a foundation model on it, and then running
MD with the fineôÙtuned model [46].

ãÂ Completely disjoint subsets: The training data was split into five nonôÙoverlapping subôÙ

In MACE, the seed is used to ensure reproducibility of data loading (batches), dataset splitting,

code such as DataLoader initialization [56], the weight initialization still relies on the global ranôÙ

reproducibility by initializing model weights with a torch.Generator seeded with args.seed.

Four fineôÙtuning methods were tested in order to identify the most suitable approach for the
implementation into the active learning loop. The following methods were tested:
ãÂ Multihead FineôÙTuning: The pretrained model is trained on the new data while also reôÙ
playing the original training data and starting from foundation model weights.
ãÂ Naive FineôÙTuning: The model is finetuned only on the new data just by restarting from
the pretrained model weights.
ãÂ ãMixedã Naive FineôÙTuning: The model is finetuned on a dataset that combines both the
new data with the original training data, starting from foundation model weights.
ãÂ Training from Scratch with Mixed Data: A new model is trained from scratch on the
combined dataset of old and new structures, without using preôÙtrained weights.
The fineôÙtuning experiments were conducted on ensembles trained exclusively on one structure
type, namely dimers and quadrumers. The ensembles were trained using bagging and MACE
initialization on different random seeds. The goal was to evaluate how well models trained on
smaller structures can be finetuned to predict properties of larger, unseen, structures (dimers ã
trimers, quadrumers ãpentamers). An ideal fineôÙtuning method would minimize the prediction
error on unseen new data while preserving the knowledge from previous training.
The pentamer data set was created by performing local neighborôÙlist cutouts from a randomly
selected nanoparticle of the nanoparticle data pool. The selected nanoparticle consisted of 43

Conclusion
This thesis explored the development of a systematic and costôÙefficient training strategy for
MACE potentials by analyzing what information can be extracted from the cheapest available
data. The results demonstrate that traditional training approaches fail to generate models that
generalize to data outside of the training domain. None of the tests showed reliable extrapolation
from small to large structures and vice versa.
The practice of expanding training datasets with large amounts of data generated in the same
way (e.g., through MD), as it is done in most active learning workflows, was found to even
worsen the extrapolation performances. Notably, even when small structures like dimers are
included in the training set, the default MACE hyperparameters, especially the low default cutôÙ
off radius, introduce systematic errors in pair potential predictions. This limits the modelãs ability
to capture the basic chemistry, making the traditional training approaches illôÙsuited for modeling
catalytic reactions, which involve bond breaking and formation or adsorption and desorption
processes.
A significant finding is that the choice of random seed strongly influences the MACE modelãs
convergence. Simply by training on different random seeds, models converge to different local
minima. Even when trained on the exact same data, the difference in random seed introduces
enough diversity for reliable uncertainty estimations with ensembles. As a result, this finding
can significantly reduce the amount of data required for robust uncertainty estimates, since
disjoint datasets are no longer necessary. Furthermore, a naive finetuning strategy that comôÙ
bines seen and unseen data outperformed the recommended finetuning protocols in the MACE
documentation across the majority of tests.
The tested active learning workflow, that iteratively added structures with increasing number of
atoms, showed no convergence towards the NP test set. On the contrary, iteratively adding
larger structures resulted in increasing errors of unphysical magnitude on the NP test set, that
far exceeded the naive isolated atoms baseline. Tests on the influence of correlation order
used for training and the coordination numbers present in the training data suggested that the
unphysical errors can be avoided by restricting the weights from the start. This can be achieved
by using an initial dataset that spans over a wide range of coordination numbers.
Finally, the knowledge gained was combined in an attempt to design an improved training stratôÙ
egy. The final model was trained starting from a dataset diverse in structure sizes and coorôÙ
dination numbers, while prioritizing keeping the computational cost minimal. The final model
achieved near baseline model accuracy on the NP test set at a fraction of the cost, while also
showing high accuracy on the small structure test sets.

Chapter 5 ã Conclusion
So far, the challenge of generating a ãuniversalã potential that generalizes to all different kind of
structures has been approached with data augumentation. MACE models have been trained
on increasingly large and diverse datasets in the hope that the model will generalize. This
gave rise to the foundation models. While this strategy has yielded models with low errors on
inôÙdomain data, the results presented in this thesis show that extrapolation remains a critical
limitation. Even finetuning a pretrained model still requires structures very similar to the target
system, and, once fineôÙtuned, further extrapolation beyond the new training data can again not
be guaranteed.
The limited extrapolation performances of MACE reflects a fundamental property of such maôÙ
chine learning models. They excel at learning patterns present in the training data but do not
inherently capture the underlying physical principles unless explicitly encoded. The cases where
extrapolation appears to succeed come at a greater surprise. These cases raise the question
of what exactly enables a machine learning model to extrapolate.
The results suggest that the extrapolation performance worsens significantly, when the training
set lacks structural diversity. Coordination number distribution appears to be correlated with the
extrapolation behavior, but further study is needed to confirm its role. However, the question
on how to exactly define ãstructural diversityã in a quantitative way remains. Is there a way to
measure where the boundary of the training distribution lies and thereby determine the degree
of similarity at which a structure becomes effectively outôÙofôÙdistribution? Ensemble uncertainty
estimates provide a useful workaround for this problem by offering confidence estimates for
individual structures. But the question that stands at the end of this thesis is whether the problem
of identifying informative and diverse training data could be addressed analytically from the
outset. Such an approach would align with the broader goal of constructing training datasets
that are not only effective, but also minimize the computational cost.
The results of this thesis demonstrate that developing robust interatomic potentials requires
more than simply increasing dataset size, it depends on the structural diversity in the dataset.
Building on this, a clear next step is to apply wellôÙdefined structural similarity measures to guide
the construction of initial training datasets that are explicitly diverse across the similarity metric.
This could reduce redundancy, ensure coverage of important chemical motifs, and ultimately
improve the generalization performance and costôÙefficiency of interatomic potentials. One apôÙ
proach could be to start by generating a large pool of unlabeled structures. This initial pool
should be as comprehensive as possible, and should ideally include all possible local atomic
arrangements. The unlabeled structures could then be clustered by applying a structural similarôÙ
ity metric. By selecting representatives from across these clusters for the initial dataset, it would
be possible to construct an initial dataset that is explicitly diverse across structural space. Once
this strategically chosen dataset is labeled and trained on, the model could be further improved
using active learning with uncertainty based structure selection and fineôÙtuning. Moreover, once
the model is trained, its learned features can also serve as the basis for a modelôÙdriven simiôÙ
larity index. Developing such a framework would represent a shift away from bruteôÙforce data
augmentation toward a more systematic and cost aware approach, hopefully, leading to more

robust MACE potentials.
In summary, MLIPs like MACE can be costôÙefficient tools for modeling complex systems if they
are trained strategically and used with an awareness of their limitations. The findings of this
thesis highlight, that while the idea of developing a single universal potential that reliably exôÙ
trapolates to all systems may remain out of reach. This is not a limitation but rather an essential
insight: even for a narrowly defined application, like a specific catalytic reaction or a material,
there will never be a point where a trained potential can be used without careful monitoring. The
results show that good testôÙset accuracy alone is not enough. Any new structure presented to
the model must be checked to ensure it lies within the training domain, otherwise, the predictions
risk being unphysical and misleading.
Generating robust, generalizable potentials is not simply a matter of adding more data, but of seôÙ
lecting diverse, informative structures and continually monitoring performance as new systems
are explored. Active learning and ensemble uncertainty estimates emerge therefore as indisôÙ
pensable tools to ensure that outôÙofôÙdistribution data points are detected reliably. This means,
the practical use of these models should be seen as an ongoing process, that requires constant
monitoring and thoughtful updates to stay accurate for each research application.
