Abstract
The local plasmon resonances are investigated in selected two-dimensional conductors. A
model based on a Drude conductivity is developed that predicts the plasmon modes in
homogeneously, as well as non-homogeously doped structures, and is applicable on both
two-dimensional metals and graphene.
A description is provided on how the theory is
implemented via computational routines to numerically obtain the data of the plasmon res-
onances. A method is also suggested on how a more precise and complicated conductivity
can be implemented.
Results are presented for a disk and triangle structure, and dimers hereof. They are in-
vestigated being homogeneous doped, and doped due to a homogeneous electric ﬁeld. The
plasmonic resonance frequencies, the induced charge and potential, and their extinction
cross sections are presented and discussed. Finally, a more sophisticated conductivity is
used that includes interband transitions. This blueshifts the resonance energies, and the
induced charge distribution are signiﬁcantly changed.

The work in this thesis has been conducted as a part of my master thesis project. Prior
to this project I did a self-study project in January 2015 for the duration of three weeks,
initiating some of the work shown herein.
The codes used in this project are based on a framework by Weihua Wang, which are limited
to only calculate the plasmon resonances in homogeneously doped graphene structures.
The main focus of this thesis has been to continue developing the framework, making it
possible to calculate the induced charge by an extern electric potential, predict the plasmon
resonances in the structure when exposed to the electric potential, and include the eﬀect of
electron interband transitions in graphene. The capabilities of the initial codes by Wang are
reported in section 4.2, except for the dimer structures. The rest of the results is original
and as of yet unpublished.

and covers a total workload of 30 ECTS.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The collective oscillations of electrons on the surface of conductors, known as surface plas-
mons, have been the focus of a lot of research in recent years. Their application has a wide
variety, from spectroscopy enhancement to increase conﬁnement of optical communication.
The discovery of graphene has led to extensive research of plasmonic properties in graphene
due to its small size and high electrical tunability. Plasmonic states takes many form, from
dipole modes that interact strongly with plane wave light, to dark modes that are very dif-
ﬁcult to excite with current methods. Depending on the structure, two distinct plasmonic
excitations can be excited from a driving ﬁeld. Surface plasmon polaritons are traveling
electromagnetic waves that are supported in structures much larger than the wavelength of
the driving ﬁeld, whereas localized surface plasmon resonances are standing wave excita-
tions, and are supported in nanostructures comparable with the wavelength of driving ﬁeld.
Predicting the plasmonic modes can be a diﬃcult task since they are solutions to partial
diﬀerential equations imposed to the systems boundary conditions. To ﬁnd the solutions,
one often exploits the symmetry of the system and clever usage of mathematical methods,
and analytical expressions are seldom available. Numeric methods exist that allows the
governing partial diﬀerential equations to be discretized into linear equations, thereby re-
deﬁning the problem to make it solvable with computational aid.
This thesis aims to investigate the plasmonic properties of selected two dimensional graphen
and metals structures using a numeric approach. Before presenting the full scope of the
thesis, a brief introduction to surface plamsons and the plasmonic properties of graphene
will be presented.

in the material. The SPP support only transverse magnetic modes, and their dispersion

1.2
Localized Surface Plasmon Resconances
When the metallic structure becomes suﬃciently small, it will no longer be able to support
traveling SPP. Instead, standing wave-like excitations can occur when exposed to an ex-
ternal electric ﬁeld. These excitations are known as localized surface plasmon resonances
(LSPR), and contrary to SPP, LSPR can interact strongly with external electromagnetic
ﬁelds. It will later be demonstrated that plane waves interacts strongly with dipolar LSPR
modes, absorbing and scattering light at energies close to the dipole LSPR energy.
The coupling of light with spherical particles was ﬁrst thoroughly analyzed in 1908 [20],
resulting in the ability to predict a size dependence for the resonance frequency for nanopar-
ticles. This discovery has since been used in various applications in which reﬂections of
single wavelengths may be desired. A metallic surface can be manipulated and reshaped
with sphere patterns to reﬂect only speciﬁc colors at speciﬁc locations, thereby coloring
a metallic surface without adding any other material [7]. Other techniques applies arrays
of metallic nanoparticles with various sizes in an array to compose pictures, reaching res-

1.3
Plasmonic Properties of Graphene
Graphene, the two dimensional structure consisting of a single graphite layer, has received
much attention since its discovery in 2004. Besides being an extremely strong material, it
exhibits many interesting electrical properties. Electrons have a linear dispersion relation
close to the Fermi level, making them behave as a massless electron gas. Also, the number
and type of charge carriers can easily be manipulated by the use of an external potential
[12]. Due to these unique electrical properties, the plasmonic properties in graphene has
caught much attention in recent years.
Although graphene has the thickness of a single atom, it has been shown to have extremely
strong light-matter interaction, reaching extinction cross section values close to the area of
its own area [8]. The plasmons in graphene also exhibits tighter conﬁnements and longer
propagation lengths than in noble metals [15], making it possible to exploit SPP informa-
tion transfer even better. It has been suggested that a sheet of graphene can support SPP
waveguides by varying the spatial electrostatic doping [29].
Much of the work done in graphene plasmonics is to better understand how light interacts
with graphene and how the ﬁeld excitations are spatially distributed. Especially plasmonic
properties in ribbons and carbon nanotubes under an uniform doping concentration has
been studied thoroughly [8]. Most of these studies, however, has been done when assuming
a Drude conductivity, thereby completely neglecting electron interband transitions. The
plasmonic properties in graphene due to an inhomogeneously distributed doping concen-
tration has only been studied in speciﬁc setups. Some of the setups studied are backgated
nanoribbons and nanoribbons exposed to a uniform electric ﬁeld ﬁeld [28], and recently,
charged graphene nanodisks and nanodisk doped by a point charge has been studied [26].
Unexplored areas include other geometries and doping methods, and when electron inter-
band transitions are included, are yet to be discovered. Exploring some of these unknown

1.4
Scope of the Thesis
The aim of this thesis is to investigate some of the unexplored plasmonic properties of
graphene. The thesis will focus on the localized surface plasmon resonances in graphene
disk and triangle structures. An analysis will be performed when the doping level is as-
sumed constant across the structure, which allow us with relative ease to ﬁnd the plasmon
resonance energies, the induced potential and induced charge, and the extinction cross sec-
tion when interacting with plane waves. Then, the charge distribution induced due to a
homogeneous electric ﬁeld in charge neutral disks and triangles will be obtained. This will
be used to ﬁnd the same qualities as found in the homogeneously doped structures. To keep
things simple, all these calculations will be performed when a simplistic Drude conductivity
is used. This assumption is valid for noble metals, and for graphene when only the electron
intraband transitions are taken into account. Finally, the plasmonic properties in said setup
will be found when electron interband transitions are included, thereby empathizing the
eﬀect of the interband transitions.
The systems investigated will be physically realizable structures. To limit ourselves, the in-
vestigated structures will be disks of 100 nm diameter, and equilateral triangles with 100 nm
from tip to opposite side. The plasmons in homogeneously doped graphene will be shown
to be scale invariant, so the chemical potential will arbitrarily be set to 0.4 eV. The doping
proﬁles in non-uniformly doped graphene will not be scale invariant with the ﬁeld strength.
Therefore two distinct ﬁeld strengths will be considered, a weak on 1 MV/m, and a stronger
on 50 MV/m. The ﬁrst is chosen because air has a dielectric breakdown at 3 MV/m [23],
making that scenario easily realizable in air. The second strength can be achieved, if the
structure is embedded in a substrate with a higher dielectric strength. For example, silicon
dioxide has a dielectric breakdown at 0.56 GV/m [3]. By having SiO2 on one side, and a
transparent medium on the other, it should be possible to achieve electric ﬁeld strengths
of 50 MV/m.

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter will present all relevant theory used through the project. First, in section
2.1 a general description of the electron dispersion in graphene will be presented. A linear
dispersion relation will be derived, which holds when the system is only slightly doped.
Then, in section 2.2, the fundamental theory required to predict plasmonic properties in
homogeneously doped structures will be derived. Section 2.3 discusses a method on how

structures is desired. The rest of the section discusses the changes the previous model must
undergo, if the plasmonic properties in inhomogeneously doped graphene are to be found.
Finally, it will be discussed how the graphene electron interband transitions are taken into
account via the conductivity.

2.2
Building the Initial Model
We will in this section discuss the fundamental equations that govern the plasmonic prop-
erties of a given 2D conductor. For simplicity, the model will ﬁrst be built assuming a
constant carrier concentration, and assuming a Drude conductivity is valid. One of the
advantages of this assumption is that the model holds for any conducting material, as long
as a Drude conductivity can be assumed. Having a simplistic conductivity also stands as
a way to validate the model as plasmonic behaviour with Drude conductivity has been
studied extensively in the literature. Finally, the model will be adjusted afterwards so it
also works for inhomogeneously doped structures.
The strategy to ﬁnd the plasmonic modes will be to use a ﬁnite element method. This
means that any function values in the continuous system will only be evaluated at selected
points, allowing any diﬀerential equation to be discretized into a matrix that describes the
relation between any two mesh points. The advantage of this approach is to reduce the
problem at hand to a set of linear equations that can be solved in a computer using stan-
dardized methods. Henceforth, any discretized function or variable will be written in bold.

2.2.4
Symmetry Considerations and Degeneracy
If the structure under consideration exhibits certain symmetries, certain predictions can
sometimes be said about the solutions to the system. For the scope of the project, only
circular disk and equilateral triangles, and dimer structures hereof, will be considered.
The plasmon resonances in homogeneously doped equilateral triangles have previous been
studied by reference [2], by the use of group theory.
The equilateral triangle remains
unchanged under rotation by 120◦and by mirroring across the three axis going from tip
to opposite side. By these symmetry operations, the equilateral triangle is said to belong
to the symmetry group D3h. From this information alone, [2] were able to conclude that
the dipole and quadrupolar plasmon resonance must be doubly degenerate. The circular
disk exhibits the same symmetries, but not limited to rotations of 120◦and not only 3 axis
of inversion. It must therefore follow that it also has at lease double denerate dipolar and
quadrupolar resonances.
We can achieve more insight in the resonance degeneracy by considering equation (2.22)
with φext = 0. The matrices A and B must remain the same under any symmetry operation,
so there can be no change to (2.22) under these operations. The solutions, however, can
change under these operations, and they must still be solutions to (2.22). It is not always
certain that new solutions can be obtained this way. For example, if the resonance already
exhibits the same symmetries as the structure itself, no new solution can be obtained by
performing a symmetry operation.
If a resonant state has a dipole moment, quadrupole monent, or higher order moment, the
state must change under a rotational operation other than a full rotation, since the direction
14
==PAGE:29==

of the moment will change with the operation. If the system only exhibit 180◦rotational
symmetry, the only mode obtainable by rotation is the mode itself times −1 to invert the
direction of the moment. This is not a unique mode since it is spanned by the original
mode. If the system exhibits lower angle rotational symmetry, it is possible to obtain states
with moments in other directions that can not be spanned by the original mode. Double
degenerate modes are therefore expected to be present in the disk and triangle systems.
In the dimer structures, the only rotational symmetry present will be the 180◦rotation.
No degenerate states will therefore be expected.

The theory in the previous section describes in detail how plasmonic excitations are cal-
culated when the structure under consideration has a constant conductivity, given by the
Drude expression 2.18.

structure, i.e. if the structure is inhomogeneously doped, the method described in section

corrections to the continuation matrix have to be performed.

when they are inhomogeneous doped by an external potential. Because the conductivity
is related to the electron-hole concentration, we will ﬁrst go into depth on how the charge
distribution is calculated from a given external potential. We will then go into depth on
how to correct for the inhomogenity in the matrix B, so that the same method as was used
previously can be used.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The objective of the project was to analyze some of the plasmonic properties in graphene for
selected structures, and the goal has been reached. In chapter 2 the theoretical foundation
was laid to ﬁnd the plasmonic resonances in a two-dimensional structure. The needed eigen
equations to be solved was derived, and the procedure to properly discretize the system
was explained. It was then explained what corrections should be made if the structure
was inhomogeneously doped by an external potential. Foremost, one solves for the induced
charges due to the exposure. Then, having the doping proﬁle, one obtains the plasmon
properties by the use of the same method as before, with only slight corrections. Finally,
a method to ﬁnd the extinction cross section was derived, and a more exact conductivity
takes the interband transitions into account in graphene was presented.
Then, in chapter 3, the computational tools and the method to implement the theory
was presented. The chapter presented a pedagogical explanation on how to reduce the
problem into standardized problems in linear algebra that can readily be solved by optimized
computational libraries. It was discovered how the plasmonic resonances were found, and
how the extinction cross section was calculated. A trust-region method was used to obtain
the doping proﬁles due to an external potential. Finally, it was shown how the plasmonic
resonances were found when electronic interband transitions were taken into account. The
calculations got much more complicated, so a brute-force method was implemented.
Finally, chapter 4 presented the results obtained from the simulations. Foremost, to reassure
the obtained results are trustworthy, a converge test on selected systems was performed.
The tests indicated that the energies in the triangle structures could be trusted within a
error margin of 1 % for the ﬁve lowest energies.
The error in the disk structure was a
bit higher, but was within 10 %. Then, the results from homogeneously doped graphene
disks and triangles were presented. Many of the modes were degenerate, in accordance
59
==PAGE:74==
with what would be expected due to the structure symmetries. The degenerate modes,
however, split when an identical structure was placed close by, similar to atomic orbits
when forming covalent bonds. The extinction cross section was rather simple, showing a
few peaks where a strong dipole mode was present. The results were in general in agreement
with previous investigations foundg in the literature. The plasmonic properties were also
investigated when neutral disks and dimers were exposed to a homogeneous external ﬁeld.
A line formed where the electron and hole gasses met that seem to have a great impact on
the plasmonic resonances. Some modes seems to live only on one side of the line. Also,
higher order modes seem to form standing waves along this line and their resonance energy
seem to be linear, which is expected for standing waves.
Finally, the inhomogeneously
doped structures were investigated when electronic interband transition was included in
the conductivity. The energy spectra blueshifted and the standing waves across the zero
Fermi energy line disappeared. Some of the modes are familiar from the previous analysis,
but some new mode also appear. The results obtained from the inhomogeneously doped
structures are novel, and thus uncover some previously undiscovered areas within graphene
plasmonics.

Outlook
The scope of the thesis was to investigate yet uncovered areas within graphene plasmon-
ics. The goal was fulﬁlled, although many areas still remain to investigate. The basis to
ﬁnd plasmonic resonances in inhomogeneously doped graphene has been developed, and
the method is not unique to a homogeneous ﬁeld. It would be interesting to investigate
the eﬀect of other doping types, such as one or several point charges located a distance
away from the structure. This would likely induce a zero Fermi circle, which might support
SPP traveling in a circle. Other structures like disks with epicentral holes has already been
studied[32], and the eﬀect of inhomogeneous doping on these structures would be interest-
ing.
The interband transitions were shown to have a signiﬁcant impact on the plasmonic modes
in graphene, and cannot justly be neglected. It was shown to blueshift the energy spectra
of the extinction cross section, and the induced potential and charge have a diﬀerent shape.
But how the interband contribution changes the modes is still unclear. It would be inter-
esting to introduce a parameter γ, so the interband contribution gradually can be turned
on via σ = σintra + γσinter, and track the evolution of the modes.

This appendix aims to oﬀer an alternative way to solve the matrix equation (2.50), rather
than using the computational heavy Trust Region method proposed in section 3.2.3.
The non-linear equation to solve for has the form
a = Ax + f [x],
(B.1)
where a is a vector, A is a matrix, and f [x] is a function that depends on the unknown
