Abstract
Quantum computers are expected give computing a paradigm shift, allowing us to solve previously
unsolvable problems. Linear optics promises a route to large quantum computers, with integrated
quantum photonic circuits being the key to scalability. Standing on the broad shoulders of silicon
photonics for telecommunications, integrated quantum circuits in silicon oﬀer CMOS compatible
fabrication and high-performance components.
Previous research has implemented large and
complex circuits on a chip, while high-speed components enable quantum key distribution at
gigahertz speeds.
This master’s thesis aims to combine a universal interferometer in a photonic circuit with the GHz
speed seen in telecommunications and quantum key distribution. Central to the proposal is the
high-speed manipulation of on-chip-generated photons with carrier-depletion-phase-modulators.
The thesis simulates the performance and feasibility of the interferometer.
Furthermore, the
properties of the chip are characterized to gain complete control of the universal interferometer.
Due to losses induced by malfunctional phase-shifters, single-photon statistics are not observed,
leading to modulation of weak coherent pulses created oﬀ-chip.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The computer started a technological revolution at a scale and with a speed never seen before. The
Second World War was a catalyst for the development of computers as a tool for decryption, most
famously at Bletchley Park where Alan Turing and others managed to decrypt Nazi Germany’s
Enigma cipher machine using computers[18]. After the war information theory was formalised
by Shannon[47], but it was not until the advent of high-purity silicon and the ﬁrst integrated
computer in 1971 that computers got their key ingredient - scalability. This lead to a explosive
development in computing and communications, that has revolutionized science, engineering, and
society as a whole. The development of integrated computers has followed Moore’s Law, which
states that the complexity of a circuit doubles every two years[38]. In science and engineering this
explosive development has allowed for the simulation of more and more complex problems and
systems. Some problems and systems, including Quantum systems, scale very badly on classical
computers[10]. Some of these complex problems are solvable by using quantum quantum systems
for encoding the information, so-called qubits[3]. This is formulated in quantum information,
where a qubit is a two-level quantum system on which operations represented by gates can be
performed. Measuring your system gives you your result. Using this quantum computations and
quantum communications can be performed, but in practice this is immensely challenging. The
conditions required to realize a quantum information processing system are formalized by the
DiVincenzo criteria[20][23]
1. Well-deﬁned state space that can be scaled - the system should be scalable and have
well deﬁned qubits
2. Initialization - the system must be initializable in a unique pure state
3. Long coherence times - he coherence time of the state must be much longer than the
gate operation time
4. Universal set of gates - it must be possible to apply any desired sequence of gates
5. Qubit-speciﬁc measurements - it must be possible to perform measurments of individual
qubits
These criteria are met with varying success by the existing candidates for quantum systems, such
as superconducting circuits, quantum dots, ion-traps, photons, topological anyons etc.[45], each
with their own beneﬁts and drawbacks.
At the moment the most promising technologies are
superconducting circuits and photons having already achieved the quantum supremacy[9, 58].
This celebrated supremacy is the feat of completing a task faster than what is possible for a
classical computer. Photons have many things going for them, they travel at the speed of light,
do not interact with the medium they are travelling in and they do not interact with each other.
The ﬁrst two reasons make photons indispensable as carriers of information for communication.

As carriers of classical information, photons make up the backbone of the telecommunications
industry, sending enormous amounts of information every single day trough advanced optical ﬁbers
at up to terabit/s speed[57]. The science of light generation, manipulation and detection is labeled
photonics. For quantum communications, many of the technologies that make telecommunications
so great can be utilized for sending quantum states of light. This forms the basis for quantum key
distribution, the most mature of the quantum technologies that even is commercially available[43].
In quantum key distribution a key is shared between two parties secured by the principles of
quantum mechanics[46].
Great progress has been done in QKD by techiques borrowed from
classical communications like modulation using carrier-depletion-modulators[26] to achieve GHz
speeds and high-dimensional encoding to boost speeds and become more robust to noise[19].
For classical computing, integration of the circuits on a silicon chip was they key to scalability.
The same is applicable for quantum information processing using photons. This forms the ﬁeld
of integrated quantum photonics. There are numerous companies developing integrated photonic
quantum computers, most notably Xanadu that has a cloud quantum computer available and a
blueprint on how to build a fault-tolerant quantum computer[12] and Psi-Quantum that has the
rather ambitious goal of having a million fault-tolerant qubits within a handful of years[44] using
what they called fusion-based quantum computation[11].
As the technology has advanced, the ﬁeld of integrated quantum photonics has has exploded.
Advanced protocols have been implemented, e.g. chip-to-chip quantum teleportation and multi-
photon entanglement of quantum states[36], advanced graph state[6], and many-mode boson
sampling[55]. All of these works, except for the boson-sampling implementation, have used low-
speed thermal phase-shifters for modiﬁying the phase for modulating their photons. High-speed
modulators are commonplace in classical communications[14] and have seen adoption for use in
quantum communication [26][13] providing up speed up to Gb/s, compared to the kb/s achievable
rate when using thermal phase-shifters.
The aim of this master’s thesis is to merge integrated quantum photonics with high-speed modu-
lators by implementing protocols in a high-speed integrated quantum photonic circuit on a silicon
chip. The chip consists of four sources that create a two-qubit state that are interfered at an
universal interferometer employing high-speed carrier-depletion-modulators. Thus any transfor-
mation of the input state can be done at high-speeds.
A map of this thesis
Following this introduction is a theory chapter where outlining the basic principles of quantum
mechanics and quantum information processing, including a review of important protocols. Next,
quantum information will be coupled to integrated quantum photonics, by using linear optical
quantum computing. Chapter 3 details the chip used in this thesis and how gates in quantum
information can be translated to phases in an interferometer. This is then simulated to show
the chips capabilities.
Chapter 4 presents the physical setup housing the chip and chapter 5
describes the characterization of the chip. Chapter 6 describes the reasoning and eﬀect of the
melting of three phase-shifters. In chapter 7 single photons are generated and chapter 8 presents
the modulation of weak-coherent pulses.

Quantum key distribution
Quantum key distribution, QKD, is the most mature quantum technology. QKD aims to share
a secret key for cryptography between two authorized partners connected by a quantum channel

Boson sampling
The general idea of quantum boson sampling is to build a quantum device that can sample an
output distribution stemming from non-classical interference of photons. This is thought to be
exponentially hard to simulate classically[4]. The idea is to send n identical bosons into an m-
mode (m>n) random interferometer and sample the output distribution[56]. The output will be
related to the submatrices of the interferometer.
With a two-qubit device, the output will be rather dull and easy to simulate on a classical
computer. However, increasing the number of photons to 76, the output distribution is so complex
that the latest supercomputers struggle to simulate it[59].
Until now, the topic discussed have applied to all quantum systems, including integrated quantum
photonics. One way of applying these principles is linear optical quantum computing.

2.4.2
Silicon as a waveguide
Integrated circuits can be made from many materials, indium phosphide, lithium niobate, silicon
nitride etc.[51], however, silicon prevails as a material for many of the same reasons that make
it the dominant material for integrated electronics: silicon is abundant, can be fabricated with
an incredible purity and doping precision, it has an inert oxide which forms precise interfaces, it
has excellent thermal conductivity and is mechanically robust. Optically it has a strong thermo-
optic eﬀect, a strong Kerr-non-linearity, transparency from near- to mid-IR, and its oxide has a
low-refractive index making it a great cladding[50]. Moreover, waveguides in silicon can easily be
integrated with microelectronics, making it easy to create complex devices.
Bandgap
Silicon has an indirect bandgap of energy 1.11 eV at room temperature [30] making it completely
transparent from 1.3 µm well into the mid-infrared, including the important 1550 nm C-band
window in telecommunications. The indirect bandgap makes it very hard to make electrically
pumped lasers[2].
Thermo-optic eﬀects
Silicon has a very high thermo-optic coeﬃcient of 1.86 · 10−4K−1 [50] making it very easy to do
phase-modulation in a waveguide. However, it also makes for a device that is highly temperature-
dependent. Thus it is crucial to monitor and control the temperature of silicon devices.

and is hard to integrate on the chip.

couple light out-of-plane from the chip into the ﬁber. The periodicity and angle determine the
direction of interference[53].

2.4.6
Sources
Two candidates for a source of single photons on the integrated silicon platform are quantum dots
and photon-pair sources from four-wave mixing. Although progress has been made, quantum dots
remain hard to engineer in silicon[21], while silicon’s large third-order nonlinearity χ(3) makes it
the prime candidate for generating single-photon input states.
The four-wave mixing process
spontaneously creates a signal/idler pair from two input pump photons, while a quantum dot can
produce photons on demand.
A universally useful photon-pair source has the following essential propoerties[50]
• Brightness - the source must be able to produce enough photons to be useful
• Indistinguishability - In order for photons to interfere, they need to be indistinguishable
from each other. This applies in all degrees of freedom: time of arrival, transverse mode
shape, temporal shape, polarization, and frequency spectrum
• Separability - The signal/idler pair needs to be separable, i.e. the measurement of the idler
photon must be measurable without collapsing the signal wavefunction and vice versa. If
the wavefunction collapses, it will ruin any subsequent interference of the signal photon with
other pairs. This quantity is diﬃcult to measure.

∆φ ∈[0, π] the code is not able to ﬁnd any phases that replicate the desired unitary for 9 of 49
gates. For ∆φ ∈[−π/2, π/2] only 5 of 49 phases are available while for ∆φ ∈[−π/4, π/4] only
ˆY ⊗ˆY is available. Thus it seems like both −π/4 and π phase must be accessible for all the
CDM’s if the chip should function as a universal unitary.

Physcial characteristics of the chip
A picture taken through a microscope of the chip is seen in ﬁgure 3.11. The chip is 2500µ m tall
and 2500µm wide. The chip fabricated Imec in Belgium. The waveguide is a strip waveguide with
a 450 nm wide core. The cladding width is 2000 nm[29]. The manufacturer reports a propagation
loss of 1.4 ± 0.2dB/cm.
The ﬁber-to-chip couplers employed are grating couplers. The insertion loss of the couplers has a
minimum at 1555 nm where −2.47 ± 0.4 dB is expected. Another dB of loss is estimated at 27.7
nm from the centre peak[29]. The wavelength dependent insertion loss falls polynomially.
Next, there are four straight waveguide sources. These sources consist of 25 straights of length
522 µm. Including the bends, the source is 13000 µm long.
Next, there are two asymmetrical phase shifters for ﬁltering purposes and then the modiﬁed
Clements interferometer. The phase shift in the interferometer is given by the CDM’s along 1500
µm of waveguide. The manufacturer reports −13.8±0.71 dB/cm at 0 V and −12.2±0.68 dB/cm
at 2 V.
The phase shifters on the chip are controlled via applying voltage to electrical pads where wires
can be soldered on in a process called wire-bonding or probed by conducting needles. We have
two chips, one bare and another attached to a PCB where the wires have been wire-bonded. The
bare chip is seen in ﬁgure 3.11 and the wire-bonded in ﬁgure 3.12. The electrical pads on the
wire-bonded chip connect the CDM’s to SMA-connectors and the thermal shifters to the pins

5.1.3
Failsafes for CDM’s
When operating no measurable current will ﬂow across the CDM and the CDM’s will melt if a
too high current is conducted. Therefore it is necessary to monitor the current drawn from the
CDM’s. As soon as it starts drawing a noticeable amount of current, it is no longer safe to increase
the voltage. The Qontrol-boards used for supplying power have an in-built functionality where it
is possible to set the maximal current the boards can provide called imax. If the software detects
a current higher than the threshold, it will set the voltage to 0.
Failsafe 1: Set Qontrol imax = 1 mA
Characterizing the phase shifters happens by iterating through a range of voltages, often for the
full range of safe voltages, 0 to 11.5 V. The speed at which the boards can detect a current higher
than 1 mA and then turn oﬀthe voltage might be slower than the speed of which it receives the
following command for a higher voltage. Therefore it is essential to break a iterating loop if the
current drawn is higher than 1 mA. Unfortunately, this part of the code was not implemented
until some parts of the characterization was done.
Failsafe 2: Break loop if I>1 mA
With the failsafe in place, we can move on to the characterization of the wire-bonded chip.

When doing the characterization of the phase shifters, three phase shifters gave no response to
the applied voltage. After hours of debugging and an inspection with the microscope, it is clear
that the three phase shifters in question were melted. The electrical pad for the melted phase
shifters can be seen in ﬁgure 6.1 to 6.3. Comparing it to the melted phase shifter from the spare
chip in ﬁgure 6.4 as described in section 5.1.2 it can be seen that all shifters share a large round
discolouring around the pad. This discolouring does not appear in ﬁgure 6.5, which is a working
CDM.
Two theories can explain how the CDM’s melted. The ﬁrst is that fail-safe 1) is too slow compared
to the time needed to melt a CDM. As soon as the Qontrol power supply detects a larger than
one mA current, it turns oﬀthe voltage through the built-in function Imax. However, if the
detection is too slow, a current larger than one mA will run through the CDM and possibly
melting it. The other theory builds on the ﬁrst. The characterization of the CDM’s is done
as a series of measurements of voltage, current, and transmitted power done in a loop using a
self-written program. The ﬁrst iterations of this program did not include a statement where the
program would break the loop if the built-in Imax function had triggered. It would just continue
the loop setting higher and higher voltages. The second theory is that this allowed a high voltage
to run across the CDM, thereby melting it. Looking at ﬁgure 5.3 a voltage V > 5 will melt the
CDM. The voltage range used when characterizing was from 0-10.5 V. If a voltage around 10 V
was allowed to ﬂow through the CDM, even for a short amount of time, it would most likely melt
the CDM because of its extremely high resistivity. In any case, methods used to ensure that the
phase shifters did not draw too much current have failed have not been strong enough.

Therefore, it must be concluded that the chip produces signal-idler pairs, but the signal photons
get lost in the losses stemming from the melted CDM. Looking at diﬀerent paths through the
interferometer, the photons cannot pass through without encountering a melted CDM. Thus it
is impossible to measure single photons. By generating the quantum states oﬀ-chip, it should be
possible to modulate quantum states using the chip.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
This master’s thesis aimed to implement protocols at high speeds using integrated quantum
photonics. The chip used in this thesis consisted of four straight waveguide sources creating a
four-mode two-qubit state. The four modes were interfered at using a modiﬁed Clements-scheme
universal-interferometer, in which any desired gate could be implemented at high speed using
carrier depletion phase modulators.
The chip was ﬁrst simulated, where it was shown that with no bounds on phase, the interferometer
was universal, and any gate can be implemented. Narrowing the available phase range reduced
the gate space available. Next, two probe stations were built, housing the chip. One was used to
break the chip’s voltage limits to operate the wire-bonded chip safely. Next, the wire-bonded chip
was characterized to gain complete control of the operation of the interferometer. The carrier-
depletion modulators are very sensitive to high currents. During characterization, the fail-saves
in place to stop any current ﬂowing above 1 mA failed, leading to three of three modulators
melting. Simulations showed that the chip no longer could be used as a universal interferometer
for manipulating the four modes. The melted CDM’s induced signiﬁcant losses in an already
lossy interferometer. The losses induced were too signiﬁcant to measure the correlated signal-
idler pairs created by the four-straight waveguide sources. By creating the input states oﬀ-chip,
the remaining modulators were used for modulating weak coherent pulses. Using a mean photon
number of ¯µ = 0.05, the pulses were modulated using an alternating signal of either identity or
a 50/50 split. Due to unawareness when performing the experiment, the clock of the signal and
the time-tagger was not synchronized. Because of the losses in the waveguide and losses caused
by melted CDM’s, the distance between detection events was too great to synchronize the two
signals after measuring. Due to high losses caused by three melted carrier depletion modulators,
no quantum protocol was implemented.
Outlook
Moving forward, much can be learned from this thesis. First, the wire-bonded chip was bonded
to a PCB that is half the thickness of what is usually used. In the future, the PCB used should
be of the standard size to increase durability. Additionally, the ground and signal should use the
same SMA connector. This would prevent any confusion leading to current sent using the wrong
polarity. Lastly, the electrical pins on the PCB should be positioned away from where the chip is
coupled to ﬁbers via a VGA.
As far as chip design, the diﬀerence in free spectral range of the ﬁlters separating the signal-idler
pair and the ﬁlter used for pump rejection should be greater. The straight waveguide sources
could be longer, which in conjunction with longer carrier-depletion modulators would lead to a
stronger phase response. Care must be taken of CDM’s when writing code controlling them. Any
sign of an over-current ﬂowing should lead to all code being stopped to save the CDM’s.
