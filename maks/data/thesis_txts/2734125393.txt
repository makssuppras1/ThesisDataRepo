Abstract
During the assembly and operation of COBOD’s flagship construction 3D printer, the BOD
2, the primary slowdown is the manual verification of alignment, the positioning of the
heavy components needs to be precise if the operators want the building to be to spec
and in the right place. During operation, the nature of a construction site leaves the com-
ponents vulnerable to shifting which can cause accelerated printer wear and errors that
might eventually lead to a failed print.
This thesis will describe the design and implementation of a custom sensor that will help
level the z-axis to a plane during assembly, which in step with a device designed in the
spring semester will enable automatic and precise measurement of all relevant angles of
the BOD 2 therefore enabling automatic alignment.

1
Introduction
1.1
COBOD
COBOD is the global leader in the field of construction 3D printing technology, having
sold the most printers of any 3D construction printer company. COBOD has completed
the largest number of- and most ambitious projects while receiving numerous awards and
recognitions for its innovations. The company has developed a large-scale 3D printer that
can print buildings and building components, such as walls, roofs, and other structural
elements, using concrete or a mortar mixture.
One of the main goals of COBOD is to promote automation and sustainability in the con-
struction industry while reducing waste. The precise control enabled by 3D printers allows
for more accurate use of materials, reduces labor needs and waste, lowering the environ-
mental impact of construction.
COBOD has partnered with several companies and organizations to develop and imple-
ment 3D printing technology in construction projects around the world. The company
has worked on a range of projects, from small-scale housing developments to large-scale
commercial buildings and recently an embassy.
1.2
The BOD 2
The BOD2 is COBOD’s current flagship 3D printer. The BOD2 can reach a size of 15m
wide, 20m high and theoretically infinitely long but the largest ever sold was 27.5 m.
It has a theoretical printing speed of up to 1000 mm/s which is usually limited to 250mm/s
and it can print with a variety of construction materials, including concrete and a mortar
mixture. See a photo of the BOD 2 in figure 1.1.
1.3
The sensor
This project will describe the design, testing and performance quantification of a custom
sensor, designed to help level the z-axis of the BOD 2 during setup. The current levelling
tolerance is set at 3mm, the objective will be to achieve a measurement precision an order
of magnitude beyond that or 0.3mm. See the hypothesized functionality of the sensor in
figure 1.2.
Since the strategy at COBOD is that no new system can require human intervention the
sensor has to be completely autonomous, there is also a desire to develop the capability
to build custom sensors using STM32s and IO-Link, setting some of the specs in stone
from the start.

Current State
2.1
Printer setup
The current setup procedure is as follows:
1. Position leg bases & verify correct positioning
2. Mount Z-axis posts on bases
3. Verify Z-Carriage relative leveling. Focus of thesis
4. Mount Y-axis rails & verify parallelism
5. Mount X-axis rail & verify square with Y-axis. Last semester project
Note: The verification of the parallelism and squareness of the last 2 steps must be carried
out continuously throughout the printing process.
Due to the large size and significant weight of the BOD2’s components, carrying out these
steps can be quite challenging. Moreover, once the printing process has commenced, the
X and Y axis can reach elevations of up to 10 meters, making the precise measurements
of the alignment an incredibly dangerous endeavour. Making it a prime candidate for
automation.
During today’s printer setup and alignment of the Z-level, a line laser is placed in the rela-
tive middle of the print area before checking each carriage by eye and manually moving
them, ensuring that the line laser hits the same reference point on all carriages, that refer-
ence point is determined by each operator respectively. This process can be completed
in <30 minutes by an experienced team but can take a single operator multiple hours for
a large printer.
2.2
Tolerances
Sadly, the world is not perfect and nothing will ever be perfectly square parallel or level
outside a mathematician’s imagination. Therefore, COBOD has specified a 3mm devia-
tion tolerance in the z height of the Carriages, meaning that they must be within 1.5mm
of a hypothetical plane drawn through the mean. Currently, the levelling is done to the
tolerance of the average human eye and the goal of this project will be to create a device
that can measure an order of magnitude beyond the current tolerance, or sub-mm preci-
sion, ensuring that the system will not represent a bottleneck in the precision of levelling
the printer.
2.3
Setup time
Currently, the printer setup is a difficult, labour-intensive task, often taking trained crews
a full construction site day with the largest slowdown being the alignment, precise move-
ment and manual measurement of the large components. This project will tackle one of
the larger steps in that process, ensuring that the z carriages are levelled to each other
before craning the Y-axis on, which must then be perpendicular to the X-axis, which can
be measured using the device designed last semester, therefore enabling fully automatic
alignment.

2.4
Potential solutions
2.4.1
Visual line level lasers
The current levelling procedure uses a visual line level laser and the human eye to verify
alignment. This method is slow and the accuracy is limited to that of the human eye but
does usually live up to the 3mm tolerance set by COBOD.
2.4.2
Off-the-shelf options
There are currently some off-the-shelf solutions available to measure the level of a line
laser, notably the Wurth Laser Reciever LR5-14 with a 120mm z-measurement range.
With a price tag of 200€ each and no integration options, it is not a feasible option.
There are also options to leverage conventional ToF distance sensors, more on those in
the following section.
2.4.3
Highlighted design concepts
Since the currently available solutions have no reference or no options for integration, a
custom sensor had to be developed, that sensor could use a multitude of existing tech-
nologies, most notably:
• CMOS sensor, similar to a camera
• ToF Distance sensor/Axis end stop
• An array of photo resistors/transistors
• Camera and computer vision
CMOS Camera sensor
The idea was to employ a sensor, similar to the ones used on cameras but having it be
1 pixel wide. Commercially available options do exist and are incredibly precise but with
that precision comes a steep price tag, a limited range and a complex design, with the
processor needing to handle 1000s of transistors.
ToF Distance sensor/Axis end stop
The idea was to employ a distance sensor or axis end stop to realize the absolute position
of the z-carriage, something COBOD currently does not know at any given time.
This solution however will not suffice as the legs cannot be guaranteed to be on a level sur-
face and often the operators will leverage the sloped nature of the printing site to position
the building.
Photo resistor/transistor array
Similarly to the CMOS sensor but at a lower resolution, an array of photo resistors or
transistors could be deployed.
The array could leverage the line laser already used by construction crews, a challenge
might be when the sensor would be distinguishing between direct sunlight and the laser
when the sensor would be hit by both, but since this is also a challenge faced by industry
giants like Honeywell and Sick, this will not be a disqualifying obstacle.
Dirt will be no less of a challenge for this system when compared to the others, a remedy
might be that it will be easier to detect for the photoresistor option as detecting the weaker
laser signal will be a feasible feature.
The implementation resolution can be tuned to meet our desired spec, the phototransistors
could be configured to output a digital signal for the laser position, however dirt or distance
between the laser and the sensor might cause the light signal to be too weak to trigger
the sensors, making the hardware side of the design very important and sensitive.

The photoresistor option could have that sensitivity be handled by the software side of
the sensor, capturing the different values and allowing for more flexibility, that will however

Camera vision
Another idea would be to apply an Aruco and a high-resolution camera to the Z-carriage

Laser Level Sensor V001
3.1
Decisions & learning objectives of version
From the ideated concepts, the photoresistors, with a line laser had the clearest path to a
cheap implementation that could meet the 3mm tolerance set. The photo resistors would
be staggered to increase the precision and a mechanism had to be created to identify
what photoresistor the laser would be hitting at any given time, and detect if it is hitting
multiple or none at all.
The main priority of this version was speed, to get something manufactured and start
learning fast as possible. The main desired learnings being:
• Photo resistor behaviours & distinguishing between then when multiple are hit at the
same time
• How IO-Link behaves and how to program it
• Verifying that photo resistors are the right direction to go into
3.2
Design
3.2.1
Photo resistor choice
Following comparisons of the photo resistors the PCB supplier, JLC PCB had in stock, see
table 3.1. The GR36516 was chosen, for its lower resistance values, and lower sensitivity
to limit ambient light effects and the supplier had the most of it in stock.
The lower sensitivity also means a lower relative change in resistance for a given change
in light. The lower resistance value should balance out in the simplicity of the circuit, re-
quiring lower resistances and therefore fewer and smaller resistors to measure accurately.
3.2.2
IO-Link Communications
For the IO-Link communications, the desire was to build the capability for COBOD to
create its own IO-Link capable sensors in the future and therefore the constraint was
placed to use an IO-Link converting chip as opposed to an IO-Link capable microcontroller.
Analog Devices, ST Micro Electronics and Texas Instruments all make very capable IO-
Link chips, yet similarly to the photoresistors, supplier capabilities led to the Texas Instru-
ments TIOL 1123 DRCR being chosen.
3.2.3
Microcontroller
For a Microcontroller, an STM32, Raspberry Pi and Arduino were all considered. To min-
imize design time, an Arduino was chosen, for its good ADC, setup speed and price.
Knowing it was a stopgap and that an STM32 would be used for the final implementation
to maximise controllability, speed and capability building for COBOD.

3.4
Programming
The goal of the program was to read the ADC channels of the sensor before applying
appropriate math to find what photoresistor the laser is hitting and ultimately, where the
laser is hitting.
Since the photoresistor’s resistance drops when exposed to light, the programming started
with finding what ADC Channel/Light bank had the lowest resistance before applying sim-
ple math to that bank to find what resistor on that channel was being hit by the laser.
Finding the laser position was done with a simple for loop, saving the lowest ADC value
and comparing it to the average across the others and multiplying with the index of the
lowest bank to deliver the distance in mm from the top photo resistor to where the laser is
hitting. See the exact math in equation 3.2, note that AllBankAverage excludes the lowest
light bank.

3.5
First observations
Following some manufacturing delays due to part shipments, the board arrived. Prelimi-
nary tests indicated full functionality, with all the photoresistors outputting consistent val-
ues, the voltage converter working and IO-link chip outputting data. See figure 3.4 for the
first manufactured version of the board.
3.5.1
Photoresistors
The first test of the board revealed that while the photoresistors were outputting consistent
values they were far below expectations, with the resistances dropping down to 400Ω in
daylight and 2 −300Ω when exposed to direct light, far below the expected lower end
of the range at 5k. When the line laser is shone at the resistor bank, the difference in
resistances delivered a 3 point change in the ADC value, less than the nominal noise,
leading to the position being undetectable. To mitigate this, a strategy was developed to
block out most light that isn’t the laser with a baffle.
3.5.2
Baffle
A baffle was designed in an attempt to reduce the amount of stray light going into the
photo resistors, several versions were made and while some were made in black and
white, those were not distinguished as different versions.
Version 1
The first version can be seen in figure 3.5, the main objective of this version was to max-
imise the flexibility of positioning, which turned out not to be necessary.
After both being printed in black and white, measurements showed that the resistance
was highly dependent on colour, with the white version having a counter-productive effect
on the resistances, also revealing that the resistances varied considerably based on the
shape of the hole, leading to the next iteration, where the holes were all made the same
shape
Version 2
Version 2 can be seen in figure 3.6, 3 different shapes were tested achieving the purpose
of consistency but the resistance achieved. However, as can be seen in the middle one,
the gaps to the sensors are still large at 1.3mm per side height-wise and 2.2 width-wise.
Version 3
For version 3 those gaps were shrunk drastically to only 0.3 & 0.7 mm between the sensor
and baffle and the resistances shot up, see the baffle in figure 3.7
During testing, another engineer suggested adding a sleeve to block even more light, this
idea was implemented in Version 4.
Version 4
Version 4 elaborated on version 3, resulting in the baffle seen in figure 3.8. This version
exceeded expectations and was able to max out the ADC of the Arduino at up to 11V.
The saturation of the ADC highlighted that there were diminishing returns for the amount
of light absorbed for the length of the baffle. Leading to the analysis done later in the
thesis but can be seen in figure 3.12.
Version 4.5
Version 4.5 was designed following the analysis done in figure 3.12 to optimize the baffle
length and features a slot to slide an optical filter in and out of, the baffle can be seen in
figure 3.9 and the subsequent filter test in table 3.4, detailed later.

get a sense for the measurement but not to be used as a source of truth. See the test
setup in figure 3.11.

some tuning, the sensor did give reasonably consistent data but when the laser hit 2

possible. For example, it was impossible for it to distinguish between photoresistors 2 & 3

in both cases.
This proved the feasibility of the photoresistor idea, being able to measure the position of

certain that the laser will never hit more than 1 photoresistor on a given channel at any

4.2.1
Numerical optimizations
Resistor values
From testing the V001 it was clear that it was not an option to have the laser shine at more
than one photoresistor on the same analog channel at once. Also with the use of a more
sensitive STM32, it had to be certain that the output never went above 3.5V.
With the better understanding of how the photoresistors behaved and their resistive val-
ues under different lighting conditions, a system of polynomials was set up to figure out
what values to set for the fixed resistances to create a series of outputs at repeating incre-
ments of 0.25V, starting at 0.25V and going up to 3.25V. The cases were set up where
the photoresistors hit by the laser had a resistance of 400Ω and ones in darkness were at
300k Ω. With the ascending photoresistors delivering an output in an increment of 0.25V,
i.e. when photoresistor 1 is exposed the ADC should receive an output of 0.25V from that
channel, when resistor 2 is exposed, 0.50. Upto 3.25V for resistor 13. See the values
listed in table 4.1, with the hypothesized resulting circuit in figure 4.1
For the full setup and equations, see Appendix section C.1
These equations were put into Matlab fsolve. See the values calculated and how they
were rounded in table 4.2. These values unintuitively follow an exponential curve, with
the first delta being 1000 Ω and the last one being almost 200k, where the expectation
was that linearly increasing outputs would mean linearly increasing resistances.
The total current draw during the worst case of direct sunlight hitting the sensor and all
photoresistors being at 400Ω, the total current per bank will be under 0.3mA. the mea-
surement output shouldn’t exceed the 3.3V measurement range of the STM32 and won’t
exceed the 3.5V ”harm” rating of the ADC.
Additional analysis of what would happen if the dark and light values of the photoresistors
were not correctly estimated, this analysis revealed a fluke where the optimal resistance
value for R0 went below 0. To better figure out what was going on, analysis was done,
calculating the less expected but reasonable resistance values, the result can be seen in
figure 4.2. As can be seen from there area where the resistance goes below 10 and even
into the negative numbers. The optimal resistance going into the negatives means that
under certain lighting conditions, the desired increment of 0.25V will not hold.

Baffle length
From the learnings of the baffle design for V001 it was clear that a longer baffle would

logarithmic curve to the angle of the sunlight shining towards the sensor. A function was

concrete impractical, ensuring that the sensor will not be a limiting factor of operations.

but reducing the openness and therefore final size requirement, with these considerations

spacing to go down to 22mm.

4.3
Programming
The program sequence consists of 3 steps, those steps being:
1. Baseline establishment
2. Self-calibration
3. Measurement & Communication. Continuous
4.3.1
Baseline establishment
When the sensor receives power, it starts by capturing the initial lighting conditions of the
day, establishing a baseline for the ADC measurements when not being hit by the laser.
This baseline is then used to adjust the expected values for when the laser hits a given
photoresistor.
The initial condition measurement is done by averaging 40 measurements of each ADC
channel, comparing that average to a value captured under lab conditions and creating a
scaling factor for each channel, that scaling factor being 1 for perfect conditions(Complete
darkness) and lower as the ambient light increases.
That scaling factor is then used to adjust the expected values from each sensor on the
channel, expected values that were measured under lab conditions, this shrinking is done
since, as the ambient light goes up, the resistance of all photoresistors goes down and
subsequently, the normal ADC value follows.
4.3.2
Self-calibration
Following the establishment of the baseline and expected values, the z-carriage moves
up as described by the setup feature. It moves up at a constant, slow pace unless it gets
an error code, see error codes transmitted by the sensor in table A.2

This section is also where efforts to leverage multiple sensors came to a close. The math
to leverage the full input from 1 sensor and partial input from multiple others became too
complex and it was decided to not pursue this effort further to avoid scope creep of the
project.
When the laser hits the sensor and the first photoresistor, one of two things can happen.
1. If the laser is relatively close to the sensor, the sensor will detect the light and note
the value as what to expect during the measurement
2. If however, the laser is relatively far from the sensor, the ADC value won’t go as
low and the sensor will note the delta between the actual measured value and the
expected value before adjusting the expected values for all following photoresistors
on that channel by that value since the deltas remain constant irrespective of the
distance between the line laser and sensor or ambient conditions.
• Note that this functionality was added following a round of testing discussed in
section 4.4
Regardless of what path the sensor takes when it meets the first photoresistor, the paths
converge immediately and the printer keeps moving up until all photoresistors have been
calibrated or the sensors gets to a point where the effect of the laser is indistinguishable
from the ambient lighting conditions on the day, noting the value from each sensor until it
gets there.
The sensor expects each ADC channel and its photoresistors to follow the curve from
figure 4.3 but allowing for tolerance in the ambient lighting conditions and the distance
between the laser and the sensor the real value is unlikely to follow that expect curve.
During the calibration sequence, the sensor calibrates all photoresistors, 1 after the other.
Allowing a 0.005V deviation from the expected value based on ambient conditions for
each one and noting the exact local minima value that the photoresistor outputs.
4.3.3
Measurement
Following the calibration, the sensor moves right into the main loop where the measure-
ment is made. Similarly to the calibration, the ADC values are captured and compared to
the values captured during the calibration sequence.
That comparison is used to identify what sensor is being hit, however, some filtering is
needed as any channel, even partially hit by the laser will give a value in the measurement
range.
These values are filtered via a confidence value, i.e. the closer the measured value is to
the calibrated value, the higher the confidence value will be. The sensor value that has
the highest confidence value is then treated as the true measurement for what sensor is
being hit by the laser.
The ID of that sensor and its ADC channel is then passed to a mathematical function (4.1),
which determines the mm value the sensor will send to the printer.

4.3.4
Communication
The desire was to have the mm value sent via the IO-Link serial standard but following
roadblocks, the value was eventually passed via a simple 8-wire digital connector.
This number is received by the printer and it is then up to the printer to move all legs to a
position where all the sensors write out the same value.
Leveraging a serial communication standard would allow a much more precise laser po-
sition float to be passed to the printer, but would also allow other data to be passed, like
the measurement range of each individual sensor, details about the ambient position and
potentially the angle of the motor.

The first round of testing following programming work was done on the printer, see the
setup on figure 4.10. This test failed since the further distance from the laser to the sensor
failed to have the ADC value of the sensor drop low enough to trigger the calibration

It did however show that the sensor could be used as when the printer was moved 10.8mm,
the distance between the photoresistors sharing a bank, the ADC value did change a
consistent amount on a single channel while the others were marginally affected.
The learnings from this test were further validation that the approach is viable with the
values being distinct as the printer moved. The test also showed that for further validation,
an adaptive approach was needed to start the calibration of the sensor and to set the max
range.

Adaptive Calibration trigger
The change required here was to make the calibration start trigger adaptive, finding the
first minima, triggering there and adapting the following expected values by the delta mea-
sured by the first one.
This allows the calibration sequence to start regardless of the laser distance to the sen-
sor(up to 5.5m was tested) and ambient light conditions. It does however create a sce-
nario where the sensor cannot leverage its entire range, and that is why that needed to
be adaptive as well.
Adaptive range
With moving up the triggering point for the calibration we will eventually be unable to
distinguish between the laser and the ambient light as the value the ADC drops down to
for a given photoresistor being exposed to the laser might not go lower than the ambient
value, given the offset starting point.
Therefore the calibration end was changed to finish when the expected value for the next
sensor to be calibrated is higher than the maximum value the sensor measured during
the baseline establishment.

Performance Quantification
With the increased flexibility of the sensor, the next objective was to finally quantify its
performance. Due to high pressure on the production department of COBOD during Q4
of 2023, this testing had to be done in the office, see test setup in figure 5.1.
With the current levelling tolerance of the printer being 3mm, the objective was precision
an order of magnitude beyond that, 0.3mm.
5.1
Inherent limitations
The construction limitations of the sensors, blocking the achievement of the 0.3mm de-
sired were:
1. The 1.2mm resolution of the photoresistor placement
2. The integer nature of the communication standard
5.1.1
Sensor placement resolution
The photoresistor position resolution of 1.2mm, coupled with not leveraging values from
multiple photo resistors simultaneously at a time, made the achievement of a 0.3mm mea-
surement resolution impossible. To achieve that resolution, another strategy would be
needed, either changing the photoresistor arrangement to bring the placement resolution
down to at most 0.3mm or realizing the math of using more than 1 photoresistor at a time.
5.1.2
Communication method
The binary nature of the communication method and subsequent integer values impose
a significant limitation on the precision of transmitted measurements, restricting them to
whole millimetres. This constraint results in the untransmitted fractional components of
the sensor readings. Coupled with the fact that 117 sensors are being used over a 139mm
measurement range, 22 millimeter values, representing almost 1 in 6 whole mm along the
range will never be transmitted.
However with the untransmitted numbers being consistent on all the sensors, this will not
be a make or break limitation, see table of transmittable numbers in the appendix in table
A.1
In an effort to address this limitation, the desire was to use a frame in the 32-bit IO-
Link communication standard to communicate the measurement, which would completely
negate this limitation while allowing the transmission of miscellaneous data like the num-
ber of sensors found and a ranking of the ambient conditions.

5.2.5
Hysteresis
Hysteresis is a phenomenon where the response of a system to changes in input is de-
pendent not only on its current state but also on its history.
In the context of a photoresistor system, hysteresis would manifest as a delay or lag in
the photoresistor’s response to changes in light intensity. This lag might lead to inaccu-
racies in pinpointing the laser’s position, especially during rapid movements or intensity
fluctuations of the laser.
The hysteresis effect would be non-linear. When the laser moves, the resistance of the
photoresistors should ideally change instantaneously and proportionally to reflect the new
position. However, due to hysteresis, there might be a lag between the change in light
intensity and the corresponding change in resistance.
This effect will not have affected the measurements mentioned previously but might have
affected the calibration sequence, to further investigate this effect, an experiment was
devised where the laser was shone onto the sensor, investigating the effect and comparing
different baffles. The experiment setup can be seen in figure 5.16.
This experiment was done on V001 of the PCB, with a modified circuit. The ADC exclu-
sively measuring the voltage across photoresistor 1, with the low side directly connected
to ground i.e:

6
Conclusion
6.1
Objectives
The aim of this thesis was to create a sensor that could automate the levelling process of
setting up COBOD’s BOD2, being capable of measuring the level down to at least 0.3mm
while developing COBOD’s capability to make its own custom sensors using IO-Link & an
STM32.
6.2
The sensor
To meet the objectives, several technologies were brainstormed before an array of pho-
toresistors, paired with an ADC & fixed resistors was chosen, the photoresistors would
leverage the line laser already used on construction sites and considerable math on the
software side to accurately measure the position of the aforementioned laser.
In this process, 2 versions of a PCB were designed and manufactured. Both delivered
extensive learnings, with the first version showing:
• That using photoresistors could be used to determine the position of the laser
• If multiple photoresistors in the same circuit were hit at the same time, the measure-
ment would be unusable
• That a baffle was needed to isolate the photoresistors from external light and optical
filters would be useful
• That the photoresistors behaved very differently from what was expected based on
the spec sheet, with a much higher resistance in darkness and much lower in direct
light
These learnings mandated some optimization to be done for a more refined version, those
being:
• Leveraging an optical filter with the optimal colour, based on the laser available
• Finding the optimal height of the baffle to balance with the size of the board
• Choosing the best values for the fixed resistors, creating an optimal, predictable
outcome
For the second version, the aforementioned learnings and optimizations were put in place
to create a sensor that could be mounted on a printer for a test. Sadly due to the high
pressure on COBOD’s production department during Q4 of 2023, no printer was available
for a large-scale test.
Instead, a test setup was improvised in the COBOD RnD office to quantify the sensor’s
performance.
6.3
Sensor performance
The ADC measurements were not as affected by the distance between the laser and the
sensor nor ambient light as initially expected, with the ADC varying by 200 points(of 4096)
or 0.16V(of a 3.3V range) when the laser was at 0.5m from the sensor and an overhead
light turned on and off. When the sensor was moved to 5m from the laser, the ADC value
went down a maximum of 400 points, representing 0.32V.
Enabling fully automatic alignment of construction 3D printer
53
==PAGE:62==
The laser position measurements showed that the sensor did track the real position of the
laser and was decently consistent across different distances and boards. While consistent
the measurements were not accurate enough to serve as a levelling source of truth.
The cumulative error of all measurements through the calibrated range of the sensor was
around 200mm for all sensors with an outlier being closer to 60mm. Mathematical efforts
were able to bring the measurements closer to reality but none managed to crack the
60mm cumulative error.
6.3.1
Hysteresis
The primary hypothesis for the large error is the large effect of hysteresis on the system.
When tested, the photoresistors needed almost 15 seconds to reset from being hit by
the laser before returning up to 90% of their original resistance. With this in mind, the
calibration sequence should have taken almost 3 minutes, with the laser moving at around
1mm/s to prevent one photoresistor’s calibration from being affected by another hysteresis.
Since this effect was not discovered until after work had been stopped on the sensor,
mitigating efforts were not pursued within the scope of the thesis.
6.4
Process summary
The final sensor was not accurate enough to reliably level the z-axis of the BOD2. This
project was however successful in:
• Showing that photoresistors can be leveraged to level the z-axis
• Learnings on what method doesn’t work for photoresistors
• Learnings on photoresistor behaviour
• Learnings on baffles & optical filtering

7
Next steps
To go from the current state to a viable customer product, the features not developed
during this thesis that need to be realized are:
• Motorized mounting of the sensor
• The PLC side of the system
Features developed during the thesis that need to be refined/changed and will be detailed
out in the following sections are:
1. Changing the photoresistor circuit to use multiplexers instead ADCs and software
math
2. Changing/Figuring out IO-Link or another serial standard communication
3. Other user feedback fixes & optimizations
7.1
Multiplexer setup
The decision was made early on to avoid using multiplexers, given struggling with them
in prior projects. In hindsight, this was the wrong decision, given efforts to leverage input
from multiple photoresistors simultaneously failed and the effects of hysteresis showed
that even if the laser does not hit multiple photoresistors on the same channel simultane-
ously they are still affected by the history of the system.
Each photoresistor needs to have its own channel, mandating a multiplexer or multiplexer-
like design.
7.2
IO-Link
The IO-Link functionality or some other serial connection needs to be set up. The current
8-bit communications are not enough to level the printer.
By implementing a serial communication standard, a more detailed measurement will be
transmittable as well as auxiliary data about the sensor, for example, the found sensors,
information about ambient conditions and the eventual motor angle. Improving the value
brought by such a sensor drastically.
7.3
Give it to customers
Once these features have been added, the sensor should be easily mountable, able to
reliably measure the position of the laser and accurately communicate it to the printer, that
will be able to calibrate itself to the sensor’s output.
Then it needs to be used by customers, their experiences noted and subsequent pain-
points addressed and mitigated.

C.2
V002 Baffle length Optimization
Version 4 of the baffle showed that there were clear benefits to a longer baffle but intuitively
there had to be an optimal length of the baffle with the effectiveness for every additional
mm dropping off after that.
To find that optimal length, a numerical optimization was run, calculating the amount of
light energy absorbed with the angle of the incoming stray light descending on the y-axis
and length of the baffle on the x-axis. It was approximated that when the light bounces
off the black 3D printed surface of the baffle, 80% of the light energy is absorbed, while
this number may not be accurate, it should show the approximate logarithmic curve and
subsequent point of diminishing returns.
From the resultant analysis in figure 3.12, we can see that the optimal length for the baffle
is approximately 30mm, which is less than expected, and with the knowledge that 80%
might not be the correct number, it was decided to go for a 40mm Baffle.
