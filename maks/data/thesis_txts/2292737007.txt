ABSTRACT  
 
The need of dealing with the excess of energy in a daily scale, as well as the necessity of 
covering the intermittent energy demand by renewable sources, has led to constantly increase 
the interest in the field of energy storage. Several technologies can be considered for large 
scale energy storage applications, such as compressed energy storage (CAES), pumped - 
storage hydroelectricity, sodium sulfur batteries (NaS) etc.  
 
This project compares two different novel storage applications, the underwater compressed 
air energy storage (UWCAES) and the thermoelectric energy storage (TEES). All the models 
are constructed in the software Engineering Equation Solver (EES). The aim of the project is 
to investigate and propose which of those two models operates more efficiently with respect 
to the first and second thermodynamic law. Furthermore, the verification of the models 
showed that they can be used as a future working tool for these applications.  
 
Firstly, a novel TEES with transcritical CO2 is presented. The analysis yielded that the 
roundtrip efficiency is closely related to the efficiencies of the machines, as well as to the 
temperature of the hot storage tank. Afterwards, the UWCAES is studied. In the case where 
combustors are used, the system is more sensitive to the efficiencies of the machines 
compared to the depth where the energy bag is placed. In the TES case the most essential 
parameter is the effectiveness of the heat exchanger. Repeatedly, the depth does not influence 
significantly the result.  
 
For the TEES system, the analysis shows roundtrip efficiencies between 45 – 64%. For the 
UWCAES the roundtrip efficiency ranges from 45-55% when combustors are used and from 
63-81% when a TES is used.  
 
This project proposes as the most promising large scale energy storage application the TEES 
since it can operate with high roundtrip efficiency, around 50%, has higher energy density 
and is site independent.  
 
Key words: Energy Storage, Under Water Compressed Air Energy Storage (UWCAES), 
Thermo-electric Energy Storage (TEES)

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
In the 19th century the finding of plenty sources of fossil fuels boosted the industrial 
revolution and thus it pushed the population on earth to increase rapidly. Following this, the 
consumption of fossil was constantly increasing since the demand for energy supply was 
continuously higher. Thus, it led to high dependence of fossil fuels and made the modern life 
highly energy intensive (Mcfarlane, 2007). Nowadays, the reliance from fossil fuels is so high 
reaching a share of 70 % of the total energy consumption (International Energy Agency, 
2009). However, fossil fuel sources are not distributed equally and are not vast as it was 
thought in the 19th century (Rogner, 1977). Hence, it is not possible to maintain the high 
reliance on them. 
Additionally, after Kyoto protocol and the restrictions about CO2 emissions due to global 
warming (UNITED NATIONS, 1999), scientists were focused to develop new more 
environmental friendly technologies to cover the energy demand without the use of fossil 
fuels. That created the incentive to them to explore the renewable energy sources (RES) 
(Dincer, 2000; Dell & Rand, 2001; Greenblatt, Succar, Denkenberger, Williams, & Socolow, 
2007). 
The last decade there is a growing number of researchers who support technologies which do 
not emit CO2, mainly renewable technologies. Renewable technologies contribute in the 
development of societies free from fossil fuels. However, there are still numerous hurdles to 
overcome. Fossil fuels are characterized by their high energy density and high profitability 
(Lubber, 2015), while RES are scattered, located outside of the major cities. Thus, the large 
scale admittance of RES is still facing difficulties. Nevertheless, European Union (EU) is 
implementing supporting policies to support and overcome the disadvantages of the 
renewable energy technologies (RET), such as providing subsidies to cover part of the 
installation, support schemes for the prices. All those measures aim towards the liberalization 
of the electricity market (Ringel, 2006).  
Additionally, the dependency of RES and hence RET, from the weather conditions 
automatically introduces uncertainties to their production. However, due to their intermediate 
nature RET production should always meet the demand. Thus, RET should always be 
available to produce electricity, unconcerned for the irradiance of the sun of the wind speed.

Introduction 
2 
 
Following this, the trend of the fuel prices is constantly increasing the last years. These two 
factors were the most important parameters which led energy storage to attract even more 
attention. The term "storage" refers to a number of technologies including technologies that 
are used to store thermal, electrical, chemical and kinetic. An energy storage system (ESS) 
store potential energy, discharge it whenever required and can provide storage services at 
different periods of time form seconds up to years. 
ESS is believed in a way it converts the existing base load power plants into peak plants. 
However, its purpose is to deal with the projected changes in energy systems around the 
world and eliminate the fluctuations due to renewable integration (Desai et al., 2005; Swider, 
2007).  
Energy storage is believed to be the cornerstone of future renewable energy systems. This 
system is gaining prominence in the last years since it allows for absorption of the fluctuation 
and the alignment of the consumption and production in an economically satisfactory manner. 
The motivation of this thesis is to present a thermodynamic comparison of different types of 
electricity storage. In addition an exergy analysis will be held in order to have a better 
understanding of the system. 
This master project is divided in four chapters. The first one presents an introduction to large-
scale ESS and mainly focuses on the compressed air energy storage (CAES) system. The 
second chapter introduces under-water CAES or Ocean CAES as it is known and afterwards 
thermoelectric energy storage with trans-critical CO2 cycle. Furthermore, this chapter 
provides the theoretical background for modeling the above storage systems. The third 
chapter provides the essential assumptions that have made as well as highlights the reference 
cycles for each case. The fourth chapter presents the verification of the models. Results from 
the literature are used, since most of the presented application are not commercial available 
yet, and thus no experimental data are available.  The fifth chapter, illustrates the result from 
the simulation for the two the models and for both the thermodynamic and exergy analysis. 
Finally, the sixth chapter cites the main conclusions from the results, discusses them and at 
the end poses future works and improvements.  
The following section introduces the ESS with a comprehensive description.

1.1. 
Large – Scale Energy Storage Systems  
1.1.1. Energy Storage 
 
The last years the high share of RES in the energy system in combination with the 
liberalization of the market create a great need for efficient large scale energy storage system 
in order to balance the intermediate nature of the RES. A large scale energy storage system 
can supply the needed energy to the system and thus it can utilize the power network (Dell & 
Rand, 2001; Var der Linden, 2006; Cavallo A. , 2007). For those reasons, the ESS has gained 
great attention the last years.  
However in order to fulfill the above statements the ESS should has the potential to store 
energy in the magnitude of Gigawatt per day (GWd)1. This restriction can be overcome by 
few ESS. The following subsections present and compare these technologies. 
1.1.2. Commercial Large – Scale Energy Storage Systems 
 
As it is mentioned above an ESS convert the electric energy into thermal, chemical or kinetic. 
The energy then is transformed again into electrical when is needed.  Currently available ESS 
are CAES (Greenblatt, Succar, Denkenberger, Williams, & Socolow, 2007; Cavallo A. , 
2007; Hounslow, Grindley, Louglin, & Daly, 1998; Mason, Fthenakis, Zweibel, Hansen, & 
Nikolakakis, 2008; Succar & Williams, April 2008), pumped hydro storage (Lerch, 2007), 
batteries (Wang, Vilathgamuwa, & Choi, 2008), supercapacitors (Abbey & Joόs, 2007), 
flywheels (Takahashi, Wu, Murata, & Tamura, 2005) and superconducting magnetic energy 
storage (Lee, Wang, Chen, & Chen, 2009).   
The evaluation of these technologies is based on their energy density, their cost, their store 
capacity and the time that they can supply. Table 1-1 presents a comprehensive comparison of 
these parameters for the mentioned ESS.

1.1.2.1. 
Pumped Hydroelectric Storage (PHS)  
 
In 1892 in Zurich, Switzerland the first hydroelectric storage plant built (Roberts, 2009). 
After the first attempt the technology has significantly improved and is globally used. The 
great advantage of the PHS is the huge store potential. It can store energy up to half a year 
(Ibrahim, Ilinca, & Perron, 2008; Task Committee on Pumped Storage of the Committee, 
2008; Task Committee on Pumped Storage of the Hydropower, 1993). This value can be vary 
and is closely related to the size of the reservoir and the availability of the water. Presently, 
the installed capacity of PHS covers 3 % of the worldwide generation capacity (Roberts, 
2009).

1.1.2.2. 
Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES) 
 
Air is an elastic medium which has the “ability” to store energy when is under compression 
and release it when is needed. The idea of mechanical elastic energy originates since 
prehistoric times, when a bow and an arrow were used for hunting. However, the need to 
control the expansion of the compressed air led to the idea of using a gas turbine. Thus, the 
CAES technology was created. The first attempt of a CAES with an under-ground cavern 
made by Stal Laval in 1949 (Ter-Gazarian & Institution of Eectrical Engineers, 1994). 
The main principles of a CAES are similar with those of a gas turbine. However, in a CAES 
the compressor and the turbine are not connected and thus can operate separately depending if 
the system is on the charge or discharge mode. In order to understand better the operation of 
the CAES a briefly explanation is given. The air is passing through the compressor where is 
compressed to the desirable pressure. Then, is driven to the cavern (underground or 
underwater) where is stored. Afterwards, when electrical energy is needed to be fed into the 
system the compressed air is driven from the cavern to a combustion chamber and then to the 
turbine to produce power. The basic configuration of a CAES is given in Figure 1-2
 
Figure 1-2: Configuration of a simple CAES plant 
 
As Figure 1-2 indicates, the main components of a CAES are the a) compressor or 
compressor train, b) motor/generator, c) reservoir and d) expansion train. The whole 
procedure can be divided into three stages. The first stage is the compression of the air, the 
==PAGE:28==
  
 
Introduction 
7 
 
second stage is the storage of the compressed air and the third is the expansion. As it is 
mentioned above, in order to compress the air, the surplus of the grid is used. The motor is 
driving the compressor. The air is compressed up to 100 bars, mainly 60 – 100 bars. 
However, in that pressure levels the temperature is really high which might lead to unstable 
conditions into the reservoir. Thus, intercoolers are used after every compression stage. The 
pressurized air when enters the cavern has a temperature around 30-50 o C (Liu, et al., 2014). 
Another reason for reducing the temperature in the cavern is to reduce the storage volume and 
also the thermal stresses on the surroundings in the cavern (Succar & Williams, April 2008).  
On the other hand, the efficiency of the expander is closely related in the inlet conditions of 
the air. Hence, a burner is used before the expander in order to increase again the temperature. 
As it is described above, a CAES has two inputs, the electricity for the compression and the 
fuel in the burner. However, those two occur in different periods in time and thus there is not 
a clear definition of the roundtrip efficiency (Elmegaard & Markussen, 2011). From the 
literature review is shown that the round trip efficiency of a CAES can reach values up to 
70%, based on the configuration of the plant (Grazzini & Milazzo, Thermodynamic analysis 
of CAES/TES systems for renewable energy plants, 2008).  
 
Existing CAES Plants 
 
CAES systems are between the commercial large-scale options that exist today, suitable for 
longer duration storage (tens of hours) and with relative low cost (Madlener & Latz, 2013).  
However, currently only two power plants exist using this technology. The limited demand 
for energy storage systems in the past decades did not force the researches to improve the 
storage technology. 
The first CAES plant with the longest operating facility is located in Germany in a place 
called Huntorf. It was built in order to provide cheap peak load power and to assist the 
nuclear power plants, by providing them electricity. Thus, nuclear plants do not rely on the 
transmission grid. It was commissioned at 1978 with an output of 290 MW. In 2006 it was 
upgraded to 321 MW (Clean Energy). Nowadays, the plant assists the industries in the area 
and also is used in order to absorb the fluctuations of the wind farms in the Germany (Var der 
Linden, 2006; EPRI & U.S. Department of Energy, 2003; Crotogino, 2001). The Huntorf

Suitable topologies for caverns 
 
Most of the cases a CAES plant requires underground caverns to store the compressed air. 
The main characteristic that a cavern should have in order to be suitable is the depth. The 
depth should be enough in order to safely withstand all the pressure levels (EPRI & U.S. 
Department of Energy, 2003). There are three different categorization s of caverns: a) salt, b) 
porous and c) hard – rock. Most of the U.S underground fulfill the depth restriction which 
gives to CAES a great potential (Succar & Williams, April 2008; Ribeiro, 2001). 
Due to the nature of salt caverns, the elasto – plastic properties of salt, are the most 
appropriate to operate since the chance for air leakage are really low (Succar & Williams, 
April 2008). Most of the salt cavern are created by dry mining or solution mining, with cost 
of 10 and 1 $/kWh respectively (EPRI & U.S. Department of Energy, 2003).  The mining 
solution poses a disadvantage since the disposal of the brine might be harmful for the water 
(Ter-Gazarian & Institution of Eectrical Engineers, 1994).

Alternative CAES 
 
After the first CAES patent in 1949, engineers and researchers are trying to improve the 
technology. Most of the suggested modifications aim to increase the heat rate of the plant and 
the flexibility. In order to achieve these improvements adding or removing components is 
required. A few examples of such modifications is the McIntosh CAES which uses a 
recuperator after the expanders, a steam injected cycle, a CAES with humidification, the 
advanced adiabatic CAES (AA-CAES), the isothermal CAES (I-CAES) and the underwater 
or ocean CAES. 
The AA – CAES, Figure 1-4, is a promising solution for a carbon free future since it does not 
emit CO2. The explanation lies on the fact that it does not use fossil fuel for the burner 
(Jakiel, Zunft, & Nowi, August 2007). The basic principle of this configuration is that after 
the expansion stage, the released heat instead of wasted it into the environment stored in a 
thermal energy storage (TES). During the discharge stage, the CAES operates as an 
intercooler, reduces the temperature of the compressed air. In the literature there is no 
homogeneity for the round trip efficiency and thus values from 50-75 % can be found 
(Grazzini & Milazzo, 2008; EPRI & U.S. Department of Energy, 2003; Calaminus, 2007; 
Zunft, Jakiel, Koller, & Bullough, 2006; Pickard, Hansing, & Shen,2009).  
However, this concept faces some difficulties which are connected with the design of the 
TES. In order to design properly a TES it should be taken into account the heat losses inside 
as well as the losses in the air cavern. Due to the heat losses the temperature for re-heat the air 
is not always the same, which results to uneven temperature during the discharge stage. Thus 
the expander output during the discharge stage is not the same (Nielsen & Leithner, 2009).

A similar configuration with the AA – CAES is the isobaric adiabatic compressed air energy 
storage with combined cycle (ISACOAST - CC). This concept is been developed by the 
Institute for Heat and Fuel Technology (Nielsen & Leithner, 2009). It combines the adiabatic 
and diabatic process since it has both a TES and a combustion chamber. It uses also a brine 
shuttle pond at the surface to ensure the isobaric nature of the application. During the 
charging mode the brine is going from the cavern into the shuttle pond in order the 
underground reservoir to fill with compressed air. In that procedure it can be assumed that the 
pressure remains constant (Nielsen & Leithner, 2009). Before entering the cavern, the 
released heat from the compression stage is stored into the TES.  During the discharge mode 
the air is passing through the TES, is heated up and then is driven into the combustion 
chamber in order to maintain the desirable temperature. This concept ensures a constant inlet 
temperature in the turbine and thus a constant output during the discharge mode. The storage 
efficiency of this configuration is calculated to be 84.02% (Nielsen & Leithner, 2009). 
Another promising approach is the Isothermal - CAES (I – CAES). The main principle is to 
nullify the heat transfer at the compression and expansion stage. In order to achieve that, a 
large surface is used between water and air side inside a reciprocating compressor and 
expander. A hydraulic piston is used to compress together air and water. Due to high heat 
capacity of the water, the temperature of it remains almost constant and much lower than the 
air. Thus heat transfer occurs from the air to the water. Due to that the air temperature 
==PAGE:35==
  
 
Introduction 
14 
 
increases only locally and slightly. The overall efficiency of this configuration is around 70 % 
(Park, Ro, & Lim, 2012).   
The major challenge of this implementation is that the heat should be removed constantly 
from the air during the compression stage and to be added constantly during the expansion 
stage in order to achieve the isothermal process. Since heat transfer is proportional to the 
gradient of the temperature multiplied with the surface area, a huge surface is required in 
order to ensure the isothermal procedure. 
Even if there are no any commercial large scale I- CAES plants there are several companies, 
General Compression, Sustain X, LightSail Energy that are trying to develop such a 
configuration (Ingersoll & Aborn, 2012; Bollinger, 2010; Fong, Crane, & Berlin Jr, 2012). 
The underwater or ocean CAES (UWCAES) is a hopeful application that deals with 
electricity storage as well. A great difference compared the above applications is that due to 
its nature, UWCAES, can be coupled with offshore wind turbines. Nowadays, two different 
types of UWCAES exist. The one that uses concrete spheres, which is also called isochoric 
(constant volume) and the energy bags or isobaric (constant pressure).  One advantage of the 
isobaric concept compared to the isochoric is that since it can be assumed that the pressure is 
constant, the expander operates with higher efficiency than the one in the isochoric 
application.  
The concrete spheres have been studied by Slocum et. al  ,and have concluded  that are 
economical feasible at shallows waters (200 m) and until 1500 m depth the cost is dropping 
(Slocum, Fennell, & Dundar, 2013). The capacity of the storage sphere is related to the inner 
volume and the depth. Thus deeper waters seem more attractive locations (Slocum, Fennell, 
& Dundar, 2013). 
The energy bag uses the hydrostatic pressure in order to maintain constant pressure inside the 
bag. The surrounding water acts as pressure restrain (Pimm, Garvey, & De Jong, 2014). As 
the concrete spheres in order to exploit the surrounding water, the deeper is placed the higher 
the pressure. In that case, since the surrounding water has the same pressure with the 
compressed air inside the energy bag, it should be mentioned that for every 100 m depth the 
pressure increases by 10 bar (Slocum, Fennell, & Dundar, 2013).

TEES model 
 
In the last years few studies have proposed a new method for large scale energy storage. As it 
was mentioned above, section 1.2, TEES is an application where a heat pump is used in order 
to store thermal energy, and a heat engine is used in order to restore the energy. The 
advantages of this application are the higher energy density, compared with other energy 
storage applications, and the independence from the geological formations.  
In this subsection a TEES system with trans-critical CO2 is presented. The basic principles 
behind the system are highlighted. Furthermore, the theory, the assumptions and the essential 
equations will be discussed.  
2.1.1. Working principle of TEES 
 
A TEES system can be divided into the charge and the discharge mode. During the charging 
mode Figure 2-1, electric power from the grid is used to drive the compressor and compress 
the working fluid. Afterwards, the fluid passes through a condenser where the heat exchange 
from the compressed fluid to the secondary working medium, which is this case is water, take 
place. The thermal energy is stored into hot water tank. Following this the cooled fluid is 
expanded through the expander and afterwards, passes through the evaporator in order to 
change phase and absorbs heat.

During the discharge mode Figure 2-2, TEES is operating antistrophe of the charging mode. 
Initially, the working fluid is compressed and heated to the ambient temperature and 
afterwards, the working fluid is heated even more from the heat that is been transferred 
through the secondary fluid before enter to the expander. The two heat exchangers are used in 
order to maintain the temperatures limits. Otherwise, due to irreversibility of the system in 
each cycle the temperatures would be different.

2.1.3.  CO2 as working fluid  
 
Carbon dioxide (R744 in refrigerant nomenclature) has been used in the refrigeration system 
for many years. Afterwards, it was replaced by organic working fluids. Nowadays, due to 
global warming avoidance natural working fluids, such as R744, are attracting more 
attention. Due to its thermo-physical properties, low critical point and high power density, 
R744 is preferred for water heating heat pumps, in refrigeration systems etc. 
Table 2-2 summarizes the most common used working fluids. R744 is distinguished by its 
low critical temperature and this allows coupling well with the water as the storage media. 
Moreover, it has great thermal properties, has no significant environmental impact; is non- 
toxic and non – flammable (Mercangoz M. et al.,2012). 
 R744 is characterized also by its high absolute pressure. However, this high pressure 
constitutes a requirement for the supercritical condition rather than a property of the fluid. 
Hence, this is not actually a disadvantage of the R744 since R744 has lower absolute pressure 
compared to other supercritical fluids.  
In addition, due to low surface tension and thus to small cavitation effect, R744 can 
compressed and expanded closely to the saturation conditions. Close to the critical region 
small compression ratios are required which results to higher efficiencies for the machinery.  
It is also worth noting that the TEES system is assumed to be a zero leakage system. By 
sealing the rotating machines any leakage of the refrigerant will be captured and recirculated 
to the CO2 circuit.

charging and discharging occurs reversible3 𝜂𝑅𝑇= 1. The two circles are running contrary.

Since it is an ideal TEES cycle the isentropic efficiencies of the machines have assumed to be

when no exergy destruction occur. If there is temperature difference in the charging and 
discharging circuit then the cycle is not reversible and 𝜂𝑅𝑇< 1.

identify the location and the magnitude of the losses. This analysis provides very important

improve particular components.

Tsatsaronis, & Moran, 1996).

2.2. The underwater CAES (UWCAES) 
 
UWCAES is based on already existing concepts, most of them; inspired from the 
conventional compressed air energy storage systems. The most important difference between 
the two concepts is how the air is stored. As it was explained in section “Alternative CAES”, 
the accumulators in this application are placed under the water.  
In this subsection a UWCAES is presented. The basic principles of the system are 
highlighted. Moreover, the theory, the assumptions and the most important formulas will be 
discussed. 
2.2.1. Working principle 
 
An UWCAES can be divided into the charging and discharging mode. During charging, the 
compressor in driven by a motor in order to compress the medium, in this case is the air. 
Afterwards, the air is driven to the accumulators where is stored. In the present study, the 
accumulators are assumed to be flexible energy bags. In that case, the stored air is in 
equilibrium with the water, thus the pressure and the temperature of the stored air and the 
surrounding water are equal.

2.2.2. TES Stage 
 
The TES system is one of the greatest challenges in UWCAES. The system has to store 
adequate thermal energy in order to re heat the stored air before entering the turbines. The 
system uses sensible heat storage6. The presence of TES in an UWCAES is essential since it 
improves the performance of the system and protects the compressors and turbines and 
furthermore it converts the system to fossil fuel free. As it will be discussed in section “Air 
Storage Stage”, an UWCAES operates in low temperature and pressures levels. Thus, water 
from lakes can be used as the thermal storage medium. The dimension of the HEXs is very 
important, since they should be sufficiently sized to guarantee the optimal heat extraction 
during the compression stage, and provide adequate heat for the expansion stage. More 
detailed analysis for dimensioning a HEX can be found in the literature (Shah & Sekulić, 
2003). 
The heat transfer among the compressed air and the TES occurs through a set of three plate 
heat exchangers, see Figure 2-8. As it can be observed from the above figure there are three 
heat exchangers that receive the heat from the compression stage and store it to the hot water 
tank. When the air is exiting the energy bag, it passes through the other two heat exchangers 
in order to be heated.  
It is assumed for the simplicity of the model that there are no-heat losses in the water tanks 
and that is a close system which means that the mass flow of the water is conserved.

4. VERIFICATION OF MODELS 
1.1. Verification of EES models 
 
In this section the results from the EES models, are compared with the literature in order to 
evaluate how accurate the models are. If the verification ensures that the models are 
providing with correct results, then they can be used as a tool in future work.  It should be 
mentioned that since the presented models, especially for the TEES model, are not 
commercial available yet and thus no experimental values are available, the verification will 
based on published papers from other researchers. The focus will be on how the models 
behave under variation of the parameters, and if these variations are following the same trend 
as in the literature. In this section the comparison will focus on the roundtrip efficiency of the 
cycles.  
1.1.1. Verification of TEES model 
 
The TEES model was compared with (Kim et al., 2013), and particularly with table 1 
“Isentropic” from  the paper. When the inputs are the same with those in paper, the models 
produces 𝜂𝑅𝑇= 50.3 % while in the literature is presented 𝜂𝑅𝑇= 51 %. Hence, it can be 
concluded that the model is providing with accurate results. The difference in the efficiency 
0.6 % might be from the different configuration of the storage tanks that is used in the paper. 
As it can be observed from, Figure 2-4, the presented model has one storage tank for the hot 
water, while (Kim et al., 2013) use 4 different to maximize the efficiency of the system. 
1.1.2. Verification of UWCAES model 
 
For the UWCAES two different models are presented and thus both of them need to be 
verified. Since the UWCAES is operating under the same principle as a conventional CAES, 
in order to verify the results, a CAES application will be used for the first model. 
The first one where a combustor, Figure 2-7, is used is compared with the Huntorf case, since 
the configuration is the same. The only difference is the pressure and temperature level of the 
compressed air. In the Huntorf case underground caverns are used to store the compressed 
air. When the same inputs are used (Raju, 2012) the model is giving roundtrip efficiency of 
𝜂𝑅𝑇= 40.59 % while in the literature Huntorf power plant operating with 𝜂𝑅𝑇= 42 %. The 
==PAGE:67==
  
 
Verification of Models

difference of 1.5% of the efficiency is due to the different stored conditions of the air. In the 
UWCAES the stored air is in thermal equilibrium with the water and thus the temperature is 8 
o C, while in Huntorf case the temperature inside the cavers is 50 o C. 
The second model, where a TES is used is compared with the results from (Cheung, 
Carriveau, & Ting, 2014). The results showed slightly different round trip efficiency, since 
from the model is been calculated 𝜂𝑅𝑇= 62.87 % while in the paper 𝜂𝑅𝑇= 56.2 %. That

pipe losses were taken into account. This of course, results to lower roundtrip efficiency, 
while for the simplicity in the model no pipe calculations were made. These reasons can 
explain the divergence in the results.

6. DISCUSSION 
 
The purpose of the present study was to examine and compare different electricity storage 
applications. In this chapter the main results will be discussed. Furthermore, the results will 
be compared with the literature in order to investigate if the created models generate reliable 
outcomes. 
6.1. TEES model 
In the TEES several parameters were varied in order to investigate the sensitivity of the 
application. The variation of the isentropic efficiencies of the expander and compressor, , by 
setting values higher than the reference, resulted to both higher energy and exergy efficiency, 
since the machines wasted less energy, in the form of heat. Specifically, when the isentropic 
efficiency set from 0.815 to 0.915 [-] the roundtrip efficiency increased by almost 35% and 
reached the value of 61.05% see Figure 5-5. The higher isentropic efficiencies of the 
machines resulted to less required energy and thus better performance of the system. 
Additionally, the output power increased from 451 KW to 610 KW. This resulted to a raise in 
the specific energy storage by 20% compared to the reference value, from 51.26 to 63.08 
KJ/Kg, see formula ( 24 ). The analysis showed that the TEES is most sensitive to the 
isentropic efficiency variation compared to the other parameters.  
On the contrary, the variation of the temperature of the evaporator to higher values than the 
reference gave lower roundtrip efficiency of the system and lower specific energy storage. 
However, when this parameter set to negative temperatures from 𝑇𝐿= 0 ℃ to 𝑇𝐿= −3 ℃, 
the roundtrip efficiency raised by almost 1 %, from 45.1 to 45.6%, while the same minor 
alternation was observed in the specific energy storage, from 51.6 to 53.2 KJ/Kg, see 
appendix “TEES: REFERENCE MODEL RESULTS AND DIAGRAM”. The reason for 
these results, is that the during the discharge mode, when the turbine is generating electricity, 
the turbine could expander more and thus could produce more power. Since the input net 
power remained constant throughout the analysis, higher production from the turbine results 
to higher roundtrip efficiency.  
Moreover, the temperatures of the storage tanks were altered. When the temperature of the 
cold tank dropped compared to the reference from 𝑇𝑆𝑇𝑂𝑅,𝐿= 20 ℃ to 𝑇𝑆𝑇𝑂𝑅,𝐿= 15 ℃ , the 
TEES operated  slightly more effectively giving an increased roundtrip efficiency by 3%

from 45.1 to 46.4%, while a slightly higher alternation was observed in the specific energy 
storage by almost 5 %, from 51.2 to 53.8 KJ/Kg, see appendix “TEES: REFERENCE

results is how the model is created. The temperature at the inlet in the hot side of the gas 
cooler is related to the hot storage temperature. Consequently, higher temperature of the 
storage tank, leads to higher temperature on the CO2 at the inlet of gas cooler. However, even 
though the pressure increased by almost 10% the roundtrip efficiency varied by only 2%. 
This comes to align with (Kim, Shin, Lee, & al., 2013) results. The higher pressure in the gas 
cooler led to higher back work ratio and thus the increase in the roundtrip was not as high.

can be observed from Figure 5-8 & Figure 5-9. When the value of the isentropic efficiency of 
the compressor and expander grew, the total destruction of the system reduced, following the 
trend of the compressor and the turbine for the charging and discharging mode respectively. 
A higher value of the isentropic efficiency resulted to less wasted energy and thus less exergy 
destruction in the compression / expansion stage. 
It can be concluded that the isentropic efficiency has a significant contribution on the 
system’s performance. A careful selection of the machines would increase dramatically the 
performance of the system.

6.2. UWCAES model 
As it is presented in the results section, two different UWCAES configurations were 
modelled one with combustor and the other with a TES. 
Firstly, the case where combustors were used will be discussed. When the depth where the 
energy bag is placed, altered; a minor change in the energy efficiency of the system occurred. 
As it can be seen in Figure 5-11 the roundtrip efficiency had an increase by 5%, when the 
depth varied from 50-100 m. The increase after the 100 m was smaller. The reason for that is 
that due to the small pressure ratio, the TIT had set constant 300 ℃. Hence, this put a barrier 
to the expansion stage. In order to achieve higher energy efficiency of the system while the 
depth varies, higher TIT should be set. However, this would lead to higher fuel consumption 
which would downgrade, probably, the performance of the system. Overall, 300 ℃ for such a 
small pressure ratio is a reasonable assumption. 
On the contrary, the specific energy storage of the energy bag, as it was expected, increased 
by almost 15% to the value of 9.53 KJ/Kg, when the depth set to be 150 m. The depth where 
the energy bag is placed has a direct relation to the potential mass of the air that can be stored 
and consequently to the output power of the turbines. However, placing the energy bag in 
depth larger than 150 m would have an impact on the total price of the system, as well as in 
its performance. By placing the energy bag in greater depths, longer pipes would be required 
which would increase the cost of the application as well as it would increase the pressure 
drop along the pipes. This might downgrade the overall efficiency of the system. A careful 
and detailed analysis is required in order to derive more accurate results. In the analysis the 
pipe losses were neglected, for the simplicity of the models. However, it has been proven 
from other researches for convectional CAES that the deeper the accumulator is placed, the 
more losses occurred in the pipes. Thus, the selection of the depth should be driven by the 
energy efficiency of the UWCAES and not from the output of the turbines. 
When the isentropic efficiency of the machines has changed the results were different than 
the previous parameter variation, see Figure 5-12. The specific energy storage remained 
unchanged, since there is no correlation between those two parameters. Nevertheless, the 
performance of the system significantly improved. When the isentropic efficiency was set to 
0.915 [-] from 0.815 [-], the energy efficiency of the system was improved by almost 15 %

Moreover, an improvement on the previous configuration was presented. A regenerator /

7. CONCLUSION 
 
The importance of the current study stems from its contribution to one of  the largest research 
areas in the energy field, the electricity storage. The presented project investigated different 
electricity storage applications, calculated the performance of the systems and highlighted the 
optimal case for each configuration. 
Firstly, the project rewieved the thermo-electric energy storage, using heat pump and heat 
engine combined with thermal energy storage, as a novel approach of large scale energy 
storage. While the system operated under its optimal conditions, the roundtrip efficiency 
increased by 40% reaching the value of 63.8%. Furthermore, it is proved that the exergy 
losses in the machines downgraded the performance of the system. Thus, with a given 
isentropic efficiency it is essential to minimize the back work ratio in order to maximize the 
performance of the cycle.  Furthermore, the system found to be sensitive to the temperature 
of the evaporator, as well as, to the temperatures of the storage medium. Thus a careful 
design of the storage tanks is vital for higher roundtrip efficiency. 
Afterwards, two different UWCAES were studied. UWCAES is a promising energy storage 
application, using the same principles as the conventional CAES. When combustors and 
regenerator were used the optimal case gave roundtrip efficiency of 56.63% and power output 
of 63MW. When a TES was used, the application gave an extremely high roundtrip 
efficiency of 81.13% for the optimal case, which is higher than the (Cheung, Carriveau, & 
Ting, 2014). The sensitivity analysis in both systems showed that the machines with better 
isentropic efficiencies and HEXs with high effectiveness should be uses in order to maximize 
the performance of the system. 
It should be stated that all the studied applications are using water as the storage medium, 
which gives to them great advantage compared to other applications, due to its low price and 
to its environmental friendly nature. 
Overall, the most promising application is the UWCAES with TES. Theoretically, it can 
operate with efficiency of 70%. However, since there is no any commercial available 
application yet, and thus under real conditions the application could operate less effectively, 
this project is suggests as the most attractive electricity storage system the TEES. The

application great prominence for the future energy system.  
 
7.1. Future work 
The presented work has focused on the thermodynamic analysis of the UWCAES and TEES, 
with respect to the first and second thermodynamic law. However, in order to apply these 
concepts under real conditions, the following proposals should be considered.  
 Thermo – economic optimization 
Thermo – economic modelling and optimization of the systems, would investigate which 
model can be integrated to the energy system. This can be implemented, by using 
optimization code, linear programming,  
 Dynamic simulations of the concepts 
In the studied models steady state analysis were presented. However, during charging and 
discharging mode the systems operate differently. Hence, a dynamic simulation of the 
system, taken into account the thermal inertia of the components should be created. 
Furthermore, the part load operation could be studied under dynamic simulation. This 
analysis will provide with important information for the system performance and also will 
provide a wider perspective of the operation conditions of the system. 
Finally, a dynamic analysis will enlighten both the air and thermal storage. In the steady state 
analysis the interactions between the storage tanks and the environment were neglected. 
Thus, temperature’s uniformity considered. However, under real conditions heat exchange 
occur between the tanks and the environment which might alter the results.
