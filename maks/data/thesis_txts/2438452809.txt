Abstract
The contribution of the international maritime sector to climate change has gained much
attention recently. The airborne exhaust gas emissions from ships and operations related
to the sector is said to be a major environmental problem. In Annex IV from the Inter-
national Maritime Organization a regulatory framework for the shipping industry is set.
The frameworks aims to minimize airborne emissions from ships.
The purpose of this research is to study the feasibility of installing a shore side connection
to the Maasvlakte 2 terminal in Rotterdam. The problem is motivated by the increase in
airborne emissions from the maritime sector, expected increase in fuel prices and the reg-
ulatory framework proposed by the International Maritime Organization. The feasibility
study is conducted by the use of well-known methods like the Cost-beneﬁt Analysis and
the Marginal Abatement Cost.
The research analysis on installing a shore side connection for the port resulted in a
feasible investment if only a proportion of the ships calling at the terminal would use the
equipment. From the ship owner perspective, the investment was under no circumstances
feasible. In terms of cost eﬀectiveness, the study showed that installing the equipment
would be cost eﬀective when approximately 20-40% would use the technique.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The International Maritime Organization (IMO) is working towards a substantial reduc-
tion in air pollution and other harmful impacts on the environment. The maritime sector
is set to play a major role in achieving that because the emissions from the sector and
their contribution to global warming is a major environmental problem. The maritime
sector is moving in the direction of using low sulphur fuels or renewable energy from
using fossil fuel or coal as the main power source. The main reasons for the magnitude
of the problem is mainly due to the fact that it has a global eﬀect, the consequences are
long term and mitigation requires major social and technological choices and investments
[Corbett et al., 2009]. The IMO is constantly applying new regulations to the maritime
industry, where the latest one is the ”International Convention on the Prevention of Pol-
lution from Ships” (MARPOL) Annex VI regulates that in January 2020, the sulphur
content of fuel can not exceed 0.5%.
The in-port emissions and their eﬀect on the coastal communities has attracted much
attention in recent decades. Even though, transporting goods with ships has been the
most environmentally friendly alternative in terms of emissions per unit of cargo. Suppli-
ers are becoming more determined to decrease their environmental footprint as there is
an increasing awareness among consumers on the environmental impact of the products,
throughout the supply chain, including the transportation sector. It is therefore really
important for some actions to be taken in order to minimize the environmental impact
on the global value chain. The expansion of international trade from globalization and
the increased traﬃc at ports make the maritime industry a key contributor to the an-
thropogenic emissions because of its high share of the global trade [Song, 2014].
Even though maritime transportation has some evident beneﬁts compared to land-based
transportation modes in terms of air and noise pollution, congestion and a small need
for infrastructure. The in-port emissions can have serious eﬀect on the local environment
and the coastal region. What characterizes large ports around the world is their density
and busy shipping activities. They are also often located very close to urban areas. It is
estimated that almost 70% of the total emissions from ships is generated within 400km
from the coast and most of the emissions is because of routine operations [Song, 2014].

Regulations of Emissions from Marine Transportation
The most known regulations that apply to the maritime industry is the ”International
Convention on the Prevention of Pollution from Ships” (MARPOL), adopted by the In-
ternational Maritime Organization (IMO). This regulation aims apply rules to reduce
emissions from the maritime industry during routine operations. The IMO uses annexes
(currently six) to design frameworks and cover the various sources of pollution from ships.
In 2005, The MARPOL Annex VI, which emphasizes on ”Regulations for the Prevention
of Air Pollution from Ships”, introduced stricter regulations in some operating areas, re-
ferred to as Emission Control Areas (ECAs). The goal is to reduce SOx and NOx emission
within these sensitive areas.

2.3.1
Slow Steaming/Vessel Speed Reduction
Slow steaming is the process where ships decrease their sailing speed, to improve their
fuel eﬃciency and to reduce CO2 emissions from international shipping [Cariou, 2011].
Regulators and port authorities have been focusing on reducing emission from the ocean-
going vessels as they are operation in populated areas. Slow Steaming or Vessel Speed
Reduction is one of the strategies that many ports and shipping companies have imple-
mented because the ships speed has been proven to have signiﬁcant eﬀect on the shipping
cost and total emissions [Khan et al., 2012].
There exists a non-linear relationship between the ships speed and its fuel consump-
tion and therefore by changing the speed of the ship will result in a proportionally more
eﬀect on the fuel consumption and the emissions than the change of speed. While fuel
consumption and emissions have a linear relationship, reducing the speed will have both
economical and environmental beneﬁts.
A research done by Psaraftis and Kontovas [2014] showed an example of container ship
having a design speed of 17.5 knots compared to the normal 22-25 knots emitted 20% less
CO2 per container and 50% less than the industries average on that speciﬁc trade lane
[Psaraftis and Kontovas, 2014]. In ﬁgure 2.5, the relationship between fuel consumption
and speed is shown for Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) which are one of the largest
cargo vessels operating in the world, both when the ships is carrying cargo (Laden) and
when the vessel is empty but with added weight for stability reasons (Ballast).

Doudnikoﬀand Lacoste [2014] examined the consequences of the low-sulphur require-
ments on vessel speed. A cost model was used to evaluate the cost-minimizing combi-
nation of speeds inside and outside the area and the total CO2 emission generated. In
order to stay within the low-sulphur limit of SECA, vessels have the possibility of reduc-
ing their speed which will lead to energy and costs savings [Doudnikoﬀand Lacoste, 2014].
Applying this technique involves a trade-oﬀbecause a vessel transferring goods between
ports are all on a time schedule, and reducing the speed within SECA will cause delay
that requires ships to increase the speed when departed from the SECA area, resulting
in more emissions and fuel burning. The authors assumed that when vessels entered the
low sulphur area, they switched from HFO to MGO [Doudnikoﬀand Lacoste, 2014].
When the reference value for the fuel prices are used and the scenario is compared to the
option of sailing at constant speed, the results show that by reducing the speed within
SECA and increasing it outside SECA results in lower cost. A option of adding a ship to

phases, maneuvering and berthing, where most of the emissions are generated while
berthing. The objective of cold ironing is to connect two separate systems, the power

Cost Beneﬁt Analysis
Cost-beneﬁt analysis (CBA) can be deﬁned as:
Cost-beneﬁt analysis is an economic evaluation technique that analyzes the generation
of economic beneﬁts and costs from a project or policy by comparing the discounted ﬂows
of beneﬁts and costs over a prescribed time horizon. [Dixon, 2012]
The cost-beneﬁt analysis can be traced back to the 1930s with the objective of analyzing
large public investment projects in the water sector in the United States. At that time,
only economical aspect were taken into account when calculating the net present value.
As time passed, including quantitative assessment of environmental and social beneﬁts
into the analysis became more and more important. The expansion of the economic based
CBA taking into account the environmental and social aspects of the projects requires
knowledge and experience [Dixon, 2012].
Tol [2003] argues in his paper that the uncertainty about climate change is too large
to include it in the CBA. He lists some research that either reduce the emission included
in the CBA to maximize social beneﬁts, ignoring uncertainty or not choosing the appro-
priate method when analysis uncertainty. His ﬁndings are that the CBA can be applied
when the critical factors are ﬁnite, there is a low probability of a catastrophic event so
the variance of the expected outcome is ﬁnite and low or negative discount rate will result
in large negative consequences of climate change [Tol, 2003].
Applying CBA as a decision making tool to cold ironing is a complex process that re-
quires detailed analysis of all aspects of the on-shore and oﬀ-shore electrical system. In
order to apply this method, all technical components of the system needs to be decided.
The current status of the amount of on-shore power available and the ships ability to
connect to an on-shore power source are important. The feasibility of the project is also
sensitive to the source of the power, for example an increase in fuel price will result in a
more eﬃciency system if the source of the power is renewable [Coppola et al., 2016].

Using the Cost-beneﬁt analysis (CBA) is a general method to assess the feasibility of
a project or a public investment. The beneﬁts of this method is that it translates all
quantitative environmental costs and beneﬁts into monetary terms. Enabling decision
makers to make comparison with economical aspects of the project. For that reason, this
method is especially relevant when analysis the feasibility of large public investments or
other projects that want to include the social beneﬁts of a project. The results will appear
in quantitative form with a balance between economical and environmental aspects of the
project [Diakoulaki and Karangelis, 2007].

and summerized into a current value to use in the decision making process. Resulting in
comparison between the discounted beneﬁts and the discounted costs associated with the

As previously mentioned, the Port of Rotterdam is the largest seaport in Europe. In
2015, the CO2 emissions in the port area accounted for 10% of the Netherlands total
emissions. The CO2 emissions in the port area have been increasing substantially since
1990, and grew by 48% until 2015 [Samadi et al., 2016]. In 2017, the Netherlands be-
came a part of the legally binding global climate deal, referred to as the Paris agreement
(adopted in December 2015).
This agreement aims to hold ”the increase in the global average temperature to well below
2 °C above pre-industrial levels and the pursue eﬀorts to limit the temperature increase
to 1.5 °C about pre-industrial levels [United Nations, Climate Change, 2015].
Addition to that, in 2011, the Port Authority of Rotterdam released a vision for 2030,
to reduce emission by 50% by 2025 compared to the emission level in 1990 and by 2030,
the aim is to reduce emissions by additional 10%. The emissions have been increasing
substantially since then, and the trend in the emissions does not reﬂect long-term cli-
mate change targets and decarbonization. The reason for the steep increase in emission
between 2013-2015 could be partially caused by the release in two new coal-ﬁred power
plants in the area [Samadi et al., 2016].
To respond to this negative development in local air pollution, the Port Authority com-
missioned the Wuppertal Institute for Climate, Environment and Energy to conduct a
study on Decarbonization Pathways for the Industrial Cluster of the Port of Rotterdam.
This study focused on analyzing the possible challenges and opportunities for the indus-
trial sector of the port. The institution developed several scenarios in order to have a
clear picture of how the industrial sector of the port could look like in 2050. The evalu-
ation from the study resulted in several options identiﬁed as especially promising for the
ports future. These items are listed below [Samadi et al., 2016]:

The auxiliary engine power is divided into two categories, the auxiliary engine total
power and the auxiliary engine output power. The auxiliary engine total power is
the total power generated from the engine to drive the generator. Taking into account,
some losses of power within the generator results in the auxiliary engine power output.
The auxiliary engine output power is the power available for the ship.
In the Clarksons database, information’s about the auxiliary engine total power usu-
ally was available. When they were not, some assumptions have to be made. Taking the
available data into account, the auxiliary engine total power is 26% of the main engines
power on average. This number was used to make assumptions about the auxiliary en-
gines power when not available.
When information’s about the power driving the generators were not available, it was

Chapter 6
Feasibility study of cold ironing
This section answers the following research questions: ”Is cold ironing a promising al-
ternative to reduce emissions from container ships in and around port areas?” and ”Is
it feasible to provide container ships at the Maasvlakte 2 terminal with shore-side elec-
tricity and what eﬀect has that on the air quality in the near area”. Furthermore, this
section will provide a cost eﬀectiveness analysis for providing a shore based electrical
power to the container ships calling at the Maasvlakte 2 terminal in Rotterdam. This is
a technique that allows ships at berth to use electricity provided by the port instead of
electricity generated by the auxiliary engines and therefore avoid burning fuel. The cold
ironing technique is mainly used in North America where it is mandatory to power up
with the port when being serviced. Therefore, many ships that operate both in North
America and in the Baltic Sea are often not equipped to connect to European ports due
to diﬀerence in frequency demand.
The main cost item involved in providing vessels electricity to reduces emissions are
the capital investment for the shipping company which consists of retroﬁtting their ﬂeet
and the resigning of the terminal. There are other cost items such as maintenance and
operations cost for the system, both oﬀ-and on-shore and the cost of purchasing electric-
ity instead of marine diesel oil. These cost items are discounted over the projects lifetime
and the net present value is estimated. The main advantages of this technique is that it is
an environmentally friendly alternative to power the ships in port in terms of emissions,
noise and vibration. This technique could signiﬁcantly improve air quality in the local
area, because using electricity instead of MGO produces less emissions and the emissions
from the electricity is generated in the power plant, often located far away from dense
areas.
In this study, it assumed that the construction period is two years and the project life-
time is 11 years. The investment cost would therefore occur in the ﬁrst two years, the
annual maintenance cost would be for 11 years and the beneﬁts would also be in the 11
years period. In order to have all the costs and beneﬁts to its current value, all costs and
beneﬁts are discounted with a discount rate of 4%.

Current Situation at Maasvlakte 2 terminal
During the data collection, which was a period of two weeks, the terminal had 172 vis-

regression, where the fuel consumption was used as the dependent variable and other fac-
tors as the explanatory variables. The building year, dead weight and the capacity were
not signiﬁcant with a 95% signiﬁcance level, meaning that these variables do not have
an individual eﬀect on the fuel consumption. There are several reasons why insigniﬁcant
relationships can occur in a linear regression [Aylin, 2010]. These reasons are listed below:
1. There is simply not a linear relationship between the dependent variable and the
explanatory variable.
2. The data set is not large enough or it is not random enough, which can lead to
insigniﬁcant results.
3. Multicollinearity can result in a insigniﬁcant results. Meaning that if there is a
linear relationships between two or more of the explanatory variables or one of the
explanatory variables is used to predict about another explanatory variable.

7.5
Study Weaknesses and Limitations
The main limitations to this analysis is the lack of data. Many attempts were made to get
data for ship traﬃc for longer period of time without any luck. This resulted in a manual
registering of the ship traﬃc for a two week period. Even thought, the main assumption
of this analysis is that the two weeks of data can be used to get the representation of
the ship traﬃc for a whole year. This can not really be justiﬁed, because the ship traﬃc
can vary a lot during the year. This limitation should be easily address by either more
time for data collection or previously registered ships visits should be available. The
secondary data proposes also some weaknesses to the analysis. The secondary data is
the data collected by someone else than the user its self. Therefore can the goals and
purpose of the data collection be diﬀerent from research to research. This can results
in diﬀerent values for the data collected depending on the assumptions made for the data.
When calculation the fuel consumption, which depends on the power, load factor, SFOC
and the time it was assumed that the load factor for all the ships were 0.17 on average.
There were many studies examined when estimating the appropriate load factor, some
of them suggested diﬀerent load factors depending on the ships capacity, when other
suggested a constant regardless of the capacity. A load factor of 0.17 might sound rela-
tively low as a constant for container ships transporting cargo with diﬀerent electricity
demands. The power demand of the ships in ports can vary signiﬁcantly. The type of
cargo is an important factor in this context because diﬀerent cargo requires diﬀerent
power. If the majority of the containers are reefer containers, the ships would require
more power compared to if the containers are transporting dry goods. It can have a lot of
eﬀect on the load factor of the auxiliary engines while in port if the ship is transporting
dry or reefer goods. If the exact load factor cannot be derived, some assumptions can be
made from the ships schedule. If the ships is going to, for example Asia or Africa, it is
possible to conclude that the proportion of reefer goods is high. Opposite to that, if the
ship is coming from Asia, going to Europe it is likely that the majority of the cargo is
dry goods.
Using Cost-Beneﬁt analysis to calculate or predict about the feasibility of an invest-
ment can also propose some limitation to the research. The method involves a lot of
assumptions and guesses of what the future might look like. The outcome of the NPV
calculations is totally reﬂected upon how accurate the cost and beneﬁts are predicted
and the discount rate. If the discount rate is far from the reality it can result in an over-
or underestimation of the projects potentials. The same applies to the costs and beneﬁts
of the project. Therefore, more methods should be used to validate the results. This
uncertainty about the calculations in the CBA are reﬂected in the MAC calculations.
Including the external cost in the CBA might give diﬀerent results than the MAC, but
the initial assumptions and guesses would still be present.

CHAPTER 7. DISCUSSION & RECOMMENDATIONS
87
7.6
Recommendations
In this section, recommendations by the author is presented and described. The recom-
mendations form the ﬁnal opinion about the thesis for possible further work for the Port
Authority of Rotterdam. The recommendation are entirely based on the results from the
study.
1. One recommendation to the Port authority of Rotterdam is to conduct a feasibility
study of using a portable cold ironing electrical power equipment. As the analysis
showed that installing cold ironing in every berth is not feasible, both because of
the high construction cost and also because most of the ships do not have the
equipment’s to connect to a shore power. Opposite the to stationary equipment,
the portable unit able the cold ironing equipment to move between ships that are
staying in the port and therefore be highly ﬂexible. In 2013, the Port of Hamburg
introduced LNG-fuelled barge for Cold Ironing which works like a ﬂoating power
plant. The LNG-barge generates power via a gas container ﬁlled with LNG for low-
emission transfer to the ships [Ship & Bunker, 2013]. As previously mentioned, the
construction cost is the predominant factor in the feasibility calculations. A towed
barge or self-propelled vessel require lower capital investments and are characterized
by smaller dimension and weight [Laviola et al., 2018]. This could be a option for
the the Port of Rotterdam because this is a very ﬂexible option as the LNG-fuelled
barge is able to move between ships due to their size and allows the feeder to
approach each ship [Laviola et al., 2018].
2. Another recommendation is to examine the feasibility of switching to LNG as an
alternative fuel based on economical and environmental performance in and around
the port. The disadvantages of installing the necessary equipments in order to use
LNG as a marine fuel instead of MGO are very expensive and also space demanding,
which could have eﬀect on the ships capacity.
There are many environmental innovations available in order to reduce emissions. One
way of identifying the most promising alternative would be to conduct a multi-criteria
analysis (MCA) with respect to cost, diﬀerent emissions, noise, time etc. of the project.
The multi-criteria analysis is a method that can be used in a decision making process to
evaluate a project based on environmental performance. The projects are given a order
of preferences from multiple alternatives, based on number of criteria that may have dif-
ferent weight [Hermann et al., 2007].
For the Port of Rotterdam, implementing cold ironing results in a major reduction in
NOx, PM10, elimination of SOx and a great reduction in CO2, while using LNG as a ship
fuel results major reduction in SOx, NOx and PM10 emissions, but a minor reduction in
CO2. The alternative choice could also be dependent on the location of the port, the
electricity composition from the grid, the investment cost and so on. Preferences may
also diﬀer from port to port. This complexity could be addressed to some extent by
conducting a multi-criteria analysis.

CHAPTER 7. DISCUSSION & RECOMMENDATIONS
88
7.7
Conclusion
This research project has analyzed the possibility of installing a shore side power connec-
tion for the Port of Rotterdam as well as provided a feasibility analysis of installing the
equipment’s on-board container ships. This research highlights the main costs and bene-
ﬁts of this installation as well as presents the potential emission reduction related to each
investment. The analysis is mainly a desk based analysis of the collected data, while the
Cost-beneﬁt analysis was used for the feasibility calculations. The cost eﬀectiveness of
the investments was evaluated through the use of the Marginal Abatement Cost method,
were the cost of every ton abated of emission is the resulting value.
The feasibility study showed that it is economically feasible to install the shore side
power supply for the port to one or two berths if 20%-40% of the ship would use the
equipment. Installing the equipment for more than 40% of usage resulted in an unfea-
sible investment, mainly due to high investment cost. As the usage will increase, more
cold ironing equipment’s are needed, with additional investment and maintenance cost.
The investment cost was found to be the most signiﬁcant cost factor in the feasibility
calculations and the decision making process is highly dependent on that factor. Whether
the port would get subsidy or using cold ironing would be mandatory, could also give a
diﬀerent view to the decision making process. Currently, there is not enough motive to
assume and invest in the cold ironing for the majority of the shipping ﬂeet.
Based on the main ﬁndings in this research, relying only on the cold ironing technique
to provide all ships that call at the Port of Rotterdam is not a feasible option. Various
reduction measures could result in a more economically and environmentally results than
only using electricity to replace fuel in ports. The results from this research apply how-
ever only to the data that was collected for a time period of two weeks. Using that data
to represent a year of observation creates a lot of error in the results.
