Abstract 
Background and moƟvaƟon: Compartmentalized microﬂuidic devices are an important 
tool in neuroscience research. SoŌ lithography has long been the method of choice 
for the fabricaƟon of these devices, but this fabricaƟon method hinders the design 
possibiliƟes for the devices. 3D printed soŌ lithography introduces a new way of 
manufacturing compartmentalized microﬂuidic devices through extrusion based ad-
diƟve manufacturing techniques, but sƟll has room for improvement. 
Method: This study builds on the novel design of extrusion-based 3D printed soŌ lithog-
raphy, trying to increase the innervaƟon potenƟal of neurons and design opƟons 
through the exploraƟon of bioink composiƟon and structure design. 
Results: By improving the design parameters of 3D printed soŌ lithography models 
through the exploraƟon of shape, sizes and channel variables an increase of neuronal 
device coverage of more than 35% was found. A new bioink was aƩempted to be for-
mulated, with improved biocompaƟbility and improved contact qualiƟes with the sil-
ica master mold wafer, but this needs more work, as the print stability was unsaƟs-
factory.  
Conclusion:  In this thesis it was demonstrated that 3D printed soŌ lithography show 
great potenƟal in improving the methodology of soŌ lithography, opening up for a 
plethora of new possibiliƟes in neuronal research.

1. IntroducƟon 
 
The brain is one of the most complex organs in the human body with more than 3000 diﬀerent 
cell types [1] out of which neurons and glial cells are the most abundant, with 86 billion neu-
rons interconnected by trillions of synapses [2]. Some neurons form networks within their 
environment, while others extend long distance projecƟons to innervate neighboring or dis-
tant brain regions. These large cellular communicaƟon networks give rise to the funcƟonal 
organizaƟon of the brain, while being subjected to a plethora of chemical, physical and envi-
ronmental factors, all factors that have an impact on the neurons’ growth and behavior, mak-
ing studying neurons in vivo challenging.  
The neuron itself has a polarized morphology, with 
clear disƟnct cellular compartments, being den-
drites, soma, and axon, as illustrated on ﬁgure 1.1.  
This clear cellular anatomical diversion has made it 
possible to culture isolated or bundles of primary 
neurons in compartmentalized microﬂuidic devices, 
trapping the soma in one compartment, while al-
lowing the thinner axons and dendrites to grow 
through microchannels into a target compartment, 
neurite guidance. These in vitro models provide a 
simpliﬁed environment in which researchers can 
control speciﬁc neuron impacƟng variables allowing for the study of e.g. neural biology, dis-
ease models, and drug screening applicaƟons. [3] [4] [5] 
Today most compartmentalized microﬂuidic devices are fabricated from a polydimethylsilox-
ane (PDMS) using standard soŌ lithography. This fabricaƟon methodology requires a series of 
manual postprocessing, that introduces the risk of human error, while also limiƟng the device 
design possibiliƟes, limiƟng the complete innervaƟon of cultured neurons.  
A novel fabricaƟon methodology was introduced in 2020 [6], that overcomes major limitaƟons 
of the tradiƟonal soŌ lithography, while encouraging new high aspect-raƟo design possibili-
Ɵes, through addiƟve deposiƟon of PDMS using an extrusion driven 3D bioprinter.

2.1.2 SoŌ lithography 
Today compartmentalized microﬂuidic devices are most commonly fabricated using soŌ li-
thography, see Figure 2.2 which was ﬁrst introduced in 1990’Ɵes by George M. Whitesides and 
Younan Xia.[9] SoŌ lithography is a prototyping technique where a PDMS is replica-molded 
from a paƩerned master mold, oŌen a silica wafer. This fabricaƟon method quickly gained 
popularity for its easy in fabricaƟon, fast and cheap producƟon, and repeatability. The fabri-
caƟon of the master mold is created though photolithography, which can be divided into spin 
coaƟng, exposure, and development[9], [10], [11], [12]. 
Spin coaƟng is the ﬁrst step of photolithography. Here a plain silicon wafer, called a mater, is 
aƩached to motorized plaƞorm and a light sensiƟve polymer, a photoresist, is applied to the 
center of the master’s surface. During deposiƟon the master is spun at a ﬁxed low revoluƟons 
per minute, rpm, ensuring the whole master is coated with the photoresist. Photoresists of a 
hydrocarbon polymer resin and a photoacƟve component dissolved in an organic solvent, 
which makes the photoresist a viscous material allowing it to ﬂow and thereby coat the wafer.

PDMS has become the most widely used material in microﬂuidic device fabricaƟon due to its 
many advantages, such as opƟcal transparency, gas permeability, low cost, biocompaƟbility, 
ease of handling, and low shrinkage when curing (1,1%) [15], [16]. PDMS is a polymeric orga-

group aƩached to these atoms, the basic repeaƟng unit of PDMS is a siloxane unit represented 
as [−𝑆𝑖(𝐶𝐻ଷ)ଶ−𝑂−], in this unit silicon is bounded by two methyl groups forming the or-
ganosilicon part of the polymer, with the oxygen atom linking the siloxane units. Sylgard 184

a curing agent and is mixed at a scale of 10% curing agent, which will result in full curing aŌer

Replica molding is the single step prototyping technique for duplicaƟng the shape, size and 
paƩerns of the features of the master, created by photolithography. Here a PDMS is mixed 
with a curing agent, degassed to remove trapped air, and poured onto the master mold. The 
PDMS and wafer is cured thermally at around 65°𝐶, depending on the PDMS and curing agent, 
which hardens the PDMS. AŌer curing the hardened layer of PDMS is peeled away, leaving a 
negaƟve imprint of the wafer in the PDMS. [9] A single wafer oŌen contains several templates 
for the compartmentalized microﬂuidic device, due to their oŌen-small sizes. AŌer the PDMS 
is cured these need to be freed from the single block of hardened PDMS, which is achieved 
through manual cuts. Depending on if there is a need to add and/or remove ﬂuids during the 
experimental phase, then a hole can be punched in the PDMS. Lastly the PDMS needs to be 
bonded to create a closed boƩom of the compartmentalized microﬂuidic device, with glass or 
another piece of PDMS oŌen being used. The PDMS will not bond naturally to any surface, 
and will therefore need surface treatment, with oxygen plasma treatment being the most 
widely used method. [17], [18]. During the oxygen plasma treatment, the plasma removes 
organic material and leaves a silanol group (Si-OH) on the surface of PDMS and glass. Combin-
ing PDMS and glass or PDMS-PDMS quickly aŌer plasma oxygen treatment will yield irreversi-
ble sealed covalent Si-O-Si bonds, thereby ﬁnalizing the closed compartmentalized microﬂu-
idic device.  
 
2.1.3 3D printed soŌ lithography. 
3D printed soŌ lithography is a newer prototyping technique introduced in 2020 by Janko 
Kajtez et al. 2020[6]. 3D printed soŌ lithography builds on an addiƟve manufacturing ap-
proach, using extrusion-based 3D prinƟng of PDMS onto a master mold. The PDMS is ex-
truded through openings in the micrometer range, which allow for very precise material dep-
osiƟon and makes open compartment designs with high aspect-raƟo walls and microstruc-
tures possible. This precise deposiƟon reduces the material amount required for tradiƟonal 
soŌ lithography, while also removing the need for cuƫng and piercing post-producƟon.

Extrusion-based 3D prinƟng is an umbrella term referring to the methodology of extruding a 
material automaƟcally in a predetermined paƩern. There is a plethora of applicaƟons for ex-
trusion-based 3D prinƟng from 3D prinƟng houses using cement as the material of choice [19] 
to bioprinƟng, which refers to the extrusion of biomaterials, biocomponents and/or living cells 
classiﬁed as bioinks [20][21].  
In tradiƟonal soŌ lithography it is important that PDMS has a low viscosity, allowing it to 
spread uniformly over the master mold, 3D printed soŌ lithography relies on the extrusion of 
a bioink driven either by PneumaƟc- or mechanical extrusion, which have more complex rhe-
ological requirements for the bioink, to ensure printability and shape ﬁdelity post print.    
An understanding of basic material mechanism of viscoelasƟc materials, like PDMS, is helpful 
in understanding the forces and changes a viscoelasƟc material undergoes during and aŌer 
the prinƟng process. 
During bioprinƟng a syringe is loaded into the bioprinter and deposits the bioink through a 
syringe nozzle of varying size depending on the requirements. The nozzle can be moved in 
three axis, x, y, and z, while some bioprinters also allow for the movement of the print plate 
containing the wafer. The bioink is extruded 
from the syringe through pressure, which de-
pending on the print type comes in three forms 
pneumaƟc, screw, and piston. See ﬁgure 2.3.  
PneumaƟc prinƟng relies on creaƟng an over-
pressure, above the bioink, pushing the ink out. 
This prinƟng method operates well with the 
use of high viscous materials. 
The piston- and screw-prinƟng methods are 
both driven by mechanical extrusion, which can 
provide a more precise control of the bioinks 
ﬂow, have a low control over very viscous ma-
terials. [22] 
 
Bioinks undergoes changes in their ﬂow and 
deformaƟon, rheology, in response to the me-
chanical forces or deformaƟons. These properƟes are parƟcular important when studying 
polymers and how they respond to mechanical stress or strain. A brief understanding of 
basic material mechanism of viscoelasƟc materials is helpful in understanding the forces and 
changes a viscoelasƟc material undergoes during and aŌer the prinƟng process. Stress (𝜎) 
occurs when a force is applied to a material over a deﬁned area of the material. When the 
material undergoes stress, it can change its length or shape, this change relaƟve to the origi-
nal length or shape is referred to as strain (𝛾). If stress is applied to the material for a pro-
longed duraƟon, or if the stress changes over Ɵme, then the strain can change and is de-
noted as (𝛾̇), this change in stress and strain can arise from oscillatory stress, referring to pe-
riodic or altering applied stress, with the rate at which the stress varies over Ɵme, the

angular frequency, denoted as (𝜔). These changes all have an impact on the viscosity of the 
viscoelasƟc material 
Here we will invesƟgate the forces of shear stress, viscosity, yield stress, storage modulus, 
complex modulus, and loss factor. All physical changes a viscoelasƟc material undergoes 
during prinƟng, these forces are what makes 3D printed soŌ lithography possible. 
Shear stress (𝝉) occurs when pressure is applied on top of the bioink. This forces the bioink 
downwards, this results in a component of the force acƟng parallel to the surface per unit 
area, created by the syringe walls. Sheer thinning materials, like PDMS, experiences in-
creased deformaƟon due to the shear forces, resulƟng in it becoming less viscous and ﬂows 
more easily.  
Viscosity (𝜂) measures a bioinks resistance to ﬂow or deformaƟon, in other words it quanƟ-
ﬁes how easily a substance can ﬂow and how readily it deforms under the inﬂuence of an 
applied force. The viscosity of bioinks can be modiﬁed through diﬀerent methods, such as 
temperature control, polymer concentraƟon, molecular weight and crosslinking agent. A 
sought aŌer bioink exhibits a high viscosity when no forces are applied to it, while also hav-
ing the ability of shear thinning. This means that during the prinƟng of the bioink it will ﬂow 
out of the print nozzle and will facilitate shape retenƟon rapidly by increasing viscosity again.  
Yield stress (𝝈𝒚) describes the minimum amount of pressure that needs to be applied to 
the bioink before it will undergo deformaƟon or ﬂow. The yield stress of a bioink is im-
portant as if it exhibit a low yield stress then it will leak at low pressure, creaƟng unwanted 
deposiƟon on the wafer, while if the bioink exhibit a high yield stress it might become diﬃ-
cult to print.  
Storage modulus (𝐺′) represents the elasƟc behavior stored during deformaƟon of the ma-
terial when subjected to shear stress. AŌer the bioink is deposited on the wafer the bioink 
facilitates shape retenƟon by increasing viscosity. The fast increase in viscosity is known as 
thixotropy.  
Complex modulus combines storage modulus while the second is a measure of the energy 
dissipated aŌer storage, denoted (𝐺′′). When the storage modulus is larger than the energy 
dissipated, 𝐺ᇱ> 𝐺′′ the bioink will show a gel-like behavior, indicaƟng that the bioink have a 
predominantly solid-like behavior rather than a liquid-like behavior. While if the bioink ex-
hibits a lower storage modulus than loss modulus, 𝐺ᇱ< 𝐺′′. Then the bioink will show a liq-
uid- like behavior. If the storage and loss modulus are equal it indicates that the material is 
behaving in a perfectly balanced viscoelasƟc manner, this point is called the gelaƟon point.  
Loss factor is a dimensionless quanƟty represenƟng the raƟo of the loss modulus to the 
storage modulus in a viscoelasƟc material. The loss factor is used to predict the printability 
of the material. Bioinks with a loss factor greater than 1 result in no shape retenƟon, while a 
loss factor between 0.6 to 1 result in poor shape retenƟon, a loss factor between 0.4 and 
0.6 leads to good extrudability and shape retenƟon, while inks with a loss factor of less than 
0.4 we are not extrudable.

Before extruding the bioink the changeable parameters need to be considered, and how 
they aﬀect the bioinks printability. The printer used for this thesis allowed for the change of 
prinƟng pressure, prinƟng speed, nozzle size, crosslinking parameters, and thixotropic 
agents.  
PrinƟng pressure represents the air pressure the printer will inﬂict upon the bioink. This pa-
rameter is the only one that can be changed during the print, as it is changed on the printer 
itself. This parameter is important to opƟmize as to allow for a conƟnuous extrusion ﬂow. 
For extrusion to occur the prinƟng pressure needs to overcome the yield stress of the bioink, 
while a too high pressure or low pressure will result in too much extrusion, too liƩle or inter-
miƩent extrusion.  
PrinƟng speed represents how fast the nozzle moves in the x- and y- direcƟons over the wa-
fer. A balance between the extrusion rate and prinƟng speed is required, if the print speed is 
too slow, then the bioink will create thicker layers resulƟng in the ink deposiƟon not being 
uniform, or the ink ﬂow to undesired areas on the wafer. If the print speed is too fast it will 
result in thinner material deposiƟon or even a spoƩed print, where some areas are not cov-
ered by the bioink.  
Nozzle size represents the diameter of the opening of the extrusion nozzle. This is decided 
by the desired width of the printed structures. It is important to check the state of the noz-
zles before usage, since the openings are small (0,650 𝜇𝑚) then any fabricaƟon errors can 
block the extruding process or aﬀect the performance of the print. 
Cross linking is used to increase the strength of the ink by creaƟng new bonds between the 
polymer chains of the bioink. Diﬀerent crosslinkers work opƟmally during diﬀerent tempera-
tures, and selecƟng a crosslinker that works at lower temperatures will have an eﬀect on the 
extrusion process. The prinƟng pressure might therefore need to be increased slightly as the 
print Ɵme increases and more of the polymers are crosslinked in the bioink, increasing vis-
cosity. 
The method designed by Kajtez et al. 2020[6] consisted of two bioinks, a ﬂowing “gasket ink” 
and a “compartment ink”.  
The gasket ink was designed on a Sylgard 184 base, with the addiƟon of SE 1700 and fumed 
silica nanoparƟcles. The commercially available Sylgard 184 has a low viscosity and behaves 
as a Newtonian ﬂuid showing no shear thinning behavior, making it unsuitable for extrusion. 
SE 1700 is an elastomer that in itself is well suited for 3D prinƟng showing shear thinning 
behavior, with an elasƟc modulus higher than the loss modulus resulƟng in stable printed 
structures. Fumed silica nanoparƟcles are commonly used as thixotropic agents, to increase 
the viscosity of a ﬂuid. The parƟcles interact with each other forming hydrogen bonds increas-
ing the viscosity, with smaller parƟcle size having a larger impact on the viscosity due to their 
surface area relaƟve to their volume[23]. Here the nanoparƟcles were made of amorphous 
silicon dioxide with a diameter of 200-300nm. The mixed gasket ink showed shear thinning 
behavior, with a loss modulus higher than the storage modulus at rest, allowing it to ﬂow and 
cover the master mold aŌer being deposited.

2.1.4 BiocompaƟbility  
This study used two diﬀerent human neural stem cells, hNSC cell lines, one from the forebrain 
region, noted as fetal human neural stem cells, F-hNSC and one derived from the ventral mes-
encephalon in the midbrain, noted as M-hNSC. During the experimental phase it was noted 
that the PDMS had a necroƟc eﬀect on the cell lines, with F-hNSC experiencing a larger cell 
loss. Exploring the necrosis, both cell lines were diﬀerenƟated in XonaChips, with the same 
design. In this design the cells did not experience necrosis, indicaƟng that the loss of cells did 
not arrive from the media. A follow up study using media precondiƟoned in the PDMS designs 
was performed, when the precondiƟoned media was added, both cell lines started undergo-
ing necrosis, illustraƟng that the PDMS aﬀected the media and poisoned the cell lines.  
An increase in cell viability was aƩempted through solvent extracƟon and prolonged curing 
Ɵme. Analysis of the media showed the presence of PDMS oligomers when the curing Ɵme 
was less than 2 hours, with no oligomers aŌer a curing period of 24 hours. Solvent extracƟon 
resulted in weight reducƟon of ~5%, illustraƟng clearing of large amounts of unpolymerized 
oligomers. These aƩempts, however, did not result in a drasƟc cell survival improvement.

3.4.1Making the gasket ink  
The preparaƟon of the gasket ink started with the mixing of Sylgard 184 Silicone elastomer 
base with fumed silica nanoparƟcles for a concentraƟon of 15%. The silicone elastomer and

weight of the fumed silica nanoparƟcles, with diﬀerent revoluƟons per minute (rpm) and 
Ɵme depending on the amount of fumed silica nanoparƟcles. The ink is refrigerated be-

Ɵon. Viscous dissipaƟon is a result of the viscoelasƟc material undergoing deformaƟon, with 
its parƟcles experiencing moƟon. The moƟon encounters resistance due to the material’s in-
ternal viscosity and the resulƟng fricƟon leads to the generaƟon of heat.  
3.4.2 Preparing the inks 
The mixed gasket ink and the compartment ink, consisƟng of Se 1700 were added into small 
tubs in amounts of 3 g and 7 g respecƟvely. Curing agent is now added to the tubs at a raƟo

added a 3 CC syringe and a 5 CC syringe for the gasket ink and compartment ink respecƟvely 
and weighted again. 
Counterweights are created for the ink ﬁlled syringes based on their weight, and loaded into 
an Eppendorf centrifuge 5804 R, where the inks are degassed at a relaƟve centrifugal force 
(rcf) of 3000 for 3 minutes.  
Finally, a piston is added to the top of the degassed inks in the syringes, a nozzle is added to 
the syringe’s extracƟon point and the inks are loaded into the printer.

%% base circle 10 um 30 dist 1 mm inner 
%inputs for 6 cm inner diameter 
%liquid heights (in um) 
Onner_height = 9000; 
Inner_height = 1000; 
 
%channel dimentions (in um) 
Channel_height = 8; 
Channel_width = 10; 
Channel_length = 650; 
Channel_amount = 550; 
 
%Calculate volume for 0,1mm liquid in hollow cylinder 
%equation: V=Pi*height*(D^2*d^2)/4 
%D is diameter of outer cylinder wall, d is diameter of innter cylinder 
%wall. 
%multiply with 10^(-9) to get it to uL 
 
%insert dimensions of cylinder (um) 
D_outer = 13300; 
d_inner = 6000; 
d_channel_area_one_side = 650; 
 
 
%vector setup 
Outer_height_vec = Onner_height; 
Inner_height_vec = Inner_height; 
 
 
%Volume interval drop test liquid: v_test (outer height of liquid in um) 
v_test = 10; 
==PAGE:30==
 
29 
 
V_drop = 3.14*v_test*((D_outer)^(2)-(d_inner+(2*d_chan-
nel_area_one_side))^(2))/(4)*10^(-9); 
 
%relationship between volume of inner and outer ring for drop = 1:3.43 
v_drop_inner = 3.14*v_test*((d_inner)/2)^(2)*10^(-9); 
ratio = V_drop/v_drop_inner; 
 
 
while Outer_height_vec(end) > Inner_height_vec(end) 
    Onner_height = Onner_height-v_test; 
    Outer_height_vec =[Outer_height_vec,Onner_height]; 
 
    Inner_height = Inner_height+(v_test*ratio); 
    Inner_height_vec = [Inner_height_vec,Inner_height]; 
 
end 
 
%data ensurance that the inner volume dont overstep and becomes larger than 
%the outer volume 
 
neg_vec = Outer_height_vec - Inner_height_vec; 
neg_values = neg_vec(neg_vec < 0); 
amount_of_error = length(neg_values); 
 
%remove last datapoint for outer and inner height vec, since these can over-cross. 
Outer_height_vec = Outer_height_vec(1:end-amount_of_error); 
Inner_height_vec = Inner_height_vec(1:end-amount_of_error); 
 
 
%One channel flow for each flowstep 
Pressure_difference = 0.000001*9820000*(Outer_height_vec-Inner_height_vec); 
 
%flowrate for single channel 
Q = (Pressure_difference*(Channel_height)^(3)*Channel_width)/(12*1.002*Chan-
nel_length)*(1-0.630*(Channel_height/Channel_width)); 
 
%full system 
Q_full_uL_per_sec = Q*Channel_amount*10^-(9); 
 
%time 
Volume_vec = V_drop*(ones(size(Q_full_uL_per_sec))); 
 
 
time_vec_sec =Volume_vec ./ Q_full_uL_per_sec; 
%new time vector with total runtime 
time_vec_min = time_vec_sec./60; 
time_vec_hour = time_vec_min./60; 
time_vec_cum_1 = cumsum(time_vec_hour); 
 
Total_time_sec = sum(time_vec_sec); 
Total_time_min = Total_time_sec/60; 
Total_time_hour = Total_time_min/60; 
disp('full 6 cm channel time until equal height is in hours') 
disp(Total_time_hour)
