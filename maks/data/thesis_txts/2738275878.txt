Abstract
In order to mitigate the global environmental impact and dependence on fossil fuels within
maritime transport, renewable fuels need to be adopted. There exists numerous alterna­
tive fuel types, but the optimal choice of renewable fuel is complex and depends on several
factors. Especially e­ammonia, bio­methanol and bio­diesel are seen as promising can­
didates in this regard. With these three alternatives at hand, the purpose of this thesis is
to determine the most preferable renewable fuel pathway for dry bulk shipping purposes.
This is achieved through conducting a series of expert interviews with stakeholders from
different parts of the maritime industry. The insights and preferences gained in the in­
terviews are assessed using Multi­Criteria Decision Analysis, where considerations such
as environmental friendliness, economic viability, technical feasibility and regulatory sup­
port are included. The results reveal that bio­diesel emerges as the optimal pathway for
dry bulk shipping due to its technical maturity and cost­effectiveness. On the other hand,
e­ammonia is ranked lowest of the three, only performing well on the environmental as­
pects such as greenhouse gas emission reduction potential. Even though e­ammonia
is the least preferred option currently, it may become an important fuel pathway in the
future if challenges are solved. Consequently, bio­diesel presents a strong short­term
solution for decarbonisation, while strategies incorporating bio­methanol and e­ammonia
may prove more viable in the long term.

Introduction
Accounting for a total share of 80% of global trade by volume (UNCTAD, 2023), the ship­
ping sector is a vital part of today’s global economy. Furthermore, the sector is projected
to grow by approximately 2% between 2024 and 2028 (UNCTAD, 2023). This highlights
the lasting importance of shipping. However, with increasing trade comes a rise in total
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. With accordance to the United Nations Conference
on Trade and Development (UNCTAD), emissions from maritime transport have risen by
20% in the last decade (UNCTAD, 2023). The shipping sector currently emits around
800 million tonnes of CO2 annually, accounting for approximately 3% of global emissions
(UNCTAD, 2023). To minimise the maritime industry’s emissions and mitigate the impacts
of climate change, keeping within the 1.5◦C limit set by the Paris Agreement of the United
Nations (2015), decisive action is required (Getting to Zero Coalition, 2021a). The Inter­
national Maritime Organization (IMO) has already established several ambitious targets
for the maritime industry, including achieving net­zero emissions by 2050 and a 5% use
of near­zero emission fuels by 2030 (IMO, 2023).
With the urgent need for decarbonisation within maritime transport, there already exists a
number of measures that can be used to assist reaching these targets, such as onboard
efficiency, voyage optimisation, and slow­steaming (Foretich et al., 2021; H. Wang, Liu, et
al., 2023). However, while these measures focus on reducing the overall fuel consumption
(Solakivi et al., 2022), they do not directly address the uptake of so­called alternative fuels.
According to Cleveland and Morris (2015), these can be defined by their technical ability
to serve as substitutes to the dominant, mostly petroleum­based, fuel options. Within
maritime transport, the highest consumption is usually caused by heavy fuel oil (HFO)
(European Commission, 2023). A variety of fuel candidates were considered for further
research in the past, including liquefied natural gas (LNG), hydrogen, ammonia, methanol,
and bio­diesel (Andersson et al., 2020). To set a more specific scope, the focus of the
present thesis was set on alternative fuels produced through the application of renewable
energy. Referred to as renewable fuels, their core characteristic is the production from
either biomass, or hydro, wind, and solar energy (Harahap et al., 2023). Existing research
showed that both renewable and fossil­based alternative fuels have the ability to reduce
CO2 emissions (Issa et al., 2022; Law et al., 2021). However, the key advantage of
renewable fuels is the sustainability of their energy sources, which could potentially reduce
the dependency on fossil energy, thus preserving the natural supply (H. Wang, Liu, et al.,
2023).
To determine the most suitable renewable fuel, it is important to assess the respective
candidates in the specific context they would be applied in. There is not a universal fuel
choice that is applicable to all maritime use cases, since related challenges and opportuni­
ties are dependent on shipping routes, as well as cargo and vessel types (Carvalho et al.,
2021). The three major vessel types, dry bulk carriers, container ships, and oil tankers,
are responsible for approximately 80% of the total CO2 emitted (UNCTAD, 2023). Ac­
cording to UNCTAD (2023), dry bulk shipping represents the largest sector with respect
to a 42.8% share on the global fleet and a dominant role in maritime trade. Furthermore,
dry bulk shipping has not yet adopted measures to mitigate the emissions from vessels
on a large scale (Chen et al., 2023; Mærsk Mc­Kinney Møller Center, 2022). It therefore
became relevant to particularly target the dry bulk sector within recommendations for a
renewable fuel choice.

This thesis will provide decision support for selecting a renewable fuel exclusively within
dry bulk deep­sea shipping. Three renewable fuels were selected for the assessment:
e­ammonia, bio­methanol and bio­diesel. A consensus emerged from current research
that these can be considered to be promising candidates for international shipping (Al­
Aboosi et al., 2021; Carvalho et al., 2021; Hansson et al., 2020). In order to provide a fair
evaluation of the ”best” choice of a renewable fuel, different considerations must be made.
Potential decision­makers could not select an optimal fuel based on a single metric, such
as the ability to reduce emissions. The following research question therefore took into
account that the fuel selection is a complex concern, and that related recommendations
must be derived from several angles:
Which renewable fuel pathway represents the most environmentally promising concept
while ensuring economic viability, technical feasibility, and regulatory support?
This topic was researched extensively, especially in recent years, where various studies
intended to find the best alternative fuel for shipping (Machaj et al., 2022). However,
these rarely directed the revealed insights towards a specific shipping sector (Carvalho
et al., 2021). The same accounts for the fuels assessed, where names such as green,
alternative, sustainable are used interchangeably, as well as naming conventions for bio­
fuels, which makes it difficult to decipher what exact fuel is being reviewed (Andersson
et al., 2020).
The observations in this thesis will be examined based on a series of expert interviews with
maritime stakeholders from the industry and academia. These interviews were conducted
to form the data foundation for the subsequent Multi­Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA).
Concerning the current state of research and its associated challenges, the contributions
of this thesis are as follows:
1. Performing the first research of its kind to identify the most optimal renewable
fuel pathway for dry bulk shipping. This paper provides a new perspective on re­
newable fuels for maritime application, since the dry bulk sector is set as specific
use case.
2. Facilitating the adoption of renewable fuels through the application of MCDA
guided by insights obtained from expert interviews. The result will be a final ranking
of the three assessed renewable fuels, identifying the most optimal renewable fuel,
among the three, for dry bulk shipping purposes.
3. Applying regulatory considerations when addressing renewable fuels by incor­
porating these into the analysis, making sure that the most optimal fuel is also sup­
ported by existing or upcoming legislation.
4. Providing managerial implications for policymakers, stakeholders, and the dry
bulk industry for the selection of a renewable fuel by addressing environmental im­
pact, economic viability, technical feasibility, and regulatory support. With the in­
sights provided by high­level, experienced maritime stakeholders at hand, this paper
puts forth an aggregation of these perspectives. This will make it easier for maritime
stakeholders to navigate the renewable fuel choice in the future.
The report is structured as follows: Firstly, an overview of the current scientific research
on multi­criteria decision­making (MCDM) within marine fuel research, as well as related
renewable fuels and dry bulk shipping as a use case will be provided in a literature re­
view in Section 2. In Section 3, the methodology will be presented, which will cover the
methods and processes used in order to assess the fuels, as well as the chosen list of

Literature Review
The forthcoming section will display the insights derived from a review of relevant litera­
ture on renewable fuels in shipping. The examinations will commence with an overview
of previous studies applying the MCDA methodology within the research field. Special
emphasis will be placed on the different analytical perspectives covered in the reviewed
sources. Subsequently, the variety of renewable fuel options addressed within the litera­
ture will be presented. An introduction to the dry bulk shipping sector as selected use case
will complete the review. Emerging from the observations, a research gap will eventually
be identified, equipping the thesis with a high degree of relevance.
2.1
Multi­criteria decision­making in marine fuel research
A comprehensive review of existing journal articles on marine fuel selection revealed a
wide adoption of the MCDA methodology to compare a set of fuel options from various an­
gles (Foretich et al., 2021; Hansson et al., 2020; Hansson et al., 2019; Munim et al., 2023;
Ren & Liang, 2017). In that context, the respective studies compiled a diverse portfolio of
evaluation criteria and fuels to be assessed. To provide guidance, Andersson et al. (2020)
carried out an assessment of a selection of existing MCDA studies with the objective to
obtain an overview of the approaches employed. Accordingly, the most frequently applied
scope encompasses the environmental impact measured in emission values, the techni­
cal feasibility considering maturity, complexity, and maintenance, as well as the economic
viability defined by related costs. Furthermore, Andersson et al. (2020) cited additional
criteria addressing safety risks, onshore infrastructure, and fuel supply.
The present MCDA applications confirmed the high level of interest in environmental, eco­
nomic, and technical aspects when evaluating alternative fuel options in shipping. As a
showcase, Foretich et al. (2021) examined the challenges and opportunities of 12 different
alternative fuels in shipping applying 12 technical, three economic, and six environmental
criteria. A comparable approach was employed by Al­Aboosi et al. (2021) although the
authors reflected on a significantly smaller number of aspects across the three different
perspectives. To provide more detailed insights, the following subsections will elaborate
on how the three commonly utilised perspectives were implemented. In addition, appli­
cations of further aspects suitable for an MCDA will be addressed.
2.1.1
Environmental perspectives
Across the scientific sources, the core concern on climate change and the respective
emissions from maritime transport were observed to represent a key contributor to MCDA
studies (Deniz & Zincir, 2016; Foretich et al., 2021; Hansson et al., 2020; Hansson et al.,
2019; Munim et al., 2023). With reference to Hansson et al. (2019) and Foretich et al.
(2021), GHG, suplhur oxides (SOx), nitrogen oxides (NOx), as well as particulate matter
(PM) can be understood as the four main types of maritime emissions. GHG thereby rep­
resents the combined emissions from CO2, N2O, and CH4 (Lindstad et al., 2021). While
Foretich et al. (2021) proposed to examine the present emissions in separate criteria, al­
ternative methods, as applied by Karvounis et al. (2022), compiled one single shipping
emission criterion. Other scientific studies focused exclusively on GHG emissions, with
the remaining three types being disregarded (Laasma et al., 2022; Munim et al., 2023).
Nevertheless, there was a substantial consensus across the literature on reflecting the en­
tire fuel life cycle, from production to propulsion, for the emission values applied within the
analysis (Foretich et al., 2021; Hansson et al., 2019; Laasma et al., 2022). A marine fuel’s

representing the sum of well­to­tank (WTT) and tank­to­wake (TTW) or tank­to­propeller
(TTP) emissions, respectively (Hansson et al., 2019; Lindstad et al., 2021).
2.1.2
Economic perspectives
To investigate the viability of selected renewable fuels, the economic perspective of an
MCDA was usually characterised by financial considerations across the literature. An­
dersson et al. (2020) referred to the total cost of ownership (TCO) along a ship’s life
cycle within their recommended set of evaluation criteria. Existing analyses related to this
term usually intended to display the cost­competitiveness emerging from both the capi­
tal expenditures (CAPEX) and the operational expenditures (OPEX) (Martin et al., 2023).
Throughout the considered literature, evaluation criteria connected to the CAPEX were
either directed towards the initial investment on a vessel and propulsion system (Ren &
Liang, 2017) or the costs to retrofit an existing ship (Foretich et al., 2021). In contrast,
the OPEX were widely understood as the recurring expenses associated with the opera­
tion of a vessel (Laasma et al., 2022; Lindstad et al., 2021; Ren & Liang, 2017). These
included the salaries and training costs for the crew, insurance premiums, maintenance
efforts, as well as fuel costs (Hansson et al., 2019; Munim et al., 2023). The latter how­
ever divided the reviewed studies into two approaches since fuel expenses were either
seen as a component of the OPEX (Laasma et al., 2022) or considered as a separate
criterion (Foretich et al., 2021). Moreover, one group of researchers focused on the fuel
costs mainly resulting from production and raw material purchases (Hansson et al., 2020)
while the other examined the market price projections of the fuels (Munim et al., 2023).
2.1.3
Technical perspectives
Within the technical perspective, the MCDA studies most commonly screened the avail­
ability and compatibility of infrastructure required to operate a respective fuel. Andersson
et al. (2020), Hansson et al. (2020), and Munim et al. (2023) targeted the entire network,
including distribution, onshore storage, ports, and bunkering facilities. Deniz and Zincir
(2016) and Foretich et al. (2021) exclusively elaborated on the compatibility of existing
bunkering facilities with a respective fuel. Further technical aspects applied within MCDA
studies addressed the design of the engine units. On the one hand, Deniz and Zincir
(2016) and Foretich et al. (2021) determined the adaptability of existing engines with se­
lected fuels. The extent of modifications required to existing propulsion systems repre­
sented a central question in this context. On the other hand, Laasma et al. (2022) and
Munim et al. (2023) focused on the technical maturity of novel fuel technology, which ad­
ditionally encompassed the research and development (R&D) of new propulsion systems.
Connected to marine engine design, the energy efficiency along the fuel life cycle was in­
vestigated by a group of journal articles (Karvounis et al., 2022; Law et al., 2021; Lindstad
et al., 2021). Lindstad et al. (2021) defined the energy efficiency as the ratio of the energy
invested into the fuel production to the energy delivered at the propeller. Within the inter­
section of safety and storage, the energy density of the chemical substances defining the
fuels was repeatedly represented within the studies (Foretich et al., 2021; Karvounis et
al., 2022). As a decisive factor for the space required to store the fuel onboard (Carvalho
et al., 2021), the density was found to play a pivotal role for feasibility and convenience. In

2.2
Renewable fuel pathways
Upon reviewing the literature, a considerable number of studies was observed to cate­
gorise the fuels not only by their basic chemical substances, often referred to as energy
carriers, but also by their respective feedstocks, production processes, and propulsion
systems (Harahap et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2020; Xing et al., 2021a). The emerging sets of
alternative configurations were commonly referred to as fuel pathways (Law et al., 2021;
Mærsk Mc­Kinney Møller Center, 2021; Xing et al., 2021a).
The importance of understanding fuel options as pathways was underlined by Hansson et
al. (2019). Accordingly, although the fuel type may be the same, different related pathways
may lead to disparate scores within the same evaluation criterion when performing an
MCDA. To address these insights, the renewable fuel options found through the literature
review were understood as pathways defined by their energy carrier and feedstock for
the remainder of the thesis. The feedstock of each renewable fuel pathway was defined
based on two main groups: bio­fuels produced from biomass and e­fuels produced from
hydro, wind, or solar energy (Herdzik, 2023; Lindstad et al., 2021). It should be noted
that, to date, scientific research discovered four variants of biomass feedstock applicable
for fuel production (Naveed et al., 2023). This led to a classification of first, second,
third, and fourth generation bio­fuels, respectively. In that context, Alalwan et al. (2019)
examined that a competitive operation of bio­fuels may require the integration of some
or all generations. Given the operational focus of the present thesis, a differentiation
of the pathways based on specific production processes was consequently left aside.
Furthermore, the applicability of studies, as published by Foretich et al. (2021) and Munim
et al. (2023), was thereby preserved.

2.3
The use case of dry bulk shipping
The prevalent view among researchers was that the assessment of different fuel pathways
with a specific shipping use case in mind is of urgent importance (Carvalho et al., 2021;
Harahap et al., 2023; Z. Wang et al., 2024). This view emerged from observations that
not any renewable fuel pathway is suitable for any potential application (Carvalho et al.,
2021; Xing et al., 2021b). Throughout research on deep­sea shipping, container ships,
as well as tankers and dry bulk carriers were discovered to play a dominant role on the
global scale, considering the world fleet of vessels (UNCTAD, 2023). In consequence,
there were recurring references to the related shipping sectors within the scientific case
studies (H. Wang, Daoutidis, et al., 2023; Z. Wang et al., 2024; Zou & Yang, 2023).
Despite the ongoing transition of container shipping towards a variety of alternative fuels,
industrial ship owners of tankers and dry bulk carriers were however observed to maintain
a tendency towards conventional fuel pathways (Chen et al., 2023). It appeared critical
to consider the implications of this trend for dry bulk shipping. First and foremost, accord­
ing to UNCTAD (2023), dry bulk shipping represents a key contributor to the total CO2
emissions from maritime transport. Accounting for a 47% share in that regard, dry bulk
was eventually observed to be the most emitting shipping sector in 2022 (Stathatou et al.,
2022). Furthermore, the dry bulk sector incorporates the largest share of the global mar­
itime trade, primarily transporting minor bulk and raw materials, such as coal, iron ore, and
grain (UNCTAD, 2023). The iron ore trade from Australia to Japan and China, as well as
from Brazil to China, thereby encompasses both the most relevant shipping routes and a
key corridor for the decarbonisation of the sector (Getting to Zero Coalition, 2021b). The
mining of iron ore, as well as that of coal, can be linked to environmentally harmful indus­
trial sectors, such as fossil energy and steel production (Pangalos, 2023). Addressing dry
bulk shipping within decision support on fuel transition may therefore initiate a significant
revision of the sustainability profile associated with the sector.
2.4
Identified research gap
Along the literature review, the considered MCDA studies and additional marine fuel
research revealed a number of limitations.
Through the investigation of previous ap­
proaches to apply an MCDA for marine fuels, it remained uncertain whether defining the
scope through environmental, economic, and technical evaluation criteria would ultimately
prove sufficient. As stressed by Andersson et al. (2020), rare inclusions of policy and reg­
ulatory aspects caused a high demand for scientific research on these perspectives. The
examination of social aspects, such as the public acceptance (Munim et al., 2023; Ren &
Liang, 2017), was further discovered as widely unpopular across the literature.
Reviewing the present sources on the applied fuel alternatives, it became evident that a
variety of concepts were employed to define the respective fuel types under consideration.
While the classification as pathways appeared as recommended concept, rather broad
analytical approaches regarded the energy carrier as a sole distinguishing characteristic
(Foretich et al., 2021; Karvounis et al., 2022; Munim et al., 2023). As a consequence, the
subsequent examinations widely treated fuel pathways from renewable sources and their
equivalents from fossil production as a single, group alternative. The inclusion of both
renewable, as well as fossil­based alternative fuels into the analysis was found to be a
reoccurring practice across the considered literature (Hansson et al., 2020; Hansson et
al., 2019). Consequently, these sources missed out to particularly focus on fuel pathways
that have the potential to mitigate the dependency on fossil energy (H. Wang, Liu, et al.,
2023).

3
Methodology
The determination of the optimal renewable fuel pathway for dry bulk shipping represents
a complex issue, which is influenced by various sub­evaluations. The presented solution
may further depend heavily on the stakeholders asked, as they may have different pri­
orities on the criteria considered. Some may find that the price and competitiveness to
be the most decisive factor, whereas others may argue that the environmental aspects,
such as CO2 emissions, play the most crucial role. With this in mind, it became evident
that a thorough and multi­dimensional methodology is required to give a comprehensive
decision support on the selection of a renewable fuel. The following examinations will
introduce the MCDA as suitable approach, as well as the multiplicative version of the An­
alytical Hierarchy Process (AHP), also known as the Ratio Estimation in Magnitudes or
deci­Bells to Rate Alternatives which are Non­DominaTed (REMBRANDT), invented by
Lootsma (1992) as the selected MCDA variation. Furthermore, a set of evaluation criteria
required for the MCDA will be derived from the literature review. Eventually, the data col­
lection approach, applying a series of interviews with industry experts, will be presented.
3.1
Multi­Criteria Decision Analysis
Upon reviewing appropriate methodologies, the MCDA was identified as a powerful tool
that allows complex decision problems to be systematically broken down into more man­
ageable sub­decisions (Eklund et al., 2024). Accordingly, the fundamental idea of an
MCDA enables decision­makers to compare different alternatives based on a set of eval­
uation criteria. In the context of this thesis, the different renewable fuel pathways, which
were selected through the literature review, are to be evaluated by a set of related crite­
ria. Each alternative is given a related score based on its performance in each criterion.
Additionally, each evaluation criterion is assigned with a weight, signifying the relative
importance of the criterion. In that sense, the MCDA represents a subjective approach
that assists the decision­maker navigate their own priorities in a complex field (Barfod
& Leleur, 2014). Both scores and weights are consolidated into a final ranking of the
alternatives (Andersson et al., 2020).
Amongst others, a major advantage of the MCDA is the ability to compare criteria that
compete in objectives or that are difficult to compare (Barfod & Leleur, 2014; Eklund et
al., 2024). When solving complex questions, such as a renewable fuel choice, both quan­
titative and qualitative insights can therefore be considered within an MCDA (Alvarez et
al., 2021). While the assessment of quantitative factors is commonly straightforward,
qualitative factors are more difficult to compare, as they are expressed verbally (Lazar
et al., 2017). Comparing both types of data to each other further complicates the assess­
ment (Eklund et al., 2024). The MCDA approach enables the integration and subsequent
comparison of both data types (Barfod & Leleur, 2014).
3.1.1
Analytical Hierarchy Process and REMBRANDT
The MCDA theory proposes several variations of the core methodology to be applicable
(Wątróbski et al., 2019). For this thesis, the AHP, more specifically REMBRANDT as its
multiplicative version, was considered. Both the traditional AHP and the REMBRANDT
approach employ pairwise comparisons between the alternatives (Broniewicz & Ogrodnik,
2021). Instead of facing the challenge to rank every single alternative for each criterion
separately, decision­makers are enabled to state their relative preference between two
10

==PAGE:23==
alternatives at a time (Hansson et al., 2019). A fundamental reason to consider REM­
BRANDT is that the traditional AHP does not address potential rank­reversal (Barfod &
Leleur, 2014). This means that the final ranking of the established alternatives could be
reversed if another alternative was added to the analysis later in the process. Since REM­
BRANDT applies a logarithmic regression, potential rank­reversal is prevented. Prior to
the data collection, this was considered a relevant factor for the case that the three se­
lected renewable fuel pathways may have turned out to be inconvenient.
The preference scale of REMBRANDT, as well as its multiplicative nature, was origi­
nally created to overcome criticism regarding the traditional AHP (Barfod & Leleur, 2013).

present thesis, a score of 0 may additionally imply insufficient knowledge of the inter­
viewed decision­maker to judge the fuel on the specific topic. The numbers in between
scores (1,3,5,7) may be used if a pairwise comparison is located between two preference

3.2.4
Structure of the interviews
In the present thesis, a total set of three renewable fuel pathways and a selection of 12
evaluation criteria were applied to build the foundation of the MCDA. The chosen REM­
BRANDT approach thereby required a 3x3­matrix of pairwise comparisons for each crite­
rion. Assuming those pairs, which compared the same fuel pathway to itself, to be 0, the
number of preferences to be made by each expert amounted to six. Considering the total
number of criteria, this would have resulted in 108 different comparisons to be discussed
per interview. However, REMBRANDT has redundancy built in, since a single compar­
ison is reflected twice in a symmetric matrix. Logically, each expert was only asked to
provide three judgments per criterion. In the case of one alternative to be ranked with an
8 over another, the inverse comparison was intuitively assigned with a value of ­8. As a
result, the total number of preferences to be assigned by each expert was reduced to 36.
Prior to the interviews, the experts were asked to determine the criteria weights through
the completion of an online survey in Google Forms (See Appendix E). For each criterion,
a brief description and a scale from 1 to 5 was given. In addition to the purpose of data
collection, the survey enabled the experts to prepare for the subjects to be discussed
during the interviews.
In the next step, the 17 expert interviews were scheduled to collect the data for the sub­
sequent MCDA. The interviews were conducted through the online meeting platform Mi­
crosoft Teams and lasted between 45 minutes and 1.5 hours. The interviews took place
between April and July 2024. For each criterion, the experts were first provided with rel­
evant introductory data and insights derived from the literature review. To display the in­

criterion (See example in Figure 3.6). After reviewing each slide, the experts were re­

Results and Discussion
After conducting the interview series, the insights and preferences provided by the experts
were analysed to compile a final result of the MCDA. The assigned scores were aggre­
gated into both a ranking of the selected renewable fuel pathways over all 17 experts and
partial results for each of the six expert groups. The forthcoming examinations will first
present the overall ranking before scoping in to each specific expert group. To finally verify

results will elaborate on the following quantitative outcomes, accompanied by qualitative

• Combined weight distributions of the evaluation criteria
• Ranking of renewable fuel pathways by pillar
• Total ranking of renewable fuel pathways
It should be noted that the compiled rankings represent an average of specific prefer­

pairwise comparison can be found categorised by expert group in Appendices F to K.
The final aggregation and calculation of the results can be found in Appendix L.
4.1
Overall ranking
Considering all experts, the weights assigned to each criterion revealed a high level of
relevance across the covered aspects, with no weight scoring below 3.5 (See Figure 4.1).
From an environmental perspective, the experts believed the WTW GHG emissions (C1)

were determined as the economically most decisive factor. The safety of storage and op­

criterion overall in the evaluation of the experts. Eventually, the existing and upcoming
safety guidelines (C11) were stated to be the most crucial regulatory field.

5
Managerial Implications
The present thesis explored the adoption of renewable fuels for dry bulk shipping pur­
poses. The applied MCDA revealed both opportunities and challenges associated with
the transition from conventional to renewable fuels, as well as important insights for man­
agers, policymakers and other maritime stakeholders. The final results imply that the
optimal renewable fuel pathway is represented by bio­diesel, given the present setup of
the MCDA methodology. However, both the bio­methanol and the e­ammonia pathway
were stated to induce respective advantages over bio­diesel, especially in the long term.
Based on the detailed findings presented in Section 4, the following managerial implica­
tions were derived:
• In the short term, bio­diesel should be applied to initiate the fuel transition of dry
bulk carriers. As highlighted by the experts, the key advantages of bio­diesel include
the current affordability and technical maturity compared to the e­ammonia and bio­
methanol pathways. This is reflected in the overall MCDA rankings, where bio­diesel
scored highest on the economic and technical aspects. Given the drop­in relation
to its fossil equivalent, the implementation of bio­diesel may prevent high invest­
ment costs and complex modifications of the technology. Through these insights,
the interviewed experts classified bio­diesel as an interim solution that can reduce
emissions until other fuel pathways with higher environmental potential achieve suf­
ficient maturity. This became evident by e­ammonia and bio­methanol outperform­
ing bio­diesel in the environmental criteria. Furthermore, many experts addressed
their concern regarding the longevity and sustainability of biomass as feedstock,
highlighting the possible competition with global food production.
• E­ammonia should be considered in long­term strategies for the decarbonisa­
tion of dry bulk shipping. As seen throughout the MCDA results, the e­ammonia
pathway was assigned with the highest environmental potential by the experts. On
the contrary, a variety of existing challenges became evident, which restricted the
short­term potential of the pathway. From a technical perspective, the lower ma­
turity of compatible engine systems and missing adaptability of existing bunkering
infrastructure were seen critical throughout the interviews. The experts also referred
to the toxicity of e­ammonia as a key obstacle to be overcome ahead of the wide
implementation. However, a majority believed that these drawbacks in comparison
to bio­diesel will be addressed by future technical and regulatory developments. As
a result, the e­ammonia pathway would become competitive and decision­makers
could benefit from the environmental potential in the long term.
• Bio­methanol can be considered as a resilient alternative for the long run,
given the fact that the implementation of e­ammonia still faces a number of chal­
lenges. Various insights provided by the experts implied that methanol as a marine
fuel is already in operation and is well established within hazard handling and safety
practices. Its chemical characteristics revealed additional advantages based on the
low spillage consequences and the combustible state at ambient circumstances. As
appended by an expert from the group of engineering companies, the coexistence
of biomass­based and electricity­based methanol may address potential issues on
the scarcity of the feedstocks. With these insights and the overall MCDA results at
hand, bio­methanol may achieve technical and economic competitiveness towards
bio­diesel sooner than e­ammonia while proposing a sound environmental profile.

• No single renewable fuel should be considered exclusively to drive the decar­

cific potentials and challenges. The experts further stated that fuel transition strate­
gies are required to contain both short­term and long­term measures with diverse
fuel pathway applications. Amongst others, this was observed through discussions
on the compliance of the fuels with the IMO emission targets (C10). In the specific
context of the net­zero emissions target by 2050, the experts repeatedly expressed
indifference when asked for the most contributing fuel pathway. It thereby became
evident that all presented fuel pathways should be considered as described in the
aforementioned implications.
• Policymakers should include renewable fuels in regulations to drive the uptake in
the dry bulk industry to a larger extent. As implied by the weights given by the ex­
perts interviewed, regulatory support and inclusion are crucial factors for selecting
renewable fuels. Therefore, policies targeted towards incentivising renewable fu­
els should be implemented in order to drive the innovation in the field, with efforts
such as the FuelEU Maritime as inspiration. Safety measures and guidance should
also be developed to foster the establishment of renewable fuel types, such as e­
ammonia.
• Ports and fuel traders should facilitate the transition from current fossil­based
propulsion to the renewable counterpart by providing the required fuel supply and
infrastructure. This is reflected in the overall weight distribution of the evaluation
criteria, which verifies the importance of fuel supply (C6) and bunkering infrastruc­
ture (C9). Both the availability and accessibility of a fuel are subject to a problem of
cause and effect. On the one hand, ship operators will avoid adopting a fuel which
is not easily available for bunkering in the ports along their shipping routes. On the
other hand, port operators will not have an incentive to provide this infrastructure
before there is a market and customers for it. Especially since dry bulk ships of­
ten transport goods between smaller, remote ports, it is of high importance that the
ports and the fuel traders jointly improve the reliable supply of renewable fuels in
that regard.
• Researchers and engineering companies should continue to initiate R&D efforts
on renewable fuel pathways, especially on e­ammonia and bio­methanol. This is
due to the fact that these carry the highest potential to reduce maritime GHG emis­
sions, as identified in the results. Specific areas of interest are the technological
challenges of e­ammonia, where issues such as safety in storage and operations,
engine maturity and adaptability to existing infrastructure require suitable innova­
tions.
• Dry bulk ship operators should put emphasis on fuel transition and decarbonisa­
tion strategies. While it is certain that upcoming regulations will play pivotal role,
the ship operators should make use of the short­term advantages connected to bio­
diesel. In the long term, the dry bulk sector should strive for a comprehensive transi­
tion towards renewable fuels with more promising environmental profiles. To initiate
these developments today, dry bulk operators should further strive to collaborate
with the policymakers, organisations, and research institutions on the path towards
the widespread inclusion of renewable fuels in policies and frameworks.

Conclusion
The present thesis was dedicated to give comprehensive decision support for the selection
of a renewable fuel pathway within dry bulk shipping. The central research question asked
to identify the renewable fuel pathway that represents the most environmentally promising
concept while ensuring economic viability, technical feasibility, and regulatory support. By
applying the MCDA methodology, three candidates were examined against 12 evaluation
criteria throughout a series of 17 interviews with experts in the field. It can be concluded
that bio­diesel represents the most preferred pathway in the short term while both bio­
methanol and e­ammonia should be prioritised in the long term. Given the current higher
certainty of hazards and operational practices with respect to methanol as a marine fuel,
bio­methanol represents the prioritised solution over e­ammonia. It eventually became
evident that an integrated approach considering a selection of renewable fuels is essential
to strive for decarbonisation while facilitating the requirements of the dry bulk shipping
sector.
The review of existing literature was conducted with the purpose of developing an MCDA
that addresses a relevant knowledge gap in research. Initially, previous MCDA studies
within marine fuel research discovered common practices and limitations with regard to
the selected evaluation criteria. This led to the scope of the thesis being defined not only
by widely adopted dimensions, introducing environmental, economic, and technical crite­
ria, but also by the relatively underrepresented regulatory aspects. Furthermore, popular
renewable fuel pathways in shipping were derived from the examined MCDA studies, as
well as specific marine fuel performance analyses. Given the entire landscape of renew­
able fuels, e­ammonia, bio­methanol, and bio­diesel were selected as most promising
candidates. The MCDA was subsequently provided with a knowledge basis for each of
the three pathways. Closing the review, references to shipping sectors within the exam­
ined MCDA studies were researched to understand the degree and value of specificity
in research on renewable marine fuels. As the urgency for the definition of use cases
became apparent, dry bulk shipping was identified as a sector in need of further action in
the transition towards renewable fuels, while representing the largest share in maritime
trade.
With the inputs to the MCDA defined, the methodology was further specified in the follow­
ing step. From the portfolio of existing MCDA approaches, REMBRANDT was selected
as a variation of the AHP. Following, the expert interviews were planned and conducted to
collect the data needed for the MCDA. Based on the literature review, it was expected that
e­ammonia would represent the environmentally most promising pathway while bio­diesel
would benefit from its technical maturity and economic viability. Bio­methanol would be
described as an established pathway that benefits from its operational safety and conve­
nience. The interviews with experts from industry associations and organisations, engi­
neering companies, ports and maritime authorities, as well as research institutions widely
confirmed these impressions. As a resilient bridging solution, bio­diesel can introduce the
long­term transition towards renewable fuels with a higher sustainability potential, such
as bio­methanol and e­ammonia. In contrast to the overall result, the experts employed
for ship operators and fuel traders placed a higher emphasis on the e­ammonia pathway.
Given the first incentives on RFNBOs, such as the FuelEU Maritime initiative, both groups
expect e­ammonia to receive substantial regulatory support in the near future.

Throughout the data collection and analysis, it became evident that further research could
build upon the limitations of this thesis by adjusting the MCDA methodology with respect
to the scope, the fuel pathways, or the shipping use case. In that context, two evalua­
tion criteria were mentioned within the thesis but not further applied. The OPEX as an
economic counterpart to the CAPEX could provide insights on ongoing vessel costs other
than fuel expenses. Furthermore, environmental impacts, such as the spill risk, could
reveal challenges of specific renewable fuel pathways apart from emissions. Referring to
additional fuel pathway candidates, e­methanol was frequently mentioned as e­fuel alter­
native to bio­methanol within the expert interviews. The e­hydrogen pathway may also be
a viable candidate to consider, given a suitable use case, such as short­sea shipping. Be­
sides short­sea shipping, tankers and further efforts on dry bulk carriers could be subject
of future research, since the literature review underlined the importance of case­oriented
decision support within critical shipping sectors. Eventually, MCDA studies, which dif­
ferentiate pathways based on production processes or engine applications, could further
examine specific components of the fuel life cycle. Future researchers should note to
align the company types considered for the expert interviews with the selected scope and
use case.
To conclude, the present thesis sheds new light on the research field of marine fuel transi­
tion, formulating a set of renewable fuel choice recommendations tailored to the dry bulk
shipping industry. Since derived from actual preferences expressed by diverse experts
of the maritime industry, the managerial implications of these recommendations do not
only represent a high degree of relevance but also wide verification. Going beyond an
exclusive decision support for maritime­based companies, the implications encompass
multiple dimensions and present further actions required from policy­making, as well as
R&D stakeholders. The coverage of regulatory insights, as well as the focus on renew­
able fuel pathways within a specific sector thereby address a considerable knowledge gap
within the current landscape of related research. Any parties related to dry bulk shipping
are now equipped with a selected compilation of the measures required for the transition
from conventional to renewable fuel pathways, both in the short and long term.

Inquiry for an expert interview on renewable fuels in shipping 
 
Dear [Recipient], 
 
I hereby would like to request an expert interview with you on the establishment of alternative fuels 
produced with renewable energy in maritime transport. The insights obtained from this, and other expert 
interviews will form the foundation of a master's thesis co-written by myself and my fellow student [Name], 
supervised by Professor Jasmine Lam from the Maritime Department at the Technical University of Denmark 
(DTU). 
 
The maritime industry is increasingly pursuing sustainable development due to concerns on environmental 
pollution and resource preservation. The demand for addressing global warming has triggered the wave 
of energy transition to use more sustainable types of energy to lower the reliance on fossil fuels. Alternative 
fuels from renewable energy sources, such as green methanol, green hydrogen, or biofuels, are promising 
options to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and pollutants. However, the adoption of renewable energy 
in the production of shipping fuels requires consideration of numerous parameters. Inside the maritime 
industry, there is a strong demand for developing new and effective solutions to achieve eco-friendly 
operations and enhance productivity, as well as growth simultaneously. Our thesis project will analyse 
green fuel options applicable for shipping and contribute to the development of sustainable solutions in a 
selected focus area. Given the complexity and significance of this topic, your expertise and insights would 
be pivotal in shaping the outcome of this study and laying the foundation for sustainable practices within 
the shipping sector. 
 
Objective 
The main aim of this interview is to use a multi-criteria decision-making methodology (MCDA) to evaluate 
and rank various alternative green fuels for their suitability and feasibility in the specific context of dry bulk 
shipping. We will consider a set of predetermined criteria to identify the most promising green fuel options 
that align with the industry's sustainability goals while meeting the unique operational requirements of dry 
bulk vessels. 
 
Agenda 
1. If you agree to participate, we will kindly ask you to complete a brief online survey prior to the 
interview. 
2. During the interview, we would finally discuss these criteria while analysing and evaluating our 
chosen green fuel options in the context of dry bulk shipping. 
 
Your participation in this interview session would be crucial to the success of this study. Your expert insights 
and contributions would not only enrich the discourse surrounding sustainable maritime practices but also 
serve as a valuable contribution to our thesis project. I am looking forward to hearing from you. If you have 
any questions or need any further information, please do not hesitate to get in touch with me.
