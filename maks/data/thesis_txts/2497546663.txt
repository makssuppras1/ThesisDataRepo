Abstract
A series of bi-concave lenses were fabricated of single crystalline diamond using
laser ablation and ICP-RIE. The lenses were characterised using both confocal
microscopy and wavefront techniques performed at local DTU facilities and the
APS synchrotron in Chicago, respectively. The surface RMS of a single bi-concave
lens with a 300 µm radius of curvature was reduced to 0.9 µm, via a combination of
simulations and optimisation of the fabrication parameters. A new simulation tool
for McXtrace has been developed, capable of simulating the performance of
fabricated lenses. Future improvements to both the fabrication scheme and the
simulation tool promise CRL made of single-crystalline diamond to be used as
standard components in the next-generation synchrotrons.

Introduction
X-rays are the primary probe in a wide range of characterisation techniques used in many disci-
plines, such as material sciences, medical sciences, and environmental sciences [1]. Large scales
facilities, so-called synchrotrons, have been established around the world to generate extremely
bright x-ray sources like the one in ﬁgure 1. Speciﬁc applications required beam dimension in the
sub-micrometer to the nanometer range, for which optical devices for focusing x-rays are used
[1]. Devices of x-ray optics are separated into three categories focusing x-ray using reﬂection,
refraction, or diﬀraction properties [2]. Examples of such devices are compound refractive lenses
(CRL), Fresnel zone plates (FZP), and Kirkpatrick-Baez mirrors (KB mirrors), respectively.
A recent development in synchrotron facilities requires the development of new focus devices
[3, 4]. Many synchrotron facilities around the world are either undergoing an upgrade or plan-
ning to upgrade into the fourth-generation diﬀraction-limited storage rings, becoming ultrabril-
liant synchrotrons sources [1]. The x-ray beam at the new synchrotron facilities will have a
high average beam power and coherence, increasing the requirement for the optical components
of handling such high beam power, heat-loads, and preservation of the beam coherence [3, 4].
Especially the increased beam coherence creates challenges for the currently available focusing
optics.

Wilhelm R¨ontgen discovered x-rays in 1895, but it was not until mid-1990s that the ﬁrst device
utilising refraction for focus x-rays was developed [5, 1, 2, 6, 3, 4]. The refractive index n turns
out to be slightly smaller than unity (n ≈1 −10−5) for materials in the x-ray range, making
the distance for focusing several tens of meters for a single lens [5, 1, 2, 7]. In combination
with strong absorption, it was infeasible to make lenses for focusing of x-ray, before it became
possible to fabricate sub-millimeter features in low x-ray absorbing materials. This challenge
was overcome in the early 1990s, and in 1996 Snigirev et. al. demonstrated the focusing ability
of a compound refractive lens [5]. This CRL had been fabricated by drilling a linear array of
∼0.3 mm holes in an aluminium alloy as seen in top image of ﬁgure 2 [5]. These holes work
as double-sided lenses, and the CRL stack achieves a shortened focus length by combining the
refractive power of each lenslet, as shown in ﬁgure 2 [5].
Some of the advantages of CRLs
compared to other x-ray focusing devices, such as KB mirrors and Fresnel zone plates are the
ability of preserving the x-ray beam trajectory, operating well for hard x-rays (Rph ≥15 keV),
capable of handling high heat loads, mechanical robustness, and the CRL stacks being compact
[2, 6, 3, 4]. These properties are greatly valued on large scale x-ray facilities such as synchrotron
and X-ray Free-Electron Lasers (XFELs), where CRLs have become a standard tool for focusing

x-rays used on many beamlines [2, 6, 3]. The spacial resolution of the CRLs used at synchrotron
beamlines today is typically limited by manufacturing to around 50 nm-100 nm[1, 2, 6].
The initial hole design of CRLs has mainly been abandoned to avoid spherical aberrations.
Modern lenses are shaped like 1D or 2D parabolas, which do not have spherical aberrations and
closely resembles the ideal elliptical shape.

Common materials used for making CRLs are beryllium (Be), aluminium (Al), nickel (Ni), poly-
mers, and silicon (Si). The most commonly used material is Be due to its low absorption and
relative good refractive index [5, 2, 3, 4]. However, Be has some downsides. Besides Be being
toxic, it requires powerful cooling to avoid degeneration when exposed to extreme photon ﬂux,
and it is a grainy poly-crystalline material [3, 4]. The grainy structures cause diﬀuse scattering
from grain boundaries, voids, inclusions, and other scattering centers, leading to a reduces pho-
ton count in the focus spot, and it generates undesired speckles [3]. An immediate improvement
is to minimise the scattering from intrinsic inhomogeneities via choosing a material with a low
small-angle scattering, suggesting a material with large grains ideally a single crystalline material
[3]. One such material is single crystalline diamond (C*), which has a series of advantages such
as not being a grainy material, being capable of handling high heat-loads due to high thermal
conductivity, and having a small thermal expansion coeﬃcient [3, 4]. While, at the same time
still having a reasonable low absorption of x-rays (slightly higher than Be), and a refractive
index greater than that of Be (|nBe| < |nC∗|) [3, 4]. At next-generation synchrotrons, coherent
experiments are becoming a more common tool, and for this reason, the focusing devices must
be able to preserve the beam coherence, calling for lenses of single crystalline materials such as
diamond [3].
Fabricated lenses will deviate from the desired proﬁle due to imperfections in the fabrication
process. The deviations in the proﬁle give rise to surface errors, which can inﬂuence the focusing
ability of the lens device. By considering the surface error as noise to the ideal proﬁle, the errors
can be divided into two categories for high and low frequency noise, called the surface waviness
and surface roughness. Due to the limitations in characterisation techniques the surface rough-
ness can rarely be determined due to the Nyquist theorem. However, the surface waviness can
still be extracted from measurement and used to analyse the achieved proﬁles quantitatively.

==PAGE:6==
Deviation in the surface proﬁle is an issue for any focusing device. For CRLs, the Mar´echal
criterion suggests that the Root-Mean-Square (RMS) of the surface error should be less than
386.4 nm for a CRL stack of 10 lenses in order for the stack to preserve the beam coherence at
Eph = 10.00 keV (λ = 12.36 nm).
These properties of single-crystalline diamond have already been utilised in a series of syn-
chrotron devices such as high-heat-load monochromators, Bragg mirrors, and even some XFEL
applications e.g. beam-multiplexing monochromators [3, 4]. Single crystal diamond CRLs has
already be fabricated by others, e.g. as 1D parabolic cylindrical lenses [3] and 2D parabolic
lenses [4].Both cases report the RMS of the surface error to be < 1 µm, but when characterised
at a synchrotron facility the dimension of the focus spot is revealed to be above the theoreti-
cal expectation [3, 4]. In the case for the 2D lens an undulator source with beam dimensions
652 µm×27 µm was expected to focus the beam into a 37.3 µm×1.55 µm spot, but the spot was
instead 52.6 µm × 21.4 µm in size [4]. The discrepancy was argued to have multiple causes: The
lens proﬁles deviating from the ideally desired shapes, surface roughness, and stacking misalign-
ment of the lenses [4]. Similar issues have been found by Paw Kristiansen et al. (supervisor)
in earlier attempts to make such single-crystal diamond CRLs (JJ Xray internal report). A
synchrotron characterisation in December 2018 of a set of lenses revealed a magniﬁcation of 2.1
times the theoretical limit, and the deviation between the shape of the lens stack and the ideal
shape forms a torus-like shape at the center of the lens.
All lenses made of single crystalline diamond discussed above have been fabricated using laser
ablation, which is a technique where a high power laser is shot in short pulses onto a surface
to remove surface material. The ablation technique will be explained in greater detail later in
section 4.2. To remove material using laser ablation is fairly easy, but to remove the material
with a micrometer resolution is much more diﬃcult and a topic of research, [3, 4]. Fabricated
lenses are usually tested by some form of synchrotron experiment, optical characterisation, or
electron microscopy. The two latter mentioned methods are able to describe the topography of
such lenses, but only the synchrotron experiments can give the focus spot sizes, magniﬁcation,
and coherence properties of a CRL.
1.1
Objectives of the thesis
The goal of the project is to further development of CRL made of single crystalline diamond
via laser ablation techniques.
To reach this goal the microfabrication technique of must be
developed further, requiring an deeper understanding the process parameters. The fabrication
scheme will be improved by analysing the process ﬂow for feature causing artefacts to appear
in the lens proﬁles, and to optimise the fabrication parameters to reduce the RMS value of the
overall surface error.
Ray-tracing simulations will be utilised to predict the optical performance of the fabricated
CRL [8]. A new tool has to be developed to perform such simulations. The developed tool is
to reproduce and help understand results from the synchrotron experiment. The synchrotron
experiment will be performed at the Advanced-Photon-Source (APS) at Argonne National Lab-
oratory in Chicago USA towards the end of the project (late November 2019).

2.1.1
Synchrotron radiation
In the middle of the 19-hundreds, high energy physicists discovered the emission of x-rays from
bending magnetic in circular particle accelerators, this x-ray radiation was called synchrotron
radiation [9, 11]. Initially, synchrotron radiation was an undesired side eﬀect, but later scientists
realised the potential for utilising synchrotron radiation as a powerful x-ray source [11].
Synchrotron radiation is generated by forcing charged particles, usually electrons, to follow
an circular path at relativistic speeds. The electrons are kept in their circular path by strong
external magnetic ﬁelds. The synchrotron radiation emitted from bending the electron path
via magnets is called bending magnet radiation. Other commonly used insertion devices are
Undulators, and Wigglers made to reduce the so-called emittance, which is the product of the
beam size and its divergence [9].

2.2.3
Materials for refractive optics
The eﬀect of the refractive index being small and close to unity causes the refraction and thereby
the optical performance of a single interface to be small. For this reason, a single lens will re-
quire tens of meters to focus the beam, which will heavily decrease the beam intensity due to
attenuation. This issue is overcome in CRLs by stacking a series of lenses after one another.
By increasing the number of lenses, the total path trough the lens material increases, thereby
increasing the amount of absorption as follows from Lambert-Beer law (eq. 2). This set require-
ments for which materials are suitable for focusing x-ray using refraction. The material needs
to have a high refractive power (large δ) and low absorption (small β or µ 1). This is typically

2.4
Simulations using ray tracing
This project also aims to utilise simulation for estimating the performance of the fabricated lens.
For this purpose ray-tracing simulations will be used, which is a type of Monte Carlo simulations
being a relatively fast method compared to wave propagation [8].
A range of tools already exists to perform ray-tracing simulations of x-ray devices such as McX-
trace, Phase, and SHADOW [8]. The goal in this project is to measure a lens surface, using
e.g. optical microscopes, and then inserting the surface proﬁle directly into the simulation tool
to estimate the performance of the given lenslet. Each simulation tool has its advantages, yet
currently, none of the tools allow for inserting a surface proﬁle directly into it. For this reason,
a custom tool-pack has to be developed. McXtrace has been selected as the tool to be used
in this project since it oﬀers the most user-customisation [8] and local support is available (via
supervisor Erik B. Knudsen).
2.4.1
The McXtrace tool
Ray tracing is used for many other purposes than simulating x-rays, e.g. as in computer graph-
ics. Generally, rays are transported using either forward or backward propagation, where the
former moved rays from their origin (source) to an object, and the latter moves the rays from an
object back to the source [8]. Computer graphics use backward propagation to avoid spending
computational power on rays never reaching the object (player) by e.g. hitting a solid object
before hand. McXtrace uses forward propagation [8], since it otherwise would require to initially
know the ﬁnal state of the ray, which in practise would result in a complicated propagation
scheme. McXtrace then overcomes the issue of wasting computational power of rays not follow-
ing the beamline, by relying on beamlines usually being composed of devices following a single
well deﬁned central optical path [8]. This makes it relatively simple to predict if a ray will stay
within the beamline and hit the next device, to otherwise be ”absorbed” via termination [8].
This is best illustrated when considering an x-ray source, which is deﬁned as emitting one ray
per second in every direction (4π), but the only rays to be propagated are those passing a square
window of dimensions xwidth × yheight placed the distance dist downstream from the source [8],
where dist, xwidth, and yheight are user deﬁned parameters representing the distance to the next
component and its spatial dimensions.
In McXtrace each ray is composed of 12 parameters:
• (x,y,z): three parameters keeping track of a rays spatial position
• (kx,ky,kz): three parameters for the x, y, and z components of the rays wavevector k
• φ: a parameter describing the phase of the ray
• t: a time parameter to ensure no ray is accidental propagated back in time.
• (Ex,Ey,Ez): three spatial parameters for the rays electric ﬁeld components
• p: a parameter for the rays so-called weight factor (explained below)
Page 9 of 100
==PAGE:13==
All 12 parameters are updated for each ray, as the rays are propagated from the source down
the beamline [8].
The weight factor each ray carries is introduced to reduce the loss of computational work [8].
Consider a component along the beamline in which all rays must pass, but the component has
a transmission of 50% thereby absorbing half the beam. Rather than termination half the rays
at the component the weight factor is adjusted, here by dividing by two [8]. This means the
computational work gone into propagating the ray up until the component isn’t lost. Note, this
means that McXtrace actually propagated ray bunches rather than single rays.
This ray tracing treats the x-ray as a particle rather than waves; it should generally not be
possible to extract any wave phenomenons such as coherence from a ray-tracing simulation.
However, by collecting all rays at some point, the wavefront can be reconstructed by a summa-
tion of the product of the weight and phase factors (PN

X-ray optics for hard x-rays
Now with the fundamentals of x-ray optics in place, this section will give an overview of some
speciﬁc devices used to focus hard x-rays (Eph ≥10 keV), mainly two widely used devices called
Kirkpatrick-Baez mirrors and Fresnel zone plates focusing via reﬂective and diﬀractive properties,
respectively [1, 2]. This is followed by a more detailed description of the refractive focusing
device compound refractive lenses (CRL). The CRL section will go into details about how they
work, their geometry and design, equations for central parameters, and the eﬀect of the surface
deviating from the ideal theoretical shape. Besides these three focusing devices mentioned above,
a series of other devices exist for focusing x-rays, such as capillaries, waveguides, and kinoform
refractive lenses [1, 2]. None of these devices shall be discussed any further here.
3.1
Other types of refractive optics
Before diving into the discussion of the three devices, the simple pin-hole device shall be brief
discussed. Using a pin-hole is a straightforward method of reducing the size of a beam by merely
placing an x-ray absorbing plate in the beam path with a small pin-hole of the dimensions similar
to those of the desired spot size [9]. However, all x-rays not ﬁtting the hole and passing the
plate are absorbed, and the total x-ray ﬂux is therefore reduce. Therefore, the intensity per
area before and after the pin-hole will be the same, making the gain factor for a pin-hole G = 1
(by deﬁnition) [12, Ch. 3]. Introducing a pin-hole will cause interference in the beam passing
the hole, reducing the gain further G < 1. To ensure complete absorption of the x-rays hitting
the plate, its thickness must be suﬃcient to block the beam, as described by Lambert-Beer law
(eq. 3). To accommodate for the thickness of the plate, the pin-hole becomes a cylinder, where
diﬀraction can take place on the sidewalls, causing an increase of the ﬁnal beam size [9, 2].
Altogether is a pin-hole, not an ideal device for focusing x-rays (on its own), since it throws
away a large portion of the photon ﬂux and causes interference eﬀects. The devices which will
be discussed below tries to maintain the beam intensity, while still reducing the beam size,
thereby having a Gain above one (G > 1).

3.2.4
Surface error, waviness, and roughness
The deviation between the achieved surface proﬁle and the ideal proﬁle will be referred to as the
surface error. Surface error is an issue for all focusing devices, and this discussion will be limited
to the eﬀect of surface errors on CRLs. The horizontal and vertical width or frequency of some
surface error dramatically inﬂuences the impact on the focusing abilities of a CRL. Wide surface
errors can be considered as low frequency noise to the ideal surface proﬁle and will be referred
to as the surface waviness.
Short range surface errors can similarly be considered as high frequency noise, and will through-
out this project be called the surface roughness.
The boundary between waviness and roughness is not generally well deﬁned. The lower limit for
the roughness for some measurement is limited by the Nyquist Theorem [8, 13], stating that the
recovered signal is half the sampling frequency. Both the surface waviness and roughness will be
measured in terms of the root mean square (RMS).
In the literature, the surface error is not as clearly separated into waviness and roughness as
here. Literature commonly refers to the RMS of the entire surface error σT (deviation from the
ideal shape), where it can be found to be ∼1 µm [3, 4]. Considering the Mar´echal criterion for

Chromatic
It might not be clear from eq. 10 how the focal length depends of x-ray energy/wavelength
without considering eq. 6 and realising δ ∝λ2. It is thereby clear that CRLs are chromatic.
Yet, CRLs are often used in polychromatic setups, which is possible via the device called a trans-
focator [2]. A transfocator can translate and rotate a CRL stack freely along and around the
optical axis and gives the possibility of adding and removing lenses to/from the stack, usually
in cartridges of 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, etc. lenses at a time [2]. By using a transfocator, it is possible
to use a CRL as a polychromatic device.
Finally, it should be noted that the major drawback of using single crystalline materials in
CRLs are the unavoidable Bragg-reﬂections [3, 4]. Close to some energies, the Bragg criteria
for the crystalline lattice will be satisﬁed, causing Bragg reﬂections creating Bragg peaks in the
focal plane. There is no direct method of breaking the Bragg reﬂections without damaging the
coherence properties of the lenses.

4.2
Laser ablation for micro-fabrication
In the mid-1970, laser tools had become reliable and powerful enough for industrial use [15].
Laser ablation removes material from a target by a combination of heating, melting, and vapor-
isation. A laser ablation process becomes possible when the laser ﬂuence excess the material
threshold Fth [15]. The laser ablation technique used in the fabrication process here, uses a a
high power (100 mW ≤Plaser ≤10 W) and shorted pulse (picoseconds) laser beam with some
discrete wavelengths ranging the low ultraviolet to the high infrared (depend on the laser instru-
ment) [16]. The laser beam pulses are irradiated in pulses onto a sample surface in consecutive
points, forming a line. The pulses are emitted with a frequency called the repetition rate or
repetition frequency (frep.). The movement of the laser beam is controlled by moving the op-
tical mirrors (or sample stage) at a constant mark speed (vmark). The distance between each
point hit by a laser pulse while writing a line (lP toP ) can be calculated by the repetition rate
and the mark speed via lP toP = vmark/frep. [16]. A plasma cloud is formed above the ablated
region composed of the removed material. Some of the ablated material will redeposit back on
the surface [15].
The ablation eﬃciency varies between materials and depends on the absorption and refraction
of the material at the given wavelength [15]. The ablation eﬃciency will generally increase as
the wavelength decreases due to an increase in optical absorptivity and a lower reﬂectivity [15].
The penetration depth of the laser beam through the plasma should also be taken into account,
since the plasma acts as both a shield and a secondary heat source [15]. The plasma cloud is
causing the ablation eﬃciency to decrease due to scattering in the plasma and increase due to
heating of the targeted surface [15]. During an ablation process, the ablated material will be
heated, causing the eﬀective ablation rate to increase since the needed energy to promote an
atom from the solid state in the surface into the plasma decreases.
The amount of material removed by a single laser pulse is referred to as the ablation rate. In this
project, it becomes convenient to deﬁned two types of ablation rates. Firstly, the so-called single
line ablation deﬁned as the depth of a hole/groove made from a single line iteration of the laser
beam. Secondly, the eﬀective ablation rate deﬁned as the total depth of structure divided by the
total number of depth iterations, Nlayers, used to make the structure. The single line ablation
rate is limited by the material threshold Fth, since each pulse has to carry enough energy to
promote surface material into the plasma phase. The eﬀective ablation rate is dominated by
thermal eﬀects in the irradiated region and the surrounding material. Each pulse can, therefore,
carry less energy than is required to overcome the static threshold. The eﬀective ablation rate
will vary signiﬁcantly in cases, where the ablation threshold is signiﬁcantly decreased during the
ablation process due to thermal heating of the targeted surface.

4.3.1
The ICP-RIE process
The ICP-RIE process used here is a combined argon-chloride and oxygen etch. The etch contains
three process steps, as shown in ﬁgure 15, each lasting 5 minutes and separated by a 5 minute
cooling period. First, an argon-chloride plasma with a low etch rate is used to reduce the surface
roughness while not greatly altering the surface height. Second and thirdly, a anisotropic oxygen
plasma etch is used with a strong chemical component, with a high etch rate. The ﬁrst etch step
(Ar/Cl2) smooth the diamond surface, and the two oxygen steps then etch down into the dia-
mond. The total three-step plasma etch is highly anisotropic and removes ∆zetch = (1.4±0.1)µm
per ’n’ iteration (n-loop), so the total achieved depth can be calculated by ∆tetch = n∆zetch.
The m-loop runs twice (m ≤2), once for each of the two oxygen steps.

4.4.1
Scanning electron microscope
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) has only been used brieﬂy at the beginning of the project.
The description here shall only describe the key concept since SEM is a complex technique that
can be operated in many ways for a broad selection of measurements.
In broad strokes, a SEM works by generating an electron beam via a so-called electron gun
typically mounted are the top of the device. The electron beam is guided downwards through
the SEM column passing a series of magnets and apertures, functioning as optics for focusing of
the electron beam. After exiting the SEM column, the electrons hit the targeted sample surface,
where the electrons penetrate some distance into the target material. The fast-moving electrons
from the electron beam are called primary electrons (Eprimary ∼[3; 30]keV [17]), and when enter-
ing the target material forms a so-called interaction volume where the primary electrons generate
secondary electrons. Some of the secondary electrons will propagate back to the material surface
and escape. The secondary electron escaping the material can then be picked up and counted
by an electron detector. The two main types of detectors used are secondary-electron-detector
(SE2) and variable-pressure-secondary-electron-detector (VPSE). The SE2 uses an electrically
charged grid to attract the secondary electrons [17]. The VPSE operates in the so-called variable
pressure mode discussed later. Is method only works if the secondary electrons can reach the
detected. Therefore are SEM systems under high-vacuum (HV ∼10−6 mbar [17]) to increase the
inelastic mean free path of the electrons to be several meters. When operating an SEM, several
parameters have to be considered and tuned/calibrated to optimise the image quality, and the
main parameters are listed below.

The SEM method assumes that the beam can hit a well-controlled spot on the surface, which
is not an issue if the electron beam moves in a straight path from the end of the SEM column
to the surface. However, for electric isolators, it is possible to have a build-up of charge on
the targeted surface, which consequently will cause a disturbance to the electron beam path.
The eﬀect of this can be extremely complicated and make a measurement completely useless.
Diamond is known as a material where charging can happen, and SEM is, therefore, not always
a preferred technique.
To combat charging of the targeted surface, a so-called variable pressure mode can be used,
where some inert gas is lead into the SEM chamber to mediate the charge away [17]. This con-
sequently increases the pressure and reducing the mean free path and thereby the quality of the
SEM images (requiring electron beam energies of ∼15 keV to work [17]). The inert gas particles
will bombard the surface, causing some of the gas particles to become charged, and then carry
the charge away from the surface as the gas particles leave it. VP-mode will be used to acquire
SEM images of regions smaller than ∼0.5 mm × 0.5 mm to reduce charging eﬀects. It should be
mentioned that operating in the VP-mode is more complicated due to the increased parameter
space for image optimisation. Using the VP-modes requires the user to also tune the chamber
pressure (pchamber) and the detector collection bias (Uc.bias), while at the same time tuning the
normal operation parameters listed above.
The SEM tool used here is a Zeiss Supra VP 40 (Supra 1) install in the basement at DTU
Nanolab [17]. The acceleration voltage can range from 20 V to 30 kV [17], where the typical val-
ues here is 3 kV in HV-mode and 15 kV is VP-mode. The best obtainable resolution is ∼1 nm in
the HV-mode and ∼2 nm in the VP-mode [17]. The working distance was typically kept around
4.5 mm. When operation in the VP-mode the parameters was usually around pchamber ≃25 Pa
and Uc.bias ≃380 V.

Confocal microscopy
Confocal microscopy is the primary characterisation technique use here, operating using optical
light to measure the surface topology of a sample.
In Confocal microscopy, light from a laser is guided to the sample via a so-called Dichroic mirror,
and focused on top the sample surface by a convex optical lens (illuminating the entire ﬁeld of
view at once), as illustrated in ﬁgure 17. For surface proﬁling, the light is reﬂected oﬀthe surface
back through the optical lens. A pin-hole aperture is placed a distance from the lens equal to
the focal length of the optical lens (so in the lens focal spot). This conﬁguration only allows
light reﬂected from within the focal plane of the optical lens to reach the detector, as seen in
ﬁgure 17. Confocal microscopy is very height sensitive (nanometer scale) because only a narrow
height-interval (equal to the DOF for the optical lens) is being measured. The focal plane can
be swept over some interval equivalent to the sample height, either by vertically translating the
sample or the entire optical system with respect to one another. Note to determine if some
lateral point is within the given focal plane, the intensity of the reﬂected light must exceed some
noise level. If some point in the focal plane does not exceed the noise level, the point will not
be measured. A threshold value typically controls the noise level.

Synchrotron characterisation techniques
While at the APS synchrotron in Chicago, three diﬀerent types of characterisation techniques
were performed. The two ﬁrst are wavefront techniques called single grating Talbot interferome-
try, and Near ﬁeld Speckle, both used to reconstruct the surface proﬁle and error of a single lens
or a combined CRL stack. The Talbot technique is much faster (∼1 min ) than the speckle tech-

images, and the speckle technique requires many sets of images (≃100) [19]. The advantage of

technique is estimating the focal length and beam spot size via so-called knife-edge scanning.

4.5.3
Knife edge scanning
A knife-edge scan is done by placing a rectangular plate (∼5 mm thick) made of highly ab-
sorbent material (usually tungsten) with one edge orthogonal to the desired scanning direction
[11]. Initially is the plate placed to the side of the beam with the edge closest to the beam being
the ’knife-edge’, which is a very well deﬁned straight edge here with a surface slope of 2◦(made
by JJ-Xray). During the measurement, the knife-edge is moved into the beam until the beam
is completely blocked [11]. The integrated beam intensity is measured with a pin-hole detector
as a function of the knife-edge position [11]. Plotting the resulting intensity as a function of
knife-edge position (or scan direction) should give a s-curve with a single step, as seen in ﬁgure
22 [11]. The step is caused by the blockage of the knife-edge, reducing the beam intensity to
zero. Diﬀerentiating the s-curve will yield a Gaussian proﬁle, which should represent the beam
shape in the scan direction [11]. The FWHM or Gaussian width of the beam spot can then be
determined from the diﬀerentiated s-curve. A single knife-edge scan can yield the spot size of
the beam at any position along the optical axis, assuming the spot size is Gaussian in shape and
smaller than the motor range of the knife edge [11].

5.1
Estimating the ablation rate
The ﬁrst challenge was to estimate the beam geometry, so ﬁnding a method of estimating the
beam diameter and the ablation rate. By measuring the width of the burning track from writing
a line (the black/grey track), the beam width can be approximated to ∼10 µm, as has been
done earlier. However, from such measurements, it is not clear how this width precisely relates
to the Gaussian beam shape.
Initially, it was desired to ﬁnd the line depth of a line written with just a single iteration,
Page 35 of 100
==PAGE:39==
the so-called single-line ablation rate. It was expected that during multiple line iteration would
increase the line depth linearly. So, by writing several lines with an increasing number of itera-
tions, linear interpolation should make it possible to estimate the single line ablation rate. The
beam geometry could be determined using an SEM, by turning the diamonds on the side and
measure the line cross-section. The ﬁrst attempt was done by writing lines starting near an edge
of the diamonds and ending just after the edge of the diamond. However, this method was not
successful in estimating the beam geometry due to a series of issue, such changes to the ablation
rate near edge surfaces due to the thermal dispersion of heat from the beam.
Another approach for estimating the beam geometry from line cross-sections is by writing lines
on the ﬂat surface of a diamond and afterward cleaving the diamond in two to extract the
line cross-section. The diamonds can be cleaved by laser ablating a deep groove through the
diamonds, which should be written on the opposite side of the diamonds to avoid damaging the
line cross-sections. Here the cutting groove was cut, so the remaining thickness was ∼100 µm,
from which the diamonds could be cleaved by hand using two tweezers.
Four identical structures were written on the diamond DWP03a namely DT13, DT14, DT15,
and DT16 with a the repetition rate frep. = [200, 300, 400, 500]kHz and the laser power Plaser =
[656, 710, 708, 1080]mW, listed respectively. Each line structure is comprised of ten lines written
in rows with increasing number of line iterations: [1,5,10,15,20,25,30,35,40,50]. Figure 24a and
24b show the line cross-sections of the ten lines in DT13 and DT16. This method suﬀers for how
accurately the line is aligned perpendicular to the cutting lines. However, since the diamond
is transparent, the cutting lines can be aligned to the line structures by looking through the
diamond with the zoom camera (inside the laser machine). Aligning the cutting lines was done
within ±0.5◦. Considering the written lines in ﬁgure 24, the line depth can be seen to increase
with the number of line iterations as desired, but as the depth increase, the cross-section curves
to the left in the pictures. The unexpected curving observed in the line cross-sections curve
to the same side for all four structures. The curving has been speculated to be caused by the
nitrogen ﬂow altering the thermal cooling of the diamond surface. Nevertheless, the cause of the
curving was not analysed to any further detail here, and the origin is still unknown. No curving
of the lens proﬁles has been observed.

5.2
Ablation simulations
After a series of measurements, the beam geometry could be described by a Gaussian proﬁle,
yet it required many hours of fabrication and characterization work.
A ablation simulation
tool was therefore desired capable of predicting the eﬀect diﬀerent laser parameters would have
on lens proﬁles.
Such a tool has been developed running in Matlab capable of preforming
ablation simulations. The simulation neglect thermal eﬀect, and assume the beam to ablating
any sample material inside the Gaussian shape beam volume. The Gaussian shaped beam volume
is determined by the beam diameter and eﬀective ablation rate found in the previous section.
5.2.1

ﬂowchart in ﬁgure 11. However, the Matlab script needs a method of simulating the ablation
process itself; for this, the scheme in the red box in the ﬂowchart is used.

5.2.2
Spirals from radial misalignment
All lenses simulated in this section will have a radius of curvature (RoC) of 150 µm and a depth
of 530 µm (t = 1100 µm and d = 40 µm). Here the deviation proﬁles are made by subtracting
the simulated surface proﬁle from the desired ideal parabolic proﬁle (working as the parabolic
background) (zDeviation(x, y) = zIdeal(x, y) −zSimulated(x, y)). Surface proﬁles from diﬀerent
simulations can be compared via the RMS value of the deviation surface proﬁle. The RMS
values are not expected to have a 1:1 relationship with the RMS for lenses fabricated with the
same parameters, but the RMS values are assumed to be related to the RMS of fabricated lenses
by scaling.
Before this project, a spiral artefact was observed in the lenses after the laser ablation, as
seen back in ﬁgure 23b. Such spirals can also be seen in deviation surfaces from simulations if
the script is set up as the laser script was before this project. The cause of the spirals has not
been understood up until this point. Previously the spiral has been handled by introducing an
additional rotation (Θ = 16) of the lens layers during the ablation process. Now, the ablation
simulation tool makes it possible to test the properties on the spiral artefact. Figure 28 shows
the deviation surface proﬁles, from lenses with diﬀerent rotation between each layer. The writing
direction is rotated by the angle φ = k(π/2 + γ), where k denotes the current layer (k = 1 : nz)
and γ = Θ(2π/nz) with Θ denoting the number of additional full (2π) rotations down through
the entire lens structure.
The two last surface plots in ﬁgure 28(c,d) are deviation of lenses written with Θ = 1 and Θ = 8,
respectively. The left surface clearly shows a spiral pattern (bottom contour plot), spiraling
from the edge towards the apex with four spiral lines. No such spiral is seen on the right surface
written with more additional rotations of the writing direction. The disappearance of the spiral
as the number of revolution of the writing direction increases matches what has previously been
observed in real lenses. From ablation simulation, the spiral eﬀect has been traced back to the
symmetry of the lines written in each layer. Some interesting features can be observed in the
two upper panels in ﬁgure 28(a,b), written with no rotation of the written direction at all and
rotated in steps of π/2 between each layer (φ = π/2 and φ = kπ/2, respectively). When the
writing direction is not changed, and the lines are written left to right (top to bottom), an
asymmetry is observed in the mirror-plane along the y-direction (in x = 0). The asymmetry is
caused by the asymmetric line positioning compared to the circle center in each layer of lines,
as illustrated in ﬁgure 29a. The radial distance between the ﬁrst line and the circle edge has
previously been deﬁned as half the line pitch, which causes an issue because the line pitch may
not equal a position integer fraction of the circle diameter (rlayer/lLtoL ̸= Z+). The results are
that the distance between the last line and the circle edge is larger than half the line pitch,
making the area of the two circle segment on either side of the given line layer diﬀerent, as
indicated by the blue segment in the left part of ﬁgure 29a. Less material is ablated in the lager
circle segment because the lines are further away, which gives rise to a valley in the deviation
surface, as seen in ﬁgure 28a (y ≤0). When the writing direction is rotated in steps of π/2
between each layer, the valley gets a four-fold symmetry, as seen in ﬁgure 28b. When a small
additional rotation (γ) is added as a continuously increasing, the valley becomes a spiral. Now,

It is believed that the torus comes from some artefact in the laser’s optical system.
When
the laser power increases, the artefact in the optical systems increases, causing an increase in the
amplitude of the torus. One possible candidate for the artefact in the lasers optical system could
be the ﬁnal focusing lens. However, this hypothesis was tested by fabricating lenses with their
apex displaced in both the x- and y-direction away from the origin of the aligned coordinate
system. These tests showed no change in the shape of the torus. The optical artefact could also
come from the optical mirrors guiding and moving the laser beam, but no direct method exists
for testing this hypothesis. So the exact origin of the torus artefact is still unknown, and no
other solution has been found other than lowering the laser power.
The laser ablation process at frep. = 200 kHz and Plaser = 1000 mW is already below the
ablation threshold and only works by thermally lowering the eﬀective ablation rate during the
ablation process. To operate at an even lower power is a challenging task since it is diﬃcult to
estimate the eﬀective ablation rate correctly. At this time, it is not fully understood which laser
pulses go to heating and which to ablating the material surface, it might even be a mix, making
it diﬃcult to predict the ﬁnal lens geometry. The smallest amplitude for the torus has been
observed at the laser settings frep. = 400 kHz and Plaser ≃300 mW (at standard parameters),
and the resulting lens surface can be seen in ﬁgure 36 with a torus amplitude similar to the
surface waviness of ≤5 µm. Further reduction of the laser power resulted in the lens surfaces
not being cylindrical symmetry, and obtaining an irregular shape.

5.4
Eﬀect of Inductively Coupled Plasma Reactive Ion Etching
The most of the laser parameters in place for optimising the ablate process, the dry-etching
plasma process was tested. The ICP-RIE process was used to reduce surface roughness, and
thin down the web thickness between the bi-concave lens sides. Due to time limitations, the
ICP-RIE process was not optimised for the lens proﬁle. The process used was developed for
etching of ﬂat diamond surfaces, and the eﬀect of instead etching a curved lens proﬁle has not
been analysis.
After etching the laser ablated lens surfaces, the surface regains its original yellow color, making
it easy to distinguish etched diamonds from non-etched, as seen back in ﬁgure 10. The RIE
Page 51 of 100
==PAGE:55==
has the eﬀect of smoothing and thinning the surface of the diamond, as explained in section
4.3, so the surface roughness is expected to be reduced while the surface waviness should be
preserved. During Sensofar measurement of etched diamonds turns out to be extremely diﬃcult,
because the lens surfaces barely reﬂect any light. It is unknown whether the reduced reﬂectivity
is caused by the loss of the black surface color or the reduction of the surface roughness (the two
phenomenons might even be related). After the RIE process, the number of measured points on
the Sensofar (20x-magniﬁcation) fell to below 30% across the entire surface. Figure 37a shows
a lens with RoC = 150 µm and depth ≃320 µm, where non-measured points are colored red
and the number of measured points is ≤15%. When the percentages of measured points get
that small meaningful reconstruction of the surface proﬁles become impossible. The numerical
aperture for the Sensofars 50x-magniﬁcation optic is almost twice that of the 20x-magniﬁcation
(NA20x = 0.45 and NA50x = 0.80). By using the 50x optics more points can be measured
(∼40%), especially near the apex of the lens as seen in ﬁgure 37b. The FOV of the 50x optics
is more than six times smaller than the FOV for the 20x, which increases the acquisition time
signiﬁcantly. To reduce the Sensofar acquisition time and ﬁle size, the lateral resolutions were
reduced by jumping 12 steps height per step. Consequencely analysis of the etched surface can
only be done near the apex using the 50x-magniﬁcation with a reduced vertical resolution of
2.4 µm.

6
Synchrotron experiment
The goal of this chapter is to present data acquired and the result obtained from the APS syn-
chrotron experiment. The surface proﬁles measured using the Talbot techniques will be discussed
and compared to the proﬁles measured using the Sensofar and presented in section 5.5.1 .
The main discussion focuses on the result from the Talbot measurements since only a few lenses
and lens-stacks were measured using the Speckle and Knife-edge scans. The analysis of data
from Knife-edge scans is limited since the lenses proved unsatisfactory at focusing as shall be
elaborated on later.
Since the wave-propagation techniques used at the synchrotron all work by detecting the beam
after it has passed through the lens stack, each double-sided lens or lens stack is seen as a single
object with the combined thickness proﬁle of all the lenses in the stack. The consequence of
this for an individual double-sided lens, the lens depth is doubled (assuming the surface RMS
is identical on both sides), and radius of curvature and thereby the focal length is halved (in
the thin lens approximation). To distinguish between single-sided and double-sided lenses, the
abbreviations SS and DS will be used in the subscripts, respectively.

7
McXtrace simulation
As mentioned earlier the simulations are based on ray-tracing using the McXtrace software.
McXtrace operate by running a so-called Instrument-script (.inst converted into c-code via the
compiler).
The instrument-script itself is a list of the components in a (usually) simpliﬁed
beamline conﬁguration. The components are listed in the relative order along the optical axis
from source and downstream. To simulated CRLs, the beamline conﬁguration and thereby the
instrument-script is relatively simple, and a sketched is drawn in ﬁgure 57.
Figure 57: Sketch of the standard beamline geometry described in the instrument ﬁle.
Generally, the instrument-script only contain a source component, the new CRL component, and
a series of monitors. The majority of monitors are so-called psd-monitors capable of determining
the shape of the beam cross-section by storing the xy-position of the ray passing through the
monitor. This detection method is non-invasive, so the monitors do not inﬂuence the rays at
all nor require any signiﬁcant computational power. The number of monitors in a simulation
hardly aﬀect the computational time.
The ﬁrst component in the instrument-script is the source. Several predeﬁned source compo-
nents exist in McXtrace. Two types of sources have been used here a ﬂat point source, capable
of generating a beam perfectly parallel to the optical axis, and a Gaussian-shaped source capable
of generating a beam representing the real speciﬁcations of the beam at APS. The entire APS
storage ring is described by a single component mimicking the undulator beam speciﬁcations.
To save computational power, McXtrace only simulates rays hitting the ﬁrst component in
the beamline, here a monitor in front of the new CRL component. After the ﬁrst monitor, the
ray enters the CRL devices, which impose a circular aperture only letting rays pass, which have
a radial distance to the optical smaller than the opening aperture of the lens. The exact method
in which the new CRL component traces the ray is be explained in the following section. The
remaining components after the CRL are a series of position-sensitive monitors, as shown in
ﬁgure 57. The monitor stack is placed around the expected focal plane. The ﬁnal and large
monitor is placed far downstream (100 m) from the CRL to magnify the focal spot shape. The
ﬁnal monitor is larger to account for the increase in beam size after the focal plane.
One monitor is not drawn in ﬁgure 57, since its properties are not physical, and its exact place-
ment can be anywhere after the CRL stack (except inside other components). This monitor is
a custom non-standard focal point component made by supervisor Erik B. Knudsen to estimate
the focal length of a focus component. It works by calculating at which point along the optical
axis a given ray is closest to the optical axis, and then deposits the intensity of the ray at this
point. In principle, the focal point monitor ﬁlls all space.

7.1
The new McXtrace component to simulate measured lens proﬁles
The black box in ﬁgure 57 is the new CRL component developed in this project. How the compo-
nent operates and trace rays through it is going to be described in this section. The component
is coded in a so-called component-script (.comp).
The purpose of the new CRL component is to simulate the optical performance of fabricated
lenses from their measured surface proﬁles including the surface errors. The component is built
as a single bi-concave (double-sided) lens, meaning that after refracting oﬀthe ﬁrst lens surface,
the process has to be repeated for the second lens surface. In order to simulate a CRL stack,
the component must be inserted once for each double-sided lens in the stack.
7.1.1
Assumption for the new CRL component
Some fundamental assumptions were made to form the base of the new CRL component.
The ﬁles providing the lens surface proﬁles are assumed to describe a parabolic lens proﬁle
on an uniform grid. It is assumed to be possible to ﬁt parabolic 2d-surface to the provided lens
proﬁle. By requiring the surface proﬁles to represent parabolas, the intersection point of the ray
on the parabolic surfaces becomes much simpler. Further, the surface variations are assumed
to be so small that they do not interfere with the position of the intersection point of the ray
on the surface. This is to say no feature in the lens is large enough to shadow any region in
the lens aperture. This assumption is most valid near the lens apex since the direction of the
surface oscillations almost becomes orthogonal to the k-vector of the rays near the apex. The
assumption worsens towards the edge of the lens aperture, but luckily this the region with the
lowest beam intensity.
Summary of fundamental assumptions:
• The lens proﬁles can be ﬁtted by a parabolic surface
• The provided surface proﬁles are based on a uniform grid
• The surface variations are small enough to not interfere with the intersection point between
the rays and the parabolic surface

8
Discussion
Looking back at the lenses fabricated for the synchrotron experiment, their optical performance
still lags in certain areas. This chapter aims to discuss the hypotheses for some observed phe-
nomenons in the performance of the lenses, such as the ring artefact observed in the lens proﬁles
and the odd beam shape in the focal point. The end of the chapter is a discussion of how to
improve the fabrication scheme and certain parameters to achieve future lenses with improved
optical performance hopefully.
8.1
McXtrace simulation of parabolic lenses with an imposed torus
The most dominant feature of the ﬁnal lenses is the oddly shaped focal spot seen in ﬁgure 56.
At the time of the synchrotron experiment, the shape was not understood, and many hypotheses
were proposed, such as reﬂections of the aluminium sample holder, excitations of optical center
inside the diamond, higher-order modes from the beams monochromator. However, all of these
hypotheses were disproved. The only possibility left was that some artefact caused the odd shape
in the lens.

From the simulations above of ideal parabolic lens proﬁles with an imposed torus shape, it
can be concluded that odd shapes of the beam cross-section can be generated by imposing a
torus artefact. The simulated beam cross-section can achieve a shape similar to the shaped
observed in the focal plane during the synchrotron experiment by displacing the lens centers
just the right amount. However, due to limitations in the resolution of the simulated lenses, the
deformed beam spot can not be seen in the focal plane, and the exact dimensions can therefore
not be compared. Simulations performed with a higher resolution are required to pursue further
the hypothesis of the odd beam shaped being caused by a torus artefact in lenses with astigma-
tism. The most ﬂexible solution would be to describe a parabolic surface with an imposed torus
analytically. An analytically described surface does not suﬀer from grid eﬀect, so stack with any
number of lenses could be simulated, and the grid spacing should not limit the resolution. Due
to time restrictions, this approach was not pursued further.
8.2
Challenges of reducing the average laser pulse power
From the simulations, the torus artefact can be speculated to have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the
focusing ability of the lenses and the shape of the focused beam. Therefore it is desirable to
Page 88 of 100
==PAGE:92==
either reduce the amplitude of the torus further or, if possible, to remove it. Back in section 5.3
it was found that operating the laser in a low power mode, the amplitude of the torus artefact
could be reduced to the level similar to maximum variations in the deviation proﬁle. In order
to reduce the amplitude of the torus, the average laser pulse power should be reduced. As a
consequence of reducing the average pulse power, the eﬀective ablation rate would also decrease.
It is believed that decreasing the eﬀective ablation rate would also reduce the surface variations
because the step height of each layer would decrease. The power setting used in the fabrication
of the ﬁnal lenses was Plaser ≃= 450 mW, which is below than half the power required to to
reach the single line ablation threshold at frep. = 500 kHz (vmark = 37.5 mm/s). Attempts were
made at operating the laser at an even lower average pulse power by reducing both the laser
power and increasing the repetition rate. However, the resulting lenses had a higher RMS value
and, in some extreme cases, irregular proﬁle shapes.
Decreasing the mark speed would, in principle, increase the heating eﬀect by decrease the dis-
tance between each radiated point, causing an increase of the eﬀective ablation rate. Decreasing
the mark speed should, therefore, make it possible to operate at a lower average power. Lenses
have been fabricated with a mark speed of vmark = 15.0 mm/s and a low average laser powers
of ∼200 mW (using the standard parameter in table 5 and frep. = 400 kHz), Nevertheless, none
of these lenses have smaller surface RMS values. Rather the overall average surface variation
increased for lens written at a lower mark speed, making it diﬃcult to estimate the change to the
torus amplitude. From these tests changing the mark speed did not yield a smaller surface RMS
value, so the approach was not pursued further. It should be mentioned that the parameter
space spanned by the mark speed, laser repetition rate, and average laser power has not been
fully exhausted (due to time limitation), so an undiscovered minimum in the surface RMS may
exist.
In general, the issue of lowering the power below the threshold is that it becomes unknown
which laser pulses go-to heating and which to ablation, or if it is a mixed of the two. The exact
manner in which the eﬀective ablation rate changes while writing a lens is currently poorly un-
derstood. This challenges the approximation of setting the nominal ablation rate to a constant
value equal to the estimate of the average ablation rate during the entire writing process.
It is hypothesised that the issue of an unpredictable thermal increase of the eﬀective ablation
rate could be avoided by thermally heating the diamond with an external heat source. Such a
heat source could be a heating pad placed directly below the diamond. By increasing the eﬀec-
tive ablation rate by heating, a lower laser power would be required to overcome the ablation
threshold. The laser could therefore be operated in an even lower power. If the majority of
the change to the eﬀective ablation rate comes form the heat pad, the eﬀective ablation rate
could be kept more stable during ablation process. Externally heating the diamonds would also
strengthen the approximation of setting the nominal ablation rate equal to a constant value. No
fabrication has been done using an external heat source.

8.3.4
Compensating for dominant artefacts
The current lens proﬁles are not free of artefacts, and some artefact might not be possible to
reduce further by tuning the process parameters. E.g., lowering the pulse power further might
not decrease the torus amplitude further.
An approach commonly used for CRLs is by fabricating a so-called phase-plate to compen-
sate for any undesired disruption to the x-ray wavefront. The idea is to know the shape and
placement of artefacts on the lens surface proﬁle. The phase-plate should have the inverse shaped
of the combined deviation proﬁle to compensate for the disruption caused to the wavefront by
the undesired artefacts. phase-plates are typically fabricated in materials diﬀerent to the less
material, where the surface shape can be controlled to higher precision. Silicon is a common
material used for phase-plate, since several high-resolution fabrication techniques exist for mak-
ing structures in silicon. Silicon is also favourable for CRLs of single crystalline diamond since
single crystalline silicon is easy and cheap to come by, and the single crystalline properties do
not disrupt the beam coherence.

9
Conclusion
At the start of the project, the beam shape of the laser ablation tool was determined to have
a width of Dbeam = (12.8 ± 1.14)µm. The beam width determined from SEM images of line
cross-sections written with a varying number of laser iterations (line writings). The depth of
the line cross-sections turned out to signiﬁcantly overestimating the ablation rate. The ablation
rate was instead estimated from the achieved depth of full lens structures. The ablation rate was
found to increase linearly with the laser’s power and inversely with the laser’s repetition rate.
At a laser power of Plaser ≃1 W and a repetition rate of frep. = 200 kHz the ablation rate was
determined to be 1.63 µm/#.
A MATLAB script was developed to simulate the laser ablation process. The simulation script
follows a process ﬂow similar to the script controlling the lasers writing process. The script
neglects material redeposition and heating eﬀect. The simulations revealed the nature of the
spiral artefact observed in earlier lenses. The spirals were caused by the circle segment on the
outside of the ﬁrst and last written lines in each layer. It was found from ablation simulations
that the amplitude of the spiral could be reduced by aligning the lines symmetrically around
the lens center while writing. However, a perfect alignment of the lines causes an interference
pattern to appear. The optimal prefactor to the alignment term was found, where the amplitude
of the spirals and the interference pattern is reduced simultaneously.
Fabricating lenses with the parameters described in table 5 (Plaser ≃1 W and frep. = 200 kHz)
revealed a distinct torus artefact in circling the lens apex. The torus artefact seems to be unaf-
fected by most of the ablation parameters, such as the mark speed, laser delays, and line pitch.
The torus artefact is hypothesised by be caused by the optical mirrors inside the laser guiding
the laser beam. The amplitude of the torus artefact could be reduced by operating the laser in
a low power mode at Plaser ∼500 mW with frep. ≥400 kHz.
The smallest surface RMS value observed was for a lens fabricated with the parameters in
table 8, where the laser power is Plaser = 450 mW, the repetition rate frep.=500 µm, and a the
mark speed of vmark = 37.5 mm/s. The average eﬀective ablation rate was at these laser settings
estimated to be 0.78 µm/#.
Lenses were fabricated on 11 diamonds with a thickness of (700 ± 50)µm and facets dimen-
sions of (2.1×2.1)mm2. Four bi-concave lenses were written on each diamond with four diﬀerent
radius of curvature; RoC = [50; 150; 225; 300]µm. To account for cross-structural-heating the
four lens type have diﬀerent desired web thickness of dweb = [50; 60; 80; 60]µm, respectively. All
the lenses were fabricated using the laser parameters described above. After the laser ablation
process, ten of the lenses were dry-etched in an ICP-RIE process with the process ﬂow sketched
in ﬁgure 15 and process parameters listed in the table 2. The ICP-RIE process was to remove
∼9.8 µm on each side of the diamonds.
The forty-four lenses were all characterised using the Sensofar with the 20x-magniﬁcation. The
achieved RoC vales were found to have lower mean values but within ≤5.5% of the desired
RoCs. The achieved web thickness was also found to closely resemble the desired values, except
for lenses with RoC = 50 µm, which had a too large web thickness of ∼100 µm. The mean
waviness RMS values for the four lens types are RMSmean = [3.50; 2.87; 1.31; 0.96]µm across the

Outlook
The lenses fabricated for the synchrotron experiment had a reduced surface RMS compared to
earlier lenses, but the surface RMS is still not below the Mar´echal criterion. Further develop-
ment is needed to reduce the surface RMS to below the Mar´echal criterion, where a CRL can
preserve the beam coherence. In order to cut the current surface, RMS in half a drastic change
to the fabrication scheme is required. Some approaches to reducing the surface RMS are already
provided in section 8.2 and 8.3, where the most promising alteration to the fabrication scheme
is the use of an external heat source to lower the eﬀective ablation rate. Using an external heat
source is expected to both lower and keep the eﬀective ablation rate more stable during the
ablation process. The amount of ablated material would, therefore, be more similar for each
laser pulse, eﬀectively providing greater control of the ablation process.
The surface RMS values measured with confocal microscopy using the 50x-magniﬁcation for
single-sided lenses are in good agreement with means surface RMS values calculated from Tal-
bot and Speckle measurements. The confocal characterisation technique is suﬃcient to estimate
the surface RMS, at least for the current range of RMS values (∼0.9 µm). Synchrotron charac-
terisation techniques such as speckle phase-contrast are recommended to achieve the amplitude
of Zernike polynomials describes the CRL aberrations, which can be used to design a phase-plate
for the CRL to compensate for the aberrations.
The current fabrication techniques do not look promising to X-FEL applications, where sig-
niﬁcantly smaller RMS values on the nano-scale are required.
The ICP-RIE process already operates stable with a predictable etch depth.
Improving the
ICP-RIE process should reduce the surface roughness, but is not expected to reduce the overall
surface RMS signiﬁcantly. An unexplored feature of the ICP-RIE process is the increasing lens
RoC. If the etch yield is parabolic across the lens aperture, the etch process could be used to
reduce the RoC on purpose. A new fabrication scheme could be developed, where the ablated
lens proﬁles are shallower and have a smaller RoC to reduce the surface RMS. A deep ICP-RIE
process should be performed to increase the lens depth by ∼100 µm until the desired depth
is reached. Now, due to the non-uniform etch yield, more material has been etched from the
bottom of the lens than of the sloped sides, eﬀectively increasing the RoC. To further increase
the lens RoC, a mask could be grown on top of the diamond to shield the ﬂat top surface from
being etched. The mask could be made of SiO2 grown before the laser ablation. This deep etch
fabrication scheme could make it possible to make a lens with a RoC of ∼25 µm commercially
available, which exceeds the current commercially limit of RoC=50 µm for beryllium lenses.
This entire fabrication scheme relies on the assumption that the non-uniform etch yield scales
linearly with the parabolic surface slope.
The new McXtrace CRL component currently oﬀers the possibility of getting a further un-
derstanding of the odd beam shaped observed in the synchrotron experiment. Future versions of
the code have the potential of becoming a part of the fabrication process. The average surface
RMS of a stack is observed to be more than three times the surface RMS of an individual lens.
Future simulations could be performed on the speciﬁc lenses stacked together to form a given
CRL, making it possible to predict the performance of a speciﬁc CRL. The simulation could
reveal which combination of lenses resulting in the best CRL performance. Simulations could
even suggest how the individual lenses should be rotated in the stack to optimise the combined
CRL performance. However, further development of the McXtrace component is required to
achieve such abilities.
Continued work by JJ-Xray could lower the surface RMS be to below the Mar´echal criterion,
making CRL of the single crystalline diamond a standard optical component in many beamlines
