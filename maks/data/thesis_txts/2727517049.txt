Abstract
This research project aims to investigate the precipitates that are formed in martensitic
stainless steel Fe-12.3Cr-0,65C-0.61Mn after high temperature solution nitriding followed
by annealing at 600°C, 650°C, 700°C and 750°C for 8, 15 30, 60 and 180 minutes. For
this purpose samples were treated and then analyzed with various techniques; nitrogen
content was determined by combustion analysis, microstructure was studied in LOM and
SEM, chemical composition was studied using EDS in TEM and SEM, phase identifica-
tion was done with synchrotron results and TEM diffraction patterns, the simulations of
precipitates composition was calculated in Thermo-Calc, durometry was investigated in
hardness tester.
It was observed that the temperature has influence on the decomposition rate - the pro-
cess goes faster at higher temperatures. It was also seen that the steel decomposes
more with the time. Decomposition begins at the grain boundaries and continues into the
grain with time.
There are different types of precipitates forming. It was observed that initially Cr2N with
>90% Cr and M7C3 with 65-80% Cr are present, with time M7C3 is transforming into more
stable M23C6.

Introduction
Stainless steels are widely used in industry for their outstanding combination of prop-
erties - ductility, strength and, especially important, corrosion resistance [1]. However,
wear-resistance of the material needs improvement. For that purpose many processes
are applied: moderate temperature nitriding, LTSH, HTSN and multi-step processes. It is
shown in literature [2] that the main problem for hardening stainless steels is loss of corro-
sion properties due to formation of chromium nitrides that capture chromium. In order to
avoid that it is necessary to heat the material higher than the region of nitrides formation
and subsequent rapid cooling [3]. That technique allows to get nitride free austenite. In
order to improve wear-resistance of the steel after HTSN subsequent treatment can be
applied. It is shown that grain refinement and precipitate formation achieved by annealing
will noticeably improve that property [4].
The technology that is presented in patent ”Process for obtaining a fine-grained marten-
sitic structure component” [5] HTSN followed by annealing below 800°C provides optimal
combination of properties for stainless steel. This study concerns characterization of pre-
cipitates that are formed after the treatment.

Literature Review
2.1
Background of nitriding
Stainless steels have a wide range of applications, because of their combination of prop-
erties: strength, ductility and corrosion resistance. The only concern for the application of
stainless steel is wear-resistance - tribological properties are much lower than for other
materials [1]. In order to improve the wear resistance thermochemical treatment can
be applied, for example by - moderate temperature nitriding [1]. Forming chromium ni-
tride if more energetically favorable than iron nitride formation, so the hardening effect
is achieved by formation of chromium nitrides dispersed in a steel matrix in the surface-
adjacent zone [6]. However, chromium nitride precipitation inevitably jeopardizes the cor-
rosion properties [2].
To avoid chromium nitride precipitation low temperature surface hardening was introduced
[4]. LTSH can be performed in gas that contains nitrogen or carbon, plasma or liquid alkali
medium. Temperatures are below 450°C and 520°C for nitrogen and carbon, respectively.
The ingress of nitrogen or carbon results in the formation of a case in the surface region
that consists of a supersaturated solid solution of carbon and/or nitrogen in austenite (ex-
panded austenite) or martensite (expanded martensite) [1]. Expanded austenite provides
improved wear-resistance and fatigue performance and can further improve the corrosion
resistance, especially localized corrosion as pitting and crevice corrosion [1]. The forma-
tion of chromium nitrides, CrN and Cr2N, remains an issue for this as well for long term
exposure at temperatures above, say, 300°C. According to the TTT diagram on figure
2.1 nitride formation does not occur at higher temperatures because the nitrides are not
stable [6].

steel containing different amounts of nitrogen [6].

The process of high temperature solution nitriding was developed In the 1990s [3, 1].
HTSN was developed for stainless steels at the temperatures above 1050°C [1, 6]. El-
evated temperatures allow the use molecular nitrogen gas, N2, as nitrogen provider. At
lower temperatures NH3 is used, because N2 which can’t decompose at temperatures
below 600°C and thus can not be applicable for low temperature processes [6]. From the
schematic diagram in figure 2.1, it can be observed that quenching is required in order to
avoid nitride formation. Moreover, the higher the nitrogen content the faster the cooling
rate should to be to avoid nitride formation.
2.2
Multiple step treatment of stainless steel
In order to improve strength and at the same time preserve ductility, a technology con-
sisting of HTSN followed by 2-step heat-treatment was developed in 2009 [4]. The inves-
tigation was performed on nickel-free ferritic stainless steel which was made austenitic
by dissolving nitrogen into the alloy (Fe–25Cr–1N) . After HTSN austenite is stabilized in
the material as nitrogen is an austenite stabilizer. In the first step after HTSN the alloy
is decomposed into ferrite, nitrides and carbides in order to create nucleation sites for
new grains that are forming in the next treatment. Nitrides and carbides form in the sites
with high stored energy that initiates initial precipitation and further growth. That process
leads to a finer microstructure that improves the material’s resistance against intergranular
fractures [4].

3.2
Heat treatments
As the first step high temperature solution nitriding was performed in Nabertherm furnace
at 1150°C for 5 hours under a total pressure of 1.2 bars of N2 followed by quenching in
helium. Next, the samples were treated in an open air Nabertherm furnace. In order to
minimize oxidation, the samples were wrapped in metal foil. The heat-treatments were
done at 600°C, 650°C, 700°C and 750°C for 8, 15, 30, 60 and 180 minutes and terminated
by quenching in water.
3.3
Metallographic preparation
Samples were embedded in Struers Prontopress 20 with first layer of Struers Levofast
and followed by Struers Polyfast resins.
Grinding and polishing was done using Struers RotoPol 22 base and RotoForce 4 head,
Struers Multidoser was used for polishing. During grounding 500, 1000 and 4000 papers
with silicone carbides were used. For polishing Struers MD-Mol disc was used with 3μm
diamond abrasive particles and Struers MD-Nap disc with 1μm.
Etching of the samples was done with Vilella’s reagent which is a standard option for ferritic
Cr steels and austenitic CrNi steels consisting of 1% picric acid and 5% hydrochloric acid
in ethanol[7].

3.6
Transmission Electron Microscopy
Extraction replicas were prepared of the samples 700-8, 700-15, 700-30, 700-60 and
700-180 using chemical etching in Vilella’s reagent. That only leaves the precipitates
after dissolving the matrix. Transmission electron microscope JEM-2100 was used for
acquiring images, diffraction patterns and conducting EDS measurements.
3.7
Analysis of Nitrogen content
Nitrogen content was determined by combustion analysis using a LECO TC500 on the as
nitrided condition. LECO calibration samples were used as the reference. Standard 502-
072 0067 with nominal weight of 0,5 g and 0,526 0,011 Nitrogen. Six samples after HTSN
were cut for the measurement. Three were approximately 500 mg and three - 167 mg. As
expected nitrogen content was 0,3 wt nitrogen, the samples weight was chosen to either
match the weight of the standard or the expected content. The measurements of different
mass did not provide noticeable difference. All the measurements were performed in
accordance with ASTM E1019 [8].
3.8
Hardness
Hardness measurements were performed using a Struers DuraScan with a Vickers Inden-
tor with respect to EN ISO 6507 [9]. Loads HV0.1, HV0.2 and HV0.3 were selected for
the analysis. Measurements were done on polished and etched samples in order to se-
lectively test grains and martensite. Also hardness profiles were performed on polished,
unetched surface.
3.9
Synchrotron
X-ray imaging was done at synchrotron facility MAX IV in Lund. The samples were tested
in transmission with 35keV energy and the wavelength 0,354Å, distance to the detector
from the specimen was 700mm. Conditions analyzed were 600-180, 650-180, 700-180,
750-180 and 700-8, 700-15, 700-30, 700-60. Peak identification was done using Bruker’s
DIFFRAC.EVA software.

4.1
Microstructure evolution observed in light optical
microscope
Light optical microscopy provides a clear trend in the evolution of microstructure. The
resolution in LOM does not resolve to see the individual precipitates but the trend in the
evolution of the microstructure can be described.
As shown on figure 4.1 after HTSN the samples have a fully austenitic microstructure.
There are stringers of inclusions perpendicular to the sample surface, which are a remnant
of the manufacturing process. They appear in all the investigated conditions and in many
cases are the locations where new precipitates start to form.
As observed on figure 4.1 in sample 600-15 the first step of decomposition is grain bound-
ary precipitation. It is followed by decomposition from the boundaries towards the centre
of the grains, for example on figure 4.2 in sample 600-180. At this decomposition state
there is a lot of martensite forming from the precipitates at the grain boundaries towards
the centre of the grains.
Full decomposition is observed in samples 650-60, 650-180, 700-30, 700-60 and 700-
180. Initially, the microstructure still reveals the original austenite grain boundaries (figure
4.3). At longer holding times, after full decomposition, austenite grain boundaries are not
visible anymore but precipitates remain in the structure figure 4.4).

Discussion
5.1
Microstructure evolution
As observed in LOM the precipitation occurs firstly at grain boundaries. As it continues to
grow into the grain, the chromium, nitrogen and carbon from the matrix form incorporated
in carbides and nitrides. Accordingly, austenite is no longer stabilized (particularly by N
and C) and Ms increases so martensite can form (figure 5.1). In LOM preparation marten-
site and martensite in the microsrtucture is appearing similar. But results from synchrotron
with widening on the left side of austenite peaks (figure 4.6) and increased hardness as
presented in section 4.7 prove that it is martensite that is formed in the microstructure
during the treatment.

carbides.
Some precipitation is observed at the surface (figure 5.2). The type wasn’t determined
but most likely these are Cr2N nitrides.

5.2
Precipitate identification and transformation
Comparison of results in section 3.10 and 4.6 proves that high chromium content precip-
itates are Cr2N. But the results for low and medium chromium content particles do not
correspond. Thermo-Calc suggests that M23C6 has lower chromium content than M7C3
but TEM diffraction and EDS results show the opposite.
As shown in section 4.6 for the particle with medium chromium content two types of pre-
cipitates match the patterns. That might occur because the particle is undergoing a trans-
formation from Cr7C3 carbide to a more stable Cr23C6 [10]. As shown in Thermo-Calc
calciulation on figure 4.23, Cr23C6 is more stable. Peaks in synchrotron results for this
type of precipitates are increasing in intensity much less than for other types. It can be an
indication that it takes longer time for Cr23C6 to form.

Conclusions and future work
• Precipitation initially starts at the grain boundaries and then continues to the centre
of the grains;
• Martensite is observed in the microstructure in not fully decomposed microstructure;
• Precipitates identified in the samples are Cr2N, (Cr2,5Fe4Mo0,1)C3 and Cr15,58Fe7,42C6;
• Precipitates Cr2N and M7C3 initially form in the steel after treatment, over time M7C3
transforms to M23C6;
• There is no considerable change in hardness measurements across the sample,
hardness results depend on the feature that is tested - precipitates and martensite
show higher hardness than austenite grains.
Future research should include statistical analysis of the precipitates. The trend for forma-
tion of different types can already be concluded but the fraction of the particles needs to be
determined. Those results need to be compared with various Thermo-Calc simulations.
Further investigation using TEM diffraction needs to be conducted in order to investigate
the transformation between different types of precipitates and their stability.
