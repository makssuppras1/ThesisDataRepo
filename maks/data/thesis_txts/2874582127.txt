Preface 
The rapid expansion of wind energy across Europe is a necessary step in the fight against climate 
change, but it also brings new challenges—particularly in balancing the need for renewable energy 
with the preservation of biodiversity. Having worked in the wind industry for several years, I am 
deeply passionate about its potential to shape a more sustainable future. At the same time, the ongoing 
biodiversity crisis and broader ecological concerns resonate strongly with me, and I felt a personal 
and professional responsibility to explore how these issues intersect. 
 
This project was born out of a desire to better understand one of the wind industry’s most pressing 
environmental drawbacks: its impact on birds and bats. While I was already aware of the general 
concerns surrounding wildlife and wind energy, I wanted to expand my knowledge and gain a clearer, 
evidence-based view of how these impacts are assessed, managed, and perceived in practice. 
 
My interest in this topic was further strengthened by focusing on Spain, France, and the 
Netherlands—countries I know well, having lived in each of them. This personal connection allowed 
me to appreciate their distinct regulatory frameworks and ecological contexts, enriching my analysis 
of how wildlife protection efforts are implemented and experienced on the ground. 
 
This thesis also marks the final step in my journey toward completing the Master of Wind Energy at 
the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It represents both an academic milestone and a synthesis 
of the skills and insights I have developed throughout the program. Through this work, I hope to 
contribute to the broader conversation on how the wind sector can continue to grow without 
compromising the ecosystems it ultimately seeks to protect.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Objectives of this study 
In recent years, wind energy development has expanded rapidly across Europe, becoming one of the 
most prominent tools in the transition toward a low-carbon future. At the same time, biodiversity is 
shrinking at an alarming rate, with many species, including birds and bats, facing growing threats 
from habitat loss, climate change, and human infrastructure. These parallel trends highlight a critical 
dilemma: while the fight against climate change demands a swift rollout of renewable energy, this 
same effort must avoid deepening the ongoing biodiversity crisis. In truth, the climate and biodiversity 
crises are not separate problems, but two sides of the same coin, both symptoms of an unsustainable 
relationship with the natural world that we are now trying to repair. 
 
This tension is especially visible in the case of wind energy, whose presence in increasingly diverse 
landscapes can lead to unintended consequences for local and migratory animal populations. 
Collisions with turbine blades, disturbance from noise, and degradation or fragmentation of habitats 
can significantly affect both sedentary species and migratory routes. Birds and bats are particularly 
vulnerable, and their protection is not merely a matter of ethics or conservation values; it is also an 
economic imperative, as growing research demonstrates the ecological and financial importance of 
preserving biodiversity and ecosystem services. 
 
Concerns about the impact of wind turbines on wildlife are often raised in public debates and can 
become central obstacles to the development of new projects. This report seeks to better understand 
these concerns by exploring the real-world impacts of wind energy on birds and bats in three European 
countries: the Netherlands, France, and Spain. Drawing on existing literature, stakeholder 
perspectives, and cross-country comparisons, the objective is to examine how different regulatory 
and mitigation approaches function in practice, what challenges they face, and how the industry can 
reconcile wildlife protection with continued growth. At the same time, this study reflects on how 
wildlife issues, and the ways they are handled, shape the social acceptance and public image of wind 
energy more broadly. 
1.2. 
Research questions 
To achieve its objectives, this study addresses the following research questions, while the first one 
is to be considered as the main research question, questions two to five also explore other aspects 
which ultimately provide more depth and details to the analysis: 
 
1. How do wind energy developments impact birds and bats in Spain, France, and the 
Netherlands, and to what extent do current assessment methods accurately capture these 
impacts and integrate the perspectives and concerns of relevant stakeholders? 
2. How do regulations structure the prevention of impacts on birds and bats in EU? 
3. How do different stakeholders perceive the need to protect birds and bats population in the 
frame of wind energy development? 
4. How are strategies for impacts mitigation defined and how do they vary across countries? 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
8
5. How do collaboration and research contribute to the development of knowledge for 
protecting birds and bats from wind energy impacts in Spain, France, and the Netherlands, 
and what opportunities exist for improving these processes? 
1.3. 
Outline of the study 
This report is drafted according to the following structure: 
- 
Chapter 2, the context: Background data are provided in the countries of study. 
- 
Chapter 3, the methodology: an overview of the methods used for this thesis are described. 
- 
Chapter 4: The impacts of wind energy on birds and bats are listed, further analysis inspired 
from the literature review are conducted, and the opinions from different stakeholders are 
outlined and compared. 
- 
Chapter 5: The regulations within the EU are listed and discussed, as well as their transposition 
at national and regional levels. The administrative structures of different countries and the 
potential implications on the application of the legislation are discussed. 
- 
Chapter 6: The perception of the impacts of wind energy on birds and bats is analysed from 
the point of view of different stakeholders. The role of the media and how they shape the 
opinion is also assessed. The levers of action of these same stakeholders are reviewed. 
- 
Chapter 7: The mechanisms which trigger the application of mitigation measures are listed 
and analysed, the different mitigation measures are listed, and their efficiency discussed 
depending on the species to be protected, their areas of application and the point of views of 
different experts. 
- 
Chapter 8: The types and degrees of collaboration between countries within the EU are 
identified. Relevant projects and leading countries are identified. The conditions and eventual 
benefits of harmonisation are discussed. 
- 
Chapter 9: A general conclusion is drawn based on the answers to the research questions. 
Improvements are suggested as part of the findings for this project. Finally, various directions 
are briefly suggested for further analysis. 
1.4. 
Perimeter of the study 
This study focuses on the impacts of wind energy on birds and bats in three countries which are 
considered representative of the development of wind energy in Western Europe: France, Spain and 
the Netherlands. 
 
These three countries present fundamental differences leading to several possibilities for a meaningful 
comparative analysis, which can be oriented through the prism of the abundance of certain species in 
these geographical areas, the differences in terms of timeline and typology of wind energy 
development and the administrative structures of these countries.

2. Methodology 
2.1. 
Process 
The process for carrying out this thesis is illustrated in the figure below, which shows a five-step 
methodology used for this stakeholder-based research project. 
 
 
Figure 1 - Illustration of the process followed for this project 
It begins with a literature review, where relevant academic sources are identified through tools such 
as Google Scholar, citation chaining, and conference listings to establish a foundational understanding 
of the topic. 
 
Following this, a list of stakeholders is compiled and categorized into four groups: 
- 
Group I: industry actors 
- 
Group II: associations 
- 
Group III: policymakers 
- 
Group IV: researchers 
 
In the interview phase, a set of tailored questions is prepared for each stakeholder group, and over 30 
stakeholders are contacted to participate. Each set of questions entails approximately 30 questions 
divided into five sections: 
- 
Section A: Wind Energy impacts 
- 
Section B: Regulatory pressure 
- 
Section C: Public opinion 
- 
Section D: Mitigation measures 
- 
Section E: Collaboration across countries 
 
In some cases, specific reduced sets of questions are designed in order to adapt the duration of the 
interview to the availability of the stakeholders. These reduced sets still keep the same structure 
divided in the above mentioned five sections. 
 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
10
The sets of questions are translated into the following languages: English for stakeholders in The 
Netherlands, Spanish for stakeholders in Spain and French for stakeholders in France. An example of 
a question is detailed in Annex A. 
 
After conducting the interviews, the transcripts (often between 40 and 60 pages) are summarized for 
each section (A to E) and translated into English when necessary to do so. 
 
These interviews are then subjected to analysis, which involves identifying recurring keywords and 
constructing comparison matrices to detect patterns across responses. For this purpose, all previously 
mentioned summaries are aggregated into a common excel file, and all aspects discussed in these 
interviews are tagged manually with a concept and a detailed concept. This step of manually tagging 
each part of the interviews appears mandatory since the attempt to conduct automatic searches of 
keywords in these interviews did not prove to be accurate enough; indeed, the same concepts are not 
addressed with the same wording by all stakeholders, and the translations of the interviews sometimes 
result in the use of different wording. A summary of the concepts and detailed concepts used for 
tagging the different parts of the interviews is presented in Annex B, wherein the numbers in front of 
each concept and detailed concept indicates the number of occurrences throughout the aggregated set 
of interviews. 
 
Finally, the insights are brought together in the consolidation phase, where findings are synthesized 
and compiled into this report. This structured process is aimed to ensure a systematic approach to 
stakeholder engagement and qualitative analysis of their answers.

3. Context 
As the European Union accelerates wind energy deployment to meet ambitious climate and energy 
targets, a crucial challenge arises: how to balance this rapid growth with the urgent need to protect 
biodiversity. Expanding wind farms can pose significant risks to bird and bat populations; these risks 
are particularly concerning given the documented decline of many species across Europe. 
 
This section aims to provide the reader with contextual elements regarding the development of wind 
energy, the decline of birds and bats species and the legal frame for preserving these species in the 
three countries of study.

in the following table and graph. While Spain had an earlier start of development, the country reached

approximately 30GW of installed capacity. France started later but showed a steady growth up to 
22GW installed capacity nowadays. Finally, the Netherlands exhibits a steady growth too but a lower 
rate, reaching around 10GW of installed capacity nowadays.

3.3. 
Why protecting birds and bats? 
Birds and bats play irreplaceable roles in maintaining ecological balance and supporting human 
economies, making their preservation not only an ethical imperative but a societal necessity. Ethically, 
these species have intrinsic value and a right to exist, and their rapid decline reflects our failure to 
safeguard biodiversity for future generations. Societally, both birds and bats protect public health by 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
19
controlling insect populations, reducing the spread of vector-borne diseases, and enhancing human 
well-being through recreation and nature appreciation. Environmentally, they are essential: birds aid 
in seed dispersal and pest control during the day, while bats continue these roles at night, contributing 
to forest regeneration, crop resilience, and insect population regulation. Scientifically, they are 
keystone species and bioindicators, offering critical insights into ecosystem health, flight dynamics, 
disease resistance, and climate adaptation. 
 
If these aspects weren’t enough, letting birds and bats populations decline also represent an 
economical cost: 
 
- 
The article (Cleveland & et al., 2006) estimates the bats’ value as pest control for cotton 
production in south-central Texas within a range of $121 000 to $1 725 000 per year, compared 
to a $4.6 to $6.4 million per year annual cotton harvest. 
- 
The article (Justin G. Boyles, 2011) analyses the value of bats as insectivorous for supporting 
the agriculture in the US and evaluates their contribution within a range of $3.7 to $53 
billion/year. 
- 
Finally, the paper (Wenny & et al., 2011) discloses that: 
o The replacement costs for the seed-dispersal services of Eurasian Jays in Stockholm 
National Urban Park would be $2115 to $9450 per ha for forest regeneration. 
o Replacing nutcracker seed-dispersal services costs the U.S. Forest Service a minimum 
of $2190 per ha in two national forests. 
o The human health costs attributable to population crashes of vultures in India amounts 
$34 billion over the years 1993-2006.

Regarding the network of Natura 2000 areas, installing wind farms in such areas is not prohibited in 
France, Spain or the Netherlands, but it is strictly regulated under the EU Habitats and Birds 
Directives. 
 
In France, onshore wind farms may be permitted in Natura 2000 areas, but projects face stringent 
scrutiny. A comprehensive Évaluation des incidences Natura 2000 must prove no adverse effects on 
protected habitats and species, particularly birds and bats. Permits can be annulled by courts if 
ecological assessments are incomplete or species protections are breached, as seen in recent rulings 
involving bat and raptor habitats. 
In Spain, installation is also allowed but requires a Natura 2000-specific analysis if the site falls 
within or near a designated zone. Courts have occasionally halted projects due to insufficient 
mitigation for avian species. For instance, the “Moeche” wind farm permit has been suspended by 
the Supreme Court in view of its partial location within a Natura 2000 area and the impacts on 
endangered species; this case is being highlighted in this article (El Tribunal Supremo suspende, de 
forma cautelar, la autorización del parque eólico Moeche, 2025). 
In the Netherlands, building in Natura 2000 areas is theoretically possible but highly constrained. A 
“passende beoordeling” (appropriate assessment) is required, and permits are only granted if no 
significant ecological impacts are expected or if overriding public interest and compensatory 
measures are clearly demonstrated. As an example, the Natura 2000 area of Veluwe will be exempted 
of wind farm installations within the reserve and 1km around it in view of the collision risk that wind 
turbines present for birds of prey; (No wind farms in Natura 2000 area Veluwe and limited in 
surrounding area, 2022). 
 
In all three countries, Natura 2000 designation significantly raises the legal and procedural thresholds 
for wind energy development.

4. Evaluating the impacts of wind energy on birds and bats 
Understanding the risks that wind energy poses to avian and bat populations requires reliable and 
species data on mortality. However, the availability, consistency, and scientific robustness of such 
data vary widely across regions and projects. Experts often stress that raw mortality numbers have 
limited value unless interpreted within the context of overall population sizes and ecological 
vulnerability. This study investigates how experts evaluate current data collection methods, the 
reliability of mortality estimates, and whether these are sufficient to guide targeted protection 
strategies and risk assessments for the most vulnerable species. 
4.1. 
What are the impacts 
Wind energy impacts birds and bats through several mechanisms. Collision mortality is the most 
direct, often affecting raptors, migratory birds, and fast-flying bats. Displacement and disturbance 
can lead to habitat loss as species avoid turbine areas due to noise, movement, or human presence. 
Barrier effects disrupt migration or commuting routes, increasing energy expenditure or fragmenting 
habitats. Rotor-induced turbulence may cause disorientation or barotrauma, particularly in bats. These 
impacts vary by species, site, and season, requiring tailored mitigation measures.

4.1.1. Collisions and Barometric traumatism 
The main impact is represented by the risk of collisions with the blades. Collisions with the tower, 
especially lattice structures, power lines or other structures in a wind farm also represent noticeable 
impacts but they are far behind. This impact is directly correlated with the avian density. 
 
Birds and bats may not detect turbine blades, especially in low light or foggy conditions. Fast-rotating 
blades are also difficult to perceive and avoid, particularly for species with low manoeuvrability 
(aquatic birds) or fast, straight flight paths (raptors). 
 
Barotrauma occurs when rapid pressure drops, such as those that occur near the trailing edge or tips 
of fast-moving turbine blades, cause gases inside body cavities (like lungs or sinuses) to expand or 
rupture tissues, leading to haemorrhaging or organ damage. 
 
Barotrauma in the context of wind farm impacts has been first documented in 2008 in Canada. This 
impact mainly affects bats due to their small size. There is not much evidence of this type of impact 
on birds; this can be explained by the fact that birds have a rigid lung-air sac system that is more 
pressure-tolerant than bat lungs. 
 
Collisions between birds or bats and wind turbine blades, and barotrauma, are well-documented and 
represented significant ecological issues, but these impacts vary widely depending on species, 
location, turbine design, and season. Variations between the flying behaviour of several species of 
birds and bats can be put in relation with the evolution of the size of the wind turbines in order to 
highlight this variability.

4.1.2. Habitat loss 
Habitat loss caused by wind farms is an important and sometimes underestimated factor affecting 
birds and bats. While most public attention focuses on collision risks, habitat loss and degradation 
can also have significant long-term consequences. Here's a breakdown of what it involves and its 
impact: 
 
When wind farms are built, they can lead to: 
- 
Direct habitat loss, when the physical space taken up by turbines, roads, substations, and 
infrastructure removes natural habitat. 
- 
Habitat degradation, when noise, movement, and human activities can reduce habitat quality 
and make nearby areas less suitable for sensitive species. 
- 
Habitat fragmentation, when roads and turbine clusters break up larger continuous habitats, 
affecting movement, migration, or feeding patterns. 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
33
- 
Functional habitat loss, when birds and bats avoid suitable habitats because of the presence of 
new infrastructures. 
 
As a result, birds may look for other nesting and breeding areas away from wind turbines, and they 
may abandon feeding areas. 
 
Bats often rely on hedgerows, forest edges, and wetlands that may be cleared or degraded, they may 
also lose their roosting areas. Bats also use linear landscape features (e.g., tree lines) for navigation. 
The fragmentation of the landscape can disrupt this. 
 
A recent article (Study measures seabirds’ habitat loss as a result of wind turbines, 2025), highlights 
that habitat loss is potentially much more detrimental to seabirds than fatalities that occur through 
collisions. 
 
Nevertheless, in many countries, the majority of wind turbines are installed in farmlands where the 
utility areas are shared with other human practices. The impact is therefore not neglectable but more 
limited. 
4.1.3. Disturbances 
Wind farms can displace birds and bats from nearby areas, effectively causing habitat loss. This 
displacement happens during both the construction and operation phases, they are caused by visual 
intrusion, noise, vibrations from turbines, human activity (e.g., maintenance), the occupation of 
surfaces, the construction of roads or parking, and also the creation of storage areas for components, 
for crane assembly, but also for topsoil during foundation excavation. 
 
Disturbances can occur during different phases including the installation, the operational phase and 
the decommissioning or repowering phases. Disturbances can force the birds and bats to frequent less 
an area or even to desert it. If deserting birds are not able to find an alternative area, their population 
might shrink. 
 
(DREWITT & et al., 2006) indicates that birds can be displaced due to disturbances up to 600–800 
meters for some wintering waterfowl (e.g., European White-fronted Geese, Pink-footed Geese). This 
study also highlights that disturbance levels depend heavily on species, site conditions, and life stage 
(e.g., breeding vs. wintering). Breeding birds may show less immediate displacement, possibly due 
to high site fidelity and long lifespans but long-term effects may still emerge over time. Short-lived 
species like grassland passerines have shown clear avoidance of turbines, with higher densities 
recorded further away. 
 
(Pearce-Higgins & et al., 2012) mentions that disturbance during construction, not turbine operation, 
is the primary driver of displacement. It also indicates that impacts were not correlated with turbine 
size or number, thereby suggesting that results are applicable across various wind farm types.

4.1.4. Barrier effect 
The barrier effect of wind turbines can be defined as the interference induced by the wind farm which 
affect the movement patterns of birds and bats, especially during migration, commuting, or foraging. 
This effect is distinct from direct mortality and functional habitat loss. Instead, it concerns how wind 
farms act as psychological or physical barriers which species may try to avoid. 
 
The barrier effect affects birds and bats as by altering their flight paths to avoid wind farms, this can 
result in an increase in energetic costs for long-distance migrants, it can also fragment flyways, 
especially in narrow migratory corridors or near the coasts. Flocks may split and even be excluded 
from entire landscapes. 
 
Then, the barrier effect does not cause immediate mortality but can reduce migration efficiency, limit 
access to roosting or foraging areas and cause energetic stress in long-distance migrants. 
 
The report (LPO, ONCFS, 2024), also explains that the barrier effect and the collisions are two 
different faces of a same medal. Birds and/or bats affected by barrier effect are usually scared away 
by wind turbines and are therefore not subjected to collisions. The example of Sandhill cranes is cited 
in this report; this migratory bird is considered to be affected by barrier effect, and until 2017 no 
carcasses of this of Sandhill crane had been found in France around wind turbines, and only 25 
carcasses appeared in Europe until 2019. This absence or very low number of collisions evidence 
support the hypothesis that this specie is scared away by wind turbines during migration.

4.1.5. Ground projection 
Due to rotor turbulences, birds and bats can be projected to the ground, leading to a death or injury 
originated by collision, not with the wind turbine itself but originated by the wind turbine. 
 
The birds and/or bats can be caught in the turbulence originated downstream of the wind turbine, in 
the rotor wake and can be destabilized in flight. This destabilization may cause a loss of control or a 
disorientation, leading to crashes into the ground, nearby structures, or even subsequent collision with 
turbine components. 
 
Bats and small birds can be affected during high wind conditions or during complex aerial 
manoeuvres (e.g. hunting or evading predators).  Large birds, such as raptors and/or aquatic birds, 
showing lower manoeuvrability can be projected downward with force. 
 
While empirical evidence is limited, this kind of impact is occasionally reported in forensic 
investigations or carcass monitoring, wherein post-mortem studies cannot definitively separate 
collision against a blade and a collision against the ground or nearby obstacles.

4.2.1. Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) 
An Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) is a formal process used to evaluate the potential 
environmental consequences of a proposed wind farm project before it is approved. The EIA identifies 
and assesses the specific impacts that a potential wind farm would have on the local populations. The 
EIA forms the legal and scientific basis for implementing protective measures, it ensures that potential 
biodiversity impacts are considered early in the planning phase, allowing authorities to enforce 
conditions that minimize harm to protected species. In the countries of study, the EIA is mandatory 
for wind energy projects and is a prerequisite for obtaining permits. 
 
Nonetheless, the EIA does not necessarily follow the same protocol everywhere; the content and focus 
of the EIA part dedicated to birds and bats might vary and it is usually framed by specific guidelines.

4.2.2. Post-Construction Fatality Monitoring (PCFM) 
A Post-Construction Fatality Monitoring (PCFM) program is a systematic method used to detect and 
quantify bird and bat mortality caused by operating wind turbines. Conducted after a wind farm 
becomes operational, PCFM involves regular field surveys to search for carcasses, assess mortality 
rates, and evaluate the effectiveness of mitigation measures. It typically includes standardized search 
protocols, correction factors for searcher efficiency and scavenger removal, and species 
identification. PCFM is essential for verifying the predictions made during the Environmental Impact 
Assessment (EIA) and ensures that actual impacts on wildlife are measured and addressed. If 
mortality exceeds acceptable rates, the results of PCFM can trigger adaptive management actions. As 
such, PCFM plays a critical role in the ongoing protection of birds and bats throughout the operational 
life of a wind farm.

4.3. 
Mortality estimations 
The estimation of bird and bat mortality linked to wind energy development relies on a variety of 
methodological and conceptual tools that aim to quantify impacts as accurately as possible, despite 
inherent uncertainties. Several core concepts shape this process. The precautionary principle 
underlines the need to err on the side of caution when data is limited or inconclusive, often guiding 
decision-making in favour of species protection. The 1% mortality rate is commonly used as a 
threshold, indicating that if estimated annual fatalities from a project exceed 1% of a species’ local or 
regional population, further action or mitigation may be required. The affordable mortality rate 
defines a level of mortality deemed acceptable for a given species without significantly impacting its 
population dynamics. More advanced approaches, such as Population Viability Analysis (PVA), 
model how added mortality from wind farms may affect species survival over time. Finally, 
translating raw carcass counts into annual mortality estimates typically involves correction formulas 
that account for biases in detection, scavenger removal, and searcher efficiency ensuring that the final 
figures more accurately reflect real impacts. 
4.3.1. Precautionary principle 
The precautionary principle is, according to the European Environment Agency, a “Principle adopted 
by the UN Conference on the Environment and Development (1992) (European Environment Agency, 
2000) that in order to protect the environment, a precautionary approach should be widely applied, 
meaning that where there are threats of serious or irreversible damage to the environment, lack of full 
scientific certainty should not be used as a reason for postponing cost-effective measures to prevent 
environmental degradation. The precautionary principle permits a lower level of proof of harm to be 
used in policymaking whenever the consequences of waiting for higher levels of proof may be very 
costly and/or irreversible.” 
 
It is supported by the concepts of uncertainty (unknown long-term effects, lack of evidence), potential 
damage irreversibility (impossibility to compensate), non-linear ecological systems (tipping point, 
butterfly effect), intergenerational responsibility (common patrimony, transmission), social 
acceptance (legitimacy, conflicts prevention), short-term thinking avoidance (immediate economic 
gain vs unknown risks). 
4.3.2. 1% mortality rate 
The 1% mortality rate is a threshold that defines the maximum number of individuals that can be 
removed from a population annually without impacting the preservation of the specie. 
This threshold is supposed to take into consideration the natural variability, it is considered as a 
precautionary and pragmatic approach. On the other side, it does not apply well to endangered 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
40
species, it requires knowledge of the population of a specie, it does not take into account cumulative 
impacts, and it is rather arbitrary. It is known to be used in conservation guidelines and industries 
such as powerlines management, fisheries, and wind energy. 
4.3.3. Affordable mortality rate 
It refers to the maximum level of mortality caused by human activities that a wildlife population can 
endure without suffering a significant long-term decline or jeopardizing its viability. The concept is 
rooted in population ecology and conservation biology, particularly in the study of population 
dynamics and demographic models. The goal is to identify mortality levels that do not exceed the 
population’s reproductive capacity and natural survival rates, ensuring population stability or growth. 
This requires knowledge of the population size, the reproductive rates, the natural mortality rates and 
other mortality sources. It often suffers from lack of data; it does not take into account cumulative 
effects, and it introduces a bias of societal consideration. 
4.3.4. Population Viability Analysis (PVA) 
PVA are quantitative modelling tools used to predict the likelihood that a population of a species will 
persist or go extinct over a given time period. It integrates demographic data such as birth rates, death 
rates, age structure, and environmental variability to simulate future population trajectories under 
different scenarios. 
 
Strengths: Predictive Power (long term trends, extinction risks), Scenario Testing (“what-if” 
scenarios, introduction of additional mortalities), Management Guidance (suitable for conservation 
strategies), Integration of Variability (incorporates environmental and demographic stochasticity), 
Customizability (tailored to specific species, populations, or sites). 
 
Limitations: Data-Intensive (Requires detailed and high-quality demographic data), Uncertainty 
(Results are sensitive to assumptions and parameter estimates), Simplifications (Often assumes closed 
populations, ignores connectivity), Complexity of the models (difficult to parameterize and interpret) 
Frequently used by government agencies, NGOs, and researchers worldwide. Increasingly integrated 
into impact studies for infrastructure projects like wind farms, roads, and dams. 
4.3.5. Extrapolations formulas

searches to obtain mortality estimations. These formulas use correction factors, among them: search 
efficiency bias, scavenger removal bias, search area bias, habitat bias, temporal bias and carcass 
decomposition bias.

Assessment methods for evaluating wind energy’s impacts on birds and bats vary across Spain, 
France, and the Netherlands, with each country facing key limitations. In Spain, the absence of 
country-wide standardized protocols and limited data sharing hinder impact assessment, and results 
vary sharply by region, raising questions about data reliability. Only 5 out of 17 autonomous 
communities have established protocols for bats, although a new national protocol is expected for 
these species. In France, while EIA and PCFM are often properly conducted and based on national

cumulative impacts remain under-addressed. In the Netherlands, PCFM protocols vary by project and

developers in these countries to ensure that enough and reliable data on birds and bats are collected 
during EIA in order to prevent the cancellation of the projects due to knowledge deficiency on certain 
species. 
 
All three countries share a common framework: EIAs typically combine visual observations with 
supplementary detection aids (SDA) such as radars, cameras, and acoustic sensors, while PCFM 
generally relies on systematic ground inspections, occasionally enhanced by SDA technologies. 
Across all three countries, stakeholders highlight the difficulty of detecting small birds and bats, and 
point to multiple factors, such as search conditions, seasonal timing, terrain, and scavenger activity 
that affect EIA and PCFM outcomes. Although emerging technologies (automatic detection systems 
in combination with artificial intelligence) are promising, human validation remains essential. 
Offshore assessments are particularly challenging, and overall, current EIA and PCFM practices are 
considered to underestimate the real extent of impacts on birds and bats. 
4.4.3. Contributions on mortalities estimations 
Common aspects regarding mortality estimations 
 
- 
Given the high number of parameters involved in mortality data collection and the 
significant variability among them, mortality estimates can be highly imprecise. 
- 
Mortality estimates should be used cautiously: on their own, they have limited utility. 
==PAGE:49==
 
 
47
- 
Mortality estimates can be used comparatively: to identify problematic wind farms and 
most lethal turbines, to monitor the evolution of mortality rates over time and evaluate the 
effectiveness of mitigation measures, to detect sudden spikes in mortality. 
- 
There are currently no clear, fixed thresholds to compare mortality data and determine what 
constitutes a concerning level. Where such thresholds exist, they are at best subjective. 
- 
Mitigation measures are generally triggered when mortality data is considered to pose a 
threat to the local population of a species, which in turn assumes a solid understanding of 
total population size in the area or the conservation status of the species. 
- 
It is possible to observe high mortality rates in some species without noticeable population 
impact, and conversely, even small losses can severely affect local populations. 
- 
The notion that significant mortality in certain species may not impact the broader 
population is controversial among some conservation groups, who argue that such 
conclusions do not support their conservation goals. 
- 
If correction factors for detectability or carcass disappearance (e.g., due to scavengers) are 
not applied, the resulting estimates may not reflect the real scale of the impact. 
- 
There is a clear correlation between more frequent and comprehensive surveys and higher 
recorded mortality figures. However, this does not necessarily indicate greater mortality, 
but rather improved detection capacity. 
- 
The lack of a common methodological foundation remains one of the main bottlenecks in 
the rigorous and comparative interpretation of mortality data. 
- 
There is no national mortality threshold defined for each species. It is up to the 
environmental consultants in charge of the Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) and 
post-construction follow-up monitoring (PCFM) to determine whether the observed 
mortality justifies mitigation or compensation measures.

thousands of dead bats per year.

absence of more precise, scientifically backed criteria, in order to obtain estimates.

Spain, France, and the Netherlands share several challenges in estimating bird and bat mortality from 
wind energy. Mortality estimates are highly variable and influenced by many factors, making them 
imprecise and difficult to compare. They are more useful for identifying trends or high-risk areas than 
as standalone data. No objective thresholds exist to define concerning mortality levels, and mitigation 
is usually based on assumptions about local species populations. The absence of standardized 
methods across countries, and the inconsistent use of correction factors (e.g., for carcass detectability 
or scavenging), often leads to underestimation. More frequent and thorough monitoring tends to 
reveal higher mortality, reflecting improved detection rather than greater impact.  
 
In Spain, there are no official nationwide mortality estimates for birds, with available figures mostly 
coming from extrapolations of a bird protection association which are based on a limited number of 
monitored wind farms. Scientific publications highlight particularly high bat mortality in southern 
regions, up to four times higher than in Germany, due to large local populations and daily inspection 
protocols, whereas northern farms often report little to no data, raising doubts about the reliability of 
their assessments. Carcass counts are sometimes used directly as mortality estimates, and the 1% 
criteria is occasionally used as a threshold for applying mitigation measures. In France, a national 
estimate of 70,000 bird deaths per year has been calculated based on systematic monitoring of a small 
number of wind farms. In some cases, mortality estimates are derived by multiplying observed 
carcasses by a factor of ten to account for detection limitations. When protected species are involved, 
specific mortality thresholds may be defined under derogation frameworks. In the Netherlands, the 
1% mortality threshold is also used sporadically, although it remains controversial and not 
consistently enforced across projects.

4.5. 
Findings about impact assessment 
This section of the report aims to answer the first research question: to examine the various impacts 
of wind turbines on bird and bat populations in France, Spain, and the Netherlands. It also looks at 
the methods used in these countries to assess such impacts and evaluates, based on the views of 
relevant stakeholders, whether these methods fulfil their purpose. 
 
To begin with, the different types of impacts have been listed. As mentioned in the technical literature 
and by most stakeholders during interviews, collisions (for both birds and bats) and barotrauma 
(mainly affecting bats) represent the most critical impacts, as they lead to direct fatalities. Bird and 
bat species are affected differently depending on a variety of factors. Flying behaviour plays a major 
role: species flying at altitudes that overlap with the rotor-swept area of turbines are more at risk. The 
ability of certain species to control their flight, or, on the contrary, their lack of manoeuvrability due 
to large wingspans, also influences vulnerability. Hunting patterns are another important factor: 
soaring birds, which focus on prey while flying, or high-flying bats hunting during insect migrations, 
are more likely to suffer from collisions or barotrauma. Large birds, such as raptors or aquatic birds, 
can also be projected to the ground due to downwind turbulence and be injured or killed without even 
colliding with a blade. The evolution of the size of wind turbines also has major implications in this 
type of impact with an increase in the blade tip speed, the rotor swept area and also the top height of 
the blade during its revolution.  
There is also a growing concern about habitat loss with some studies considering that it can be even 
more detrimental to seabirds than fatalities that occur through collisions. In countries and areas where 
offshore wind energy is under development, the assessment of this impact is therefore primordial. 
Disturbances originated from the start of the construction until the decommissioning of a wind farm 
also need to be assessed as they can affect different species depending on their degree of site fidelity, 
their lifespan and their life stage (breeding, wintering). The so-called barrier effect, originated by 
rows of wind turbines extending perpendicularly to migratory corridors can lead to exhaustion of 
species which are scared off by wind turbines. All these other impacts, despite being relevant, are 
much more difficult to assess since they do not lead to direct fatality. 
From the stakeholders’ interviews, it can be seen that the focus on certain impacts and species in the 
countries of study is put on the local populations of birds and bats, for instance raptors on Spain or 
aquatic birds in the Netherlands, and the local development of wind energy, onshore in Spain, offshore 
in the Netherlands, and a mix in France. More attention is also dedicated to birds than bats despite 
the very dense population of chiropters in Southern Europe.  
 
Procedures have been developed in order to assess such a wide panel of impacts which can affect so 
many different species. The EIA provides an early estimation of the potential impacts on local species 
before the permit delivery for constructing a wind farm, mitigation measures are applied when 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
51
required based on the outcome of the EIA. The knowledge of the local species, of their populations 
and of their behaviours is crucial for assessing the impacts properly on these species. Once the wind 
farm has been constructed and it has entered its operational phase, PCFM are performed to monitor 
these impacts and eventually update the mitigation measures. EIA and PCFM are mandatory in the 
countries of study, but important variations exist across countries in the enforcement of national 
guidelines for performing EIA and PCFM and in the way results are reported, and data are stored. 
France in this regard seems to be more advanced with national guidelines widely enforced. The 
interviewees underline the lack of standardisation, the lack of transparency and the bias in the method 
as the main obstacles to properly assess the impacts via EIA and PCFM, and the fact that barrier 
effects, habitat loss and cumulative effects are extremely difficult to address. Offshore, EAI and 
PCFM also presents additional logistical and technical challenges. The results of impacts assessment 
are therefore difficult to analyse and compare. 
 
The delivery of permits for constructing wind farms, the application of mitigation measures, or their 
adjustment, are decisions which rely on the thorough completion of these assessment methods. In 
view of the above-mentioned shortcomings in the realisation of EIA, the precautionary principle is 
often applied in France and in the Netherlands in case of lack of knowledge on the potential impacts 
on given species, not so much in Spain. When evaluating the outcomes of PCFM, correction factors 
for different bias need to be applied. Unfortunately, the lack of standardization in the method leads to 
the impossibility to apply scientific formulas for the obtention of mortality estimations, leading to the 
use of more arbitrary criteria instead. The 1% mortality rate or the affordable mortality rate are used 
as thresholds. Sometimes the carcass counts are used directly without extrapolation, or an arbitrary 
multiplier is applied to extrapolate the results. From the stakeholders’ point of view, the mortality is 
often underestimated by current methodologies and nationwide estimations often come from the 
extrapolations of few wind farms with robust monitoring. Mortality figures as standalone values are 
often useless and can only be used for comparison to highlight the more lethal wind farms or wind 
turbines, or seasonal peaks. 
 
These findings suggest that improvements could be implemented in order to better assess the impacts 
of wind energy on birds and bats. Increasing and sharing understanding of local species and their 
populations seems essential for better addressing the impacts at the early stage of EIA. This aspect 
seems even more important for bats species which suffer from a lack of knowledge in comparison 
with birds. Increasing public reporting and data storage would definitely go in that direction and this 
line of work has already been identified with the existence of DEPOBIO in France and projects led 
by the administrations and supported by the associations in the Netherlands and Spain for creating 
digital databases. Addressing the concerns of stakeholder regarding the lack of standardization of EIA 
and PCFM across wind farms would lead to the obtention of more comparable data, better analysis 
and transposition and, in fine, more robust decision processes for the delivery of permits and/or the 
application of mitigation measures. This improvement could be made via the mandatory use of 
guidelines enforced by the administration as it is happening in France. Finally, enhancing the 
collaboration between associations, developers, and regulatory entities, has been identified by most 
stakeholders as the angular stone for improving impacts assessment on birds and bats. The positive 
outcomes of projects led by RGI and BWEC meant to foster this type of collaboration in different 
ambits illustrate the potential of this measure. 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
52
 
In conclusion, wind energy developments in Spain, France, and the Netherlands affect birds and bats 
primarily through collisions, barotrauma, habitat loss, and disturbance, with specific species impacted 
depending on local conditions, flying behaviour, and turbine characteristics. It appears that the focus 
in each country is shaped by the local development of wind energy and the species most affected. 
While all three countries employ Environmental Impact Assessments (EIA) and Post-Construction 
Fatality Monitoring (PCFM) to evaluate these impacts, France enforces standardized guidelines more 
rigorously, resulting in more consistent data collection and public reporting. In contrast, Spain and 
the Netherlands often rely on non-mandatory or locally applied guidelines, which contributes to 
methodological variability and data gaps. Stakeholders across the board express concern over 
underestimated mortality rates and a lack of data transparency. These shortcomings limit the accuracy 
and usefulness of current assessment methods, especially offshore which presents additional practical 
challenges. Ultimately, improving species-specific knowledge (especially for bats), standardizing 
methodologies, mandating public data sharing, and fostering collaboration among stakeholders are 
seen as key to better assessing and mitigating the impacts of wind energy on avian and chiropteran 
populations.

5. Analysis of the regulation for protecting birds and bats 
This chapter explores how existing regulations, and policymaking frameworks influence the 
prevention of wind energy impacts on birds and bats within the EU. By examining national 
approaches in Spain, France, and the Netherlands, it assesses how legal requirements and enforcement 
mechanisms try to balance the need for renewable energy growth while protecting birds and bats. 
5.1. 
Stakeholder’s contributions 
The stakeholder’s contributions are divided into four categories for a better assessment: their 
contributions regarding the current regulation, the enforcement of the regulation, the balance between 
wind energy growth and wildlife protection, and finally the perception that they have of the pressure 
exerted by the regulation on the sector. 
5.1.1. Contributions regarding the current regulation 
Key contributions regarding the current regulation in Spain 
 
- 
The regulations that govern the evaluation processes of wind energy projects do not 
establish a minimum quality standard EIA conducted by developers, which can be poor and 
inaccurate. 
- 
The current state of regulation in Spain and its various regions is perceived as very 
inconsistent. Spain is composed of 17 autonomous communities, which in practice behave 
like different countries. 
- 
The regulatory variability between autonomous communities is largely due to the 
commitment and rigor of the early teams responsible for defining and implementing 
environmental guidelines in each region. For example, the presence of biologists and 
ornithologists in these initial teams likely contributed to early awareness of the issue. 
However, this variation is ultimately seen as arbitrary and inconsistent. 
- 
The granting of a license to install a wind farm depends greatly on the individual reviewing 
the information and the criteria vary widely because each autonomous community has 
different personnel with different requirements. 
- 
The goal should not be to regulate “more,” but to regulate “better”: using technically sound, 
coherent, and territorially integrated criteria across all administrations. Such an approach 
would greatly facilitate compliance and enhance the environmental quality of wind energy 
projects. 
 
Key contributions regarding the current regulation in France 
 
- 
When an impact study identifies a risk to a protected species, a derogation for protected 
species is required under Article L. 411-2 of the Environmental Code. 
- 
Protocols for impacts assessment are regularly updated through more advanced directives. 
- 
EIA are essential for determining endangered species and the subsequent inspection 
parameters. But in practice, it is the developer who chooses the agency in charge of carrying 
out the EIA, leading to potential conflicts of interest.

Key contributions regarding the current regulation in the Netherlands 
 
- 
The national water plan gives the national overall policy in terms of environment protection. 
- 
Emphasis is put on compliance with Natura 2000 areas. The government also uses 
sensitivity maps and strategic area planning, but enforcement varies across projects and

In addition to the Natura 2000 areas, most of the provinces have areas designated for

Current legislation for protecting birds and bats from wind energy impacts varies significantly across 
Spain, France, and the Netherlands. In Spain, there is no national minimum standard for EIAs, 
resulting in inconsistent practices across its 17 autonomous communities, each applying its own rules, 
some benefiting from experienced local authorities. The challenge lies not in increasing regulation 
but improving its coherence and quality. In France, EIAs are considered as crucial for identifying 
endangered species and guiding mitigation efforts, with regular protocol updates. However, 
developers selecting their own assessment agencies can raise concerns over impartiality. A derogation 
is required if significant impacts on protected species are expected. In the Netherlands, the National 
Water Plan frames environmental policies, with strong emphasis on Natura 2000 sites and additional 
protected areas designated by provincial governments.

5.2. 
Findings about regulation 
This section aims to understand how the regulation is designed for the protection of birds and bats. 
The analysis combines a review of legal frameworks at EU, national, and regional levels with an 
overview of the administrative structures in France, Spain, and the Netherlands. It also incorporates 
stakeholder insights on the content, enforcement, and perceived balance of current regulations, and 
their broader implications for the wind energy sector. 
 
The regulation for protecting birds and bats in EU is defined by a mix of directives, international 
guidelines and lists of endangered species, which then translate into the national frames of each 
country with certain variations. At EU level, the regulation might not answer to the specificities of 
certain regions, such as addressing local species or considering the local density of wind energy 
development. These aspects are left to the appreciation of the countries or sometimes the regions, 
thereby creating disparities. The same happens with Natura 2000 areas, wherein the installation of 
new wind farm infrastructures can be allowed in the countries of study, although with greater 
scrutiny and specific assessment. This aspect illustrates how variations in the regulations can be 
added by changing its level of application. 
The countries of study all have different degrees of centralization in their administrative structure, 
ranking from France being the most centralized, then the Netherlands, and finally to Spain. The 
wind energy competencies being, as expected, heavily concentrated at national level in France, 
although with regional antennas of the administration. Wind energy competencies are delegated to 
the provinces for smaller onshore projects in the Netherlands, and to the autonomous communities 
for mid-sized onshore projects in Spain. The development of regional competencies in Spain and 
the Netherlands can be seen as an opportunity to better address local concerns, but also as a risk of 
creating disparities within a same country. 
The contributions of the stakeholders regarding the current regulations in the countries of study reveal 
that, in Spain inconsistent practices exist between communities and the goal should be to improve the 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
61
coherence of the different regional regulations instead of increasing it. In France, the EIA protocol, 
which is seen as a crucial tool for protecting birds and bats, benefits from regular updates and in case 
of severe impacts identified on protected species, the national law provides a framework for a 
derogation procedure. In the Netherlands, a specific structure is provided with the National Water 
Plan for designing the environmental regulations and a strong emphasis is made in respecting Natura 
2000 areas. These aspects highlight the effects of administrative decentralisation in different countries 
and its influence on the corresponding regulations. 
The enforcement of bird and bat protection regulations also varies between countries. The 
administrative capacity to treat licensing procedures swiftly is seen as an asset in this regard. In Spain, 
delegating competencies to regional level can be productive, especially in the case of experienced 
local administrations, but it is not always the case, and it also increases the risk of opportunism to 
bypass more stringent regulations. In France, despite benefitting from robust EIA procedures, some 
projects still lack proper assessments (the agencies are often chosen by the developers and some 
regional variability can be observed), and the belief that the law is not uniformly applied is strong. 
Finally in the Netherlands, the enforcement of the regulations seems to vary between provinces.  
Gaps between the existing regulations and their enforcement throughout the projects is therefore 
highlighted in the three countries of study. 
Balancing wind energy development with the protection of birds and bats is a common concern in 
EU which is voiced by several stakeholders in the three countries of study. Either by expressing their 
concerns about new EU directives, by acting as referee between developers and naturalist 
associations, or by highlighting the dual objectives of developing renewable energy via public 
tenders. This needs to address both the climate crisis and the crisis of biodiversity loss illustrates the 
duality of satisfying the regulations for protecting bids and bats and the national commitments of 
developing wind energy. 
Finally, the way regulatory pressure for protecting birds and bats from wind energy impacts is 
perceived varies greatly among stakeholders. Developers are concerned by the lack of new 
development areas, the increase of stringent biodiversity regulations and the pressure exerted by 
policymakers and NGOs. In the worst case, regulation is seen as a burden, while some developers 
have a more standardized approach and integrate it as early as possible based on a risk-analysis. NGOs 
and associations advocate not necessarily for more regulation but for a stricter application, and they 
also observe that the regulatory pressure varies depending on the presence of protected areas. 
Policymakers, as well as Researchers and academics, acknowledge the common distrust between the 
two former groups, although with different degrees between the countries, with a strong emphasis in 
Spain and very few in the Netherlands. These different points of view could be summarized as two 
antagonist positions (NGOs and developers) with a referee (policymakers and researchers). Although 
when analysed in regard to the regulation itself, we notice that the more consistent the regulation is, 
the less conflicting are the positions of the antagonists. 
 
Based on the findings, it can be concluded that the regulation structuring the prevention of impacts 
on birds and bats in the EU is marked by significant variation between the EU, national, and 
regional levels. While the EU provides a common legal framework through directives, national and 
especially regional authorities implement these regulations differently, particularly in countries with 
strong decentralisation. This leads to uneven enforcement and varying levels of protection. 
Moreover, a gap often exists between the content of the regulation and its actual application in the 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
62
field. National commitments to develop wind energy can also come into tension with biodiversity 
protection goals, creating challenges for consistent implementation. However, in contexts where the 
regulation is more coherent and uniformly applied stakeholders report fewer conflicts, suggesting 
that regulatory consistency fosters better cooperation and more balanced development.

6. Analysis of the public opinion on wind energy and its impacts 
Public perception plays a pivotal role in the acceptance of wind energy projects, especially when 
environmental trade-offs, such as impacts on birds and bats, come into play. Media narratives, NGO 
campaigns, and misinformation can all shape these perceptions, often with tangible effects on policy 
decisions and project approvals. This chapter focuses on how experts perceive the public’s 
understanding of wildlife impacts, the extent of misinformation, and the influence of public concern 
on shaping the trajectory of wind energy development.

France, biodiversity is more commonly used as a political tool, with some actors invoking birds and 
bats protection in bad faith to block wind development. The Netherlands also presents a polarized 
debate, where high public support for wind energy coexists with growing concerns over ecological 
impacts, particularly in coastal and Natura 2000 areas. Legal and public opposition has significantly 
shaped policy, especially against onshore wind projects, where biodiversity protection is often 
invoked alongside other arguments. Across countries, the public increasingly demands that climate 
action does not come at the expense of wildlife, highlighting the need for transparent, evidence-based 
communication and early stakeholder engagement to build trust and legitimacy in wind energy 
development. 
6.3. 
Findings about the opinion of the stakeholders 
Understanding how different stakeholders perceive the need to protect bird and bat populations in the 
context of wind energy development requires examining the broader communication and awareness 
landscape. Media plays a central role in shaping both public and industry perceptions, influencing 
how risks are framed and prioritized. Industry awareness of biodiversity impacts is evolving, often in 
response to public scrutiny. Meanwhile, public awareness varies widely, shaped by media narratives 
and visibility of local projects. These elements converge in an increasingly active public debate, 
where conflicting interests and values emerge, highlighting the complexity of reconciling wind 
energy goals with birds and bats protection. 
 
Articles reporting bird and bat mortalities linked to wind energy vary from scientific to dramatic 
statements, but their increasing number, at least for birds who benefit from more media coverage 
highlight a growing concern for the wind energy industry expressed by its impact on the public 
opinion. 
The industry awareness regarding the impacts of wind energy on these populations is perceived 
differently by the stakeholders in this field. Industry players tend to communicate on these issues 
trying to show environmental convictions, but NGOs and naturalist associations see it often as tainted 
of greenwashing; the main priority according to this group of stakeholders remains the economical 
profit at the expense of wildlife preservations. It is also to be noted that the wind energy sector 
concentrates a high density of professionals with a genuine personal interest in ecological matters, 
including the preservation of wildlife, even though their level of knowledge on the impacts of birds 
and bats is still developing. 
According to the stakeholders, the public awareness on these issues remains limited and uneven 
across regions, with antagonist positions between rural and urban areas. On one side of the spectrum, 
some iconic bird species focus more attention, and on the other side, bats are often ignored. A lack of 
knowledge is often identified on these questions. Therefore, the role of NGOs, as well as effective 
communication based on scientific data, are crucial in raising awareness in order to improve the 
==PAGE:75==
 
 
73
understanding of the public and to structure its opinion around the question of balancing wind energy 
development with wildlife preservation.  
The public debate on the development of wind energy in view of the impacts on birds and bats is 
complex, it is also influenced by regional disparities, such as the density of existing wind farms in 
the area, the political context, the local opposition. Some actors invoke wildlife protection in bad faith 
in order to oppose wind energy development by all means. As a general trend and in view of both, the 
contributions of the interviewees and the growing weight of these issues in the media, the public is 
increasingly concerned about the impacts of wind energy, but its opinion can be biased or not evidence 
based. 
 
In conclusion, stakeholders show differing perceptions of the need to protect bird and bat populations 
in the context of wind energy development. The media significantly shape public perception, 
indirectly influencing industry attitudes. While the industry acknowledges the issue, it still tends to 
prioritize economic factors over biodiversity concerns. The public shows signs of concern but often 
lacks accurate knowledge, with opinions sometimes driven by local or political dynamics. These 
findings highlight the need for improved communication strategies and transparent dialogue to bridge 
gaps in understanding and foster more balanced, informed stakeholder engagement around wildlife 
protection in wind energy planning.

7. Evaluating current efforts to protect birds and bats 
As wind energy expands, so too do efforts to mitigate its impact on wildlife. These range from 
curtailment strategies to emerging deterrent technologies, yet their adoption and effectiveness vary 
across projects and countries. This study collects expert insights on which mitigation measures are 
currently favoured, what challenges hinder their implementation, and which practices are considered 
most promising in reducing bird and bat mortality. The goal is to highlight both existing gaps and 
innovative approaches in operational mitigation. 
7.1. 
The Avoid-Reduce-Compensate procedure 
The Avoid–Reduce–Compensate (ARC) procedure, also referred to as the mitigation hierarchy, forms 
the cornerstone of the European Union’s approach to managing the environmental impacts of 
infrastructure developments, including wind energy projects, on biodiversity. This procedure is rooted 
in the Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) (THE COUNCIL OF THE EUROPEAN COMMUNITIES, 
1992) and the Birds Directive (2009/147/EC) (THE EUROPEAN PARLIAMENT AND THE 
COUNCIL OF THE EUROPEAN UNION, 2009) and aims to protect biodiversity by first avoiding 
ecological harm wherever possible, then reducing unavoidable impacts, and finally compensating for 
residual effects through ecological restoration or offsetting. 
 
Avoidance and reduction are considered mitigation measures, as outlined in the European 
Commission’s Guidance Document on Wind Energy Developments and EU Nature Legislation 
(European Commission, 2020). When residual adverse effects on biodiversity, particularly within 
Natura 2000 sites, cannot be fully mitigated, compensation measures become necessary. These are 
legally defined under Articles 6(3) and 6(4) of the Habitats Directive (92/43/EEC) (THE COUNCIL 
OF THE EUROPEAN COMMUNITIES, 1992) and are further detailed in the Annex to the 
Commission Notice on the Assessment of Plans and Projects in Relation to Natura 2000 Sites 
(EUROPEAN COMMISSION, 2021).  
 
The ARC framework is closely aligned with the EU’s “no net loss of biodiversity” (Institut for 
European Environmental Policy, 2020). This principle aims to balance development with ecological 
sustainability by ensuring that any residual biodiversity losses are fully compensated, thereby 
achieving a net neutral or positive outcome. 
 
The ARC procedure is then transposed into the national legislations of each EU country, included the 
countries of study.

7.1.1. Avoidance and Reduction measures 
Avoidance and Reduction measures are now presented in more details. 
7.1.1.1. 
Macro-siting 
According to the Guidance document on wind energy developments and EU nature legislation 
(European Commission, 2020), for habitats affected by onshore wind energy development, the 
appropriate siting of wind farms, including access roads, is the most effective way to avoid significant 
effects on protected habitats. This can be best achieved by using strategic planning over a broad 
geographical area, at administrative, regional, national or even international level. 
Macro-siting appears to be more challenging for bats, because the most affected bat species tend to 
be common and widespread, rather than habitat specialists. The extent to which macro-siting can play 
a role in bat conservation in practice is therefore not totally clear. 
7.1.1.2. 
Micro-siting 
Micro siting consists in considering the finding of the EIA when designing the arrangement of the 
wind turbines in a specific wind farm. The following main considerations are often contemplated:  
- 
Wind turbines should not be arranged near to wildlife habitats, such as cliffs and ridges for 
raptors, or hedgerows for bats. 
- 
An arrangement in rows is preferable to arrangements following specific patterns, such as in 
X, L or Y, which might force birds and bats to fly closer to wind turbines at some point, or a 
clustered layout surrounding an area which would restrict the access to the central zone. 
- 
In case of wind farms arranged across migratory routes, the rows of wind turbines should 
ideally be parallel to the flying corridors. 
- 
The distance between wind turbines is also important and shall be chosen according to the 
specific identified impacts: 
o An increased distance favours local birds providing them with more space for 
navigating between wind turbines. 
o A smaller distance between wind turbines provides a more compact design to the wind 
farm and is visually easier to detect, but it generates a barrier effect for migratory 
species who tend to circumvent the whole wind farm. 
- 
In case of several wind farms implemented in a same area, several kilometres distance should 
be ensured between wind farms to avoid cumulative and/or barrier effects.

7.1.1.3. 
Scheduling 
According to (European Commission, 2020), scheduling aims to avoid or reduce the disturbance and 
displacement of birds during certain critical periods. It may be of most use during construction, 
repowering and decommissioning, rather than during operation. Scheduling means that activities are 
either suspended or reduced during ecologically sensitive periods. Another option is to phase the 
activities, so that they continue but only at less sensitive locations. This can be done by using existing 
ecological knowledge of the species likely to be present at the wind energy development, baseline 
field survey data or operational monitoring data. It is common practice to undertake potentially 
disturbing activities during periods where sensitive and vulnerable species are absent.  
 
At the time of publication of this document (2020), no examples were known of offshore wind farms 
where scheduling measures were applied. For offshore wind, the ability to schedule to avoid effects 
is very limited, largely due to the scale of construction and the likely construction timetable. The 
increased capability of construction vessels also means that weather is largely the only constraint to 
construction offshore.  
 
Specifically for bats, the EUROBATS guidelines (EUROBATS, 2014) provide guidance on 
scheduling construction activities: 
- 
Avoid the vicinity of occupied hibernacula and nursery roosts and the time of year when they 
are used. 
- 
In general, avoid the time of day and year when bats are actively foraging and commuting. 
- 
Phase the activities so that the entire site is not subjected to disturbance at the same time 
- 
Phase the activities so that the programme for certain disturbing activities or construction of 
certain areas within the development occurs when bats are least sensitive to the disturbance.

The EUROBATS guidelines further mention that in order for these measures to be effective, it is 
essential to have a full understanding of the location and use of bat roosts and flight activity over the 
entire zone of influence of the wind energy development. 
7.1.1.4. 
Habitat management 
Habitat management considers the two following aspects which must go hand in hand: 
- 
Increasing off-site attractiveness, by restoring and conserving environments outside the wind 
farm (i.e. restocking with rabbits to attract raptors). 
- 
Decreasing site attractiveness, ensuring that site development does not lead to increased 
attractiveness for target species, by implementing appropriate vegetation cover management. 
To reduce the attractiveness of the site, it is necessary to reduce the quality of the habitats 
present, particularly those used by avifauna and chiropterans, but also by their prey.  
 
Taking all species into account is almost impossible, some measures might be effective for some 
species and counter-productive for others (sparse vegetation may reduce chiropteran activity, but at 
the same time may attract birds of prey; and vice versa with tall, dense vegetation). An assessment of 
the local species shall therefore be conducted beforehand.

7.1.1.5. 
Deterrents 
Deterrent systems are designed to reduce the impact of wind energy on birds and bats by repelling 
animals that come too close to operating wind turbines. These systems typically rely on two main 
components: an automatic detection system (radar, camera) that must be continuously active and 
mounted on the wind turbine, and a deterrent mechanism that uses acoustic, visual, or olfactory 
signals to scare animals away. 
Among the various options, acoustic and visual deterrents, already in use in fields like agriculture and 
aviation, have shown the most promise. 
For birds, traditional acoustic deterrents often lose effectiveness over time due to habituation, though 
bioacoustics signals mimicking birds of prey are more resistant to this effect. Visual deterrents, such 
==PAGE:83==
 
 
81
as mirrors and reflective surfaces, work well during daylight, while UV and violet-sensitive lighting 
can be effective at night for certain migratory species. 
Ultrasound systems have proven effective for bats by disrupting their echolocation, but their range is 
limited (around 15 meters) and can be affected by humidity. These systems must emit at variable 
frequencies to prevent bats from adapting. 
Other deterrent options like electromagnetic or chemical signals, scarecrows, and kites are generally 
ineffective or unsafe. 
7.1.1.6. 
Timing of turbine operation 
(ONCFS, LPO, 2019) explains how the measures relying on adjusting the timing of wind turbine 
operations incorporates curtailment procedures, i.e. programmed shutdowns of wind turbines 
depending on weather conditions, season or even the direct proximity of a bird or bat. This type of 
measure is often referred to as curtailment. 
 
There are two main categories of curtailment: 
- 
Presence (or dynamic) based control: the wind turbine is stopped when a bird or bat is detected 
nearby. 
- 
Ambient (or static) based control: the operation of the wind turbine depends on the period of 
the year, the time of the day, the meteorological conditions, and the influence of these 
parameters on the birds and bats. 
 
Agricultural control is also a category where the wind turbines are stopped depending on the activity 
realised in farmlands on site which might attract bird populations. This type of control also depends 
on the cooperation of farmers for notifying the wind farm operators. It falls in the category of ambient 
based control but influenced by human activities. 
 
Human sentinels can also monitor the presence of birds, this method requires a consequent manpower 
investment and is one of the less effective. Although it can be applied during a reduced period and 
relying on the expertise of aviary experts (ornithologists). It falls in the category of presence-based 
control but relying on human observations. 
 
The presence-based control measures are limited by the accuracy of the automatic detection systems 
which might generate false alarms, and the time required for stopping a wind turbine. Although these 
technologies are improving, and therefore such methods offer improving results. 
The installation of a presence-based control system is to be assessed in regard to the following criteria: 
the species on site (the size of the birds shall be consequent enough to be detected), the frequency of 
passage, the economic rentability in regards to the cost of the system and the loss of production when 
compared to a more prolonged stop of the wind turbines as for ambient based control measures. 
 
Ambient conditions control measures are highly effective for chiropters, which activity is highly 
related to ambient conditions. Bats are more active during the first hours of the night, and during the 
period spanning between Summer and Fall. Wind speed slower than 5-6m/s and, temperatures higher 
==PAGE:84==
 
 
82
than 8-10°C also favour their level of activity. The cloud coverage, the humidity and the moon phase 
are also important factors. 
 
 
Figure 21 – Bat activity versus wind speed superimposed with power curves (Voigt & et al., 2015) 
Modifying the value of cut-in wind speed for the wind turbines can be a very effective solution for 
both, the developers or wind farm owners and bats populations, since the power production at these 
wind speeds is also low. This type of measure could also lower the fatigue on the wind turbine 
structure by limiting the number of start and stop of the wind turbine in low conditions. Nevertheless, 
modern wind turbines having improved power production at low wind speeds might suffer from 
financial losses due to this control method. 
 
Migratory birds have been studied extensively and benefit from international cooperation in terms of 
radar surveillance. The time of departure of a bird flock or swarm can easily be identified and 
therefore the approximate time when it will reach a given area as well. The altitude of flight is 
influenced by the Ambiental conditions, Therefore the presence of migratory birds in wind farms 
areas, as well as their flying altitude at an eventual collision height, can be easily predicted. Wind 
farms can also be controlled according to these variables to attenuate the mortality of migratory 
species. 
 
It is worth noting that the specific activity parameters of specific species vary from wind farm to wind 
farm and therefore should be analysed carefully before applying such methods. 
7.1.1.7. 
Dismantling and relocating 
Installing wind turbines at new locations or increasing the height of the tower, or the length of the 
blades, leads to a new or updated EIA. In the case of older wind farms or projects, wherein incomplete 
EIA had been performed, the extension or repowering of the wind farm is an opportunity to improve 
the mitigation measures on wildlife. 
==PAGE:85==
 
 
83
The wind farms concerned by repowering are often among the oldest ones; these wind farms have 
been developed before or shortly after 2000 at a time when environmental considerations were less 
important. Repowering or extending these wind farms might therefore have a greater impact than 
developing new ones taking into consideration the latest qualifications of protection areas. 
Finally, in the case of repowering an existing site with wind turbines of greater size, the increase in 
tower height and rotor diameter shall be considered. 
Additionally, according to the Guidance document on wind energy developments and EU nature 
legislation, for bird and bat species, decommissioning can be an effective measure in reducing 
collision risk. Where monitoring identifies that one or more turbines are responsible for an unforeseen 
but significant effect, a review by the competent national authority should include the possibility of 
decommissioning or relocating of these turbines. 
7.1.1.8. 
Dismantling and restoration 
Dismantling and restoration includes removing all indices of human presence from the wind farm 
site, including the access roads and the foundations of the wind turbines. An ecological restauration 
of the site might also be necessary. The provision of decommissioning costs is the guarantee for the 
necessary fulfilment of these operations.

7.2.1. Contributions regarding the ARC procedure 
Common aspects regarding the ARC procedure 
 
- 
For birds and bats, macro siting appears to be the most effective way to ensure lower 
mortality levels in a given area; this would include abandoning initial locations or seeking 
alternative sites for a wind farm. This approach would benefit all stakeholders including 
developers. 
- 
Installing wind farms in agricultural plains where biodiversity is at its lowest, avoiding bird 
and bat migration corridors and passage zones, and far from hedgerows, might be 
considered one of the best practices. 
- 
For bats, timing of turbine operations during low wind is considered to be one of the most 
effective mitigation measures. The turbine curtailment should be considered between cut-
in wind speed and 6 m/s, because bats and insects only fly in light winds. This would likely 
reduce mortality by 50-90% with an energy loss around 1%. 
- 
Seasonal curtailment by stopping turbines during peaks of bird’s migrations or bats activity 
is also considered as a very effective mitigation measure.

7.2.2. Contributions regarding challenges in applying the 
ARC procedure 
Common aspects regarding the challenges with the implementation of the ARC procedure 
 
- 
The cost of automatic detection systems is one of the main obstacles to the implementation 
of dynamic curtailment, especially if it must be incorporated into a project after construction 
and was not included in the initial budget. 
- 
The production losses due to turbine curtailments are also very costly, particularly during 
peak bat activity (July to October). 
- 
Many developers are unwilling to bear these costs unless explicitly required by the 
authorities. 
- 
Integration of automatic detection systems into new turbines is complex, and even more so 
for older turbines already in operation. 
- 
Another significant obstacle for the implementation of ARC measures is regulatory, with 
the need to demonstrate that these measures are effective enough to be included in the ARC 
sequence.  
 
Key aspects regarding the challenges with the implementation of the ARC procedure in Spain 
 
- 
According to a conversationist association for bats, the main obstacles to implement 
mitigation measures lie with the administration. If regulation is passed, the sector will have 
to comply. It is also necessary to ensure that financial concerns never override the 
precautionary principle. 
- 
Spain has about 23,000 wind turbines across roughly 1,400 wind farms. But mitigation 
measures are not applied to older wind farms, and cannot be applied retroactively, because 
those projects were authorized under specific conditions. The authorities do not want to 
face legal challenges they would likely lose. 
- 
Currently, only about 150 wind farms (less than 10% of the national total) benefit from 
curtailment measures to protect bats. 
- 
Although there is goodwill within the renewable energy sector in Spain, collaboration 
among all parties involved in the protection of birds and bats is not always easy. 
- 
The experience of developers and manufacturers with automatic detection systems is 
limited and generally quite negative. These systems can be highly effective at detecting

7.3. 
Findings about the current efforts to protect birds 
and bats 
To address the research question "How are strategies for impact mitigation defined and how do they 
vary across countries?", this analysis begins by examining the conceptual and legal foundations of 
the Avoid / Reduce / Compensate (ARC) framework, which forms the backbone of environmental 
mitigation strategies in wind energy development. First, the definition of the ARC procedure and its 
legal embedding in national and European regulatory contexts is explored to clarify how mitigation 
obligations are framed. Second, a closer look is taken at the specific measures falling under each 
category, highlighting the diversity of tools and practices. Finally, the perspectives of key stakeholders 
are analysed to understand how ARC measures are applied in practice and what major obstacles 
hinder their effective implementation. These insights offer a comparative understanding of how 
mitigation strategies differ across the countries studied and what factors shape their design and 
execution. 
 
The ARC procedure outlines the basis of protection measures for birds and bats from the impacts of 
wind energy, the procedure is defined at EU level in the Habitats and Birds directives, with clear 
transposition in national laws of the countries of study. An EU guidance document describes the 
avoidance and reduction measures which form the compensation measures, while compensation 
measures are described in an annex to the Habitat Directive as necessary to apply if compensation 
measures do not lead to a “no net loss” of biodiversity at least. There is therefore a clear structure for 
the implementation of protection measures for the protection of birds and bats. 
The most commonly applied compensation measures are well documented, but their effectiveness is 
highly variable. Macro siting appears challenging for bats, while the application of micro siting might 
clash with other requirements (wind resources, orography). Scheduling is also challenging, especially 
offshore due to weather conditions, and habitat management offers significant difficulties as it cannot 
take all species into account. Some deterrent technics give good results despite the habituation effect. 
But the most effective measure remains the curtailment of wind turbines, especially for bats with a 
high decrease in mortality associated to little production losses, and migratory species which are 
predictable and can be easily detected on their migratory path. 
Compensation measures are also well defined in the technical literature with clear examples of their 
application. The need to apply them on a standard basis is also highlighted in view of the remaining 
character of the impacts and the fact that the extent of these measures is difficult to quantify. 
According to stakeholders, macro siting is the most effective strategy to reduce bird and bat mortality. 
Turbine shutdowns during low wind conditions are also very effective, especially for bats. 
Nevertheless, variations in the application of curtailment strategies can be appreciated between 
countries, their implementation being more limited in Spain due to administrative issues, and some 
==PAGE:94==
 
 
92
improvements being also expected in France in regard to species-specific curtailment systems and 
standardized rules for bats. While, In the Netherlands, the efforts are more focused on new approaches 
to reduce bird collisions and compensate for habitat loss. The agreement among developers in the 
countries of study regarding the most effective mitigation measures is therefore underlined despite 
different points of focus. 
Regarding the difficulties in implementing mitigation measures for protecting birds and bats, the 
economical obstacle is mentioned commonly by all stakeholders as the primary one, considering both 
the cost of automatic detection systems for dynamic curtailment and the loss of production incurred, 
the adjustment and validation of automatic detection systems and their integration in older turbines 
are also a concern for developers. Additionally, stakeholders across the countries of study point to the 
need for improved regulatory frameworks and better evaluation mechanisms to ensure effective 
application of mitigation measures. 
 
In conclusion, protection measures for birds and bats are symbolized by the ARC (Avoid / Reduce / 
Compensate) procedure which is firmly established through EU legislation and has been transposed 
into national frameworks across the countries studied, its application in practice reveals important 
limitations. Although a wide range of ARC measures is documented, technical articles and scientific 
publications emphasize that no single measure is sufficient on its own to ensure effective protection 
of birds and bats. Instead, a strategic combination of avoidance, reduction, and compensation actions 
is seen as necessary. According to stakeholders, macro-siting emerges as the most impactful 
avoidance strategy, while curtailment is recognized as particularly effective for bats and migratory 
birds. However, several obstacles hinder the broader implementation of curtailment, including 
financial constraints, the lack of validation for automated detection systems (SDA), and technical 
difficulties integrating such systems into existing turbine infrastructures. Moreover, weak or 
inconsistent enforcement by public authorities further limits the effectiveness of mitigation efforts. 
These findings suggest that while mitigation strategies are homogeneously defined, scientifically 
backed-up and conceptually aligned across countries, their practical success depends heavily on 
overcoming economic and technical barriers. An enforcement of these measures at institutional level 
would seem the most powerful tool to overcome these barriers.

8.2.2. Contributions regarding harmonisation of policies 
Key aspects from Spanish stakeholders regarding international harmonisation 
 
- 
Harmonizing biodiversity protection policies at the European level would be beneficial. 
Some bat populations are migratory, meaning German bats die in Spain and vice versa. 
There is movement of individuals across borders, so it would be best to have a unified policy 
to protect populations that span multiple countries. 
- 
Harmonizing EU policies on wind energy and wildlife protection would be highly 
beneficial, especially regarding minimum technical standards, EIA protocols, ARC 
measures, and PCFM. Such harmonization would help reduce disparities between countries, 
facilitate the work of developers operating across borders, and most importantly, improve 
the consistency of conservation strategies at EU level, something especially important for 
migratory species or those with wide distributions. 
- 
In Spain, there are significant orographic differences between provinces and regions, which 
means that certain impact mitigation solutions, whether regulatory or technical, cannot be 
easily transferred or applied with the same success. Therefore, it may be useful to 
standardize the level of rigor and commitment when addressing these issues, but not 
necessarily the specific methods. Also, in any harmonization process, it is important to 
"level up" raising competencies and the thoroughness of evaluations. 
- 
There should be a harmonization of wildlife protection policies within the EU, provided 
that the European Directives are uniformly incorporated into each country’s legal system. 
But that is not the case and in Spain, it is even more complicated, as each autonomous 
community must incorporate them into its own legal framework since environmental 
matters are a delegated competency, making uniformity more difficult and introducing 
inconsistencies.

Key aspects from French stakeholders regarding international harmonisation 
 
- 
Although big differences can be appreciated in the regulations on the impact of wind energy 
on wildlife between countries, there is a trend toward harmonisation in Europe. EU policies 
would benefit from harmonisation if they were to be levelled up. 
- 
What is valuable is the knowledge that each country can contribute to generate, helping to 
assess which solutions might be implemented, as well as benefiting from feedback and 
experience sharing. A harmonized policy is therefore not desirable, but rather a 
centralization of knowledge. 
- 
As for the harmonization of practices and regulations, it makes scientific sense, as it allows 
for temporal and geographical comparisons. However, one must not forget that biodiversity 
has a local dimension that cannot be standardized on a large scale. We cannot apply the 
same protocols in a forest, in agricultural fields, or in mixed green environments. Protocols 
must always be adapted to the local context. 
- 
Harmonizing regulations at UE level is not seen as a good idea, as there are too many 
different issues—both in terms of biodiversity (which varies by region, population, and 
species involved), and in terms of regulations, which may differ depending on wind 
resources and land-use planning constraints in each country.

The importance of aligning with broader European practices is highlighted, especially under

Regarding regulations and guidelines harmonisation, there are a lot of differences between

by the industry through EIAs, PCFM, and specific studies, generating valuable knowledge. 
International frameworks like the Convention on Migratory Species, EUROBATS, ITTECOP, and

developers and cross-border projects along migratory routes further support knowledge exchange. 
In Spain, late adhesion to EUROBATS (2024) and limited NGO and ministerial resources hinder

foreign environmental associations than domestic ones. Collaborations with local universities and the 
arrival of experienced foreign developers are seen as promising in fostering collaboration and 
knowledge build up. France encourages research through its renewable energy law and national 
initiatives such as the Observatory for Renewable Energies and Biodiversity, and the MAPE and

counterparts. In the Netherlands, collaboration in the North Sea is particularly strong among the 
countries involved in offshore development in this area, in the Frisian Islands with Germany and

Overall, if the collaboration already exists among countries via quantity of projects and initiatives, it 
seems often limited to the shared areas of interest (e.g. North Sea), and the involvement of local actors 
in cooperating at international level is often limited due to a lack of resources. 
Spain seems to be quite behind on these aspects, while France is making important efforts at national 
level, and the Netherlands have created strong bonds with neighbouring countries. 
It is worth mentioning that the number of technical reports and scientific publications either funded 
or generated by the wind industry is an accelerator of knowledge on the issues of birds and bats 
protection.

8.3. 
Findings about collaboration and research 
This part of the study examines how research and collaboration play a key role in generating the 
knowledge necessary to understand and mitigate the ecological impacts of wind turbines in Spain, 
France, and the Netherlands, three countries with different timelines and strategies for wind energy 
deployment. Drawing on stakeholder perspectives, it explores the extent to which national and 
international research efforts, policy frameworks, and collaborative practices contribute to advancing 
wildlife protection, and identifies opportunities for strengthening these mechanisms. 
 
When discussing research and collaboration, the different stakeholders across the countries of study 
mentioned the existence of several relevant projects aimed at better assessing the impacts of human 
activities on wildlife, and especially the impacts of onshore and offshore wind energy on bird and 
bats. Most of these projects have a national outreach, while some of them include international 
collaboration within and beyond the EU. Half of the projects cited have been launched around or after 
2020, thereby highlighting increasing concerns on these issues. All of them are publicly funded while 
few of them also benefits from private funds. This sheds light on how research on the problematics 
of protecting birds and bats is ongoing and even accelerating, with high level projects being publicly 
funded and framed at national or international level. 
When addressing the question of collaboration, several aspects emerge from the stakeholders’ 
interviews. Local actors seem to be less invested in collaboration partly due to their lack of resources, 
either in terms of economic or manpower. The notion of collaborating and investing time and efforts 
in collaboration seems also much more relevant in the Netherlands than for instance in Spain. The 
industry also contributes to build up knowledge via the funding of EIA and PCFM reports, and 
research studies, which can be seen as an important asset as long as the outcome of these reports are 
made public. 
Regarding the harmonization of policies for protecting birds and bats from wind energy impacts, 
stakeholders expressed mixed views. The concept of “levelling up” is mentioned several times 
highlighting a concern that policies could be lightened in order to favour economic development 
instead of wildlife protection. The regional specificities and perceptions of wildlife, including birds 
and bats species, is also mentioned with some stakeholders mentioning the risk that emblematic 
species might be depreciated by an one-fits-all policy. Harmonisation is therefore seen as positive for 
setting up minimum standard requirements in terms of rigour and protocols. But centralization of 
knowledge is preferred to uniform regulation in order to better account for regional ecological 
differences. 
According to stakeholder feedback, Germany is seen as a clear leader in protecting birds and 
especially bats from wind energy impacts, United Kingdom and the Netherlands are strongly 
recognised in terms of their spatial planning offshore, Denmark is also mentioned for balancing wind 
energy development and the protection of wildlife, and the Valencian community in Spain which 
developed specific protocols in the 2000s leading to a controlled expansion of wind energy in this 
region. This shows how some countries or regions having experienced early wind energy 
development seem to have developed more abilities in the protection of these populations within their 
specific areas of wind energy development. Although France with a later development is also 
recognised for its thorough monitoring and mitigation measures; this might hint that rigor in the 
application of protocols is an asset. 
==PAGE:107==
 
 
105
 
In conclusion, the development of knowledge to protect birds and bats from the impacts of wind 
energy in Spain, France, and the Netherlands is shaped by national and international research efforts 
and collaborations, which are publicly funded. The Netherlands appears to be the most engaged in 
international collaboration, while Spain lags behind, partly due to more limited resources. 
The systematic accumulation of impact assessments and post-construction monitoring reports, 
although funded by industry, also makes a significant contribution to knowledge development when 
the outcomes are publicly accessible and stored in national databases. This system, where publicly 
funded research projects are complemented by industry-funded environmental reporting, particularly 
in France, reflects a form of collaboration between public institutions and the wind energy sector. It 
helps address the financial limitations often faced by research initiatives, contributes to a more robust 
knowledge base, and signals a growing commitment from the wind industry to wildlife protection. 
A major opportunity for improving wildlife protection lies in strengthening knowledge exchange 
between countries and promoting a shared baseline of rigor in impact assessment, monitoring, and 
mitigation practices, particularly in ecologically sensitive and transboundary areas such as the North 
Sea or major migratory flyways. Countries and regions that have built up early and context-specific 
experience can play a key role in guiding this process and helping others strengthen their own 
practices.

9. General conclusion 
This thesis has examined the multifaceted relationship between wind energy development and its 
impacts on birds and bats in Spain, France, and the Netherlands, through five complementary research 
questions. While all three countries recognize and attempt to address the ecological challenges posed 
by wind energy, such as mortality from collisions, habitat disruption, and species-specific 
vulnerabilities, important gaps persist in how these impacts are assessed, regulated, mitigated, and 
integrated into decision-making processes. 
 
Across the case studies, the current assessment and monitoring systems are hampered by 
inconsistent methodologies, uneven data quality, and limited transparency, particularly in Spain and 
the Netherlands. While France benefits from stronger national guidance and enforcement, it too faces 
practical limitations. A key improvement identified across stakeholder groups involves the need to 
enhance species-specific knowledge (especially for bats), adopt standardized yet flexible assessment 
protocols, and require public data sharing. Importantly, harmonization of impact assessments should 
not be interpreted as imposing identical procedures everywhere. Instead, what is needed is a shared 
baseline of rigor, allowing for contextual adaptation while ensuring that data can be compared across 
projects and aggregated at larger scales to better guide policy and practice. 
 
On the regulatory front, while the EU provides a common legal backbone, the fragmented 
implementation across national and regional levels weakens the coherence of bird and bat protection 
efforts. Stakeholders report that where regulatory frameworks are more consistent and predictable, 
cooperation is stronger, and project outcomes are more balanced. This highlights the importance of 
regulatory alignment, not necessarily through uniformity, but through clarity, enforceability, and 
coordination between biodiversity protection and renewable energy goals. 
 
Stakeholder perceptions further reveal gaps in mutual understanding. Industry actors acknowledge 
ecological concerns but often weigh them against economic constraints, while the public expresses 
concern yet frequently lacks access to clear and reliable information. These findings underscore the 
pressing need for more effective communication and structured, transparent engagement 
mechanisms, to foster trust and encourage more informed participation in wind energy planning. 
 
Regarding mitigation, the Avoid–Reduce–Compensate (ARC) approach is well-established across 
the three countries but falls short in practice due to limited enforcement, technical challenges, and 
economic trade-offs, particularly in the application of curtailment strategies and real-time detection 
systems. A key improvement lies in ensuring that the financial implications of mitigation, such as 
production losses from curtailment, retrofitting costs, or investment in automated detection systems, 
are anticipated and incorporated early in project planning. By provisioning for these costs from the 
outset, project viability and wildlife protection can be more effectively balanced. Institutional 
enforcement, combined with clearer financial planning, will be essential to closing the gap between 
conceptual frameworks and real-world outcomes. 
 
Finally, the role of research and collaboration emerges as a cornerstone for future improvement. 
When publicly funded studies are complemented by industry-supported monitoring, particularly 
==PAGE:109==
 
 
107
when this data is made publicly accessible, there is clear progress in building a robust knowledge 
base. The Netherlands is notably advanced in international cooperation, while Spain’s efforts remain 
more nationally confined. A significant opportunity lies in expanding cross-border knowledge 
exchange, promoting more consistent scientific standards, and leveraging the experience of 
frontrunner regions to support others, particularly in ecologically sensitive and transboundary zones 
such as major migratory flyways; the collaboration among countries bordering the North Sea can be 
considered as an example. 
 
Taken together, the findings suggest that more effective bird and bat protection in the context of wind 
energy is not a matter of reinventing frameworks, but of reinforcing their application: through 
stronger coordination, better knowledge, smarter enforcement, and sustained stakeholder 
engagement.

10. Future works 
Building on the findings of this study, future developments could aim to expand the geographic and 
stakeholder scope to strengthen comparative insights and identify best practices across a broader 
European context. Including additional countries such as Germany, Denmark, the United Kingdom, 
Portugal, and Belgium would be particularly relevant, not only because some are considered leaders 
in wind-wildlife integration, but also because they share ecological environments with Spain, France, 
and the Netherlands. This extended comparison could enhance understanding of transboundary 
challenges and opportunities. 
 
Moreover, future research should seek to increase representation from underrepresented stakeholder 
groups, particularly professional unions, whose perspectives are currently under-documented in view 
of their low level of response to interview requests, but central to understanding implementation 
challenges. 
 
Finally, integrating the latest scientific and policy developments presented at the 8th Conference on 
Wind Energy and Wildlife Impacts (CWW 2025), scheduled for September 2025 in Montpellier, will 
be key to capturing emerging trends, innovations, and evolving consensus within the field.
