Abstract
With the increasing trends in wind energy, wind farms have started to be located on
non-conventional sites more frequently. Despite its advantages, non-conventional areas cause
a vital cost of atmospheric icing on the structures. In-cloud icing, one of the two types of at-
mospheric icing, causes many problems on wind turbines. These problems can show variety
such as aerodynamic performance degradation, structural load increment, instabilities etc.
Therefore, ice phenomena need to be understood to overcome these issues. Hence, this study
makes an aerodynamic blade design and ice accretion experiment on the rotor to identify
changes in the ice build-up, ice form, and acting forces on the turbine.
One-point blade design/Glauert optimized rotor design method used to make the aerody-
namic design of the blades.
This blade design was optimized to have a wider chord in
observing ice accretion better, instead of maximizing the aerodynamic performance. Design
evaluation made in BEM code and HAWC2S. All the needed parts designed in SolidWorks
to the manufacturing process. Blades are made by the prepreg method using various types of
carbon fibers. By obtaining all the parts, assembly of the turbine made. RPM was controlled
by PID controlled servomotor. Therefore, tuning was made to ensure smooth operation.
Main tests and pre-tests were made in Climatic Wind Tunnel that was built in the col-
laboration between the Technical University of Denmark and Force Technology.
In pre-
tests, CWT’s operation conditions were observed and documented. A cross-talk signal was
obtained from the force transducer during the pre-tests that corrupted the measurements.
Despite the cross-talk, the force pattern still can be followed in the main test. The main tests
aimed to observe variations on accreted ice and acting forces for different turbine operation
parameters. A photogrammetry workflow was introduced with a statistical background to
obtain the ice formation on the blade. A Matlab script was created to gather the information
from the point cloud.
As a result of the study, thrust signals were regulated mostly with aerodynamic efficiency.
Aerodynamic performance degradation was observed for some of the tests. However, cross-
talk was effective on the thrust signal. Torque was dominated by the structural excitation
mainly due to side-to-side natural frequency. The mass signal showed increment through the
tests for each case. RPM change was observed to affect both the ice type and ice formation
on the blade due to flow separation and increment on the friction. Pitch angle was affected
the impingement area on the blade.
The photogrammetry method with the established
workflow in the study gave promising results despite some minor misalignment issues.

Introduction
Wind energy is becoming more popular as a part of the green transition to replace fossil
fuels. According to the GWEC[11], wind energy total installed capacity has been reached
743 GW thanks to 93 GW new installations in 2020. Since the installed capacity and in-
stallation trends are increasing, it is expected to see wind turbines in non-conventional sites
(e.g. cold climate). BTM World Market Update 2012[8] shows that 69 GW of installed
wind energy capacity is located in cold climates. Cold climate areas are arctic or subarctic
areas such as Northern Europe, Canada, Siberia and high altitude locations, e.g. Alps. Even
though there can be advantages such as higher air pressure, wind turbines are exposed to
cold weather and icing events in these locations. Eventually, this event creates many issues
for wind turbines and may even cause shutdowns. Therefore, cold climate and its effects on
wind turbines should be understood to overcome these issues.
Cold Climate
Cold Climate (CC) regions are described in the IEA report[31] as the sites which atmospheric
icing occurs or the temperatures are lower than the standard wind turbines’ operational con-
ditions (Generally, -10◦C for operation, -20◦C for survival[9]). As shown in Figure 1, in CC
regions, either or both Icing Climate(IC) and Low-Temperature Climate(LTC) can be expe-
rienced.
Figure 1: Definition of Cold Climate[31]
Low-Temperature Climate areas can be described as where the temperature is lower
than -20◦C on more than nine days per year, and the average annual temperature is below
0◦C. However, atmospheric icing occurs in the Icing Climate. As a description of IC based

1.1
Atmospheric Icing
Atmospheric icing is defined as the ice accretion on the structures due to atmospheric
conditions[31]. Atmospheric icing types can be divided into in-cloud icing and precipitation
icing. In-cloud icing occurs due to minus degrees’ temperature and low clouds on site. On
the other hand, precipitation icing can be happening at positive degrees’ temperature as a
form of wet snow. The ice forms can be seen on turbine blades in three different formations
based on parameters described in the following parts.
Figure 2: Type of Ice Accretion as a Function of Wind Speed and Temperature[14]
1.1.1
Forms of Ice
Rime Ice
In the rime form of ice, supercooled water droplets in the clouds are moved due to the wind
and hit the blade. These water droplets froze fully on the surface when attached to the
body. It typically forms in cold temperatures (-4◦C to -12◦C[14]). If the median volumetric
diameter(MVD) size is small and liquid water content(LWC) in the air is low, the occurred
ice shape is called soft rime. In this type, the colour is white with a feathery form, and the
density of the ice can vary from 200 kg/m3 to 600 kg/m3. On the contrary, MVD size is
medium, and LWC in the air is medium, hard rime type with an opaque colour and density
variation from 600 kg/m3 to 900 kg/m3 occurs.
Glaze Ice
As shown in Figure 2, glaze ice is an ice formation that occurs in higher wind speeds and/or
higher air temperatures for the same LWC and MVD values as rime ice. Despite the dif-
ferences in wind speed and temperature, LWC and MVD in glaze ice formation is higher
than in both forms of rime. Since the air temperature is higher in glaze formation, not all
the water droplets freeze instantly on the body. Due to the rotation, wind and gravity of

the wind turbine blade, these non-frozen water droplets might flow on the blade, and the
ice accretion can be observed through the leading edge. Therefore, the shape of the ice can
be described as distributed. It has the highest density of all ice forms, 900 kg/m3 and is
observed as transparent.
Mixed Ice
Mixed or glime ice formation can be described as intermediate forms between the other two
ice formations, and it forms when the temperature is decreasing. In Figure 3, an example of
the ice shapes can be seen. For mixed formation, it is easy to observe that rime ice formation
occurs around stagnation point, whereas the glaze ice formation followed around stagnation
point, close to feathers. This ice form can be observed due to the change of icing conditions
during the icing events, such as temperature changes.

1.1.2
Ice Accretion Process
The ice accretion process occurs when the cooled or super-cooled particles, regardless of
solid or liquid, are colliding with the structures. Makkonen[33] introduced the Equation 1.1
to calculate the rate of icing on the objects based on the flux density of these particles and
3
==PAGE:17==
Master Thesis
1.1
Atmospheric Icing
three different efficiency or correction factors. Since the ice accretion process is a function
of time, the rate of icing can be calculated by following equation.
dM
dt = α1 × α2 × α3 × w × v × A
(1.1)
Where
• w is the mass concentration of the particle
• v is the droplets velocity relative to the body
• A is the cross-sectional area of the body
• α1,α2 and α3 are the collision, collection and accretion efficiencies, respectively.
Collision, collection and accretion efficiencies are correction factors (from 0-1) of the
equation since the flux density(w × v × A) value is the maximum. Collision efficiency, α1,
indicates the flux density ratio of particles that reach the airfoil to the maximum density flux.
As shown in Figure 4 below, small particles can follow the streamlines around the cylinder or
airfoil while the bigger particles are hitting the surface. Thus, collision efficiency will always
be lower than 1. However, it is possible to increase collision efficiency by increasing the size
of droplets and/or increasing the air velocity.
Figure 4: Droplet Trajectories[14]
Collection/Sticking efficiency, α2, represents the ratio between the flux density of the
particles stuck to the body to the flux density of the particles that hit the body. For super-
cooled droplets(rime case) due to them freezing immediately, and for the liquid layer on the
body(glaze case) due to the spreading of the droplets in the surface, α2 can be taken as 1.
However, fully solid particles such as dry snow can bounce back from the surface. Therefore,
in that situation, α2 can be estimated as 0[33].
Last, accretion efficiency, α3, shows the ratio of the rate of icing of the particles on the
body to the flux density of the particles. In the case of rime ice, where super-cooled water

cles leave the surface due to gravity, rotation, or wind due to water film. These phenomena
occur because the latent heat can be transferred to the air from the freezing surface[37].
1.1.3
Icing Effects on the Wind Turbines
Measurement Errors
The wind data has a vital importance in the assessment part of the project or the research.
For this purpose, cup anemometers and wind vanes are frequently used due to their low costs
and relatively less complex theories compared with, e.g. LIDAR measurements. However,
these instruments can be highly affected by icing. According to the Laakso et al.[30], a
small amount of ice accretion on the cups can decrease the wind speed measurements by
a significant amount(around 30%-40%) due to the friction, whereas the high amount of ice
accretion can even stop the anemometers.
Power Losses
To explain the power losses on the wind turbines, first, iced airfoil aerodynamics should be
introduced. Ice accretion on the wind turbine blades or airfoils causes the change of the
airfoil’s shape and the roughness on the airfoil surface[15]. Consequently, airfoil character-
istics will be affected. Roughness increment due to icing has effects of increasing the drag
coefficient and decreasing the lift coefficient. Bragg et al[7] mentioned the roughness effect
of ice thickness and surface on the airfoil. Also, it has to be said that ice shape is important.
For rime and some glaze ice formation cases, roughness effects can be observed, whereas the
ice horns can cause another problem such as separation bubbles and shadowing effects (see
more[7]).

Mechanical Failures
Due to decreased aerodynamic efficiency, aerodynamic load channels can show decrement.
On the other hand, due to the ice mass, deterministic loads will show higher values. Uneven
icing or different ice shredding from blades can lead to blade edgewise and tower vibrations
due to the power spectral density increment for 1P, as well as it may cause imbalance[42].
Also, the resonance may occur since the ice mass can affect the structures natural frequen-
cies. Consequently, this will lead to higher fatigue loads, which may cause to decrease of the
lifetime.
Safety Issues
Ice thrown from rotating wind turbines can cause a serious safety problem, especially for the
wind farms close to the residential areas. Equation 1.2 is used to determine the ice throwing
distance from a wind turbine.
d = 1.5(D+H)
(1.2)
Where the D is the diameter of the turbine, H is the height of the nacelle, and d is the
distance of the ice throwing. By the WECO[42], some suggestions have been made to solve
this problem, such as shutting down the turbine close to the living areas and roads, re-siting
the wind turbines, etc.

Where Vrel is relative velocity, the local radius of the blade r, local chord length c(r) and v
is the kinematic viscosity. Most of the airfoils on the catalogues are for regular WTs, which
operates at high Reynolds Number. On the other hand, these airfoils are unsuitable for

the design purpose of the low Reynolds number airfoils as:
• Reduction of the laminar separation effects
• Decreased relative thickness
where both have positive effects on the aerodynamic performance. The laminar separation
effect usually occurs at low Reynolds numbers on the suction side of the airfoil. Airfoil’s
suction side has high pressure because of the decrement of the flow speed, which causes
an adverse pressure gradient, leading to laminar separation bubbles[29]. When the flow is
separated, turbulent flow occurs on the airfoil, leading to reattach to the surface[32].Figure 10
shows the laminar separation phenomena for different Reynolds number values. At Figure 10-
c, LSB occurs at the leading edge and reattaches fast for Re>500,000. However, At Figure 10-
b, reattachment of the flow and the turbulent boundary layer is higher, which leads to severe
drag increases. Between Re=100,000-500,000, relatively shorter bubbles can form. Also, the
size of the bubbles can be affected by the angle of attack, which causes the short bubbles to
turn into big bubbles(as Figure 10-b). Relative thickness decrement is possible due to the
inherent of SWT. Since the low Re airfoils are thought for SWT, wind loads and gravitational
loads decrease with the smaller rotor area. On the other hand, the rotational speed of the
SWT is higher, so the proportion of the centrifugal loads are higher for SWTs. This leads
to an increment of centrifugal stiffening on the blades, which lower structural concerns.

ϕinflow and limited βtwist. Also, BEM code will need larger polar data because of convergence
process includes high fluctuations on early steps. Therefore, airfoil extrapolation has to be
done. For the extrapolation process, there are two methods are commonly used, which are
Montgomerie and Viterna. There is no better one to define between those two methods in
the literature. However, it can be commented that the Montgomerie method shows more
severe results for stall conditions. It also needs to be mentioned that using these different
methods for extrapolation would not create a big difference since the aerodynamic part of
the blade should take the characteristics of experimental data due to aerodynamic efficiency
being high on those αaoa values. Extrapolated polar data with from DTU[5] has been used,
matching Selig’s experimental data as shown in Figure 14. This extrapolated data shows
the similarity with Viterna extrapolation of Selig’s data, since the stall condition seems to
be less severe.
Additionally, more experimental data were tested in DTU’s wind tunnel
for different Re numbers in this dataset. This gives the advantage of interpolating other
Reynolds numbers to ensure better performance analysis.
After the polar datasets were obtained, the next design step is to decide the cldes. As
explained in Appendix A, the chord is a function of cl and λTSR. Additionally, dCP is a
function of λTSRloc, clloc and cloc. It is known that the one-point design method aims to
maximise the dCP. It means that, for a given blade element, if the cl decreases and λTSR is
kept constant, chord length increases to maintain the aerodynamic performance for a given
blade. It is also known that λTSRloc inherently increases along the blade due to its dependency
on rloc; cldes distribution can be decided by this behaviour. If the cl is kept constant along
the blade, the chord decreases sharply with a relation of λTSRloc. As the constraints were
given earlier, the chord should have 50mm for at least 90% of the blade. Thus, cl should
decrease along the blade as much as the chord decrement becomes more linear than sharply.

To have a 3D blade drawing, obtained blade geometry needed to process to have point cloud

were created, defining 51 planes on 3D drawing. Then for each plane, for a given chord,
twist and relative thickness values were interpolated since the original blade data has 42
data points. Relative thickness data has been used in the script; thus, it can decide which
airfoil profile for each plane. The airfoil profile must be arranged for given chord lengths
and twist information for each plane. As a result of this process, six different .dat files were
obtained: blade information for x, y and z coordinates for both suction and pressure sides
of the blade. It is also needed to mention that the pitch axis arranged as 30% of the chord
is on the leading edge side of the blade, where the rest is on the trailing edge part.
After six different .dat files were obtained, these files were converted to. ibl extension
in another Matlab script. The purpose of this is to lower the processing time of data input
in SolidWorks by using only one file instead of six different files. Also, it is known that .dat
or .txt files can be used as one point cloud files. However, the .ibl file type also includes the
information of the curves, which makes the process way easier due to how SolidWorks works.
Since the points and curve orders are known in the .ibl extension, this file can be directly
put inside the program. On the other hand, for .txt files, it is needed to create other planes
to work with the ‘Curve Wizard’ option of SolidWorks, which can create some internal issues
on the structure.
.ibl extension file, which has been used for 3D blade drawing, consists of 51 different
sections to represent 51 different blade cross-sections. Each section consists of three different
curves, which are:
• Trailing Edge: Consists 2 points for the pressure side and suction side, respectively.
Also, it has the information of the curve between those points.
• Suction Side: Consists of 30 points, including the same point on the trailing edge
section for the suction side. It will provide the suction side curve for each plane.
• Pressure Side: Consists of 30 points, including the point on the trailing edge section for
the pressure side. It also shares one same point with the suction side, which represents

4.3
Photogrammetry Tests
Analyzing the ice accumulation on the blade, the most common method is contour
tracing, which is highly used in the experimental study. However, since the SWT blades
are relatively small, this process might create issues with tracing the contour. That is why
photogrammetry was tried to analyze the ice distribution on the blade.
Photogrammetry is a technique for obtaining a 3D digital model of an object by taking
pictures of it on 360◦. This technique is commonly used for different disciplines such as
digital elevation maps, archaeology, forensic etc. However, there was no relevant study for
obtaining the ice accumulation from SWT by photogrammetry found online. Therefore, more
than ten tests had to be made to have satisfying results and understand the methodology of
the technique. Additionally, there is no standard given in this technique, which means the
methods that have been used in the part of the project were taken either by the experience
during the tests or by the expertise of professionals around the world.
Agisoft Metashape has been used to process pictures and turn them into a 3D model. Ag-
isoft’s workflow can be described as[1]:
• Loading pictures and inspection of the pictures: Agisoft Metashape has no
limits to uploading pictures on the software. However, more pictures mean more com-
putational time, depending on the GPU and RAM of the computer. On the other hand,
it is suggested to have more pictures rather than fewer pictures, since the insufficient
number of pictures will result in inaccurate alignment and a bad model. Inspection
of the pictures can be made visually on Agisoft’s interface. It is recommended that
the low quality or blurry pictures decrease the accuracy of the alignment and finalised
model. Thus they need to be removed.
• Aligning Photos: The alignment process has been used to decide the location of the
pictures with respect to the model. Also, in the program, selected key points and tie
points in this process can be defined as a point of interest caused by high contrast or
texture differences on the surface that determines as important by Agisoft. On the
other hand, tie points are the same key points identified by Agisoft in two or more
pictures, leading to a 3D position of the points. After the alignment process, a sparse
point cloud can be obtained, a 3D point cloud with tie points. It will have a relatively
low number of points, which will depend on the alignment parameters that will be
introduced. Although the sparse point cloud is useful to understand the alignment
quality, one can estimate the general success of the project by that.
• Dense Point Cloud: After the sparse point cloud is obtained, it is possible to obtain
the dense point cloud in the ‘Workflow’ selections.
Agisoft Metashape decides the
dense point cloud distribution by using the camera information, sparse point cloud and
distortion parameters, if any given. By that process, Agisoft Metashape can create a
dense point cloud since the knowledge from the alignment process makes it possible
to generate stereo pairs and interpolation, which leads to depth maps. This process
57
==PAGE:71==
Master Thesis
4.3
Photogrammetry Tests
depends on the dense cloud parameters on the workflow that will be introduced.
• Mesh Model: Agisoft can generate a polygonal mesh model based on dense point
cloud or depth maps generated for the dense point cloud. Results of this process are
also dependent on the parameters. In the workflow of this project, the aim is to have
a dense point cloud. However, it is a good option to have a mesh model since virtual
controls can be made, such as holes in the structure or kink edges.
In this project, it is aimed to use Agisoft Metashape to have point clouds of dry and iced
blades for further processing in the Matlab. The expectation is to subtract the point clouds

4.3.1
Camera Equipment and Settings
For taking the pictures, it is suggested to use a DSLR camera since the images will
have higher quality rather than the phone’s camera. Therefore, Canon EOS 7D Mark II[38]
was used in the project. DSLR cameras have some settings which affect the quality of the
pictures directly. At the beginning of the tests, these values were taken from the previous
BSc study[13].
However, these values were changed due to the experienced results and
conditions. These changes will be described in the test section to give the reasons for those
changes. Therefore, initial selections can be found below.
• Lens: Lenses are described in terms of mm based on focal length. The wider lens
indicates the lower mm, whereas the narrower lens will have higher mm. The DSLR
can capture more areas and more light when the lens gets wider. However, too wide
lenses such as fish-eye cause too much distortion that would affect the process[20].
Additionally, lenses with zoom capability have multiple focal lengths, creating some
small distortions. It can also risk the process if the focal length changes throughout
the images. Therefore, Sigma 50mm F1.4 DG HSM fixed lens was used.
• ISO: It describes the sensitivity of the DSLR’s to light. It means that higher ISO
values lead to DSLR having more light and brighter pictures. As a cost of having
much light in the picture, high ISO values causes noise. It is because, since the ISO
is not able to create more light but amplifies it, it also causes to amplification of the
noise[41]. Suggested ISO range is the default setting of the DSLR and can be increased
up to 200 ISO[20].
• Aperture: It can be defined as the opening of the lens[[34]].
In the DSLR, it is
described with the f-stop number. If the f/stop number increases, the lens’ opening
gets narrower (small aperture) and vice versa. A large aperture cause to more light
pass through the lens resulting in a brighter picture. Additionally, aperture affects the
depth of field in the pictures. Depth of field means how much the image is actually on
the focus. Mostly, for the low f/stop numbers, the background tends to be out of focus
which leads blurry view of the background. On the other hand, low f/stop numbers can
cause a blurry foreground for close shots. Generally, the suggested value for aperture
is f8 to f11[20]. However, depending on the object, it is possible to go over f11. On the

• Shutter Speed:It defines the speed at which the DSLR’s shutter closes[12].

4.3.3
Stationary Camera Test
As a second main test, the stationary camera/turntable method was tried. The reasons
to try this method are:
• Overlaps can be arranged better with the help of a turntable and controlled servomotor.
• By taking the pictures from exact distances and heights, point clouds coordinate sys-
tems can be arranged similarly, which reduces data manipulation in Matlab. Addition-
ally, with the turntable method, tilt and cone angles of the camera can be kept the
same through the process easily.
• Working with more than 300 pictures, moving the tripod is a time-consuming process.
Especially when it is considered to work with a blade located in the hub, with the
other two blades around, therefore, it is estimated that it will decrease the time cost
during the main test.
The turntable built for this process consists of a small scale servomotor connected to the
gear. Then the blade was connected to another gear to connect it with servomotor. Also,
with the help of a micro-controller, it is possible to decide the total rotation angle, each
step’s rotation angle and delay between each step. Another student in DTU did the design
and manufacturing of the turntable. After some experiments to decide the total rotation
angle, 1150◦was chosen for each level of pictures. Due to the gear ratio difference, 1150◦is
equal to 360◦-380◦. As a step, 25◦was chosen, and the delay time was 15 seconds since it
takes time to focus on the object from the edges.
The turntable test has some differences from the stationary object test. In the alignment
phase of workflow, Agisoft Metashape uses the information taken from the background, such
as changing the position of the objects in the background. However, in the turntable case,
the background is constant, but the object rotates with the help of a servomotor system.
Therefore, the stationary background confuses the Agisoft Metashape. Thus, the background
should be masked in every picture. There is also another option to use, such as ‘Exclude
Stationary Tie Points’ in Figure 61. This option allows Agisoft to use stationary data points
obtained by the pictures in the background. However, for not ‘ideal’ pictures, this option
might create some issues. Therefore, the masking option was used. To use masking, Agisoft
offers several options such as:
• Manual Masking: It is possible to create masks for every picture using rectangle
selection, intelligent scissors etc. In this way, it is possible to create perfect masking.

4.3.4
Post-Process of Point Cloud
To extract the 3D ice distribution from the blade, post-process is necessary. At first, a
dense point cloud from Agisoft was thought to obtain. However, due to the randomness of
71

the points, a script was written in Matlab to gather the information from random points.
The workflow of the script can be described as:
• Import the data as .txt.
• Create planes between the biggest point in the x-direction and the tip point.
X-
direction defines the chord length, and by finding the z coordinate of that point, planes
can be created as a percentage through the size of the blade. The advantage to creating
planes as a percentage, regardless of the scale of the different scans the plane locations
would be the same.
• For each plane created, planei, two different plane information needs to be gathered.
These are planei,b, which gives the information around the points backwards of the
plane and planei,p, which provides the information with around the points afterwards
of the plane. However, an offset had to be put to obtain those points. The high offset
will result in too many points, but disorganised information and low offset will result
in fewer points but better information. Disorganised information means that since the
bigger changes on the blade span, point distribution will be affected by bigger thickness
distribution. Therefore, this offset should be selected to have enough numbers to create
an airfoil distribution, but not more than one airfoil.
• For planei,b and planei,p, suction and pressure sides needed to be defined. For this
reason, a second-order fitting was made between leading edge, trailing edge and average
airfoil height. Average airfoil height was calculated by the highest and lowest airfoil
heights for a given plane. After that, this fit was used to separate points for sides.
• 80 points were created periodically for each side of the planei,b and planei,p, and they
were used to gather the information from points with linear interpolation. In total,
160 points for planei,b and planei,p was collected together.
• Linear interpolation between planei,b and planei,p in z-direction was made to decide
the chord length of planei. After that 40 points were created with the same periods
for each side of planei.
• Afterwards, data points from planei,b and planei,p were fitted the planei, between them
y using the information from z-direction.
By this workflow, a script was created. Results of the script can be seen in Figure 72.

5.1.2
Test Conditions
To decide test conditions, pre-test information was used. Different air and water pres-
sures were tested at early runs of the rotor. From the previous experiments that could not
be completed and presented due to equipment failure, it is known that there is a direct
correlation between LWC and MVD values to air and water pressures. High water pressure
will lead to higher LWC since the given cloud will have a higher water proportion. On the
other hand, higher air pressure cause to decrement in the LWC since the focus of the cloud
increases, which means the same amount of water for higher volume. In terms of MVD,
higher air pressure causes to decrease due to the higher pressure on the water particles in
the nozzle[28]. On the other hand, for higher water pressures, the behaviour of the MVD
was thought as mostly dependent on the nozzle cap selection.
Wind speed for the test was chosen as 6 m/s since that leads to Remean ≃100.000. Higher
wind speeds did not think for the main test due to the blockage effect. Since the SWT has
a 1 m2 rotor area, and the test section has a 4m2 area, there is a 0.25 blockage effect. This
will cause an increment of the velocity distribution on the blade and finally will increase the
Ct[36]. Ultimately, it was aimed to obtain rime ice or mixed-rime ice on the blade. Since
the ice distributions from different cases were obtained by the photogrammetry technique, it
was assumed that glaze ice would create some issues since it is a see-through type. By trying
different air and water pressures, an ideal option was identified. LWC and MVD should be
obtained in lower levels for the rime case, and temperature must be at a minimum level but
steady. The test matrix is shown in Table 14.
During the pre-tests, the best formation of mix-rime ice was obtained in Wp = 3 and Ap = 3.
Additionally, it was decided to use the test period as 10 minutes due to a high amount of ice
accretion might create issues for the alignment process since the texture on the blade may
be covered with ice fully. The target temperature was -8◦C, for the reasons introduced in
section 4. Finally, it was aimed at the main test to observe different run parameters of the
SWT and their effects on ice shapes and forces.

Conclusion and Future Work
The study aimed to make an aerodynamic blade design for SWT and to make experi-
mental ice accretion tests on an SWT rotor. The one-point blade design method was used to
obtain the design with given constraints. Design evaluation was made with BEM code and
HAWC2S that gave similar results with the design parameters. Manufacturing of the blades
was made by using the prepreg method. As a result of this method, SWT blades were ob-
tained with some imperfections inherently. Nevertheless, structurally consistent three blades
were obtained by negligible differences in mass and mass center. Assembly was made after
having all the parts drawn during the design process. Tuning of servomotor was investigated
to achieve an ideal run for the SWT.
Pretests and main tests were made in CWT. In pretests, first, the temperature perfor-
mance of the CWT was investigated. A relation between the voltage and temperature was
defined for conversion purposes. Additionally, temperature tests were made to understand
and record to cooling unit’s performance. Calibration control of the force transducer was
made. Fx and Fy channels caused and suffered from cross-talk, while Fz was affected only
by other channels’ cross-talks. To obtain ice shapes from the blade, the photogrammetry
method was used. However, there was not any standard process or related study on SWT
blades. Therefore, a novel workflow that covers generating a valid 3D model of ice accreted
blade, aligning the iced blade with dry blade and extracting accumulated ice from the blade
in 3D. As a target, 0.5 pixel error was defined. Additionally, a statistical background of
the photogrammetry method was investigated. In the main tests, problems due to various
reasons were tried to overcome. Target ice type was defined as rime ice, and ideal cloud pa-
rameters were searched for that ice type. Finally, main tests were made to observe differences
in ice shape, ice type and differences in the forces for each pitch and RPM configuration of
the SWT.
Thrust signal was obtained as directly proportional to the increasing RPM since the selected
RPMs are relatively low due to structural issues. A side-to-side excitations were observed for
torque signals of Case-3 and Case-4, whereas the signal was increased but stabilized eventu-
ally. Due to the time limitation, Case-2 could not test for a similar RPM value to verify the
behaviour. Fz showed a constant increase for both cases due to increasing mass and shared
wind load. On the other hand, it was impossible to interpret the amplitudes since the signal
was affected by Fx and Fy due to cross-talk. Thrust signal in pitch cases showed inverse
proportion to increasing pitch because of the aerodynamic efficiency degradation. Since the
RPMs were the same side-to-side, excitation was obtained for Fx of each case. Fz showed
increasing behaviour for each case but different amplitudes. This could be due to ice accu-
mulation, shared wind load difference and cross-talk. The results for the complete force tests
depicted that thrust signal was highly related to aerodynamics of SWT, torque signal was
highly dominated by the excitation from the structure, and Fz showed increment for all cases.
Obtained scan results were satisfactory since the pixel error was around 0.3, 40% lower than

aimed. There were some issues in the alignment phase. Here, excessive ice accumulation on
the surfaces of the point clouds made the process inconvenient when selecting the proper
alignment points. Therefore an ideal alignment process would be using the meshes instead

the methods and quality of the results:
• Corrupted force signals formed the main issue of this study. A further verification
of the force tests should be performed with the calibrated equipment to examine the
consistency between the assumptions and the results. Additionally, SWT performance
validation can be made with a dry test.
• Structural issues should be fixed to have better operation configuration intervals.
Therefore, RPMs can be studied in more detail, without having an excitation from
the structure. Let us say, for example, a steel tube by increasing diameter through
the ground would increase the stiffness in all directions, leading to a decrement of the

as increasing the contrast. The blade texture can be improved through these changes.
Additionally, since the blades are carbon fiber, the reflection problem should be solved
with a better quality light diffuser.

formance. Even though more advanced instruments exist, such as CFD, it is still common
due to low costs of operating and fair results. BEM theory was introduced by Glauert[19] as
a combination of 1D momentum theory and blade element theory. It should be noted that

corrections to have reliable results, and this process will be introduced in this section. The
section will also introduce some of the equations directly linked to the process. To have a
full understanding of the theory or derivation of the equations, see more[22][2][40].

B.2
Blade Manufacturing
After the mould was obtained, the blade manufacturing process was started. For this
process, the vacuum casting method was used with epoxy resin. First, wax was applied
to the mould, and after 5-10 minutes of waiting, the mould was polished with a piece of
tissue. This process should be done 3-4 times for a new mould, depending on the porosity
of the mould filling. The necessity of the additional wax can be decided by touching the
mould since wax aims to prevent the blade from sticking to the mould. Mould can be seen
in Figure 109.
Figure 109: Mould
Since the wax needs to stay on the mould for some time, materials can be prepared
meantime. First, blade shapes were drawn on suction tissues from the mould and cut. After
that, these suction tissues were used to cut other materials. The materials were:
• Spread-Tow Plain Carbon Fiber: This material was used on the outer side of the
blade. This fabric maximises the mechanical strength while minimising the weight
because they are spread into thin tapes. Due to the plain weaving of the fabric, it
gives the strength on 0◦and 90◦, which leads to stiffness on edgewise and flapwise.
[200 g/m2, 15k]
• 45◦Biaxial Carbon Fiber:This fabric has two unidirectional plies at ± 45◦, giving
the torsional stiffness. Due to the fabric weaving, the cutting process can be made
without an offcut. [200 g/m2, 12k]
• Unidirectional Carbon Fiber: Unidirectional fabric uses if the load is running in
a particular direction since its high-stress benefits. In this application, two layers of
unidirectional carbon fiber have been used which one is a rectangular shape located in
the centre of the blade, and the other is a smaller rectangular on top of the first one.
[200 g/m2, 50k]

• Plain Weave Carbon Fiber: This fabric was located in the inner of the blade. Fibre
orientation is 0◦and 90◦; therefore, stiffness is also on the same angles. Since the fabric
is lighter than normal versions, specifically used for aerospace, turbine blades etc. [93
g/m2, 1k]
‘k’ in the material information gives the idea of how many thousands of filaments are in

mould. The purpose of this is that the resin drives up through the fabric, which reduces
the amount of air trapped on the surface. After that, spread tow carbon fiber placed on the
mould and then glared. A small additional piece of spread tow carbon fiber was added to
the root section for both sides since there is a bigger curvature on the root section, and it is
necessary to cover it fully. Then small pieces have also glared with the epoxy mixture.
The process above with the placement of the carbon fibers and glaring them was made
for all the blade layers. It is important to observe all the corners of the surfaces on the
mould were covered, and there is no space on the surfaces of the mould. Additionally, one
should be careful to use the brush gently while putting the resin on the fabric since the harsh
movement might cause the disorientation of the filaments. After all the carbon materials
were placed on the mould, the peel ply was placed on the top of the mould, covering all

again. As an advantage of using epoxy resin, it takes time to be rigid on the surfaces as
well as it is a strong glue. Therefore, the alignment process can be made carefully and
slowly to ensure good results. To have the finalised blade assembly, 24 hours waiting
was needed.

• Alignment success of the pressure and suction sides of the blade

• Smooth shape
• Mass of the blade
