Abstract
Algorithms can understand text data by ingraining them in a continuous vector space
and represent them as vectors, called embeddings.
New technologies using these
vectors are constantly being created, and now they can be used to perform all sorts
of complicated tasks. In this work, the main focus is on using a new technology by
the Facebook AI research team, called StarSpace, to work with text data. StarSpace
can be used for different tasks, such as generating embeddings, topic classification,
information retrieval, and recommendation systems.
First, the StarSpace method was used to categorize topics into classes. It was
compared to one of the most used methods for topic classification, latent Dirichlet
allocation. The tasks consisted of classifying topics into a different number of classes.
In the first experiment, the focus was on few data and many classes, and the results
showed that the StarSpace method was at least 34.5% more accurate than LDA. In the
next experiment, more data and only two classes were used. Now, LDA performed at
95.9% accuracy. However, it was shown that StarSpace only needs 1.5% of the same
data, as well as 1.4% of the training time, to reach this level.
In the second two experiments, a conversational agent was built, using Rasa, a
set of open source machine learning tools for developers to create contextual chatbots
and assistants.[51] Some of the technologies used in Rasa are inspired by StarSpace.
First, using 58 different categories in three languages, it was shown that by using
the StarSpace-inspired Rasa, it is possible to build AI assistants that can work on
limited data, in any language. In the last experiment, the attention span of an agent
was investigated, using even fewer data, only 11 different conversation examples. The
results from that experiment indicated that the bot is capable of reacting in the right
way when it receives an affirmative or dissenting answer. However, with this few data,
it was unable to follow a path as a human would do.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In 2005, the number of internet users in the world was at one billion. In 2018, it
had grown to 3.9 billions, and in that year alone, almost a million users joined the
internet every day.[42] Each of these users uploads various kinds of data, including
text, to the internet that can be used for infinite purposes. Since text data are messy,
a way for algorithms to understand them is to embed them in a continuous vector
space, that is, represent them as vectors. Vectors have been used to represent words
since the 1950s when Charles Osgood developed a technique to measure the seman-
tics or meaning of words.[44] Around 1990, different methods for using automatically
generated contextual features were introduced with vector representations more sim-
ilar to those we know today.[43] These vectors, now commonly known as embeddings,
are continually applied to new tasks. With new technology, we are able to use text
data when performing tasks that would have proven very difficult only a few years
ago. Technology giants like Facebook, Google, and Amazon are investing in artificial
intelligence, and along with other smaller companies, they are constantly creating
new technologies.
1.1
Motivation
Companies that collect data often face challenges when working with text. These
challenges are of many kinds and are often solved in inefficient ways with technologies
that have remained popular even though new and more efficient ones have emerged. A
new method from the Facebook AI research team, called StarSpace, was presented in
the fall of 2017. The predominant feature of StarSpace is its generality. Additionally,
it has proven to perform well, outperforming many popular methods for a variety of
tasks.
1.2
Research objectives
The main focus of this thesis is working with text data using StarSpace. The model
will be used to perform topic classification and the results compared to an older
topic labelling method, LDA. Since this mode of using StarSpace is supervised, the
comparison will focus on how few data, and accordingly how little time, are needed
to outperform older unsupervised methods. Furthermore, the use of StarSpace in

Research questions
Since two different kinds of experiments using embeddings are performed, regarding

CHAPTER 2
Making sense of words
This chapter focuses on the evolution of the vector representations of words called
embeddings; how they were developed, when they are pre-trained and when it is
better to learn them from scratch. Some embedding methods that have been used
over the years will also be reviewed as well as the way embeddings have evolved from
being only for words into being for larger or smaller chunks of text, such as sentences
or parts of words. Finally, the manner of which embedding models can capture an
even larger context by looking at all the words in a sentence will be discussed.
2.1
The development of embeddings
Humans use a limited number of sounds and signs to form an unlimited number of
sentences. These sentences have different meanings that help us express the informa-
tion we need. The Distributional Hypothesis is a theory of linguistics, which states
that words that appear in the same contexts share semantic meaning. Essentially, “a
word is characterized by the company it keeps”,[22] i.e. the geometric relationships
between word vectors should reflect the semantic relationships between the respective
words.
2.1.1
The Bag-of-words model
The structure of text data is messier than machine learning algorithms prefer, mak-
ing it hard to model. These algorithms cannot work with raw text directly, so the
text must be converted into vectors of numbers. One of the most popular and sim-
ple methods of extracting features from text enabling the use of machine learning
algorithms is called the Bag-of-Words model. It describes the occurrence of words
within a document and involves a vocabulary of known words as well as a measure
of the presence of them. The Bag-of-words model typically includes a vector for each
document in a given text. In each vector, all the words of all the documents have a
slot, indicating how often that given word appears in the document. An example of
this would be:
• Document 1: Thesis writing is gratifying
• Document 2: It is so rewarding, writing a thesis

The model is only concerned with whether known words occur in the document,
and has no information about the order or structure of the words. It appears that
documents are similar if they have similar content, and from that, we ought to be able
to learn something about the meaning of the document.[11] Some of the disadvantages
of Bag-of-Words models are that we don’t know anything about the words semantics
from them, and the vectors are very sparse. Few words in a large model are likely to
appear in a single document, i.e. words that appear rarely get their own slots and
very similar words are in different slots. Only the frequency of words is measured,
so highly common words such as “and” and “the” could be deemed important for a
document.
2.1.2
Term Frequency - Inverse Document Frequency
Tf-idf (Term Frequency - Inverse Document Frequency) is another way to examine
the subject matter of a text. However, instead of measuring frequency, it measures
relevance. The Term Frequency stands for measuring the number of times that words
appear in a given document, like in a Bag-of-Words model. For Inverse Document
Frequency, a word count is normalized by the frequency of that word in the overall
document collection. The more documents a word appears in, the less relevant it is
to a specific document. Like in the bag-of-words model, each document corresponds
to a vector with the tf-idf values, and each position in the vector is the tf-idf value
of a word. Both the bag-of-words and the tf-idf models produce one number per
word.[68] Tf-idf is explained in equation (2.1), where wi,j is the tf-idf value of word

2.1.3
LSA
Tf-idf can be used as a first step for Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA). LSA is a
technique for creating a vector representation of a document, in which Singular Value
Decomposition (SVD) is used to perform dimensionality reduction on the tf-idf vectors.
It reduces the computational load as well as trades a large number of features for a
smaller set of more contextual features, capturing the largest amount of co-variance
in a term document matrix and making it possible for the computer to distinguish
synonyms. LSA maps words and documents into a “concept” space in which similar

2.1.4
Embeddings
Vector representations can also be learned from scratch, based on deep neural net-
works, where the first layer in the network is called an embedding layer. This layer
functions as a sort of dictionary lookup, which has at least two arguments: a) the num-
ber of possible tokens and b) the dimensionality of the embeddings. It looks up those
variables and returns the associated vectors.
These vectors are three-dimensional
floating-point tensors of shape (samples, sequence length, embedding dimensionality).
Such a tensor can, after the look-up, be processed by either a recurrent or a con-
volutional neural network layer.[14] Word vectors open up many opportunities, such
as boosting the performance of a neural network, extracting features automatically,
establishing synonyms, reducing dimensionality, and creating document vectors.[9]
The role of embeddings is to put linguistic items with semantic similarities into the
same categories, in accordance with their distribution in large samples of language
data. Synonyms, and other words that appear in a similar context, are thus embedded
into similar word vectors and the geometric distance between any two word vectors
should correspond to the semantic distance between the associated words. Directions
in the embedding space also convey meaningful information.
Rather than learning vector representations from scratch in an embedding layer by
training a deep neural network, embeddings can also be generated using algorithms in-
spired by neural networks. These include two distinct model architectures to produce
distributed representations, the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBOW) and Skip-Gram.
These are shallow neural networks which consist of an input layer, a single linear
projection layer, and an output layer. CBOW seeks to predict the word in the middle
given some surrounding words, while Skip-Gram does the opposite: it seeks to predict
the surrounding words given the word in the middle. CBOW is faster, but Skip-Gram
is better for infrequent words and generates more generalizable contexts, making it
more accurate for large datasets.[34] The structure of the CBOW and the Skip-Gram
is shown in Figure 2.1.
The architecture of a neural network with two layers is shown in Figure 2.2. Its input
is a one-hot word vector (a vector where every coefficient except for one is zero) and its
output is a vector with all the words in a vocabulary. Each item in the output vector
matches a word, and the coefficient of the vector is the probability of the respective
word appearing in the same context as the input word in a text. The output of the
network is important to decrease the penalty for a bad prediction, the loss. This is,
however, not necessarily the focus of attention. The remaining layer, the hidden one,

2.2
The acquisition of embeddings
One way to obtain word embeddings is to start with random word vectors and then
improve them in the same way as with weights in a neural network. When the weights
of the embedding layer have been learned, the embedding space will show a structure
specialized for a specific problem which the model can exploit. Training embeddings
from scratch can, however, be difficult, as it takes time and requires considerable
amounts of data for them to be good. They greatly improve when they are run on

Since embeddings vary depending on the domain space and the task to be solved, it
can be practical to learn a new embedding space for every new task. It is nonetheless

that have been pre-trained with another machine learning task. Then, the embedding

into a model. Researchers have now developed a variety of techniques for pre-training,
and the pre-trained model can later be fine-tuned on small-data NLP tasks. This often
results in models with much higher accuracy than training embeddings on a particular
dataset from scratch.
2.3
Word2vec
Word2vec is a popular algorithm that includes pre-trained word vectors. It was devel-
oped by Mikolov et al. at Google in 2013,[37] and is an unsupervised approach based
on the distributional hypothesis. The field has in some ways advanced greatly since
the algorithm was developed, but Word2vec is still popular and had a great impact

from its ability to train vector space models faster than the approaches developed
previously, and the fact that it is easy for anyone to use the accompanying package
to generate their own embeddings.
The algorithm is a two-layer neural net, like shown in Figure 2.2. It processes text
by making embeddings, and despite its shallowness, it turns text into a numerical
form that deep nets can understand. The algorithm detects similarities mathemati-
cally and groups vectors of similar words together in vector space. Without human
intervention, it creates vectors that are distributed numerical representations of word
features, such as the context of individual words. When Word2vec has enough data,
usage, and contexts, it can, based on past appearances, guess a word’s meaning very
accurately and thus establish a word’s association with other words. Another use of
those guesses is clustering documents and classifying by topic, which can then form
the basis of search, sentiment analysis and recommendations in diverse fields. It is
possible to feed the output of the Word2vec neural net, a vocabulary where each item
has a vector attached to it, into a deep-learning net or to query it to detect relation-
ships between words, using cosine similarity.
Word2vec trains words against other neighbouring words in the input corpus. To learn
good embeddings, we need information about the context of a word. Skip-Gram is
preferred to CBOW in Word2vec,[36] and will thus be the focus of this discussion.

2.4
Applications of Word2vec
Word2vec can learn more than just similarity between words. It can, for example,
learn the relationship between words in one language and map them to another, as
shown in Figure 2.3. It also maps gender analogies, where the difference in the vector
space between the vectors for the words “woman” and “man” proves to be the same
as for “queen” and “king”. Another recognized example is for verb tenses. The differ-
ence between “swam” and “swimming” is the same as between “ran” and “running”.
Using the bonus web app on Řehůřek’s Word2vec tutorial web page,[54] which uses
the Word2vec model trained by Google on the Google News dataset on about 100
billion words, I experimented with some additional examples. Firstly, the tool is able
to predict the most similar word in the vector space. Examples of this include Dan-

Another functionality of the tool is that it can determine which word doesn’t fit in
a list - it predicts Norway in Bolivia Peru Argentina Norway (as the only country
not located in South-America). Finally, as previously mentioned, it maps relations of
words. In Figure 2.4, additional examples generated with the tool are explored and
in Figure 2.5 the relationship between capitals and countries in a Word2vec space is
illustrated. Capitals like Lisbon, Berlin, and Copenhagen will cluster. They will also
have similar distances in vector space to the countries they are the capitals of, that
is, Lisbon – Portugal = Berlin – Germany = Copenhagen – Denmark. With this, one
only has to know the capital of one country to get the coordinates of another in a
vector space. For instance, if the capital of Germany is unknown to an individual,
they can look up the coordinates of the vector Lisbon – Portugal + Germany. The
vector should point approximately to Berlin, and whether or not it does is a very
simple way to check if the word embeddings have been computed correctly.

include GloVe (Global Vectors) and FastText.[29] GloVe’s algorithm is derived from
algebraic methods that are similar to matrix factorization.[45] It converges faster than
Word2vec and performs well.

2.5
Embedding other types of entities
Word2vec can be applied to more than just words, i.e. any verbal or symbolic series
that has patterns. The reason is that it only seeks the likelihood that discrete states
will co-occur. The authors of the algorithm, Mikolov et al.,[36] developed a tool to
uniquely represent larger units than words, like phrases and sentences. They found
that by simply adding the word vectors together, they could get meaningful results.
Another approach was to simply represent phrases with a single token. A combi-
nation of these approaches is a general, simple, and yet powerful way to represent
longer pieces of text. The aforementioned FastText is an extension of Word2vec that
uses n-grams, which allows making vectors for words that do not appear often or at
all in the text it is trained on, and thus generally performs better than its predecessor.
Sentence embeddings can often catch more of a structure in the document than word
embeddings. A good algorithm to make baseline sentence embeddings is detailed in
the work of Arora et al.[2] It uses a popular word embedding, encodes a sentence in
linear weighted combination and removes the projection of the vectors on the first
principal component. An even stronger Bag-of-Word baseline is the Concatenated
p-mean Embeddings from the University of Darmstadt, where they generalize the con-

What can be thought of as the sentence version of the Skip-Gram model for word

2.6
Looking at the larger context
Word2vec and GloVe generate a single, context-free word embedding representation
for each word in the vocabulary, but pre-trained representations can also be con-
textual. Then the algorithms generate a representation of a word based on other
words in the sentence, mostly unidirectionally, i.e. based on the preceding context.
Many methods that considerably improve embeddings have emerged recently. An
unsupervised approach, Deep Contextualized Word Representations (ELMo), was de-
veloped by the Allen institute for AI[46] in 2018. In ELMo, each word is assigned
a representation which is a function of the entire corpus sentences to which they
belong. Its inputs are characters rather than words so like FastText, it can get mean-
ing from out-of-vocabulary words.
ELMo also uses different layers of a language
model, with different kinds of information on a word, which it concatenates so it can
freely combine a variety of word representations for improved performance. These
representations were the state-of-the-art contextual word vectors until the emergence
of Google’s Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers (BERT).[18]
BERT builds upon recent work in pre-training contextual representations but is the
first deeply bidirectional, unsupervised language representation, pre-trained using
only a plain text corpus (in this case, Wikipedia). A contextual, bidirectional model
builds on the context of both the preceding words and the following. BERT is called
deeply bidirectional because it starts from the very bottom of a deep neural network.

of-the-art contextual pre-training methods, the unidirectional OpenAI GPT and the

The methods mentioned in this section are unsupervised. They learn to predict an
understandable succession of sentences or clauses inside a sentence, and as a result,
they learn sentence embeddings. In theory, they could use any text dataset with
sentences/clauses placed in a comprehensible way. For a long time, it was thought
that supervised learning of sentence embeddings would give lower-quality embeddings
than unsupervised approaches, but now advances have been made for the former. Su-
pervised learning requires a labelled dataset annotated for a specific task.

3.1
LDA
LDA[6] is a method that has been very popular since its emergence in 2003. It is
a Bayesian model on three levels that generates probabilities for data such as text.
With LDA, a very specific structure of documents is created, where each item of a
collection is modelled as a finite mixture of an underlying set of topics and each topic
is an infinite mixture of an underlying set of topic probabilities. The documents and
the words of the documents are known but the method is unsupervised and the topics
are unknown. The topic level, middle, is thus latent, hidden.
The topic probabilities represent a document and specify what topics each document
is associated with as well as how the word distribution of each topic is. The developer
feeds the algorithm with the number of topics it should cluster the documents into.
Otherwise, the model is unsupervised so the topics don’t have particular names, but
are described with the words they are mainly associated with. That way, it can be
relatively easy for a person to distinguish between the topics and assign a label to
them, but would be difficult for a computer. In other cases, the topics are far from
being obvious and a developer has to go through the procedure repeatedly to get a
sensible classification. The clustering of documents and classification by topic can
form the basis of various techniques, such as search, recommendation, spam detec-
tion, topic labelling, and intent detection. LDA is unsupervised, and many data are
required to get good results. For instance, the algorithm is often trained on all the

3.2
StarSpace
The main focus of this paper is a neural embedding model, developed by the Facebook
AI Research Team in 2017, named StarSpace.[70] It is applicable to various different
problems, such as labelling tasks (text classification), ranking tasks (information re-
trieval) and collaborative filtering-based or content-based recommendation.
What is interesting about StarSpace is its generality, mainly its ability to embed en-
tities of different types in the same vectorial space. It learns to rank a set of entities,
documents, or objects given a query entity, document, or object, where the query is
not necessarily of the same type as the items in the set. An example of this, men-
tioned in the paper,[70] is embedding users in the same space as the pages they have
liked on Facebook, in order to predict whether a certain user would like a new page.

The StarSpace model consists of learning entities, each of which is described by a
bag-of-features coming from a fixed-length dictionary. For instance, documents and
sentences are entities that can be described by a bag of words or n-grams, users are
entities that can be described by the bag of documents, movies, or items they have
liked, the bag of documents they have already or are able to translate, the bag of
questions they have already or are able to answer, etc. The model learns to embed
the entities in the same space such that comparisons are meaningful - by optimizing
with respect to the metric of interest.
Like other embedding models, StarSpace starts by assigning a d-dimensional vector
to each of the discrete features in the dictionary in order to embed them directly.

3.3
The Rasa Stack
Rasa NLU and Rasa Core[7] are the two parts of the Rasa Stack. They are open source
python libraries for building conversational software, mainly built from a recurrent
neural network, an LSTM. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) have loops in them,
allowing information to persist. LSTM stands for Long short-term memory, and it
is built to remember information for long periods of time.[25] In the Rasa Stack, the
LSTM maps from raw dialogue history directly to a distribution over system actions.[4]
Rasa NLU is the natural language understanding component and Rasa Core is the
dialogue management component. The purpose of them is for non-specialist software
developers to be able to use machine-learning based dialogue management and lan-
guage understanding. They are intended to be easy to use and require few initial
training data. The main challenges of an individual contextual AI assistant are to
understand the users, choose the right extractor for each entity, and select the right
hyperparameters and optimize them.[69]
Contextual AI assistants can be very domain specific and have to be custom-tailored
for their users. Rasa NLU provides this customizability by processing user messages
in a so-called pipeline. In this work, the newer TensorFlow pipeline is used, with

Rasa NLU was open sourced in late 2016 and has grown immensely with people build-

using Rasa, is to represent sentences as a sum of word vectors, and then train a

difference. It is mostly the quality or suitability of the word vectors that matters, the
choice of a machine learning model is not significant.
The old pipeline, using spaCy,[26] has its downsides. The use of pre-trained vectors
means that word vectors for some words, for example specific to a domain or contain-
ing a common typo, don’t exist. Most languages, as well as informal language, don’t
have word vectors either, so these words never carry any signal with this approach.
It is also memory inefficient, since tens of thousands of vectors are stored in memory.
Most of them will never be used as conversational chatbots are mostly domain-specific.
One of the most interesting things about StarSpace is its ability to represent objects
of different types in a common vector space. The TensorFlow pipeline was released
in April 2018. It works similar to StarSpace in the sense that its NLU word embed-
ding model maps user utterance text inputs to labels that define the corresponding
intents in the same vector space. After training the model, this allows the Rasa NLU
model to label new utterances not seen before in the training set based on their co-
sine similarity to previously learned labels. This pipeline uses very little memory. It
handles hierarchical intents and messages containing multiple intents and has fewer
out-of-vocabulary issues than the old pipeline. In addition to that, it can perform
intent recognition in any language. It doesn’t use any pre-trained vectors, but learns
embeddings for both the intents and the words simultaneously, from scratch.
No
classifier is trained, but these embeddings are instead used to rank the similarity
between an input sentence and all of the intents. This helps considerably in terms
of learning embeddings for domain-specific language. The algorithm works well on
both small datasets and bigger, although the spaCy pipeline is recommended when
the total number of training examples is under a thousand and there exists a spaCy
model for the language in which the model is being trained.[48]

intents are more similar than others. The intents can be split up as well as combined
and messages that contain multiple intents can be modelled. With a multi-intent

in this work, further demonstrating its generality.
In November 2018, they intro-
duced the Recurrent Embedding Dialogue Policy (REDP),[64] which embeds system
actions and dialogue states in the same vector space. Instead of learning utterances
and intents like in the TensorFlow pipeline in the Rasa chatbot, they use a technique
inspired by StarSpace to learn vector embeddings for dialogue states and system ac-
tions in a supervised setting. This results in the system knowing which intents and
system actions in dialogue sequences contribute the most to the final outcome.
A use case of this technique is that this new embedding policy is much better at
dealing with uncooperative users than the standard LSTM. Most often, in conversa-
tional software, a developer has to either codify every possible conversation manually,
or, conversely, train a system on unannotated dialogue transcript where no domain-
specific logic or code is included. A model is, according to the developers of REDP,[64]
considered “end-to-end” when there is no requirement for domain-specific code and
a policy can be trained on unannotated example dialogues. Even though this tech-
nique is promising, domain-specific slot definitions are still required to fully solve their
tasks, as is pointed out by Bordes et al.[10] and Wen et al.[65] REDP is not end-to-end,
but trained on the output of a Natural Language Understanding system, dialogues
annotated with system actions and dialogue acts. There is no classifier that selects a
system action, but using the StarSpace technique, embeddings are jointly trained for
the dialogue state and each of the system actions, and a similarity function is used
between the state and the actions to rank each candidate.

Topic classification
The first task StarSpace was used for was classification into domains using labelled
data. The results were then compared to those of LDA. For this usage of StarSpace,
the positive pair generator comes directly from a training set where the data specifying
(a, b) pairs have a as documents (bags-of-words) and b as labels (singleton features).
The negative entities, b−, are sampled from the set of possible labels.
4.1
Dataset
For these experiments, the 20 Newsgroups dataset was used.[20] It consists of around
18,000 newsgroups posts on twenty topics. The version loaded was from the datasets
that Ana Cardoso Cachopo used in her PhD work,[12] with the text cleaned of non-
words and words that are less than three characters long. The data were downloaded
with a train/test split of 60%/40%, based on the time stamp of messages posted.
Instead of using that, I shuffled the training and test sets and made a split of 80%/20%.
The text was preprocessed by lemmatizing1, stemming2, and tokenizing3 it, and then
removing English stopwords (listed in the Gensim library[55]). For LDA, the text was
unlabelled and for StarSpace it was labelled and detokenized (after the preprocessing).
4.2
Experiments
For the experiments with this dataset, the accuracy of StarSpace and LDA were
investigated for the split of twenty topics into twenty, fourteen, and five classes. Both
the accuracy of an LDA’s parallelized bag-of-words model and its tf-idf model were
examined.

Discussion
In this chapter, the results of this research will be reviewed, in relation to prior work
and to the research questions stated in the introduction. The chapter is split into
four sections, the first three discuss the results of the experiments conducted and
their significance in context of prior knowledge. In the final section, the limitations
of this research are considered.
8.1
Discussion of the results for topic classification
The first experiment focused on comparing StarSpace to LDA for the task of topic
modelling with few data and many topics that were split into a different number of
classes; twenty, fourteen and five. StarSpace always performed much better using
the same data as LDA, with approximately the same training time. The difference
between the quality of StarSpace and the bag-of-words model for LDA, i.e.
the
classification accuracy, grew with more classes, ranging from 34.52% for five topics
to 43.53% for twenty topics. The difference between StarSpace and the LDA tf-idf
model was much greater, ranging from 43.79% for five topics to 73.30% for twenty.
For the second experiment, where there were only two classes with a lot more data,
LDA was quite good. Both the bag-of-words and tf-idf models had over 43.79% clas-
sification accuracy. However, we only need 1.5% of the same data (using a 3-gram)
and running time to get better accuracy using StarSpace. With all the data, the
difference isn’t as big. StarSpace acquires 99.13% accuracy, 3.27% higher than the
LDA bag-of-words model and 3.64% higher than the LDA tf-idf model. For these
data, the bag-of-words and tf-idf models of LDA return very similar results.
8.1.1
The results of the LDA model
The main drawback of the LDA model is that since it is unsupervised, it can divide
data into topics by a structure that is of no use to the user. For the second experi-
ment, when only two topics were made available for a larger dataset, LDA’s choice of
topics was the same as the pre-existing labels. The model performed well, with over
95% accuracy for both the bag-of-words and the tf-idf models. On the other hand,
the performance in the first experiment, with more topics and a smaller dataset, var-

ied each time it was run. The results depended on how easy the generated topics
were to assign to the labels. This could be a problem for any company that relies
on assigning documents to a domain in order to choose which employee, possessing a
domain-specific knowledge, is best suited to work on it.
LDA’s models performed worse with a growing number of topics that were difficult

its topic generation were from the label Middle East. The accuracy of the models
increased as the number of topics to assign labels to decreased. The fourteen topics
were chosen with the help of crowdsourcing, considering the name of the topic and
the most descriptive words according to tf-idf. The five topics were chosen mostly
based on the category of the data in the dataset.
8.1.2
Discussion of the results for different parameters
Gensim’s LDA, used in this work, offers a tf-idf calculation. However, calculating
the inverse document frequency of a document can be counter-productive since tf-idf
generally performs better for singularity tasks like search and ranking and tells us
what is unique about each document. Classification, on the other hand, is supposed
to gather similarity and convey information about what is common among documents.
Tf-idf does not perform well when the classes are unbalanced, or when words are very
common in documents of one class. Then these words might get a low idf-score even
though this prevalence should make them an excellent predictive feature for a class.
The classes in the data used in this work are well balanced but tf-idf performed very
poorly, especially in the twenty class classification, reaching only about 16% accuracy.
The data produced with tf-idf mostly eliminated common words that were descriptive
for a certain class and left behind proper nouns, which proved difficult to use for the
classification.
For the classification tasks performed with StarSpace, different n-grams were run for
each split. When classifying, it can be helpful to get the context of words by using
n-grams. With n = 2, 3, 4, this turned out to be helpful in all four experiments and
led to a higher correct prediction rate, but the accuracy of the correct classification
started declining when n got bigger. For the bigger dataset with few classes, the
accuracy started declining already with n > 2. With longer n-grams, each n-gram is
seen more rarely in the data, making them more sparse and harder to classify. Many
phrases only appear once, with little apparent relation to existing ones.

8.1.3
The choice of methods
LDA has, since its introduction in 2003, been one of the most popular topic classifi-
cation methods. It is very popular for clustering large amounts of unannotated data.
When a user has a big data collection with no knowledge of what it contains, LDA is
thus an excellent method to uncover the basic underlying structure of the data, as it
classifies topics in an unsupervised manner based on some kind of similarity. However,
since the method is popular and performs reasonably well trained on, for example,
the whole Wikipedia corpus, many companies that have a clear view of what their
data consist of and which categories they should be divided into, are using LDA for
topic classification. For those who wish their data to be classified into certain groups,
the method is inferior. In this work, it has been shown that StarSpace performs much
better than LDA, needing only a small fraction of the data and training time for more
accuracy. These results indicate that companies that collect data and have the means
to annotate them as soon as they are received could, by using StarSpace, get a more
accurate, fitting, and memory-efficient topic classification.
Many supervised embeddings have shown promise on classification tasks, including
fastText and TagSpace.[67] In the StarSpace paper,[70] the method is compared to
newer, competing methods for this task of text classification. There, it was shown to
outperform a number of methods, including bag of Words[74] and char-CRNN.[71] It
performed similarly to fastText and LSTM, much faster than deep learning methods
but slower than fastText. Since the main aspect of StarSpace is its generality, this is
not a concern. It can’t be expected to perform the best and fastest for every possible
task.

Generally, as is evident by the experiment, the Rasa NLU performs much better than
the Rasa Core. Often, the user input is exactly like a defined intent but is still not
recognized by the core. For example, the sentences How do we relieve ourselves from
suffering? and How do I relieve myself from suffering? both occur as NLU examples
in the source code. The interpreter recognizes them with around 95% confidence,
but the agent doesn’t understand the questions and reverts to its fallback response.
Another case was when the user asked a question that was part of the NLU examples.
NLU recognized the question How do enlightenment and liberation work together?
correctly with 94% confidence. The core, however, was even more confident (96%)
that the question had been about mindfulness.
This experiment was conducted with very few data. The agent was able to react in an
appropriate way when it received an affirmative or dissenting user input. With this
amount of data and short conversations, it was unable to choose the correct response
based exclusively on where it was located on a pre-defined path. For example, a dis-
senting user input could mean that the user did not want an explanation for a certain
concept, or conversely that the user wanted to finish the conversation altogether.
8.3
Analysis of the research questions
After investigating the results of this work, the focus is now shifted to the research
questions regarding how few data we need when using word embeddings to get ac-
ceptable results. For the first experiments, on topic classification, the result was fixed
==PAGE:86==
72

when comparing StarSpace to LDA. We need 1.5% of the dataset, and 1.4% of the
training time, to get better classification accuracy using StarSpace than using LDA
with the whole dataset.
In the latter experiments, on the design of a conversational interface using Rasa, the
question is how few data are needed for a conversational agent to perform at a level
that a user can accept in a dialogue. The results of a 10-fold cross-validation using all
languages are 76% accurate, peaking at around 80% when the conversation is limited
to English. Most users of chatbots would say that this is not an acceptable result, as
people would in general not like to participate in a conversation where the other party
responds incorrectly 20 −25% of the time. Additionally, the accuracy was measured
on the Rasa NLU performance, which recognized intents better than the Rasa Core,
but was not responsible for producing answers.
However, considering that the bot is only trained on ten possible utterances for each
intent, the results are pretty impressive. The last experiment gave an example on
how a conversational agent built with Rasa can use previous dialog to predict future
actions. Its results indicate that even though it requires much fewer data than con-
versational agents have generally needed, a considerable amount of thought has to be
put in to building the dataset and designing the bot in order for it to perform as well
as the technology offers.
8.4
Research limitations
The main disadvantage when conducting research using StarSpace was that as the
technology is very new, it was hard to grasp. The documentation does not provide
much information, so it took a long time to get examples running. Because of the nov-
elty of the method, it was also difficult to get answers to questions that arose as few
other users had worked on the same problem. This was especially true when building
the conversational bot with Rasa. Example code is generally given in Bash, Python,
or both. Many examples are only given in Bash, which made them hard to fit together
in the iPython notebook I was using. For instance, the new embedding policy, REDP,
is an interesting improvement of the model. I wanted to add the technology as an ex-
tra step but was unable to find any examples on how to implement it in Python, thus
I was unsuccessful. Rasa is still under development, so the few users that have had
the same problems were likely running their code, now outdated, on previous versions.
Another limitation was the absence of personal data. For the first experiments re-
garding topic classification, it would have been interesting to see the method applied
to domain-specific data, in order to test StarSpace in an actual work setting.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This thesis has focused on the beneficial use of word embeddings in various tasks.
Experiments were conducted on modelling documents and AI chatbots. In the first
two experiments, the accuracy of a new general-purpose neural embedding model,
StarSpace, was compared to an older method, LDA, for the task of topic classifica-
tion. StarSpace proved to perform much better on all accounts, both faster and more
efficient, using only 1.5% of the data for better results. The biggest difference was
when the data were few and the topics into which they were classified were many.
Using a supervised method like StarSpace also allows users to control which topics
the data should be distributed to, as well as having data that fit their domain better.
In the later experiments, a conversational AI chatbot was built, using a technique
inspired by StarSpace. It was relatively simple to build, and required very few data
as it doesn’t use pre-trained embeddings. This also made it general, as it is non-
specific to language. Furthermore, it can handle multiple intents at once, handles
out-of-vocabulary issues well, and has an attention mechanism that allows it to use
previous dialogue to predict future actions. In the third experiment, each intent was
only trained on ten different utterances and many similar intents, and performed at
72.1-80.8% accuracy. The language it performed worst in was Icelandic, which has a
difficult grammar and a tradition to close compound words. It performed best in En-
glish, which is a more computer friendly language. Considering that the small dataset
is built from scratch and many intents are similar, this technology clearly has poten-
tial. In the latter experiment focusing on the conversational agent, the bot’s memory
was tested.
With only 11 different paths, the bot could easily respond based on
whether the answer it received was positive or negative and that way, trace the paths
as expected. However, it could not follow a path based exclusively on its location.
That is, when receiving an answer that could potentially point to different points in
a path and thus, invoke different responses, it was unable to choose the right response.
Future work should include trials with more data as well as data that are personalized
for the topic classification. The chatbot has many uncharted territories, including
using the new REDP pipeline instead of the older spaCy one for a technique based
even further on StarSpace.
Finally, further exploring the generality of StarSpace,
such as training word embeddings in an unsupervised manner, could be combined
with some of the experiments conducted in this work.
