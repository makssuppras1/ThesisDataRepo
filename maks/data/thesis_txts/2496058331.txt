Abstract
Traditionally, urban water planning only had one or a few objectives, but long-term planning
with more objectives is gaining increasing focus, since multi-objective strategies potentially satis-
ﬁes multiple urban planning goals and attracts many stakeholders, which is crucial in cities with
limited space. This thesis evaluates the economic feasibility of three nature-based climate adap-
tation strategies in 12 future urban development and climate scenarios in Højme and Sanderum,
Odense. A rain water basin, a channel, and a restored creek are assessed using a multi-objective
cost-beneﬁt analysis. The assessment applies the planning objectives framework by Skrydstrup
et al. (2019b,a) and includes 6 planning objectives: Flood risk, Traﬃc safety, Noise, Recreation,
Air quality and Low costs. A sensitivity analysis is conducted to explore and quantify the uncer-
tainty of models and methods inﬂuencing the planning objectives, and to evaluate the robustness
of the decision-making parameter. 14 parameters or factors inﬂuencing the decision-making pa-
rameter are selected based on a preliminary uncertainty assessment and tested using plausible
ranges.
The cost-beneﬁt analyses shows, that the rain water basin proves to be the best strategy yield-
ing a positive net present value in 10 out of 12 scenarios, while the channel yields a positive
net present value in 4 out of 12 scenarios, and the creek strategy is demonstrated to not be
cost-eﬃcient in all scenarios. Thus, the basin is deemed an economically feasible solution across
varying urban development and climate scenarios. The planning objectives Flood risk and Low
costs are dominating factors for feasibility in all strategies and scenarios, while the remaining
objectives seem of less signiﬁcance. The potential added values of adaptation strategies seem to
be underestimated due to limitations in applied methods and possible omitted relevant indica-
tors and objectives. Scenario comparison shows larger impact of climate change than of urban
development on ﬂood risk. Subjective choices for model parameters and ways of implementing
adaptation measures seem crucial for the magnitude of estimated damages. The results outline
the importance of performing sensitivity analyses in ﬂood modelling, when limited knowledge
and calibration data is available. The noise introduced through modelling proves to inﬂuence the
cost-beneﬁt analysis and could be the determining factor of economic feasibility for adaptation
strategies.
The thesis concludes, that methods for quantiﬁcation and valuation are of signiﬁcant importance
for socio-economic assessment of adaptation strategies and stresses the importance of including
and understanding all relevant indicators, as these can aﬀect the preference between strategies
and inﬂuence the choice of optimal adaptation.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Flood risk management has gained increased attention during the last decades, due to the chang-
ing climate and urbanisation (IPCC, 2012). The world’s population is increasing and factors such
as jobs, network, culture and prosperity pull people to the cities across the globe. As urban areas
struggle to accommodate this accelerating population growth, it results in an increase in imper-
meable surfaces and high rise buildings, as well as the transformation of natural green spaces
into new urban areas to meet the increasing demand for urban dwelling (Kaspersen and Halsnæs,
2017). The increasing population density implies major environmental and social challenges; poor
air and water quality, environmental degradation, diversity losses and income disparities. These
are some of the challenges that cities need to manage in order to increase their liveability.
During the last decades, urban ﬂooding due to cloudbursts and heavy precipitation events has
happened more often and caused major damage and losses in northern Europe (IPCC, 2012;
Olsen et al., 2015; Zhou and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018). The potential damage from ﬂooding is
particularly high in cities, due to the dense concentration of people and assets (Freni et al.,
2010), and at the same time the continuing addition of impermeable surfaces puts pressure on
the existing sewer systems leading to higher ﬂood risk. Sewers and storm water pipes are of-
ten aﬀected by surcharging problems due to a combination of ageing pipe infrastructures and
continuously increasing urbanisation (Freni et al., 2010). Frequently, surface runoﬀcannot be
completely collected by drainage systems, and sewer systems may be insuﬃcient to convey peak
discharges resulting in ﬂooding on terrain (Fontanazza et al., 2011), why long-term sustainable
urban development will be essential to manage this as well as other challenges (Skrydstrup et al.,
2019b). It is complex but necessary to plan and develop urban areas, which can sustain and
harmonize society with the environment, both for tomorrow and for the future.
Traditionally, urban ﬂood management has focused on grey solutions, such as pipes and under-
ground infrastructures (Alves et al., 2019). Today, focus on the use of blue and green infrastruc-
ture (BGI) as ﬂood risk mitigation measures has increased (Fletcher et al., 2015). BGI in urban
spaces provides numerous complementary beneﬁts besides ﬂood risk reduction, such as air qual-
ity improvement, noise reduction, and physical health improvements (Alves et al., 2019). The
added values from climate adaptation solutions can be diﬃcult to evaluate, since they typically
are non-market values which are hard to quantify and valuate. Previous studies have analysed
costs and beneﬁts of BGI solutions and ecosystem services, but most studies seem to either be
very case-speciﬁc or diﬃcult to transfer to other contexts (Kaval and Baskaran, 2013; Gallus,
2018; Skrydstrup et al., 2019b). Therefore, the methods for quantiﬁcation and valuation of added
value from BGI must be chosen carefully to provide reliable results for decision-makers (Alves
et al., 2018).
Modelling and assessment of ﬂood risk is complex and aﬀected by large uncertainties associated
with present and future hazard and vulnerability conditions. Knowledge of these uncertainties
1
==PAGE:14==
and their inﬂuence on the model outcome is crucial to validate the robustness of the risk analysis
and for decision makers to choose optimal adaptation strategies. The aleatory uncertainties can
be determined and thereby reduced, while epistemic uncertainties and uncertainty related to
future scenarios are more diﬃcult to assess (Beven et al., 2015; Maier et al., 2016). In literature,
sensitivity analyses have identiﬁed damage cost functions, roughness parameters, grid size, runoﬀ
volume, thresholds for damage, and costing of ﬂoods, as some of the most inﬂuential parame-
ters (Wagenaar et al., 2016; Zhou and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018; Adeogun et al., 2015). Zhou and
Arnbjerg-Nielsen (2018) found that discount rate, investment cost, and runoﬀvolume inﬂuence
the decision most when comparing adaptation strategies. Furthermore, an increasing recogni-
tion of non-stationarity due to e.g. climate change or urban development means that ﬂood risk
management includes consideration of the uncertain ways in which ﬂood risk may change in the
future, as it may be economically unfeasible to plan for just one ’best-guess’ scenario (Hall and
Solomatine, 2008; Gersonius et al., 2013; Maier et al., 2016). Gersonius et al. (2013), Maier et al.
(2016), and Walker et al. (2013) describe how to make robust decision making under great uncer-
tainty about future scenarios, and discuss the concepts of ﬂexible or dynamic decision-making,
which involves long-term strategies that can develop in diﬀerent directions.
This study aims to evaluate the eﬃciency of three climate adaptation strategies in diﬀerent
future scenarios inﬂuenced by a changing climate and plausible urban development in a selected
case area. The climate adaptation strategies will be evaluated using a holistic analysis including
valuation of added values of climate adaptation strategies. Furthermore, the study aims to assess
the inﬂuence of uncertainties on the decision-making parameter and evaluate robustness.
Thus this study has three objectives:
• Assess the ﬂood risk of urban development scenarios in Højme, Odense using hydrodynamic
modelling in present and future climates
• Evaluate the eﬃciency of climate adaptation strategies using multi-objective economic val-
uation of deﬁned planning objectives (including added values)
• Explore and quantify uncertainties in the analysis and assess their impact on decision-
making parameters using sensitivity analysis
The thesis is outlined as follows: Chapter 2 presents the case study and includes a description of
future scenarios and adaptation strategies. Chapter 3 presents the methodology and theoretical
concepts involving cost-beneﬁt analysis, assessment of ﬂood risk and added values of climate
adaptation, and analysis of uncertainties related to modelling, methods and future scenarios. In
Chapter 4, the input data, software environment, and model setup are presented. The main
ﬁndings are presented in Chapter 5, and a discussion of the results follows in Chapter 6. Finally,
the thesis is summarised into main conclusions in Chapter 7.

Case Study
This chapter presents the study area of Højme and Sanderum in Odense, along with proposed
future urban development scenarios. The climate adaptation strategies are introduced and de-
scribed.
2.1
Study area: Højme and Sanderum
The study area is located in the southwestern part of Odense on both sides of the motorway
as shown in Figure 2.1. The southern area is in the surburb Højme, while the area north of
the motorway is in Sanderum. Odense is the third largest city in Denmark with a population
of more than 200,000 inhabitants, and with a continuous urban expansion (DST, 2019; Odense
Municipality, 2019b) that increases the demand for new areas developed for housing and industry.
The elevation in the catchment varies and slopes downwards towards east from around 50 metres
above sea level (masl) in the western part to 10 masl in the eastern part near Odense ˚A (Odense
Creek).

2.1 Study area: Højme and Sanderum
This study focuses on two areas: the western part, where the new urban areas will be developed
and the eastern part in Sanderum, which is downstream of the new area, and where the climate
adaptation strategies will be implemented. Sanderum is a suburb which is mainly dominated by
low-rise residential buildings, but also has some public and commercial buildings (Laursen and
Linderberg, 2017). In the last decade, ﬂooding has been frequent in the area around Holkeb-
jergvænget, as well as in the area close to the intersection between Vestre Boulevard and Assensvej
(Odense Municipality, 2014), both highlighted on Figure 2.1. The former Sanderum Creek ran
through this area as shown in Figure 2.6, but is now running in a pipe below surface.
The land in the western part of the study area was until recently mainly used for agriculture,
but in 2017 the city council decided to buy the land south of the motorway and parcel it out to
industrial companies as a part of the local urban plan (Odense Municipality, 2017). To manage
the increased amount of storm water runoﬀfrom the new industrial area, a rainwater basin was
constructed in the area north of the motorway. The areas further west and south are the next
to be developed, and plausible development scenarios will be described in the following section.
2.1.1
Urban development scenarios
Four future urban development scenarios for the area west of Højme and Sanderum are proposed
and analysed in this study. The scenarios are developed in collaboration with the local author-
ities, and include diﬀerent types of development for the area (see Figure 2.2. The municipality
is currently planning aﬀorestation in a large area north of the motorway, which is included in all
scenarios along with the already developed 49 ha industrial area south of the highway. The four
development scenarios consist of additional aﬀorestation (F), housing development (H), expan-
sion of existing industrial area (I), and a combination of housing and industrial development (HI).
The scenario generating the lowest amount of runoﬀis scenario F, while scenario HI results in the
largest amount of runoﬀwhich needs to be managed. It is assumed that a separate wastewater
pipe system will be established with the future urban development, and wastewater and storm
water will therefore be handled separately. The scenarios are introduced separately below, and
supporting calculations for assumptions can be found in Appendix A. Diﬀerences between the
scenarios are summarised in Table 2.1.
Scenario F: Aﬀorestation
The area north of the motorway will be included in the aﬀorestation plan in this scenario, as
shown in Figure 2.2. Trees are planned to cover the area, which will increase the permeability.
Most precipitation will be inﬁltrated in the forest, hence, the storm water which needs to be
handled downstream is mainly runoﬀfrom the industrial area.
Scenario H: Housing development
The population growth is expected to continue in the future, which increases the demand for
housing in the area. In this scenario, 18.4 ha land north of the motorway will be sold for real
estate development. This will increase the amount of impermeable surfaces, and thereby generate
more storm water runoﬀ. It is assumed that the new housing area will be home to approximately
1,043 residents in 348 households. The population growth in this area is assumed to increase the
overall traﬃc in Sanderum by 5%, causing an increase in noise level. The increase in households

Methodology
The study is structured around the steps outlined in Figure 3.1. Two main ﬂows deﬁne the
work in this study: assessment of adaptation strategies in scenarios and sensitivity analysis.
The assessment of adaptation strategies in scenarios ﬂow includes a multi-objective cost-beneﬁt
analysis used for evaluation of the adaptation strategies in various proposed urban development
and climate scenarios, and a comparison of strategies between scenarios to explore scenario un-
certainty. The sensitivity analysis ﬂow includes initial screening of uncertainties, followed by
selection and testing of various parameters to explore uncertainty related to model and methods
(model uncertainty). Finally, the outcomes of each ﬂow are used for evaluation of robustness of
adaptation strategies.
In this chapter the basic principles, the terminology, and the methodology applied in the analysis
are presented. First, the methodology behind cost-beneﬁt analysis and the relevant planning
objectives is introduced. Thereafter, the diﬀerent methods to quantify and valuate the planning
objectives are described, starting with the diﬀerent steps in the pluvial ﬂood risk modelling
framework, e.g., the hazard and vulnerability assessments, before the methodology behind the
remaining objectives, is introduced. Finally, the uncertainties related to scenarios, the model and
quantiﬁcation methods are presented, and sensitivity analysis and its application in this study is
introduced.

3.1 Cost-beneﬁt analysis
3.1
Cost-beneﬁt analysis
Cost-beneﬁt analysis (CBA) is a well known tool for analysing and comparing various economic
scenarios.
CBA can help decision-makers in the evaluation of ﬂood adaptation measures by
strengthening the basis for making sound investment decisions (Halsnæs et al., 2017).
CBA
aims is to provide economic estimates of how the beneﬁts associated with implementing speciﬁc
adaptation measures can balance the implementation costs. The principle of the analysis is that
all costs and beneﬁts associated with a project must be quantiﬁed and expressed in the same
value system to arrive at a single number, answering the question of whether an implementation
is economically viable or not. Using CBA, multiple projects can be compared to ﬁnd the best
socio-economic investment.
For any year within a project’s planning horizon, the net beneﬁts of climate change adaptation
can be calculated as beneﬁts minus costs. The beneﬁts and costs of adaptation measures accrue
with time (Zhou et al., 2012), why the time value of money must be taken into account when
evaluating the feasibility of projects.
CBA addresses the issue of making costs and beneﬁts
occurring at diﬀerent points in time comparable by applying a discount rate to convert future or
historic economic impacts into present-day values, i.e., applying a positive discount rate places
a higher weight on present beneﬁts than on future beneﬁts (Halsnæs et al., 2017). All costs and
beneﬁts for an adaptation project are summarised for a speciﬁed planning horizon using this
Net Present Value (NPV), where the project is economically feasible if the NPV is positive, i.e.,
having beneﬁts outweighing costs. The NPV is calculated as shown in Equation 3.1:

The discount rate has a large inﬂuence on the NPV, especially for projects with a long planning

projects (Concito, 2011), and even small modiﬁcations can alter the feasibility of an adaptation
project. A high discount rate results in low present value of future beneﬁts and costs, hence it
will be less beneﬁcial to make large investments today to reduce loss in the future. Appropriate
values have been discussed in literature (Arnbjerg-Nielsen and Fleischer, 2009). The discount
rate is based on the economic growth, and it is in general recommended to use national or inter-
national recommendations depending on where the project is implemented (Halsnæs et al., 2017).
The use of CBA requires a monetary quantiﬁcation of all costs and beneﬁts associated with
an adaptation strategy. Traditionally, climate adaptation measures have been evaluated from a
single-goal perspective of ﬂood mitigation (Engstr¨om et al., 2018), where the beneﬁts are quan-

maintenance costs of adaptation measures (Zhou et al., 2012). However, when it comes to assess-

3.1.1
Overview of planning objectives
Historically, water management strategies only had one or a few objectives, i.e., related to re-
duce ﬂooding, but today it is more common to plan holistically for more objectives (Skrydstrup
et al., 2019b; Engstr¨om et al., 2018). Multi-objective strategies are potentially satisfying more
urban planning goals and attract more stakeholders, which is crucial in cities with limited space
to be shared between many. Multi-objective projects appeal to more stakeholders, but are also
often more expensive, and can furthermore be more comprehensive and involve long bureaucratic
procedures. Besides reducing the risk of ﬂooding, the objectives can contribute to beneﬁts such
as improving urban livability, health, and safety (Skrydstrup et al., 2019b; Alves et al., 2019).
Quantiﬁcation and valuation of multiple objectives might add additional monetary value to a
climate adaptation strategy, making the investment strategy sound, while other objectives might
have a negative eﬀect by e.g., increasing the noise level or aggravating air quality.
A framework for quantiﬁcation and valuation of planning objectives by Skrydstrup et al. (2019a,b)
is applied in this study. The framework is still under development. Six relevant planning objec-
tives are chosen for assessing and comparing the three climate adaptation strategies. The selected
objectives are ﬂood risk reduction, improvement of traﬃc safety and air quality, reduction of noise
level, increase of recreational possibilities, and low implementation cost of adaptation. Some of
the objectives are only relevant for one or two of the evaluated climate adaptation strategies. A
brief description of the challenges attributed to each objective is given in Table 3.1.

3.2
Flood risk assessment
The pluvial ﬂood risk assessment framework by Zhou et al. (2012) (see Figure 3.2) is used for
evaluation of the eﬃciency of climate change adaptation measures in terms of ﬂood damage
reduction. The framework is an approach to quantify ﬂood risk in monetary terms and consists
of two main components: a ﬂood hazard assessment and a ﬂood vulnerability assessment. The
hazard assessment focuses on characterisation of pluvial ﬂoods in terms of probability and extent
of ﬂoods of diﬀerent magnitudes, and includes investigation of overland ﬂow paths and relevant
hazards indicators. The vulnerability assessment is based on an analysis of the damages and losses
incurring when a physical asset or intangible good is exposed to a given hazard. Combining the
12
==PAGE:25==
3.2 Flood risk assessment
analysis of the hazard with the vulnerability gives an assessment of the risk related to ﬂooding
for a speciﬁc area. This is done by means of a Geographical Information System-based (GIS)
risk model yielding the risk at a given point in time.
Figure 3.2: The pluvial ﬂood risk framework by Zhou et al. (2012).
3.2.1
Hazard assessment
The ﬂood hazards are computed by coupling a two dimensional (2D) inundation model with a one
dimensional (1D) drainage system model. The use of a coupled model allows for an integrated
simulation of urban ﬂooding that includes water exchanges between surface and subsurface and
thereby ﬂooding from exceeded sewer capacity (DANVA, 2011). Given input rainfall, a rainfall-
runoﬀrelationship, the drainage system hydraulics and the physical layout of the terrain, the
coupled 1D-2D hydrodynamic model computes the extent and depth of the ﬂooding in the urban
setting. The result of the ﬂood hazard assessment is the maximum water depth and ﬂood extent
within the simulation period for the input rainfall at a given point in time. The components of
the model are described below, whereas the data is described in Chapter 4.
Extreme rainfall
Precipitation is the primary driver of pluvial ﬂooding (Zhou and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018) and is
used as external loading. Design rainfalls constructed using the Chicago Design Storm (CDS)
principle are used, as these represent rainfall intensities across a spectrum of durations for a
deﬁned return period (DHI, 2015). Thus, simultaneous occurrence of extreme events of various
duration can be simulated using a single CDS proﬁle, testing all parts of the complex drainage
system (Spildevandskomiteen, 2005). The CDS rainfalls of various return periods used are based
on a regional Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) relationship in accordance with Danish design
13
==PAGE:26==
3.2 Flood risk assessment
practices, a relationship which is estimated by statistical analysis of historical rainfall measure-
ments from high-resolution rain gauges (DHI, 2015; Madsen et al., 2002).
The impact of climate change on extreme rainfall is described using climate factors (CF) express-
ing the future rainfall volume relative to the current. A CF of 1.3 indicates a 30% increase in
future rainfall volume and a CF of 1.0 indicates no change. Climate factors of 1.2, 1.3 and 1.4
are recommended as design guidelines in Denmark for 2, 10 and 100 year return periods for a
planning horizon of 100 years (Spildevandskomiteen, 2014).
Urban hydrology and hydraulics
The catchment hydrology and hydraulics of the urban drainage system is computed using the
modelling software MIKE URBAN. A precipitation-runoﬀrelationship based on a kinematic wave
computation is used to derive runoﬀfrom deﬁned urban catchments (DHI, 2017b). Parameters
of importance for runoﬀinclude catchment speciﬁc imperviousness and initial losses (Zhou and
Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018; Davidsen et al., 2018). The sewer ﬂow is described by numerically solv-
ing the vertically integrated Saint Venant equations of conservation of continuity and momentum
(DHI, 2017a), where parameters of particular importance for ﬂow conditions are Manning number
and pipe slope. Limited measurements of hydrological and hydraulic parameters are available,
therefore standard model settings are often adopted (Zhou and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018). The
quantity of waste water in the urban setting is static and pre-deﬁned for each catchment.
The 2D inundation model is used to simulate the unsteady two dimensional overland ﬂow by
integrating the equations for mass and momentum conservation (DHI, 2016). The terrain to-
pography is represented by a two dimensional digital elevation model (DEM) in a grid system.
A hydrologically ﬁtted terrain model ensuring possible ﬂow under bridges is used, and buildings
and roads are introduced in the DEM for proper representation of surface ﬂow paths. A surface
Manning number introduces the surface roughness in the model. Parameters deﬁning the ex-
change of water between the 1D and 2D models include coupling inlet area and maximum ﬂow.
Guidelines for deﬁning coupling parameters are rare, but Titterington et al. (2017) recommends
deﬁning these using standard weir equations.
3.2.2
Vulnerability assessment
The vulnerability assessment is conducted to estimate the damage cost related to the hazard of a
precipitation event of a given magnitude. Knowledge about the vulnerability of elements at risk
is necessary to identify appropriate risk reduction measures, and economic damage assessment of
hazards supply crucial information to support decision-makers in the ﬁeld of adaptation planning
to climate change (Merz et al., 2010). In the ﬂood risk framework by Zhou et al. (2012), damage
is divided into tangible two parts: physical damage and intangible losses.
Tangible physical
damage refers to direct impacts, e.g., on private property, public buildings and spaces, and
infrastructure. Intangible losses cover eﬀects of pluvial ﬂooding, e.g., recreational values, health,
and traﬃc disturbances. In practical applications, the main focus of the vulnerability analysis is
often on the tangible damages because they can be easily estimated based on monetary costs, and
can be related to hydraulic variables such as ﬂooding depth (Freni et al., 2010; Merz et al., 2004,
2010). In most models, ﬂooding depth is treated as the determining factor for expected damage,
14
==PAGE:27==

and although factors such as ﬂow velocity and duration of inundation also inﬂuence damage costs,
there is no comprehensive approach for including such factors in damage modelling (Merz et al.,
2010; Thieken et al., 2005).
Damage functions
The relationship between ﬂood depth and the resulting monetary damage is typically represented
by staged depth-damage functions. For a given ﬂood depth the function gives expected losses to
a speciﬁc property or land-use type, either as a percentage of a pre-deﬁned asset value or directly
in ﬁnancial terms (Jongman et al., 2012). The structure, inputs, and outputs of a speciﬁc damage
model are deﬁned by the available data and the purpose of the model, and are by deﬁnition based
on a degree of generalisation (Jongman et al., 2012). The level of detail of diﬀerent depth-damage
functions can vary signiﬁcantly, and depth-damage functions are largely unavailable for many ur-
ban property and land-use types due to the limited observation availability and poor knowledge
of variation of studied damage (Zhou and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018). Freni et al. (2010) suggests to
generally use simpliﬁed approaches unless more reliable data about damage is available. In this
study, a simpliﬁed approach is used, which assigns uniform unit costs to ﬂooded units when the
ﬂooding depth rises above certain critical thresholds (Zhou et al., 2012; Olsen et al., 2015). L¨owe
and Arnbjerg-Nielsen (2019) found, that damage estimations from pluvial ﬂooding in Odense
proved to be more robust when using a threshold-based approach than depth-damage curves, un-
derlining that the applicability of depth-damage functions in dense urban areas is questionable.

3.4
Uncertainties and scenarios
Flood risk assessments are often based on several assumptions about present and future hazard
and vulnerability conditions, and input data is related to some degree of uncertainty as well
24
==PAGE:37==
3.4 Uncertainties and scenarios
(Kaspersen and Halsnæs, 2017). The outcome of the assessment represents a propagation of
all associated uncertainties in the analysis, and the result and following conclusion are therefore
inherently uncertain (Zhou and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2018). Since the outcome of a ﬂood risk assess-
ment is used for decision-making, it is highly relevant to distinguish between and understand the
impact of diﬀerent uncertainties. Analysis of uncertainties illustrate the implications of diﬀerent
assumptions, thus helping improve the underlying models in order to yield more reliable results
(Apel et al., 2008). Furthermore, an uncertainty analysis may signiﬁcantly improve the decision
quality because a wide range of unknowns is investigated (Merz and Thieken, 2005).
Future ﬂood risk is highly impacted by climate change and urban development, and projections of
these parameters are subject to substantial uncertainties over the lifetime of climate adaptation
measures (L¨owe and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2019). When designing adaptation solutions, it is of great
importance that the solutions are able to accommodate future changes, hence, the necessity to
describe the uncertainties associated with future conditions. However, these uncertainties are
characterised by unknowns about the future that cannot be reduced by gathering more informa-
tion and are not statistical in nature (Walker et al., 2013). Such uncertainty is known as ’deep
uncertainty’ (see e.g. Walker et al. (2013)), and is something that decision-makers are facing
in many strategic planning problems such as climate adaptation. Scenario analysis of multiple
plausible futures is performed to consider deep uncertainty. The aim of a scenario analysis is to
identify adaptive strategies that are robust under a wide range of future scenarios.
Part of the uncertainty in ﬂood risk modelling originates from statistical (aleatory) uncertainties
and from epistemic uncertainties consisting of model and parameter uncertainties. Epistemic
uncertainties may arise from lack of knowledge about boundary conditions, processes, model
structures and parameter values (Beven et al., 2015). In modelling and evaluation of climate
adaptation measures, many epistemic uncertainty components are introduced as a result of sim-
pliﬁcations and assumptions, and these are not easily quantiﬁed due to lack of information. An
assessment how of these uncertainties inﬂuence on the result, will illustrate the robustness of the
decision-making parameter.
Figure 3.7: Outline of the overall uncertainty analysis, repeated from the workﬂow seen in Figure
3.1. The two parts, scenario and model uncertainty, are analysed separately and subsequently
compared to evaluate the robustness of adaptation strategies.

and model uncertainty.

studies and tested. The outcome of the test is maximum and minimum values of each planning
objective for each strategy, yielding the overall range of the model uncertainty for each planning
objective. These ranges are used to compute the range of NPV for each strategy in a cost-beneﬁt
analysis.

Elevation
Knowledge of surface elevation is crucial for hydrological modelling, as the topography controls
the surface ﬂow of the water. For delineation of the surface ﬂow, digital elevation data from
the Agency for Data Supply and Eﬃciency (2019) is used. The model is processed using data
measured by airplanes using laser-scanning-technology. The Digital Elevation Model (DEM) is
provided in a spatial resolution of 0.4 m. Surface ﬂow paths are represented in the model, en-
suring representation of water ﬂow in creeks and below bridges. Spatial data on buildings and
roads are provided by VandCenter Syd, and incorporated in the DEM using GIS operations. The
roads are made 7 m wide and lowered 0.15 m. The DEM is aggregated to a spatial resolution of
2 m.

developed and provided by Spildevandskomit´een and described in Skrift 30 (Spildevandskomi-

from ﬁve stations in Denmark, a 10 x 10 km gridded precipitation product from the Danish Me-
terological Institute and four climate simulation from the ENSEMBLES database covering the
period from 1950-2100 (Spildevandskomiteen, 2014). The model covers Denmark and the regions
are georeferenced using WGS84 Zone 32. The coordinates for the area of interest in Højme and
Sanderum are 6,137,000N and 585,000E. All rain series used are extracted as four hours CDS
rain series with a temporal resolution of 1 min, an asymmetry coeﬃcient of 0.5 (e.g. symmetric
rainfall) and the precipitation is expressed in µm/s. Time series for return periods of 1, 2, 5, 8,
10, 20, 50, 75 and 100 years are generated and used as external model forcing data.

4.2.2
Modiﬁcations to the provided model
The MIKE Urban drainage model is coupled to the DEM. Unsealed manholes in the 1D drainage
model within the deﬁned model-extent are coupled to the terrain using MIKE21 allowing ex-
change of water between the surface and sewer system. The model extent used in the 1D2D-
coupling spans from Holkebjergvænget in West to Odense ˚A in East and from the motorway
south of Sanderum to Odense Dyrskueplads in north, as shown in Appendix D.1. A rectangular
model grid of 4 m x 4 m is used for the terrain model, which is a spatial resolution deemed
ﬁne enough for accurate ﬂood results while maintaining a reasonable computation time. The
temporal model resolution is 1 s, ensuring model stability. The simulation time is set to 6 hours,
ensuring that peak ﬂow conditions are captured within the simulation period. All 1D2D model
settings are summarised in Appendix D.2.
Surface Manning number
The Manning number represents the friction of a surface. Spatially varying Manning settings are
used to improve accuracy of time of concentration for surface ﬂows. A value of 62.5 m1/3/s is
used for roads (Chow, 1959) and a value of 20 m1/3/s is used for larger green areas. A value of
32 m1/3/s is used for the remaining areas in the surface model.
Coupling settings
The number of inlet cells for each coupled manhole is deﬁned as 3 x 3 DEM grid cells, yielding
an area of 6 x 6 m. The coupling inlet areas and the maximum ﬂow are deﬁned speciﬁcally
for each manhole, considering the manhole area and the cross-sectional area of the connecting
pipes to ensure that the coupling settings will not be limiting the exchange of water between the
surface and the drainage system. The maximum ﬂow and the inlet area are estimated using the
Oriﬁce equation and Manning equation. The manhole speciﬁc settings allow for more water to
be exchanged for larger manholes and manholes connected to larger pipes, and less exchange of
water at manholes connected to smaller pipes. A more detailed description of the calculations
can be found in Appendix D.2.
Implementation of urban development scenarios and adaptation strategies
The adaptation strategies are implemented separately in the model, resulting in three diﬀerent
adaptation models and a baseline model with no adaptation implemented (see Appendix D.3).
The four urban development scenarios are implemented separately in the adaptation and baseline
models by changing permeability settings of catchments (see Appendix D.4). Thus 16 diﬀerent
models are constructed. The models and a description of simulations can be found in Appendix
D.1.

5.5
Sensitivity analysis
The results of the sensitivity analysis is presented in the following. First, the outcome from
the analysis of ﬂood model parameters is presented, followed by the uncertainty ranges of the
planning objectives in each adaptation strategy.
Sensitivity analysis of ﬂood model
The impact of the MU parameter variation on damage is shown in Table 5.6, with further details
and damage results from all simulations in Appendix H.3. The results show that the damage
changes with variation of the speciﬁed model parameters. Diﬀerent tendencies are seen in the
results. By changing the Manning number at surface, nsurface, the damage costs varies only
slightly, with the constant number resulting in the lowest damage and the varying Manning
number settings with 62.5 for roads causing the largest damage cost. The opposite eﬀect is seen
when comparing the ∆damage, where the constant Manning number results in slightly larger
∆damage. The inﬁltration parameters seem to have a larger eﬀect. The default values result in
25-30% larger damages than the values using inﬁltration parameters from Davidsen et al. (2018).
Likewise the ∆damage is approximately 35% larger using the default inﬁltration parameters.
The damages resulting from use of the parameters from Dybkjær et al. (2016) and Pitt et al.
(1999) are almost the same, but Dybkjær et al. (2016) resulted in marginally lower ∆damage.
Varying damage results are also seen when using the diﬀerent Qmax values. Basing the maximum
ﬂow exchange parameter on either the pipe or manhole diameter results in similar damage costs,
while using a constant maximum ﬂow restriction leads to a lower ∆damage. From the results it
seems that the interaction of water between the 1D drainage model and the 2D surface model in
the channel is largely aﬀected by setting Qmax equal to one.

Discussion
6.1
Comparison of strategies
Among the three evaluated adaptation strategies, the basin proves to be the best solution yielding
a positive NPV in 10 out of 12 scenarios. The basin performs better compared to the channel and
creek strategies across all scenarios, i.e., independent of future urban development and climate.
The channel strategy yields a positive NPV in 4 of the 12 scenarios, and the creek seems to be
a non-viable investment yielding a negative NPV in all of the evaluated scenarios.
The main drivers behind the results from the cost-beneﬁt analysis are the two objectives Flood
risk and Low costs, which proves to be the primary contributors to NPV. Due to the large poten-
tial of ﬂooding in the catchment, the other added values are less signiﬁcant in the CBA and the
strategy evaluation. However, without the high value of Flood risk, the NPV could be negative
for the strategies, due to the relativly large investment costs. The planning objectives Traﬃc
safety, Noise, Recreation, and Air quality, only have a minor eﬀect on the decision-making pa-
rameter and are therefore not driving the ﬁnal conclusion of economic feasibility. Among these
four objectives, the largest added value is seen for Noise in the basin strategy, while Recreation
yields the largest beneﬁt in the channel and creek strategies. The results are largely impacted
by the implementation of strategies in the model and are subjected to several assumptions (see
Section 6.3).
Events of frequent return period are seen to be driving ﬂood risk, thus the basin strategy performs
best as it is most eﬃcient at reducing ﬂooding from these events. Expected annual damage for
the basin strategy is independent of future urban development, proving that the basin is solving
problems related to increased runoﬀfrom developed areas. The channel and creek are not able to
reduce the ﬂood risk from small events suﬃciently, why they do not perform as well as the basin.
The results furthermore show, that the adaptation strategies solve problems in diﬀerent parts
of the catchment: the basin reduces ﬂooding in the crucial upstream area Holkebjergvænget,
whereas the channel and especially the creek partly solve problems related to downstream ﬂood-
ing, hence the strategies serve as complementary solutions. All strategies perform similar in terms
of ﬂood reduction from large events, but the distribution of ﬂood reduction varies. Overall, the
channel and creek strategies are not able to compensate for their ineﬃcient risk reduction of
small events resulting in lower beneﬁts in the Flood risk planning objective by their performance
in the other planning objectives.
The implementation costs are of crucial importance for comparison of the economic viability of
strategies. The implementation costs of the basin strategy was given, since it already has been
built, but the remaining strategies were more diﬃcult to assess. The channel is slightly less
costly compared to the basin, but this does not change the overall evaluation. The creek has
large implementation costs dominating its negative performance. The costs are estimated using

6.2
Inﬂuence of uncertainties
As outlined in this study, decision-makers are faced with various types of uncertainty in strategic
planning of climate adaptation. The conducted analysis of uncertainty components serves as
a tool to identify adaptation solutions that are robust under the inﬂuence of a wide range of
uncertainties, thus aiming to describe the reliability of results for decision-makers.
The outcome of the sensitivity analysis illustrates the importance of exploring uncertainties in
ﬂood modelling, when limited knowledge and calibration data is available. The results indicate,
that the noise introduced through modelling could be the determining factor of economic feasibil-
ity, as the NPV ranges from positive to negative (for the basin and channel strategies). It seems,
that the main driver of uncertainty for the decision-making parameter is Flood risk, and large
changes in the value of this objective will impact the NPV. For the channel and creek strategies,
the planning objectives of Noise, Recreation and Low cost are also seen to be of inﬂuence on
model uncertainty. Comparison of model and scenario uncertainty shows, that the uncertainty
related to the applied models and methods is larger, than the uncertainty related to future sce-
narios. However, when comparing the strategies, the model uncertainties seem to have the same
inﬂuence on all strategies and might be considered as negligible.
The uncertainty of Flood risk has been explored comprehensively. Damage assessment parameters
are seen to drive uncertainty of Flood risk. Also the uncertainty attributed the MU parameters
proved to be signiﬁcant for Flood risk uncertainty, with changes in parameters Qmax and I yield-
ing the largest inﬂuence on avoided damage. These results illustrate the importance of knowledge
about parameter settings as their inﬂuence on ﬂooding seem non-linear and correlated with the
amount of water on the surface. The results indicate that the model implementation of the adap-
tation strategies might inﬂuence under- or overestimation of ﬂooding results, and is of crucial
importance in assessment of ∆EAD. Furthermore, as strategies are often designed to adapt to
ﬂooding from speciﬁed return periods with large contribution to EAD, the results illustrate that
ﬂood model parameter settings might inﬂuence the design and size of the strategies. Further
investigation is necessary to determine general applicability of the results.
The ﬁnding that the uncertainty contribution of Flood risk is larger than that of most of the
other planning objectives’, is inﬂuenced by the assumptions and simpliﬁcations made in the anal-

large rain water basin becomes economically unattractive. However, if they instead decide to use
the area for housing as well as industry expansion, the rain water basin is the best solution and
solves the increased ﬂooding problems, which would be induced in an already vulnerable area.
Both the model and scenario uncertainty impact could thus serve as valuable information for
decision-makers. In practice, the scenario analysis and the illustrated scenario uncertainty can
be used for decision-making when deciding which adaptation strategies to pursue; when examin-
ing each scenario, the model uncertainty is of importance for evaluation of design and feasibility
of a strategy.

Methods: assumptions and limitations
The assumptions and limitations in the analysis will be brieﬂy discussed in the following sections.
6.3.1
Planning objectives
The planning objectives included in the assessment have been identiﬁed as relevant for the study
area and the speciﬁed adaptation strategies based on a screening performed in collaboration with
Skrydstrup (2019). The planning objectives are subject to several assumptions, some of which
are only of minor impact on the decision-making parameter, while other might play a crucial role
in the ﬁnal decisions related to strategic urban planning. The included planning objectives and
the limitations related to the quantiﬁcation and valuation indicators do not seem to justify the
implementation of a creek strategy. The primary limiting factors of the analysis, which have not
previously been mentioned, relate to assessment of Traﬃc safety and Recreation, and possibly to
the exclusion of the planning objectives related to Biodiversity, Mobility, and Urban heat island
(UHI) mitigation.

6.3 Methods: assumptions and limitations
The method to estimate Traﬃc safety is solely based on traﬃc intensity on speciﬁed roads in the
area. A crucial assumption behind the approach is that the change in the daily number of cars
on these speciﬁc roads results in a proportional change in traﬃc safety. The channel and creek
strategies are expected to separate bicycle lanes and pavements from the roads, which increases
the traﬃc safety for cyclists and pedestrians, but these eﬀects are not included in the assessment.
The recreational value is proven to be positive for all strategies in all scenarios. The three methods
used for the assessment show large variability in added values, indicating diﬃculties in assessing
the impact of recreation. The method used by the Danish EPA (2016) quantiﬁes the recreational
value using the size of the park independently of location and nearby living residents without
addressing marginal eﬀects of expansion of green area, which should in reality be expected to be
decrease. The two other methods by Treadwell (2019) and Bertram et al. (2017) quantify the
recreational value using the number of nearby households, which is evidently larger for the creek
strategy since the creek runs through a larger area. The recreational possibilities might be limited
if the green area covers a large but narrow area like in the channel and creek strategies, compared
to the basin where larger green areas are better suited for social gathering and activities. An
expansion of those areas by a few meters in width might increase the recreational opportunities,
but might only aﬀect a few additional households. It is deﬁnitely important to consider how
many people have access to the recreational area, but both the accessibility and the actual size
of the area seems to be reasonable to include in the assessment. None of the methods addresses
the inﬂuence of existing green areas, which are also expected to be of importance for determining
the added value of recreation. In a previous study by Zhou et al. (2013), a cost-beneﬁt analysis
including environmental amenity eﬀects showed, that open basins in an urban area had a large
recreational eﬀect and were likely the best adaptation strategy compared to pipe enlargement
and local inﬁltration. The analysis showed larger recreational beneﬁts from areas with lakes and
trees compared to the results obtained in this study. Zhou et al. (2013) used a hedonic valuation
model based on data on property prices for more than 12,000 Danish properties. None of the
three methods used in the current study includes house prices, but this might have improved the
total value of adding recreational areas.
Biodiversity was excluded from the analysis due to lack of transferability of methods from pre-
vious studies. Improvement of biodiversity in the urban area is one of the main incentives for
choosing the creek strategy, hence exclusion of this parameter in the evaluation likely leads to un-
derestimation of the added value of the creek. Furthermore, the creek and channel are expected to
provide better mobility for cyclists and pedestrians. Residents will have access to well-connected
green infrastructure with cleaner air, which might get people to change their mode of transport,
decreasing the total number of vehicles on the roads. Furthermore, including UHI-mitigation
(i.e., the regulating eﬀect by green areas on temperatures in a city) in the analysis, is expected
to yield larger beneﬁts for the channel and creek strategies, as paved areas are removed in favour
of green areas. These non-market values are diﬃcult to quantify and downscale to a case-speciﬁc
adaptation strategy, but if included they could potentially aﬀect the choice of adaptation. It has
proven to be of signiﬁcant importance to include all relevant objectives and their indicators, and
not to under- or overestimate the economic feasibility of an adaptation strategy. Inclusion of the
above listed planning objectives could potentially increase attraction of investors seeking holistic
sustainable urban solutions and thus enhance co-funding options of the proposed strategies.

6.3 Methods: assumptions and limitations
A number of assumptions related to the applied methods are relevant and needed for the anal-
ysis, but have not been identiﬁed as the main drivers of results. These include the assumptions
related to assessment of Air quality and Noise. The method for assessment of Air quality does
not take the existing Air quality into account, but decreasing marginal eﬀects with improved Air
quality would be expected. In the study area, a large forest area is planned and there are several
existing green areas, which could indicate a good Air quality. However, the motorway is located
very nearby and might in the other hand inﬂuence the air quality negatively. The methods do
not diﬀerentiate between various kinds of green vegetation, but it would be expected that small
and large trees might have diﬀerent impacts on air quality. The Air quality assessment is rele-
vant to include, but does not seem to have any signiﬁcant eﬀect compared to the other planning
objectives. A study by Gallus (2018) evaluated diﬀerent adaptation strategies and identiﬁed air
quality and physical health as the most signiﬁcant objectives, besides the adaptation costs and
avoided ﬂood risk for a nearby area in Odense.
The valuation methods used for Noise yield large variation in results, illustrating the diﬃculty of
assessing the monetary values of noise changes. A crucial assumption introduced in the quantiﬁ-
cation method is the proportional relation between the traﬃc intensity on speciﬁed roads and the
noise-level in the area. Marginal decreasing eﬀects and other sources of noise in the area could
have been taken into consideration to obtain more realistic values. The basin strategy involves
a green covered hill serving as a noise barrier, which is assumed to a have a constant eﬀect on
reducing noise-level from the motorway for all nearby households, though the assumption of a
constant noise reduction might not be proper. The methods applied for the channel and creek
strategies are solely based on changes in traﬃc intensity and does not include any noise-reducing
eﬀects from green areas, which would be expected.
6.3.2
Adaptation strategies
The three adaptation strategies vary greatly in designed storage volume and area of ﬂood risk
reduction impact. It is outside the scope of this study to optimise and redesign the strategies
to ensure optimal adaptation, however, the results show, that there is a need for re-evaluation
and improvement of the design. The channel and creek strategies do not suﬃciently reduce risk
of ﬂooding for small events in the upstream vulnerable area ’Holkebjergvænget’. The results
show that the storage volume should be increased in the upstream part of the channel as well
as the creek. Especially the channel strategy should be optimised, as the results show how the
implementation of the channel actually increases ﬂooding in some vulnerable areas compared
to the baseline. These results could however be a result of the model implementation not fully
being able to capture how the strategies in reality would lead water away from critical areas.
This could potentially be oﬀ-setting the calculated ∆EAD, which would have a large impact on
the choice of optimal strategy.
Modelling of the eﬃciency of the strategies to reduce ﬂooding depends on numerous parameters,
and simpliﬁcation and generalisation is crucial for reduction of model complexity. As proven in
the sensitivity analysis, the assumptions related to settings of model parameters have an impact
on ﬂooding results. A few of the ﬂood models had abnormal completion for very extreme pre-

Future scenarios

adaptation strategies, helping decision-makers choose measures that are feasible under a wide

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
This thesis demonstrates the application of a multi-objective approach for assessment of nature-
based ﬂood adaptation strategies. The assessment applies the planning objectives framework
developed by Skrydstrup et al. (2019a,b) and evaluates six planning objectives selected speciﬁ-
cally for the case study: Flood risk, Traﬃc safety, Noise, Recreation, Air quality and Low costs.
Three adaptation strategies are evaluated using cost-beneﬁt analysis in 12 diﬀerent future urban
development and climate scenarios in Højme and Sanderum, Odense.
Through sensitivity analysis the uncertainty related to the applied models and methods is ex-
plored, and compared to uncertainties of future scenarios to evaluate the robustness of the
decision-making parameter.
A preliminary screening of uncertainties leads to selection of 14
parameters or factors inﬂuencing the decision-making parameter, which are tested using plau-
sible ranges.
Through scenario analysis, this thesis illustrates some of the challenges related
to non-stationary urban planning, that decision-makers face when choosing optimal adaptation
strategies.
The main conclusions of the thesis are:
• The rain water basin proves to be the best strategy yielding a positive net present value
in 10 out of 12 scenarios, while the channel yields a positive net present value in 4 out of
12 scenarios and the creek strategy results in negative net present values in all scenarios.
Thus, the basin is deemed a cost-eﬃcient solution, which is a robust result also for varying
urban development and climate scenarios. The creek solution is demonstrated to not be
cost-eﬃcient in all scenarios.
• The planning objectives and the indicators used for quantiﬁcation and valuation, does not
seem to justify implementation of the creek strategy. The exclusion of planning objectives
expressing the added value of Biodiversity and Mobility is identiﬁed as a plausible limiting
factor in the analysis. Inclusion of these objectives in addition to indicators related to the
eﬀects of separating pedestrians and cyclists from road traﬃc and modiﬁed methods for
assessment of recreation could potentially lead to a more positive evaluation of the creek
strategy when performing socio-economic assessments.
• The pluvial ﬂood risk analysis shows high ﬂooding potential in the area, with high risk of
ﬂooding in low-lying parts of the residential area. Scenario comparison shows larger impact
of climate change than of urban development on ﬂood risk.
• The results show, that the planning objectives Flood risk and Low costs are dominating
factors for feasibility across all strategies and scenarios in the speciﬁc case study, while the
remaining objectives seem of less signiﬁcance.
65
==PAGE:78==
• Subjective choices for model parameters and ways of implementing adaptation measures
seem crucial for the magnitude of estimated damages. It aﬀects the choice of return period
and thus also the investment level.
• The results illustrate the importance of performing sensitivity analyses in ﬂood modelling,
when limited knowledge and calibration data is available. The noise introduced through
modelling proved to inﬂuence the cost-beneﬁt analysis and could be the determining factor
of economic feasibility of the adaptation strategies evaluated.
• Quantiﬁcation and valuation methods are of signiﬁcant importance for multi-objective eval-
uation of adaptation measures. The results illustrate the importance of including and un-
derstanding all relevant indicators, as these can aﬀect the preference between strategies
and inﬂuence the choice of optimal adaptation.

between 0 and 100 dependent on soil type. Equation A.1 and A.2 describes the relation between
the parameters.

Analysis of 2D coupling parameters
The inﬂuence of the coupling parameters Qmax and IA, on water depth of ﬂooding was initially
examined. Four diﬀerent approaches were tested, which will be brieﬂy presented and discussed
in this section. One of the methods is used in the CBA, and two of the approaches are used in
the sensitivity analysis.
Qmax deﬁnes the maximum ﬂow of water between the 1D drainage model and the 2D terrain
model. A large Qmax allows for large quantities of water to be exchanged, whereas a small value
will limit the water ﬂow and can be a constraint, thus resulting in underestimation of the inunda-
tion levels. Qmax should be large enough to capture normal ﬂow conditions between the surface
and the drainage system, however also small enough too prevent instabilities and oscillations that
can occur. IA is the coupled inlet area.
In the training material for MIKE Flood (DHI, 2015) used in the Urban Flood Risk course at
DTU (L¨owe and Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2019), a value of 0.1 m3/s is used for Qmax and IA = 0.16

large ﬂooded areas quickly.

D.3
Implementation of adaptation strategies
The sections below presents brief descriptions of the implementation of the adaptation strategies
in the ﬂood model.
Baseline
The baseline model is a model without the implementation of any climate adaptation strategies.
The MIKE URBAN model from VCS has the large Sanderum rain water basin implemented, and
the basin needs to be removed in order to create the baseline. In the pre-study of the area by
Dybkjær and Hansen (2019) all basins in the MIKE URBAN model were inspected using Google
Maps in order to avoid double representation of these in the 1D drainage and 2D terrain models.
Dybkjær and Hansen (2019) removed basins represented in the 2D terrain model from the MIKE
URBAN model and converted these to manholes. The basins ‘bas2’ and ‘forbassin’ as well as
the weir ‘vandbremse soe’ are deleted and a manhole is inserted instead. Pipes connecting the
industry area to Holkebjergvænget are inserted instead of the weirs. As the elevation of the area
previously containing the basin is approximately 2 m above the elevation in Holkebjergvænget,
there is little chance of back-ﬂow in the short pipe connecting the two. The impermeability of the
catchment previously containing the storm water basin is changed to 5% and the parameter-set
for runoﬀ-model B is changed to Natural (see Section D.4 for description of runoﬀparameters),
thus giving the catchment the same inﬁltration setting as other large green areas in the model.
Implementation of Basin
The basin has already been implemented in the model by VandCenter Syd, as it has already
already been built. The basin is implemented as two basins in MIKE URBAN; a smaller pre-
basin ,’forbassin’, and a larger basin, ’bas2’. From the basin, a smaller weir ’vandbremse soe’
leads water to the main storm water pipe in Holkebjergvænget.

All ﬂood maps were initially sieved using a python script developed by Roland L¨owe.
This
ensured that pixels on the map were only shown as being ﬂooded, if at least two neighbouring
pixel cells had a water level above 0.03 m. Thus, single ﬂooded pixels that does not have any
ﬂooding immediately close by are removed from the results and left out of the damage assessment.
This is done under the assumption that ﬂooding in one single pixel is not causing any damage.
The ﬂooding of one single pixel could be a result of the aggregation of the DEM.
Note on damage for green areas
Damage is calculated using a GIS-based risk model. The input to the model is shapeﬁles contain-
ing the spatial location of the various vulnerability classes, the ﬂooding generated by a particular
event and the extent for which the damage should be calculated. During the sensitivity anal-
ysis, strange behaviour of the damage for green areas was noticed. After further exploration,
it was discovered that the risk-model did not use the speciﬁed extent for damage computation
for green areas, but instead calculated the damage for all green areas in the greens shapeﬁle.
Unfortunately, this was discovered after completing the damage calculations for all simulated

likely overestimated. However, as the focus is avoided damage, the inﬂuence is expected to be

APPENDIX H
Sensitivity analysis
H.1
Selection of test ranges
The selected test ranges for parameters are brieﬂy described below.
The parameters selected for further analysis related to ﬂood modelling are parameters governing
inﬁltration in green areas, the surface Manning number as well as the maximum ﬂow exchange
allowed between the surface and the drainage system. The default Horton inﬁltration values from
MIKE Urban is used in the CBA, while values from previous studies and diﬀerent soil types were
used in the sensitivity analysis (from Dybkjær et al. (2016) and Pitt et al. (1999) in Davidsen
et al. (2018)). A lower Manning number for road surfaces and constant Manning values for all
surfaces are tested. The restriction on ﬂow exchange between the surface and drainage system is
based on the pipe diameter in the CBA, and in the sensitivity analysis values based on manhole
diameter are tested as well as a constant value of 1 m3/s. For the parameters used to quantify
and valuate the damage cost of ﬂooding, a relative increase and decrease of 20% in unit costs and
50% in ﬂood threshold are used in the sensitivity analysis. An increase in threshold level reduces
the ﬂood damage, while an increase in unit costs corresponds to an increase in ﬂood damage.
The traﬃc intensity was only increased in the sensitivity analysis, since the numbers from the
municipality’s traﬃc model seem to underestimate the actual traﬃc density (see Appendix E.2).
This argument is supported by a local ‘Traﬃc counting’ by the municipality, conversations with
employees from the municipality, and the local utility company. Based on a comparison between a
local traﬃc counting and the traﬃc model, the traﬃc intensity was increased by 57% on all roads.
The quantiﬁcation of recreation using method 2 is varied by limiting the number of households
aﬀected, by only included households within a distance of 300 m from the recreational area.
The quantiﬁcation of the change in tree cover area after implementation of the diﬀerent strate-
gies, is varied by increasing and reducing the added tree cover area by 20% in the channel and
creek strategy. The change in tree cover area in the basin strategy is still assumed to be zero.
The adaptation costs are tested in a range of +/- 20%. A discount rate, r, of 6% is used as an
upper level, while 2% is used as the lower range based on studies by (Zhou and Arnbjerg-Nielsen,
2018).
