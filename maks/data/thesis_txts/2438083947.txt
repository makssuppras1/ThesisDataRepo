Abstract 
Healthcare-associated infections are an increasing problem associated with high rates of morbidity and 
mortality across the world. The emergence of antibiotic resistance and multidrug-resistant pathogens has 
considerably worsened this and consequently, the interest in alternative antimicrobial strategies is growing. 
The metals copper and silver both have antimicrobial activity and a synergistic effect has been observed when 
combining them in an alloy, as the galvanic coupling of the metals induces an accelerated release of toxic 
copper ions in comparison to pure copper. Thus, previous studies have demonstrated a marked antibacterial 
effect of copper-silver alloys; however, it is known that repeated use of antimicrobial agents can induce 
resistance in microorganisms. Hence, the purpose of the present study, was to assess the risk of increased 
tolerance or resistance upon repeated exposure to an electroplated copper-silver alloy coating (60 wt.% Cu, 40 
wt.% Ag). This was evaluated on the major pathogen Staphylococcus aureus. An adaptive laboratory evolution 
experiment was performed to continuously expose S. aureus JE2 to copper-silver alloy coated stainless steel 
coupons in suspension. Survival was then evaluated by exposing the lineages to copper-silver coupons in 
suspension and estimating the number of surviving cells in suspension and attached to the coupons. Lastly, the 
importance of copper homeostasis and oxidative stress genes was evaluated in suspension with copper-silver 
coupons, using S. aureus JE2 Tn mutants from the Nebraska Transposon Mutant Library, and so was the 
importance of a plasmid-borne copper resistance gene discovered in Enterococcus faecium isolates. 
Tn mutants in the copA, csoR, ahpC and sodM genes all had less than one log-fold difference in survival and 
adhesion in comparison to the WT, when grown in suspension containing copper-silver coated coupons. 
Thereby, these genes did not have a statistically significant effect on adhesion and survival of S. aureus JE2 in 
suspension with copper-silver surfaces. Furthermore, the plasmid-borne gene tcrB was observed to lead to at 
least three log-fold increased survival of E. faecium in suspension, while a less marked increase was observed 
in regard to cells adhering to the copper-silver surface. 
 
After 180 to 210 generations of adaptive laboratory evolution, no increased resistance to the copper-silver 
coating had developed in S. aureus JE2. Whole-genome sequencing revealed few mutational changes and no 
mutations unique to copper-silver-exposed lineages, supporting the application of this antimicrobial copper-
silver coating in hospitals and other healthcare settings. However, it is still a possibility that increased 
resistance can be observed after longer exposure and thus is it relevant to extend this experiment to further 
assess this potential. No correlation was observed between resistance to copper and the tested antibiotics. 
Thus, the results of this study suggest that the potential of S. aureus to develop increased tolerance or resistance 
to this copper-silver alloy is low. Furthermore, due to the multiple mechanisms behind contact killing, 
individual copper resistance factors appear to have minor effects on survival.

1 Background and Purpose 
Infectious diseases are one of the leading causes of morbidity and mortality across the world [1]. Healthcare-
associated infections (HAIs) are an increasing problem during hospitalisation or stay in healthcare facilities, 
where pathogens are often shed by patients, hospital personnel and visitors [1,2]. Globally, approximately one 
out of ten patients acquires an HAI during hospital stays, which is likely to result in prolonged hospitalisation, 
antibiotic treatment, re-admission, and in the worst cases long-term disability, mortality and increased 
antimicrobial resistance [1]. Patients in intensive care units are the most vulnerable, due to the severity of their 
disease, invasive procedures and more frequent interaction with the healthcare staff [3]. In Europe, every year 
approximately 37,000 deaths are attributed to HAIs and this results also in a significant economic burden on 
healthcare financial resources, as the costs of HAIs have been estimated at €7 billion annually in Europe alone 
[1]. 
The emergence of antibiotic resistance and multidrug-resistant pathogens, as a result of the selective pressure 
induced by an uncontrolled use of antibiotics, has worsened this scenario even more. Therefore the interest in 
alternative antimicrobial strategies has been increasing [4]. Antimicrobial materials have been widely studied 
as a potential strategy for reducing the contamination of frequently touched surfaces, such as door handles and 
furniture in hospitals, with the aim of controlling the spread of pathogens in healthcare settings [5,6]. 
 
Both metallic copper and silver have broad-spectrum antimicrobial activity. Copper and copper alloy surfaces 
possess biocidal properties and have been used for frequently touched surfaces, such as door handles and bed 
rails [7]. Silver also has a well-known antimicrobial activity and has been used in wound dressings [8] and 
catheters [9]. The antimicrobial potential and the possible synergic effect of these two metals has led several 
authors to combine them [10–14]. One study used an electroplated a copper-silver alloy coating (60 wt.% Cu, 
40 wt.% Ag) on stainless steel and found that the alloy had a higher antimicrobial activity than the individual 
metals, suggesting that this particular surface treatment can be a promising tool for controlling the spread and 
transmission of pathogens in hospitals [14]. 
The major concern with any novel antimicrobial agent is the potential risk of development of resistance. The 
opportunistic pathogen Staphylococcus aureus is indeed a major concern in healthcare settings, because of its 
ability to adapt to diverse environments. In addition, it is also well documented that several strains of S. aureus 
have developed resistance towards any known class of antibiotics [15]. Therefore, the present project aims at 
investigating the potential of the pathogen S. aureus to develop resistance or increased tolerance towards a 
novel copper-silver alloy electroplated coating. For this purpose, the survival of S. aureus JE2 will be evaluated 
through exposure to copper-silver alloy coated stainless steel coupons, and an adaptive laboratory evolution 
experiment will be performed to assess if S. aureus JE2 is able to develop resistance or increased tolerance to 
the copper-silver coating in a period of 30 days. These lineages will also be examined for co-resistance to 
antibiotics and will be whole-genome sequenced. In addition, the importance of copper homeostasis systems

2 Introduction 
2.1 Healthcare-Associated Infections 
Over the last decade, HAIs have been a growing concern in healthcare worldwide [1]. According to the Centers 
for Disease Control and Prevention, the most predominant HAI pathogens from 2011 to 2014 were Escherichia 
coli and S. aureus, which accounted for approximately 27% of all reported cases, but other pathogens, such as 
Klebsiella spp., coagulase-negative staphylococci, enterococci and Pseudomonas aeruginosa were also 
responsible for many diseases [18]. However, most cases of HAIs have progressively been caused by 
multidrug-resistant pathogens, such as Clostridium difficile, methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus 
(MRSA) and vancomycin-resistant enterococci (VRE) [19] and in 50% of the cases MRSA was responsible 
[18]. In addition, the most common types of HAIs were reported to be catheter-associated urinary tract 
infections, central line-associated bloodstream infections, surgical site infections and ventilator-associated 
pneumonia [18]. 
2.2 Fighting Healthcare-Associated Infections 
Various measures have been taken in order to prevent HAIs. Antibiotic treatment is the primary prophylactic 
measure against bacterial infections, however, the emergence of antibiotic resistance, including multidrug-
resistant pathogens, has considerably increased the prevalence of HAIs. Therefore, alternative antimicrobial 
agents, and especially the approaches that can prevent spread of pathogens, are receiving increasing attention 
[1,4]. 
Transmission of HAIs is often associated with invasive devices, such as catheters, but healthcare workers are 
actually the most common source of transmission because of the daily physical contact with patients and 
contaminated objects [20]. Thus, the most important measure to reduce the spread of infectious diseases is 
hand hygiene, and several studies have shown that the transmission of healthcare-associated pathogens can be 
significantly reduced by an improved hand hygiene [20,21]. However, hand hygiene is strongly dependent on 
staff compliance and unfortunately it has often been observed to be insufficient, as studies have indicated that 
on average 61% of healthcare workers do not follow recommended hand hygiene practices [1,20]. A second 
line of defence, independent on human behaviour, is the use of materials with intrinsic antimicrobial activity 
to control and prevent the spread of pathogenic microorganisms. Antimicrobial materials can be used for 
frequently touched surfaces, such as door handles, bed rails, faucets and other similar fomites [22]. Studies 
have shown that many nosocomial pathogens, including S. aureus, can proliferate and survive for months on 
stainless steel and plastic surfaces, being in this way continuous sources of direct or indirect transmission

[22,23]. French et al. have further shown that the conventional methods used for cleaning stainless steel 
surfaces are insufficient to eradicate strains of MRSA, which was present on 74% of tested samples before 
cleaning and on 66% of the samples after cleaning. This is likely due to the formation of biofilms, which have 
increased tolerance to antimicrobial agents, such as surface disinfectants [24]. Thereby, the use of bactericidal 
materials on frequently touched surfaces could be an alternative approach to reduce the bacterial contamination 
on these. In collaboration with antibiotics and disinfectants, this could be a promising tool for controlling the 
spread of pathogens across hospitals [5,6]. 
In accordance with Hans et al. [25] antimicrobial metals need to possess three fundamental properties in order 
to ensure an effective and prolonged action against microbes (Figure 1). Oxidation under the working 
environment conditions, such as in moderately humid air, high ion solubility and metal ion toxicity, due to e.g. 
high thiophilicity or soft ionic character, are required [25,26]. Metallic copper [5,27] and silver [28] have been 
known and used widely as antimicrobial agents for centuries. However, according to Hans et al. [25], metallic 
copper surfaces possess high antimicrobial activity in ambient conditions, in contrast to metallic silver 
surfaces, which are not oxidised and therefore do not release metal ions due to its scarce solubility in these 
conditions.

2.3 The Antimicrobial Properties of Copper 
Copper is an essential trace element in most organisms, as low amounts are required e.g. as enzyme cofactors 
in respiration [29], iron metabolism and protection against oxidative stress through the eradication of free 
radicals [30–32]. A wide range of copper-containing proteins are currently known and in these proteins, copper 
functions as either electron donor or electron acceptor [33] through redox cycling between its two oxidation 
states: cuprous (Cu(I)) and cupric (Cu(II)) copper [34].

Despite the essential role of copper in redox balance [35], the metal is highly toxic, if present in excess [36]. 
For this reason, the use of copper as an antimicrobial agent has been studied widely and its efficacy confirmed 
on several bacterial strains [26,34,37,38]. However, the killing rate is highly dependent on the experimental 
setup (Figure 1) [5], the copper alloys and the bacterial strains [22], the temperature [39,40] and the eluent or 
buffer, since these parameters strongly influence the release of copper ions from the metallic surfaces [41]. For 
example, buffers containing chlorides have been observed to highly induce the release of copper ions [42], 
while growth media contains different nutrients, which can sequester copper ions and thus protect the bacterial 
cells to some extent [43,44]. Despite these variations, copper has been able to effectively inactivate E. coli 
[26,34], Bacillus cereus [34], Enterococcus faecalis [45], Listeria monocytogenes [46], S. aureus [22], P. 
aeruginosa [40], Salmonella enterica and Campylobacter jejuni [44].

Higher killing rates have been observed on dry metallic copper surfaces than on wet copper surfaces or in 
copper ion suspensions [26,47]. The rapid killing on dry copper surfaces has been termed contact killing, and 
to a lower degree this is observed on wet copper surfaces as well, when there is direct contact between bacterial 
cells and the metallic surface [5,48]. In contrast, killing in suspensions containing copper ions is solely based 
on the toxic effect of the ions. This difference can be partially explained by the formation of copper oxides 
under these different environmental conditions. Under dry conditions, it has been shown that mainly cupric 
oxide (Cu2O) is formed, while cuprous oxide (CuO) is preferably formed under wet conditions regardless of 
the buffer used [48]. Hans et al. [48] have further tested the antimicrobial activity of the two copper oxides on 
Enterococcus hirae and showed that Cu2O exhibited a similar antibacterial effect as pure copper, while CuO 
had a lower antibacterial effect. They also tested the release rate of copper ions from coupons of pure copper, 
Cu2O and CuO and observed decreasing ion release rate from pure copper to CuO, which corresponded to 
lower antibacterial effect. This correlation strongly suggests that copper ion release is a primary factor in 
contact killing and multiple other studies have reached the same conclusion [40,49]. 
A number of possible mechanisms of action for copper toxicity have been investigated. One model suggested 
that DNA damage was the primary mechanism behind the contact-mediated killing. Under aerobic conditions, 
the redox cycling of copper can lead to DNA damage, as it induces the formation of reactive oxygen species 
(ROS). Cu(I) is Fenton active, therefore ROS can be generated through redox cycling when Cu(I) is further 
oxidised to Cu(II) [34,47]. Cu(II) is first reduced to Cu(I) by superoxide (O2-) and then reoxidised to Cu(II) by 
dihydrogen peroxide (H2O2), leading to the formation of the hydroxyl radical OH˙. The unpaired electron of 
the hydroxyl radical is highly reactive and capable of causing oxidative damage to cellular macromolecules, 
including as lipids, proteins and DNA [34]. DNA damage as a consequence of copper-mediated contact killing 
has been observed in vitro in eukaryotic [33,50] and prokaryotic cells [51]. However, multiple studies have 
shown that killing of E. coli was not caused by DNA damage during contact killing on metallic copper surfaces 
[34,39,47,52] or in liquid systems containing toxic concentrations of copper salts [31]. In these studies, no 
DNA damage was observed prior to cell death. The same behaviour was observed during contact killing of the 
Gram-positive bacterium Bacillus subtilis [47] and Staphylococcus haemolyticus [38]. Instead, in Macomber 
et al. [31] it was observed that copper ions were able to protect DNA in E. coli against H2O2-mediated oxidative 
damage, although the mechanism was unclear. To further support these observations, the behaviour of the 
bacterium Deinococcus radiodurans, which is known to be able to recover from extensive DNA fragmentation 
due to efficient DNA repair mechanisms [53], has been investigated when exposed to copper. D. radiodurans 
was observed to be almost as sensitive as E. coli on dry metallic copper surfaces, leading Santo et al. [47] to 
conclude that DNA damage cannot be the primary killing mechanism of copper. In addition, it has been shown 
that growth of E. coli was inhibited markedly under anaerobic conditions, where ROS cannot form, both in 
suspension and during contact killing. Instead, elevated concentrations of Cu(I) ions have been suggested to 
be the cause of the killing, since Cu(I) has showed higher toxicity compared to Cu(II) ions, but is an unstable

ionic species and highly insoluble under aerobic conditions [34,54]. The increased toxicity of Cu(I) is probably 
thanks to its ability to diffuse into the cytoplasm [36]. 
 
Therefore, from these studies, it appears that DNA damage is not a primary factor in copper toxicity. Another 
model proposed that cell membranes, rather than DNA, are the primary targets [47]. In this model, it has been 
suggested that copper ions released from a metallic copper surface cause lesions in cell membranes and 
subsequently lead to accumulation of toxic ions in the cell. Membrane damage had been shown directly through 
microscopy and live/dead staining, but also through proteomic profiling, based on the knowledge that proteins 
involved in cell envelope synthesis are upregulated in E. coli when in contact with copper [25]. It has also been 
suggested that DNA damage and oxidative damage to other intracellular components, such as proteins and 
lipids, are caused by the copper ions only after the cell membrane has already been compromised and high 
levels of copper ions have accumulated in the cell [47,55]. Thus, it was likely that the DNA damage observed 
by Warnes et al. [51] happened at a later stage after cell death, and that it was not the primary target. Hong et 
al. [52] has further investigated the specific mechanism of membrane degradation by copper in E. coli. In this 
study, it was suggested that surface-mediated killing of E. coli was caused by oxidative damage of the 
membrane phospholipids, leading to loss of membrane integrity and subsequent cell death. Thus, it appeared 
that ROS could play a significant role in copper toxicity by degrading the bacterial cell membrane and thus 
allowing the influx of copper ions. 
 
Other studies have also shown that high levels of copper ions can damage Fe-S clusters in proteins containing 
metal cofactors by binding the metal binding site. In this way, copper sequesters and makes these proteins 
inactive, and this results in growth inhibition and growth defects due to the importance of metalloproteins in 
bacterial metabolic and biosynthetic pathways [55,56], such as dehydratases, which are involved in amino 
acids synthesis [57]. 
Therefore, it appears that the primary mechanism of action for contact killing in dry condition and killing in 
suspension or aqueous environments is the release of copper ions, and that contact killing is more effective 
probably thanks to the higher local concentration of copper ions releasing from the copper surface [40,48,49]. 
However, studies have found that wet and dry environmental conditions can actually significantly affect the 
killing mechanism of copper surfaces. That is, not all of the described mechanisms of copper toxicity play an 
important role under killing on dry copper surfaces [25]. In suspension with nutrients and copper ions, cells 
are growing, while they are in a non-growing state, when exposed on a dry copper surface [47,55]. In the latter 
condition, the damage to metalloproteins has less importance, as it leads to growth defects and growth 
inhibition, which is not crucial when cells are in a non-proliferative state [47]. Furthermore, the importance of 
ROS production and oxidative damage is still under debate, as some believe that it plays a major role in contact 
killing rather than in aqueous environments [25], while others are against this theory [31]. 
 
The distinction between killing mechanisms during contact with copper surfaces and in presence of copper 
ions in suspension is supported by Santo et al. [58], where 294 bacterial isolates from copper alloy coins were

exposed to dry and moist metallic copper surfaces and to a suspension of copper ions. A number of these 
isolates were especially resistant to dry copper surfaces, as they were able to survive for 48 hours or more. 
However, when the same strains were exposed to moist surfaces and copper ion suspensions, they were as 
sensitive as the control strains, indicating that different killing mechanisms are involved under the different 
conditions. In addition, they showed that staphylococci on coins did not exhibit increased levels of resistance 
to antibiotics.  
In this respect, Mathews et al. [59] investigated the influence of physical contact between bacterial cells and 
metallic copper surface on the antimicrobial activity of copper. Here, they designed a copper surface, which 
was covered with an inert polymer having holes (Ø<1 µm) that allowed the passage of copper ions but 
prevented bacterial cells to pass through. In this way, the direct contact between bacterial cells and copper 
surface was prevented, but not the effect of dissolved copper ions on bacteria. Although the concentration of 
copper ions was not significantly reduced by the polymer coating, it was observed that killing of E. hirae was 
significantly reduced in comparison to uncoated copper surfaces (Figure 2). Therefore, they concluded that the 
metallic surface was able to weaken the bacterial cell membrane, consequently allowing copper ions to enter 
and damage the cells more rapidly and efficiently than in a copper ion suspension. Based on the 
physicochemical properties of copper, they hypothesized that efficient contact killing required that the metallic 
surface was redox-active and could release toxic ions. The reduction potentials of copper and silver are in the 
range of biological reduction potentials, therefore Mathews et al. [59] speculated that the redox-active metals 
could disturb the cellular redox chemistry or catalyse damaging reactions at the cell surface, making it more 
permeable to toxic ions. They supported this hypothesis with an additional test of contact killing using metallic 
iron surfaces. Copper and iron have similar redox potentials and are both Fenton active, thus they should both 
be able to cause damage to cells through contact killing and release of toxic ions. However, iron ions are not 
very soluble under aerobic conditions in contrast to copper ions, rendering metallic iron surfaces 
antimicrobially inactive on their own. As expected, they observed that metallic iron did not cause any 
significant contact killing, however, when supplemented with copper salts, complete and very fast killing was 
observed due to the synergistic action of the metallic iron and the dissolved copper ions. These results clearly 
indicate that both toxic ions and bacterial-metal contact are fundamental for efficient contact killing by metallic 
copper. However, the exact mechanisms behind the membrane damage caused by physical contact with a 
metallic copper surface is still uncertain and needs further investigation.

The antimicrobial activity of copper surfaces has also been investigated in clinical trials in hospitals, where 
standard stainless steel surfaces were replaced with copper and copper alloy surfaces. Metallic copper can be 
easily implemented on surfaces such as door handles, bed rails and other frequently touched surfaces [7,37,60] 
and also used for medical devices [61,62] in hospitals and in other medical environments. In one study 
performed across three different hospitals [63], stainless steel surfaces and components in patient rooms were 
replaced with copper alloy surfaces (99.9 wt.%) and an 83% reduction in bacteria was observed. In addition, 
a 58% reduction in infection rates was observed in patient rooms with components made of copper compared 
to standard patient rooms. Significant reductions in HAIs, due to the use of copper surfaces, have also been 
observed in other studies [3,64]. 
 
An important concern about the use of metallic copper surfaces in clinical settings is the possible reduction of 
the antimicrobial effect of copper surfaces over time due to surface oxidation. However, Hans et al. has shown 
that the natural oxidation of copper in dry environmental conditions does not decrease its antimicrobial activity 
[48]. In a clinical study, they have also considered the effect of surface oxidation by implementing the copper 
surfaces six months before the actual tests in order to determine if the antimicrobial properties of copper 
surfaces reduced with time. Also in this case, no reduction in the effect of the tested copper surfaces was 
observed [60]. 
2.3.1 Bacterial Copper Resistance 
Due to the inherent toxicity of copper to bacteria, the intracellular copper concentration is strictly controlled 
to maintain homeostasis and thus avoid toxic levels [36]. For this purpose, all bacteria contain multiple systems 
for sensing and regulating copper levels in the cell [26,34,35]. Three primary mechanisms have been suggested 
to control copper homeostasis in bacteria [65]. The first relies on transport of copper out of the cell by CPx-
type ATPases, which are specialised ATPases for heavy metal ion transport [66]. Another copper homeostasis

control mechanism is the sequestration of free copper ions by copper-binding chaperones in the cytoplasm or 
by metallothioneins in the periplasm or outside the cell [65]. Copper chaperones bind to copper ions inside the 
cell and guide them to their destination, protecting the cell from potential damaging reactions [32]. Lastly, 
oxidation of copper ions from Cu(I) to Cu(II) is used to generate less toxic forms [65]. 
There are differences between the copper homeostasis mechanisms of Gram-positive and Gram-negative 
bacteria. In Gram-positive bacteria, ATPase export and chaperone gene transcription are regulated by a 
repressor in conditions of low copper concentrations. Currently, the two most abundant and known types of 
repressors are CopY- and CsoR-like repressors (Figure 3), although other less common regulators exist [67].

increases, a copper chaperone delivers copper ions to a repressor (CopY- or CsoR-like depending on the organism), which 
is then released from the DNA allowing transcription of different genes involved in copper homeostasis control [65]. 
 
CopY repressors are widespread among Gram-positive bacteria, including Enterococcus faecalis,

is released upon binding of one copper ion with each monomer, potentially via delivery by the putative 
chaperone protein CsoZ. 
In Gram-negative bacteria, copper export and chaperone gene transcription are generally regulated by 
activators in the presence of high copper concentrations in the cytoplasm or periplasm. The two most abundant 
types of activators are the CueR- and CusRS-like activators (Figure 4) [65].

increases, copper ions induce the binding of CueR to the promoter region of the cueO and copA genes, which 
activates transcription. CueO is a multicopper oxidase (MCO), which oxidises periplasmic Cu(I) to the less

stress. CopA is an ATPase that exports copper ions from the cytoplasm to the periplasm. It has been reported

experimentally confirmed yet [67]. A recent study revealed through mutational analysis that the copA gene in 
E. coli encodes both the ATPase and a chaperone protein CopA(Z), which is needed to deliver copper ions to 
the ATPase [74]. In addition, it was shown that CopA(Z) was produced from a truncated copA transcript, while 
the CopA ATPase was encoded by the complete copA gene. Thus, the copA gene appears to play a central role 
in the survival of E. coli when exposed to toxic concentrations of copper ions.

Additional copper resistance mechanisms have been discovered in several bacteria. In the gut microbiota of 
pigs on diet supplemented with copper sulphate as growth promoter, E. coli isolates have been discovered to

copper ions in the periplasm and delivers them to PcoA, which functions as a MCO similarly to the CueO 
protein of E. coli. The function of PcoB and PcoD is unknown, but it clearly appears that PcoA is the central 
protein of the Pco system [36]. It has been observed that this plasmid-borne system allowed E. coli to survive 
under copper salt concentrations five times greater than the ones the WT strains were able to withstand, thanks 
to the efficient export of copper ions [76]. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that this copper efflux 
mechanism is energy-dependent [78]. 
 
Another copper resistance mechanism has been found in E. faecium and E. faecalis isolates from pigs fed with 
copper sulphate-supplemented feed. In these isolates, the tcrYAZB operon was found on a plasmid and was 
believed to function in the same way as copYZAB in E. hirae [79,80]. In addition, a statistically significant 
correlation has been observed between tcr-conferred copper resistance and resistance towards glycopeptides 
and macrolides in E. faecium isolates during the period 1997-2000, indicating that copper resistance may lead 
to co-resistance against these classes of antibiotics. However, from 2001 to 2003 the prevalence of resistance 
to both antibiotics had been falling in the E. faecium pig isolates, independently from the use of copper 
sulphate, which supported the hypothesis that the antibiotic resistance was not co-selected for by the tcr system. 
Instead, it was observed that the reduction in both resistance genes was directly correlated with the ban of the 
antibiotic growth promoters avoparcin and tylosin, a glycopeptide and a macrolide, respectively [81].

a CopB ATPase and an MCO [82], and this operon has been proposed to also be regulated by CsoR [83]. A 
recent study has also suggested a significant role of the copXL operon, encoding the putative copper efflux 
transporter CopX and the lipoprotein CopL, in the increased copper tolerance of the MRSA lineage USA300 
[84].

Since a role in the toxicity of copper ions had been attributed to oxidative stress, oxidative stress response is 
important in the protection against copper as well. Oxidative stress response is well-characterised in E. coli 
and S. Typhimurium [85]. E. coli produces two superoxide dismutases, encoded by sodA and sodB, which 
catalyse the conversion of O2- to H2O2 and water [86]. Furthermore, H2O2 can then be degraded to oxygen and 
water by catalases. In E. coli, two catalases have been identified, which are encoded by the katE and katG 
genes. E. coli and S. Typhimurium further possess alkyl hydroperoxide reductases, which reduce organic 
hydroperoxides and hydrogen peroxide to alcohols and water, respectively. In E. coli, this enzyme consists of 
two subunits, namely AhpC and AhpF [85]. The degradation of H2O2 by either mechanism ensures that it does 
not oxidise Cu(II), which leads to the formation of highly reactive hydroxyl radicals [34]. Lastly, the bacterial 
response to oxidative stress is coordinated through two regulatory proteins, namely SoxR and OxyR. SoxR is 
induced by O2- and positively regulates superoxide dismutase genes, while OxyR is induced by H2O2 and 
positively regulates transcription of catalase and alkyl hydroperoxide reductases genes, such as katG and ahpC 
[87]. 
Finally, as survival mechanism under extreme conditions, it is well-known that certain Gram-positive bacteria, 
including Bacillus spp. and Clostridium spp., can form endospores. Under unfavourable conditions of heat, 
radiation and denaturation that inhibit or prevent growth, it has been observed that sporulation allows survival. 
Therefore, endospores have been exposed to metallic copper surfaces in order to evaluate their capacity to 
survive in presence of copper. Slightly prolonged survival on copper surfaces has been reported for endospores 
of C. difficile, however, complete killing was still observed [88,89]. 
In conclusion, despite the various bacterial resistance mechanisms and the use of copper and copper salts as 
antimicrobial agents for thousands of years, no bacterial mechanisms have been found able to confer complete 
resistance to copper so far. 
2.4 The Antimicrobial Properties of Silver 
In contrast to copper, silver has no biological function in bacteria, thus it is a non-essential metal [56]. Non-
essential metals, such as silver, mercury and tellurium, can be poisonous to most bacteria and have microbicidal 
activity. Entry of such metals into bacterial cells is hypothesised to occur through ionic mimicry. That is, the 
uptake of non-essential metals can happen through transporters, dedicated to the acquisition of essential metal 
ions, with low levels of specificity. This behaviour has been observed in various types of transporters, and it 
has also been shown that some metals can be co-transported into the cell by binding to various ligands, such 
as phosphate, peptides or siderophores, through metal-binding functional domains in these compounds [56]. 
Metallic silver surfaces have repeatedly been shown to have no antibacterial activity, except under high pH or 
oxidising conditions, such as wet or highly humid conditions [25,26,39,90,91]. Under such conditions, the 
antibacterial activity of silver surfaces results from the formation of silver oxides. The antibacterial activity is 
==PAGE:20==
Potential for bacterial resistance or tolerance to a copper-silver alloy

due to the physicochemical properties of the silver oxide AgO, i.e. its high solubility required for the release 
of ions, and the toxicity of the Ag+ ions [12,25,92]. It is well documented that silver ions have antibacterial 
activity [93], therefore silver nanoparticles, which can effectively release silver ions, have shown high 
antibacterial activity [94–96] and have even been used in e.g. clothing, bed linens, vacuum cleaners and 
washing machines [93]. 
The mechanism of action of silver toxicity has been studied extensively. An essential factor in the antimicrobial 
activity of silver is the formation of stable bonds between silver and thiol-containing proteins present in the 
bacterial cell membrane, which are involved in transmembrane ATP generation and ion transport. 
Consequently, the formation of silver-thiol-containing protein bonds hinders these membrane processes 
(Figure 5) [97]. More specifically, it is hypothesised that silver ions can catalyse the formation of disulphide 
bonds between the thiol groups of different cysteine residues by oxidation, thus blocking the active sites and 
causing protein inactivation [97,98]. This hypothesis was confirmed by experimental validation, when it was 
shown that several compounds containing thiol groups, such as cysteine, were able to neutralise the toxic effect 
of silver ions, if present at high concentrations [98]. Furthermore, it has been observed that certain Ag+-
modified membrane proteins were able to hinder respiration and electron transfer causing proton leakage. In 
this way silver ions may be able to stop the proton motive force across the membrane, which is vital for the 
integrity of the membrane and cell survival [99,100]. 
 
The disruption of the cell membrane can then facilitate the influx of silver ions into the bacterial cell, where 
they cause additional damage. Feng et al. [101] has shown that silver ions can bind and condense DNA 
molecules, rendering the bacteria unable to perform DNA replication and thus lead to cell death. Lastly, a 
significant role in the bactericidal activity of silver has also been attributed to the production of ROS, mainly 
H2O2, which has been observed during treatment with silver ions under aerobic conditions. In contrast to 
copper, silver only has one oxidative state (Ag+) and therefore is not Fenton active [12]. For this reason it is 
believed that ROS are generated by the interaction of silver ions with thiol groups present e.g. in enzymes that 
are part of the respiratory chain [102]. In agreement with this, Feng et al. [101] has observed decreased 
antimicrobial activity under anaerobic conditions. However, other studies have not detected significant 
differences between silver toxicity in the presence or absence of oxygen [103]. 
However, silver has been used in hospitals in a wide range of medical applications, such as creams for the 
treatment of burn wounds, dental fillings and as a coating for medical devices, such as catheters and artificial 
heart valves [93]. In Barnes et al. [104], clinical trials with a total of 2,355 patients were performed to test the 
effect of using silver alloy-coating in urinary catheters. Here, it was observed that urinary catheters coated with 
a silver alloy significantly reduced the incidence of nosocomial urinary tract infection. Furthermore, a 
significant reduction in morbidity and mortality from ventilator-associated pneumonia has been observed in 
several clinical trials, when using silver oxide-coated endotracheal tubes [105,106].

2.4.1 Bacterial Silver Resistance 
Since silver is a non-essential metal, bacterial mechanisms for controlling the silver concentration are not 
fundamental as for copper. However, silver resistance has been observed in isolates coming from various 
silver-containing environments. The first clinically silver resistant bacterium was isolated from a burn wound 
treated with silver nitrate in 1968 [107], and since then silver resistant bacteria have been isolated frequently. 
McHugh et al. [108] reported a plasmid, designated pMG101, capable of conferring silver resistance in 
Salmonella enterica serovar Typhimurium isolates from a group of patients in a burn unit. This caused the 
death of several patients and forced the burn unit to close down. The plasmid pMG101 conferred resistance 
towards silver, mercury and the antibiotics ampicillin, chloramphenicol, tetracycline, streptomycin and 
sulphonamides. Therefore, the use of silver nitrate, for example, could lead to horizontal transfer of this 
plasmid and subsequent co-resistance towards some very effective antibiotics. This particular plasmid contains 
a gene cluster consisting of nine genes (silCFBA(ORF105)PRSE) responsible for silver resistance, which 
encode two membrane-bound efflux pumps (SilP and SilCBA). SilP is an ATPase efflux pump located in the 
inner membrane, which transports silver ions from the cytoplasm to the periplasm, while SilCBA transports 
silver ions from the periplasm to the outside of the cell. This complex spans both the inner and outer membrane

located in the inner membrane, SilC is located in the outer membrane and SilB connects the two other proteins

transcription of the sil genes, except silE, in response to silver ions. SilE is a periplasmic silver ion-binding

increased silver ion concentrations. SilF is believed to be a silver chaperone that delivers silver ions from SilP 
to SilCBA [93,109]. 
 
The sil gene cluster is the most widespread silver resistance cluster and it has also been found in several other 
plasmids, e.g. in isolates of Salmonella spp., Serratia marcescens [111] and Enterobacter cloacae [112]. 
However, other, less studied plasmids-borne silver resistance mechanisms have also been found in various 
bacteria from both clinical and environmental settings, such as silver mines [113]. 
Finally, oxidative stress response mechanisms play a role in protection against silver ion toxicity, similarly to 
copper ion toxicity, due to the role of oxidative stress. 
2.5 The Antimicrobial Potential of a Copper-Silver Alloy 
It is clear that copper and silver are both antimicrobial metals. Copper and copper alloys have been observed 
to be most effective in dry conditions and to have a reduced antimicrobial effect in wet conditions, in contrast 
to silver, which is antimicrobial only in wet conditions [39]. However, it has been observed that copper and 
silver combined have a higher antimicrobial effect than the two elements alone against E. coli and Legionella 
pneumophila [11,12]. This is a result of a synergistic interaction of the two metals, where the release of copper 
ions from the surface is induced and accelerated by the presence of silver. This has been demonstrated in 
several studies [12,14], e.g. by Hans et al. [12], where the release of copper ions during wet plating was 28-
fold increased on a copper-silver alloy (90 wt.% Cu, 10 wt.% Ag) in comparison to pure copper after a period 
of 100 min. In contrast, the release of silver ions was 4-fold reduced in comparison to pure silver, indicating 
that silver ion release is not the cause of the increased antimicrobial activity in the alloy. This behaviour can 
be explained by the electrochemical oxidation of copper induced by silver, namely galvanic corrosion. Copper 
is oxidised due to the galvanic coupling with silver, and the corrosion mechanism starts when in the presence 
of an electrolyte. In a galvanic couple, the least noble metal corrodes, while the other is protected, meaning it 
is not oxidised [57]. Therefore, copper is oxidised, i.e. copper ions are released, while silver is the site for the 
counter reduction reaction, which in presence of water leads to the formation of OH-. Hydroxyl ions can locally 
raises the pH, adding a further damaging effect to the antimicrobial activity of the materials [14].

S. aureus is a Gram-positive, coccus-shaped, facultative anaerobic bacterium and an important pathogen 
worldwide. It is a member of the commensal nasal microbiota of approximately 30% of the human population,

immunocompromised individuals [116]. S. aureus is responsible for diseases of varying degree ranging from 
mild skin infections to potentially life-threatening diseases, such as pneumonia, bloodstream infection and

resistance [137,138], demonstrating the wide array of phenotypic analyses that the library can be used for. 
2.7 Methods for the Assessment of Antimicrobial Resistance 
Several techniques for the study of resistance have been developed with major focus on antibiotics. Resistance 
can be defined as clinical or microbiological resistance. The definition of clinical resistance is when infection 
by a bacterium is not eradicated by antibiotic therapy. On the other hand, microbiological resistance is defined 
as the presence of an acquired or mutational resistance mechanism that is not present in a susceptible WT and 
==PAGE:26==
Potential for bacterial resistance or tolerance to a copper-silver alloy 
June 22nd 2018 
Page 20 of 69 
 
therefore is examined through the detection of specific genes involved in the resistance mechanism [139]. Most 
standardised methods analyse clinical resistance, where the organism-compound combination is compared to 
a clinical breakpoint value, which is related to the clinically used dosage. However, these can also be used to 
evaluate the role of an acquired resistance mechanism by comparison to a susceptible WT. 
A standard method for investigating the antimicrobial susceptibility of bacteria is the minimum inhibitory 
concentration (MIC) assay. In this assay, increasing concentrations of a compound are prepared in liquid 
medium, and the lowest concentration, which leads to no visible growth, is defined as the MIC. For the sake 
of reproducibility, it is important that the inoculum, incubation conditions and medium are kept the same 
[140,141]. This method is rather time-consuming, but it generates qualitative results and quantitative results, 
since a MIC value is obtained. The use of microtiter plates has made the MIC assay slightly more rapid and 
practical [141]. 
 
In order to determine the antibiotic sensitivity of bacteria, the disk diffusion susceptibility testing is often used 
due to its simplicity and practicality. Here, a disk impregnated with a fixed concentration of antibiotic is placed 
on an agar plate streaked with the relevant bacterium. If the bacterium is susceptible or intermediate 
susceptible, the antibiotic then diffuses from the disk forming a concentration gradient, which causes an 
inhibition zone where bacterial growth is inhibited. This zone extends until the point, where the concentration 
of the antibiotic is insufficient to inhibit growth and is measured to the nearest millimetre. Also in this case, it 
is necessary that inoculum, incubation conditions and the growth medium follow the recommendations of the 
standards [142], the European Committee on Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing (EUCAST) or the Clinical 
and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI). However, since there is no universal consensus on the definition 
of a clinical breakpoint, the different standards often have different breakpoints for antimicrobial susceptibility 
and resistance for each organism-compound combination. In addition, the breakpoint values can also change 
over time, depending on changes to drug dose and mode of administration [143]. Therefore the disk diffusion 
test gives qualitative results, as it classifies organisms as susceptible, intermediate and resistant, but it is not a 
quantitative measure of susceptibility [141].  
A similar test for antimicrobial susceptibility is the antimicrobial gradient method, or E-test. Instead of 
antimicrobial disks, strips with antimicrobial gradients are used, and the MIC can be determined by the 
intersection of the inhibition zone and the test strip. Thus, in contrast to the disk diffusion test, a MIC value is 
obtained from this test [141].  
These three methods are all performed manually and can be time-consuming. Therefore, various automated 
systems for susceptibility testing have been developed in order to provide test results and analysis more rapidly, 
reducing technical errors and consequently increasing accuracy [141].

2.7.1 Adaptive Laboratory Evolution 
The methodologies above all use a one-time exposure to determine resistance or sensitivity. However, often 
antimicrobials are used over longer time and with repeated use. Thus, experimental designs to study this have 
been developed. Adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) is a standard method for the study of the development 
of increased tolerance or resistance towards a certain stress with repeated exposure. ALE is an effective tool 
in genetic engineering, as it applies an artificial selective pressure to a community of microbial cells with the 
purpose of inducing the selective development of mutants that can survive the particular conditions better. This 
type of evolution experiment has showed high efficacy in many cases, since the development of the desired 
characteristic is linked to the survival of the population. ALE has been widely used in relation to metabolic 
engineering for the optimisation of industrial strains [144], such as Saccharomyces cerevisiae with increased 
tolerance to heat [145] and saline stress [146], E. coli and S. cerevisiae with altered substrate specificity [147–
149] and increased tolerance to toxic compounds [150,151]. Thus, ALE can be used to adapt strains to 
alternative nutrients, environmental stress [152], antibiotics [153] and it has also been observed to lead to the 
activation of latent metabolic pathways [154]. Most ALE experiments have been performed with E. coli and 
S. cerevisiae, but the potential of ALE has also been proven in several other organisms [155–157]. 
 
In a study by Jahn et al. [153], the potential of E. coli K12 to develop increase resistance to the three antibiotics 
amikacin, tetracycline and piperacillin was evaluated by ALE. After 14 days of adaptive evolution (approx. 
56-70 generations), a 170-fold, 15-fold and 80-fold increase in tolerance was observed towards sublethal 
concentrations of amikacin, tetracycline and piperacillin, respectively. In addition, Zipperer et al. [158] tested 
resistance development towards sublethal concentrations of rifampicin and their newly discovered antibiotic 
lugdunin for 30 days (approx. 300 generations). Here, a 100,000-fold increase was observed in the MIC of S. 
aureus USA300 LAC against rifampicin, but no resistance development was observed against lugdunin. It was 
observed that lugdunin impaired a broad range of metabolic processes, but the exact mechanism of action was 
not identified. In contrast, rifampicin inhibits a single enzyme [159] and it has in fact been observed that highly 
increased rifampicin resistance can be attributed to only two missense mutations [160]. From these studies, it 
can be concluded that antibiotic resistance can develop rapidly, but that this potential is drug-specific and tied 
to the complexity of the mechanism of action of the drug. 
A standard ALE experiment is performed by continuously exposing a bacterial population to an increasing 
stress factor, thereby allowing random mutations to accumulate. Some mutations may encode phenotypic 
changes that allow the particular cell to have increased fitness under the stress conditions. In this way, 
subpopulations with advantageous mutations will be able to grow, divide faster and potentially become 
dominant in the population [144]. ALE experiments can last for different periods of time, ranging typically 
from 100 to 2000 generations, which correspond to a few weeks or months [152]. Although the mutation rate 
remains constant, the increase in fitness is the highest between 100 and 500 generations, and subsequently the 
rate of fitness decreases [161].

Knowing that bacteria can easily develop resistance towards any class of antibiotic, investigation of the risk of 
resistance development towards potential antimicrobial agents (or systems) is crucial. In this project, the 
purpose was therefore to investigate the potential of S. aureus JE2 to develop increased tolerance or resistance 
to a copper-silver alloy coating during prolonged exposure. In order to accomplish this, an adapted ALE setup 
was designed ad hoc. Since it was not feasible to increase the amount of the antimicrobial agent, which is a 
general principle in a standard ALE, the media composition (nutrients-to-buffer ratio) was adjusted 
accordingly. The nutrients-to-buffer ratio was decreased over time in polystyrene tubes containing single 
copper-silver alloy coated coupons. In such a manner, S. aureus progressively had less nutrients available and 
therefore became more exposed to the toxic effect of the alloy. This was also done considering that various 
organic compounds present in the nutrient media could sequestrate free copper ions due to their high binding 
affinity, thereby protecting the bacteria in the suspension [43]. Therefore, reducing the content of nutrients 
would result in an increasing amount of accessible free toxic ions. In continuation with the ALE experiment, 
S. aureus JE2 and the ALE lineages were whole-genome sequenced to evaluate any genomic changes that had 
occurred during exposure to the copper-silver alloy coating. Lastly, disk diffusion tests were performed on the 
adapted strains to evaluate the potential of co- or cross-resistance to antibiotics, as such links have been 
observed in other organisms [17]. 
 
In addition, the roles of the genes copA and csoR, involved in copper homeostasis, and ahpC and sodM, 
involved in oxidative stress response, were evaluated in the survival of S. aureus JE2 exposed to copper-silver 
alloy coated surfaces [16]. The importance of the plasmid-borne copper resistance gene tcrB in the survival of 
E. faecium was evaluated as well [17].

3.1 Materials 
The bacterial survival and adhesion to a copper-silver alloy coating (59±2 wt.% Cu, 39±2 wt.% Ag) was tested 
using Ø101 mm and 10201 mm stainless steel coupons (AISI 316L, cold rolled, 2B surface finish) 
electroplated with the alloy (Figure 8). Uncoated Ø101 mm and 10201 mm stainless steel coupons (AISI

Ø101 mm stainless steel coupons were used for the ALE tests for practical reasons. Prior to the tests, the 
copper-silver alloy coated coupons were sterilised by UV light at 254 nm for two hours and the stainless steel 
coupons were sterilised by autoclaving at 121℃ for 15 min. UV light was used in order to avoid any surface 
modification on the copper-silver alloy coating due to the heat treatment.

3.3 DNA Extraction 
Genomic DNA from pure overnight cultures of the S. aureus JE2 copA, ahpC, csoR and sodM mutants was 
extracted using the Genomic tip 100/G kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) according to the manufacturer’s 
guidelines with minor adjustments. To lyse the staphylococcal cell wall, lysozyme was replaced by 200 µL of 
0.5 mg/mL lysostaphin solution in each reaction. After the addition of isopropanol, precipitated DNA was 
clearly visible in the samples. 500 µL of this suspension was centrifuged for 20 min at 20.000 rpm and the 
supernatant was removed. 500 µL of 70% ethanol was then added and the suspensions were centrifuged for 15 
min at 20.000 rpm. The ethanol was removed, and the tubes were left in a sterile bench to allow the remaining 
ethanol to evaporate for approximately 60 min. Lastly, 100 µL of milliQ water was added and the samples 
were stored at 5°C for three days to let the DNA dissolve. The quality and quantity of the extracted DNA was 
then assessed using a DeNovix DS-11+ Spectrophotometer (Saveen Werner) (Appendix I) and stored at -20°C.

3.4 Confirmation of Transposon Insertions in NTML Mutants 
Tn insertions in the S. aureus library were verified through polymerase chain reaction (PCR). The primer pairs 
were designed based on the gene sequences and the Tn sequence according to the following criteria. GC 
content was between 40 and 60%, the melting temperature was between 55 to 65°C and, the difference between 
the melting temperatures of two primers of a pair was no more than 5°C. The optimal primer and amplicon 
length was in the 18-22 bp and 500-1000 bp range, respectively. The presence of Gs or Cs at all ends and no 
regions with more than three repetitions of the same base were ensured. CLC Main Workbench version 7 
(Qiagen) was used for verification of binding to the relevant genomes and PrimerBLAST [165] was used to 
check for other potential binding sites within the genomes. All PCR amplifications were run using a T100 
Thermal Cycler instrument (Bio-Rad Laboratories) and the Blue TEMPase Hot Start DNA polymerase 2x 
Master Mix (Ampliqon, Odense, Denmark) containing the TEMPase Hot Start DNA polymerase, dNTP, 
MgCl2, TEMPase buffer and inert blue dye for subsequent visualisation. The first cycle for the initial 
denaturation was run at 95°C for 15 min, followed by 35 cycles of 30 s at 95°C for the denaturation, 30 s at 
55°C for the annealing and 30 s at 72°C for the extension. This was followed by one final cycle at 72°C for 10 
min. The total reaction volume was 25 µL, all amplifications were run in duplicates and non-template controls 
(NTC) were included. The DNA fragments were visualised by running an analytical gel (1% w/vol agarose) 
using a 1 kb DNA ladder (Thermo Fisher Scientific SM0314). The DNA was visualised by UV irradiation in 
a gel doc (Bio-rad Universal Hood II) after staining on a 3 µg/mL ethidium bromide solution (Sigma Aldrich 
E1510) for 20 min. 
3.5 Design and Optimisation of Experimental Setups 
Prior to the ALE experiment and the bacterial survival and adhesion tests, the experimental setups were 
optimised in order to ensure good conditions for studying bacterial tolerance to the metallic copper-silver alloy 
surfaces. Therefore, different media and nutrient concentrations, inoculation tubes of various materials and 
coupon sizes were tested. The purpose was to find a reliable and reproducible setup that would allow for a 
difference in killing efficacy between test and control coupons, and any variation between the survival and 
adhesion capability of the different strains. Therefore, the aim was to find a specific medium composition that 
would allow monitoring the efficacy of the copper-silver coating through bacterial time-kill curves, where the 
difference between the stainless steel control surfaces and the copper-silver coated surfaces would be 
approximately 2-3 log CFU/mL. In contrast, it was important to ensure a sufficient level of growth for the ALE 
experiment, but to also progressively reduce the amount of nutrients, as a way to increase the amount of toxic 
copper ions, in order to expose the bacteria to increasingly challenging conditions over time to mimic a 
standard ALE experiment. 
BHI growth medium, phosphate buffered saline solution (PBS; Oxoid BR0014G) 0.85 % saline solution 
(Sigma-Aldrich 7647-14-5) were tested with the aim of finding a medium capable of ensuring growth but

3.6 Test of Bacterial Survival and Adhesion to Copper-Silver Alloy Coating 
Survival was tested using stainless steel coupons electroplated with the described copper-silver alloy coating 
and uncoated stainless steel coupons were used as controls. The copper-silver alloy coated coupons and the 
control coupons were transferred to sterile 10 mL polystyrene tubes and inoculated with 1.5 mL of a 1000-fold 
diluted overnight culture of S. aureus JE2 in 1/16 BHI volume in PBS suspension. The samples were incubated 
at 25℃ with 200 rpm shaking for 24 h. Coupons were sampled after 30 min, 2 h, 8 h and 24 h using sterile 
tweezers and rinsed with 2 mL sterile PBS to remove loosely-attached cells. The coupons were then moved to 
sterile polystyrene tubes containing 2 mL sterile PBS and the firmly-attached bacterial cells were removed 
from the coupons by sonication for four min at 28 kHz (Delta 220; Deltasonic, Meaux, France). Lastly, the 
samples were vortexed for 15 s to further release all the attached cells [166]. Number of cells attached to 
coupons and in suspension were determined by serial dilution and plating on BHI agar. CFU count was 
performed after overnight incubation at 37℃ overnight. All tests were performed in biological triplicates. 
3.7 Adaptive Laboratory Evolution 
S. aureus JE2 was exposed to copper-silver alloy coated stainless steel coupons and uncoated stainless steel 
coupons continuously for a period of 30 days, equivalent to 180 to 210 generations, assuming six to seven 
generations per transfer. An overnight culture of S. aureus JE2 was 100-fold diluted in fresh BHI broth and 
1.5 mL culture was added to four sterile 10 mL polystyrene tubes containing a copper-silver alloy electroplated 
coupon, and to four sterile 10 mL polystyrene tubes with uncoated stainless steel control coupons. The samples 
were incubated at 25℃ with 250 rpm shaking for 24 h. This was done in order to avoid sedimentation and any 
diffusion-dependent bias. On the next day, the overnight culture was 100-fold diluted in fresh BHI medium 
and 1.5 mL added to a sterile tube containing new metal coupons. Tubes were incubated in the same conditions 
for 24 h. After five days, the BHI medium was 1/2 diluted in PBS (i.e. 1/2 BHI volume in PBS suspension) 
and the concentration of BHI in the suspension was further halved after 10 days (Figure 9). Every five days, 
the cultures were checked for contaminations through plating in BHI agar and incubation at 37℃ overnight, 
and samples were extracted and mixed with glycerol to a final concentration of 25% and frozen at -80℃.

polymorphisms (DIPs) with a frequency of 50% and 70% in the sequencing reads were detected for each 
lineage using the “Basic Variant Detection” tool with default settings.

volumes were adjusted according to the ratio of the areas. 
First, adhesion of S. aureus JE2 was tested in PBS, BHI and 1/10 BHI volume in PBS in polystyrene tubes

PBS at all sampling points, while there was no statistical difference between stainless steel and copper-silver 
in BHI. In 1/10 BHI, there was instead approx. a two log-fold difference between the adhesion to stainless

below the limit of detection at all other sampling points.

and copper-silver surfaces. Thus, a detectable level of cells survived in suspension and at the same time test 
and control coupons differed significantly in killing efficacy.

4.4 Adaptive Laboratory Evolution of S. aureus 
Four S. aureus JE2 lineages (referred to as CA1-4) were exposed to copper-silver surfaces in BHI suspension 
for 30 days and four control lineages (referred to as SS1-4) to stainless steel surfaces. The cell density was 
checked every day to ensure sufficient growth, and every five days the cultures were plated on BHI agar to 
check for contaminations and samples were extracted for frozen stocks. Furthermore, the BHI concentration 
was halved in PBS suspension every five days. After 15 days, the BHI concentration could not be lowered to 
1/8 due to insufficient growth and consequently, the BHI concentration was kept at 1/4. Thus, the first five 
transfers were made in BHI, the next five were made in 1/2 BHI in PBS suspension and the least 20 transfers 
were made in 1/4 BHI in PBS suspension. The experiment lasted for 180 to 210 generations.

4.4.3 Mutational Changes in S. aureus During Exposure to a Copper-Silver Alloy Surface 
The four lineages exposed to copper-silver for 180 to 210 generations, the four control lineages exposed to 
stainless steel and the WT, S. aureus JE2, were whole-genome sequenced. The closed genome of S. aureus 
USA300, which consists of 2.87 Mb, was used as a reference for assembly of the S. aureus JE2 genome, while 
the JE2 genome was further used for assembly of the genomes of the ALE lineages. Each genome was 
assembled into seven contigs and had an average coverage of 90-100. 
 
SNP and DIP analysis of the genomes revealed two SNPs with frequencies above 70% in the reads to be 
present in all eight ALE lineages, including the stainless steel controls (Table 6). One SNP was in the 5S 
ribosomal RNA gene, while the other was in a noncoding region (SNP2). No DIPs were found in any lineages. 
At a frequency above 50%, between one and five SNPs were observed in the mraZ gene in all lineages and a 
multi-nucleotide polymorphism (MNP) was observed in CA1, CA3, CA4 and SS1. Two additional SNPs were 
observed in the CA3 lineage in the sdrC gene and in a noncoding region (SNP1) and a SNP was found in a 
noncoding region (SNP3) in CA4 as well. In SS1 and SS4, a SNP was observed in different noncoding regions 
(SNP1 and SNP3, respectively). When looking at frequencies below 50%, all of these mutations were found 
in lineages exposed to both copper-silver and stainless steel. Thus, similar mutational profiles and mutation 
rates were found in lineages exposed to copper-silver and stainless steel and no large genetic rearrangements 
were discovered.

5 Discussion 
Metallic copper-silver alloy surfaces are promising bactericidal agents thanks to their high antimicrobial 
activity [12,14]. A novel electroplated copper-silver alloy coating for stainless steel has a high antimicrobial 
activity in comparison to pure copper, due to an electrochemical mechanism governed by the galvanic coupling 
with silver, which induces accelerated dissolution of copper ions depending on the copper and silver area ratio 
[12,14]. Furthermore, electroplating of copper creates a favourable metallic surface structure. Electroplated 
structures have increased antimicrobial activity compared to standard industrial rolled or polished copper 
surfaces, due to the increased release of copper ions and the tendency of bacteria to place themselves along the 
typical surface grooves, thus increasing the area for bacteria-metal contact [49]. However, the most critical 
concern regarding novel antimicrobial agents is the potential of pathogens developing resistance similarly to 
what has been observed with antibiotics. 
 
In this project, the role of different mechanisms believed to increase tolerance to copper ions was investigated 
towards a novel copper-silver alloy coating. Furthermore, the ability of S. aureus JE2 to develop increased 
tolerance or resistance towards this coating was evaluated in an ALE experiment through exposure for 180 to 
210 generations. 
First, the role of the copper homeostasis genes copA and csoR and the oxidative stress genes ahpC and sodM 
was evaluated by testing Tn mutants from NTML [16] for their adhesion and survival in suspension with 
copper-silver coated coupons. Overall, no significant reduction in survival or adhesion was observed between 
the WT and the four mutants over a period of 24 hours and none of the mutants had tendencies to consistently 
have higher or lower survival than the WT either. 
 
The role of CopA, AhpC and SodM in the response towards excess levels of copper ions has been confirmed 
through mutational analysis [69,169,170]. Supporting the role of these proteins, reduced survival of copper 
homeostasis mutants has previously been observed in copper ion suspensions, indicating a potential for 
increased tolerance under such conditions, where a metallic surface is not present [75,80]. However, in regard 
to contact killing, several studies has shown that single copper homeostasis genes can at most lead to slightly 
prolonged survival, but cannot prevent killing [34,40,41]; thereby supporting the results of this project. 
Furthermore, the repressor CsoR is currently the only known copper sensor to regulate the copper homeostasis 
mechanisms in S. aureus. Knocking out the csoR gene has been observed to result in increased expression of 
copA [171] and potentially other copper ATPases and chaperones [73], and thus possibly increased survival. 
No increased survival was observed for the first eight hours, but after 24 hours a statistically significant one 
log-fold increase in survival was observed on copper-silver coupons. However, due to the high variation of 
these adhesion and survival assays, one log-fold difference or less is still not sufficient to indicate a 
considerable diversity in survival between the mutants and the WT. 
 
Since a single gene is not enough to confer a significant level of resistance, these results suggest that multiple 
==PAGE:50==
Potential for bacterial resistance or tolerance to a copper-silver alloy 
June 22nd 2018 
Page 44 of 69 
 
resistance mechanisms are needed, supporting the theory that bacterial killing by metallic copper surfaces is 
indeed based on multiple mechanisms. Investigations of triple and double mutants also support this, as a 
significant effect has been observed in E. coli cusCFBA copA cuoO [34] and S. aureus copA copX 
mutants [84]. Regarding the oxidative stress factors, AhpC and SodM, it is also a possibility that other 
protective mechanisms are able to compensate for their loss. In fact, it is known that S. aureus contains another 
superoxide dismutase, SodA [172], and this may be the reason that the sodM mutant showed similar survival 
to the WT. 
 
It would be interesting to test other important copper homeostasis mechanisms as well, such as the copXL 
operon, which is found in S. aureus USA300 and has recently been suggested to play a significant role in 
increased copper tolerance [84]. Mutation in one of these genes was observed to significantly decrease survival 
in contrast to copA mutation. However, this was published by Purves et al. [84] after the experiments in this 
study had begun and thus it was not possible to include these genes. 
Additionally, the role of a plasmid-borne copper resistance gene, tcrB, found in Enterococcus isolates from 
pigs on diet supplemented with copper sulphate [17], was evaluated in its ability to confer increased resistance 
in tcrB-positive strains compared to tcrB-negative strains. Here, E. faecium 74-30017-3 (tcrB-negative) and 
74-30022-2 (tcrB-positive) were tested in their adhesion and survival in suspension with copper-silver coated 
coupons. The tcrB-positive strain had increased survival on copper-silver surfaces in comparison to the tcrB-
negative strain, however, it was still reduced at least three log-fold on copper-silver in comparison to stainless 
steel. This statistically significant reduction is likely due to the other mechanisms, apart from copper ion 
release, that are part of contact killing. The physical contact between bacterial cells and copper surfaces has 
been shown to lead to major damage and additionally, the galvanic coupling of copper and silver leads to the 
reduction of silver and consequently a rise in pH, which can also be damaging [14,57]. Furthermore, due to 
the high release of copper ions from copper-silver alloys, it is also likely that the concentration of copper ions 
exceeds the concentration that the copper resistance mechanisms can manage. 
 
In suspension, survival of the two isolates was similar for the first two hours, but after eight hours, one log-
fold lower survival of the tcrB-negative strain was observed. After 24 hours, at least a three log-fold difference 
was observed between the tcrB-negative and tcrB-positive strains. Again, a significant reduction was observed 
in the survival of the tcrB-positive strain in suspension with copper-silver in comparison to stainless steel. In 
this case there is little direct contact with the metal surface, but the strain is still exposed to increased pH and 
high concentrations of copper ions that are released from the surface. Thus, these results indicate that some 
copper resistance can be conferred by TcrB especially in copper ion suspensions and to a lesser degree on 
metallic copper-silver surfaces. The increased survival of the tcrB-positive strain is supported by Hasman et 
al. [80], which observed that E. faecium strains harbouring the tcrB gene were able to survive seven times 
higher copper sulphate concentrations in suspension than tcrB-negative strains. However, the study did not test 
the efficacy on metallic surfaces.

To further assess the relevance and potential risk of TcrB, a BLAST was performed on the protein sequence 
to evaluate the abundance of organisms producing this copper resistance factor or other closely related proteins. 
High sequence homology was found with proteins in many bacteria. The majority of these proteins were 
labelled as cadmium transporters, however, considering the high levels of sequence identity it may be possible 
that these proteins have binding affinity to copper as well. Supporting this, cadmium is a transition metal, 
similarly to copper, and due to structural similarities among transition metals, it has been suggested that 
ATPases that are specific to one are able to transport other transition metals as well [71]. Thereby, it is very 
likely that copper can actually be transported by cadmium-specific ATPases. The presence of TcrB in S. aureus 
was also checked. Here, the nearest protein had 50.0% identity to TcrB and this protein was also categorised 
as a copper-translocating ATPase. However, it did not appear that the TcrB protein had been found in any S. 
aureus isolates yet. Furthermore, Hasman et al. [80] investigated E. faecium isolates from a group of Danish 
pigs, broilers, calves and humans and detected tcrB-positive isolates in 75% of pigs, 34% of broilers, 16% of 
calves and 10% of humans and concluded that this gene was responsible for the majority of E. faecium copper 
resistance in Denmark at the time of the study [80]. Therefore, this gene could pose a potential threat 
concerning copper ion resistance in suspension, however, regarding metallic copper surfaces, such as the 
copper-silver coating, its effect appears to be limited. 
To further examine the potential of increased tolerance to the copper-silver alloy coating, an ALE experiment 
was performed on S. aureus JE2. This experiment differed from a classical ALE experiment, because it was 
not possible to increase the amount of the antimicrobial agent as in presence of soluble compounds. Therefore, 
the media composition (nutrients-to-buffer ratio) was adjusted to mimic the standard scenario. BHI contains 
nutrients, such as meat extract, which can protect the bacterial cells from the toxic effect of the copper-silver 
alloy coating by sequestering copper ions [43,44]. In contrast, PBS contains high concentrations of sodium 
chloride and phosphates which together induce increased release of copper ions [42]. The extent of exposure 
of the bacterial cells to toxic copper ions was adjusted through gradual reduction of BHI and corresponding 
increase of PBS. After 10 days, the BHI-to-PBS ratio reached 1/4 BHI volume in PBS suspension, and when 
the ratio was decreased to 1/8 BHI volume in PBS after 15 days, insufficient growth was observed after 24 
hours. Therefore, 1/4 BHI was again used for the rest of the experiment. In retrospect, this probably could have 
been overcome by performing the transfer every two days.  
After 30 days of ALE, S. aureus JE2 did not display increased tolerance to the copper-silver alloy coating. To 
explore the evolutionary pattern during exposure to copper-silver alloys, the genomes were sequenced and 
analysed for SNPs and DIPs. It was demonstrated that copper-silver exposure did not lead to any unique 
evolutionary changes, as the mutational pattern was similar in the control lineages. 
 
In general, only variants with a minimum of 70% or 80% frequency in the sequencing reads are considered in 
ALE experiments [153,173,174]. However, in this experiment, the genomic DNA was not extracted from 
clonal cultures, but instead directly from the heterogenous populations of the ALE cultures. Therefore, a lower

frequency might be expected, since not only the sequencing quality affects the frequency, but also the actual 
occurrence of the mutation in the heterogenous population. Nevertheless, a favourable mutation that leads to 
increased fitness would be expected to become dominant in the population and thereby still have quite high 
frequency in the sequencing reads. However, due to the heterogenous nature of these cultures, variants with 
50% frequencies in the sequencing reads were examined as well. Few mutations were detected, and no major 
differences were observed in variants with frequencies above 50% and 70% between the control lineages and 
the copper-silver exposed lineages. To further confirm this, SNP and DIP analysis was also performed, when 
the genomes were assembled by two alternative approaches: Using the S. aureus USA300 chromosome 
(GenBank accession number NC_007793.1) as reference for all genomes or using a de novo assembled S. 
aureus JE2 genome as reference. In both cases, the same tendency was observed; test and control lineages had 
similar mutational profiles and none of the affected genes appear to be directly involved in copper or silver 
resistance. Since multiple of the same mutations were observed in all eight ALE lineages, this could also 
indicate that some of the mutations have instead happened in the WT strain between the beginning of the ALE 
experiment and DNA extraction. Lastly, the number of detected mutations in copper-silver- and stainless steel-
exposed lineages was similar after 180 to 210 generations, indicating that the antimicrobial surface did not 
increase the mutation rate, as has been observed with several antibiotics [175–177]. This is an advantage when 
considering the potential of antimicrobial resistance development, as an increased mutation rate will increase 
the possibility of resistance development. 
Thus, these results indicate that increased tolerance or resistance towards this copper-silver alloy coating seems 
to not develop easily. In support of this, copper has been used widespread for thousands of years and so far, 
few resistance mechanisms have been observed and their effects have been limited [5]. In contrast, since the 
discovery of penicillin in 1928 [178], clinical resistance towards all known antibiotic has been discovered 
usually after just a few years on the market [179,180] and the use of sublethal concentrations in ALE 
experiments have led to antibiotic resistance within a few weeks [153,158]. 
 
In general, widespread bacterial resistance to contact killing by copper seems unlikely for several reasons. 
First, cells are not proliferating on metallic copper surfaces, which prevents the development of resistance by 
selective mutation [5]. Secondly, plasmid DNA is unlikely to be able to spread between cells, as copper ions 
can damage and degrade extracellular DNA [51]. Therefore, both vertical and horizontal gene transfer are 
hindered during contact killing [181]. In addition, it could be speculated that since copper kills through multiple 
non-specific mechanisms and not just one mechanism, in contrast to most antibiotics, the probability of 
developing increased resistance is much lower, as more genetic modifications would be needed [182].  
However, initially it was also assumed that resistance could not develop towards antimicrobial peptides, as 
they targeted fundamental features in bacteria, but several adaptive evolution studies have since proven this 
wrong through long-term exposure (500 to 700 generations) [183,184]. Thereby, it is possible that resistance 
could still develop towards this copper-silver alloy as well.

Based on these considerations, it seems that a period of exposure longer than 30 days would be needed to more 
realistically assess the potential of bacteria to develop increased tolerance. 
The modified setup of this ALE experiment with antimicrobial metallic surfaces could potentially be used in 
other situations as well, where an investigation through ALE is needed, but it is not possible to increase the 
stress gradient that the bacteria are exposed to. In this way, laboratory evolution is not limited to soluble 
stressors, but can include metals releasing toxic ions in oxidising conditions and other systems capable of 
releasing toxic molecules.  
Next, it was examined if the continuous exposure to the copper-silver alloy coating could lead to co- or cross-
selection of resistance towards antibiotics. This was done through disk diffusion tests, and as expected the 
eight ALE lineages were all resistant to ciprofloxacin, oxacillin and penicillin G, similarly to the WT strain 
JE2. One ALE lineage exposed to copper-silver (CA3) showed to be clinically resistant to gentamicin, due to 
a smaller inhibition zone. However, a lineage exposed to stainless steel (SS1) also developed resistance to 
gentamicin, and this occurred through the formation of satellite colonies within the inhibition zone, indicating 
heteroresistance in the population. However, since gentamicin resistance was developed on both copper-silver 
alloy coated and stainless steel surfaces, it can be concluded that this particular resistance development was 
not necessarily linked to the copper-silver alloy coating. 
 
In addition, it should be noted that the clinical breakpoints used in relation to vancomycin are from 2013 [185], 
since these were the most recent values available. The reason, in fact, is that disk diffusion has proven too 
unreliable for vancomycin since 2013, as it cannot distinguish between susceptible strains and glycopeptide 
resistance, which is not mediated by the vanA gene [167,186]. It was also noted that strains were suspected to 
be resistant to vancomycin when the edge of the inhibition zone was fuzzy, even if the inhibition zone diameter 
indicated susceptibility [185]. However, in the present study all strains presented sharp inhibition zone edges, 
demonstrating that they most likely were susceptible to the antibiotic. However, to be completely certain, a 
broth MIC assay would be required [167].  
Recently, there has been much concern about co-selection of resistance to metals, biocides and antibiotics 
[187–191]. Animal guts, biofilms and clinical settings have been recognised as environments where co-
selection of resistance towards metals and antibiotics can occur [58]. Co-selection of resistance towards copper 
and antibiotics has especially been identified in livestock isolates of E. faecium and E. faecalis. In these 
isolates, the copper resistance gene tcrB was found on a plasmid, which also carried the genes erm(B) and 
vanA, encoding resistance to macrolides and glycopeptides, respectively [17,80]. Other studies have observed 
similar co-resistance to copper, macrolides and glycopeptides in Enterococci isolated from different livestock 
fed with copper sulphate as a growth promoter [192–194]. In contrast, Santo et al. [58] have examined 115 S. 
aureus isolates from copper coins, which had increased tolerance to copper after prolonged exposure to 
metallic copper surfaces, and observed no co-selection of resistance towards copper and antibiotics in any of

the isolates. Likewise, Cavaco et al. [195] examined co-resistance towards copper and methicillin in 593 
MRSA isolates from pigs and calves from Europe, Canada and China, and no association was seen between 
the two. Instead, a strong co-selection of resistance towards zinc and methicillin was observed in these isolates, 
due to the acquisition of a plasmid containing the zinc resistance gene czrC and the methicillin resistance gene 
mecA. Though co-resistance with copper was not reported, this does demonstrate the risk of co-resistance 
towards metals and antibiotics in S. aureus.  
Altogether, these results indicate that the risk of resistance development towards heavy metals, such as copper 
and zinc, varies depending on the mode of use. It appears that there is an increased risk of resistance 
development, and subsequent co-resistance, when the metals are used continuously at sub-lethal 
concentrations, such as in agriculture and aquaculture, where copper sulphate and zinc chloride are added to 
feed [187]. Under such conditions, bacteria are still able to grow, and horizontal gene transfer is possible, 
which would allow the transfer of plasmids carrying metal and potentially also other resistance genes. 
However, since dry copper surfaces do not support growth of microorganisms [58], the development of co-
resistance seems unlikely on the surface of a copper-silver alloy coating, particularly in the intended setting, 
i.e. frequently touched surfaces in hospitals.

6 Concluding Remarks 
Copper and copper alloy surfaces have shown high antimicrobial activity. Consequently, there has been an 
increasing focus on the potential use of metallic copper for frequently touched surfaces in hospitals and 
healthcare facilities, since these surfaces are important vehicles of transmission of nosocomial pathogens. It 
appears that the main mechanism of action of contact killing by copper surfaces is a combination of severe cell 
envelope damage and release of copper ions, which extensively damage DNA and intracellular components 
through multiple non-specific mechanisms. Due to the numerous mechanisms of action that participate in 
contact killing, it has been suggested that resistance development is an unlikely possibility. 
In this study, the role of known copper resistance mechanisms in S. aureus and E. faecium was evaluated 
during exposure to a highly antimicrobial electroplated copper-silver alloy coated surface (60 wt.% Cu, 40 
wt.% Ag). The chromosomally-encoded genes copA, csoR, ahpC and sodM in S. aureus showed to have a 
limited effect on adhesion on wet copper surfaces and survival in suspension, while the plasmid-borne gene 
tcrB in E. faecium showed to increase survival at least three log-fold after 24 hours in suspension. However, it 
had a limited effect in adhesion and survival on copper-silver surfaces. 
 
Furthermore, a novel, modified ALE setup was designed, where increased exposure to the copper-silver alloy 
coating in suspension was obtained through decreasing nutrient concentrations in the media. In this experiment, 
increased tolerance was not obtained after approximately 180 to 210 generations of laboratory evolution, 
supporting the assumption that resistance development towards such metallic surfaces is difficult. In addition, 
exposure to copper-silver did not appear to select for a specific mutational pattern and no development of co- 
or cross-resistance to antibiotics was observed. However, it would be highly relevant to extend the duration of 
the ALE experiment to evaluate the potential for resistance further. 
All in all, the risk of resistance and tolerance development towards this copper-silver alloy coating appears to 
be low based on this and previous studies. Furthermore, the intended working environment, i.e. dry surfaces 
in hospitals, is highly unfavourable to growth and consequently development of increased resistance. Should 
new resistance mechanisms develop, the results of the present study suggest that their effect is likely to be very 
limited. Despite these encouraging results, it is still important to elucidate the extensive mechanisms behind 
contact killing by copper and this copper-silver alloy.
