Abstract
Hip fractures in the proximal femur primarily affect the elderly and lead to severe complica-
tions, with only 50% regaining their pre-injury mobility. Globally, cases are expected to rise
from 2.6 million (2025) to 6.25 million (2050), with more than 2% classified as morbidly obese
(BMI > 40). Osteosynthesis of trochanteric hip fractures, a subtype of hip fractures, presents
unique challenges in morbidly obese. Excessive adipose tissue complicates surgical access and
necessitates larger incisions. This leads to significantly poor prognoses, including high risks of
deep wound infections and 91% higher 30-day mortality rates compared with normal-weight
patients (BMI 18.5-24.9). This thesis introduces a novel osteosynthesis for trochanteric hip
fractures using a retrograde intramedullary hip nail (RIMHN) inserted through the knee,
as opposed to a conventional antegrade intramedullary hip nail (AIMHN) inserted through
trochanter major. Reduction of perioperative exposure and overall improvement of patient
prognosis is investigated by developing and testing a RIMHN prototype.
Finite element
analysis (FEA) and biomechanical compression- and load-until-failure tests were conducted
to evaluate the compressive yield strengths. The load at yield point was 4000 N for AIMHN
and 5000 N for RIMHN indicating comparable strengths, although the results were impacted
by sliding of the neck screws during load-until-failure tests. FEA predicted loads at yield
point (3700 N for AIMHN and 2900 N for RIMHN) more accurately than displacement at
yield point, which proved to be 4 and 10 times larger in the load-until-failure tests, respec-
tively. Additionally, the FEA prediction of the exact location of the fracture was incorrect
for AIMHN. Testing in a plastic femoral bone proved the operational feasibility of RIMHN
successful, but further refinement is required for closed reduction techniques in vivo. This
novel osteosynthesis using RIMHN will improve the prognosis in morbidly obese patients by
significantly reducing the operative time from 4 hours to 1 hour, minimizing the incision
length from 40 cm to 5 cm, thus lowering the risk of complications.

1
Motivation
Hip fractures represent a severe category of fractures in the proximal femur, predominantly
affecting the elderly population, with the highest incidence rates in Scandinavia [20]. It is
expected that 2.6 million people will suffer a hip fracture globally in 2025, which will increase
to 6.25 million in 2050 [50]. In Denmark, the incidence of hip fractures is more than 10,000
cases annually, with over 200 patients having a morbidly obese Body Mass Index (BMI)
(BMI>40) [5, 48, 66]. Hip fractures often occur from low-impact trauma and are highly
associated with advanced physiological age and impaired cognitive and/or functional state
[6].
Hip fractures are traditionally classified according to the Evans classification modified by
Jensen and Michaelsen (Evans) [40], and the Arbeitsgemeinschaft für Osteosynthesefra-
gen/Orthopaedic Trauma Association (AO/OTA) classification [51]. The classifications are
commonly used as guides for surgical intervention and the choice of osteosynthesis. In prac-
tice, the preferred osteosynthesis is either Dynamic Hip Screw (DHS) or Intramedullary Hip
Nail (IMHN), but the implant of choice is not solely determined by the classification. Other
factors include government procurement, availability, and experience of the orthopedic sur-
geon.
The main challenge during the surgical procedure, in morbidly obese patients, is accessing
the entry point in trochanter major. The jig and the operational tools are not easily ap-
plicable to this patient group, due to excessive adipose tissue. This cause the need for a
very large incision, in order to insert the nail and screws, thus prolonging the surgical pro-
cedure. Furthermore, it is technically difficult for the orthopedic surgeons to properly align
the fracture sites, and place the screws correctly, as the excessive adipose tissue complicates
and limits access. The large incision and the prolonged operation time increase the risk of
post-operation deep wound infections and thereby worsen the prognosis. Overall, patients
with morbid obesity have longer hospital stays, high rates of reoperation and morbidity, and
lower quality of life. The 30-day mortality for this patient group is 91% higher than for
same-aged normal-weight patients [5, 21].

==PAGE:12==
1
MOTIVATION
There is a large potential for developing a new approach that eases the osteosynthesis, re-
duces the operational time, and improves the prognosis for patients with morbid obesity.
Furthermore, with the global population gradually increasing in weight [45], the demand for
an innovative solution becomes of greater interest. A new osteosynthesis of trochanteric hip
fractures, a subtype of hip fractures, has been patented by orthopedic doctors from Denmark.
They have discovered firsthand the need for a new solution targeted at patients with morbid
obesity. The new approach utilizes a retrograde surgical entry point through the intercondy-
lar notch in the knee, as opposed to the antegrade entry point in trochanter major located
in the proximal femur.
The objective of developing the new retrograde osteosynthesis is to assess its feasibility and
compare its strength with conventional osteosyntheses. Furthermore, the necessary measures
for a potential sale of the solution to a larger production company of orthopedic implants
are addressed.
1.1
Problem Statement
Osteosynthesis of trochanteric hip fractures is complex in morbidly obese patients (BMI>40),
as excessive adipose tissue complicates the surgical procedure. Utilizing the state-of-the-art
methods, DHS and IMHN, is found to be suboptimal in cases of high BMIs. This is due
to post-operative complications caused by infections resulting from extended operation time
and perioperative exposure.
How may an innovative retrograde hip nail with less perioperative exposure, patented by
orthopedic doctors from Danish hospitals, inserted utilizing a redesigned Stryker jig fur-
ther developed by engineers from Kapacitet (DK), improve the temporary stabilization of
trochanteric hip fractures and the prognosis for morbidly obese patients?

FOUNDATIONAL CONCEPTS OF HIP FRACTURES
2
Foundational Concepts of Hip Fractures
This chapter provides the foundational knowledge of relevant biological theory necessary for
understanding hip fractures and osteosynthesis. By exploring key concepts related to femur
anatomy and hip fracture classifications, orthopedic implants, and surgical procedure, the
framework to understand the need of a new retrograde intramedullary hip nail for trochanteric
hip fractures in morbidly obese patients is established.
2.1
Anatomy of The Femur
The femur is the largest and strongest bone in the human skeleton, situated in the thigh
between the hip and knee [33]. The femur is on average 42±3.1 cm long, varying by gender
and ethnicity, with men having longer femurs than women [30, 73]. The strength of femur
varies with geometry and bone mineral density among other factors. The force resilience is
proved to be strongest when applied in the anterior-to-posterior direction as opposed to the
lateromedial direction [47].

2
FOUNDATIONAL CONCEPTS OF HIP FRACTURES
The femur is divided into a proximal- and distal extremity. The proximal extremity, seen in
Figure 2.1, consists of the femoral head separated by a 4-5 cm long femoral neck and two
bone prominences: trochanter major and trochanter minor separated by the intertrochanteric
line [17, 33]. The femoral head interlocks with the cavity of the acetabulum in the pelvis
and creates the ball-and-socket hip joint, facilitating movement. A layer of cartilage eases
movement in the hip joint in the articulating surface, which on the femoral head is more con-
centrated towards the centre [33]. The angle between the femoral- shaft and neck is defined
as the inclination angle and is on average 128 degrees in adults. The inclination angle tends
to decrease with age, potentially impacting the surgical approach [17].
The trochanters act as binding sites for tendons and muscles, thus facilitating force trans-
mission and movement. Trochanter major acts as a fixation site for extensors and flexors,
among others, the gluteal muscles. More specifically, these muscles facilitate hip joint move-
ment and rotation [33]. Major hip flexor muscles fasten to the trochanter minor, allowing
hip- and trunk flexion while contributing to hip joint stabilization.
The midsection of the femur is referred to as the femoral shaft, a cylindrical, robust structure
with a slight curvature inherent to the femur. This curvature tends to change with age, but
its primary functions include assisting with optimizing stability and strength while prevent-
ing fractures [33].
Continuing further distally, the femur ends in two eminences called the condyles. The lateral-
and medial condyles are located where the femur meets tibia and patella and form a hinge
joint. The primary functions of the distal femur and the condyles are weight-bearing by dis-
tributing forces, joint articulation and acting as binding sites for muscles. The intercondylar
notch, located between the condyles on the patellar surface, provides a passageway for the
anterior and posterior cruciate ligaments, both of which contribute to the overall stabilization
of the knee joint [33].

2.2
Epidemiology and Classification of Hip Fractures
Hip fractures are common injuries in older adults, with the average age of the patients being
82 years old, and with women having a significantly higher prevalence by a factor of 2 to
3 [50, 66]. The higher prevalence in women is attributed to postmenopausal osteoporosis,
which leads to reduced bone mineral density, and increased fragility [66].
In a paper by Mattisson et al. (2018) [50], a big data study was conducted including 10,548
Swedish patients aged 18-110 years who had suffered a hip fracture between January 2014 and
December 2016. They found that 75% of the fractures happened in the patients’ residences,
with the cause being a low-impact fall from their standing height in 83% of the cases. A
smaller percentage of the causes included high-impact trauma in traffic accidents, commonly
seen in younger patients [50]. These fractures represent a substantial burden on the health-
care system, with hip fractures being the leading cause of bed occupancy among inpatients
in Danish hospitals, accounting for a total of 300,000 bed days annually [11]. Consequently,
patients with hip fractures constantly occupy 20-25% of the beds at orthopedic departments
[66].
Hip fractures are defined as fractures in the proximal femur and can occur in different parts
of the bone. An overview of hip fractures and their subdivisions can be seen in Appendix
10.1 for ease of understanding [24, 50]. Femoral- head and neck fractures are intracapsular
hip fractures, and they occur in the red and blue areas in Figure 2.2. Trochanteric fractures
(green area in Fig. 2.2) occur in the region below the intertrochanteric line, and above a
horizontal transverse line at the lower part of trochanter minor. Pertrochanteric hip fractures
travel along the intertrochanteric line (Fig. 2.1) (between the blue and green areas), whereas
intertrochanteric hip fractures occur along a horizontal transverse line in the green area
[51, 66]. Subtrochanteric hip fractures (yellow area in Fig. 2.2) involve all fractures below
trochanter minor and up to 5 cm distal from this point.

2.2.1
Risk Factors
Several factors contribute to the risk of sustaining a hip fracture, with age and the female
gender being the most significant risk factors. The increased risk for females is attributed to
postmenopausal osteoporosis, as mentioned in Section 2.2, along with the fact that women
live longer than men [7]. It is estimated that 1 in 3 women above the age of 50 years will suffer
any fracture due to osteoporosis during the remaining time of their life, and it is estimated
that 1 in 6 women will suffer a hip fracture [7, 29]. The risk of hip fractures increases by
a factor of 2 to 3 in women, with decreasing bone mineral density, measured at the femoral
neck [7]. Another factor that lowers the bone mineral density is tobacco smoking which more
than doubles the risk of hip fractures.

Comorbidities are found in a majority of the patients suffering a hip fracture. In a large
Danish study by Kannegaard et al. (2010) [41], 42,076 patients born before 1945, who suf-
fered a hip facture between 1999 and 2002 were included. 87% of the women had a previous
hospital-treated chronic comorbidity within the last 3 years compared to 65% of the men. A
minority of the hip fractures (0.5% in women, and 1.2% in men) were caused by bone metas-
tases [41]. On average, patients have 2.7 comorbidities [60]. Furthermore, having diabetes
mellitus increases the risk of hip fractures by a factor of more than 3 in women and more
than 6 in men [7].
Based on an American study (2018) [5], the majority of patients with a hip fracture span from
healthy- to overweight bodyweights with a BMI between 20-30. A definition of BMI and the
classifications can be found in Appendix 10.1.1. Approximately 20% are underweight, 13%
are obese, and 2% are morbidly obese. In general, female patients have a higher tendency to
be either underweight or morbidly obese. Furthermore, the number of comorbidities tends to
increase with increasing BMI [5]. Some studies suggest that a high BMI is associated with a
lowered hip fracture risk, also referred to as the obesity paradox [55]. However, other studies
argue that above average BMI do not possess a protective factor but rather a risk factor as
many comorbidities often is seen in this patient group [5, 6, 7].
2.2.2
Classification of Trochanteric Hip Fractures
Patients sustain different types of fractures depending on the force and impact of the trauma
they suffer. Trochanteric hip fractures are traditionally classified using the Evans classifica-
tion (updated in 1975) [40] and the AO/OTA classification (updated in 2018) [51]. The Evans
classification is used specifically for pertrochanteric hip fractures, while the AO/OTA classifi-
cation covers both pertrochanteric and intertrochanteric hip fractures. The Evans classifica-
tion in Figure 2.3 categorizes pertrochanteric fractures based on their stability, displacement,
and number of fragments. Stability refers to the ability to achieve a weight-bearing alignment
between the fracture surfaces, allowing effective load transfer. Displacement refers to the mis-
alignment of fracture surfaces. Type I and II are stable two-fragmentary fractures, where
type I is undisplaced and type II is displaced. Type III is an unstable three-fragmentary

2.3
State-of-the-Art in Trochanteric Hip Fracture Surgery
Osteosynthesis is the surgical procedure aimed towards stabilizing bone fragments through
fixation most often using surgical screws, plates or intramedullary nails to achieve stabiliza-
tion of a fracture [52]. In hip fractures, osteosynthesis is crucial for early weight-bearing and
is essential during the first 3-6 months post-surgery, when the bone fragments heal. Improper
alignment of the fracture surfaces can have a massive impact on bone remodelling and recov-
ery. Consequently, osteosynthesis’ primary goal is restoring the anatomical integrity, unifying
the bone fragments whilst facilitating bone healing with minimal complications. When suc-
cessful, osteosynthesis enables weight-bearing with effective load transfer in the consolidated
bone, which is vital for the regain of pre-injury functionality. In clinical practice, it is crucial
to perform the chosen surgical procedure within 24 hours of injury to ensure the best out-
come for the patient [24]. Depending on the fixation type, excessive adipose tissue can pose
difficulties, as the amount of adipose tissue surrounding the fracture site correlates with the
required surgical incision (Dr. Med., Jes B. Lauritzen, 2024).

Inter-observer variability among orthopedic surgeons regarding fracture classification and the
preferred surgery of choice has been documented [26]. However, in clinical practice, the choice
of osteosynthesis is primarily determined by the type of fracture, lateral wall integrity, the
country of origin where the hospital is located, and their procurement process for new hospital
equipment (Dr. Med., Jes B. Lauritzen, 2024). This inter-hospital variability in equipment
selection can lead to differences in surgical approaches, with no universal standard for treating
certain trochanteric fracture types [12]. However, despite this lack of standardization, trends
and recommendations are observed across healthcare facilities.
2.3.1
Dynamic Hip Screw
The DHS (Fig. 2.5a) is an orthopedic implant designed to stabilize trochanteric hip fractures
with intact lateral support, more specifically Evans type I-II (Fig. 2.3) or 31A1.2-31A1.3
fractures (Fig. 2.4). The DHS is in most cases the preferred orthopedic implant for osteosyn-
thesis of pertrochanteric hip fractures with intact lateral wall regardless of displacement [25,
62]. The construction of the DHS allows controlled collapse through sliding, making it opti-
mal for stabilizing simple fractures and promoting osteosynthesis through weight-bearing [18].
The DHS consists of a large main screw (neck screw), a side plate attached by cortical
screws, and a compression screw. The neck screw is secured in the proximal femur, passing
the intertrochanteric line, through the intact femoral neck and into the femoral head. A side
plate is attached to the femoral shaft using cortical locking screws [18]. Once the side plate is
fastened, a locking screw can be inserted to secure the neck screw to provide stability of the
fracture through compression while still allowing for a certain degree of movement. During
locomotion, this ultimately contributes to controlled compression and weight-bearing in the
fracture site that promotes healing through stimulation of bone remodelling by increased
mechanical load [18, 25, 61].

2.3.2
Intramedullary Hip Nail
For hip fractures that are multifragmentary and unstable without lateral- or medial support
(Evans III-V, Fig. 2.3 and 31A2-31A3, Fig. 2.4), literature suggests the use of intramedullary
implants that offer medial stability [25, 32]. The IMHN is a relatively newer orthopedic
implant, compared to the DHS [54], which offers earlier mobilization and improved weight-
bearing.
The construction of the IMHN and the fixation in the medullary canal provides a better
ability to distribute the weight along the femur, thus reducing the risk of implant failure
and reoperation [83]. An IMHN (Fig. 2.5b) provides a more rigid fixation compared to
DHS by utilizing an intramedullary nail of varying lengths, dependent on the fracture type.
Additionally, neck screw(s) are inserted proximally in the femoral head, and anti-rotational
screw(s) are inserted distally in the femoral shaft. The number of screws depends on the
displacement and stability of the fracture. This increased load-sharing can withstand higher
amounts of stress and weight-bearing, while inhibiting rotation of the nail and sliding within
the bone. The implant’s load-sharing capacity, as opposed to the load-bearing capacity of

The osteosynthesis requires high precision and expertise from a trained orthopedic surgeon.

surgeon identifies the fracture(s) and measures the inclination angle between the entry point
in the trochanter major, and the center of the femoral head. If IMHN is selected, the specific
IMHN is based on this angle and is usually 125° (Dr. Med., Asger M. Haugaard, 2024).

2.4.1
Prognosis
The peri- and postoperative prognosis is highly dependent on age, gender, BMI and comor-
bidities.
In general, 50% regain the same level of mobility they had pre-injury, whereas
patients living in nursing homes rarely regain the same level of function [27]. The mean sur-
gical time, the incidence of deep wound infections, and total length of stay increase linearly
with increasing BMI [5].

Patients with morbid obesity face significantly poorer prognoses following hip fractures, un-
derscoring the need for innovative approaches in osteosynthesis. This section explores one of
the site-specific standards at Bispebjerg Hospital, namely the antegrade IMHN, T2 Alpha™
Femur Antegrade GT Nailing System (AIMHN) by Stryker, and a novel patented osteosynthe-

the RIMHN, which ultimately aims to enhance patient prognoses and post-surgery quality
of life for this high-risk group.
3.1
T2 Alpha Femur Antegrade GT Nailing System
The AIMHN is a patented osteosynthesis produced by Stryker "intended for temporary stabi-
lization of bone segments or fragments until bone consolidation has been achieved" [67]. The
AIMHN is one of the site-specific standards for osteosynthesis of trochanteric hip fractures
at Bispebjerg Hospital. More specifically, the AIMHN is used for all Evans types and for all
AO/OTA types, with the exception of 31A1.1. It has an antegrade entry point through the
trochanter major and is fixated in the knee. It is applied to trochanteric hip fractures with
additional subtrochanteric or femoral shaft fractures.
The AIMHN shown in Figure 3.1 is constructed using the Ti-6Al-4V alloy in lengths spanning
from 240 mm to 480 mm. All nails are cannulated with a Ø5 mm hole used for insertion with
a k-wire. The nails are curved with a radius of curvature increasing with nail lengths and
span from 750 mm to 1350 mm. The curvature follows the natural curvature of the femur as
described in Section 2.1. The nail is available in different diameters from 9 mm to 15 mm.
Furthermore, the inclination angle between the neck screws and the nail is fixed at either
125°, 130° or 135° to accommodate different inclination angles [67].

3.3
The Innovation Process of Developing Hip Implants
Developing new implants is a demanding endeavour that requires expertise from multiple
fields, including medicine, engineering, and the industry among others. These work fields
must collaborate and ensure optimal knowledge-sharing to obtain a successful outcome of the
innovation process. Developing the RIMHN and the redesigned Stryker jig are demanding
tasks. However, assessing the feasibility of the RIMHN compared to the AIMHN poses an
arguably even greater challenge and responsibility given its potential market entry. A risk
assessment has been conducted, which has resulted in the identification of three potential
barriers to the project presented in a non-prioritized order:
• Functional failure or misalignment of the jig designed for RIMHN insertion.
• Difficulties in achieving a closed reduction of trochanteric hip fractures without the use
of a boot on a traction table.
• Significant differences in strength properties of the two implants (AIMHN and RIMHN).
The first challenge will be addressed by Kapacitet, and the second barrier, requiring knowl-
edge and expertise from orthopedic surgeons, will be managed by the inventors of the RIMHN.
The final challenge will be assessed and addressed using various approaches which will be
documented.

4.1
X-Ray Diffraction
XRD is a nondestructive technique applied to characterize crystalline and amorphous ma-
terials. Atoms in crystalline materials are arranged in periodic lattice planes in a regular
manner, whereas atoms are randomly oriented in amorphous materials. For crystalline ma-
terials, these unique repeating atomic lattice structures have been quantified for over one
million crystalline materials using an X-ray diffractometer [10].

During an XRD measurement, the X-ray source emits electromagnetic radiation in the range
from 0.01 nm to 10 nm, which is similar to the distance, d, between the lattice planes. The
short wavelength radiation collides with atoms in the lattice planes, where electrons absorb
and reemit the waves through elastic scattering. The scattered waves interact with waves
from neighbouring atoms in either constructive or destructive interference. When scattered
waves from neighboring atoms constructively interfere, the waves are diffracted and detected.
The detector measures the intensity of the diffracted wave against the diffraction angle, re-
sulting in diffraction peaks. Constructive interference, and thus diffraction, occurs only at
specific angles depending on the crystal lattice, and they occur according to Bragg’s law.

4.4
Simulating Object Deformation
Analyzing an object’s deformation under a particular load can be mathematically complex.
Existing standards for testing biomedical implants drive manufacturers to conduct exten-
sive in vitro tests and simulations to ensure reliability and safety. The FEM and FEA are
industry standard tools that, in unison, provide information on object behavior and are com-
monly applied to validate in vitro biomechanical tests. Examining how an object responds
to externally applied forces can reveal insights into the material performance and structural
resilience based on the material it is constructed of. Material properties and geometries of
the object influence the deformation under load and the complexity. In unison, they define
the analysis and the computational requirements of the simulation, impacting the FEM [1].
Material - Properties of the material, including yield strength, density, and Poisson’s ratio,
are essential in simulating object deformation. They are defined by the crystalline structure
of the object and are used as input properties in the simulation, utilized to generate a re-
sponse. Accurately determining the response is critical to reliably predict the performance
of the object [1].
31
==PAGE:42==
4
THEORETICAL CONCEPTS APPLIED IN HIP NAIL STUDIES
Geometry - The shape and structure of the object also significantly influence the complexity
of a simulation.
Length, cross-sectional area, moment of inertia, and curvatures, among
others, directly affect the load distributions across the object. For more complex geometries,
finer model resolution is required to accurately describe the object in 2D and 3D space. The
resolution is determined by the mesh applied to define the size of the elements that constitute
the object. Threaded screws and other small complex objects, require a high level of detail to
accurately describe the object. This requires finer meshing, which, on the contrary, involves
greater computational power to run advanced simulations [1].
4.4.1
Finite Element Method
Figure 4.7: Neck screw reconstructions indicating differences in mesh dynamic range with
5111, 10256, and 5178829 No. of elements respectively (left to right).
In FEM, information about the object geometry is utilized to divide the object into smaller,
more tangible, parts called elements through mathematical discretization. Discretization is
particularly useful since the equilibrium condition only needs to be satisfied over a finite
number of discrete elements, as opposed to continuously over the entire body of the object
[22]. These elements are interconnected at strategically placed nodes, which act as reference
points for calculations. The material properties assigned to individual elements constitute the
object’s characteristics and ultimately determine the model’s behavior in smaller segments.
Together, the elements form the object mesh - a mathematical description of an object’s
surface, which can be used for simulation. An example of this is depicted in Figure 4.7, where
a screw is reconstructed using meshing at different resolutions. Furthermore, the equilibrium
condition is crucial for modeling and maintaining balance under load.
The condition of
material equilibrium is the assumption of the material’s state when internal stresses within
32
==PAGE:43==
4
THEORETICAL CONCEPTS APPLIED IN HIP NAIL STUDIES
the material counteract any external forces, resulting in a stable configuration [76]. This
principle allows for calculating stress and strain distributions by ensuring that each part of
an object meets the balance of forces applied to the material.
Figure 4.8: A neck screw with first order triangular meshing.
The mesh type can be either 2D (triangular or quadrilateral) or 3D (pyramid or cubic), as
depicted in Figure 4.8, where a screw is meshed using first-order triangular surface elements.
In addition, resolution can be increased by adding nodes in between first-order nodes at the
edges only. These are second-order elements, which introduce more complexity and thus
computational requirements. However, they add flexibility to the model and increase the
accuracy. Determining the mesh type and modeling approach can have a large impact on
the simulation’s results.
Ultimately, the simulation accuracy is a balance between mesh
resolution and computational requirements [77].

4.4.3

Depicting and evaluating the magnitude and position of stress can be performed using the
maximum distortion energy theory, also known as the von Mises Yield Criterion (1913) [28,
76, 77].
The criterion states that yielding in an object occurs when the maximum distortion energy
in a material, denoted ud, is equal to the distortion energy at yielding, denoted ud,y in a
uniaxial tensile test [28]. In simple terms an object breaks when a maximum stress applied
results in yielding, causing a transition from elastic to plastic deformation. The criterion is
an often utilized failure theory [53, 76], used to predict when and where a material will fail
under applied load. In ductile materials such as metals, failure occurs at the transition from
elastic to plastic deformation, where there is an onset of loss in the load-carrying capabilities

Simulations of elastic deformation were conducted in SolidWorks® on the three FE models
presented in Section 5.2.2. These simulations were compared with the biomechanical com-

The study type utilized for the FEAs was a ’static study’ using a linear model to calculate
displacement and von Mises stresses among other parameters [69]. The static study thus
simulates deformation in the elastic area only and cannot predict deformation after the

the FE models in steps of 500 N until the compressive yield strength (970 MPa) was reached.
The von Mises stress at the surface element with the highest value was noted for each step

5.3.1
Designing and Manufacturing Fixtures
A half sphere fixture with Ø80 mm shown in Figure 5.7a was created using Creo. The half
sphere design was chosen to ensure optimal contact with the load cell surface having a concave
shape. This ensured a consistent applied load throughout the test while accommodating
movement of the half sphere during the test. The half sphere has two Ø6.6 mm holes with a
depth of 30 mm each to hold the neck screws. The holes are separated by a distance of 10.5
mm, identical to the distance between the neck screws. Due to non-standard threading on the
neck screws, the holes were designed to be smooth without threading. The half sphere was

the design iteration, two planar surfaces were added for manufacturing purposes. Two half
sphere fixtures were manufactured in Stainless Steel 316 at DTU Chemistry’s Workshop,
each weighing 1037 g. The given material was chosen due to its strength properties and the

5.3.3
Load-Until-Failure Test
The ultimate load-carrying capabilities of the AIMHN and RIMHN with two neck screws
were tested in a load-until-failure test. Failure was defined as an abrupt drop in load and dis-
placement or visible rupture of the test specimen. The procedure followed the test protocol
found in Appendix 10.3.1 and was thus executed after the compression tests. The applied
load was 10 N/s with a data sampling of 10 Hz. Before initiating the load-until-failure tests,
the specimens were adjusted to have the same starting positions as for the compression tests.
The data from the load-until-failure tests was analyzed in Python, using a linear regression
fit analysis. A list of R2 values was calculated using linear regression and used to define the
transition point from elastic to plastic deformation through a local maxima analysis. The
data was filtered to remove noise. The list of R2 values was obtained using a for-loop that
iterated over a window spanning from the first data point to the sum of the data points. For
each iteration in the for-loop, 10 data points were removed, and the R2 values were recalcu-
lated and appended to the list. The for-loop ended before reaching an inadequate amount of
data points for calculating linear regression.
The R2 list was examined and investigated for the first local maxima, indicating the most-
linear tendency and, thus the point in time and displacement where plastic deformation
initiated.

5.4.2
New Closed Reduction Approaches
The AIMHN procedure involves closed reduction using a boot on a traction table, with the
leg fully extended. This procedure is not applicable with the insertion of the RIMHN, as
described in Section 3.
Inserting the RIMHN requires that the leg is flexed in a 30° to
45° angle, and therefore new approaches have been tested under the hypothesis that closed
reduction of trochanteric fractures could be achieved without using a boot on a traction table.
On December 6th 2024, a female patient from Holbæk Sygehus with a trochanteric hip
fracture consented to have her fracture initially reduced testing a new approach under full
anesthesia. Afterward, the procedure would return to the standard method, followed by the
insertion of the conventional AIMHN.
In the first approach, leg rollers were placed above the dorsum of the foot, and in the poplit,
flexing the leg in a 30° to 45° angle. It was possible to obtain closed reduction using this
approach, however, the pressures caused by the leg rollers were found to be too high. After
10 minutes in the position, large pressure marks could be seen on the shin. Another approach
was therefore tested on the patient, utilizing a Walker Boot between the leg and the leg rollers.
This approach was inefficient, as the upper part of the boot caused unwanted pressure on
the calf. Other approaches have been discussed but not tested nor documented.

DISCUSSION
7
Discussion
Developing a new hip implant for trochanteric hip fractures in morbidly obese patients is
an innovation process, as outlined by the project plan in Appendix 10.5, which included
the relevant and necessary project goals. A risk assessment identifying possible barriers to
the project was conducted, of which the potential strength differences between AIMHN and
RIMHN were the main focus of this thesis. The innovation process to investigate these dif-
ferences included consultation with experts, prototyping, and testing.
The discussion will focus on comparing the IMHNs compressive yield points in simulations
and biomechanical tests, and the operational feasibility of the RIMHN prototype.
After
discussing and assessing differences between the two IMHNs, the potential reduction in the
total surgery-related expenditure using RIMHN is discussed. This is followed by further work
including additional tests and regulatory requirements considerations.
7.1
Finite Element Analysis Fails to Predict Yield Point
As stated in Table 6.4, simulations indicated yield points of 3738 N, 2918 N, and 3864
N for AIMHN, RIMHN, and the 90 mm neck screw, respectively.
Comparing this with
the compression test results of 4000 N, 5000 N, and 3100 N, for the same test specimens,
respectively, indicate a poor predictability of the FEA. Expanding the comparison to the
displacement at yield point, determined using FEA and the compression test, once more, poor
reliability can be concluded from Table 6.4. No linearity was found analyzing the differences
in load and displacement at the yield point found between the two approaches. For AIMHN,
the load at yield point was 7.5% larger in the compression test compared to the FEA, and
the displacement was 4 times larger at the compression test. For RIMHN, the load at the
yield point was 1.7 times larger in the compression test, while the displacement was 9.7 times
larger. Finally, the FEA of the neck screw indicated a load at yield point 20% larger than the
actual load found in the compression test. Conversely, the displacement showed an increase
in the compression test by a factor of 2.3.
71
==PAGE:82==
7
DISCUSSION
Additionally, the exact location of fracture was correctly predicted for the RIMHN, fractur-
ing in the upper neck screw (Fig. 6.7a and 6.12c), while it was incorrectly predicted for
the AIMHN (Fig. 6.1a and 6.6c). The FEA thus fails to reliably predict the compressional
behavior of the test specimens under applied load. However, the load prediction at yield
point was more accurate than the prediction of displacement. Other factors and uncertainty
contributions could be in play to explain the large deviations.
The most severe contribution is found to be the CAD models and their conversions into
FE models. The von Mises stress was observed to be concentrated along sharp edges and
at assemblies. This potentially explains the point of fracture predicted in the AIMHN FE
model, deviating from the compression test. Furthermore, the FEA is primarily affected by
internal and external parameters. Internal parameters worth highlighting include material
properties and study type, whereas external parameters include boundary load, meshing, and
fixed constraints. The applied fixed constraints were, in the case of the neck screw, observed
to generate high concentrations of stress, directly deviating from the stress concentration
generated in a three-point bending test.
It is also worth highlighting that the protruding portion of the neck screws differed between
the FEA studies and the biomechanical tests. In both FE models of AIMHN and RIMHN,
the upper- and lower neck screw protruded 19 mm and 22 mm, respectively. The protruding
portion of the neck screws in the biomechanical tests are summarized in Table 6.5. Addition-
ally, the free lengths of the AIMHN and RIMHN, not covered by box fixtures, varied from 80
mm to 113.4 mm, in the FEA and biomechanical tests, respectively. These differences have
arguably minimized the comparability between the two studies and are deemed a significant
uncertainty factor.

7.2
AIMHN and RIMHN Have Comparable Strengths
The test setup for compression- and load-until-failure tests were designed and constructed
to best resemble the industry standards presented by Stryker and to contain elements from
relevant ISO standards. However, the vertical distance from the top of the box fixture to
the center of the half sphere deviates from ISO 7206-4 by 18 mm, but complies with the 10°
angle. One test specimen was used in each test, with the AIMHN and RIMHN originating
from one product as described in Section 5.1.2. In spite of this, variations in geometries
and rutile coatings were present due to the modification. Multiple contributions, possibly
explaining the differences in yield points, have been identified and evaluated on their degree
of severity. The severity is defined as the most impactful factor contributing to uncertainty,
directly affecting the results and, thereby, the reliability of the methods applied.
In the compression test, identical fixtures, load speeds, and neck screw lengths were used
to test the AIMHN and RIMHN. The test setups vary from industry standards utilized by
Stryker and test setups presented by Luo et al. [49] and Weiser et al. [80] on several levels,
which was a conscious choice. The scope of the compression tests was to obtain high dynamic
resolution in the data range. This was achieved using a high sampling frequency and a low
applied loading rate of 10 N/s. On the contrary, utilizing the same loading rate of 4.6 mm/s
from Luo et al. and Weiser et al. would arguably have resulted in an exceedance of the yield
points within a data range of 2-3 seconds. In comparison, the applied loading rate specified
in the test protocol extended the elastic area to a duration of 400-500 seconds. Research
further shows a direct correlation between loading rate and yield point, with higher speeds of
applied load resulting in higher strengths [81]. This argues, that higher strengths could have
been achieved in Section 6 Results, if higher loading speeds were applied. Thus, possibly
better resembling the results from Lou et al. and Weiser et al.
The results presented in Table 6.4 reject the hypothesis that the RIMHN would have a lower
compressive yield strength compared to the AIMHN. Several factors may have impacted
this difference, with the most likely factor being the sliding of the neck screws presented in
Table 6.5. Sliding was ascertained in both test specimens, with the most pronounced sliding
73
==PAGE:84==
7
DISCUSSION
present in RIMHN. The sliding directly affects the lever arm and, thereby, the force required
to deform the neck screws. This could explain the 1000 N load difference required to fracture
the two IMHNs, although they both had displaced 24 mm at the time of fracture (Fig. 6.4b,
6.10b).
Furthermore, the region of plastic deformation is expected to follow an inverse exponential
tendency as seen in Figure 6.17b and Figure 4.3, but instead, an increasing linear tendency
is seen for both AIMHN (Fig. 6.5b) and RIMHN (Fig. 6.11b). This indicate that sliding has
occurred in during plastic deformation in both test specimens with most pronounced sliding
in RIMHN. A potential explanation could be the differences in geometry and rutile coating
in the neck screw holes, albeit other uncertainties most certainly have contributed. Sliding
introduced a significant uncertainty factor, making it challenging to quantify the effects.
Combining the sliding with only two test specimens investigated, it cannot definitively be
stated that AIMHN is superior to RIMHN.
However, it can be argued that they have
comparable strengths, as sliding was seen in both test.
7.3
The Redesigned Jig Facilitates Correct RIMHN Insertion
The osteosynthesis of a plastic bone using the RIMHN with two neck screws was achieved
by using the redesigned jig developed by Kapacitet as described in Section 5.4.1. Although
this validates the design of the jig, ensuring neck screw insertion at the 125° angle, osteosyn-
thesis of a morbidly obese person is required to fully deem the operational procedure feasible.
It is expected that the retrograde entry point will reduce the peri- and post-surgery com-
plications, by limiting the incision length from up to 40 cm to 5 cm, and exposed tissue
area. Furthermore, the operative time is expected to be significantly reduced from up to
4 hours to 1 hour, using the retrograde entry point. Surgeries utilizing a retrograde entry
point are documented to be associated with low risks and reduced operative time. Research
by Tucker et al. [75] compares osteosynthesis of femoral shaft fractures, using antegrade
and retrograde entry points, in obese and non-obese. They conclude that operative time,
as well as other beneficial factors such as radiation exposure, is significantly reduced in the
74
==PAGE:85==
7
DISCUSSION
retrograde approach. To the best of our knowledge, no research or documentation comparing
the two entry points in the osteosynthesis of trochanteric hip fractures exists. Nonetheless,
it is expected that the retrograde entry point will make the surgery of the morbidly obese
patients less technically demanding and significantly reduce the operational time of 4 hours.
During the plast bone experiment, it was decided to reduce the upper length of the RIMHN
by 15 mm in order for it to not protrude the femoral cortex. It is estimated that this will
not have an effect on the compressive yield strength of the RIMHN, given the results of
the biomechanical test showing fracture in the neck screws, and given the common areas of
failure shown in Figure 2.8.
The osteosynthesis with RIMHN is only possible if a closed reduction of the fracture can be
achieved. The new approaches tested and documented in Section 5.4 proved inefficient in the
reduction of a trochanteric hip fracture in vivo. It is evident that further work on optimizing
a new closed reduction approach is crucial. This might be a substantial undertaking, but the
orthopedic doctors are confident in their ability to develop this new closed reduction method.
7.4
RIMHN Reduces The Total Surgery Related Expenditure
Developing the RIMHN with intended use for the majority of Evans and AO/OTA hip
fractures in morbidly obese raises the question of whether or not the population of the target
group is large enough. In Denmark, approximately 10,000 patients undergo surgery following
a hip fracture injury [48, 66]. Among these, it is estimated that 200 patients are classified
as morbidly obese, according to the American study [5]. While this seems to be a relatively
low proportion, it is the same patient group who have the worst prognoses, the longest
hospital stays, and high risks of readmission, reoperation, and treatment of surgery-related
comorbidities [5, 46]. The total annual costs related to hip fractures in Denmark are estimated
to be DKK 1,500,000,000 constituting 1% of the total annual societal healthcare expenditure
[65]. Complicated osteosynthesis of morbidly obese has an associated expenditure totaling
DKK 500,000 per patient, including multiple readmissions and reoperations.
75
==PAGE:86==
7
DISCUSSION
Compared to normal-weight BMIs, the total surgery-related expenditures drop to DKK
50,000 for uncomplicated osteosynthesis (Dr.Med., Jes B. Laurisen, 2025).
The price for the conventional AIMHN by Stryker is approximately DKK 17,500, with neck
screws in the price range of DKK 1,200 to DKK 2,000 per neck screw (Dr. Med., Jes B. Lau-
ritzen, 2024) [35]. The new RIMHN will have similar costs given the same material choice
and object geometry. However, costs related to the working hours of the healthcare personnel
present during the surgery, including orthopedic surgeons, perioperative nurses, anesthetic
nurses, and porters, are expected to be significantly minimized due to reduced operative time.
In addition, the world’s population is increasing in weight [45], making the RIMHN a par-
ticularly suitable solution to meet the needs of this growing patient group. Furthermore,
by extending the intended use of RIMHN to include obese patients, an estimated additional
1,300 patients will be targeted according to data from the American study [5].
7.5
Further Work
To prepare a potential release of the RIMHN to the market, further biomechanical tests are
necessary combined with further technical development of the test setups to fully comply
with industry- and ISO standards. Specifically, the RIMHN should be tested in a human fe-
mur with induced trochanteric hip fractures following the Evans- or AO/OTA classifications,
or Hvidovre Algoritmen. This ensures better resemblance with in vivo conditions while com-
pletely eliminating sliding of neck screws by maintaining a constant lever arm. Furthermore,
the compression test and load-until-failure test should be repeated using more test specimens.
Additional tests include a fatigue test, where ISO 7206-4 specifies a minimum of 5 million
load cycles of 2300 N, testing at a minimum of six specimens. This corresponds to a five-year
lifetime of the implant in a 75 kg person with normal gait patterns. This estimate has been
criticized in studies for being too generalized without taking the body weight of the person
into account [19]. It can, therefore, be argued that a lower number of cycles would be suffi-
cient with a higher load of 3200 N. This corresponds to the load of a 110 kg person during
76
==PAGE:87==

gait. This is further supported by the fact that obese on average, take fewer steps compared
to normal weight, and given bone remodeling is achieved within 3-6 months, assuming no
complications [79]. Once the in vitro studies are concluded, the development process should
proceed to clinical pilot studies in patients.
Further work should also focus on optimizing the FE model by improving the CAD models,
testing different surface elements, and dynamic resolutions as a measure of total number of
nodes. Additionally, the FEA should be refined by expanding the model to include deforma-
tion in the plastic area. The FEA should be validated using mesh convergence, describing
the reliability of the method by providing insights into how accurately the mesh describes
the object geometry. Once the reliability is improved, the FE model, in unison with the FEA
can also be utilized to find optimal combinations of RIMHN lengths and diameters, and neck
screw lengths. This could be achieved using topology optimization, being an obvious method
to maximize strength by calculating optimal combinations.

and its operational feasibility were presented. The objective of the meeting was to provide
insights into the project’s progress and potentially initiate a close industrial collaboration
between Stryker and Bispebjerg Hospital.
Bispebjerg Hospital suggested a clinical pilot
study conducted on-site, including 6 patients osteosynthesized using RIMHNs produced by

collaboration. It is Bispebjerg Hospital’s ambition to establish this collaboration in 2025.

CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
The first steps have been taken toward advancing osteosynthesis of trochanteric hip fractures
in morbidly obese patients. Several standards for classification of trochanteric hip fractures
and recommended osteosynthesis exist. The site-specific standard at Bispebjerg Hospital, the
AIMHN, is a technically demanding operative procedure complicated by excessive adipose
tissue. A very large incision is required, prolonging the operative time and perioperative
exposure, thereby increasing the risk of deep wound infections and 30-day mortality rates.
FEA and biomechanical tests were applied to investigate differences in strength between
AIMHN and RIMHN. FEA provided a relatively accurate prediction of the load at yield
point. In contrast, the prediction of displacement and the exact location of fracture was in-
correctly predicted. The compression test successfully operated in the elastic area for all test
specimens. The load-until-failure test for AIMHN and RIMHN resulted in comparable yield
strengths, albeit the tests were heavily influenced by sliding of the neck screws. Therefore,
concluding one of the specimens’ strengths to be superior is impossible, using the recorded
data and the applied methods. The redesigned Stryker jig, further developed by Kapacitet,
succeeded as a proof-of-concept by allowing neck screw insertion at correct angles in a plastic
femoral bone. New closed reduction approaches, applied before RIMHN insertion, require
rethinking and additional testing by orthopedic surgeons.
These findings suggest that RIMHN offers surgical advantages that could significantly benefit
the osteosynthesis of trochanteric hip fractures in morbidly obese patients while reducing the
total surgery-related expenditure. The smaller incision required for retrograde insertion, in
combination with reduced operative time, will decrease the risk of deep wound infections, and
the overall prognosis for morbidly obese. There appear to be no contraindications against
expanding osteosynthesis with RIMHN to include patients of all BMI categories, assuming
the retrograde entry point can be accessed.

10.3.3
Procedure for Load-Until-Failure
For each of the two test specimens, after the completion of the compression protocol seen in
Table 10.2, the load until failure protocol is applied in a sequential manner. After ensuring
proper fixtation of the specimen, loads of 4500 N are to be applied over a speed of 0.5 ± 0.1
kN/s or 0.04 ± 0.01 mm/s or lowest possible velocity. This applied load is determined using
references from prior studies and simulations from Finite Element Analysis and is expected
to be operating within the plastic deformation region of the material.
Warning: At any time during the protocol’s execution, the experiment should be able to
be terminated if anything out of the ordinary occurs, or if deformation does not match the
expected results. Any deviations or notable occurrences should be written in the comment
section. Furthermore, though unlikely according to calculations, if any of the test specimens
fail to fracture after the application of 4500 N, the load is increased to 6000 N without
resetting to zero in between.

10.5
Reflections on Project Plan
The project plan presented in Figure 10.7 was created in early September 2024 and finalized
on October 2nd 2024.
Figure 10.7: Project plan created in early September 2024.
The objective of the thesis changed towards simulations and biomechanical tests, thereby
adding new project goals to the plan. Furthermore, unforeseen events and delays occured
during the process which had to be managed. As an example, COMSOL® was initially used
to simulate deformation of the test specimens. However, DTU’s licence terminated in mid
november 2024, before the simulations were finalized. Measures were taken to mitigate delays
in simulations and SolidWorks was utilized instead. Additionally, initiating and managing
contact with several stakeholders (Bispebjerg Hospital, Kapacitet, Elos Medtech, DTU Risø,
DTU Chemistry Workshop, Stryker etc.) became an influential part of the project. Overall,
the project plan was followed in a satisfactory manner, and was continuously advised during
the process to stay on track. The cadaver study (project goal 10) was initially set to a date in
late January 2025, but it was discarded after the plastic bone study. This decision was made
by the orthopedic doctors based on ethical purposes, and given that it could not contribute
with new scientific knowledge relevant to the scope of the test.

Litterature Review
A comprehensive and thorough review of the literature has been conducted from September
2nd to October 8th, 2024, and has formed the basis of Section 2. At the time, a total of 65
records had been assessed according to the search strategy, and 41 records were included in
the section based on the following keywords:
• "hip fractures"
• "obesity"
• "BMI"
• "trochanteric hip fractures"
• "hip fracture classification"
• "hip fracture mortality"
• "hip fracture risk factors"
• "hip fracture prognosis"
