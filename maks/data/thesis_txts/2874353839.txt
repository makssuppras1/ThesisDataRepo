Abstract
This thesis developed and verified a semi-analytical computational framework for
modelling linear elastic pavements with an edges and a stress-free side slopes. An
open-source C library named taamak.h was first implemented to solve the homoge-
neous, isotropic, weightless, linear elastic half-space with an edge. The underlying
formulation of the implementation was based on the Method of Fundamental So-
lutions (MFS). The code was then extended to address multi-layered domain by
employing Equivalent Layer Thickness (ELT) theory. Verification against reference
implementations showed close agreement: the homogeneous solver reproduced Leven-
berg’s solutions [1], and the multi-layer solver matched reference results. taamak.h
was thereafter applied to a case study on a three-layered system for producing
synthetic FWD deflections near an edge. The influence of the side-slope angle on
the results was evaluated for different slope angles and for two orientations of the
FWD sensor array: transversely to the edge and parallel to it. Moreover, a new
entity was introduced: Effective Slope Angle (ESA), characterising the available
or remaining “edge support” for a given pavement near discontinuities. The ESA
can be back-calculated from FWD deflections with taamak.h. Finally, numerical
experiments confirmed surface deflection convergence behaviour, providing rationales
for collocation parameter selections in taamak.h.
Keywords: Pavement modelling, Method of fundamental solutions, Equivalent layer
thickness, Effective slope angle, Falling weight deflectometer.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Pavement infrastructure is important for economic growth and societal equality in
every country [2, 3]. The vast majority of this infrastructure is comprised of asphalt
pavements [4, 5]. This pavement type is made of (top to bottom): one or several
layers of asphalt concrete, one or several compacted unbound aggregate layers, and
soil (or rock) medium extending to a large depth. Accordingly, for design and analysis
purposes, pavement infrastructure is mechanistically modelled as a stratified (or
laminated) medium, where each model layer corresponds to a different layer within
the pavement system.
In the modelling of asphalt pavements, it is commonly assumed that layers
extend to infinity in the two horizontal directions [1]. This assumption enables the
application of semi-analytical formulations for calculating responses (i.e., stresses,
strains, and displacements). Compared to purely numerical schemes, semi-analytical
formulations offer superior accuracy, lower computational cost, and greater flexibility
to deal with a wide range of input parameter values.
The modelling assumption of infinite horizontal layer dimensions is valid as long
as traffic loads are applied “far enough” from the edges, e.g., in the case of highways
that are built with wide shoulders. In other words, when tyre loads are applied “too
close” to a pavement edge, current models do not apply. In many real-life situations,
e.g., urban road networks, pavements are relatively narrow and vehicles end up
driving close to the edges. This leads to damages that cannot be analysed with
the common models. Pictures of edge-related loading scenario and the subsequent
damages are included in Figure 1.1.
The current work is motivated by the need for a computational tool, based on a
semi-analytical formulation, that can deal with edge loading conditions in asphalt
pavements.

Chapter 2
Review of Literature
The focus of this literature review is on three aspects: the development of semi-
analytical pavement modelling formulations, the efforts of edge-related damage
analyses and modellings, and the current application and limitation regarding Falling
Weight Deflectometer (FWD) deflection testing and analysis.
2.1
Semi-analytical pavement modelling
A semi-analytical method is defined as a computational approach that combines
analytical expansions with numerical techniques to solve complex problems. Purely
analytical approaches usually require no discretisation, yet such solutions are gener-
ally available only for problems with elementary geometry. Numerical approaches
resort to the discretisation of fields, geometry, and field equations in the domains or
the boundary conditions [6]. Conversely, semi-analytical approaches normally require
only a discretisation of the boundaries, balancing numerical accuracy and computa-
tional cost, thus is desired in wide range of modelling applications. Semi-analytic
method in existing literature encompass a diverse spectrum of phenomena and im-
plementation strategies. In the context of differential equations, semi-analytical
techniques are methodologies capable of resolving linear and non-linear differential
equations without discretisation, linearisation, or specialised transformations, of-
fering approximate solutions through infinite series or polynomial representations
[7]. Within galaxy formulation, the primary benefit of the semi-analytic approach
lies in its computational efficiency compared to purely numerical simulations. This
efficiency enables the generation of significantly larger model, while maintaining low
computational cost [8].
Semi-analytical method is also commonly used within pavement modelling efforts.
The aim of which is to quantify responses, i.e., displacements, stresses, and strains,
due to traffic loads. In early modelling attempts, pavements were considered as a
half-space that is linear, elastic, homogeneous, and weightless. The solutions of stress
state in such half-space, subjected to a stationary concentrated force normal to the
upper boundary was presented in the work of Boussinesq [9, 10]. Similar problem
with concentrated force acting parallel to the traction-free surface of a semi-infinite
solid was solved by Cerruti [11]. These solutions form fundamental tools for pavement
modelling, and later extended by Love [12] to half-space with vertical load distributed
in a circular area on the top surface, which is better-suited for the representation
of tyre footprints. These solutions remain the basis for mechanistic formulation of
asphalt pavement systems.
Layered semi-analytical models were developed for the study of more realistic
and complex pavements. Burmister’s two-layered [13, 14, 15] and three-layered

formulations [16] allow stratified asphalt, base and subgrade layers, which serve as
core of modern multi-layered elastic pavement modelling. The general solutions of
stress and displacement field, though being given explicitly in closed-form, required
complex algebraic and numerical evaluations and thus faced constraints in engineering
applications due to the limitation of computational powers in the 1940s. Odemark’s
Equivalent Layer Thickness (ELT) theory [17], bridging the gap with engineering
approximation, posted a reliable way to represent multi-layer systems and evaluate
vertical surface displacements. Nevertheless, Odemark’s original formulation did not
account for variations in Poisson’s ratios, as his initial work assumed uniform ratios
across all layers. The methodology was subsequently refined by Pronk [18], whose
technical report presented the modern ELT formulation that accommodates different
Poisson’s ratios across pavement layers, addressing the limitations of the original
approach.
Beyond Burmister, later works extended multi-layer theories and accounted for
various engineering scenarios. Maina et al. studied pavement structures under
non-circular loading by replacing the cylindrical coordinate system with Cartesian
coordinate system and subsequently solved the governing equilibrium equations,
and the solutions were extended to multi-layered system. Worked examples were
demonstrated to represent and simulated unconventional load shapes using the
geometric combination of square and circular load patches [19]. In the work of Chen
et al. [20], analytical formulations or an elastic multi-layered system subjected to an
asymmetrical (elliptical) vertical load were derived, enabling more flexible tyre contact
simulation. Based on the principle of superposition, Levenberg et al. [21] combined
circular loads to represent farming vehicle footprints, thereby modelling multi-layered
pavement responses under sophisticated loading geometries. The integration of
linear viscoelasticity into Burmister’s formulation was subsequently established by
Levenberg [22], where a spreadsheet application was developed, capable of complex
pavement analysis. This tool allows vertical loading on linear viscoelastic pavement
systems comprising up to five fully bonded, isotropic, homogeneous, and weightless
layers. The application simulates uniformly distributed circular loads moving at
constant speed along a straight line, with the capability to compute stress, strain,
and displacement within the pavement system.
2.2
Edge-related damages and modelling
Edge cracking is a common pavement distress observed where the asphalt layer meets
an adjacent shoulder or slope, typically within 0.3 to 0.5 m of the edge, characterised
by several survey works [23, 24]. Pavement management surveys report a higher
incidence of transverse or longitudinal cracking along edges of low volume roads or
narrow shoulders, such mechanics have been reportedly confirmed by instrumented
tests [25, 26]. The trend of higher tensile strain and deflection under near-edge loads
is consistent with the elevated cracking and failure observed next to unsupported
edges.
Early efforts of mechanistic pavement modelling under edge-loading include
coupled analytical-numerical solutions and finite element studies. Hanson & Keer
[27] formulated elastic quarter-space in terms of open-form integral expressions from
two coupled half-spaces solved by Love [12], with results derived in cooperated
with numerical methods. Similarly, Guo et al. [28, 29] presented explicit matrix
solutions for elastic 3D wedge with one or two free edges under arbitrary surface
loads. Such solutions confirm that a free edge amplifies stresses near the corner
relative to an infinite half-space. Meanwhile, Finite Element Method (FEM) have

been used to simulate edge effects in several studies. Aksnes [30] instrumented a
full-scale pavement with a sloping edge and used 3D ABAQUS models of circular
load and linear elastic layers to predict responses. The study shown that the FEM
model, assuming homogeneous and elastic layers confirmed the correlation of changes
in stress or deflection and the load approaching the edge, which schematically match
field measurements, but insignificant numerically due to the inadequate assumption
of linear elasticity for granular layers. In the work of Korkiala-Tanttu and Dawson
[31], a low-volume pavement with different shoulder slopes were built and modelled
in Plaxis 2D, the comparison of which show numerical model’s underestimation
of the measured edge effects. In other studies [26, 32], failure of sloped shoulders
under very heavy trucks was evaluated using wedge stability calculations or 3D FEM.
While these works accounted for gravity and sliding failure, they often applied ad-hoc
“correction factors” or used overly stiff material assumptions.
Very recently, Levenberg [1] introduced a semi-analytical approach specifically
for a “trimmed” layered pavement, defined as an asphalt-base structure on an
embankment with an exposed side slope. The formulation extends the conventional
layered-elastic solution by allowing a finite slope on one side. This method explicitly
enforces the free-edge boundary on the slope side and computes the full stress and
displacement field under a rectangular load near the edge. By contrast with previous
FEM attempts, Levenberg’s model predicts the significant increase of stress and
deflection as the load moves toward the edge, providing a new tool for mechanistic
analysis of edge cracking and shoulder settlements, enabling future incorporation of
edge effects into design procedures.
2.3
FWD deflection testing and analysis
The assessment of asphalt pavements’ mechanical condition is often based on the
interpretation of deflections obtained from Falling Weight Deflectometer (FWD) tests
[33, 34]. Originating in the early 1970s, the FWD is a device designed to simulate
the loading of a wheel on pavement and measure its vertical deflection response. It
uses a known weight dropped onto a circular load plate, with sensors around the
plate to measure the resulting deflection, serving as a standard method for assessing
pavement structural capacity and subsurface properties [35].
For evaluating pavement conditions at the project level, mechanical interpretation
typically involves matching observed deflection measurements with corresponding
model deflections [36, 37, 38]. The most common modelling approach for this purpose
is Layered Elastic Theory (LET), which treats pavements as stratified half-space
systems comprising linear elastic, isotropic, homogeneous, and weightless layers. The
process of aligning measured deflections with model predictions involves assumed
Poisson ratios, layer geometries, and interface bonding characteristics, ultimately
deriving the elastic moduli of individual layers.
LET, or generally, semi-analytical frameworks, emerge as the preferred formulation
for inverse modelling due to its computational efficiency, numerical robustness, and
ability to characterise asphalt pavement load responses using minimal parametric
inputs [39]. Given that the model layers in LET extend to infinity in the lateral
directions, standard FWD testing protocols typically recommend avoiding load
applications near pavement edges [21]. Conversely, if tests are carried out near edges,
it is accepted that their interpretation with LET will yield biased/incorrect results.
Recent study by Larsson et al. [40] revealed that for narrow pavement structures
(e.g., cycle path), FWD measurements along the centre line is generally not feasible,
making edge measurements especially problematic. The proposed alternative of using

Light Weight Deflectometer (LWD) measurements near edges shows promise, but
requires further validation to determine accuracy and repeatability. Based on the
FWD/LWD tests conducted near the edges of the narrow cycle path, back-calculation
of modulus values utilising traditional method confirmed poor fit with stated realistic
values, and have thus not been further analysed.
These findings underscore the critical limitations of current FWD back-calculation
techniques when addressing deflection test results near pavement edges. The pavement
engineering community urgently requires a back-calculation algorithm capable of
accounting for edge and crack effects, thereby enabling more precise structural
assessments.

Chapter 3
Objective and Methodology
This Master’s thesis project set out to achieve three interrelated objectives: (i)
develop an open-source C library to efficiently introduce and solve the formulation
for a homogeneous, isotropic, weightless, linear elastic half-space with an edge and a
stress-free side slope; (ii) extend the formulation and code to solve for deflections in a
multi-layered half-space with an edge and a stress-free side slope; and (iii) introduce
the idea of an Effective Slope Angle (ESA), which can be back-calculated from
deflections measured near pavement edges or cracks.
3.1
Code development
The first objective is to develop a C library to efficiently introduce and solve an edge
into a homogeneous, isotropic, weightless, linear elastic half-space. In this context,
two related activities are included: implementation of the the homogeneous solver in
C programming language, and verifying the implementation against various reference
solutions.
The underlying formulation is semi-analytical, based on the Method of Funda-
mental Solutions (MFS), a technique for solving boundary value problems [41, 42, 43]
(refer to Section 4.1). Essentially, “fundamental solutions” are point forces within
the half-space domain (Appendix B), that inherently satisfy the governing differen-
tial equations for an elastic half-space. The point forces are placed on a fictitious
“force boundary” outside the actual domain of interest and are chosen such that
their combined effect cancels out unwanted mechanical responses along a nearby
“simulated boundary”. In doing so, this configuration mimics an edge and a free
sloping surface, effectively creating a trimmed half-space that matches the desired
boundary conditions.
3.2
Multi-layer extension
The second objective is to extend the code to solve for surface vertical deflection in a
layered half-space. In this context, three related activities are included: integrating
the multi-layer solver implementation into the framework established in Objective 1,
verifying the implementation based on benchmark solutions, and presenting some
case study findings of engineering relevance.
The multi-layer extension is pursued based on the Equivalent Layer Thickness
(ELT) theory [17, 18] (refer to Section 2.1).
In brief, ELT enables the surface
deflection of a linear elastic multi-layered half-space to be computed by aggregating
the deflection of the bottom-most layer’s top interface and the compressions of all
overlying layers. Such decomposition enables the use of homogeneous solver to
7

evaluate each decomposed homogeneous half-space with an edge sequentially. The
cumulative deflection at the surface thus accounts for both the elastic property
transitions throughout the pavement’s vertical profile, and the influence of the
pavement edge slope.
3.3
Effective Slope Angle
The third objective is to introduce the idea of an Effective Slope Angle (ESA), which
can be back-calculated from deflections measured near pavement edges or cracks. In
this context, three related activities are included: presenting comprehensive definition
of ESA, demonstrating a ESA back-calculation algorithm, and documenting case
study observations of engineering relevance.
In pavement systems adjacent to local soil and experiencing top-down cracking,
ESA serves as a metric to represent the lateral soil support contribution and stress
transfer mechanisms from intact material (refer to Figure 6.4). Utilising FWD
measurements, ESA can be back-calculated as a complementary parameter to layer
moduli. The computational approach involves an iterative methodology: for a trial
ESA, the established solvers from Objectives 1 and 2 first calculate the surface
deflection profile using a constant FWD loading and predefined pavement layer
configuration. Subsequently, the algorithm refines the ESA value, progressively
aligning modelled deflections with field-measured deflections near the pavement
boundary. Through this precise matching of predicted and actual responses, the
procedure derives an ESA that quantifies support conditions beyond the pavement
edge.

Chapter 4
Modelling a Pavement Edge
This chapter formalises the pavement-edge model by first presenting a general
homogeneous formulation along with its computational discretisation framework,
then extending the model through equivalent layer thickness theory and detailing its
integration.
4.1
Homogeneous formulation
Back in 1927, Love [12] published his closed-form expressions for the stress field
in a semi-infinite elastic solid under a distributed load on its free surface. These
solutions were later be refined and collected by Hanson & Keer (1990) [27], and Guo
et al. (2017) [28], which considered pressure applied over a rectangular area. Given
these solutions, one could obtain the Cauchy stress tensor and displacement field at
any given point within the half-space, apart from the singularities near the edge of
the loading area. In the seminal work of Mindlin [44, 45], a complementary set of
“fundamental solutions” (point forces) was derived based on the method of images.
These solutions satisfy Navier–Cauchy equations throughout the half-space, they
exhibit singularities at source point, as well as decay to zero at infinity.
With these analytical building blocks in hand, two questions naturally arise. First,
given a homogeneous, isotropic, weightless and linear elastic half-space, subjected to
a known stress patch load, can one choose a finite set of interior point forces whose
combined stress field exactly cancels the patch-induced stresses (tractions) on some
arbitrary interior surface? In other words, can we engineer a “stress-free” artificial
boundary by superposing point force solutions? Second, once that new boundary
is created, can we then represent stresses and displacements anywhere inside the
region bounded by the original free surface and by a linear combination of both the
patch-pressure solution and those point-force solutions?
Fortunately, both questions are answered by the Method of Fundamental Solutions
(MFS). In essence, MFS approximates the true field by a finite sum of fundamental
solution “kernels” (in our case, Mindlin point forces), whose strengths are chosen to
satisfy the prescribed boundary tractions. Linearity then guarantees that once the
artificial boundary is rendered traction-free, any interior point’s stress or displacement
follows by the same linear combination of patches and point forces [46, 47]. In a
recent study, Levenberg [1] adopted the idea of MFS for pavement modelling and
established a homogeneous pavement model with an edge.

Chapter 6
Application to Deflection Testing
This chapter extends the previous homogeneous solver to multi-layered pavement
systems, presenting a comprehensive approach to advanced pavement analysis. An
overview of the algorithmic developments is provided to demonstrate the computa-
tional approach of the code extension. A novel analytical concept is also introduced:
the Effective Slope Angle (ESA). In brief, ESA represents a critical characteristic that
quantifies the available “edge support” for a given pavement structure. Case studies
are presented correspondingly to demonstrate the capability of solving layered elastic
pavement system and back-calculating ESA. Both functionalities are packed in the
multi-purpose solver tmk_solve, implemented within the taamak.h framework.
6.1
Multi-layer code extension
The extension of the homogeneous solver, previously verified in Chapter 5, is funda-
mentally based on the Equivalent Layered Thickness (ELT) theory [17, 18]. This
theoretical approach enables transforming multi-layered pavement systems into an
analytically manageable equivalent single-layer representation.
The formal mathematical formulation of the ELT theory is comprehensively
detailed in Section 4.2, which establishes the theoretical foundation for the com-
putational approach. Algorithm 2 presents the algorithmic interpretation of this
formulation, whereas C implementation of this algorithmic approach is done through
the internal function tmk__evaluate_elt within the taamak.h framework. This im-
plementation encapsulates the computational logic, allowing for efficient and accurate
evaluation of multi-layered pavement systems using the ELT methodology.
Verification effort was made to examine the reliability and accuracy of this
implementation. The verification was based on Case 6.1.1 (also see Figure 6.1) and
the reference solution was provided by Levenberg.

6.3
The concept of ESA
The ability to model pavement structures with near-edge loadings enables the deriva-
tion of an Effective Slope Angle (ESA). The ESA concept is most clearly understood
through two FWD testing scenarios – as depicted in Figure 6.4. The first scenario,
illustrated in Figure 6.4a, involves a pavement system abutting with a local soil
medium. The structural layers of the pavement terminate abruptly, directly contact-
ing the surrounding soil. Figure 6.4c presents a corresponding model where the local
soil material is entirely removed, replaced by an edge and a stress-free side slope
characterised by an angle of ESA1.
The second scenario, shown in Figure 6.4b, depicts a pavement system experi-
encing significant cracking. In this situation, certain structural layers are segmented
by a top-down fracture, with the FWD load plate positioned tangent to one side
of the crack’s opening. Figure 6.4d illustrates the corresponding model, where the
pavement material to the left of the crack is completely eliminated, and an edge with
a stress-free side slope at an angle of ESA2 is introduced.
In both models, the ESA quantifies the lateral support provided to the pavement
system by the omitted region. For the first scenario, ESA1 represents the side support
contributed by the local soil. In the second scenario, ESA2 characterises the support
offered by the intact material or through a stress-transfer mechanism across crack
interfaces (or a combination of both). Under ideal conditions, ESA is 0◦(representing
a half-space model), while the most extreme scenario presents ESA at 90◦.
The proposed approach suggests that the ESA characteristic can be back-
calculated from FWD measurements as an additional parameter alongside layer
moduli. Algorithm 3 demonstrate the algorithmic approach for such back-calculation,
which formulates and resolves the inverse problem using forward solvers developed
previously, namely tmk__solve_single and tmk__evaluate_elt. The implementa-
tion of this algorithmic method is embodied by the internal function tmk__solve_esa
within the taamak.h framework, allowing for efficient back-calculation of ESA.

7.1
Over-determined system
An over-determined system is advantageous in the Method of Fundamental Solutions
(MFS) collocation compared to a square system. The impact of minor perturbations in
the data Σ in Equation 4.8 is mitigated through least squares regularisation, resulting
in reduced round-off errors and modelling noise [53]. This approach offers several
generic benefits, including improved numerical stability, enhanced error tolerance,
and more robust computational methods.
In our specific application, the over-determined system is preferred for additional
reasons. We employ an approach analogous to kernel-based approximation theory.
Considering Mindlin’s fundamental solutions (Appendix B) as kernels, denoted by Φ,
we interpolate stress responses on a given surface (in this case, a pavement structure
slope). Let the stress responses for traction cancellation on the slope be R, we seek

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis pursued three primary objectives: (i) create an open-source C library
to efficiently model and resolve the mathematical formulation for a homogeneous,
isotropic, weightless, linear elastic half-space featuring an edge and a stress-free side
slope; (ii) expand the computational framework to analyse surface deflections in
a stratified half-space with an edge and a stress-free side slope; and (iii) propose
the concept of an Effective Slope Angle (ESA), which can be back-calculated from
displacement measurements near pavement edges or cracks. This chapter synthe-
sises the outcomes and key discoveries, draws substantive conclusions, and outlines
suggestions for future work.
8.1
Summary and main findings
For the code development, a C library named taamak.h was developed. The core
algorithmic approach was outlined in Section 5.1, and the implementation was
verified through Case 5.2.1: an investigation of a trimmed homogeneous elastic
half-space subjected to a square patch loading (illustrated in Figure 5.1), across
multiple parameter configuration scenarios. The case originated from Levenberg’s
research [1], which also provided reference solutions. Consequently, the verification
process was comprehensively executed by comparing a broad range of quantities
between taamak.h and Levenberg’s implementation.
The verification outcomes
encompassed precise agreement of surface deflection in z and surface stresses in x
across diverse loading centres (x0/b →1, = 2, = 4) (depicted in Figure 5.2, Figure 5.3
and Figure 5.4), exact alignment of normalised surface deflection uz (Figure 5.5),
nearly identical deflection peaks (Table 5.2), and virtually identical stress (Table 5.4)
and displacement (Table 5.5) evaluation results within the trimmed half-space at
specified points.
taamak.h was subsequently extended to solve multi-layered domain. This exten-
sion was based on adopting the Equivalent Layer Thickness (ELT) theory to the
homogeneous solver. In brief, the surface vertical deflection of a linear elastic multi-
layered half-space can be computed by aggregating the deflection of the bottom-most
layer’s top interface and the compressions of all overlying layers. This decomposition
enables the use of homogeneous solver to evaluate each decomposed homogeneous
half-space with an edge sequentially. The extended multi-layered solver underwent
rigorous verification against Levenberg’s implementation [1] (refer to Case 6.1.1),
achieving precise correspondence with Levenberg’s reference solution (Table 6.2).
A comprehensive case study simulating near-edge Falling Weight Deflectometer
(FWD) deflection testing (Case 6.2.1) was performed. The case modelled an FWD
test conducted adjacent to a three-layered pavement edge featuring a stress-free slope,

with sensor arrays positioned both transversely (perpendicular) and parallel to the
edge. Four distinct simulations were conducted with slope angles of 0◦(half-space),
45◦, 75◦, and 90◦, with deflection outcomes measured by both sensor orientations

incrementally increases with slope angle.
A novel concept of Effective Slope Angle (ESA) was also introduced in the thesis
work. In short, in scenarios involving a pavement system abutting to local soil (Fig-

serves as a quantifier to represent the local soil side-support contribution (Figure 6.4c)
and the support mechanism from intact material or stress-transfer processes (Fig-
ure 6.4d). ESA can be back-calculated from FWD measurements as an supplementary
parameter alongside layer moduli, as demonstrated in the comprehensive algorithmic
approach (Algorithm 3). The algorithm is integrated into taamak.h and verified
through Case 6.1.1, wherein FWD measurements were simulated using Levenberg’s
implementation with a predefined set of original ESAs. The taamak.h resolved
results exhibited remarkably close alignment with the reference data, substantiating
its accuracy and computational robustness.
Numerical perspectives in the implementation of taamak.h were comprehensively
investigated. Drawing from the equation proposed in Levenberg’s research [1] (Equa-
tion 7.2), two numerical experiments were performed to analyse pavement response
convergence relative to the expansion of simulated boundary sizes. Investigations
utilising a dynamically sized boundary (Subsection 7.3.1) demonstrate that the
collocation parameters in taamak.h are sufficient to confidently encompass the area
necessary for stress cancellations, thereby generating a stress-free slope. Furthermore,
the convergence extrapolation experiments (Subsection 7.3.2) provide compelling
evidence of surface vertical deflection convergence in both homogeneous and multi-
layered half-space, with the selected collocation parameters in taamak.h yielding
surface deflection results within an acceptable range of the converged value, while
simultaneously ensuring computational efficiency and rapid processing.
8.2
Conclusions
The present thesis develops a computational framework for modelling linear elastic
pavement with an edge, through the implementation of taamak.h, an open-source
C library (refer to Appendix A) that bridges the gap in current pavement-edge
modelling approaches. taamak.h presents a comprehensive approach to modelling
homogeneous and multi-layered pavement systems with an edge by generating stress-
free side slopes. Through adapting rigorous verification processes against established
reference solutions, the taamak.h library demonstrates exceptional computational
accuracy and efficiency. Numerical experiments also verified the robustness of the
taamak.h implementation, demonstrating reliable convergence while maintaining
computational efficiency. Overall, The taamak.h implementation successfully resolves
edge effects in pavement modelling, providing engineers and researchers with a robust

computational tool.
Another significant contribution of this thesis work is the introduction of the
ESA concept. This novel parameter offers a quantitative approach to understanding
local soil side-support contributions and fracture-related stress transfer mechanisms
in pavement systems.
ESA provides a nuanced framework for analysing FWD
measurements, extending beyond traditional moduli back-calculation.
The thesis work also revealed critical insights into the relationship between
the existence of side slope and the surface deflection characteristics. Case study
demonstrated that slope angle significantly influences deflection magnitudes and
FWD measurements. For overall engineering practice, it is concluded that when
FWD tests are conducted near edges, the steepness of the side slope will considerably
affect the deflections. In such cases, the existence of an edge and slope angle must
be taken into consideration to ensure unbiased interpretation.
8.3
Future work
The primary focus for future research is developing an implementation that provides
a precise model for a trimmed layered half-space. Specifically, for multi-layered
pavement modelling, the approach involves replacing Equivalent Layer Thickness
(ELT) theory with semi-analytical solutions that account for layer interface bonding
mechanisms. This can be accomplished by adapting the current study’s formulation,
substituting the expressions in Appendix B with fundamental solutions within a
layered half-space [59, 60], and replacing those in Appendix C with surface stress
patch solutions for layered elastic half-space [19, 20, 61] (refer to Section 2.1). Such
an exact model necessitates numerical integration of complex oscillating functions
over a half-infinite interval, traditionally addressed through methods such as double
exponential transformation [62, 63]. Emerging approaches such as hybrid symbolic-
numerical integration [64, 65] could also offer promising alternatives, with their
ability to decompose oscillating functions and optimise resolution across different
frequency ranges to balance numerical precision and computational efficiency. The
next research phase will focus on extending the model to incorporate viscoelastic ma-
terial properties, and distributed loads parallel to the pavement boundary simulating
tyre-pavement tractions. This approach aims to better represent vehicle dynamics
in urban environments, particularly the recurring breaking and acceleration pat-
terns. Implementation could leverage the method of approximate Laplace transform
inversion [66], which was successfully employed in established research on layered
half-space models [39, 67, 22].
Subsequent research should focus on more sophisticated numerical analysis of the
pavement-edge modelling method’s stability and robustness. Grid sampling strategies
present a promising avenue of investigation. Low-discrepancy sequences, such as
Halton sequences, show significant potential in improving current grid sampling by
providing more uniform sample space coverage, thereby enabling fast convergence and
more accurate numerical approximations [68, 69, 70]. Further efforts could explore
grid point reduction through innovative thinning approaches, including gradient-based
methods1 and Poisson-disc sampling [71]. Additional geometric considerations are
worth exploring, such as analysing curved boundaries where curvature correlates with
original stress patch response intensities, or investigating symmetry-based strategies
to minimise linear system scales.

Appendix A
taamak.h Reference
The C library taamak.h developed in this thesis is available on GitHub at https:
//github.com/veslrs/taamak.h. It is distributed as open-source software under
the MIT License; users should note the additional notices and licensing terms for
third party dependencies included with the repository. Source code, example usage,
and compile instructions are provided in the repository to facilitate reproducibility.
We also acknowledge any external libraries and tools used (listed in the repository’s
LICENSE files) and the developers who maintain them. This reference describes the
programming interface (API) of taamak.h.
A.1
Compile and linking
An taamak.h program begins with including the header file. Note that by default
only declarations are included, flag TMK_IMPLEMENTATION should be added before
the header file to enable definitions of the functions.
#define TMK_IMPLEMENTATION
#include "taamak.h"
taamak.h depends on OpenBLAS for numerical linear algebra functionalities. To
compile the program, you will have to link it to the OpenBLAS library. You would
normally link with a command like this:
compiler -I<OpenBLAS include path> -L<OpenBLAS lib path> -lopenblas
-Wl,-rpath,<OpenBLAS lib path> input.c -o output
A.2
The tmk_model object
The taamak.h API revolves around an “object” of type tmk_model (a struct type).
Via this object, all of the parameters of the pavement model are specified (number
of layers, loadings, layer compositions, elastic properties, slope angle, etcetera), and
then one finally passes this object to tmk_solve in order to perform the model
evaluation. The object is initiated by passing it to:
void tmk_init(tmk_model *mdl, const int n_layers);
which initiate tmk_model object in place, given the number of layers of the elastic
pavement model, n_layers. When you are finished with the object, you should check
out the program by calling:
int tmk_checkout(tmk_model *mdl);

which checks result flags within tmk_model and return success (0) or failure (1) to
terminate the program.
A.3
Model specification
A general pavement-edge model requires the definition of several parameters (load,
layer information, material properties, etcetera). Normally the first parameter to
specify is loading, which is done by calling:
void tmk_set_load(tmk_model *mdl, const tmk_vec3 center,
const double half_width, const double intensity);
where given the centre of the load patch (center, where tmk_vec3 is a customised
struct type representing three-dimensional vector/point), the half-width of the
load patch b (half_width) and the intensity of the uniformly distributed load
q (intensity), the loading information for the model will be initialised. Note that
tmk_vec3 is defined as:
typedef struct {double x, y, z;} tmk_vec3;
To set elastic properties of the pavement model, you can pass the tmk_model
object to the function:
void tmk_set_material_properties(tmk_model *mdl, const tmk_hs *hs);
where tmk_hs is the customised struct type for elastic properties of an individual
layer, defined as:
typedef struct {double youngs_modulus, poissons_ratio;} tmk_hs;
By passing an array of tmk_hs containing all Young’s moduli Ei and Poisson’s
ratio νi (for homogeneous model, such array has length 1), the material properties of
all layers will be specified.
For multi-layered pavement structure, user should also setup layer thicknesses for
the model. This is done by the function call:
void tmk_set_composition(tmk_model *mdl, const double *top_depths);
where top_depths is an array of all layers’ top depths. For instance, given a three-
layered system with layer thickness h1, h2 and a semi-infinite h3, user should pass
the array (double[]){0.0, h_1, h_1 + h_2} to this function.
From here, the setup of tmk_model diverges into two, depends on the use case
of taamak.h. For the evaluation of surface deflection in a pavement system with
known slope angle α, user should set up the coordinates of evaluation points and
slope angle, before the final evaluation. This is done by the following function calls:
void tmk_set_evaluation_points(tmk_model *mdl,
const int npts, tmk_vec3 *pts);
void tmk_set_slope_angle(tmk_model *mdl, const double degree);
The first function, tmk_set_evaluation_points, accepts number of points
(npts), and an array of type tmk_vec3*, representing coordinated of evaluation
points. The second function, tmk_set_slope_angle, namely initialise slope angle
for the model.
For the back-calculation of Effective Slope Angle (ESA), user should set up both
FWD measurement array coordinates, and the corresponding measurement data. By
calling function:

Note that taamak.h offers a function to quickly setup a line with uniformly

A.4
Perform the computation
Once all of the desired model parameters have been specified in a given object mdl,
you can perform the computation by calling:
void tmk_solve(tmk_model *mdl);
where the function automatically detect the case to solve, perform the evaluation/back-
calculation, then print the results to standard output.
A.5
Redefinable macros
taamak.h also provides a list of redefinable macros with default values. Experienced
user could fine tune these parameters in order to reach the desired balance between
numerical accuracy and computation efficiency. These macros include:
TMKDEF: Appends additional keyword to function declarations. Possible modifica-

#define TMKDEF static inline
TMK_GEOM_M: MFS collocation parameter, indicating number of points per side
on simulated boundary (equivalent to

TMK_GEOM_N: MFS collocation parameter, indicating number of points per side

√

TMK_ESA_ITER: Maximum iterations for ESA back-calculation. Default value is

TMK_ESA_DEG_LIM: Slope angle limit for ESA back-calculation. When a trial ESA
is shallower than this limit, tmk_solve returns ESA = 0◦. Default value is 10◦.

Appendix C
Half-space Solution for a Square
Stress Patch
Outlined are expressions for calculating stresses and displacements in a homogeneous
elastic half-space (characterised by E and ν) due to a square stress patch at the
surface. The expressions are compiled from Hanson and Keer [27] and Guo et al.
[28] which are essentially based on the work of Love [12]; they are adapted for a soil
mechanics sign convention by Levenberg [1]. A right-handed Cartesian coordinate
system is considered with its origin placed at the upper boundary of the half-space,
the x- and y-axis coinciding with the surface, and the z-axis pointing down into the
medium. The stress patch centre is located at (x0, y0, 0); it has dimensions of 2b × 2b
(i.e., b is half the side length), and exerts uniform stress of positive intensity q in the
z-axis direction. The evaluation point for which responses are sought is identified
(x, y, z), where z ∈R+. All expressions involve the function R3, defined as follows
