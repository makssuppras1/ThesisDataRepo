Abstract
A highôÙorder numerical pseudospectral model based on the complete incompressible
NavierôÙStokes equations is developed and presented in this thesis. The model can be
viewed as a highôÙorder extension of the finite difference model by Li and Fleming (2001)
[2], even though the spatial discretization technique is different. The model can be used to
propagate nonlinear water waves freely with periodic boundaries and within a numerical
wave tank setup. The NavierôÙStokes equations are transformed from an irregular domain
to a regular timeôÙinvariant domain by using a variant of the üôÙtransform. The model is adôÙ
vanced in time by a flexibleôÙorder explicitôÙimplicit RungeôÙKutta scheme, with the ability to
be total variation diminishing. The RungeôÙKutta scheme is derived and verified by solving
the TaylorôÙGreen vortex problem, as it has an analytical solution to the incompressible
NavierôÙStokes equations.
The numerical model is discretized with a collocation method with a mixed basis consisting
of Fourier and Chebyshev functions. The Fourier basis functions are inherently periodic,
hence making the model periodic. The derivative of both basis functions is evaluated efôÙ
ficiently by using the fast Fourier transform. A numerical wave tank is designed to handle
the inherently periodic boundaries, by extending the domain with an additional generation
zone. The numerical wave tank is based on relaxation functions and enables the use of
efficient computation of derivatives with the fast Fourier transform. The twoôÙdimensional
model is applied on different benchmarks, confirming the modelãs ability to accurately repôÙ
resent nonlinear water waves. The model is tested for nonlinear solitary wave reflection
with results similar to other work solving this problem. The numerical model is also tested
in comparison to the experimental results carried by Beji and Battjes (1994) [3], where it
showed excellent agreement at eight different measurement gauges.

1
Introduction
Offshore structures and floating bodies accounts for a major part of both energy proôÙ
duction and logistics, hence making it a large worldwide industry and a big part of the
global economy. It is important that offshore structures and floating bodies are robust
and reliable to ensure that this supply chain does not fail. The primary load and danger
to offshore structures are water waves, hence making it important to be able to predict
these as precisely as possible. Wave predictions are done by imperial formulas based
on knowledge and experiments, or by solving differential equations describing fluid moôÙ
tion. The numerical models used to solve the latter is a crucial part of coastal and ocean
engineering since it can model water waves and waveôÙstructure interactions with high preôÙ
cision. Simulations of water waves are however difficult and computationally costly and
the development of efficient models is therefore still a scientific problem of high interest.
The practical use in naval engineering is still dominated by the use of simplified models
which lacks fidelity when approaching structures. Close to structures are nonlinear and
dispersive effects due to varying bathymetry and complex geometry an important part of
the wave formation, and needs to be considered.
Figure 1.1: Sketch of water waves traveling over varying bathymetry with a offshore strucôÙ
ture at the highest point.
Litterateur study:
Frequently used models for simulation of water waves are the mild slope equations [4]
and Boussinesq model extend to include the entire depth range by [5] and [6], which are
both models including only the free surface. This reduces the domain by one dimension
making it computational less expensive and more feasible for largeôÙscale problems. With
the increase in computer power over the last years and theoretical knowledge on numerôÙ
ical methods, a push for more complicated models including the depth has been made.
Extensive work has been done on potential flow models which are based on idealized
irrational fluids with no viscosity, as these can handle nonlinear wave effects over the enôÙ
tire depth ranges e.g. [7] and [8]. The mentioned work is discretized with a flexibleôÙorder
finite difference method and a spectral element method, respectively, both ensuring high
accuracy in space with the latter being able to handle complex geometry. More physical
accurate models including rotational flow based on the Euler equations have also been imôÙ
plemented successfully by [9] and [10] both using finite difference. The Euler models lack
energy dissipation and the more complicated and precise NavierôÙStokes model including
viscous effects are needed for this. Such models have been implemented using finite difôÙ
ference [2] and recently using the discontinuous finite element method [11]. The models
include pressure and both use the widely popular pressureôÙcorrector technique for time

integration, originally proposed by [12] and [13] independently. The method is an explicitôÙ
implicit method that steps the momentum equations without pressure explicitly forward in
time as an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) system and corrects the step by solving
an implicit Poisson boundary value problem for the pressure. The method determines
the pressure by using the continuity equation to obtain a divergence free system, hence
fulfilling conservation of mass. The mentioned NavierôÙStokes models use a secondôÙorder
RungeôÙKutta (RK) scheme in combination with the pressureôÙcorrector method, which in
general is too low for higherôÙorder spatial discretization techniques. HigherôÙorder AdamôÙ
Moulton and AdamôÙBashforth schemes have been successfully implemented together
with the pressureôÙcorrector method by [14] for the incompressible NavierôÙStokes EquaôÙ
tions (NSE), but without a free surface. Similar work has been done for RK methods by
[15] and recently [16], which have been able to solve the system with fourthôÙorder acôÙ
curacy in time, both using spectral methods for spatial discretization. HigherôÙorder time
integration with a pressureôÙcorrector method and a free surface is obtained by [17] and
[18], which both solves the Euler equations using finite difference.
Object of thesis:
The objective of this thesis is to combine a highôÙfidelity nonlinear physical model with a
highôÙorder numerical method and prove that such a concept is possible by showing that
it can handle nonlinear wave effects and obtain high numerical accuracy. The goal is
to achieve this with the incompressible NSE, which includes both viscous and rotational
effects, to describe the fluid motion of water waves. High numerical accuracy is to be
achieved by discretizing the Partial Differential Equation (PDE) system with a highôÙorder
pseudospectral method based on Fourier and Chebyshev basis functions ideal for effiôÙ
cient computation for wave propagation. Wave propagation is the key to accurate simuôÙ
lations and the model is to be advanced in time by the use of a flexibleôÙorder pressureôÙ
corrector type method which seeks to satisfy the mass conservation through minimization
of the divergence as a key component of the numerical highôÙorder scheme. Lastly to test
the model against experimental results in a numerical wave tank designed with periodic
boundary conditions, through a Fourier continuation technique allowing for the use of Fast
Fourier Transform (FFT).

This Chapter presents a time integration method based on a method of line approach for

4.7.3
Complexity of the solution model
Even though scalability and fast computations are not the scope of this thesis, these topics
are still important and we, therefore, wish to shed a bit of light on them here.
Differentiation of the velocity and the pressure is done a lot using the proposed time inteôÙ
gration technique in Chapter 3, hence making it an important quantity to make efficient.
The complexity of differentiation is of O(n log(n)) due to the FFT and FCT outlined in
Section 4.6. This was not initially implemented and differentiating was the computational
bottleneck, making computation of benchmarks extremely timeôÙconsuming.
The pressure boundary value problem is solved with a direct solver which solves the linear
system of equations with very little error. In this work is MATLABãs direct solver ã\ã used,
which in general is a quite efficient solver, but due to A being dense is the work effort
O(n2). This slows down the solver a lot since this boundary value problem needs to be
solved multiple times just to complete one time step. For clarification please see Section
3.4. For any practical use of the solver is this a problem that needs to be addressed, as
the work effort has to scale linearly. This can be achieved by implementing an iterative
solve like a pôÙmultigrid or preconditioned defect correction method, as done by [28], [29]
or [30].
Another costly operation is the assembly process of the system matrix A as this needs
to be done every time the boundary value problem is solved. This is necessary as the
model problem is nonlinear with finiteôÙamplitude waves, hereby the üôÙtransform timeôÙ
dependency. The assemble requires a lot of computational effort as the differentiation
operators are of size Nx ôñ Nz û Nx ôñ Nz and matrix addition has complexity of O(n2). The
summation can be parallelized but it is still expensive since four operators need to be
added together.

Numerical Wave Tank
In order to compare the modelãs performance against real life experiments, a numerical
wave tank is needed. The design of this wave tank is based on work by [31], where the
generation and absorption of waves are done inside the computational domain, in contrast
to other methods, where it is applied at the boundaries or as a part of the governing equaôÙ
tions. The method modifies the solution during the time integration process by applying
soôÙcalled relaxation functions to the physical solution. The method has been successfully
implemented for highôÙorder methods by [32] and [33] for solving nonlinear Boussinesq
models. It has more recently been used for waveôÙstructure interactions [34] based on the
free surface framework REEF3D, which uses the incompressible NSE.

method increases the domain size by having a longer generation zone than usually but

derivatives. This ultimately leads to a more efficient computation as the solution algorithm
computes a lot of derivatives. The procedure used to derive and build a stable and working

5.3
Wave generation and absorption
Wave generation is done by an exact wave solution, which for different wave problems
can take different forms. In this work, we use a stream function solution, which is the
solution to the fully nonlinear potential flow, presented in Chapter 2. The stream function
solution is a good general choice as it does not limit the steepness nor the depth of the
waves generated. The stream function solutions do however require that the seabed in
the generation zone is flat. Another varying factor by using this wave tank design is the
length of the different zones. Short zones are preferable as the total number of degrees of
freedom in the system grows with complexity O(n) as the zones get longer. On the other
hand, longer zones stabilize the solutions and contribute to absorption without reflections.
For this model and the cases tested 8 wave lengths for the generation zone and 4 for the
absorption zone were found to work well.

6.4
Discussion of verification results
The verification results in the three previous sections show that the model is able to obôÙ
tain an accurate velocity field ôÙ if the computational resources are available ôÙ and that it
can compute free surface waves with an excellent visual agreement to benchmarks and
experiments. All the results are produced without any stabilizing techniques, making the
model prone to failure due to aliasing and other nonlinear instabilities. The NSE have inôÙ
herently damping through the viscous term, which should stabilize the model to a certain
degreeëƒ however, it was found that the physical damping was far from enough to stabilize
the model entirely. Consequently, the results in this chapter are obtained through multiple
trials of constantly minor adjustments to make it work. It was found that the instabilities
were primarily caused by nonlinear effects that would cause the model to blow up. This
was quite clear when running the wave reflection test for steep solitary waves. After the
wave has reflected off the wall, the dispersive tail of highôÙharmonic waves would cause
the model to blow up. The same tendencies could be observed when running the subôÙ
merged bar test, but in this case, the Gibbs phenomenon also played a role making it
unclear which one causing the model to blow up. Some of the instabilities could be due
to under resolution of the problems as the work effort of O(n2) and the impatience of the
author lead to simulations with maybe too few points in either direction.
This section is just to shed some light on the fact that even though the results shown in
this chapter agrees with other models and experiments, additional work is still needed to
make the model robust.

Conclusion
In this final chapter, we summarize the most important results and findings throughout the
thesis and point out possible future work to enhance the model even further.
The presented work showed that it was possible to construct a higherôÙorder free surface
model based on the incompressible NSE. The numerical model was derived based on
a pseudospectral method using mixed basis functions and a flexibleôÙorder RK method,
with the possibility of being total variation diminishing. Several tests were carried out,
which showed the modelãs ability to represent nonlinear water waves with high accuracy,
simulate dispersive waves, and capture other water wave phenomena.
The governing equations were presented with assumptions of incompressibility and no
surface tension, and relevant boundary conditions were outlined. The equations were
mapped to the timeôÙinvariant üôÙdomain, where all wave amplitudes can be represented
without reôÙmeshing. The theoretical background was outlined and a deviation of the transôÙ
formation was carried out, including a deviation of the üôÙcoefficients containing information
about the original domain.
A flexible explicitôÙimplicit RK method based on the pressureôÙcorrection method was deôÙ
rived. The time integration method was designed to fulfill the continuity equation discretely
in time, which resulted in a mixed stage Poisson type boundary value problem for the
pressure. The boundary conditions for the pressure problem were derived and a solution
procedure was outlined. The method was verified with the TaylorôÙGreen vortex problem
where the method showed excepted convergence rate in time for the classical ERK4 and
the 3ôÙorder TVDRK scheme. The test showed that the method was able to preserve the
spectral properties over time, as spectral convergence was achieved after multiple time
steps.
The theoretical foundations for spectral methods were outlined including continuous and
discrete function representation, numerical integration, Fourier and polynomial methods,
nodal and modal duality, and discrete operators. It was shown that evaluation of derivaôÙ
tives could be done efficiently by using the FFT and FCT. Lastly, the model equations
were discretized using a nodal collocation approach with Fourier basis in the horizontal
direction and Chebyshev polynomials in the vertical direction.
A numerical wave tank with wave generation and absorption inside the computational doôÙ
main was constructed based on relaxation functions. The wave tank presented in this
work differs from other work using this method as it was modified to have periodic boundôÙ
ary conditions allowing for Fourier basis functions and efficient evaluation of derivatives
with the FFT. The wave tank was tested with two different cases both showing conservaôÙ
tion of mass and effortless propagation through the target zone with no reflections in the
absorption zone.
The model was tested against two known benchmarks (i) solitary wave reflection and (ii)
waves traveling over a submerged bar, both including nonlinear water wave effects. The
solitary wave reflection off a vertical wall was simulated as two colliding solitary waves
reflecting off each other inelastically. The attachment height, maximum run up height,
and detachment height to the vertical wall was recorded for different amplitude waves
and showed excellent agreement with the benchmark.
The submerged bar test was carried out by using the developed periodic numerical wave

7.1
Future work
In this section, we present a prioritized list of things that should be implemented in the
future for the solver to perform optimally and to be used in realôÙlife applications.
ãÂ Stabilize the numerical method by deriving and applying a deôÙaliasing filter that reôÙ
moves a small amount of energy from the system preventing it from blowing up. A
deôÙaliasing filter enables the model to propagate strongly nonlinear water waves for
longer time periods without destroying the spectral properties. Furthermore, a filter
exploiting the FFT and FCT would be ideal.
ãÂ Implement an efficient iterative solver for solving the pressure boundary value probôÙ
lem used for accurate time integration. The multigrid method is believed to be a
good choice for this as [2] solves the NSE using such a method, with a similar time
integration technique as presented here. A multigrid method would reduce the comôÙ
plexity from O(n2) to O(n log(n)) lowering the CPU time drastically, which enables
computations of complex benchmarks.
ãÂ Discreitize the physical model using a multiôÙdomain spectral element method as opôÙ
posed to the singleôÙdomain method presented here. This would make it possible
to obtain a sparse system matrix making it extremely efficient to solve the presôÙ
sure boundary value, especially if combined with a multigrid method. The spectral
element method is able to discretize complex geometries, hence opening up for
computations of waveôÙstructure interactions.
ãÂ Extending the model to three dimensions, allowing it to solve realôÙlife problems. This
is, however, only feasible if an iterative solver is implemented as computation time
would be too long with the current complexity of O(n2).
