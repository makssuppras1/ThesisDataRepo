Abstract
Population growth and socio-economic changes are leading to an increase in the global
protein demand, which is challenged by climate change and a large pressure on the
arable land. These challenges push for innovative protein supply chains with low envi-
ronmental impact and less land use. Using methane-oxidizing bacteria (MOB) to pro-
duce single-cell protein (SCP) from renewable biogas is a promising solution. This
study investigated the use of MOB for the bioconversion of raw biogas using the 3
strains of MOB: M. capsulatus, Methylocystis and Methylomonas. First in pure gases
to characterize the growth at optimal conditions, next at different CO2 levels to examine
the tolerance, and last using raw biogas to evaluate the potential of biogas conver-
sion to SCP. The experiment conducted using pure gases, highlights Methylocystis
and Methylomonas for methane bioconversion, achieving higher oxygen tolerance, in-
creased cell density, and better dry cell weight (0.4-0.5gDCW) compared to M. cap-
sulatus. While M. capsulatus is sensitive to oxygen levels above 40%, Methylocystis
and Methylomonas maintain robust performance, albeit with slower growth rates. Max-
imum CO2 levels for growth are around 10%, with concentrations above 25% inhibiting
growth. Cultivation using raw biogas showed high growth rates and yields of approxi-
mately 0.4gDCW/gCH4, but generally lower yields than using pure gases. Lower oxy-
gen concentrations enhance growth rates with M. capsulatus achieving 0.21h−1. This
study indicates that raw biogas can support cost-effective growth of MOB, and suggests
future research in co-culturing to improve biomass yields and commercial viability of
biogas conversion to SCP.

Introduction
The world population is estimated to reach 9 billion people by the year 2050, which
poses a major risk to achieving food security. This development is increasing global
protein demand, which is projected to rise by 70% by 2050, with the alternative protein
market valued at $2.2 billion in 2019 [1] [2]. This increase is driven by socio-economic
factors such as increased wealth, urbanization, demographic changes, and nutritional
needs [3]. The food demand needed to accommodate an increasing population and
consumption is also challenged by growing effects of climate change [4, 5]. Currently,
half of the world’s habitable land is used for food and feed production, exacerbating the
scarcity of natural resources. Additionally, agricultural expansion and human activities
cause 80% of global native habitat loss, leading to the extinction of over 10,000 species
annually [6]. The increased pressure on arable land necessitates innovative protein
supply chains that require significantly less land area and have a low environmental
impact.
The concept, known as Single-Cell Protein (SCP) or Microbial Protein (MP), is an
emerging biotechnology that produces nutrients for human or livestock consumption
with minimal land use [7]. SCP cultivation involves producing food or food ingredients
using unicellular microbial cultures in bioreactors [8]. The SCP concept originated in
the late 1960s [9]. Sources include various microorganisms such as bacteria, yeast,
fungi, and microalgae. It has a potential of providing a sustainable protein source with a
low environmental impact, making it a suitable solution for the problems emerging from
a growing population and food demand [10]. For instance, large-scale SCP production
can reduce land use by 99% compared to conventional European meat production [11].
Especially livestock production has proven negative environmental impact, which cur-
rently utilizes large amounts of soybean meal and fish meal as the main protein source.
SCP has the advantage of taking up less arable land area and is less dependent on
climate and soil than soybean, and generally less impact on society and ecosystems
[12, 13].
SCP is approved by the European Union for the use as feed for livestock and has
been shown to be used commercially for the same purposes [14, 13]. SCP has a high
protein content and is rich in various amino acids, including many essential amino acids.
Analyses have shown that SCP has the potential of replacing 10-19% of crop-based
feed for livestock by 2050 and reduce crop land use, nitrogen loss and GHG release
from agriculture by 6%, 8%, and 7%, respectively. Furthermore, the cells can reach
up to 100% nitrogen conversion efficiency and 70-75% protein content making it an
important alternative protein feed source. [15]. Table 1.1 highlights some of the most
recently researched sources of SCP.

Objective
This thesis aims to investigate three main objectives: 1. Characterize the growth of
MOB using synthetic pure gases. 2. Investigate the effect of elevated CO2 concentra-
tions to simulate the effects of CO2 in biogas. 3. Evaluate the effect of raw biogas and
its impurities on the growth of MOB. These objectives will collectively be an assessment
of the potential methanotrophic bioconversion of biogas to SCP.

1.2
Methane-Oxidizing Bacteria
Methanotrophic bacteria belong to the larger group of methylotrophic microorganisms,
which utilize C1-substrates like methane, methanol, and other methylated compounds,
assimilating the resulting formaldehyde as a source of cellular carbon [33]. The unique
ability of methanotrophs to use methane as their sole carbon and energy source, directly
oxidizing it and converting it into other cellular products, distinguishes them from other
methylotrophs. They transfer the released electrons to oxygen during this metabolic
process [34].
CH4 + NADH + H+ + O2 →CH3OH + NAD+ + H2O
(1.1)
The current list of published methanotrophs includes over 59 species from 24 different
genera and has been continually updated since the early 20th century, starting with the
initial observations of a methanotrophic culture by Söhngen in 1906 and the pioneering
isolation and classification work by Whittenbury et al. in 1970 [35, 36]. Additionally, six
species from the phylum Verrucomicrobia have been isolated from fumaroles, demon-
strating methanotrophic metabolism even under extreme conditions with pH values be-
low 1 [37].
Methanotrophs are found in three phyla: Proteobacteria (the largest group), Verrucomi-
crobia, and the newly identified candidate phylum NC10 [38]. Proteobacterial methan-
otrophs are divided into two groups: Gamma-proteobacteria and Alpha-proteobacteria.
Gamma-proteobacteria include a wide range of methanotrophs such as Methylomonas,
Methylococcus, Methylomicrobium, Methylosarcina, Methylocaldum, and Methylobac-
ter, while alphaproteobacteria include only five genera: Methylosinus, Methylocystis,
Methylocella, Methyloferula, and Methylocapsa [39].
Methanotrophs are separated into two classes based on their metabolic pathway used
for formaldehyde assimilation: type I/type X, and type II [33]. Type I and type X MOB
assimilate carbon through the ribulose monophosphate (RuMP) pathway (Gamma-
proteobacteria), whereas type II MOB assimilate through the serine pathway (Alpha-
proteobacteria), and lastly a newly discovered group of thermoacidophilic methanotrophs
utilize the Calvin Cycle, but belongs to the phylum of Verrucomicrobia [40, 41, 28].
1.2.1
Metabolism
Methane metabolism in methanotrophs (Figure 1.2) begins with the oxidation of methane
into methanol, catalyzed by methane monooxygenase (MMO) [33]. The MMO enzyme
complex activates the strong C-H bond in methane and oxidizes it into methanol using
oxygen, which can happen even at ambient temperature and pressure [42]. Methan-
otrophs can express two types of MMO: soluble methane monooxygenase (sMMO)
located in the cytoplasm and particulate methane monooxygenase (pMMO) bound to
the membrane [43].
Bacteria can express either or both forms, with pMMO being the most prevalent among
MOB. sMMO has a higher substrate affinity and its expression is controlled by the pres-
ence of high copper concentrations [45]. pMMO has better access to methane as it is
associated to the membrane, while sMMO is thought to oxidize methane faster, but is in
the cytoplasm and further from methane [43]. After methane is converted to methanol

1.2.2
Methanotrophic SCP
Methanotrophs are particularly advantageous for SCP production because of their higher
protein content compared to fungi and yeast, and their more digestible cell walls com-
pared to algae [48]. Currently, SCP produced from methanotrophs has a high protein
content and a favorable amino acid profile. MOB are also an excellent source of essen-
tial amino acids, which makes them highly suitable for SCP production. These essential
amino acids include histidine, valine, phenylalanine, isoleucine, leucine, threonine and
lysine which are not synthesized by mammals and have to supplied through the diet.
They can also reach upwards of 70% protein content and show great potential for uti-
lization as a protein source for farm animals [40, 49]. This has also caused commercial
interest in the use of methane as substrate for biomass growth. It is currently produced
from natural gas by Calysta as FeedKind® protein and by Unibio as Uniprotein®. Unibio
A/S uses a patented U-Loop fermenter, which is a 4m tall U-shaped fermenter, to pro-
duce 4kg m−3 h−1 of proteinous biomass (70%) marketed as UniProtein®. The shape of
the fermenter is designed to increase gas-liquid mass transfer of methane to the MOB.
UniProtein® is approved for use as animal feed [50]. The strategy for SCP production
focuses on maximizing cell yield from methane while minimizing yield from ammonium
as a nitrogen source. When biomass is produced using ammonium, the cells tend to
have a lower protein content [48].

or N source and the use of mixed microbial cultures have gained interest. Studies on
raw biogas containing H2 have reported lower yields compared to pure methane [51,

cultures in a symbiosis of methanotrophs with other microorganisms does not always
increase protein content, as in the co-culture of M. capsulatus and Chlorella sorokini-
ana [53]. Besides differences in the protein content, the amino acid profile also varies
depending on the microorganisms and process conditions, but most are suitable as a
substitute for conventional protein.

1.2.3
Growth medium and gas composition
MOB possess the capability of utilizing CH4 and/or CO2 as carbon source while assimi-
lating nitrogen from a cultivation medium leading to SCP production. SCP ideally has a
high protein content and the cells therefore require readily available nitrogen for assimi-
lation in the protein synthesis. Different nitrogen sources can influence the growth of the
MOB and their nutritional composition. Most MOB can use nitrate, ammonia or both as
their nitrogen source. Ammonia requires less energy to assimilate and can more easily
be converted into cell biomass. Ammonia can also competitively inhibit the MMO, as
ammonia can be oxidized by MMO in some MOB because of the similar structure of the
compounds. This inhibition is varying between MOB, and the oxidation products of am-
monia can have toxic effects on some MOB. Generally type I MOB will prefer ammonia,
while type II are more suppressed by it. Nitrate as a nitrogen source will generally sup-
port growth of both types of MOB [58]. Type II MOB will predominantly prefer a slightly
acidic environment, as they are usually found in soil, while Type I MOB are often found
under neutral of slightly alkaline environments [59]. This is not necessarily the case for
MOB cultivated in bioreactors, which can show other pH dependencies than from their
natural environments [60].
The growth of MOB is dependent on the presence of specific metals traces in the growth

medium. The most important metals for the use of methane as carbon source is iron,
copper, zinc and molybdenum, where copper and iron are important for the first step in
methane oxidation [61]. Copper was shown to be crucial for maintaining a high growth
rate and to achieve a high biomass production. When extracellular copper scarcity oc-
curs, the MOB with both sMMO and pMMO, will use sMMO instead of pMMO, which is
the prevalent isoform at copper excess. pMMO is usually detected in copper concen-
trations of >0.85–1 μmol/g dry mass. Besides high growth rates and biomass yields,
copper can also increase lipid content in the membrane. This can be the case when
using AD digestate as a nutrient source, where a scarcity of trace metals is present and
supplement is necessary [40].
MOB also utilize methane as both carbon source and energy source. Methanotrophic
bacteria rely on O2 to oxidize CH4 into CO2 while building up their new cellular material.
The concentrations with which these are supplied are important and the ratio between
the two have an influence on growth rate and cell density. Based on the stoichiometry
of methane assimilation, a ratio of 1:1.5 of CH4:O2 would be the most optimal [51]. The
concentrations influence the mass transfer, and the cells with lower affinities, will prefer
higher concentrations of either methane or oxygen. But increasing the concentration of
one gas lowers the other, and the optimal ratio depends on thermodynamic consider-
ations and the bacteria’s tolerance to elevated levels of potentially harmful gases [62].
Gas fermentation uses high entropy substrates to generate energy for biomass forma-
tion, resulting in products with lower entropy than the reactants. For a reaction to run
spontaneously with the help of an enzymatic catalyst, the overall change in free energy
must be negative. This means the process must be exothermic to compensate for the
decrease in entropy through heat release [44].
1.3
Biogas to SCP Conversion
Using biogas as substrate for SCP production is starting to emerge as a viable method
to valorize biogas. Biogas utilized for power production faces steep competition with oil
and natural gas, but also renewable sources such as wind and solar have out-competed
biogas to become a cheaper and more efficient source of energy [31]. As a result the
use of MOB to produce SCP has reemerged as a promising technological solution to
utilizing the biogas produced by AD. Methane being the largest constituent of biogas,
makes it suitable for cultivation of MOB and is an important step towards a more techno-
economically viable biogas business. Current SCP production methods use natural
gas, as the biogas contains contaminants that inhibit the cell growth. Using biogas
instead would require removal of H2S to avoid toxic effect to the cells. Furthermore
the use of raw biogas would also result in the loss of large amounts of unused carbon
through biogas CO2 which is unwanted. Therefore biogas upgrading is also desired, to
capture as much carbon and avoid release of GHG [30].
1.3.1

Biogas is the product of anaerobic digestion(AD) which allows for the conversion of or-
ganic waste into a renewable energy source. AD uses different microbial species under
anaerobic conditions to convert organic matter from different sources. The source of
the organic waste usually comes as sludge from waste water treatment, agriculture,
livestock manure, food waste and paper waste. Biogas is a mixture of different gases,
where the two main components are methane and carbon dioxide, with some traces of

OD measurement

blank sample containing only medium was measured and used to subtract from the
sample OD.
2.3.2
Microscopy
Microscopy of the cultures was done by using the Nikon Eclipse Ti2 inverted microscope
(Nikon Corporation, Tokyo, Japan). The microscope was equipped with a 60x zoom
objective with a polarizer and a high sensitivity camera for imaging. NIS-Elements
software was used along with the microscope to capture images and apply correct
filters and settings for appropriate resolution and contrast.
2.3.3
Gas Chromatography
Gas samples were taken daily or every few days of cultivation from the headspace of
each bottle. With a syringe made specifically for sampling gas using a valve, 0.2mL
sample was taken and CH4 and CO2 levels in the gas was analyzed. Gas chromatog-
raphy was conducted using Trace 1600 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA).
Calibration was done by injecting triplicates of 3 different standards containing CH4 and
CO2 to create a calibration curve.
2.3.4
Dry cell weight determination
The dry cell weight was determined by sampling the remaining cell culture and cen-
trifuging the sample at 3500RPM for 30 minutes. The supernatant was removed and
approximately 2mL of liquid was left with the pellet. The sample was transferred to a
2mL eppendorf tube and put in -18C°for 24h. Water was subsequently removed by
lyophilization for 24-48h using a Labogene Scanvac CoolSafe Pro Freeze Dryer 4L
-110°C (LaboGene, Allerød, Denmark).

3.1
Impact of gas ratio on MOB growth
This objective aimed to investigate the growth of M. capsulatus, Methylocystis and
Methylomonas under varying CH4:O2 ratios. The growth was assessed by monitor-
ing the OD at 600nm, methane consumption and the final DCW was estimated. The
findings of this objective will offer insights into the growth characteristics of the these
MOB in batch operation using pure gases.
Figure 3.1a shows the growth curves for M. capsulatus which revealed no significant
increase in the OD at any of the investigated CH4:O2 ratios. This lack of growth suggest
high sensitivity to the oxygen concentrations. This is consistent with the work performed
in the preliminary research, which showed a visible change in OD using a 1:1 ratio of
CH4:Air.
Figure 3.1b shows the growth for Methylocystis where in contrast, robust growth was
observed for all investigated gas ratios. The maximum OD achieved at the stationary
phase was identical for all 3 gas ratios, but with a shorter lag phase for 60:40 CH4:O2
ratio. This suggests that a higher methane concentration and/or lower oxygen con-
centration could favor a quicker initiation of growth. The optical density reached ap-
proximately 0.5 at 600nm which is very similar to other batch experiments conducted
on MOB, but the OD can vary with the equipment and measuring technique [49]. The
growth rates (Figure 3.1d) were relatively low (0.042-0.048h−1), which is also lower than
growth rates described in literature (0.12-0.16h−1) which other Methylocystis species
have shown [43]. The high standard deviation during exponential growth hints at experi-
mental inconsistencies such as gas leakage or variations in inoculum size, underscoring
the need for more stringent experimental controls in future studies.
Figure 3.1c shows the growth for Methylomonas which exhibited similar growth to Methy-
locystis, with observed growth in all CH4:O2 ratios. Group 60:40 had the shortest lag
phase and highest overall growth rate for Methylomonas in the 3 groups as seen in
Figure 3.1d, which reached a µmax of 0.05h−1. This again suggest that a high methane
availability is preferred for optimal growth. The slower growth rate at the 50:50 ratio of
0.016h−1 and the extended lag phase at the 40:60 ratio and lower growth rate 0.027h−1
further highlight the impact of gas composition on the growth kinetics of this strain.

creasing methane levels for both Methylocystis which had a yield of 0.48±0.02gDCW/gCH4
for the 50:50 group and 0.44±0.03gDCW/gCH4 for the 60:40 group. Methylomonas had
a similar biomass yield in the 50:50 group of 0.59±0.02gDCW/gCH4 and a much lower
yield of 0.38±0.00gDCW/gCH4 for the 60:40 group. This is in the range of expected
biomass yields for both type I and type II methanotrophs at similar gas conditions [71,
72].
3.1.3
Suitability as SCP
For a microorganism to be suitable for SCP production it requires a high growth rate, a
high biomass yield and a high cell density. This objective highlights that strain specific
responses occur to the different CH4:O2 ratios. M. capsulatus had in the preliminary
research shown to be sensitive to high oxygen concentrations where no growth was
often seen and can be due to the accumulation of reactive oxidizing species [73]. This
is also consistent with some other studies on M. capsulatus where it was mainly grown
using air as the oxygen supply or lower oxygen concentrations, but no upper limit was
documented [74, 75, 76]. No growth was observed for neither 40:60, 50:50, 60:40,
but in the preliminary research growth was achieved using a mixture of 1:1 ratio of
methane and air. This had a very high growth rate of 0.21h−1 which is similar to other
experiments in the same conditions [58]. The optical density never surpassed 0.32 at
600nm in Figure 2.2a, which is much lower than for Methylocystis and Methylomonas,
but is due to oxygen depletion. In an industrial setting the limited oxygen levels can
be prevented by a continuous oxygen supply and pH adjustments. In such a setting a
high growth rate is much more beneficial, and Unibio A/S utilizes this in the commercial
production of SCP. M. capsulatus is co-cultured with Alcaligenes acidovorans (NCIMB
13287), Aneurinibacillus danicus (NCIMB 13288) and Bacillus firmus (NCIMB 13289) to
consume carboxylic acids formed under ferementation using natural gas which would
otherwise inhibit the growth of M. capsulatus and shorten the fermentation time [77,
78]. This process can achieve up to 0.8gDCW/gCH4, and a productivity of 4kg m−3 h−1
[79].
Methylocystis did not particularly high growth rates, but showed more robust growth
across the gas ratios investigated. In 2016, Patel et al. reported on the methanol
production of Methylocystis and also described the growth rates which were between
0.017±0.07h−1 and 0.031±0.18h−1 at 30C°and using NMS medium in a 1L reactor
with a 20% CH4 content [80]. The yield Methylocystis achieved is close to the theoreti-
cal yield of 0.65gDCW/gCH4 for type II methanotrophs. Methylocystis can accumulate
methanol if the methanol dehydrogenase gets inhibited or formate accumulates, low-
ering the actual biomass yield [80].

Methylomonas had more robust growth using high concentrations of methane and oxy-

yields across varying gas ratios. Overall favoring higher methane and lower oxygen
conditions for better growth rates. However, their slower growth rates compared to M.
capsulatus present a challenge for industrial applications.

For biogas conversion to SCP to be viable, the MOB need to be able to tolerate ele-

much lower than CH4:Air ratio of 1:1 with a growth rate of 0.205h−1, observed in the

Effect of raw biogas on the MOB growth
Using raw biogas directly as a source of methane for gas fermentation has not been
used commercially. This is because of the high levels of H2S, even after sulfide strip-
ping, and up to 55% CO2.
In this experiment the strains were cultivated using raw biogas, which was not industry
grade, as it only contained 40-45% methane and 14-18% CO2. This lowers the concen-
tration of CO2 the cells will be subjected to compared to using raw biogas from indus-
trial AD reactors. The gas composition used had a initial CO2 concentration at approxi-
mately 8%, when mixed with air. The use of raw biogas showed promising results which
contradicts literature, where raw biogas inhibited growth, but better biomass yields [48].
One study did achieve similar results, where the growth rate was higher when using bio-
methane, but did ascribed the difference to the lack of H2S in bio-methane [40]. The
H2S levels was not determined in this experiment, but it can be assumed that low levels
were present as the growth was generally better than with pure methane [18].
Despite the anticipated lower DCW due to oxygen limitations ( 10.5% in the headspace),
biogas cultures achieved comparable or slightly higher DCWs than pure methane cul-
tures. The biomass yields using biogas were consistent across strains ( 0.4gDCW/gCH4),
although lower than theoretical maximums and previous studies with high oxygen en-
vironments or mixed cultures, which had a biomass yield of 0.87gDCW/gCH4 [48]. The
pure methane yields varied significantly, with M. capsulatus showing notably low yield,
potentially due to less efficient CO2 assimilation in the absence of additional CO2. A
study on the CO2 assimilation of M. capsulatus also showed, that the a presence of

4
Conclusion
In growing single-cell protein (SCP) efficiently, organisms with high growth rates, high
biomass yields, and high cell densities are required. This study highlights the advan-
tages and challenges of utilizing M. capsulatus, Methylocystis and Methylomonas for
conversion of biogas to SCP.
The use of Methylocystis and Methylomonas enables the use of higher oxygen con-
centrations and sustained growth periods, leading to increased cell density and better
DCW, higher methane consumption, and biomass yield in batch mode. However, no
commercial applications for these strains have been documented in the literature. The
observed DCW of approximately 0.4-0.5g/L suggests that growth limitations could be
due to nutrient deficiencies, high cell densities, pH drops, or oxygen depletion, neces-
sitating further investigation to pinpoint the exact causes.
The sensitivity of M. capsulatus to oxygen levels above 40% contrasts with the more
robust performance of Methylocystis and Methylomonas, indicating that optimal gas
ratios are strain-specific. Methylocystis and Methylomonas demonstrate industrially
relevant biomass yields but exhibit slower growth rates at high oxygen concentrations
exceeding 40%.
CO2 levels also significantly impact the growth of MOB, which thrive at 10% CO2 and
lower methane and oxygen concentrations. This CO2 level supports growth across all
three strains with varying growth rates but similar yields. However, CO2 concentra-
tions above 25% inhibit the growth of MOB, with 10% CO2 emerging as a threshold for
sustaining growth at a CH4:O2 ratio of 40:60.
The study also finds that lower oxygen concentrations can enhance growth rates, a
trend also observed when using raw biogas with low CO2 levels, where M. capsulatus
exhibited high growth making it a prospect for industrial production of SCP. However,
this approach reduces the achievable DCW and slightly lowers yields compared to pure
methane bioconversion. This indicates that the raw biogas could still support the growth
of methanotrophs and would be a cost-effective substrate for SCP production.
4.1
Future Perspectives
Looking forward, several strategies could enhance the commercial viability of these
strains. Synergistic growth using multiple MOB strains, co-culturing with hydrogen-
oxidizing bacteria (HOB) or algae for CO2 removal, and integrating heterotrophs to con-
sume secondary metabolites are promising routes. These approaches could optimize
growth conditions, improve biomass yields, and address current limitations, paving the
way for more efficient and sustainable biogas conversion processes.
