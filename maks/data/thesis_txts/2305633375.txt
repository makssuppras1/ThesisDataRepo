Abstract 
Building commissioning is a quality control process that ensure the quality throughout the life cycle 
that ensure that owner’s, end-users, operational staff etc. needs are fulfilled. Building 
commissioning’s origin was in America where the energy crisis in 1970’s was the start for 
commissioning. Decades later, it was adopted in the Europe where the owners demand for receiving 
a functional building requested. 
Buildings are getting more complex along with the exponential technological development. 
Building commissioning covers the interdisciplinary aspect of a building projects and it is therefore 
interesting how commissioning is carried out in practice versus theoretical and how to perform risk 
assessment for commissioning. As it is today are there no guideline within commissioning on how 
to identify potential risks and there is no formulation in the commissioning plan that requires a 
description on how to assess the potential risks.  
One of the methods to enhance the understanding between risk assessment and building 
commissioning is with the knowledge of systems engineering practices. With the study of systems 
engineering, suggestions for improvements on how to handle high complex building projects is 
carried out to highlight important areas. The suggestions are how to identify the stakeholders and 
how to approach risks in structured manner. Among many risk assessment methods defined in the 
international standard ISO 31010, are the probability/impact matrix and the HAZOP method 
strongly applicable to building commissioning.  
In the study of how Sweco practices building commissioning, observations during meetings and 
conducted interviews with the client consultant, Henrik Rossen and Ole Teisen, Sweco. With this 
study it gave the insight of how commissioning works in practice and illustrates the advantages and 
disadvantages there is with working processes within building commissioning.

1 Introduction 
Building commissioning is relatively new in Denmark, but the concept has existed for a long time. 
Commissioning has existed in the ship industry for decades and the process is prescribed as part of 
the process when building a ship and put into operation. Building commissioning is big in America 
where it had its origin because of the energy crisis in the 1970’s. One of the challenges within 
building commissioning is that the owners think they already paid for this service, and therefore do 
not see the idea to pay for it again. When dealing with the risk assessment on construction projects, 
the commissioning guidelines does not satisfy that aspect of the process. The management of the 
risks is done from personal experience, because there in general are no formal methods on how to 
address risks, the risk management is done from personal experience.. This report will include 
systems engineering as a supplement to the commissioning process to make a guideline on how to 
address the risks and the same time manage the commissioning process.  
1.1 Problem statement 
The definition of the problem in relation to this thesis is: 
How to apply risk assessment to commissioning processes, and how to do this with the knowledge 
of systems engineering practices. In addition, which suggestions can be addressed to make the 
working process better. This gives the basis for following sub-questions: 
 Which guidelines to follow when planning commissioning processes and how are risk 
assessment approaches applied within the processes? 
 What are the advantages and the disadvantages with the current working processes 
compared to the theoretical approach and is the risk assessment carried out in building 
commissioning? 
 Which contribution can systems engineering deliver when comparing risk assessment and 
commissioning? 
1.2 Contribution 
This thesis will clarify current guidelines used when working with commissioning and risk 
assessment. It will also clarify the advantages and disadvantages with the working processes when 
working on a project and evaluate the outcome of the procedures included in the commissioning 
process. An interdisciplinary field of engineering is systems engineering, which purpose is to focus 
on how to carry a project through its life cycle and how to manage complex systems. The study of 
systems engineering will help the understanding to combine the commissioning process and the risk

working with the existing guidelines within commissioning and compare that to the daily practice. 
Lastly, recommendations for improvement for the current practices, and an evaluation of benefits

The following methodology to work with the thesis is as follows: 
 Use literature research to get knowledge of the building commissioning process and systems

 Following AT2, an ongoing project where Sweco is performing a commissioning process to

2 Introduction to building commissioning 
Commissioning has a new era in Denmark and is an upcoming process that becomes more and more 
a daily business. Before defining the different phases that building commissioning process involves, 
it is necessary to understand the background of commissioning and how it has evolved through 
time. Commissioning is most common and started in America where it is standard implementation 
when constructing buildings. Hence, in the reference of the historical timeline, it will show the 
American development process.  
2.1 Background 
A building commissioning processes was needed because the quality of the delivered buildings with 
systems did not satisfy the owners’ and end-users requirements. The consequence of not having a 
commissioning process was that the building did not work as expected when delivered. That caused 
delays on the delivery time since the faults had to be rectified before occupation permit.  
Building commissioning is a quality-focused process and handles both non-complex projects and 
complex construction projects. Commissioning has been around for decades and can be traced back 
to the ship industry where the ship and its equipment and systems were tested to verify that they 
worked as intended before take out on journeys. Commissioning has therefore existed for a long 
time, but commissioning’s foundation stone was in connection with war ships such as an aircraft 
carrier. Before the ships had its ship trials, the equipment on the ship had to through performance 
tests to ensure the system could e.g. work across disciplines. Taking warships into perspective, it is 
a high complex ship which systems have to work together in a broader sense and therefore was 
commissioning implemented to insure the requirements from the owner and users where fulfilled. 
When the equipment was installed and tested, the problems, which was identified, was corrected 
and lastly the crew on the ship was trained to operate the ship in a safe manner. Therefore, a 
completed commissioning process is when the materials, systems and staff have passed a thorough 
quality assurance process (Haasl & Heinemeier, 2006).  
The building commissioning process takes the same approach as in the ship commissioning. The 
building commissioning process is a quality control process, which is done to verify and document 
a building’s system and installations. This process is to make sure the installations and systems is 
planned, projected, implemented, tested, further operated and maintained to ensure they live up to 
the total economy, the building regulation requirements, other legislation, and lastly, the stakeholder 
==PAGE:11==
4 
 
requirements. Commissioning is an ongoing process that continue the building’s life cycle 
(DS3090, 2014). 
New buildings have in general a high complexity. This is due to the technological evolution where 
the contractors and designers have not been able to follow the exponential evolvement. The 
increased requirements within legislations have not come up with the processes that ensure the 
quality of the buildings and its systems to ensure the legislations can be complied. Therefore, many 
projects has not been able to comply the schedule because of all the requirements that is included 
with build a new building. An example from Denmark is the new building regulation from 2015 
where the requirements regarding energy consumption is increased. In 2020 the gross energy 
consumption needs to decrease by 4 pct. in comparison with the consumption in 2006, this is stated 
in the energy policy agreement from 2008 (Energistyrelsen , 2016).  
The following list, describes the complexity of these elements: 
 Energy conservation have become stricter. 
 Stricter requirements for working environments. 
 Increasing demands for indoor air quality and comfort control. 
 Technological requirements for the building’s systems and office equipment. 
 Increasing variations of the operational needs for buildings. (Heinz & Casault, 2016) 
The increased requirements for buildings and the influence between each other means that the 
complexity in general has been heightened within the last decade. One example of such an increase 
is that the lighting generates more heat, forcing the ventilation system to supply more cooling for 
the desired indoor climate. This increases the overall energy consumption.  
To decrease the energy consumption is it not enough to control, regulate and monitoring the 
building’s technical system. Automatic controls are used during the building’s initiation to verify 
the system’s interaction with the building. (Teisen, 2005)  
One of the challenges with commissioning is to convince the owners or stockholders to understand 
the value in commissioning. Currently they feel that they are paying for the same product more than 
once. It is however partly true because the sub-contractors’ contracts are formulated in way to make 
sure that their respectively system is balanced and adjusted, which is known in Denmark to be 
kvalitetssikring (KS). On the other hand, is it not true because the general- or turnkey contractor

typically does not have the competences to quality assure the systems. This is due to poor or no 
formulation of the each sub-contractors interaction between each system across disciplines.  
When the sub-contractors are doing their quality assurance without the commissioning process, it 
involves that their equipment are in balance but also must adhere to the regulations, rules and 
requirements which is stated in the contract. This could for example be the carpenter that has set the 
ceiling up with the requirements that specifies that the ceiling has to be sealed and the ceiling must 
not be awry. The carpenter are filling out the quality assurance paper to ensure that the completed 
work comply with the requirements. The inspection method can for example be executed by doing a 
visual inspection. A problem can then arise where the ventilation system is not working as intended, 
because they have calculated that the ventilation happens though the ceiling and the ceiling is too 
sealed and gives no flow. That example is among many others in the real world where the sub 
systems in the building is not in balance with each other. One of the main problems where the 
quality assurance is not sufficient is that the inspection is performed when the construction project 
is finished and it leads to a high risk for faults and imperfections before delivery. If the faults is too 
extensive often there will be made a stopgap that will lead to a bad quality in the end.  
The building commissioning process is however a little different from the quality assurance. The 
building commissioning process has several benefits, which is not guarantied in the normal quality 
assurance. The benefits of the building commissioning process includes following points: 
 More energy efficient building. 
 Ensure good indoor air quality. 
 Reducing the construction costs. 
 Reducing operation costs. 
 Certifications within LEED1, BREEAM2 and DGNB3. 
When applying the building commissioning process, the energy efficiency is among others in focus 
to optimize the building’s performance to comply with today’s building regulations etc. There is 
endless ways to ensure energy efficiency. Most common pitfalls when the tests are executed and the 
throughout the inspection are:

2 Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment Methodology, which is a labelling scheme.

 Powered heating, ventilation, air-conditioning (HVAC) when rooms are unoccupied. Light

If these common pitfalls can be avoided before installing the system, then the owner will save

requirements. 
 Keep the relevant requirements in the building regulations and other legislation. 
 Ensure compliance of indoor climate requirement. 
 Verify the systems across disciplines by doing cross-disciplinary tests. 
 Minimize flaws and imperfections. 
 Ease the transition between the construction phase and the occupancy and operation phase.

Definition of building commissioning 
Every specification, manual, stand etc. has its own way of define the concept. Many of the 
specification is similar and the bottom of line they want to achieve the same. They want to specify 
how to carry out the building project best as possible regarding economy and performance. 
When taking the Danish Standard (DS3090, 2014) definition of commissioning process, is it 
formulated as:  
“kvalitetsstyrende proces, som skal forbedre udførelsen af et projekt, og som fokuserer på at 
verificere og dokumentere, at et byggeri og dets installationer er planlagt, installeret, testet, betjent 
og vedligeholdt, så det lever op til bygherrens krav”

2013 (Byggeproces & Realdania, 2013): 
”Commissioning er en proaktiv dialog- og kvalitetsstyringsproces, der fokuserer på totaløkonomi 
og installationernes samspil”

Building commissioning is not here to substitute the current quality assurance. It is a known fact 
that many mistakes are made when making the normal quality assurance. Building commissioning

2.3 Pros and cons with building commissioning 
The building commissioning process has to create value. The value can consist of reduced energy 
consumption, saved operation costs and or sustainability certification. When looking at the benefits 
from the owners’ and operation personnel perspective, (Byggeproces & Realdania, 2013) has listed 
beneficial outcomes from the building commissioning process: 
 The building is operational from day one 
 The indoor climate live up to the requirements. 
 The operations costs is minimized, predictable and possible to budget. 
 The building is flexible and robust 
 The operation personnel received the required training and can maintain and operate the 
facility. 
 High satisfaction from end-users. 
 Documented performance and consumption. 
 Increased in value makes for the building. 
In Denmark is it not only owners who sees the value of commissioning. Contractors has begun to 
realize there is value for the money by including commissioning processes to ensure that the quality 
and requirements as described in the tender documents are satisfied. This will not necessary keep 
the quality high for the owner, but the delivery phase will go smoother for the turnkey contractor 
and therefore also benefit the owner in the end. According to (Byggeproces & Realdania, 2013), 
there are the following benefits for the contractor: 
 The building is built the right way the first time. 
 Delivery on time. 
 Hand over the documentation for performance and quality. 
 Minimizing the flaws. 
 Less warranty repairs.  
It is in everyone’s interest that the elements described above are fulfilled. Generally is it best if 
company that is responsible for the commissioning works for the owner and thereby cover all the 
assets in the building process. The commissioning process an investment, which normally will be 
visual in the occupancy phase where the operation costs is minimized. However, the problems and 
discussions arise in the earlier phases where the contractors will argue about payments, methods

and future performances. When these elements fall into place, the contractors’ are focused on 
central requirements is not overlooked. The contractors will therefore avoid reconstruction and 
redoing the project engineering. The commissioning process includes the focus on overall economy, 
which can give some challenges regarding cost-cutting measures, and one-sided focus on the 
construction economy. If the owner put some effort in the requirement specification beforehand and 
put limits on economy for the contractors and advisors, then the calculations for the overall 
economy can have much more influence in building process.  
 One of the pitfalls with the building commissioning process is the planning of the activities 
in the scheduling. It is important that there is time for the commissioning process so the 
scrutiny and tests are accomplished as planned. The processes takes times and resources but 
it prevents lack of time in the building period and up to the deadline.

culture on the building site. Building commissioning is relative new in Denmark and the

2.4 Building commissioning and its limits 
Building commissioning is basically a communication and validation process that begins in the pre-
phase of the construction project and continues throughout its lifespan. The communication is a 
crucial part, which is general, is the key to success. When constructing a building, it is necessary to 
involve many stakeholders at the right time to make the right decisions. There are two main 
elements within commissioning. The first key element is the building commissioning process 
guarantees a well-documented communication among the owners, designers, contractors and 
operators. The process seems logic but making sure these elements is in place, many pitfalls and 
flaws are avoided beforehand. The second key element is the validation of the sub-systems and the 
system itself. Building commissioning process a cross-disciplinary validation of the equipment, 
assemblies and operations are tested to ensure that the owner gets what is expected. (Grondzik, 
2009) 
The advantages of building commissioning when applied in the pre-phase from (Grondzik, 2009) 
and (Appendix D) is: 
 Help defining the owner’s requirements and their expectations. 
 Validating the design solutions held up against the OPR. 
 Reducing the change orders caused of poor communication. 
 Verifying the building’s system to make sure it works as intended. 
 Verifying the training material. 
 Make sure the documentation is sufficient before owner takeover. E.g. well documented 
indoor climate and energy consumption without surprises.

 Provide measurements of the system to visualize the building’s performance. 
 Delivery with minimized faults and imperfections. 
 Overall economy instead of construction economy. 
The following paragraphs point out what commissioning is not and shows what not to expect from 
commissioning (Grondzik, 2009) and (Appendix D): 
 Be able to make up for the poorly defined objectives and unrealistic expectations. 
 Be able to make of the insufficient design and/or construction budget.

 Lastly, it is not a police task.

3.1.1 Different building commissioning processes 
Depended on the state of a building there are four variants of building commissioning processes: 
 Initial commissioning: Starts in the Pre-Design Phase and starts out with defining the 
requirements for the projects and continues throughout all the phases.  
 Retrocommissioning: Commissioning process of an existing building that has not been 
through a commissioning process earlier. 
 Recommissioning: Commissioning of an existing building, which has been part of a 
commissioning process earlier. 
 On-going commissioning: Continuous commissioning process, which continually maintain 
and optimize the results that was achieved by a previous commissioning process. 
In Figure 8 the different commissioning processes throughout a buildings lifetime is illustrated. 
 
Figure 8 - The four different commissioning types (Appendix D). 
Initial commissioning 
The initial commissioning is present from the project is initiated in the program phase and continues 
throughout all the phases of the construction project. This process ensures that all new buildings can 
be operated at the time that is intended. Furthermore, it means that the building staff is trained to 
operate and maintain the building’s systems and equipment. It is an intensive quality assurance 
process, which continues throughout all the processes.  
Retrocommissioning 
Retrocommissioning is as mentioned an application of the commissioning process to existing 
buildings that has not been through a commissioning process earlier. The purpose is to improve the 
equipment and systems to perform together. Retrocommissioning can often resolve problems, 
which has occurred during the design and construction phase. It also addresses the problems that 
==PAGE:27==
20 
 
have been developed throughout the buildings lifetime. Overall, it improves the operation and 
maintenance (O&M) procedures to improve the buildings quality and performance.  
Recommissioning 
Recommissioning is also as stated above a commissioning process, which is applied on existing 
buildings that has been through a previous commissioning process. A recommissioning can be 
applied when the building e.g. shift owners, operating problems or some other needs for the 
building.  
Important elements within building commissioning is the transparency and the traceability. It is 
important that every part, which is included in the process, knows what is going to happen and what 
their roles are in the different activities. All observations etc. will be available for all the involved, 
which are contractors, architects, project advisors etc., and that will strengthen the communication 
and knowledge sharing. The traceability is to trace the different tasks, report etc. throughout the 
project’s lifespan, so that the responsible can have a better overview of the tasks that has been 
solved and yet not been settled. All the reports, accept documents etc. are dated and the responsible 
will appear from the document.  
On-going commissioning 
The on-going process is a continually process that maintain and optimize the results in relation to 
the previous commissioning process.  
 
3.1.2 Commissioning organization 
To get an idea of how the commissioning organization is organized, this paragraph will define what 
the commissioning organization is and what its purpose is. 
The commissioning group for the building project take care of the commissioning process, which in 
run parallel with the building process itself. The group is typically staffed by a commissioning 
leader, a representative for the owner, a representative for the project supervisors, and head of 
operations. The organization is continuously supplemented by commissioning specialists, project 
supervisors, contractors, suppliers, users and operating staff which is the most relevant for the 
particular subtask. In Figure 9, it is illustrated how the organization is structured. (Byggeproces & 
Realdania, 2013)

The commissioning leader is responsible for carrying the commissioning process out, and that 
includes choosing the different specialists who shall assist the activities. Furthermore, the 
commissioning leader has to call in the advisors, contractors and suppliers when their participation

When the owner select the commissioning leader, it is basically a free choice since there is no

However, some necessary competences that the leader should have are: 
 Broad technical insight within building automation, HVAC, indoor climate etc. 
 Experience with operating the actual systems. 
 Experience with the building process. 
 Good flair with project management and communication.

3.2 Program phase 
This phase is initiated as soon as possible to get an early start on the commissioning process. In this 
phase, the measurable requirements from the owner are defined before the program itself begins. 
Therefore, the project is projected, built, put into operation and lastly operated according to the 
owners’ requirements and expectations. Hence, it is important to clarify the needs and expectations 
before the project enters the program phase since the commissioning process will include the 
requirements as a starting point. 
3.2.1 Activities 
The main activities that is in this phase is to establish the commissioning organization. The 
requirements that the commissioning process has to follow through its lifespan is being defined to 
increase the quality of the building but also to satisfy the owner. Furthermore, one of the most 
important tools in the commissioning process is the commissioning-log. Here all observations are 
registered to avoid risks and/or to visualize issues. The commissioning-log is created and used 
throughout all the five phases.  
3.2.2 Commissioning specifications 
The owners specifications are also known as the owner’s project requirements. The owner and 
specialists that are experts within the respectively fields will in corporation define the needs and 
expectations of the building. This is one of the most crucial elements in the process since the 
commissioning process and future decisions will rely on these decisions. Therefore, it is important 
that the specialists make a carefully analysis to capture flaws and eventually add some extra 
requirements to the specification.  
==PAGE:30==
23 
 
The specifications will include the requirements that the owner expects functionally and physically 
and the performance level and acceptable criteria that for each system and installation is.  
The specifications that is defined only includes requirements and expectations of the systems, and 
therefore not a document containing solutions such as design specifications.  
In Denmark, and other countries, there is a similar specification phase called “byggeprogram” 
(building program), which describes the owner’s and sometime the users wishes and requirements 
to the building. The building program does not have the same level of details as in the 
commissioning specification e.g. performance requirements are clearly from the start of the project 
engineering in the commissioning specifications. This is however, for the better and worse; by 
making specifications in such detail puts limitation on the project and can cause problems or 
unrealistic solutions. On the other hand, the owner get what is expected and required without any 
surprises.  
3.2.3 Commissioning-log 
In the program phase, the commissioning-log is created to visualize the observations after that 
informing the relevant contractor to correct the observation or to decide what to do about it. The log 
will also prevent future risks to occur or to some level accept the consequence of them if they occur. 
A detailed description of the commissioning-log. The commissioning follow the commissioning 
process throughout all the phases and will be handed over to the owner in the end to clarify the 
issues, flaws or imperfections that has been observed. The commissioning-log is continuously 
updated and in the end of every phase, the log is evaluated. Based on the evaluation a decision is 
made whether to continue into the next phase or to stay until every need is fulfilled.    
3.2.4 Planning the commissioning procedure 
The commissioning leader formulate an overall plan about the future expected commissioning 
activities. The plan has to relate in detail to the disposition and project proposals phase where the 
time periods for the activities have to be scheduled in the main schedule of the construction project.  
3.2.5 Commissioning plan 
The commissioning plan contains the necessary activities that have to be executed in the 
commissioning process. The commissioning leader is responsible for planning the whole procedure. 
The plan contains an overall expectation to all the activities in all the phases. The owner has to 
specify the project supervisors’ responsibility and their cooperation obligation according to the 
==PAGE:31==
24 
 
commissioning process. The commissioning organization have to work together with the project 
supervisors to plan how to utilize their resources best as possible to maximize the value for the 
owner. Furthermore, the commissioning plan is the key to get involved, understanding the effort 
and money required, and how to manage the commissioning process. When deciding which sub-
systems that have to be tested, a risk assessment will be a preferred way of deciding how and why 
they are tested. 
3.2.6 Budget for the commissioning process 
The Danish Standard 3090 (DS3090, 2014) does not contain a template for budgeting 
commissioning, because when it was developed commissioning is considered a normative process 
instead of a perspective process. Generally, the standard is about the process and nothing else. 
However, the budget is mainly dependent on the commissioning plan which contains all the 
different activities that have to be performed and which sub-systems that is implemented in the 
different tests. Typically, commissioning will give the most value within plumbing, heating, 
ventilation, automatics and cooling system including systems that works with these sub-systems. 
And therefore is there most value for the money within these areas.  
3.2.7 Acceptance of the completed commissioning process 
The owner has to accept the commissioning activities in the program phase and then give the 
acceptance to continue the commissioning process into the disposition and project proposals phase. 
If the owner approves the accomplished commissioning activities then this is proper documented, 
and the project can continue into the next phase.  
3.3 Disposition and project proposal phase 
The disposition and project proposal phase is where the project is accepted and verified before 
applying for a building permit. This phase is very important since the project is verified against the 
owners requirements and expectations. The verification process is executed by the commissioning 
organization.  
3.3.1 Activities 
The activities in this phase includes verification that the project fulfills the owners’ requirements 
and the purposed calculations and solutions are scrutinized to increase the quality of the project.  
The activities in the program phase is updated in case of there has been any changes. The following 
documents that are in focus is according to (DS3090, 2014): 
==PAGE:32==
25 
 
 Updating the requirements. 
 Updating the commissioning-log. 
 Updating the commissioning plan. 
The new documents in this phase is: 
 Project basis. 
 Scrutiny reports. 
 Accept document. 
3.3.2 Project basis 
The project supervisors have to document how they are going to fulfill all the owners’ requirements 
in the project basis. The project basis consists of how the project advisors are going to fulfil the 
needs from the end-users and owner. The project supervisors have the responsibility for the project 
basis where they have to update it continuously if there is any changes or supplements. 
The commissioning organization and owner have to approve the project basis to ensure the 
documentation is sufficient and has to be dated to have traceability of who made the latest changes 
and when they are made. The project basis secures the commissioning specifications and therefore 
ensures that the specifications are respected.  
3.3.3 Scrutiny of purposed project 
The commissioning organization have to scrutiny the project’s solutions to make sure that the 
owners’ requirements are fulfilled. That includes simulations and calculations incorporated in the 
construction project. The scrutiny is done on the basis of observations, project basis and the owners 
requirements. Parallel with the scrutiny process, a scrutiny report is created to document which sub-
systems that have been examined, how they are chosen, the checklists that has been used during the 
examination, a description of which documentation that is the foundation for the examination, 
participants in the scrutiny process, and lastly what the end-results are. All observations are 
registered in the commissioning-log e.g. doubts about the solutions, proposed coverage of the risks 
etc.  
3.3.4 Update of the requirements 
The owner can be aware of new requirements, changed requirements or acceptance of deviating 
from existing requirements for the project. These changes has to be made in the commissioning 
requirements specification.

3.3.5 Update of the commissioning-log 
The commissioning-log is running in parallel. As mentioned all new observations has to be added to 
the log. Furthermore, all the observations that has been resolved change status to “clarified”.

3.7 Commissioning and risk management 
Every building project has its risks whether it occurs on the areas of health and safety, activities and 
environment, which will affect the overall costs, and success of the project. The commissioning 
authority can affect the whole project, as they have the technical qualifications to combine the 
process, analysis and responses to the risks. The consequence of that can also include the 
investment and operational goals of the project. By analyzing a threat potential, the factors in focus 
are the internal, external, man-made and naturally events. Throughout this process, risk mitigation 
models and policies are developed during the program phase, a further detailed development and 
implementation and validation schedule can be integrated in the main project schedule. The main 
goal to initiate this process is to ensure that all risks and threats have been considered, highlighted 
and shared between the involved people in the project. It will give a full risk profile with the threats 
and risks than can occur during the project’s construction. According to (Weiss, 2007), the risk in a 
construction project contains the following three factors: 
 Risk to the health and safety of people. 
 Risk to the environment e.g. pollution to groundwater. 
 Risk to activity such as damage on equipment, loss of function and loss of use due to 
construction delays. 
When addressing risks in the abovementioned areas, are they combined with a cost of how much 
time and money there should be spent on mitigating the risk to a certain level. The main goal for 
commissioning is that the system and equipment work as described in the commissioning 
specification so the building will be delivered on time with the right quality. Since there is no 
projects that have unlimited resources, then the owner has to find a certain level between cost and 
risk. It is possible to do commissioning on every area if costs was not a factor, but it is important to 
look at the most critical areas including e.g. equipment, systems etc. to make sure to maximize the 
value and minimize the risks (Weiss, 2007). There is however another way to address the risks if 
the performance disappoints or if the measurements and verification is insufficient. According to 
(Mills, 2010), the following risks relates to commissioning: 
 Risk to owner 
o The investments into commissioning does not reflect in the savings. 
o There is no way to track the actual savings that was been regarding to the 
commissioning process. 
==PAGE:45==
38 
 
o The potential savings does not last. 
 Risk to energy efficiency programs 
o The savings, which are claimed, cannot be backed up by a third-party review. 
o The saving does not last and will disappear over time. 
o The negative impact on the program regarding the realization rates. 
 Risk to Regulatory Agencies 
o  The unreliability regarding the program planning and the accurate forecasting. 
As part of the commissioning process, the two practices, which are part of the commissioning 
process, is continually monitoring and verification of the systems and equipment that enhance the 
benefits of risk management. When the owner initiate a commissioning process, whether it is initial 
commissioning for new buildings or retrocommissioning for existing buildings, the estimated 
savings are rarely verified. To prevent that issue, the process of monitoring, there can be provided 
tools to monitor and track the savings, but also keep surveillance if the building keeps performing 
according the commissioning specifications. On the other hand, despite the degree of monitoring 
and verifications, if the commissioning process is omitted, it will increase the likelihood of risks 
occurring and prevent reaching the goals for the project. The commissioning process is highly 
related to handling the risks and the decisions made to treat the potential risks.   
The following section will go in depth with the procedure of how to assess risks and how to address 
them and which procedures that is effective. It will be elaborated with the theory of, among others, 
systems engineering.

4 Risk assessment for commissioning 
The commissioning process is a tool in its self to identify and monitor risks throughout the lifespan 
of the project. Risk assessment is a critical part of project management in every category within 
building constructions. Every building project is unique where risks and uncertainties can occur 
from different sources. The risk assessment of a building provides information to the decision-
makers and responsible parties about an increased understanding of the risk that can affect the 
project. The output of risk assessment is the input to the decision-making processes of the 
organization. Risk assessment is the overall process of risk identification, risk analysis and risk 
evaluation (ISO 31010:2009, 2009). Figure 10 illustrates a risk management flowchart.  
 
Figure 10 - Illustration of the area of risk assessment within risk management process (ISO 31010:2009, 2009). 
The idea and purpose of risk assessment is to provide evidence-based information and analysis, 
which will give the desired information on how to treat the risks and how to select between possible 
options. Risk assessment is not a standalone process, but is a part of risk management, which means 
that risk assessment provide a structured process that identify on how objectives can be affected, 
and analysis of the risks on terms of consequences and their probabilities before deciding whether a 
further treatment is required. According to the ISO 31010:2009, a risk assessment answers the 
following fundamental questions: 
==PAGE:47==
40 
 
 What risks can happen and why do they occur? 
 What are the potential consequences? 
 What is the probability of the risks occurring? 
 Are there any factors that can mitigate the consequence of risks and reduce the probability 
of the risk? 
 Is the level of risk acceptable and does it require further treatment? 
Risk is a wide concept with many perspectives. The traditionally definition of risk is the likelihood 
of an even occurring with a negative consequence. Risks is something every owner, project 
manager etc. will try to avoid it if possible or reduce the impact of the risk. So generally, it is seen 
as a problem (Walden, Roedler, Forsberg, Hamelin, & Shortell, 2015). Another well-accepted 
definition is “risk is an uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative 
effect on one or more project objectives such as scope, schedule, cost, and quality” (Project 
Managmenet Institute, 2013). Risks has its origins in projects that is caused by uncertainty. In this 
chapter, it is elaborated between the risks that is known that can be identified and analyzed that 
makes it possible to respond to those risks that are unknown. In big and complex projects, risks is 
accounted for since it can have consequences on the total cost. Project managers have to account for 
the uncertainty and risks on projects. There is four groups of the knowledge of the risks that will be 
elaborated later in the this paragraph. However, a known risk that is not possible to respond to is 
assigned a contingency reserve. A contingency reserve is the cost or time used to manage the risks 
within the area of known-known or known-unknown. The contingency reserve is not a random 
number, but is estimated based various risk management techniques. When it is known, it is 
identified, when it is unknown, it is equal to risks. The management reserve is then used for 
unknown risks, as it is not possible to manage these proactively. A management reserve is the cost 
or time used to manage the unidentified risks, also called “unknown-unknown”. The estimated 
money for management reserve depends on the expertise, which means if the organization has less 
experience then the uncertainty will be higher and because of that, the estimate will be higher.

project to identify the different risk sources there could occur. 
4.3.1 Identification of risk sources 
Through brainstorming gives a comprehensive list of project risk sources. This is done with the 
project team in cooperation with multidisciplinary experts who is not part of the team. The 
gathering of risk sources is traditional a free-form brainstorm as open workshop or a structured 
mass interview. The categories of the risks, as in a risk breakdown structure will be a way to 
structure a framework. The risks will therefore be identified and categorized by its type and their 
definitions will be refined (Project Managmenet Institute, 2013). The risks are registered as known 
knowns where a risk register gather all the information.  
4.3.2 Analyze the risk sources 
The risk analysis is done to get a better understanding of the risk. This approach is to provide an 
input so a decision can be made whether the risk need to be treated and about the treatment 
strategies and methods. The risk analysis consider the causes and sources of risk, their 
consequences and probability that the consequences can occur (ISO 31010:2009, 2009).  
Risk analysis include an estimation of the potential consequences that might arise from a risk event, 
and what their probabilities in order to be able to measure the level of risk. A risk event can occur 
because of different event or conditions. The focus of the risk assessment is to analyze the 
importance and the vulnerability of components of the system where the goal is to define the 
treatments which is the protection or recovery strategies if an event should occur.  
Methods of analyzing risks is either qualitative, semi-quantitative or quantitative. The degree of 
detail that is required depending upon the application, the available information and the needs for 
the project team to make a decision. Within commissioning process, the most common method 
when gathering data, is the qualitative assessment, which defines the consequence, probability and 
the level of risks by categorizing them as “low”, “medium” or “high” (ISO 31010:2009, 2009).  
When assessing the risks, there is several of methods and techniques. To assist the risk assessment 
and the risk assessment process within commissioning, the most used method is the 
probability/impact matrix and Hazard and operability studies (HAZOP). In the report, a further

4.4 Risk evaluation 
When the analysis is performed and the risk sources are categorized, the next task is then to decide 
what to do about them. The evaluation is to make decisions about them. The four different terms 
from Figure 16 are: 
 Accept the risk – and plan to respond to the risks event.  
 
 Externalize the risk – down to the sub-contractors. This is only done if the contractor has a 
better position to manage the risk than the principal does because they have either more 
information or greater managerial capability. If the contractor is not in that position it will 
generate another risk since the sub-contractor is not able the handle the risk and therefore 
return to the principal.  
 Mitigate the risk by change the project mission or scope to minimize the risk event 
occurring. This often the way to respond to the risk source which also the reason to start 
early in the project life cycle.  
 Insure or hedge against the risk to the extent it is possible. With the low probability, which 
will include indicate rare catastrophes such is fire. Then will usually be possible get a 
insurance (Winch, 2009). 
The decisions can include if the risk has to be treated, how the priorities are, if an activity should be 
undertaken or which number of paths should be followed. There is however three common groups 
to divide the risk into. The three severity groups:  
 The risk is intolerable and the risk treatment is essential no matter what the costs are. 
 The risk is in the middle where the cost and benefits is considered. The opportunities are 
balanced against the potential consequences.  
 The risk is in the low category and there is no need to treat the risk.  
When assessing the risks, the high risks is in general a potential harm and therefore must be reduced 
until the cost is disproportionate to the benefit gained. This means that the cost to reduce the risks 
has to be beneficial, so the owner gets value for the money.

5.1 Introduction 
Systems engineering (SE) is a discipline that focuses on the design and the application of the whole 
system. SE is covering the big perspective of the system and its interaction with other systems and 
the environment, but as well from the inside. Systems engineering is covering most facets and 
variables. That contain from the social aspect to the technical aspects of the system. SE is an 
interdisciplinary approach, which means the realization of complex systems. This includes the 
identification of the costumers’ needs and the required functionality in the development cycle and 
documenting requirements (Kossiakoff, Sweet, Seymour, & Biemer, 2011). The following elements 
in projects that SE contains is the operations, costs and schedule, performance, training and support, 
test, manufacturing and disposal. SE cover the business aspects and the technical needs from the 
costumers and at the same time providing a system that will meet the end users’ needs. The bottom 
of line of how SE can be explained as an interdisciplinary, iterative, socio-technical discipline, and 
cover the project as a wholeness.  
SE perspective is based on systems thinking. System thinking appears through discovery, learning, 
diagnosis, and dialog. Those elements lead to sensing, modelling, talking about the real world to get 
a better understanding, define, and work with systems. System thinking helps getting the overall 
perspective of reality and will enhance the understanding how the different elements in the system 
as whole interrelate. A systems thinker knows how the system fits in a larger context of a daily life, 
how they behave and how to manage them. The essential properties of a systems thinker is as 
following (Walden, Roedler, Forsberg, Hamelin, & Shortell, 2015): 
 Will always seek to understand the big picture. 
 Observing how the elements regarding a system change over time, how it generating 
patterns and trends. 
 Recognizes how a systems’ structure have impact on behavior. 
 Identify circular nature of complex cause-effect relationships.  
 Surfaces and tests assumptions. 
==PAGE:59==
52 
 
 Change the perspective to achieve better understanding. 
 Think through an issue before making any conclusions. 
 Use the benefit from system structure to identify the possible leverage actions. 
 Thinking in short- and long term consequences of the actions. 
 Discovers where unintended. 
The process of SE is as mentioned above, has an iterative nature, which supports learning and 
continuous improvement. As the processes unfold, the system engineers reveal the real 
requirements and the evolving properties of the system. When dealing with complex projects the 
risk factor for the occurrence of unexpected and unpredictable behavior of systems is increased. 
Thus, an important objective is to minimize the undesirable consequences. Experienced experts 
across the relevant disciplines, which are coordinated by system engineers, can address this problem 
(Walden, Roedler, Forsberg, Hamelin, & Shortell, 2015).

engineers has assess if the modifications will conflict with the specifications for the system.

5.4 Risk assessment approach within systems engineering 
The risk assessment approach for systems engineering is according to ISO 31010, Risk management 
– Risk assessment techniques (ISO 31010:2009, 2009). The guidance is there is 31 different tools 
and techniques, and as described earlier, the probability/impact matrix and HAZOP is among them. 
This paragraph will highlight the important aspects within systems engineering which can be 
adopted to the commissioning process. The approaches for handling risks according to (Walden, 
Roedler, Forsberg, Hamelin, & Shortell, 2015) is: 
 Avoidance of the risk through change of requirements or design. 
 Accept the risk, and take no further action. 
 Spend the available resources to reduce the likelihood of risks. 
 Transfer the risks to the right party, which has the expertise to mitigate the occurrence of the 
risk.  
When dealing with commissioning, the biggest focus rely on the technical solutions. When dealing 
with high-risk technical tasks, risks avoidance is often insufficient, but can be covered by following 
approaches according to (Walden, Roedler, Forsberg, Hamelin, & Shortell, 2015): 
 Early procurement. 
 Initiation of parallel developments. 
 Extensive analysis and testing. 
 Contingency planning. 
When dealing with high complexity technical solutions, it can have high impact on the schedule and 
costs. If any difficulties with the system and equipment, it have high impact on the schedule and 
costs. The schedule risk can be controlled by early procurement of long-lead items and 
requirements for parallel-path developments. By executing these activities, it will result in increased 
early costs. When testing and analyzing the system, useful data will be provided that can be used to 
make key decisions. Finally, the contingency plan involves evaluating alternative risk mitigation 
options.  
==PAGE:71==
64 
 
5.4.1 Risk assessment during life cycle phases 
Many activities, projects and products have a life cycle, which starts from an initial concept and 
definition and end up with a decommissioning and disposal of hardware. Risk assessment can be 
applied to all stages of a life cycle and can applied many times in each phase and the level of detail 
can variate to assists the decisions than are needed to be made. Each life cycle phase has different 
requirements and therefore needs different techniques (ISO 31010:2009, 2009). An example could 
be in the program phase within the commissioning process where it is decided whether to continue 
to next phase depending on the level of measurable details in the commissioning specifications. 
During the design and development phase, risks assessment contributes to: 
 Ensure the system risks are acceptable. 
 Refinement process of the design. 
 Cost effectiveness studies. 
 Identify risks that affect the following phases. 
When the activities proceeds, the risk assessment can provide information that can assist developing 
procedures for normal and emergency conditions.  
5.4.2 Documentation of risk assessment 
Documentation is one of the strongest tools within risk assessment, but also in general when 
information sharing between stakeholders have to be on a certain level to keep them satisfied. The 
risks assessment process have to be documented together with the results. The documentation has to 
be fit the recipient, so it have to in understandable terms, and the level of risk has to be clear in the 
documentation. The level of detail in the documentation is described in detail in ISO 31010 (ISO 
31010:2009, 2009). The risk assessment have to performed and documented in a way that it can be 
updated throughout all the phases in the project. They purpose of that, is the assessment have to be 
updated when new information is available in the project.

8 Value for building commissioning 
The Danish Standard (DS3090, 2014) does not directly introduce risk assessment methods for 
construction projects. To support the current commissioning guidelines, an application of systems 
engineering to support the standard and the commissioning process guide (Byggeproces & 
Realdania, 2013). There is various risk assessment tools, methods and techniques and depending on 
the complexity of the problem, the degree of uncertainty based on the amount of information 
available, the extend of resources required in terms of time, level of expertise, data needs or cost. 
When taking construction projects into perspective, not all the tools, methods and techniques are 
appropriate for construction projects.  Within the construction business, three overall methods is 
common used when assessing risk in the project, which will fit for the commissioning process.  
==PAGE:81==
74 
 
8.1 Information gathering 
The first method to gather information is structured interviews and brainstorming which can be 
carried out in for example a workshop to gather information about potential risks, needs etc. The 
method is time-consuming and expensive, since the planning and execution of the workshop is 
comprehensive. This method is strongly applicable to identify risks, but is applicable in the risks 
analysis and risk evaluation. Another method is to make structured interviews as a gathering tool. 
This method requires less preparation and allows more freedom for a conversation to discover 
issues. The application of the method fits in situations where participants cannot be part of a 
workshop.   
8.2 HAZOP 
The HAZOP technique is a structured and systematic examination of the system. The technique 
identify risks to people, equipment, environment and/or organizational objectives. With this 
technique, it is possible to provide a solution for treating the risks. The HAZOP process is a 
qualitative technique where a multi-disciplinary team evaluating the operation condition in the 
design and system. The HAZOP process takes the design and specifications of the system into 
review and scrutinize the project with in mind what potential causes and what consequences it 
might have if the risks occur. The process examine every part of the system and design. To execute 
this process, the normal steps for a HAZOP study will include the following steps: 
 Nomination of the person who is responsible for the study. 
 Define the scope and objectives. 
 Define the HAZOP study team. The team is multi-disciplinary and the design and operation 
staff shall participate in the technical expertise to evaluate the current design. It is however 
best to involve a third party team members who has no part of the design or system, process 
or procedures which are reviewed. 
 Establish guidewords for the study, so when examine the system, the team can use the words 
to identify possible deviations. An illustrated example of guidewords appears from ISO 
31010 (ISO 31010:2009, 2009). 
 Collection of required documentation. 
The facilitated workshop will be in cooperation with the team that scrutinize the project. The 
processes, which that HAZOP involves is: 
 Splitting the system into sub-systems and scrutinize the system. 
==PAGE:82==
75 
 
 Divide the system into sub-system and use the guidewords to see possible deviations, which 
will have undesirable outcomes. 
 The identified deviations is evaluated and a cause and consequence is stated and a 
suggestion of how to treat them occurring or mitigate the consequences if they should occur. 
 Document the results with specific action to treat the identified risks. 
This method is identified as one of the best approaches when scrutinizing a project, and therefore 
strongly applicable as a suggested tool.  
8.3 Probability/impact matrix 
The last method that is applicable to the commissioning process is described in section 4.3.2, which 
is the probability/impact matrix to rank the risks sources. This will give the best overview of the 
risks. This method give an indication of the likelihood and consequence of the risk. A prioritizing of 
risks can hereafter be categorized whether to take action or just accept the risk.

9 Conclusion 
In this chapter a summarization of the thesis main points, results and suggestions that is stated in 
chapter 1. The main aim of the thesis was to look into how commissioning is practiced versus the 
theoretical process. Building commissioning is still relatively new in Denmark and the focus is how 
ensure high quality of buildings and its systems. Furthermore is the  aim for this thesis to clarify 
how risk assessment for commissioning is carried out and which approaches that is used, how 
systems engineering practices can support commissioning, follow an ongoing project to see how the 
guidelines is followed and how risk assessment is approached, advantages and disadvantages with 
current working processes and how the commissioning process can be improved.  
A new era for commissioning in Denmark happened in 2014 when Dansk Standard released the first 
commissioning process standard DS 3090:2014. The standard includes all the relevant phases and 
activities that are minimum requirements for the commissioning process. The building project, 
AT2, performs Sweco the commissioning process where their main responsibility is to scrutinize 
the project, to maintain and update the commissioning log, take control over the commissioning 
procedures, site inspections, test of the systems and to preparation of documentation. The 
commissioning stage that the project is in today is currently in the main project phase where the 
latest activity was an information meeting with the participants where it was clarified how much 
they are involved in the commissioning process and what is expected from them. The working 
practice from Sweco is in accordance with their current situation due to the late involvement of the 
project and fulfill the minimum requirements.  
Utilizing systems engineering to this project is to enhance the connection between risk assessment 
and building commissioning. Systems engineering practices can handle high complexed projects, 
which is relevant for new buildings today. The approach to manage complexed systems cover the 
full aspect from stakeholder analysis to the system is decommissioned. The risk assessment 
approach within systems engineering correlates with the international standard ISO 31010. Hence is 
the reason for the approach of handling risk assessment based on the systems engineering approach. 
The application of systems engineering works universally whether it is products, systems, 
equipment etc.  
The advantages with the current working processes is that commissioning is a comprehensive 
quality control process that e.g. ensure that the engineering project have no flaws and imperfections. 
Furthermore is major flaws discovered in an early phase and the owner save money in the long-term 
==PAGE:85==
78 
 
even though there is a higher expense in the program phase. The disadvantages of the 
commissioning process are that the participants in the activities sees the commissioning authority as 
police officers that does not trust their work. With the lack of structure of risk assessment within the 
commissioning process is that incompetent companies, which does not know how to run a 
commissioning process, can deliver an unsatisfied product and thereby put commissioning in a bad 
light.  
The contribution from systems engineering have delivered and understanding of how to approach 
complex projects and how to combine risk assessment and building commissioning. The findings 
included the importance of stakeholder analysis and what benefits there is to perform such an 
activity. Furthermore, is there provided knowledge of how to clarify the potential risks and how to 
categorize them. Moreover, a suggestion on how to enhance the scrutinizing method by following 
an international standard, ISO 31010, to assess potential failures in the system.
