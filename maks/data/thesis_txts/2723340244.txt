Abstract
Simultaneous detection of weeds and joint stem is essential for precise farming
and effective weed control. However, weed detection has received significant at-
tention and researches, joint stem detection has been relatively neglected. In this
thesis, we address this research gap by presenting a multi-task learning model
that enables real-time simultaneous detection of both weed and joint stem for agri-
cultural robots. We begin by developing our own baseline model, YOLO-HRNet,
which is a two-stage model inspired by Mask R-CNN. YOLO-HRNet combines
YOLOv5 with HRNet for end-to-end training of weed and joint stem detection task.
Then we propose our advanced model, YOLO-Pose, which is a one-stage model
that can detect both weed and joint stem simultaneously in real time. To eval-
uate our models, we conducted experiments on the RumexWeeds dataset with
grassland settings. The final results supports our three claims: Firstly, compared
to single task of weed detection utilizing YOLOv5, both YOLO-HRNet and YOLO-
Pose achieves improved mAP on weed detection by leveraging multi-task learning
method. Secondly, both YOLO-HRNet and YOLO-Pose achieve the aim of simul-
taneous detection for weed and joint stem keypoint. Thirdly, our advanced model,
YOLO-Pose, not only achieves both higher mAP for weed detection and higher
OKS for joint stem detection but also achieves impressive detection speed with a
minimum of 88.5 FPS on GPU Tesla V100 with respect to the two-stage baseline
model, proving its superior performance on real-time simultaneous detection of
both weed and joint stem for agricultural robots.
Keywords: multi-task learning, weed and joint stem detection, YOLO-HRNet,
YOLO-Pose, real-time simultaneous detection, ablation study.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In today’s world, agricultural robots play a crucial role in promoting sustainable
development [1]. Weeding robots have emerged as one of the most successful
and widely utilized robots in the field of agriculture, contributing significantly to
the growth and profitability of industry. By leveraging the capabilities of weed-
ing robots, farmers can improve efficiency, reduce labor costs, minimize pesticide
usage, enhance crop health, and promote sustainability, revolutionizing the way
farmers manage their crops and contributing to a sustainable future [2].
There are mainly two steps for the operation of weeding robots. Firstly, precise
detection of weeds is executed. Secondly, these detected weeds are cut by the
mechanical structure of robots. For weed detection task, object detection models
based on Convolutional Neural Networks(CNN) are proposed for precise detec-
tion and have achieved great success [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9]. However, for weeds
removal, due to the difficulties of finding the precise position of roots, i.e.the joint
stem, classical methods, such as tube-stamp which utilizes a mechanical stamp
surrounded by a tube to destroy weed by pushing it into the soil, have been proved
to be not suitable for all types of weed species especially for grass weeds [10],
since the root of weed cannot be removed completely, causing weeds growing
back in the future.
With the goal of complete weeds removal, models for accurate joint stem detection
is compulsory. Nevertheless, the topic of joint stem detection has not been widely
investigated. Most of researches and attention are given to crop/weed detection
and segmentation based on laboratory or farmland settings [6, 7, 11, 12, 8, 9, 13],
the models from which are not suitable for joint stem detection on grassland. Addi-
tionally, although these proposed models in [6, 7, 8, 9] can achieve simultaneous
weed and joint stem detection, their inference speed is extremely slow, making it
impossible for real-time application on robots. Therefore, research is needed to
develop a fast and accurate simultaneous weed and joint stem detection model
that can be utilized in real time by agricultural robots.
The main contribution of this thesis project is twofold. Firstly, we show the case
that Multi-task Learning is a viable approach to boost performance in the weed de-
tection task by integrating joint stem detection as the second task. Secondly, two
detection models, which can achieve both weed detection and joint stem detec-
tion tasks end-to-end, are developed to address the existing research gap in the
field of joint stem detection on grassland and the challenge of real-time simulta-
neous detection of both weed and joint stem keypoint for agricultural robots. The
baseline model, YOLO-HRNet, is an end-to-end two-stage model combining the

1.2
Related work
Vision-based systems for precise farming, including crop/weed classification [8, 9,
16], detection [6, 7, 12] and stem localization [6, 7, 8, 9, 13], have been greatly
developed and achieved impressive success in recent years. Classical methods
rely on handcraft features extraction [13], while recent state-of-art methods utilize
Convolutional Neural Networks(CNNs) to learn hierarchical features directly from
annotated images [17].
Crop/Weed Detection. In the field of crop/weed classification, classical vision-
based methods were proposed in the past [6, 18, 11]. A two-stage model to differ-
entiate crops and weeds utilizing RGB and infra-red(NIR) images are proposed
by [11], where plants are detected by random forest classifier based on handcraft
features [6].
Recently, Convolutional Neural Networks have been applied to build up detection
models. Near real-time classification of sugar beets and weeds are achieved by
the model detecting individual plants as connected components and feeding each
of them to a CNN [16, 6]. [18] proposes a system of fruit detection to enhance yield
mapping and robotic harvesting [6]. But this method is based on Faster R-CNN
[19], which is a two-stage model with low inference speed. [3, 4, 5] researched
the detection models for Rumex weeds for UAV application. However, they only
proposed methods for weeds detection without root localization.
Joint Stem Detection. Most of the joint stem detection methods are conducted in
farmland or laboratory environments [6, 7, 12, 8, 9, 13]. For classical vision-based
method, a two-stage vision-based algorithm, called Geometrical Stem Detection
[13], is proposed for the task of both vegetation detection and joint stem localiza-
tion. Firstly vegetation mask is obtained to separate each leaf of plants. Then the
keypoints of each leaf defining the direction are utilized for final joint stem localiza-
tion. This method relies on handcraft features extraction instead of deep learning.
However, these handcraft features are always geared toward plants in specific
==PAGE:17==
application and suffer from tweaking of parameters to be adapted to various sit-
uation [9]. Additionally, although Geometrical Stem Detection can achieve near
real-time detection speed, its performance is reduced owing to the limitations that
visual noise can be observed in provided mask images [13].
Neural network architecture containing one encoder and two decoders is pro-
posed to achieve the goal of simultaneous joint plant detection, semantic seg-
mentation and joint stem localization utilizing Convolutional Neural Network [8].
In this method, features of input RGB image are extracted by encoder and then
the encoded feature volume will be passed to task-specific decoder to get final
predictions [8]. In following improvements, spatio-temporal decoder is added af-
ter encoder and features from spatio-temporal decoder will be merged with those
features from each task-specific decoder in order to improve model robustness
and performance by incorporating spatial information through considering consec-
utive image sequences of local field strips [9]. However, the inference speed of
this method is extremely slow [8, 9], making it impossible for real-time application.
[6] proposed a one-stage network which predicts bounding box of crops/weeds
based on CenterNet [20] and simultaneously detects leaf keypoints via heatmaps
for plant detection and leaf-count estimation [6]. But this method also suffers from
slow inference speed [6]. [7] proposed a new algorithm for stem detection and
tracking. This two-stage method firstly utilizes YOLOv4 for fast stem detection,
then a temporal aggregation algorithm is leveraged to refine the stem predictions
further by taking advantages of the temporal redundancy of detection caused by
overlap of successive images [7]. Although this two-step method improved the
detection speed [7], it still cannot be utilized for real-time detection. Besides, the
position of joint stem is predicted with bounding box format instead of keypoints
[7]. Similar to [8], [21] proposed an architecture with one shared backbone, but
two separated encoder paths to generate segmentation masks for segmenting
weeds as well as keypoint predictions for joint stems, based on heatmaps. Addi-
tionally, they provide the feature maps from the weed segmentation path to the
joint stem prediction path and claim to increase accuracy by doing that. However,
their architecture only handles input images including single plants as objects
In contrast, firstly, our experiments are conducted based on grassland environ-
ments, which is more challengeable compared to farmland or laboratory settings
mentioned above since weeds on grassland are much more difficult to differenti-
ate and joint stem is always covered by adjacent weeds or grasses. But satisfying
detection accuracy can still be achieved based on our model. Additionally, our
model bridges the gap of simultaneously weed detection and joint stem detection
in real-time with an end-to-end model in the field of agriculture robots. Compared
to the slow detection speed of all previous discussed models, inference speed
of at least 88.5 FPS on GPU Tesla V100 can be accomplished by our manner,
making it possible for real-time application. Finally, no parameters tweaking and
no post-processing operations are required when conducting inference procedure
with our single-stage model, leading to robust and efficient utilization.

1.3
Structure of Thesis
This thesis is structured into seven chapters: Introduction, Background, Basic
Theory, Dataset, Approach and Methodology, Experiments and Evaluation, Con-
clusion. Figure 1.1 depicts the structure of thesis.
Introduction. The chapter outlines the motivation of this thesis and related work
in the field of agricultural robots, and provides an overview of the thesis structure.
Background. This chapter firstly covers the development of object detection,
where comparison between two-stage and one-stage detection models is con-
ducted. Then it discusses keypoint detection methods. The chapter also provides
an overview of multi-task learning models.
Basic Theory. This chapter firstly introduces the theoretical knowledge of deep
learning and convolutional neural network. Then the details of YOLOv5 and HR-
Net, which are all utilized in approach organization, are discussed carefully.
Dataset. This chapter provides an overview of the dataset utilized in this thesis,
including file structure, annotations for weed and joint stem keypoints detection
and data separation.
Approach and Methodology. This chapter covers detailed explanation of how
both the YOLO-HRNet baseline model and the advanced YOLO-Pose model are
organized and work.
Experiments and Evaluation. This chapter displays the results of overfitting
experiments on a small dataset for model verification, and provides results of ab-
lation study with single-class experiments on the whole RumexWeeds dataset uti-
lizing both YOLO-HRNet and YOLO-Pose. The chapter also compares YOLOv5,
YOLO-HRNet, and YOLO-Pose to demonstrate the improvements introduced by
multi-task learning method.
Conclusion. The chapter summarizes the conclusions of the experiments and
discusses future improvements.

Background
2.1
Object detection
2.1.1
Traditional method
Before the advent of Graphic Processing Units(GPUs), deep neural networks were
infeasible for running on computers and humans lacked the ability to extract high-
level features from images [22]. Traditional object detection methods relied on
extracting low-level features without deep learning and are mainly composed of
three steps [23].
Firstly, several candidate regions need to be proposed for an image. Normally,
these small regions are framed by traversing the whole image with sliding win-
dows [23]. Secondly, local scale-invariant features in each candidate region are
extracted by computer vision algorithms. SIFT [24], SURF [25], ORB [26], HOG
[27] and other methods for extracting low-level features are utilized in this step
and the quality of extracted features is extremely important for processing the next
step. The final stage is to build up a classifier, such as SVM [28] and AdaBoost
[29], to classify and separate object categories in terms of extracted features.
Representative algorithms, like HOG+SVM detection [27] and DPM [30], showed
limited success at that time. However, since only low-level features are utilized,
these detection models cannot achieve high mean Average Precision(mAP) even
with more training data [23]. Additionally, candidate regions are proposed based
on sliding windows traversing the whole image, making traditional algorithms suf-
fer from long processing time, making it infeasible for real-time detection [23].
2.1.2
Two-stage model for object detection
Successful application of neural networks on computers has revolutionized com-
puter vision algorithms. Convolutional Neural Network(CNN), in particular, allows
human to extract high-level features from images, leading to improved perfor-
mance in tasks like semantic segmentation, object detection, and keypoints detec-
tion. The Regions with CNN features(R-CNN) [31] family (R-CNN, Fast R-CNN
[32], and Faster R-CNN [19]) were the first proposed object detection models
based on CNN and consists of two stages: region proposal and object detection.
R-CNN is a simple and scalable detection algorithm that the mAP on VOC 2012
dataset is improved by more than 30% relative to previous best results predicted
by traditional methods [31]. Firstly, in order to decrease the complexity of gener-
ating regions by sliding window traversal, selective search [33] is utilized to gen-
erates around 2000 category-independent proposals for each input image. Then
affined image warping is utilized to compute fixed-CNN inputs from each propos-

als, regardless of different shapes of regions. Thirdly, a large convolutional neu-
ral network is utilized to extract fixed-length feature vectors of each warped pro-
posal. Finally, in terms of these feature vectors, category-specific linear SVMs
with bounding box regressors are utilized for classification and regression. How-
ever, although better mAP can be achieved by R-CNN compared to other classical
methods, there are still some drawbacks. One the one hand, training of R-CNN is
a complex and multi-stage pipeline. Convolutional network firstly will be fine-tuned
using a log loss. Additionally, SVMs which works as object detectors for each cat-
egory and bounding box regressors will also need to be tuned. With lacking of
ability of ”end-to-end” training, training so many networks could be costly in both
space and time [32]. On the other hand, feature vectors are extracted by each
proposal forward passing CNN without computation sharing, causing R-CNN ex-
tremely slow [32].
Figure 2.2: Structure of fast R-CNN [32]
Fast R-CNN is a more efficient and effective object detection algorithm compared
to its predecessor R-CNN. Firstly, unlike R-CNN extracting features from each
proposal, fast R-CNN computes feature map of the whole image in the begin-
ning and then separates the corresponding region of each proposal in the feature
map. Since there are 2000 proposals from selective search, among which over-
lapping areas are contained, leading to duplicated calculation when extracting
features, new extraction method can accelerate computation speed [32]. Sec-
ondly, Regions of Interest(ROI) pooling, is utilized to warp region proposals with
different shapes into a fixed size. Thirdly, after convolutional neural network, a
fully-connected network, replacing the SVMs and regressors in R-CNN, is utilized
for accomplishing classification and regression. In conclusion, Fast R-CNN ex-
tracts feature map of the whole image in the beginning and replaces SVMs and
==PAGE:23==
regressors with only a single neural network, making it possible for one-step train-
ing. Compared to R-CNN, fast R-CNN is a true two-stage model for object de-
tection. The first step in fast R-CNN is obtaining region proposals using selective
search and the second step is a one-stage neural network for classification and
regression. Fast R-CNN is 9 times faster in training time than R-CNN [32], but
still struggles with long detection time due to the time-consuming region proposal
step [19].
Figure 2.3: Structure of faster R-CNN [19]
Faster R-CNN is the latest iteration of the R-CNN family and is capable of pro-
cessing frames nearly in real-time due to its utilization of Region Proposal Net-
work(RPN). Selective search is an order of magnitude slower with 2 seconds per
frame on CPU implementation [19]. The authors of faster R-CNN note that region-
based CNN takes full use of GPUs but region proposal method only runs on CPU
instead, which makes run-time comparisons inequitable. To solve this issue, RPN
is introduced as a solution to speed up proposal generation and achieves ”end-
to-end” training of faster R-CNN. RPN takes an image with any size as input and
outputs a set of rectangle object proposals [19]. To generate proposals, a small
network sliding over the feature map from the last convolutional layer is utilized.
A sliding window of 3 × 3 will be used to traverse the whole feature map and
each window will be mapped to a lower-dimension vector, which is then passed
through two sibling fully-connected layers: one for box regression and the other
for objectness detection. The regression layer outputs 4k predictions encoding
the coordinates of k boxes, while the objectness layer outputs 2k scores repre-
senting foreground and background possibilities. Then, these proposals will be
adjusted and fed into the second neural network, which is the same as that in
fast R-CNN, to complete detection task. By introducing RPN to replace selective
search for region proposals generation, the training of faster R-CNN becomes an
end-to-end task. Additionally, faster R-CNN is capable of processing around 22
fps, nearly fitting the requirements of real-time detection [19].
2.1.3
One-stage model for object detection
Two-shot models have shown high accuracy in object detection tasks, however,
their processing speed is limited to 25 FPS at best due to the region proposal
shot, making them unsuitable for real-time detection [19, 34]. One-shot models,
in contrast, can detect categories in each frame quickly with a slight trade-off in
accuracy [35]. SSD [36], CenterNet [20], and YOLO [35] are some representa-
tive one-shot models for object detection. Among them, YOLO family is the most
==PAGE:24==
widely utilized due to its high efficiency and stability, with many updated versions
released after YOLOv1 .
YOLOv1 is the first iteration of the YOLO family and is renowned for its real-time
object detection capabilities. Compared with two-shot models, YOLOv1 predicts
both object category and bounding box coordinates through regression, eliminat-
ing the need for region proposals [35]. In YOLOv1, firstly, images are divided
into S × S grids, each of which is responsible for predicting objects that are cen-
tered within it. Secondly, predictions, including 4K predictions for box coordinates,
K predictions for object confidence, and K × C predictions for category scorer
are generated. As all detection results are predicted by regression without any
region-proposals, YOLOv1 achieves fast detection speed but with low accuracy
or missed detection in crowded scenes [35].
YOLOv2 introduces some updates to YOLOv1 to solve the problem of missed
detection. Firstly, since the bounding box predictions in YOLOv1 is predicted di-
rectly by fully-connected layers, it is difficult for YOLOv1 to learn how to fit different
shapes of objects and how to predict crowded objects, causing low recall per-
formance. Therefore, fully-connected layers in YOLOv1 are replaced by anchor
boxes derived from k-means cluster algorithm [38]. These anchor boxes help fit
objects with different shapes, including crowded objects. Thus, only the offsets
of center point, height and width for each anchor box need to be predicted. Addi-
tionally, batch normalization [39] is utilized after each convolution layer and pass-
through layers, which concatenates low-level features with high-level features, are
incorporated in YOLOv2 to improve small object detection accuracy. With these
updates, YOLOv2 can achieve better mAP compared to Faster R-CNN while still
providing real-time implementation.
YOLOv3 incorporates Darknet-53 architecture as its backbone and Spatial Pyra-
mid Pooling(SPP) block is introduced for multi-scale feature fusion [37]. Despite
of the utilization of pass-through layers in YOLOv2 for small objects detection en-
hancement, there are still some space for improving. Thus, a new detection head
that predicts bounding boxes on three different scaled feature maps instead of
just a single feature map, is utilized in YOLOv3 to replace pass-through layers,
which enables YOLOv3 to detect objects with a variety of shapes and sizes more
effectively [37]. The structure of YOLOv3 is depicted by Figure 2.4
In YOLOv4, Cross Stage Partion(CSP) block and Path Aggregation Network(PAN)
[40] are utilized to aggregate features from multiple scales, which helps with im-
proving the accuracy of small object detection [41]. Additionally, new anchor de-
sign strategy based on k-means clustering and features scales, which helps to
improve detection accuracy and stability further, is also incorporated. All these
modifications help YOLOv4 achieve even better accuracy and real-time perfor-
mance compared to all previous YOLO models.

YOLOv5 [14] adds some tricks to update model structure of YOLOv4 to improve
efficiency. Firstly, a 6 × 6 convolution kernel is utilized in backbone to replace the
original focus block for fast computation. Secondly, new SPP block, called SPPF,
is introduced to speed up model processing time by operating a serial of convo-

Finally, in order to balance the losses on three scaled detection layers, different
weights are assigned to different losses.
2.2
Keypoint detection
Keypoint detection, also known as pose estimation, used probabilistic graphical
or pictorial structure models prior to the advent of deep learning [42, 43]. With
the development of deep learning, especially the convolutional neural network,
==PAGE:26==
keypoint detection has been revolutionized. Currently, there are two main ap-
proaches: regression-based and heatmap-based methods.
2.2.1
Regression-based method
keypoint detection is naturally a regression problem. However, the common loss
function for regression, Mean Square Error(MSE) loss, is not effective for training
keypoint detection models, since oscillations will be introduced by MSE loss dur-
ing backpropagation, leading to difficulty in convergence [44, 45]. As a result, the
accuracy of model trained with regression-based method is always lower that that
of heatmap-based model. Thus, regression-based method receives less research
and attention [44, 45]. By taking full use of heatmap and regression, integral pose
[46] ignores post-processing and quantization error problems but has been con-
firmed to contain potential bias compared to models with only regression method
[47]. RLE [48] is a regression-based method that has comparable performance
with heatmap-based model but its backbone is trained by heatmap losses. In con-
clusion, the problem of regression-based method exists in finding an appropriate
criteria that can be constrained within fixed range to avoid oscillation.
2.2.2
Heatmap-based method
Heatmap-based method for keypoint detection requires heatmap-formatted ground
truth to estimate likelihoods for each pixel position. In heatmpap-based method,
MSE loss is utilized for training, but a number of positive samples are compul-
sory to avoid data imbalance issues when computing loss. This method is easier
to converge and has gained popularity. SHnet [49] and HRNet [15] create new
backbone to keep high-resolution features for heatmap supervision. Despite the
efficient training and implement, heatmap-based method suffers from quantization
error owing to numbers of down-sample layers and it is difficult to be improved for
more accurate predictions [44, 45].
2.3
Multi-task learning
Multi-task learning (MTL) is a popular research topic and has demonstrated suc-
cess in various applications including speech recognition and computer vision [50].
Generally, MTL involves optimizing multiple loss functions for two or more tasks
simultaneously [50]. It improves generalization by leveraging the domain-specific
information contained in the training signals of related tasks [51]. There are mainly
three advantages for utilizing multi-task learning. Firstly, implicit data augmenta-
tion and attention focusing can be introduced by MTL. By training related tasks
together, some parameters will be shared to help models learn the exact meaning-
ful features. The reason why useful features are learned is that all tasks are full of
noise in real world and different tasks will contain unequal noise patterns. Thus,
a model containing multiple tasks can learn more general feature representations
[50]. Secondly, MTL can propose eavesdropping effects [51, 50]. Some features
X are easily learned for task A but difficult to learn by task B because A can
extract such features with more complicated manners or there are other features
==PAGE:27==
preventing task B learning features X [50]. By leveraging MTL, both tasks can
share features they learned. Finally, MTL can work as a new regularizer since
the new task added in the model brings an inductive bias, which can reduce the
risk of overfitting during training procedure [50].Normally, there are two network
structures for MTL: one is called hard-parameter sharing and the other is called
soft-parameter sharing.
2.3.1
Hard-parameter sharing structure
Figure 2.5: Structure of hard-parameter sharing methods [50]
Hard-parameter sharing is the most common structure of MTL and widely used by
many applications like adversarial networks [52, 53] and joint learning [6, 7, 8, 9].
The main idea of hard-parameter sharing is that all tasks keep their task-specific
output heads with utilizing the same backbone. The advantages of this method
are that firstly, the risk of overfitting is greatly reduced and the more tasks are
trained simultaneously, the less probability of overfitting is [50]. Secondly, this
method is easy to converge during training. However, owing to the fact that only
backbone is shared, shared parameters are constrained [50].
2.3.2
Soft-parameter sharing structure
Soft-parameter sharing is a totally different method compared to hard-parameter
sharing. In soft-parameter sharing, each task has its own backbone and output
head. Then each hidden layer in backbone of all tasks will be connected by small
neural networks to share parameters [50]. The weights in these connected layers
==PAGE:28==
Figure 2.6: Structure of soft-parameter sharing methods [50]
can also be updated during training procedure and the distance of all parame-
ters in backbones can also be regularized by such as l2 distance [54] or trace
norm [55] to encourage parameters to be similar [50]. Cross-connected network
[56] and cross-stitch network [57] are two representative examples utilizing soft-
parameters sharing structure to combine object detection task with semantic seg-
mentation task. Although soft-parameter sharing shares and connects much more
hidden layers and parameters, sometimes it is difficult to converge during training
[50]. Additionally, it is difficult to choose how many hidden layers should be shared.
Thus soft-parameter sharing adjustment becomes an experience-based task.

3.1
Deep Learning
Efficient GPU computing has fueled widespread adoption of deep learning, revolu-
tionizing various industries. Unlike traditional learning ways which are processed
with multi stages including feature engineering, selection, extraction(e.g.PCA [58])
, construction(e.g.SIFT [24]) and machine learning, deep learning eliminates the
multi-step procedure by providing an ”end-to-end” training solution [59]. Further-
more, deep neural networks benefits from learning hierarchical representations of
data, including low-level, mid-level, and high-level features, outperforming tradi-
tional methods limited by low-level features extraction.

numerous hidden units. The value of jth unit in (i + 1)th hidden layer is computed
by summing the product of values from each unit of ith hidden layer multiplied by
their corresponding weights, adding a bias term, and applying an activation func-
tion.

While batch gradient descent (BGD) may converge to the global minimum of the

these limitations, advanced optimization algorithms such as Stochastic Gradient

Descent (SGD), Root Mean Square Propagation(RMSP) and Adaptive Moment

timization

Stochastic gradient descent
Stochastic gradient descent(SGD) [61] is proposed to solve the time-consuming
problem. Different from BGD, several mini-batches are separated from the whole
dataset and utilized to optimize model parameters per mini-batch.

3.2
Convolutional neural network
There are various models utilizing neural network except Fully Connected Neural
Network(FCN), such as Convolutional Neural Network(CNN), Recurrent Neural
Network(RNN) and Generative Adversarial Neural Network(GAN) [53] etc.. FCN
has mainly three disadvantages when applied on big-size images. Firstly, missing
of spatial information and features will be caused by expanding an image into a
vector representation. Secondly, as the size of image grows, the number of model
parameters in FCN also increases, leading to a slow and challenging training pro-
cess with low efficiency. Thirdly, the risk of overfitting will be raised owing to huge
parameters contained in FCN. CNN can efficiently solve these problems and has
achieved great success in the field of computer vision.
Different from FCN, CNN takes inputs as volumes, neurons in each hidden layers
of CNN are permuted as height×width×channel. Convolution kernels are where
==PAGE:36==
Figure 3.2: Convolution kernel sliding an image
the power of CNN comes from. The shape of kernel is height × width × channels,
where channels of kernel should be equal to that of outputs from last hidden layer.
The whole image will be traversed by each kernel to extract features and then
feature maps with the same number of kernels are generated. This process is
depicted by Figure 3.2. The whole image is slided by 6 convolution kernels with
size of 7 × 7 one by one to generate 6 feature maps, which will be concatenated
later and passed forward the next convolution layer. Compared to FCN, features
of the whole image are extracted by only a single kernel, which only contains 7
× 7 parameters. Thus, fewer parameters are utilized for feature extraction, re-
sulting in faster training speed and lower risk of overfitting. Besides, the size of
convolution kernel can be adjusted to acquire receptive field with various levels
of coverage, enabling the extraction of local, spatially connected features. Finally,
since parameters-sharing are also utilized in CNN, the number of parameters are
decreased further and it becomes possible for parallel training.
Figure 3.3: Compare traditional convolution with dilated convolution
In application, convolution operations are always implemented with different padding
and stride sizes. Besides stride convolution, dilated convolution is also utilized to
expand receptive field. Compared with traditional convolution, gaps in terms of
dilation rate among each element in kernel are utilized to extract more spatially
connected features in dilated convolution. This process are shown in Figure 3.3.
Convolution is a kind of downsample operations where the size of input is reduced
after generating feature maps. Inverse process can be achieved by transpose con-
==PAGE:37==
volution, where information of corresponding element in feature maps and related
convolution kernels is utilized to map the single element in current map back to
relative receptive field area in the last feature map.

3.3.1
Model structure
You Only Look Once(YOLO) [35] is a kind of one-stage objection model that can
achieve real-time detection task. YOLOv5 [14] is developed from YOLOv3 and
YOLOv4. Compared with YOLOv3 shown in Figure 2.4, a more efficient back-
bone, faster neck layer, more powerful aggregation strategy and better prediction

Neck
Figure 3.7: Structure of path aggregation network(PAN)
For neck part, SPP block in YOLOv3 is replaced by SPPF in YOLOv5 and a CSP

Compared to YOLOv3 executing max pooling operations with different kernel
sizes and padding operations to build up SPP block, a new method called SPPF,
which leverages a serial of pooling operations with the same kernel, is utilized
in YOLOv5 for promoting computation efficiency. For example, the result of two
serial pooling operation with kernel size of 5 × 5 and padding of 2 is equal to that
of a single max pooling with kernel size of 9 × 9 and padding of 4, but less com-
putation is required for the first one.
In order to merge features of different levels, path aggregation network(PAN) [40]
is introduced to YOLOv5. Figure 3.7 depicts the structure of PAN. Firstly, several
feature maps are generated by backbone. Secondly, up-bottom feature pyramid
is set up based on these feature layers, where high-level semantic features are
aggregated to low level layers. Finally, bottom-up feature pyramid is utilized to
accomplish final aggregation in opposite direction, from low-level layers to high-
level layers. In YOLOv5 application, PAN is utilized combining with CSP structure
shown in Figure 3.4.

3.3.5
Non-maximum suppression
Non-maximum suppression(NMS) is a kind of method of extracting global maxi-
mum result by suppressing local maximum values. In object detection task, lots
of candidate bounding boxes are generated for each detected object after model
inference process. Hence, NMS is utilized to extract the optimal predicted bound-
ing box and remove duplicated predictions.
The procedure of NMS is shown below:
1. Select and save the box with highest confidence score
2. Compute IoU between each box with selected box in step 1
3. Delete boxes whose IoU is bigger than threshold. These boxes represent
the same object as selected box
4. Iterate from step 1 to step 4 until no boxes left

3.4
Keypoint detection model: High-Resolution

3.4.1
Model structure
High-Resolution Net(HRNet) [15] is a kind of heatmap-based keypoint detection
model for single object pose prediction, which means only one object is contained
in the input image for HRNet. The main idea of HRNet is to aggregate the in-
formation from layers with different levels and scales. The structure of HRNet is
depicted by Figure 3.13.
Figure 3.13: Structure of HRNet [15]
Firstly, two convolution layers with 3 × 3 convolution kernel are utilized to down-
sample input image by 4×. Secondly, the number of channels is increased by 4×
without changing the size of feature map through Layer1 block, which is similar
to the Layer1 in ResNet [67] and composed of stacked Bottleneck block. Then, a
serial of Transition and Stage blocks are utilized to achieve feature aggregation.
A new scale branch will be introduced every time feature map passing forward a
Transition structure. For example, based on original two scale branches, a new
scale branch downsampled by 16× is created by applying a 3 × 3 convolution ker-
nel with stride of 2 to the scale branch downsampled by 8×. After features with
different scales are introduced by Transition structure, Stage structure is utilized
to merge these information. For instance, in Stage3, each scale branch will pass
forward four basic blocks, which is the same as that in ResNet. Then outputs of
each branch are merged from outputs of all branches.
Figure 3.14: Structure of Up block and Down block in HRNet
==PAGE:48==
Up block and Down block are utilized in Stage structure for multi-level features
alignment. In Up block, firstly, a 1 × 1 convolution layer followed with batch normal-
ization is utilized. Then the output will be upsampled by Nearest method. Down
block is a little complex compared with Up block. Feature maps are downsampled
by 2× through stride convolution. Finally, the final exchange block in Stage4 only
outputs the highest-resolution feature map which is the output of branch down-
sampled by 4×.
3.4.2
Heatmap estimation
Figure 3.15: Performance of heatmap enhancement strategy
HRNet is a heatmap-based keypoint detection model and for each category of key-
point, a heatmap will be generated to represent the predictions for current class.
Thus, MSE loss is utilized to measure the distance between prediction and ground
truth. However, if the format of ground truth is a point and only this point is consid-
ered as a positive sample in ground truth heatmap, data imbalance problem will
be sharpened shown in Figure 3.15. In order to solve this problem, a 2-D Gaus-
sian distribution around keypoint is utilized to convert ground truth to be heatmap
to increase the number of positive samples.

5.1.1
Model structure
YOLOv5 combining with HRNet is considered as the baseline model for multi-task
learning of weed and joint stem detection, the idea of which is inspired by mask R-
CNN [69] where a faster R-CNN model followed by a simple segmentation model
is leveraged for instance segmentation task. Since the input of HRNet is required
to only contain a single object while a number of predicted bounding boxes are
proposed from YOLOv5, it is meaningful and efficient to combine both two mod-
els. Hard-parameters sharing method is utilized in YOLO-HRNet. The structure
of YOLO-HRNet is depicted in Figure 5.1.
Figure 5.1: Structure of YOLO-HRNet
As shown above, The backbone are shared by both YOLOv5 and HRNet for fea-
ture extraction.
After input image passing forward the backbone and neck in
YOLOv5, three feature maps with different scales are generated. Then for ob-

utilized for weed detection. The predicted bounding boxes in each feature map
are taken as the input of HRNet to finish the task of joint stem keypoint detection.
Thus, YOLO-HRNet is a kind of two-stage models achieving end-to-end training
for weed detection and joint stem detection.

ROI pooling utilizes two rounding operations for features alignment. Firstly, since

ally, the features at each block are calculated by bilinear interpolation among sam-
ple points. Figure 5.3 depicts the process of ROI align.

YOLO-Pose
5.2.1
Model structure
YOLO-Pose is an enhanced YOLO model initially for multi-person pose estimation
[70]. Different from YOLO-HRNet, YOLO-Pose is a one-stage network for object
and keypoint detection. In YOLO-Pose, regression-based method is utilized for
keypoint detection part. The structure of YOLO-Pose is depicted in Figure 5.4.
Figure 5.4: Structure of YOLO-Pose
In YOLO-Pose, both detection head and pose head share the same backbone and
neck networks, indicating the utilization of hard-parameter sharing method. After
three feature maps generated from neck, the detection head and pose head will
work on parallel. For detection head, 1 × 1 convolution kernel will be utilized to
output predictions containing object confidence, category score and coordinates
of bounding boxes for each proposal in each grid cell.
Meanwhile, pose head will also be utilized to output the keypoint estimation. There
are two kinds of pose head structures, one called simple head and the other called
complex head. Simple head is the same as detection head where only a single
convolution layer is utilized for prediction. Complex head is composed of a serial

of convolution layers to extract underlying features for keypoint detection. The out-
puts from pose head incorporate three parts. Firstly, the confidence of keypoint
is predicted to denote the score of existence. Secondly, scores of each category
are predicted to define the class of keypoint. Finally, the coordinates of keypoint
are predicted.

but also the segmentation area of object should be paid attention. Because for the
same distance between prediction and ground truth, the criteria for small objects
should be higher than that for big objects. That is why segmentation area is also
included in OKS to balance the influence of objects with various sizes.
LOKS = 1 −OKS
(5.3)
Similar to IoU loss, OKS loss is developed from OKS and is also scaled between
0 and 1. Compared with MSE loss, when facing long distance between prediction
and ground truth, instead of generating an unstable loss from MSE, loss is still
constrained between 0 and 1 in LOKS, which decreases the risk of introducing
huge oscillation to parameters optimization. Additionally, when there is a small
gap between prediction and ground truth, small value from MSE loss will be gen-
erated, which has less effects on model learning. However, by adjusting the σ in
==PAGE:60==
OKS, meaningful loss will be produced by OKS loss to help with training.
5.2.3
Training and inference
During training process, there are three losses for object detection and also three
losses for keypoint detection model. For object detection, firstly, BCE loss is uti-
lized for objectness loss. The objectness label is set to 1 for all positive samples
while that for all the other samples is set to 0. Secondly, BCE loss is also utilized
for classification loss, which is only computed for positive samples. Thirdly, CIoU
loss is utilized for regression criteria. For keypoint detection, firstly, BCE loss is
utilized for root confidence loss. The label of confidence is set to 1 for these posi-
tive samples whose corresponding annotations contain visibility labels, which are
equal to 1, and that for all the other samples are set to 0. BCE loss is also utilized
for root category loss, which is only computed for positive samples of keypoint
detection. Thirdly, OKS loss is utilized for root coordinates regression. In order to
balance the six losses in YOLO-Pose, six weights, the sum of which is equal to 1,
are assigned to each loss.
LY OLO−Pose = wboxLbox + wobjLobj + wclsLcls + wrconfLrconf + wrclsLrcls + wkpLOKS
(5.4)
During the inference procedure, predictions of keypoint are related with predic-
tions of object. Firstly, final object detection results are generated after NMS.
Then, for each predicted bounding box, the final root confidence score, which is
equal to the multiply of root confidence and highest category score, is computed.
These keypoints whose final score is bigger than threshold will be confirmed and
their predictions of coordinates will be considered as the keypoint detection re-
sults.

Conclusion
In this thesis study, we present a multi-task learning pipeline to achieve simultane-
ous and fast weed detection and joint stem detection. We hope our approach can
be considered as a constructive block promoting more attention and researches
paid to the field of joint stem detection and precise weeds control. Both our base-
line model(YOLO-HRNet) and advanced model(YOLO-Pose) enable a sharing of
feature extraction in backbone network while predict weed and joint stem positions
utilizing distinct detection head, which improves learning efficiency by leveraging
end-to-end training. The final results of experiments with YOLOv5, YOLO-HRNet
and YOLO-Pose support our three key claims. Firstly, compared to YOLOv5, the
mAP of both YOLO-HRNet and YOLO-Pose is boosted benefiting from sharing
backbone network for better feature extraction. Secondly, with the utilization of
multi-task learning, the position of weeds and joint stem keypoints can be jointly de-
tected by YOLO-HRNet and YOLO-Pose without any post-processing. Addition-
ally, simultaneous weed and joint stem detection can be achieved by YOLO-Pose
expected in real time. Thirdly, compared to YOLO-HRNet, YOLO-Pose achieves
impressive detection speed with 88.5/101.0 FPS (complex/simple pose head) on
GPU Tesla V100, proving its potential for future application on agricultural au-
tonomous robots.
Future work will focus on two parts. On the one hand, an efficient normalization
method can be investigated in order to scale the radius of joint stem keypoint an-
notation to replace variable σ values in OKS loss for criteria improvement. On the
other hand, more powerful and efficient structure of pose head can be developed
to improve the accuracy of joint stem detection.
