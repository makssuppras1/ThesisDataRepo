Abstract
This Master's Thesis describes the design, implementation and testing of a ra-
diometer receiver at X band with as many o-the-shelf components as possible.
The design has the capability to run in both total power mode and Dicke mode.
While a working prototype was not implemented, because of time constrains
and a long delivery time for a crucial component, the radiometer receiver was
very close to completion.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The subject of this thesis is the design and implementation of a microwave
radiation sensing instrument called a radiometer.
A radiometer is a passive
instrument that measures the thermal radiation in the microwave spectrum.
Using the data of the radiometer together with models of the observed thermal
radiator allows the determination of its temperature and other physical parame-
ters. Radiometers are often space-borne and used for monitoring the oceans and
thereby giving information about the surface temperature and salinity, which
are important factors to determine weather patterns and track global warming.
The Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity (SMOS) mission from ESA observes the
globe's oceans, but also the soil moisture of the landmasses, allowing scientists
to learn more about climate change.
The main focus of this thesis is the design and implementation of a portable
radiometer specically for teaching purposes. A radiometer that could easily
be handled by students and teachers alike would be of great benet for courses
dealing with remote sensing and radiometers, since a hands-on session could
then be facilitated. Furthermore, students studying on high school level or rst
year college level could benet from such a proposed radiometer, since they could
get an easy introduction to remote sensing and calibration, hopefully fostering
an interest for studying engineering later in their academic careers.
Currently, DTU oers several courses for high school (Danish: Gymnasium) stu-

dents when they have to do their student project in the last year of Gymnasium.
Hopefully, the product of this Master's Thesis will be a new project oered to
these students, namely that they can come and visit DTU, use a radiometer and
learn about calibration and microwave radiometry.
This thesis is written with focus on theory and test. Chapter 2 is the theory
chapter, as well as handling the comparison and choice of components and re-
ceiver architecture. Chapter 3 is a short chapter on the system level design
of the radiometer system. It highlights the design constraints regarding power
levels and noise contribution, which are important factors of the whole system.
Chapter 4 is about calibration and details the methods a back-end for a ra-

factor to assess radiometer performance. Chapter 5 details the implementation
of the radiometer modules. Chapter 6 contains the descriptions of the tests of
the dierent blocks and modules of the radiometer and the results of these tests.
Finally, the conclusion about the radiometer design is in chapter 7 along with
possible further projects and improvements.
1.1
Specications
The radiometer should have a small enough size that the label of "portable" is
thoroughly justied. Furthermore, the design should consist of available o-the-
shelve components to decrease cost and shipping time of components, except
where it is benecial to get free samples from manufactures or more exotic
components are needed. The output should be easily readable, either as a digital
output that be logged in some manner or as an analog output that can be read
by a voltmeter or some similar device, but most importantly, the output shall be
easily readable by person with little or no background in electrical engineering.
1.1.1
Choice of band
Two bands were potential candidates for this project, X band and C band.
Higher frequency requires more stringent design requirements, as loss from
impedance mismatching or production errors will be higher at high frequencies.
This means the X band is a more dicult frequency to design a radiometer at,
but also a band where there exist fewer sources of radio frequency interference
(RFI). At X band, no private-user components exist to pollute the band, but
military and airports often use radars in the X band. While components oper-
ating at X band might have been costly to acquire a couple of years ago, the

which means that it is feasible to design microwave instruments with commercial
components in this frequency band.
The X band was chosen as the band, both because it oered a relative protected

newly developed components for this frequency and show the feasibility of doing

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter concerns the basic theory of a radiometer as well as the intricacies
of the two type of receivers considered for the radiometer. The two types are
the superheterodyne receiver and the direct receiver. The radiometer will be
able to work in two modes, the Total Power mode and the Dicke mode, which
also will be covered in this chapter.

for this project. This section is heavily based on the class notes from DTU-

2.1.2
Radiometric modes
An ideal radiometer would always produce the same voltage corresponding to a
specic antenna temperature, which is sadly not the case because of added noise
in the receiver chain and gain uctuations. A radiometer consists of a gain stage
that amplies the incident power to a level that is appropriate for detection, a
lter that removes all but the spectrum of interest and a detector, which converts
the incident power to voltage that can be sampled by digital means, or simply
displayed directly. Since no ideal components exist and microstrip technology is
lossy, noise will be added through the losses through the system. This noise is
referred to as a noise temperature rather than a noise power, which is convenient
for calculations, since it will directly aect the resolution of the radiometer.

2.2
Superheterodyne Receiver
The superheterodyne receiver works very dierently than the direct receiver
that will be discussed later in this chapter.
Superheterodyne receivers work
by using a 3-port, non-linear component called a mixer to produce the sum
and dierence product of an input frequency and the frequency of the local
oscillator (LO) (gure 2.4). The reason this is benecial is that the modula-
tion, amplication and ltering can be done at a lower frequency, which means
that lumped-element lters and cheaper components can be used, but also that
analog-to-digital (ADC) or digital-to-analog (DAC) can be used on the low fre-
quency side. Quite often, it is the maximum frequency of digital components
that are limiting factors regarding the range of frequencies that can be used in
a receiver or transmitter. While heterodyning is also used for transmitters, only
the superheterodyne receiver will be focused on here because of the nature of a
radiometer.
Figure 2.4: Simple block diagram of generic superheterodyne receiver
Since the X band has been chosen as the band of choice where the radiometer will
operate in, a heterodyne receiver is a logically choice for receiver architecture.
A heterodyne receiver will downconvert the high frequency of the spectrum
that is investigated, producing the dierence of the LO frequency and the input
frequencies. Broadband voltage-controlled oscillators are widely available in the
X band spectrum, meaning that the IF frequency can be chosen for whatever
components are cheaply available or if the other uses are possible with the IF
frequency output.
The heterodyne receiver will need a low noise amplier as far in the front as
possible. This amplier has to have a signicant amount of amplication and
a low noise gure, since this noise gure has far reaching consequences for the
==PAGE:22==
12
Theory
native noise of the receiver design. After the low noise amplier, a lter has
to be placed to ensure that when the signal is fed to the mixer, removing the
unwanted mixing product and the image frequency.
2.3
Direct receiver
The direct receiver does not downconvert the input frequency to a lower fre-
quency such as the heterodyne receiver does. All ltering and amplication is
done at the RF frequency, which adds higher requirements for isolation and sig-
nal integrity (gure 2.5). Since the radiometer will operate at X band, it means
that a very narrow lter is necessary. Else, it works similar to the heterodyne
receiver, using a detector, often some kind of diode, to create a voltage pro-
portional to the incoming power. After the detector, both direct receiver and

The subharmonic sampling receiver, which is a receiver that strongly resembles
the direct receiver, works on the principal of Nyquist's Theorem. It is also an
implementation of a receiver with a higher degree of digital processing of the
signal. Rather than using a detector, the signal is directly sampled by an ADC
after the microwave lter and amplication, which is similar to the front-end
of the direct receiver. Nyquist's Theorem is not as commonly interpreted, the
statement that the sampling frequency should be double the highest frequency
to be sampled, but rather it should be twice the frequency of the bandwidth.
If the erroneous belief about Nyquist's Theorem is followed, a direct sampling
receiver would need to have over 22 giga samples per second to work for the
specic conditions of this project, which is still not really commercial available.
If the correct interpretation of the Theorem is followed, it can be seen that only
a sampling frequency of more than 400 MHz is needed, which is commercial
available today. The reason that this is possible, is that the spectrum mirrors
around the sampling frequency and the harmonics of the sampling frequency.
Of course, it requires an extremely high quality lter. For information about
subharmonic sampling see [14].

Detector
While there exists several detectors for the frequencies 275 MHz to 475 MHz, a
requirement of the radiometer was that it should use a tunnel diode detector.
Commercial tunnel diode detectors are produced by companies like Macom,
Krytar, Herotek and Cobham's Test and Measurement Microelectronics divi-
sion.
A tunnel diode diers to normal diodes in such a way that it has an
equally large forward and break voltage and that the I-V characteristics in the
breakdown and forward areas are linear, or very close to linear. This means that
it can rectify the input signal, essential creating an output voltage proportional
to the input power. The only problem with tunnel diodes is that they are quite
hard to obtain, as they cannot be bought through normal electronics vendors.
Rather, specialized small scale electronics vendors have to facilitate the link
to the company actually making the tunnel diode detectors, which can take a
very long time. Furthermore, tunnel diode detectors are expensive. The tunnel
diode detector obtained for this project would have costed approximately 600
USD from new (October 2017).
The tunnel diode detector used in this design will be the ACTM-1114NM47,
since it was available at the laboratory.

saturate the LNAs, drowning out the signal of interest.
2.7
Comparison
Disregarding the high isolation and care that has to be considered in a direct
receiver design, there is a compelling reason to use a heterodyne receiver. At
DTU Space an RFI mitigation device for radiometers is being developed and this
device works at the same frequency as the IF frequency chosen in this project
for the heterodyne receiver [23]. This means that the portable radiometer could
be upgraded with this device in future projects and it is therefore a very good
reason for choosing the superheterodyne receiver over the direct receiver.
That said, a direct receiver would need to have 90 dB of amplication at X band
that would lead to an instrument which would need careful designed shielding,
since it highly likely that the unshielded instrument would have feedback loops
through the air. The microwave lter at X band would also be almost impossible
to produce in-house with normal microstrip technology or very expensive, since
==PAGE:34==
24
Theory
highly special substrates would have to be bought at a high cost. Furthermore,
the program mainly used for RF designs, Keysight's ADS, has a less stellar track
record for higher frequency designs, meaning that it would be a daunting task to
design everything at X band as it would be required by a direct receiver. Overall,
a direct receiver would be far more expensive than a superheterodyne receiver,
mainly because the detector for a superheterodyne design is free. Hence, the
design for the radiometer will be a superheterodyne receiver.

Chapter 4
Calibration
This chapter concerns the calibration of radiometers. While this report mainly
concerns the implementation of a working radiometer, an introduction to cali-
bration is also required, since only a calibrated instrument will produce useful
data. Calibration is needed since the characteristics of the radiometer will uc-
tuate or drift as function of time. The two radiometric modes implemented in
the design of the radiometer, TPR and Dicke, need dierent calibration, but for
simplicity's sake, calibration will be based on TPR mode. To really understand
calibration, a parameter has to be dened that assesses the performance of a
radiometer. Racette et al [9, 2005] dene the measurement uncertainty as the
basis of assessing radiometer system performance rather than radiometric res-
olution, since radiometric resolution is actually a component of measurement
uncertainty.
Furthermore, the dierent tiers of calibration can also be dened.
Racette
denes calibration as existing in three tiers, as shown in gure 4.1, which is
a sound and robust way of classifying calibration methods. Tier 1 is internal
calibration that consists of switched references and noise injection, which is the
methods employed in this design.
This calibration tier does not regard any
losses introduced by the antenna or anything outside the receiver chain, but
is the fastest method to track uctuations in the receiver and is also the most
cost-eective.

Chapter 5
Implementation of receiver
This chapter concerns the physically implementation of the radiometer design,
concerning substrate technology, PCB layout and other design considerations.
For the design choices and calculations in regards to the designed radiometer,
please consult chapter 2 and 3. Since the design has to be built in-house and
in a cost-eective manner, there are design limitations with regards to available
components and tolerances. The layout of the design was designed in Keysight's
Advanced Design System (ADS), version 2016.1. The tool used for calculating
transmission line widths at a specic frequency for the specic substrates were
done with the LineCalc tool of ADS.
5.1
Substrate
The substrate mainly used for the design is Rogers Substrate 4003C, both in
32 and 62 mil thickness (1 mil = 0.0254 mm) [12], since it is readily available
at the in-house laboratory/workshop. This substrate has a copper plating of 35
µm, a dielectric constant of 3.55 and a dissipation factor tan δ of 0.0027. This
substrate does pose some serious limitations due to its dielectric constant and its
thickness. Because of signal integrity concerns, co-planar waveguide (CPWG) is
always the preferable technology to use for transmissions, except when special
==PAGE:54==
44
Implementation of receiver
microstrip structures such as lters or power dividers are concerned, since they
often require that no other microstrip structure are placed in the vicinity of
them. Co-planar waveguide is a microstrip transmission line, where ground is
situated a xed distance away for the whole length of the transmission line. A
diagram of CPWG is shown in gure 5.1.
Figure 5.1: Sketch of a cross-section of a CPWG transmission line. CPWG is
typically dened by its width (W) and the xed distance to ground
(G) from the signal trace.
Vias for any design has to be drilled and riveted manually, which is time-
consuming and annoying workload. The rivets for vias are only available for
the 62 mil thickness, but are also used for the 32 mil thickness substrate where
they create ugly vias that stick out from the copper. This is a real problem when
the vias are supposed to be placed under a small SMD component, since a bulge
might be created which does not allow the component to be properly mounted.
The reason for using the 32 mil thickness substrate when vias are so annoying at
this thickness, is that the transmission lines will have a smaller width, resulting
in easier design at 32 mil substrate thickness. A 50 ohm microstrip transmission
line (not co-planar) will have a width of 1.813 mm, while at 62 mil substrate
thickness, the width will be 3.787 mm, which is more than the double. 3.787
mm is a quite ridiculous width when the components themselves only are 3.0 x
3.0 mm, like the HMC553 mixer. For CPWG, 32 mil substrate, with a 0.5 mm
gap to the ground plane, the width of the transmission line will be 1.492 mm,
while 62 mil thickness will result in a width of 2.374 mm.
Considering the image rejection lter, using Rogers 4003C proves infeasible,
both at 32 and 62 mil thickness. As discovered by Rasmus Simling in his Mas-
ter's Thesis[13], building a lowpass lter specically for this radiometer design
is impossible in the thickness that Rogers 4003C substrate is available in, in-
house.
This partly because the in-house method of PCB production has a
tolerance of 0.2 mm, which is the absolute lower limit for transmission lines, but
==PAGE:55==
5.2 Modules
45
for a stepped-impedance lowpass lter on Rogers 4003C 62 mil substrate, some
of the line widths would have to be smaller than that. This means that a thicker
substrate is necessary with a smaller dielectric constant. Luckily, some small
pieces of Rogers RT/duroid 5880 [12] were available for Rasmus and that meant
that he could create a lter with that substrate. RT/duroid 5880 available in
the laboratory was 62 mil, has a dielectric constant, or relative permittivity, εr
of 2.2, with lower loss tangent, tan δ = 0.0009. The higher thickness and lower
relative permittivity allows the lter elements to have widths that realisable
with the in-house production method.
When designing microstrip structures on substrates with too large relative per-
mittivity, a problem is that high impedance mistrostrip lines will have minuscule
line width, which is unrealisable in real-life application. This means that in some
situations, a unequal Wilkinson Power divider cannot be implemented. Since
the output of the noise diode needs to be attenuated 10 dB, a Wilkinson power
divider was proposed, but such a split would require one microstrip line to have
a characteristic impedance of ≈270 Ohm, which would have resulted in a line
width of 0.0002 mm.
The main reason why the whole design was not designed and implemented on the
RT/duroid 5880 substrate is that the pieces used for the image rejection lter
were leftovers and that the RT/duroid 5880 substrate is not readily available
through local vendors. This means that the acquisition of the RT/duroid 5880
substrate would be quite expensive and would be an unnecessary burden for the
budget, as Rogers 4003C is an appropriate substrate for the rest of design. The
supply module, module 4 and module 5 are fabricated on Rogers 4003C 62 mil,
while the rest of the design is fabricated on Rogers 4003C 32 mil.

5.5
Decoupling capacitors and RF chokes
Decoupling capacitors and RF chokes are important components that ensure
that the components operating at RF frequency or the operational ampliers do
not get any additive noise through their supply pins. In a large design, like this
radiometer system, it is especially important to have proper decoupling. Since
there are many components operating at the same frequencies, and potentially
producing noise at the same frequencies, noise will propagate through the supply
network.

5.5.1
Decoupling capacitors
Decoupling capacitors provides a path to ground for unwanted noise that might
enter components through their connections to the power supply.
Some RF
components' data sheets provide information about the necessary decoupling
capacitance, but since the design's RF side is above 10 GHz, but still uses
some wideband components, some lower capacitances have been used in the
implementation, to ensure no added noise at these higher frequency.
In the
data sheet for the HMC564 LNA [16], the values of the two proposed decoupling
capacitors for each of the two supply pins are set to 2.2 pF and 100 pF, but to
ensure better performance at higher frequency, instead of the 2.2 pF capacitor,
a 0.5 pF capacitor is used.
Decoupling capacitors are always placed such that the capacitor with the lowest
capacitance is placed closest to the component.
The reason is that the low
capacitance will mean that capacitors are decoupling at high frequencies and at
high frequencies line length matters a lot. For good decoupling at high frequency,
the capacitors with the lowest capacitance needs to be placed closest to the
component that needs decoupling.
At high frequency, decoupling capacitors
with high capacitances will already have turned inductive, meaning that they
are operating as an inductor. All decoupling capacitors are in the 0603 footprint,
since they are readily available in-house.
==PAGE:61==

5.5.2
RF choke
RF chokes do not allow high frequency noise to pass, but allow DC voltages to
pass. Essentially, it is a one component lowpass lter, which is great to ensure
that noise does not spread in the supply network. Normal inductors have a high
quality factor (high Q), since they are meant to be lter components where a
high Q is desirable. A high Q also signify a very little loss in the inductor,
but a high Q is not desirable for a RF choke where a little loss is acceptable
and high impedance to changing signals is needed. A high inductance is also
favourable, around 1 µH. A RF choke typically has a ferrite core, but also cores
lled with iron powder are used. All RF chokes used in this design are in the
0805 footprint.

5.9.3
VCO
While the frequency requirements of this project are not the strictest, a stable
VCO is important. In the test blocks, simple voltage division with a 500 Ohm
potentiometer enables the VCO to be tuned on the spot. This is not necessary
and also detrimental to the usability of the VCO in this module, as potentiome-
ters have a tendency to have a high drift as a function of temperature. Also,
it would be benecial to secure the stability of the VCO against any problems
with the stability of the power supply.
An optimal solution to the control of the VCO is not found in this thesis, as it
turned out to be a more complex problem than anticipated. A solution with a
Zener diode was considered, but currently an adjustable precision zener shunt
regulator is considered to solve this problem. Added by experience gained by
the tests described in next chapter, a LNA and coupler is added, to increase the
output power of the VCO and to sample the output frequency of the VCO.

The integrator also uses the ADA4807-1 as op-amp. The implementation of the
integrator will be as a Miller integrator, where a large feedback resistor ensures
that gain is nite at DC. It is also important to remember that the integrator
is inverting. For an Arduino to be able to sample the output voltage, it needs
to be positive. The chain of ampliers should produce a negative input voltage
for the integrator, thus resulting in a nal positive output voltage.
The time constant, here meaning the integration time, of the Miller integrator
is determined by the feedback resistor and capacitor. The time constant is τ =
Cfeedback · Rfeedback, which in this case should be 1 ms, resulting in Rfeedback =
10kΩand Cfeedback = 100 npF. If the integrator is thought as a low pass lter,
the inverse of the time constant determines the -3 dB point of the lter response,
where afterwards the magnitude will decrease by -20 dB/decade. The point of
unity gain is given by the series resistor times the capacitor. Since no further

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This chapter concerns the evaluation of the whole project. Its goal is to establish
if the design and implementation is a success, as well as to discuss the choices
made and if they could have been done dierently. Furthermore, some space
will also be devoted to the possible improvement of the prototype as well as
necessities that need to be addressed to have a fully operational radiometer
prototype.
7.1
Discussion
While many sound design choices were made, some choices turned out in retro-
spect to be slightly unnecessary. These will be discussed here.
7.1.1
Simulations
No extensive simulations where done to prove that the design was feasible before
moving on to the implementation. A small harmonic balance test was done in
Keysight's ADS (version 2016.1) to acknowledge that the power budget was
correct or within limit of what could be deemed correct.
Furthermore, the
==PAGE:104==
94
Conclusion
coupler was also simulated using ADS with its EM simulation suite. The result
of the harmonic balance simulation is left out of this thesis because it does not
add any insight to the thesis.
The whole design could very well have been simulated in an appropriate design
program, such as ADS, or LTspice for the op-amps, but since there was no
extensive knowledge regarding the simulation of such a complex project in a
design program, it would very properly have doubled the time necessary to
create a full design. The focus in this project was the creation of a useful end-
product and it is felt that the many test blocks and tests make it possible to
nd the pit falls that would also have been found through extensive simulations.
Lastly, it should be mentioned that it is very hard to create working designs at
X band, which means that although the microstrip structures implemented in
this project are working ne, far more extensive simulations (3D and otherwise)
might be necessary in other circumstances to ensure that the other structures
near the microstrip structures do not interfere with them.
7.2
Conclusion
While the physical size of this radiometer prototype enables the sobriquet "portable",
the mounting of PCBs, battery system and digital back-end will determine if it
really can be called portable. Nonetheless, the design described in this thesis is
a cost eective design for a such a sophisticated system as a radiometer receiver
at X band and its design, implementation and testing can be deemed a success.
Sadly, some last components were missing for the completion of the radiometer,
but these obstacles are minuscule compared to all the other obstacles overcome
in this project.

7.3
Further Work
While the prototype represents a long development and implementation period,
it is not a perfect radiometer system. This section explores the improvements
that can be done to the prototype, beyond implementing the two modules that
were missing the last components.
7.3.1
Digital Back-end
The most basic improvement a digital back-end could perform, would be the
control of the multiplexer and microwave switch. These could potential be run
by an Arduino or similar microcontroller, which is far smaller than the bulky
==PAGE:106==
96
Conclusion
laboratory currently used. Furthermore, a microcontroller with an appropriate
ADC could perform the sampling of the output of the integrator, meaning that
whole control and measurement scheme could be unied in a single instrument.
A more advanced improvement could be the integration of the methods shown
in chapter 4. If a microcontroller should perform control and measurement, it
would probably be overworked with applying a calibration scheme to the data,
thus it would be better if the data was sent to a computer that could calculate
the measurement uncertainty and apply a calibration scheme in real time. Since
it was established in chapter 4 that the measurement uncertainty was the right
indicator of radiometer performance, this also means the true evaluation rst
can be done when the measurement uncertainty of the radiometer system has
been calculated for a specic set up.
Especially applying the method discussed in section 4.3 and 4.4 would decrease
the measurement uncertainty of the radiometer and thus improve the perfor-
mance of the radiometer. Implementing these calibration methods in a digital
back-end could very well be Bachelor's Thesis or the very least, a special course
for Master's student.
7.3.1.1
RFI mitigation
As mentioned in the chapter 1, DTU Space has developed an instrument to help
mitigate the impact of radiofrequency interference. The instrument allows to
pinpoint which samples taken by the radiometer that have been contaminated
by RFI, which means that they can be discarded. Integrating the RFI mitigation
device and its output to the data processor and applying the calibration schemes
discussed in chapter 4 by Racette [9] could very well be the subject for a Master's
Thesis.
7.3.2
Temperature Control
A simpler task than the implementation of the digital back-end, but also part of
it, is the implementation of a temperature control scheme. Aiming for an error
as small as possible, a PI regulator could be used to regulate the temperature of
the radiometer system, assuming it is in an insulated housing. The sensors that
the regulator would use could be some temperature probes which uses I2C or
SPI. The placement of the probes would have to be considered, since some of the
components produce heat by themselves, meaning any probe close to such an

the component's temperature and the temperature of the internal space of the

7.3.3
Battery
While the prototype itself cannot be deemed portable, it has the possibility to
become portable, if a battery-powered supply is designed. The prototype has
limited possibility for decreasing power consumption, the only method being the
powering on or o of the op-amps in the amplication circuit for Dicke mode
radiometry. Some more ecient voltage regulators could probably be found,
also in conjunction with the design constrains caused by the choice of battery.
Depending on the choice of battery type, the output voltage of the batteries
might need to be increased to allow the 15 V power supply for the tuning port
of VCO and the supply of the noise diode. The task of designing a system to
deliver all needed voltages and run on batteries seems to be likely candidate for
a Bachelor's Thesis or a special course.
7.3.4
Casing and mounting
Another aspect that only has been briey addressed in this project, is the casing
for the radiometer and the internal mounting of the modules in this casing. The
casing for the radiometer needs to be thermally insulated to allow the instru-
ment to have an uniform temperature, hence avoiding temperature gradients.
Since the design is modular, tting it in such a box should not be challenge.
Furthermore, the casing of the dierent modules has to be considered. In an en-
closed box, the modules might be so close to each other that they might interfere
with each other, meaning that shielding might be necessary.
