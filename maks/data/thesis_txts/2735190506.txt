Abstract
The Advanced Telescope for High ENergy Astrophysics (ATHENA) is a flagship mis-
sion of the European Space Agency (ESA), dedicated to addressing the topic of the ’Hot
and Energetic Universe’. Scheduled for launch in the late 2030s, ATHENA has been
subjected to mission redefinitions aimed at enhancing cost-effectiveness without compro-
mising its ambitious scientific goals. At the heart of its design are the mirror optics, with
X-ray coatings playing a critical role in capturing high-energy astrophysical phenomena.
This Master’s thesis delves into the intricate world of NewAthena’s X-ray coatings. The
initial objective is to assess the baseline coating design, searching for a tailored approach
that can maximize performance with a X-ray mirror bilayer coating of Ir/C. Additionally,
this study explores two multilayer coating designs being developed at DTU Space, inves-
tigating their impact on specific scientific cases within the NewAthena mission, spanning
compact objects such as Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN) and extended structures like galaxy
clusters.
To simulate fiducial X-ray observations and gauge the telescope’s performance across
diverse scenarios, this study leverages astrophysical models developed by ESA and the
scientific astrophysical community for the emissions of AGN and galaxy clusters. In addi-
tion, accurate simulations of ATHENA’s optical performance are enabled by the existing
ray-tracing software SPORT, developed at DTU Space.
In conclusion, this thesis serves a dual purpose: it offers a detailed examination of
X-ray mirror coatings within the framework of the ATHENA mission, aimed at optimizing
scientific outcomes through the use of bilayer and multilayer coating technologies. By
evaluating the potential enhancements in telescope performance these coatings offer,
this research supports the decision-making process in the selection of mirror optics tech-
nologies, thereby facilitating a deeper understanding of the universe through improved
observational capabilities.

Introduction
”If the hand be held between the
discharge-tube and the screen, the
darker shadow of the bones is seen
within the slightly dark shadow-image
of the hand itself… I shall use the
expression ’rays’... ’X-rays’”.
Wilhelm Röntgen
Since ancient times, humans have been mesmerized by the sky, seeing in it not just
celestial bodies but stories and mysteries. This fascination, deep-rooted in our ances-
try, has been a constant in our quest for understanding the universe. Ancient people,
under clear, unpolluted skies, closely observed the stars for navigation, agriculture, and
interpreting omens, linking survival to these celestial patterns [1].
With the advent of the telescope in the 17th century, our celestial understanding ex-
panded dramatically. Astronomers challenged prevailing views of the cosmos, sparking a
scientific revolution. This growing curiosity and improved observational technology set the
stage for future discoveries, such as in 1895, when Wilhelm Röntgen, a German physicist,

Röntgen’s discovery was revolutionary. For the first time, we could see beyond the

It would still take some time since Röntgen’s discovery, however, for us to be able to
marvel directly at the universe with our new set of eyes. Like a distracted pedestrian sur-
rounded by chaos, we had no idea X-rays could reach us from such different, magnificent,
terrifying places in the vast Universe. Today, we know that the atmosphere that protects
us is very competent at blocking high-energy photons, so before we could see the light, it
was first necessary to open the curtains.
It seems a great deal of discoveries - be it in science or in life - are made unintention-
ally. As such, the first extrasolar X-ray source ever detected was observed in the 1960s
by an American group led by Riccardo Giacconi, who conducted a rocket observation ex-
periment to detect X-rays emitted from the Moon’s surface as a result of energetic solar
wind particles (fluorescence). Giacconi and his group unexpectedly discovered a power-
ful X-ray source (Scorpius X-1), ultimately leading to part of a Nobel prize in physics in
2002 [2]. The curtains had been lifted, and yet another realm of knowledge laid before
us.

1.1.1
The AGN science case
Most galaxies are known to host a supermassive black hole (SMBH) at their centers.
The mass of these SMBHs is found to be tightly correlated with the velocity dispersion
σ of the host galaxy, a relationship known as the MBH −σ relation . This correlation
has significantly altered our understanding of galaxy formation and evolution, highlighting
the need for a self-regulating mechanism that links the accretion-powered growth of the
SMBH with the star-formation-powered development of the host galaxy. Deciphering this
feedback mechanism is crucial for comprehending the concurrent growth and co-evolution
of Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN) and galaxies [10].
Substantial evidence has emerged indicating that AGNs undergo significant mass loss
via winds [11]. These winds have estimated mass-loss rates of a few M⊙per year and
velocities reaching up to 0.4c. Given these parameters, the kinetic energy carried by these
outflows is comparable to the total radiated power of the AGN. Such outflows are integral
to the energy budget of AGNs and are often associated with the feedback mechanism.
A pivotal phase in the evolution of massive galaxies is believed to involve AGN-driven
winds expelling gas from the host galaxy, consequently inhibiting star formation. High-
speed winds, as depicted in Figure 1.3, are considered the most effective method for
transferring energy from the nuclear scale to the host galaxy [12]. Models suggest that
the energy from these powerful AGN-driven winds is deposited into the host galaxy’s in-
terstellar medium (ISM). This interaction leads to the formation of galactic-scale molecular
outflows, capable of depleting the galaxy’s gas reservoir and suppressing star formation
activities.

winds, shedding light on a previously elusive aspect of galactic phenomena.
1.1.2
Galaxy clusters
Several decades worth of advancements in astrophysics have shown that the matter
content of the observable Universe consists of ≈80% dark matter and ≈20% ordinary,
baryonic matter [16]. The distribution of dark matter dominates the gravitational potential
in which stars and galaxies grow. Galaxy clusters, being the largest gravitationally bound
structures in the universe, play a pivotal role in our understanding of dark matter, and
are excellent laboratories for studying such phenomena that lie on the edge of current
knowledge.
X-ray emission from galaxy clusters serves as an important tracer for dark matter due
to the relationship between the hot intra-cluster medium (ICM) and the cluster’s gravi-
tational field, which is predominantly influenced by dark matter. In galaxy clusters, the

ICM is heated to temperatures of 108K, causing it to emit X-rays. This X-ray emission is
directly proportional to the density and temperature of the gas, which in turn is dictated
by the cluster’s gravitational pull. Since dark matter constitutes the majority of a clus-
ter’s mass, it significantly influences the cluster’s gravitational potential [11]. Therefore,
by mapping the X-ray emission and analyzing the distribution and temperature of the hot
gas in galaxy clusters, it becomes possible to infer the underlying dark matter distribu-

observable, its gravitational effects on the observable hot gas can be meticulously traced
through X-ray emissions.
In support of the ATHENA mission, the scientific community has outlined fundamental
questions that X-ray observations of the evolving cluster population can address [17]:
1. How do hot diffuse baryons accrete and dynamically evolve in dark matter poten-
tials?
2. How and when was the energy that we observe in the ICM generated and dis-
tributed?
3. Where and when are heavy elements produced and how are they circulated?
To unravel these dynamics, it is necessary to move beyond current observational ca-
pabilities. A significant improvement in spectral resolution and collecting area is essential
to map the velocities, turbulence and metallicities in the intra-cluster gas with the required
precision [18]. Such advancements will not only illuminate the ongoing assembly pro-
cesses in clusters but will also offer crucial insights into the three-dimensional dynamics
of these colossal cosmic structures.
1.2
ATHENA current status
The motivation for a new large space-bourne X-ray observatory has been around since
as early as 2003 with the X-ray Evolving Universe Spectroscopy mission (XEUS). The pro-
posal by ESA for XEUS envolved an enormous collecting area of 62m at 1keV, and two

ESA originally proposed ATHENA in 2011 [22], a de-scoped version of IXO.

in 2023 when ESA appointed a Science Re-Definition Team (SRDT) to revise the science
objectives of the Athena mission, adapting them to new discoveries since the mission
was originally proposed, and matching them to new programmatic constraints. Neverthe-
less, to answer the missions’ ambitious scientific questions in detail, a substantial leap
in capabilities in comparison to previously flown X-ray observatories is necessary, plac-
ing challenging constraints on ATHENA’s optics’ requirements [23]. ATHENA will be the
largest X-ray observatory ever built, featuring a single X-ray telescope with a fixed focal

The manufacturing of ATHENA mirrors can be summarized in the following steps:
1. Plasma cleaning of the Si substrate [43].
2. Coating of thin films through DC magnetron sputtering [44] (see section 3.5).
3. A wet chemistry process with two phases: (a) photoresist stripes are chemically re-
moved with dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) to allow bonding of the SPO ribs [45], and (b)
a modified version of Standard Clean 1 (SC-1) procedure for activation of bonding
areas before stacking [38].
4. Stacking of SPO plates.
5. Annealing procedure to improve Si bond strength [46] and reduce stress on Ir layer
[47].

Thesis scope and limitations
This research primarily aims to evaluate the impact of using multilayers in selected
science cases of ATHENA, following a nuanced approach. The initial phase involves sim-
ulating X-ray reflectometry (XRR) using various coating solutions based on the recent
developments and characterizations at DTU Space [29, 30, 31, 32, 37, 34, 35, 33, 38].
Subsequently, the study employs existing ray-tracing software to simulate ATHENA’s op-
tical performance to the highest level of fidelity achievable with current technology. The
final step entails generating simulated observations of AGN and galaxy clusters using
prevailing astrophysical models. This process assesses the expected improvement in
measurement accuracy for each coating design.
An additional focus is to validate optimization of ATHENA’s baseline coating design.
The efforts behind ATHENA’s bilayer design have so far been conducted under the as-
sumption of the same single coating design for all the rings of Athena’s optics [32]. Recog-
nizing the potential benefits of tailoring coating recipes to optimize performance at differ-
ent grazing angles for each ring, an extensive sweep of the layer thicknesses was tested
through simulations with IMD. The goal is to identify a tailored row-based bilayer coating
solution that can further improve the efficiency of ATHENA’s mirrors.
Therefore, the objectives of this thesis include:
• Investigate ATHENA’s baseline coating design for a balance between simplicity and
enhanced performance, using a row-based design.
• Conducting ray-tracing simulations of ATHENA’s optics using various coating de-
signs as initial assumptions.
• Simulating X-ray observations based on selected science cases to evaluate the im-
pact and benefits of different coating designs.
• Evaluate the trade-offs between scientific performance and design complexity in
multilayer coatings.
A notable limitation of this research is the computational intensity of the simulation
steps involved. While the limitations imposed by the available hardware did not render
any phase of the study infeasible, they did restrict the range of potential solutions that
could be explored in detail. This constraint primarily affected the depth and breadth of

Introducion to X-ray physics
Since their discovery in the late 19th century, X-rays have emerged as a pivotal tool
for unraveling the mysteries of the universe, allowing us to peer into the mechanisms
of high-energy phenomena such as black holes, neutron stars, supernovae, and the ex-

missions like ATHENA.

ions interact, their acceleration and deceleration give rise to bremsstrahlung.
Bremsstrahlung radiation follows a characteristic shape that falls exponentially with

2.1.3
Synchrotron radiation
Synchrotron radiation, a specific type of bremsstrahlung, occurs when charged parti-
cles, typically electrons, are accelerated to relativistic speeds along curved paths within
strong magnetic fields. In astronomical settings, this radiation is prevalent in regions dom-
inated by intense magnetic fields, where charged particles are corralled by the Lorentz
force into helical trajectories around magnetic field lines, consequently emitting radiation.
A high energy electron traversing a region with intense magnetic field is deflected by
the Lorentz force perpendicularly to its velocity, creating an acceleration that will result in
the emission of electromagnetic energy.

2.1.4
Inverse Compton Scattering
Inverse Compton scattering is a process where relativistic electrons impart energy to
photons via collisions proportionally to the squared Lorentz factor γ2 = [1 −(v/c)2]−1.

the electromagnetic spectrum. Such mechanism is pivotal around black holes, particularly
within their accretion disks, and is a significant contributor to their characteristic power-law
emission spectra, which provides astronomers with valuable insights into the high-energy
environments near these enigmatic objects.

2.2.2
Bragg’s Law
Electromagnetic waves traveling through a medium that mimics the structure of a crys-
tal lattice can experience reflections across the various layers of this structure. The nature
of these reflections — whether they cancel each other out (destructive interference) or am-
plify the overall signal (constructive interference) — depends on the path the waves travel
and the phase of the waves at the point of reflection. Specifically, constructive interfer-
ence occurs when the path length difference between two waves traversing through the
lattice equals an integer multiple of the wavelength. This results in the wave amplitudes
being in phase. This is the condition described by Bragg’s law:
nλ = 2d sin θ
(2.14)

in future sections of this work, Bragg’s law plays a central role in focusing X-ray optics,
and multilayer coatings leverage constructive interference due to Bragg’s condition to
maximize reflectance at given wavelengths and incidence angles.

3
Soft X-ray Optics
The interaction between electromagnetic radiation and matter is too weak in the X-
ray regions of the light spectrum to allow substantial and efficient deflection of a beam
in a single step and at near-normal incidence angles. This chapter discusses design
techniques for X-ray imaging systems. Key to these design strategies is the use of grazing
incidence angles, where X-rays strike the mirror surface at very shallow angles, allowing
for effective reflection despite the weak interaction. Additionally, it explores the design
of mirror surface coatings, such as multilayer coatings, that enhance reflectivity at higher
X-ray energies.
3.1
Grazing incidence telescopes
As alluded to in Section 2.2.1, the phenomenon of total external reflection is of central
importance to X-ray mirror design, and occurs only at very low grazing incidence angles.
This can enforce strict constraints on an imaging systems design. Nevertheless, the de-
velopment of X-ray optics over many decades has shown many possibilities to tailor the
optics to a given mission or science case within such constraints.
Hans Wolter explored [58] how pairs of mirrors built from revolution surfaces of co-
focal conic sections (i.e., hyperboloids and ellipsoids) could be used to make an X-ray
microscope for biological imaging. Wolter eventually considered three possible design
families:
• Type-I: a primary reflection off the concave surface of a paraboloid, followed by a
secondary reflection off the concave surface of a hyperboloid.
• Type-II: a primary reflection off the concave surface of a paraboloid, followed by a
secondary reflection off the convex surface of a hyperboloid.
• Type-III: a primary reflection off the convex surface of a paraboloid, followed by a
secondary reflection off the concave surface of an ellipsoid.
These three possibilities are illustrated below in Figure 3.1. Generally, the Wolter type-I
design family is employed to design imaging X-ray telescopes. The primary reason is that
it is typically far easier to nest multiple mirror shells and increase the telescope’s collecting
area beyond that of a single shell. The nested shells’ design results in a composite mirror
and has been the predominant method for designing imaging X-ray optics for many years.
One such example is the NuSTAR observatory, developed by DTU Space [5].
Wolter later demonstrated [59] that by introducing higher-order terms to surface equa-
tions of type-I (i.e., adding convexity to the parabolic shape and concavity to the hyper-
bolic shape), comatic aberrations could be eliminated. Wolter was largely inspired by
Schwarzschild’s work on visible light telescopes, and this variation is now referred to as
a Wolter-Schwarzschild X-ray telescope. This design, however, has been sparingly used
in the past, so far being reserved for instruments in the extreme ultraviolet (EUV) to very
soft X-rays (up to ≈50-250 eV) [60], such as the wide field camera on ROSAT [61] or the
EUV Explorer [62].

3.4
Material Selection
The performance of X-ray coatings, whether single-layer or multilayer, is contingent
upon the interplay between the materials’ optical properties and the interfaces’ quality.
While differences in surface roughness exist among the materials discussed, this factor
assumes a secondary role in most cases. Our primary focus lies in selecting materials
based solely on their optical properties.
Single-layer coatings are most effective when applied with materials with a high atomic
number (Z), owing to their reflective characteristics. Historically, gold (Au) and iridium
(Ir) have been the top choices. An interesting deviation is seen in the IXPE mission,
which opted for a nickel-cobalt (NiCo) coated surface rather than the traditional mirrored
finish [70]. Additionally, tungsten (W) and platinum (Pt) have been recognized as viable
alternatives.
The critical angle, denoted as αc, is a key factor in selecting the appropriate material
for these coatings. However, it’s important also to consider other aspects like absorption
edges. For example, a graph in Figure 3.8 (left) illustrates the attenuation properties of
Au, Co, Ir, Ni, W, and Pt at their respective M-, L-, and K-edges. Au, Ir, and Pt display
remarkably similar patterns in this regard. When considering costs, Au and Ir are often the
materials of choice. Conversely, W shows energy edges leaning towards the lower end
of the spectrum, which can diminish the performance of soft X-ray instruments, making it
a less desirable option.

Thin-film deposition using DC magnetron sputtering
Several deposition techniques are available for production of thin film coatings [71].
Direct current (DC) magnetron sputtering is a suitable vacuum technique for astrophysical
instrumentation applications, having been employed in other X-ray observatories such
as NuSTAR [5], Hitomi [6], and will be used for ATHENA [64]. It is a physical, vacuum
technique, allowing for deposition of both single element materials and compounds.

Thin Film Coatings Optimization
ATHENA’s bilayer design has been developed based on a uniform coating approach for
Athena’s optics. However, customizing coating formulas for each ring’s specific grazing
angles could enhance performance. Through simulations with IMD, we explored var-
ious layer thicknesses to find an optimal, straightforward coating solution that boosts
ATHENA’s mirror efficiency. The study initially focused on adjusting the top carbon layer’s
thickness from 2 to 20 nm in 1 nm steps. It then broadened to assess the effects of varying
both the carbon and iridium layer thicknesses within the 2 to 20 nm range.
4.1
Modeling optical properties of thin films with IMD
Estimating the optical properties of thin films is integral to instrument design, particu-
larly in the field of high-energy instrumentation. IMD is a computer program for modelling
optical properties (reflectance, transmittance, absorptance, phase shifts and electric-field
intensities) of multi-layer films, i.e., films consisting of any number of layers of any thick-
ness [55].
The optical functions of a layered film in IMD are based on the Fresnel equations.
These are modified as discussed in section 2.2.3 to account for surface imperfections
(roughness, diffuseness etc.), describing the transmission of rays between optically dis-
similar materials.
The method employed here relied on the built-in IMD function fresnel, and the general
computation is summarized in the following steps:
1. Structure specification: defining the thicknesses and disposition of the layered
structure and substrate.
2. Material designation: definition of layer materials and referral to optical constants
database from the Center for X-ray Optics (CXRO) [72].
3. Variable designation: For all calculations, at least one wavelength (or energy) and
incidence angle must be specified.
Furthermore, the determination of optical functions through experiments is generally
made with instrumentation of limited angular resolution. As such, an estimated angular
resolution of ≈25” is used to calculate the optical functions. In IMD, this is achieved by
calling the function fresnel with the optional parameter ARES, thereby convolving the optical
functions with a Gaussian of width 25” [55].

Ray-tracing
As previously discussed, one of the pillars of this work is the ability to simulate the
performance of a given X-ray optics configuration through raytracing. The software used
to generate the ray-traces for this study is SPORT, the Silicon Pore Optics Ray-Tracer
[73]. SPORT is a software tool developed as part of the SIMPOSIUM project. It builds on
McXtrace [74], the Monte Carlo X-ray tracer, and uses its AstroX package [75] to simulate
the inner workings of the X-ray optics. The simulations in SPORT employ the current
reference design of Athena which uses Silicon Pore Optics (SPO) [76], a realization of a
Wolter-I grazing angle telescope. In this conceptual design, the mirror is segmented into
a large number of mirror modules.
Figure 5.1: Picture of an SPO mirror module. Credit: Cosine
The SPORT GUI allows the user to define a SPO X-ray telescope configuration, set
an experimental configuration and compute the optical response in terms or PSF and
effective area. The software is very flexible and implements the tools to simulate all the
perturbations previously listed. SPORT is an open source tool for Mac OS, Windows
and Linux operative systems. The installation package, the manual and the updates can
be downloaded on-line. However, the specific version of SPORT used for this project
(v0.5.29) was provided by the authors at DTU Space.
A full description of SPORT, its functionalities and operation are well described by
the user manual available on the download page. Nevertheless, the main features are
summarized through the course of this chapter.

5.1
Instrument and component definition
The definition of optical instruments is performed via the Optics Editor. It allows the
user to define an SPO-based X-ray telescope configuration organized on hierarchical lev-
els of petals or rings, mirror-modules, reflective plates and pores. Such a configuration
may be defined from scratch, or it may be predefined from spreadsheet containing geo-
metrical information regarding each reflective plates. It is also in this section where the
user defines the reflectivity tables (simulated or measured), describing the optical perfor-
mance of the coatings design under consideration. For the simulations in this project, a
file containing the ATHENA current MM layout as defined by ESA was provided by Matteo
Guainazzi and Erik Kuulkers. The specific reflectivity files for each coating design were
generated using IMD software, according to the methodology discussed in chapter 4.
Within this reference design of SPORT, several distinct implementations of mirror cur-
vature and wedging are possible. For all ray-tracing simulations performed for this project,
the ”secondary polynomial, wedging -1/1” variant was employed [77]. Beyond the effects
of wedging, SPORT can accurately model many of the optic’s performance losses such
as misalignments or effects of the SPO’s manufacturing that can be predicted.
5.2
Simulation Editor
The Simulation Editor allows the user to specify the experiment configuration. Here,
source characteristics are defined, such as off-axis position, spatial extension, and most
importantly for our purposes, the spectrum of the source.
In this work, ray-tracing is employed exclusively within the evaluations concerned with
the AGN science case, where sources can be defined as point-like and on-axis. When it
comes to defining a spectrum for the simulations, two primary methods were identified:
1. Fiducial X-ray spectrum approach: In this method, users can define a standard
X-ray spectrum representing an astrophysical source, using models from software
like XSPEC. SPORT will then simulate the specified number of photons according to
the distribution described by the input spectrum. The key output here is a histogram
of photons at both the entry and focal plane of the telescope. The performance of
the telescope optics is expressed by a loss of intensity of the photons between these
two points. In practice, this is achieved by exporting the simulated rays as a text file
in SPORT’s options.
2. Flat spectrum approach The alternative method involves setting a uniform spec-
trum across the mission’s energy band (0.1 - 12 keV). Instead of simulating photons
directly, this approach focuses on using SPORT to derive an effective area curve.
The first method comes with several considerations. Primarily, the spectrum gener-
ated at the telescope’s focal plane reflects the optics’ effectiveness. In other words, the
telescope’s effective area influences the final spectrum. However, as we will explore in
more depth in chapter 6, a real observed spectrum includes additional elements: the op-
tical filter response of the telescope, the Quantum Efficiency (QE) of the detector, and the
energy response of the detector. These factors are not simulated in SPORT, and their
omission means that the simulated spectrum would need complex adjustments to mimic
a real observation accurately. Moreover, simulating new spectra with minor variations in
model parameters becomes a repetitive and time-intensive task.
The second method, generating effective area curves through SPORT, also serves to
express the telescope’s optical performance and requires subsequent modelling of the
additional effects. The advantage here is that the effective area curve generated can

be used as an input in XSPEC. XSPEC provides widely used and tested tools such as

The primary tool employed for simulating and fitting X-ray spectra in this project is

as the preeminent software application for fitting X-ray spectra from celestial sources.
Xspec is a command-line-driven interactive program. It is detector-independent, meaning
it can be used with any kind of spectrometer, provided the physical and performance
characteristics of the detector are faithfully modeled.
XSPEC is widely acknowledged as the standard analysis package for X-ray astronomy
and is utilized extensively in missions across various X-ray observatories. Its capability
to handle spectral fitting, plotting, and data manipulation makes it an indispensable tool
for astrophysicists working in high-energy astrophysics and, in many cases, for instru-
mentalists investigating new X-ray instrumentation. The extensive details of XSPEC’s
inner workings and operation are well covered in the user manual [79]. Here, an abridged
version covering the relevant capabilities is given.

6.2.1
Basic models
Relativistic reflection
X-ray reflection from the innermost parts of accretion disks is heavily influenced by
general relativistic effects, causing significant broadening of reflection features. This phe-
nomenon has been observed in numerous active galactic nuclei (AGN), including MCG−6-
30-15 [82], 1H0707−495 [83], and NGC 1365 [84].
Historically, the detection and modeling of relativistic reflection focused on the Fe Kα
emission line at 6.4 keV, a prominent feature of intrinsic reflection. Early models like
diskline [85] and laor [86] targeted this line for non-rotating and maximally rotating black
holes, respectively. However, with advancements in models and the availability of high-
quality observational data, it’s become clear that relativistic broadening impacts the entire
reflection spectrum, not just a single emission line, making a comprehensive modeling
approach essential.
The RELXILL model, developed by Thomas Dauser and Javier García [87], computes
this relativistic reflection from the inner regions of accretion disks around black hole bi-
naries, neutron stars, and AGNs. It models the irradiation of the disk’s atmosphere by a
primary source and the resulting inherent reflection and relativistic broadening. By offer-
ing different versions with varying assumptions, RELXILL allows for the tailored selection
of models to suit specific analytical needs, ranging from simple empirical approaches to
complex, fully integrated ionization gradient calculations.

is also known as the effective area. It is a multiplicative product of the combined

profile across the cluster’s radial extent. This approach not only refines the temperature
profile based on empirical mass and radius determinations but also enhances the model’s
fidelity in capturing the intricate thermal dynamics of the ICM.
The final step of pre-processing involved the simulated cluster’s profiles into 30 differ-
ent concentric rings, or annuli, each defined by an inner and outer radius to the center, a
normalization factor of the emission model (to be used in the simulation), and the scaled
temperature profile. For all simulations, an average spectroscopic temperature of kT = 8
keV and a source redshift z = 0.2 is assumed.

Thesis Conclusions
Baseline coating optimizations and ray-tracing simulations
This thesis has validated baseline X-ray mirror coatings for the ATHENA telescope
as a well-optimized approach, underscoring the significance of over a decade of research
efforts at DTU Space. While our exploration of tailored row-based coating designs reveals
potential for minor effective area improvements, these enhancements are accompanied
by penalties in other energy bands, highlighting a disadvantageous trade-off.
Raytracing simulations have further demonstrated a good agreement with expected
performance losses, showcasing SPORT’s capability to accurately predict performance
declines related to optical design.
AGN and galaxy cluster simulations
The AGN simulations and subsequent analysis indicated that the improvements in
effective area offered by multilayer coatings only minimally enhance the resulting confi-
dence in fitted model parameters. This minimal impact stems from several nuanced fac-
tors. Firstly, the measurement of outflows near AGN predominantly leverages the lower
energy range, where higher fluxes make this segment more statistically significant. This
is further supported by the substantial benefits of adding a C overcoat to the Ir coating.
Moreover, the analysis itself might inadvertently focus on lower energy bands due to their
statistical weight. The chosen model parameters manifest themselves over a large part
of the AGN spectrum, meaning that the lower energy band - more statistically significant -
may be driving the fitting results and thus partly ignoring the spectral features at energies
where the multilayers offer improved performance.
Furthermore, another important aspect of spectral fitting is the instrument’s energy res-
olution. Our findings reveal that assuming a finer energy resolution significantly enhances
the performance of even the least effective Ir coatings, yielding more precise confidence
contours than those achieved with multilayer designs. This indicates that the driving ca-
pability for the AGN science case could be, in fact, energy resolution rather than a local
improvement in effective area.
The clusters simulation initially revealed a similar trend, showing a modest advantage
for multilayer coatings over baseline designs in most parameters, except for velocity dis-
persion, which saw a noticeable precision increase. This specificity suggests that the true
value of multilayer coatings might be best assessed with a focus on particular scientific
objectives, highlighting the importance of a targeted approach in enhancing X-ray mirror
coatings for future astronomical observations.
Outlook and future work
The exploration of X-ray mirror coatings for the ATHENA telescope has opened several
avenues for future research. While this thesis has improved the understanding of how the
X-ray mirror coating designs influence the potential scientific output of the mission, further
investigations are necessary to represent the benefits of multilayers fully. Future research
should delve into the nuanced effects of multilayer coatings on X-ray mirror performance,
focusing on various and more specific science cases. Investigating different models and
parameters, especially those that subtly influence the spectrum of celestial objects, is
crucial. This approach would enable a more detailed understanding of how multilayer
