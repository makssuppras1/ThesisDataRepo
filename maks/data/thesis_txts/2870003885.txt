Abstract
Emotion recognition models struggle with class imbalance, demographic biases, and cul-
tural sensitivity, leading to unfair performance across different groups. This study explores
the potential of Transformer-based models, particularly Vision Transformer (ViT), to en-
hance both accuracy and fairness in Facial Emotion Recognition (FER). We introduce a
fairness-aware ViT framework by integrating data augmentation, architectural modifica-
tions, and a fairness-aware CLS token weighting mechanism. Our optimized ViT model
(ViT+Aug+T3+A3) achieves 82.72% accuracy, which surpasses CNN and VGG while en-
hancing fairness across gender and race groups. However, challenges remain in ad-
dressing age-related disparities. To evaluate fairness, we employ Equalized Odds, De-
mographic Parity, and proposed a Balanced Fairness-Accuracy metric. Results are statis-
tically validated using McNemar’s test. Despite these improvements, limitations such as
dataset constraints and high temporal cost persist. Future research could focus on mul-
timodal emotion recognition, efficiency-focused and fairness-aware techniques to further
enhance equitable FER systems.
Keywords: Facial Emotion Recognition, Fairness, Vision Transformer, Balanced Fairness-
Accuracy Score, Deep Learning

Introduction
Emotion recognition is an important research direction in the field of Artificial Intelligence
(AI) and Computer Vision (CV), and has been widely spread for years [1, 2, 3, 4]. The
technical principle of emotion recognition is to extract, detect and classify emotional states
from different kinds of input data, which include facial images [5, 6], biosignals [7, 8, 9],
sound [10, 11, 12], etc. Facial Emotion Recognition (FER) is one predominant area in
emotion recognition. The development of FER technology has evolved from traditional
methods to modern deep learning methods. Early FER systems mainly relied on hand-
crafted feature extraction methods, such as geometry-based [13, 14] or appearance-
based classification algorithms [15, 16]. In recent years, the rise of machine learning,
especially the emergence of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), has brought new
breakthroughs in FER. By using CNNs, the model can automatically extract hierarchical
features from images, which significantly improves the robustness of FER systems. For
example, DeepID [17] demonstrated the effectiveness of CNNs in facial analysis tasks.
Study [18] further optimized feature extraction, balancing model accuracy and computa-
tional efficiency.
However, fairness issues in AI systems remain challenges. For example, a study analyzed
the fairness performance of models for different racial groups and raised the trade-off
between fairness and accuracy [19]. Similarly, researchers have explored how to intro-
duce fairness addressing method in classification tasks by studying fairness constraint
mechanisms [20]. In addition, many commercial emotion recognition systems have sig-
nificant differences in performance according to race and gender, among which women
and dark-skinned groups are often the most affected groups [21]. This unfairness prob-
lem not only limits the practical application value of the model, but also brings controversy
at the ethical and social levels. Therefore, how to improve the fairness of the model for
underrepresented groups while ensuring the overall performance of the model is one of
the key issues.
The social value of emotion recognition is that it can promote practical applications in
fields such as Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), mental health monitoring, and affec-
tive computing, all of which are based on the fairness and accuracy of the model perfor-
mance [22, 21]. However, as we mentioned above, many current AI models still have
poor performance on underrepresented groups, which make them difficult to apply the
technology to diverse social scenarios. To address this problem, the introduction of Trans-
former provides a breakthrough for emotion recognition technology. The Transformer
model proposed by Vaswani et al. effectively replaced the traditional CNNs through the
self-attention mechanism, significantly improving the ability to model high-dimensional
sequence data [23]. On this basis, Dosovitskiy et al. further extended the success of
Transformer to the field of CV and proposed Vision Transformer (ViT), demonstrating its
potential to outperform traditional CNNs in image classification tasks [24]. These stud-
ies provide new ideas for emotion recognition tasks, especially in modeling emotional
features of diverse populations.
Based on the Transformer architecture, this project will explore its potential in FER tasks,
especially its application value in enhancing the fairness of underrepresented groups
recognition.

Research objectives and hypotheses
To address the above challenges and motivations, this project defines the following ob-
jectives and hypotheses:
Objectives:
• Improve model accuracy. Improve the overall performance of Transformer-based
emotion recognition model by using different strategies.
• Improve fairness for underrepresented groups. Design a fairness adjustment mech-
anism for underrepresented groups so that the model can perform more consistently
across different gender, races, and age groups.
• Explore the advantages of Transformer in emotion recognition. Analyze the poten-
tials of Transformer models compared to traditional CNNs and other deep learning
methods in emotion recognition tasks.
Hypotheses:
• H1: Transformer-based emotion recognition model can improve accuracy and fair-
ness in underrepresented groups (such as specific races and age groups).
• H2: Transformer-based emotion recognition model performs better both on accu-
racy and fairness than traditional deep learning models (e.g. CNNs).
By achieving the above goals and verifying the hypotheses, this project can not only solve
the fairness problem in existing emotion recognition technologies, but also promote the
further application of Transformer models in complex emotion tasks.
Thesis structure
This thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 1 introduces an overview of this project, in-
cluding the research objectives and hypotheses. Chapter 2 gives review of existing work
and applications related to FER, including the overview of recognition techniques, the con-

Literature Review
This chapter reviews three key areas: the current status and trends in emotion recognition
research, fairness issues in machine learning, and the core mechanism of Transformer
models and their applications in FER. Firstly, we introduce an overview of FER, outline the
basic process of it, and analyze the current applications of FER. We also focus on the chal-
lenges in FER in terms of different aspects (e.g., underrepresented groups performance,
data imbalance), and explored the possible future deployment directions. Secondly, in
terms of fairness, we focus on the fairness issue in emotion recognition, and reviewed
several key fairness metrics (e.g., Demographic Parity, Predictive Parity) including their
differences. At the same time, we discuss how to choose an appropriate fairness met-
ric in different tasks. Finally, in the Transformer section, we introduce the Transformer,
especially their self-attention mechanism and multi-head attention module. In addition,
we summarized ViT and its advantages in image processing tasks and applications in
FER. Finally, we analyze the main challenges of using Transformer models in emotion
recognition tasks, such as high complexity. This chapter explores the current status of
research, existing problems, and future trends, which aim to provide theoretical support
for the subsequent research.
2.1
Emotion Recognition
As we mentioned, emotion recognition is a broad field that combines psychology, cognitive
science, and AI. The main goal of emotion recognition is to detect and recognize emotional
states (e.g., happy, sad) using different types of data, such as facial expressions, body
movements, voice tone, physiological signals, and even written text.
2.1.1
Overview of FER
Among emotion recognition, FER has been a prominent research area, which recognizes
emotion through facial expressions.
One of the earliest studies in FER, conducted by Ekman in 1971 [1], explored whether
emotional expressions are universal. His research showed that certain facial expressions,
such as happiness, sadness, anger, and fear, are recognized in different cultures. This
finding was significant because it provided a foundation for later research that aimed to
analyze and classify emotions, particularly through facial expressions. Building on this,
Ekman and Friesen further developed the Facial Action Coding System (FACS) [25], a
method to analyze facial expressions by breaking them down into individual muscle move-
ments, known as Action Units (AUs), associated with specific emotions. This system pro-
vides a standardized way to interpret facial expressions. Since then, FACS has become
the cornerstone of FER research, combining psychological insights with computational
methods. In the early 21st century, affective computing was introduced by Rosalind Pi-
card [2]. In this seminal work, Picard proposed that machines that recognize and respond
to human emotions could impact Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) by facilitating natu-
ral communication between people and technology. This perspective extended emotion
recognition from psychology to practical applications, such as education, health care, and
customer service, where understanding and responding to emotional states may improve
user engagement and experience. These approaches provided a strong foundation for
modern FER research and helped the development of computational models that auto-
matically analyze and classify emotions from facial expressions.

FER systems typically follow a structured process, including face detection, feature rep-
resentation, and emotion classification. Each of these steps involves different techniques
and presents unique challenges. Figure 2.1 shows a detailed explanation of each step.
Figure 2.1: FER system pipeline.
Face Detection
The first stage of FER is face detection, which identifies facial regions in images or videos.
It is the foundation of an accurate emotion recognition. Face detection methods have
evolved over the years. There are a variety of techniques designed to handle the chal-
lenges of scene complexity, facial angles, and environmental conditions. Over the past
decades, face detection has evolved significantly, and transitions from traditional feature-
based methods to deep learning approaches [26, 27].
Early face detection algorithms relied on handcrafted features and statistical models. One
of the pioneering methods is the Viola-Jones detector, which introduced the concept
of Haar-like features combined with an AdaBoost classifier and a cascade structure to
achieve real-time face detection [28]. This method is highly efficient, but sensitive to vari-
ations in lighting and pose. With the advancement of machine learning, researchers have
started using more sophisticated feature representations and statistical learning methods.
A major breakthrough is CNN, which automatically learns hierarchical feature represen-
tations from data. Early CNN-based models such as DeepFace [29] and DeepID [17]
demonstrated better performance compared to traditional methods when applied to large-
scale datasets and implemented deep architectures. Deep learning revolutionized face
detection, with models that achieve high accuracy in complex environments, such as
YOLO [30], RetinaFace [31]. Advances in face detection technology reflect the growing
need for flexibility in FER applications.
Feature Representation
Once the face is detected, the next step is feature representation. This process involves
converting raw facial data, such as image pixels, into a format that machine learning
models can understand to capture emotion-related information. Feature representation
usually consists of three main stages: feature extraction, feature selection, and dimen-
sionality reduction.
The goal of feature extraction is to extract the core information related to emotions from
facial images. It can capture structural patterns, such as the geometric relationship of
facial key points, and microscopic texture changes, such as wrinkles or shadows, which
are important for recognizing emotions and provide rich input data for subsequent steps.
Feature selection aims to select the most emotionally reflective features of the extracted
features and remove redundant or useless information. Dimensionality reduction further
optimizes the feature space by mapping high-dimensional features to a more manage-

able low-dimensional space through algorithms such as Principal Component Analysis
(PCA) [32], while retaining key emotion information. These stages work together to gen-
erate efficient feature representations.
Feature extraction is the core step of feature representation. Generally, feature extrac-
tion methods in FER have been divided into three main categories: traditional geometry-
based methods, appearance-based methods, and deep learning methods that have emerged
with the development of deep learning:
• Geometric-Based Methods. Traditional geometric methods extract key facial land-
marks, such as the positions of eyes, eyebrows, nose, and mouth, to construct fea-
ture vectors representing facial emotions. These methods rely on the assumption
that specific facial muscle movements correspond to different emotions. Early ap-
proaches include FACS [25], Active Appearance Models (AAMs) [13]. AAMs are ef-
fective in constrained environments, such as detecting subtle geometric differences
in posed facial emotions on datasets such as JAFFE and CK+ [14].
• Appearance-Based Methods. Appearance-based methods analyze pixel intensity
variations and texture features instead of explicit geometric structures. Common
approaches include Local Binary Patterns (LBP) [15], Gabor filter [33] are widely
used for this purpose because they can effectively analyze and quantify these local
changes [16, 34]. Examples of applying the Gabor filter and LBP are shown in
Figure 2.2 and Figure 2.3.

2.1.2
FER Applications
FER has been widely used in various fields, taking advantage of its ability to interpret
human emotions from facial cues. In education, FER systems analyze students’ facial
emotions to assess their level of engagement and understanding, dynamically adjusting
teaching strategies to improve learning outcomes [45, 46]. In the field of mental health,
FER systems help diagnose and monitor emotional disorders such as depression and
anxiety, providing clinicians with an effective tool [47]. Integrating FER into multimodal
systems, such as combining facial analysis with EEG data, can further improve the ac-
curacy of emotion recognition by fusing emotions and physiological signals, and address
challenges caused by poor lighting or occlusion [9].
2.1.3
Challenges in FER
Despite significant progress in FER technology, many challenges remain. There are dif-
ficulties faced by current FER systems. Firstly, facial emotions can vary significantly due
to individual differences, including age, gender, ethnicity, and personality traits [48]. The
same emotion can be expressed differently by different people, making it difficult for FER
models to generalize across diverse populations [49]. Secondly, illumination changes,
occlusions, and head poses introduce noise, making feature extraction difficult. Addition-
ally, FER systems can exhibit bias when training datasets lack diversity in demographic
groups, leading to disparities in performance across different races, genders, and age
groups [50]. Ensuring that FER systems are trained on diverse datasets is essential to
mitigate these fairness disparities [51]. More detailed discussions on fairness techniques
in FER will be explored in the next section 2.2.
Looking ahead, the FER field will need to continue to balance improvements in accuracy
with considerations of fairness and ethical deployment. Although deep learning models
have made such progress in emotion recognition, future research will need to focus on
developing systems that are robust and inclusive [49]. Addressing challenges of fairness
issues, multimodal FER systems, and real-world variation will be important to ensure that
FER technology can be ethically and effectively deployed in a wide range of applications,
from healthcare and education to public safety and entertainment.

These trade-offs are formalized by the “Impossibility Theorem” [65], which states that it
is mathematically infeasible to satisfy all fairness metrics when there are differences in
the base rates (label distributions) for different population groups. For example, if Group
A has a higher proportion of positive labels (Y = 1) than Group B, a model satisfying
Demographic Parity (equal prediction rates) may predict more false positives for Group
B, violating Equalized Odds or Predictive Parity.
The choice of fairness metrics depends on the goals and constraints of the application. In
the context of FER, emotion is multi-class and extending these metrics increases further
complexity because of the interactions between different classes, demographic groups
and performance metrics. Balancing fairness requires making trade-offs in the following
aspects:
• If the main goal is to ensure fair predction distribution of different demographic
groups, focusing on Demographic Parity. This metric ensures that each group has
a similar proportion of classification predictions, regardless of the actual label
distribution. In FER, this means ensuring that the probability of assigning an emo-
tion label (e.g., happy or angry) is not disproportionately biased by any particular
demographic attribute.
• If the focus is the reliability of predictions, considering Predictive Parity. This met-
ric ensures that the probability of a correct classification is consistent across
different demographic groups. In FER, if the model predicts that someone is expe-
riencing sadness, the likelihood that this prediction is correct should be comparable
across all groups.
• If achieving fairness in classification accuracy is a key concern, considering Equal
Opportunity or Equalized Odds.
– Equal Opportunity focuses on ensuring that the TPR is equal across demo-
graphic groups, meaning that individuals who actually experience a particular
emotion (e.g., disgust) have an equal chance of being correctly identified
regardless of their demographic characteristics.
– Equalized Odds requires both TPR and FPR across groups. This not only
ensures fairness in identifying correct predictions, but also avoids misclassi-
fications, which is particularly important in sensitive FER applications where
incorrect predictions can lead to biased decisions or outcomes.

2.3.2
Self-Attention Mechanism
The core innovation of the Transformer architecture is the self-attention mechanism [23],
which works by having each word, image pixel, or data point “pay attention” to other parts
of the input and then decide how important each part is. This mechanism is done through
a series of mathematical operations involving three main components: Querys (Q), Keys
(K), and Values (V ). These components are vectors, or lists of numbers, that help the
model assign importance scores between different parts of the input. The components
are briefly introduced here:
• Q: Represents an element that is evaluating the importance of other elements.
Think of it as a “focus point” that determines where attention is focused.
• K: Encodes information about each element so that other elements can “look at” it
when deciding relevance.
• V : Contains the actual information of each element, weighted according to the cal-
culated attention score.

The ViT Models
ViTs are visual models based on the Transformer architecture proposed in [24] in 2020,
which extends Transformer from NLP tasks to the field of CV. And it’s the first paper
that successfully trains a Transformer encoder on ImageNet, attaining very good results
compared to familiar convolutional architectures [67]. ViTs divide images into fixed-size
patches, then flattens these patches into sequences and inputs them into the Transformer
encoder for processing.
Figure 2.6 shows the architecture of a ViT model. The left side illustrates its workflow.
The input image is split into multiple blocks, which are then flattened and linearly projected
into the embedding. Positional embeddings are added to these blocks, and a learnable
[class] embedding (i.e., classification token, also called CLS token) is added to the se-
quence for classification. The resulting sequence passes through a Transformer Encoder,
which learns the relationships between blocks, and then the MLP Head uses the [class]
embedding to produce the final class prediction. The right side shows the structure of the
Transformer Encoder, including Multi-Head Attention for capturing relationships, Add &
Norm for residual connections and stabilization, and MLP for further feature processing,
all of which are repeated in multiple layers to refine the learned representations.

Applications of Transformer in FER
FER is a challenging task that involves recognizing human emotions from facial emotions.
Transformer provide approaches to FER with their self-attention mechanism, allowing the
model to focus on different facial regions and capture the global dependencies between
them. A study proposed a three-stream network based on the Swin Transformer which
integrates visual, temporal, and audio streams to fuse multimodal information for FER [71].
Its method improved the model performance, especially on the Aff-Wild2 dataset.
Some other researchers developed a Visual Transformer and Feature Fusion (VTFF)
method, which uses the feature map generated by a dual-branch CNN and the atten-
tion selection fusion mechanism to convert facial images into visual word sequences, and
models the relationship between these visual words through global self-attention, achiev-
ing new best performance on datasets such as RAF-DB, FERPlus, and AffectNet [44].
In addition, a POSTER network [72] has also shown up. It combines facial keypoint fea-
tures with image features using a pyramid cross-fusion Transformer structure. This inno-
vative design effectively captures both local and global features, achieving new state-of-
the-art results on RAF-DB, FERPlus, and AffectNet datasets.
These studies show that the Transformer architecture, especially its self-attention mech-
anism and multimodal fusion capabilities, has great potential in FER tasks. By capturing
the global relationship between facial features and integrating multiple data modalities,
the Transformer provide a new way to improve the accuracy and robustness of FER.
2.3.5
Advantages and Challenges of Transformer
The Transformer architecture performs well in many areas, but it also faces some chal-
lenges. This section will analyze the characteristics of Transformer from two aspects:
advantages and challenges.
Advantages:
• Global dependency modeling capabilities. The Transformer’s self-attention mech-
anism can directly model the long-distance dependencies of input data, which is
beyond the reach of traditional CNNs.
• Multimodal fusion capability. Transformer performs well in multimodal tasks such
as joint processing of vision, text, and audio. For example, the CLIP model uses
the Transformer architecture to align image and text representations, making break-
through progress in multimodal tasks [73].
• Efficient parallel computing. Transformer uses a fully parallelized architecture,
allowing the model to process large-scale data faster [23]. This advantage is partic-
ularly suitable for modern big data application scenarios.
• Flexibility and scalability. The modular design of Transformer (such as the ability
to independently adjust the encoder and decoder structures) enables it to be easily
extended to different tasks, such as the application of ViT in the field of CV [24].
Challenges:
• High computational complexity. The self-attention mechanism of Transformer
needs to calculate the relationship between each input element, and its compu-
tational complexity is O(n2) [23]. Training the Transformer models requires a lot of
memory and computing resources, which may bring difficulties in actual deployment.

This chapter details the research methods for enhancing FER for underrepresented groups
using a Transformer-based model. The entire method is designed for data preparation,

Data Augmentation
Augmentation
Besides the general transformation, we utilized data augmentation techniques to enhance
the diversity of the training dataset. These augmentations simulate various real-world
conditions, which can improve the model’s robustness and ability to generalize effectively
to unseen data. Below are the key augmentation techniques applied:
• Radom Crop. The cropped region is selected with a scale ranging from 80% to
100% of the original image size, increasing variability of the input image frames.
• Horizontal Flip. Flips the image horizontally with a probability of 50%. This tech-
nique helps the model learn invariance to horizontal orientations, which is especially
beneficial for images where the left and right sides carry similar semantic informa-
tion.
• Random Rotation. Rotates the image randomly within a range of ±10 degrees. It is
used for model to handle scenarios where the subject may not be perfectly aligned.
• Color Jitter. Randomly adjusts the brightness, contrast, saturation, and hue of the
image, which simulate changes in lighting conditions, color distribution, and overall
appearance. It helps the model adapt to diverse environmental conditions.
These augmentation techniques are designed to prevent overfitting and improve the per-
formance of the model [56]. By introducing randomness in cropping, flipping, rotation,
and color properties during training, the model can focus on the underlying features of the
data rather than memorizing specific patterns or conditions.

Elastic Transformation
Elastic transformation is also a data augmentation technique, which simulates elastic dis-
tortions by applying smooth, random deformations to images, mimicking the natural vari-
ations in the shape and contours of objects or faces. By introducing these deformations,
elastic transformations expand the diversity of training data, helping the model to recog-
nize facial expressions under a wider range of conditions. The process of this distortion
Enhancing Emotion Recognition in Images and Videos for Underrepresented Groups Using Transformer 21
==PAGE:30==
contains the following steps:
1. Create a random displacement field to define the magnitude and direction of the
distortion at each point in the image.
2. Smooth the displacement field with a Gaussian filter to ensure realistic, continuous
deformation without abrupt transitions.
3. Warp the image according to the smoothed displacement field to obtain a trans-
formed version of the input.
Figure 3.2 shows an example of elastic transformation applied to an image. The elastic-
transformed image (b) exhibits smooth nonlinear distortions, such as slight changes in
the position of facial features such as eyes and mouth.

To quickly illustrate how such distortions can affect emotion recognition, we used Deep-

results show that the original image (a) is classified as Neutral, and the elastic-transformed
image (b) is classified as Surprise. This might because subtle distortions introduced by
the transformation may exaggerate or suppress certain features, which leads to different
emotion classifications. This highlights the importance of carefully tuning the transforma-
tion parameters to avoid introducing unintended biases in the training data, especially for
tasks such as FER, where subtle changes in facial features may have a significant impact
on the results.
The two key parameters of elastic transformation:
• Alpha (α): A scaling factor that determines the intensity of the deformation. Larger
values of α result in more pronounced distortions, while smaller values produce
subtler transformations.
• Sigma (σ): The standard deviation of the Gaussian filter applied to the displacement
field. A higher σ value results in smoother, more gradual deformations, whereas a
lower σ introduces sharper changes.
Through a grid search [80], the optimal values of α and σ can be identified for the specific
model and dataset. This ensures that the elastic transformations enhance the robustness
of the model without introducing unrealistic distortions that could negatively impact per-
formance.

• Moderate model size. It is the basic version of the ViT series, with a 12-layer Trans-

• Community support. The model has extensive documentation, tutorials, and com-

changes.
• Scalability. Its architecture is scalable, which means that larger or smaller variants
(e.g., ViT-Large or ViT-Small) can be used depending on resource availability and
task complexity. However, this base version represents a sweet spot which offers
good performance while remaining computationally feasible.

be adapted to a variety of tasks. Generally, when training a model on a smaller dataset, it

Equalized Odds
A study [84] demonstrated the application of Equal Opportunity on the RAF-DB dataset,
achieving promising results in addressing fairness. In our project, we opted for Equalized
Odds, as described in Section 2.2.2. Unlike Equal Opportunity, which focuses solely on
true positives, Equalized Odds evaluates fairness by balancing both TPRs (correct pre-
dictions) and FPRs (missed predictions). This makes it particularly suitable for scenarios
where biases in model predictions might affect underrepresented groups.
For example, in an FER context, Equalized Odds can address two common sources of
bias:
• Underrepresented Gender Groups: Consider a case where the model detects
Happiness more accurately for males than females but frequently misclassifies fe-
male emotions, such as predicting Neutral instead of Happiness. Equalized Odds
helps mitigate this bias by assessing both correctly identifying Happiness and failing
to identify Happiness.
• Underrepresented Race or Age Groups: If the model overpredicts Neutral for
Asian individuals or fails to detect Sadness in older adults, Equalized Odds high-
lights these disparities and ensures that the model performs equitably across all
demographic groups.
By considering both types of prediction errors, Equalized Odds offers a robust mechanism
to ensure fairness and reduce biases in model predictions. So we consider it the optimal
choice for this project.

McNemar’s Test
Comparing the performance of classification models is a fundamental task in machine
learning. Traditional accuracy-based metrics may not always reveal statistically signifi-
cant differences between models, especially when their scores are very close[87]. One
common statistical test for such comparisons is the McNemar’s test [87], a nonparametric
statistical test used to determine whether two classifiers have significantly different error
rates when evaluated on the same dataset. The test constructs a 2×2 contingency table
by classifying the test samples according to the classification decisions of two models A
and B, as shown in Table 3.3:

• n10 is the number of samples correctly classified by model A but misclassified by

• Null Hypothesis (H0): The error rates of the two classifiers are the same, i.e., n01 =
n10.
• Alternative hypothesis (H1): The error rates of the two classifiers are different, i.e.,

To determine statistical significance, the calculated chi-square value is compared to the
critical value of the chi-square distribution at the 0.05 significance level, which is 3.84 for
one degree of freedom:
• If chi-square value is larger than 3.84, or the p-value is smaller than 0.05, then
H0 is rejected, which means that there is a significant difference between the two
models.
• Otherwise, H0 is not rejected, indicating that the performance difference is not sta-
tistically significant and may be due to random variation.

RAF-DB Dataset
The Real-world Affective Faces Database (RAF-DB) is a widely used dataset for facial
emotion recognition tasks [75]. It contains 29,672 facial images labeled with both ba-
sic and compound emotions, annotated by 40 independent taggers. The dataset pro-
vides diverse samples with variations in subjects’ age, gender, and race, as well as a
range of head poses, lighting conditions, occlusions (e.g., glasses, facial hair), and post-
processing effects (e.g., filters). This makes it highly suitable for real-world emotion recog-
nition applications.
The RAF-DB dataset used in this project is the basic emotion set (excluding compound
emotions) downloaded from [74]. This dataset includes aligned facial images, containing
a total of 15,339 images divided into 12,271 training samples and 3,068 testing samples.
The aligned images are resized to a resolution of 100 × 100 pixels. The seven emotion
labels are defined as follows: 1 for Surprise, 2 for Fear, 3 for Disgust, 4 for Happiness, 5 for
Sadness, 6 for Anger, and 7 for Neutral. Additionally, this dataset provides manual labels
for gender, race, and age. These annotations include the following attributes: gender (0:
Male, 1: Female, 2: Unsure), race (0: Caucasian, 1: African-American, 2: Asian), and
age, categorized into five ranges (0: 0–3, 1: 4–19, 2: 20–39, 3: 40–69, 4: 70+).

To better assess the results of models’ accuracy and fairness, we created a new met-
ric, which combined the results of accuracy and fairness metrics. We got the inspiration
from [88], where the authors compared relationships between fairness metrics in the do-
main of graphs. We defined a new metric based on a linear combination that reflects
the balance between fairness score and accuracy. We denoted this new assessment
that reflects the quality of the trade-off using ϱ, which often was used as a rate in other
domains [89]. The new assessment score is given by
ϱ = β1 × (1 −F) + β2 × Acc,
β1, β2, F, Acc ∈[0, 1].
(4.1)
Here:
• β1 and β2 are positive weight parameters that control the trade-off between fairness
and accuracy. β1 + β2 = 1.

• Acc represents the model performance in terms of correct predictions.

In our case, β1 = 0.5 and β2 = 0.5, which means that both fairness and accuracy con-

5
Discussion
In this chapter, we discussed the key issues, challenges, limitations, and potential direc-
tions in FER researches, especially for underrepresented groups by using Transformer-
based models.
5.1
Lack of Diversity in FER Datasets
Existing FER datasets (such as RAF-DB, AffectNet, and FER-2013) generally have the
problem of imbalanced classes. Studies have shown that these datasets have too high
a proportion of happy and neutral emotions, while there are fewer samples of emotions
such as fear, disgust [90]. This imbalance in the distribution of emotion classes leads to,
for example, FER models being more likely to recognize high-frequency emotions and
performing poorly on rare emotions. Or, due to insufficient training data, the model easily
confuses emotions such as fear and surprise, anger and disgust [91].
In addition, FER datasets also have demographic biases in terms of age, race, and gen-
der. Most datasets have more samples of young people (20-39 years old), which leads to
poor performance of the model in recognizing the expressions of the elderly [50]. Many
datasets are dominated by Caucasians and East Asians, while the number of samples
from African Americans, South Asians, and Aboriginals is relatively small [21]. These
deviations make the FER model perform unevenly on different populations, which limits
its reliability in practical applications. If dataset diversity issues are not addressed, FER
systems will continue to reinforce biases and may fail in real-world cross-cultural applica-
tions.
5.2
Cultural Sensitivity in Emotion Recognition
Although Ekman [1] proposed a basic emotion theory that human expressions have cer-
tain commonalities around the world, more and more studies have shown that the expres-
sion and interpretation of facial expressions are deeply influenced by cultural norms and
social environment [61]. For example, east Asian culture tends to rely more on changes in
the eye area to express emotions rather than exaggerated mouth movements [92]. Mid-
dle Eastern and Latin American cultures pay more attention to situational cues, that is,
facial expression is only part of emotional expression, and voice tone and body language
are equally important [93]. African and indigenous cultures may express emotions such
as anger and happiness in social situations differently from Western standards [94].
Since most FER models are trained on Western expression datasets, when they are ap-
plied to multicultural backgrounds, the following problems are prone to occur: the clas-
sification error rate of the model in non-Western populations increases significantly. The
model may not correctly identify culturally specific emotional expressions. Incorrect ex-
pression recognition may lead to unfair decisions, such as in the fields of automatic re-
cruitment, emotion monitoring, and security analysis. How to improve the adaptability of
FER models to multiple cultures is a research problem that needs to be solved.
5.3
Benefits and Challenges of Applying Transformers for
Emotion Recognition
Transformers provide greater flexibility for integrating multiple modalities, such as facial
images, text, sound, and external signals such as EEG. This makes them a promising
Enhancing Emotion Recognition in Images and Videos for Underrepresented Groups Using Transformer 53
==PAGE:62==
direction for multi-modal learning research [43]. In contrast, CNN models are very ef-
fective for structured data such as images, but are less adaptable when incorporating
other inputs such as text or sound. And combining features from convolutional layers
with other modalities requires additional engineering work. In addition, the self-attention
mechanism in Transformers enables them to capture long-range dependencies in facial
expressions more effectively than CNNs that rely on local feature extraction. Moreover,
pre-trained Transformer models also allow transfer learning. This enables adaptation to
new emotion datasets with minimal labeled data, making them suitable for applications
where annotated emotion datasets are scarce.
However, Transformers face limitations, especially their higher computational cost com-
pared to CNNs. Transformer-based models, such as ViT, require more processing power
and memory, making them less suitable for real-time applications. Unlike CNNs, which
efficiently extract spatial features through layered convolutions, the self-attention mech-
anism of Transformers increases their complexity. Although reducing encoder layers im-
proves efficiency, it does not fully solve the issue of high computational requirements. In
low-resource settings, such as mobile devices or embedded systems, deploying ViT mod-
els may not be feasible. Additionally, bias in pre-training datasets is another challenge.
Since Transformers are trained on large-scale datasets, any population imbalance (e.g.,
race, age, gender differences) in these datasets can lead to biased emotion recognition
results that penalize underrepresented groups.
5.4
Strategies for Improving Performance and Enhancing
Fairness
Data augmentation is an effective strategy to improve the generalization ability of the
model and address fairness issues [56]. By introducing transformations that mimic real-
world variations, the model is exposed to a wider range of input conditions, helping it
learn more stable and general representations. Our results suggest that data augmenta-
tion can improve model performance, especially for ViT, but its effectiveness depends on
the technique used. Some methods will unintentionally widen fairness gaps (e.g., elas-
tic transformation). Augmentation also does not fully compensate for missing diversity in
the dataset. If certain groups are underrepresented, augmentation may reinforce fairness
issues instead of reducing them. Additionally, real-world factors like lighting and occlu-
sion affect facial emotions, which limits the ability of generalization of augmented data.
In global applications, where environmental conditions vary widely, robustness across
different contexts is necessary.
The structure of Transformer-based models is inherently complex, and optimizing their
architecture is critical to balancing performance and computational efficiency. In our ex-
perimental results, reducing encoder layers is an effective strategy in ViT model archi-
tecture modification, which implies that ViT models may contain redundant layers that
do not contribute significantly to performance. This is consistent with broader findings
in deep learning that emphasize the trade-off between model depth and efficiency. This
optimization is particularly valuable in real-world applications with limited computational
resources. However, modification of core components (e.g., patch size) in ViT signifi-
cantly affects the model performance, which may due to disruptions in feature extraction
mechanisms. These results reinforce the view that while some architectural optimizations
can improve efficiency, changes that affect the core representation learning process need
to be carefully considered.
We applied a weight adjustment mechanism in ViT. CLS tokens play a key role in ViT
54 Enhancing Emotion Recognition in Images and Videos for Underrepresented Groups Using Transformer
==PAGE:63==
by aggregating global information for classification. Adjusting their weights affects how
the model prioritizes global and local features, ultimately affecting accuracy and fairness.
When the CLS token has too much influence, the model may rely too much on the global
representation. As a result, it may ignore finer local patterns that are critical to certain
classes or underrepresented groups. Conversely, if its contribution is too weak, the model
may have difficulty in capturing high-level features, which gives a worse classification
results. Our results show that fine-tuning CLS tokens for underrepresented groups helps
address fairness issues in representation. It leads to more balanced decisions without
sacrificing much accuracy. Future work could explore dynamic weighting mechanisms
where the impact of CLS tokens is adaptively adjusted based on data characteristics,
rather than relying on a static weighting scheme.
5.5
Challenges in the Fairness Evaluation
The fairness of FER models depends on how well they perform across different demo-
graphic groups. To evaluate fairness, we used Equalized Odds (EO) and Demographic
Parity (DP). However, these metrics do not fully capture the complexity of fairness. EO fo-
cuses on error distribution but does not consider the types of errors made. DP measures
outcome fairness but does not account for cultural differences in expressing emotions.
Our results also illustrate that when applying the same strategy to a model, the impact on
fairness can vary: while it improves DP, it does not necessarily enhance EO. Since no
single metric can fully define fairness, future research should explore additional measures
such as disparate impact and equal opportunity.
The trade-off between fairness and accuracy of FER models should also be considered.
High-performing models like VGG achieve better accuracy overall, but still struggle with
fairness in underrepresented groups. Techniques such as re-weighting on ViT help re-
duce fairness gaps but do not eliminate them completely. Adjusting model parameters
and architecture can improve fairness, but often at the cost of accuracy. This raises an
important question for researchers and developers: should models prioritize overall ac-
curacy or fairness performance across demographic groups? Finding a balance between
these goals remains an open challenge in FER research.
For these reasons, we proposed a metric that combines fairness and accuracy. This
metric assigns equal weights to fairness and accuracy, which makes it a straightforward
and interpretable approach to model evaluation. The flexibility of adjusting the weight
parameters allows customization based on specific priorities.
For example, fairness-
sensitive applications can be optimized by increasing the weight of fairness, while high-
stakes accuracy-driven tasks may emphasize the weight of accuracy. However, the linear
combination assumption may oversimplify the trade-off between fairness and accuracy,
as real-world interactions between these factors may be non-linear. For example, small
improvements in fairness may require sacrificing large amounts of accuracy, and vice
versa, making linear weighting potentially imperfect. Our results also illustrate that using
balanced scores can lead to different conclusions than analyzing accuracy and fairness
separately. When considering accuracy alone, the improved ViT outperforms VGG, not
only because of its higher accuracy, but also because of the fairness-aware adjustments
applied to underrepresented groups. This suggests that ViT outperforms VGG overall.
However, when evaluating using balanced scores, ViT’s advantage over VGG is only
seen in specific demographic groups, highlighting the complexity of fairness-aware per-
formance evaluation.

5.6
Limitations in our Work
In spite of the conducted experiments and evaluation of multiple models and transformer
optimizations, our study also presents several limitations.
• Limited Generalization. Firstly, RAF-DB is not fully representative of all popula-
tions, so our findings may not apply to other FER datasets. Secondly, the dataset
we used does not contain compound emotions, which means our results may not
capture the complexity of human emotions. Thirdly, the demographic labels in RAF-
DB may also introduce annotation bias, which could affect fairness assessments.
These dataset-related constraints highlight the challenge of ensuring that FER mod-
els generalize beyond the training data.
• Static Image Constraint. Our analysis only considers static images, whereas real-
world FER applications require tracking facial emotions over time. This means our
results may not fully reflect how models perform in dynamic settings, such as video-
based recognition. In practical applications such as surveillance, HCI, and health-
care, temporal dynamics play an important role. Future research should investigate
methods for integrating temporal information into FER models to improve perfor-
mance in real-world scenarios.
• Definition of underrepresented groups. Our definition of underrepresented groups
is based solely on sample proportions in the dataset rather than model accuracy,
which means that some groups may perform well while others with larger samples
still face bias. Additionally, RAF-DB distribution may not reflect real-world demo-
graphics, which means that underrepresentation in the dataset does not always
indicate real-world minority status.
• Limited ViT Architecture Exploration. In the evaluation of ViT architecture modi-
fications, each modification was tested with only one specific value, which may not
fully represent its overall impact. Exploring a range of values might have different
results. Moreover, each modification was tested independently, without examining
potential effects when combined. Some modifications may work better together,
while others may have negative compound effects.
• Choice of Pre-trained ViT. The pre-trained ViT we chose was pre-trained on a
dataset which is much larger than RAF-DB, which may introduce overfitting and
increase time consumption. However, based on the study of [24], there are ViT
variants such as ViT-small, which might be more suitable for our work as a baseline
model, rather than the one we employed.
5.7
Future Directions
Deeper Exploration of ViT Architecture
As we mentioned in limitations, we did not comprehensively explore the architecture of
ViT. This is because the temporal cost of ViT is really high. However, based on the findings
from our results, optimizing ViT’s structure can significantly impact its performance and
fairness in FER. Therefore, further architectural exploration is worthwhile, as it may lead to
more efficient and fair ViT-based FER models, making them more practical for real-world
applications.
Extension to Video-based FER
We focused on image-based FER, the dataset only contains static facial images. Unlike
single-image analysis, videos provide richer context through facial expression changes
over time. A natural extension of our work could be applying the knowledge gained from

static images to consecutive video frames. By incorporating temporal modeling tech-
niques such as Transformer-based video encoders or recurrent architectures (LTSM), we
can capture emotional transitions more effectively.
Developing New Fairness Metrics
Our proposed balanced fairness-accuracy score is a first step toward improving fairness
assessments, and it can inspire new evaluation metrics for AI fairness research. Future
research could build on this metric to following aspect:
• Extend fairness assessments to cross-bias analysis (e.g., race × gender × age bias
effects).
• Develop fairness-aware loss functions that dynamically adjust to mitigate disparities.
• Create domain-specific fairness benchmarks for FER, sentiment analysis, and health-
care AI to ensure standardized evaluations across key AI applications.
Developing Multi-modal Transformers
Because Transformers do not rely on a specific input format and can simultaneously pro-
cess data in different modalities, an important future direction is multi-modal Transformer
models that combine multiple data sources to improve recognition performance and fair-
ness. Examples include:
• Fusion of vision and audio. Facial expressions and vocal intonation together provide
a richer representation of human emotions. Future models can combine audio-
based emotion recognition with FER to improve accuracy, especially in ambiguous
situations.
• Context-aware emotion analysis. Instead of analyzing facial expressions in isola-
tion, contextual information (e.g., surrounding text or environmental factors) can be
combined to gain a deeper understanding of emotional states.
AI Ethical Concerns in the FER Domain
The real-world deployment of FER models must consider ethical AI principles to prevent
bias amplification and ensure responsible use. Achieving fair FER requires multiple ap-
proaches, for example:
• Fairness-aware training and evaluation. Employing fairness learning techniques
to mitigate bias at the dataset, model, and evaluation levels, and tests models on
different demographic groups to ensure fair performance.
• Standardized Fairness Benchmarks. Establishing consistent evaluation protocols
to ensure future models achieve both high accuracy and population fairness.
• Privacy and user consent. FER models process sensitive facial data, so future re-
search must focus on privacy-preserving techniques to protect user identities and
maintain ethical AI deployment.

Conclusions
In this work, we provided an in-depth analysis of challenges in FER and developed new
strategies to improve model accuracy and fairness. And we enhanced traditional FER by
incorporating fairness-aware learning into a Transformer-based architecture.
During the experimental procedure, we investigated multiple approaches to improve FER
model performance while addressing fairness concerns. The key steps we took include
dataset analysis, where we examined the RAF-DB dataset to understand demographic
imbalances and identified underrepresented groups (e.g., male, African-American, young
children). We evaluated pre-trained models, including ViT, CNN, and VGG, to establish
benchmark accuracy and fairness levels. We also compared different augmentation tech-
niques to see if this strategy could improve accuracy, especially for ViT. Additionally, we
tested six architectural modifications in ViT to study their impact on model’s performance.
To enhance fairness, we introduced a weighting mechanism on the CLS token of ViT for
underrepresented groups. And we computed EO and DP metrics to assess fairness im-
provements and validated results using our balanced fairness-accuracy metric. Finally,
we conducted McNemar’s test to confirm the statistical significance of our proposed mod-
ifications.
Our experiments’ results reveal that the baseline ViT model initially underperformed com-
pared to CNN and VGG, which demonstrates that the pre-trained ViT struggles with FER
tasks without modifications. However, applying data augmentation significantly improved
ViT’s accuracy from 70.54% to 78.13%, allowing it to surpass CNN but still behind VGG’s
82.5%. This underscores the importance of data augmentation in enhancing ViT’s feature
extraction capabilities for FER. Further improvements were achieved through architectural
modifications, particularly by reducing ViT’s encoder layers from 12 to 6 (ViT+Aug+T3),
which further increased accuracy to 79.38%. This suggests that simplifying ViT’s structure
can reduce computational complexity while maintaining strong performance. To address
fairness concerns, we introduced a weight adjustment strategy that emphasized underrep-
resented groups. With an optimized weight parameter, ViT’s accuracy improved further
to 82.72% (ViT+AUg+T3+A3), slightly surpassing VGG+Aug (82.5%). These results in-
dicate that targeted fairness interventions not only help address fairness issues but can
also enhance overall model performance. In terms of fairness, ViT+Aug+T3+A3 exhibited
more balanced performance across underrepresented groups compared to VGG+Aug,
particularly in addressing racial and gender disparities. However, fairness concerns re-
lated to age remained persistent, with ViT showing notable performance gaps between dif-
ferent age groups. This suggests that while our approach effectively improved fairness in
some dimensions, age-based biases remain an ongoing challenge. Statistical validation
using McNemar’s test confirmed the superiority of ViT+Aug+T3+A3 over the baseline ViT
model, with significant differences in prediction accuracy (p-value = 1.29×10−6), indicating
meaningful improvements. The test also confirmed that the optimized ViT model offers
a more stable and generalizable FER performance than VGG+Aug, reinforcing the effec-
tiveness of our modifications. Overall, these findings highlight that Transformer-based
models, when properly optimized, can match and even surpass CNN architectures in
FER, both in accuracy and fairness. However, challenges in mitigating age-related fair-
ness discrepancies persist, warranting further exploration in future research.
The results partially confirmed our hypotheses given in chapter 1. Hypothesis H1, which
proposed that Transformer-based models can improve accuracy and fairness for under-
