Abstract
In this thesis the prospect of using reduced graphene oxide and boron nitride as a protective
coating of aluminium alloys against abrasive and corrosive environments was investigated.
The two materials, characterized by Raman spectroscopy, were added to polyvinyl bu-
tyral (PVB) matrix. The anticorrosive performances of reduced graphene oxide and boron
nitride were characterized by Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) and Poten-
tiodynamic Scan (PDS). The spread in the reproducibility of the results for each material
were discussed. The superior efficiency of corrosion protection was achieved by adding
reduced graphene oxide with a concentration of 10 mg.mL−1 to the PVB matrix. The
PDS results gives a corrosion rate of 2.10−8 mm/year. The EIS results show that this
performance sustains after one week of immersion in 3.5 wt% NaCl electrolyte, with an
impedance modulus at 0.01Hz remaining at 1010Ω.cm2.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Corrosion costs represent approximatively 3% of the national gross domestic product
(GDP) of the United States [1]. Virtually, all industries are affected and the consequences
can have a dramatic impact on humans and environment [2]. Not only expensive, corroded
parts can be dangerous and need to be replaced over time (i.e, let’s just think of a corroded
metal bridge)
Today, many methods are used to reduce or prevent metal corrosion, such as adding
inhibitors or by applying coatings. The anticorrosive coatings can be classified in three
different protective mechanisms, barrier coatings, sacrificial coatings and inhibitive coat-
ings [3]. Sacrificial coatings is based on the galvanic corrosion of another metal (usually
zinc) more electrochemically active than the metal substrate. Inhibitive coatings relies on
passivation of the substrate. The inhibitive pigments inserted in the coating dissolve and
are carried to the substrate surface. The dissolved ions react with this latter to build-up a
protective layer consisting of insoluble metallic complexes. The barrier protection coatings
are the most used for immersed metal. Inorganic and organic coatings can be used as
barrier, the sol-gel technique is widely used [4, 5, 6]. However, performance and durability
of anticorrosive coatings depend on a wide range of parameters, such as type of substrate,
pretreatment, composition of coatings etc.[3]
The increasing interest of 2D materials oriented the research towards new coatings
based on these 2D materials such as graphene [7, 8, 9] and more recently hexagonal boron
nitride (h-BN) [10, 11]. Graphene and h-BN are atomiccaly thick materials with a honey-
comb lattice, and are chemically stable and virtually impermable to all gases [12]. These
two properties make them very good candidates for anti-corrosion coating aplications. In
most studies, a single or multiple layers of graphene (or hexagonal boron nitride) were
transferred on top of a substrate. However, it has been demonstrated that graphene in
direct conatct with the metal to protec actually promotes the corrosion of the substrate
in long term, due to galvanic corrosion effect. [13, 14]. Therefore, the effectiveness of
graphene or boron nitride as corrosion barrier is now subject to debate.
Graphene-based polymer composites are considered as an alternative corrosion protec-
tive barriers. Graphene oxide (GO) and reduced graphene oxide (rGO) are used as filler
in these composites. The production of these coatings is more adaptable to large scale
applications. GO is synthesized from the oxidation of graphite following the method pro-
posed by Hummers and Offeman in 1958 [15]. Graphite is treated by the combination of
sulfuric acide, sodium nitrate and potassium permanganate. The resulting graphite oxide
is then exfoliated to obtain the graphene oxide. During the oxidation, the honeycomb
lattice is preserved and functional groups, such as hydroxyl or epoxy groups, are formed

on the carbon plane, as depicted in Figure 1.1. However, GO does not seem to show great
performance of water permeation compared to rGO [16]. This permeation can be due to
the presence of these functional groups which can interact with water. Therefore, the re-
duction of graphene oxide can remove these groups, at least partially. GO can be reduced
either thermally or chemically. The latter is one of the most common and the mainly
used chemical reagent reduction is hydrazine [17]. The functional groups are reduced, the
structure of rGO is similar to pristine graphene, see Figure 1.1. Moreover, the reduction
of graphene oxide causes the narrowing of the interlayer separation, down to 0.36 nm.

h-BN, as mentioned, is chemically inert and impermeable to all gases. It is an insula-
tor with a large bandgap (around 5.5eV [19]), contrary to graphene which is a semi-metal
[20]. Nowadays, h-BN nanosheets have been mainly studied, and boron nitride polymer
nanocomposites are usually produced to enhance other properties apart from being used
as protection barrier, such as thermal conductivity [21].
The objective of this thesis is to study the effectiveness of rGO and BN, mixed with
a polymer, as anti-corrosion coatings. Moreover, the evolution of this effectiveness with
respect to the concentration of the filler will be analysed.
The comparison between a
conducting and an insulating material could highlight the importance of the electrical
properties of the filler. The preparation of the coatings can be applied to large-scale pro-
duction and makes these coatings, if efficient, valid solution for industrial issues.
The thesis will first present the experimental method. The preparation of the coatings
as well as their characterization will be explained and a more detailed description of the
performed electrochemical tests will be carried out. The presentation of the results alonf
with the discussion will then follow. In particular, the results will lead to a comparison
between the two filler materials, rGO and h-BN. The report will be concluded by comparing
the best performance achieved to the literature followed by the possible improvement and
perspectives of these 2D materials based coatings.

2.1
Description of samples and their preparation
The first process in this project is the preparation of the different samples. This a critical
step because it will have an influence on the efficiency of the coatings. The constituent of
those samples are, the substrate chosen to be a commercial aluminium alloy (AA2024) from
Wilson Metals; a polymer film, the polymer being Polyvinyl butyral (C8H14O2)n(PVB) and
a filler which can be either reduced graphene oxide (rGO) or boron nitride (BN).
The metal substrate is a QQ-A-250/4 AA2024-T3 2x2cm² aluminium alloy, which is
2mm thick. Its composition is detailed in Table 2.1

The rGO powder was purchased from Graphenea, which has been obtained from chem-
ical reduction of graphene oxide. It is a black powder with an electrical conductivity of
666, 7S.m−1 and a density of 1.91g.cm−3. More properties are presented in the data sheet
established by Graphenea [23]. The BN powder comes from Graphene Supermarket. It is
a micropowder with 98% of purity with a flakes size about 5 microns. Since the process
is the same for rGO and BN, the following description of the samples preparation will
concern both of them.
The powder is first mixed in ethanol. Ethanol has been chosen for two reasons: (i)
it is safer to handle than other possible solvents such as Dimethylformamide(DMF) or N-
Methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) and (ii) it gives a better results on the dispersion efficiency.
Indeed dispersion in different solvents has been tried, those solvents being DMF, NMP,
Tetrahydrofuran (THF) and ethanol.
For this comparison 6mg of rGO powder has been mixed in 3mL of each solvent, a
picture of the dispersion in Figure 3.3. rGO in THF solution shows no dispersion, contrary
to DMF and NMP. The following day some aggregation at the bottom could be seen
in NMP whereas in DMF and in ethanol the dispersion seemed stable. This results are
in agreement with Konios et al. [24]. Moreover, another advantage of ethanol is that it
dissolves PVB very well, and this is of great importance since the goal is to mix the powder
dispersion with the PVB solution in order to make the composite film.

2 Experimental Setup and Methods
Figure 2.1: Dispersion of rGO in THF, NMP, DMF and ethanol, in the order of presen-

Therefore, ethanol was chosen to be the solvent of all the solutions made during this
project. In order to have comparable results, the chosen solvent for the BN powder is also
ethanol.
After mixing the powder with ethanol, the solution is kept under stirring at 400 rpm
during 2 hours at room temperature to improve the dispersion of the powder as much
as allowed by this technique. Different solutions with different concentrations have been
prepared:
• for rGO : 1mg.mL−1, 5mg.mL−1, 10mg.mL−1 and 20mg.mL−1
• for BN : 1mg.mL−1, 5mg.mL−1, 10mg.mL−1 and 15mg.mL−1

The polymer is then added to the solution. PVB is added in order to obtain a mass
percentage of 12 wt% with respect to the solvent.
The addition of the polymer powder is progressive in order to let the powder dissolve
in the solution, and is done while the solution is under stirring at 600 rpm. Once all the
amount of polymer is added, the solution is kept under stirring overnight and the solution
is then ready.
The following step is the preparation of the films. The filler(rGO or BN)/PVB solu-

1000 rpm for one minute with an acceleration of 1000 rpm/s. Next, the foil is put on a
hot plate at 60°C in order to cure the coating and remove the solvent residues. After two
hours, the copper foil is placed in an etching solution (100mL HCl 5%, 3mL 30% H202) in
order to etch the copper foil away and to obtain the free standing film with the filler flakes
dispersed in the PVB matrix. The film is then rinsed in DI water several times. The film
is now ready to be transferred on the aluminium alloy substrate. The substrate had been
previously coated, if not stated otherwise, with a layer of PVB. This primer layer of PVB

The samples are characterized by optical microscopy, scanning electron microscopy and
Raman spectroscopy. A description of their functioning is presented in the following.
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
A Scanning Electron Microscope is used to produce a 2D scan image of a sample. It is
composed of two scanning deflectors, an electron source, an objective lens, and a secondary
signal detectors. The electron source fires an electron beam whom a part of it is scanned
by the deflectors, and the secondary signal detectors gather all the information from the
secondary electrons emitted by the sample. Indeed, the electrons carry a certain amount
of kinetic energy, when they reach the samples they interact with it, lose their energy,
and create different signals due to different reactions of these electrons. For instance, the
interaction can correspond to backscattering, transmission, heat, or secondary emission
as depicted in Figure 2.2. The latter is the main used and has been used here for two
purposes: (i) to characterize the coating and (ii) inspecting its surface.

Raman Spectroscopy
Raman spectroscopy is based on light scattering effect. The Raman effect corresponds
to a shift in frequency of the scattered light [28]. Indeed the light that stroke the sample
is scattered at three different frequencies. One frequency corresponds to the incident light,
the two others are shifted (±∆ω) with respect to the incident light. These latter scattering
frequencies compose the Raman spectrum.
Raman Spectroscopy is one of the main characterization techniques especially con-
cerning 2D materials, like graphene [29] and BN [30] because it allows to obtain a lot of
information (number of layers, defects) without damaging the sample as could do the SEM
[31]. It has been used in this project to ensure of the quality of the product bought from
the suppliers, to characterize the initial product.
Optical microscopy was also used for characterization. It has been used for checking
the obtained dispersion, as well as for inspecting the grains size and any possible defects
present in the coating.

2.3.1
Theory
The term corrosion means that the substrate (metal or alloy) has suffered chemical or elec-
trochemical reactions caused by the interaction with the environment. A short description
is made below.
Corrosion consists of two reactions, the oxidation of the alloy and the reduction reaction.
The first equation is the dissolution of the aluminium, described by the reaction of the redox
couple: Al/Al3, usually called the anodic reaction.
Al →Al3+ + 3e−
(2.1)

The reduction of oxygen is the cathodic reaction, it involves O2/OH−redox couple
and is described by the reaction:
2H2O + O2 + 4e−→4OH−
(2.2)
The electrolyte being salt water (NaCl 3,5wt%), the ions Al3+ formed will attract the
ions Cl−from the electrolyte to form aluminium salt.
2Al3+ + 4OH−+ 2Cl−→Al2(OH)4Cl2
(2.3)
This leads to the dissolution of aluminium and as a consequence leads to the formation
of pits on the alloy surface.

energy. Indeed, each electrochemical reaction induces a change in the free energy ∆G =
−EnF [32], where E is the thermodynamic potential, F the Faraday’s constant and n the

reduction) at equilibrium, one can obtain the Nernst equation:

cathodic currents are considered to be positive and negative, respectively. The relationship

Reversed samples
Reduced graphene oxide is a conducting material, although its conductivity is lower than
the graphene one. Therefore, as it is the case for graphene, contact between rGO and the
metal substrate can induce galvanic corrosion of the metal surface.
The primer PVB layer aims to facilitate the adhesion of the rGO/PVB film on the
system but also to prevent any contact between the filler and the aluminium alloy.
In order to assess the role of this primer PVB layer, a new kind of samples have been
prepared. These samples are called reversed samples, where the order of the two layers
composing the system are inversed. As depicted in Figure 3.31, the filler/PVB layer is
transferred directly on the aluminium alloy. A PVB layer is then transferred on top of
this primer, following the process described in Experimental Set up and Methods. The
reason for the presence of this top layer comes from the willingness of keeping a comparable
thickness with the initial systems described in the previous sections.
Figure 3.31: Illustration of the Reversed coating preparation.
Samples with either rGO or BN as filler were prepared. Contrary to rGO, BN is an
insulator. Therefore, this inversion of layer should not change the performance of BN-filler
coatings. On the other hand, a significant decrease of the performance is expected for the

the aluminium substrate. The concentration for both filler is chosen to be 10 mg.mL−1,
because it corresponds to the best potential performance of rGO as a filler. Four samples
have been prepared for each type of filler, in order to gain a useful statistics.

Samples prepared with different concentrations and types of filler have been studied. So

By comparing the results of the two kinds of samples, it seems that the rGO coatings
perform better than the BN coatings.
The former ones show great promises although
the preparation procedure needs to be improved significantly to increase the number of
successful coatings. If the sample does not have intrinsic defects due to the preparation
procedure, an improvement of three orders of magnitude can be achieved and this perfor-
mance sustains even after one week of immersion.
Nevertheless, the thickness has to be taken into account. The rGO coatings are thicker
than the BN coatings which could explain the greater improvement observed.
On the
other hand, the probability of obtaining an efficient coating is higher for the rGO coatings
than it is for the BN coatings. It seems easier to prepare functioning rGO coatings than
BN coatings, due to a more efficient dispersion of the filler in the PVB matrix, or by its
intrinsic properties. The fact that by increasing the concentration of BN in the film the
performance is not enhanced, indicates that the potential of rGO is greater than the po-
tential of BN as corrosion protection barrier.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
This Chapter summarizes the work accomplished throughout this thesis and compares the
results to literature. An outlook for possible improvement and application will conclude
this thesis.
The perspective of large scale application was the driving force of this project. The
preparation process must satisfy two requirements: it has to be easily reproducible and to
produce very efficient anti corrosive coatings. Therefore, the polymer mixing method was
used the task because it uses only three different components, the polymer, the filler (rGO
or h-BN) and the solvent.
Further tests were performed to assess the efficiency of the different coatings prepared.
Coatings with different concentration of filler were prepared and then transferred on top
of aluminium alloy AA2024.
From the electrochemical tests results, discussed in Chapter 3, it seems that overall the
rGO coatings are performing better than the BN coatings. Besides, the efficiency, in terms
of improvement from the reference samples, of the BN coatings does not increase with the
concentration. While the rGO coatings seem to perform very well if the rGO concentration
is increased to 10mg.mL−1. Interestingly, for both cases, if the concentration of the filler
is too high, the performance decreases. However, there is a spread in the reproducibility of
the results which is due to the presence of structural defects on the coating. The variability
is likely due to the dispersion of the filler in the PVB matrix as well as the preparation
steps of the coatings, which need to be optimized and improved.
The great performances achieved with some samples need to be compared with litera-
ture.
4.1
Comparison with Literature
Studies concerning new corrosion protection techniques are numerous and, recently, they
concern the application of 2D materials as protection barrier. As a consequence, the re-
sults presented in Chapter 3, and more precisely the best performance achieved with a rGO
concentration of 10mg.mL−1, is compared to literature. As a reminder, with this concen-
tration an improvement of three orders of magnitude of the coating resistance (|Z|0.01Hz),
with respect to the reference sample has been achieved. The corrosion rate of this sample
is four orders of magnitude smaller. This performance will be compared with two articles,
the work of Wen Sun et al. [50] and the study of Jing Li et al. [51]. These two articles
use a similar method for the coating fabrication and also use rGO as filler. The substrates
of these cases are not aluminium alloy, therefore only the improvement induced by the

The first article uses rGO encapsulated by (3-aminopropyl)-triethoxysilane (rGO@APTES)
as filler. These composites are dispersed in the PVB matrix. The resulting coatings, with
a thickness around 97µm, are placed on copper substrates and immersed in 3,5wt% NaCL
solution. Their performance is compared to a reference sample consisting of a coating with-
out rGO@APTES composites, that is to say only a polymer layer. The best performance
seems to be achieved by adding 0.1g of rGO@APTES. The corrosion rate of this coating
is 6.10−6mm/year, corresponding to an improvement of two orders of magnitude. The
|Z|0.01Hz is three orders of magnitude higher but decreases by an order, in comparison to
the reference sample.
The second article uses GO, rGO and functionalized graphene mixed with waterborne
polyurethane(PU). The coatings, with a thickness of 17µm, are immersed in 3,5wt% NaCl
solution. The best performance is achieved by the addition of 0.2wt% of rGO. This coating
induces an improvement of the low frequency impedance module of two orders of magni-
tude. |Z|0.1Hz is about 109Ωand sustains even after ten days of immersion, while the
impedance module of the reference sample (neat PU coating) decreases by two orders of
magnitude.
In comparison with these two articles, the results obtained from this project are bet-
ter. Moreover, the preparation of our samples is easier and involve less components, this
showing the great potential of our approach.
4.2
Improvement and Perspective
Although the performance achieved during the project can compete with the literature,
the spread of efficiency is too high. The preparation process does not allow to control the
performances of the different coatings. The main parameter to focus on is the dispersion
of the filler in the PVB matrix. A coating with well dispersed rGO can protect the sub-
strate from corrosion, even for a long immersion time. Some improvements can be done
concerning the preparation process.
For instance, it is necessary to understand and control the dispersion of the filler in
the matrix. One could decide to take care of the reduction of the graphene oxide, using
Vitamin C as a reduction agent. It has been shown that Vitamin C can be an more environ-
ment friendly alternative to hydrazine for graphene oxide reduction [52]. Using Vitamin C
seems to prevent the aggregation that can occur during the reduction of graphene oxide,
thus facilitating the future dispersion of rGO in the solvent.
As an additional work, it would also be useful to perform electrochemical test with a
longer immersion time. The objective of corrosion protection is to find a solution which
can sustain for a very long time, avoiding supplementary costs from reparation. Therefore,
increasing the immersion time would give a more accurate estimation of the long term
performance of the coating.
==PAGE:71==
4.2 Improvement and Perspective
55
This project has shown that great corrosion barrier can be made with a rather sim-
ple preparation process, using the chemical stability and impermeability of 2D materials.
Their astonished mechanical properties allow to be considered as very good candidates for
industrial application. If the dispersion of rGO is successfully controlled, coatings with im-
pressive anti-corrosion properties can be easily produced. These coatings, combined with
their mechanical properties, can be applicable to any substrate with any shape.
