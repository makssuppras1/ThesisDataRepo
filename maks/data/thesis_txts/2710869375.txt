Abstract
Animal models are essential within biomedical research, and recently the number of
preclinical studies using zebrafish have escalated. Zebrafish embryos and larvae are
transparent, making them ideal for optic microscopy imaging methods, but as they de-
velop pigmentation with maturity in vivo visualisation methods become limited. This
study explores the capabilities of imaging adult zebrafish using ultrasound, focusing
on imaging of the heart and brain.
An experimental setup is developed to acquire ultrasound images of adult ze-
brafish. Tissue harmonic plane wave imaging is implemented to obtain ultrasound
images at ultrafast frame rates, and power Doppler imaging is used to estimate blood
volume within zebrafish. Spatiotemporal clutter filtering is performed using Singular
Value Decomposition (SVD). The possibility of measuring activity within the adult
zebrafish brain using functional ultrasound is investigated by implementing a visual
stimulation paradigm.
Imaging slices are obtained throughout adult zebrafish creating an atlas showing
the anatomical structure and the vasculature. Furthermore, power Doppler images
of the heart are obtained and spectral analysis is performed to estimate blood flow
direction and cardiac pulsatility. Finally, ultrafast ultrasound imaging reveals blood
vessels within the zebrafish brain.
The aims of this study are largely achieved through the development of a versatile
platform for the ultrasound exploration of intact adult zebrafish, including imaging
of blood flow in the heart and the brain. Further work is needed before brain activity
in adult zebrafish can be measured using ultrasound.

Resumé
Dyremodeller er essentielle indenfor biomedicinsk forskning, og gennem de seneste år
er antallet af prækliniske studier baseret på zebrafisk eskaleret. Zebrafisken er trans-
parent i foster- og larvestadiet, hvilket er ideelt for billeddannelsesmetoder baseret
på optisk mikroskopi, men efterhånden som pigmentering udvikles med alderen be-
grænses in vivo visualiseringsmetoder. Dette studie undersøger mulighederne for at
visualisere voksne zebrafisk vha. ultralyd med fokus på billeddannelse af hjertet og
hjernen.
En forsøgsopstilling udvikles med det formål at ultralydsscanne voksne zebrafisk.
Tissue harmonic imaging baseret på plane bølger er implementeret, hvilket gør det
muligt at optage ultralydsbilleder med ultrahurtige frame rates.
Power Doppler
bruges til at estimere blodvolumen, og spatiotemporal clutter-filtrering udføres ved
brug af Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). Et visuelt stimuleringsprotokol er im-
plementeret for at undersøge muligheden for at måle aktivitet i den voksne zebrafisks
hjerne med funktionel ultralyd.
Et atlas over den anatomiske struktur og blodkredsløbet opnås ved at optage
todimensionelle ultralydsbilleder ned gennem voksne zebrafisk.
Derudover bruges
power Doppler billeder af hjertet samt spektral analyse til at estimere retningen af
blodgennemstrømning samt hjertets cyclus.
Til slut afslører ultrahurtig ultralyds
billeddannelse blodårer i zebrafiskens hjerne.
Målene for dette studie nås i vid udstrækning gennem udviklingen af en alsidig
platform til ultralydsudforskning af intakte voksne zebrafisk herunder billeddannelse
af blodgennemstrømning i hjertet og hjernen. Der er behov for yderligere arbejde, før
hjerneaktivitet hos voksne zebrafisk kan måles ved hjælp af ultralyd.

Introduction
Animal models are essential within biomedical research both to test new medical
treatments in vivo before introducing them to humans and to help understand hu-
man anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology [1][2]. When working in vivo both in
the clinic as well as in preclinical studies, medical imaging techniques are indispens-
able tools for visualising structures and processes within the body [3].
A wide variety of animal models exists, from mammalian models, like the mouse,
to invertebrates such as worms and flies [1]. The mouse model is widely used because
of a genome resembling humans as well as similarities in anatomy, physiology, and cell
biology, but in recent years the zebrafish (Danio rerio) have gained an increase in at-
tention [1][2][4][5]. Even though zebrafish are not mammalian, they share anatomical
structures and physiological mechanisms with humans. Furthermore, with the intro-
duction of developmental genetics, zebrafish have been used to model a wide range
of human diseases [1][4][6][7]. Moreover, zebrafish have the great benefit that devel-
opment spans only a few days and takes place outside the mother, making them very
suitable for embryological and developmental studies [1][4]. Finally, the fetus and
larvae are transparent and can be imaged using optical microscopy imaging methods.
However, as zebrafish mature and develop pigmentation, the penetration depth of
optical techniques is reduced, limiting the visualisation of later developmental stages
[1][8].
Ultrasound imaging has the advantage that it can image several cm through opaque
tissue [8].
For decades ultrasound imaging has been used in the clinic to visu-
alise anatomical structures, and Doppler ultrasound has been implemented to map
changes in blood flow estimating, i.e., cardiac activity [3][9]. Recently, with the utili-
sation of ultrasound plane waves, ultrasound imaging with kHz frame rates has been
reached, known as ultrafast ultrasound or high frame rate ultrasound. The high frame
rates have, in turn, resulted in a significant increase in the sensitivity to blood flow
compared to conventional Doppler enabling imaging of blood flow in small vessels
[10][11][12].
The adult zebrafish has a standard length of approximately 25-30 mm and is thus
much smaller than most mammalian animal models with a correspondingly small
vascular system [8][13]. The increased temporal resolution and sensitivity to blood

modality to image the opaque adult zebrafish, thus extending the usability of the ze-

hemodynamic changes resulting from, i.a. light-evoked neural activity in rodents [14].
Functional ultrasound could pose a usable method to measure higher-order brain

Aims
The motivation of this project is to investigate the potential of using ultrasound to
image adult zebrafish and thus increase the usability of this preclinical animal model
even further. Therefore the specific project aims of this study are to:
• Develop an experimental setup for ultrasound imaging of adult zebrafish
• Explore ultrasound imaging capabilities in adult zebrafish focusing on cardiac
and brain imaging

physiology are presented in sections 1.9 and 1.10, respectively.
1.1
Ultrasound Transducer
Ultrasound images are recorded using an ultrasound transducer; see figure 1.1 for a
schematic of a linear array transducer. Most ultrasound transducers utilise a piezo-
electric material that has the unique property of converting electrical energy into
mechanical energy and vice versa, enabling transmission and reception of acoustic
waves [9]. For linear array transducers, piezoelectric elements (often 128) are posi-
tioned linearly side by side, with an inter-element distance termed the pitch [9][15].
A matching layer is placed in front of the piezoelectric crystals to optimise energy
transfer between the elements and the medium. A backing layer is placed behind
the piezoelectric elements to dampen crystal oscillations and, in return, maximise the
energy transfer and increase the transducer bandwidth. The transducer bandwidth
is the spread of frequencies of the ultrasound pulse. A transducer with heavy back-
ing will result in a short ultrasound pulse with a broad bandwidth [9]. The centre

1.3
Plane Wave Image Reconstruction

1.3.2
Beamforming
A beamformer is used to convert the backscattered echoes recorded by the ultra-
sound transducer into an ultrasound image [18]. A simple delay-and-sum beamform-
ing scheme is described in the following to introduce the concept of beamforming.
However, as beamforming is outside the scope of this project, it is not described in
further detail.
The beamformer is implemented to translate the RF or I/Q signal from the time
domain into a spatial image. First, the time it takes an emitted wave to travel down
to a scatterer at a specific location in a medium and back to the transducer array
elements is estimated. Secondly, the travel time is combined with an assumed speed
of sound of the medium to estimate the location from which the received echo origi-
nates [18].
Conventionally, a coordinate system for a linear array transducer is defined as seen in
figure 1.5. The x direction is set along the transducer array, while z denotes depth.
The coordinate of each element centre is given by: xn = (p/2)(2n −Na −1) for
n = 1...Na and z = 0, where n is a given element index along the transducer array,
p is the pitch (the spacing between the elements), and Na is the number of elements
in the active sub-aperture [18]. The length of the aperture is Da = (Na −1) · p.

1.6.1
Clutter Filtering
The ultrasound signal recorded for blood flow imaging consists not solely of blood
signal qb, but also signal originating from static tissue qcl as well as electronic and
thermal noise qn[28]:

The signal from stationary or slow-moving tissue qc is also called clutter and can
introduce artefacts in blood flow imaging [28].
The motion of tissue is more spatially coherent than blood; when tissue moves at
one position, tissue in the immediate vicinity also moves. However, blood movement
does not exhibit the same level of coherence which is exploited in clutter filtering
using SVD [29].

1.8
Functional Ultrasound Imaging of the Brain
This section outlines the method for visualising brain activity using ultrafast ultra-
sound, i.e. functional ultrasound imaging of the brain.
1.8.1
Neurovascular Coupling
Neurons increase their metabolic demand for oxygen and nutrients when firing ac-
tion potentials. This demand is supported by a local increase in cerebral blood flow
[32][14]. The spatiotemporal link between local changes in neuron activity and related
local hemodynamic changes is called the neurovascular coupling [33][34]. The spatial
resolution of the neurovascular coupling, i.e. the proximity in which hemodynamic
changes occur in response to neuron activation, has been measured to be in the range
==PAGE:40==
20
1 Theory of Ultrafast Ultrasound Imaging of Adult Zebrafish
of ∼100 µm [35]. The electric propagation of an action potential travelling along
neuronal axons happens almost instantaneously, being in the order of microseconds
to a few milliseconds. The hemodynamic response is slower starting at ∼0.3 s after
a short stimulation and peaking after ∼1 s [35][36].
The gold standard for visualising brain activity through the neurovascular coupling
has been functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). Ultrasound methods have
previously lacked the temporal and spatial resolution for functional brain imaging;
however, this was changed with the introduction of ultrafast ultrasound imaging [11].
By visualising the regional changes in cerebral blood volume in response to neural
stimulation Doppler ultrasound can be used to indirectly map activated areas of the
brain [14][32][35].

Cardiovascular System
The main components of the adult zebrafish cardiovascular system are a closed-loop
blood system, the heart, and a separate lymphatic system [4]. Blood and lymphatic
vessels of a zebrafish 19 dpf are seen in figure 1.20. The following explains the blood
vascular system and the heart in further detail.
1.10.1.1
Blood Vasculature
The aorta is the main artery of the zebrafish vasculature. Blood is pumped from the
heart through the aorta and subsequently distributed to the body via arteries, which
branch out into arterioles and finally into a capillary system. In the capillary system,
an exchange of nutrients and waste takes place. Venules and veins of increasing size

return the blood to the heart [4].
The blood consists of blood cells, where, like in humans, erythrocytes are the pri-
mary means for transportation of oxygen and, to some extent, carbon dioxide. As
opposed to human erythrocytes, the zebrafish cell is nucleated, and more oval in shape
[4][6].

1.10.1.2
Heart
The adult zebrafish heart is located on the anterior side of the fish just inferior to
the skull [6]. Zebrafish do not rely on the pulmonary circulation, instead, the heart
pumps deoxygenated blood through the gills for oxygenation and from there out
into the body [4]. The gills are heavily vascularised structures, which are constantly
moving to ensure oxygenation of the blood [6].

2.1
Experimental Setup for Imaging of Zebrafish
This section describes the setup built for imaging adult zebrafish. First, the design
requirements are presented, followed by a description of the individual parts of the

2.1.1
Design Requirements
To enable imaging of changes in blood volume within zebrafish, the zebrafish should
be alive during imaging with a heart rate suﬀicient to pump blood through the vas-
cular system.
The zebrafish should be positioned within the field of view of the
transducer and any movement during imaging should be minimised to limit clutter.
The transducer is positioned far enough from the fish to avoid the near field while
still maximising the pressure received by the target tissue. Given the dimensions
of the zebrafish, a maximum distance of approximately 2 mm between the zebrafish
and the transducer was deemed suﬀicient to limit the amount of signal attenuation
before reaching target tissues. It should be possible to image the zebrafish in both the
sagittal and transverse planes. For functional Ultrasound (fUS) brain imaging, visual
stimulation needs to be perceived by the animal within the imaging setup, therefore,
the zebrafish should have a clear field of view.
Based on the aforementioned considerations, the following key setup design require-
ments are formulated:
1. The animal should be alive during imaging
2. The animal should be immobilised during imaging

4. It should be possible to image the animal in both sagittal and transverse planes

The zebrafish are anaesthetised during imaging using Tricaine. Tricaine methane-
sulfonate (TMS) is an anaesthetic licensing operating on sodium ions entering cells
and thus silencing action potentials. 10 mL Tricaine is added to 250 mL water within
the experimental setup, and the water is stirred for even distribution. Next, a ze-
brafish is added to the water-Tricaine solution and allowed to move freely in the
water tank. The zebrafish is deemed suﬀiciently anaesthetised when it loses its sense
of balance and gill movement slows. The level of Tricaine is tuned to the individual
zebrafish due to variations in animal size. 10 mL is the initial dose, which is incre-
mentally increased until the aforementioned sedation requirements are met. Once
anaesthetised, the zebrafish is placed in the channel of the setup.
After one hour, the experiment is halted, and the fish is euthanised by an ice wa-
ter bath.

of the zebrafish is estimated by identifying local maxima along a manually selected
frequency of interest in spectrogram magnitude over time and calculating the time
between peaks. A minimum temporal duration of 0.5 s between peaks based on knowl-
edge of the zebrafish cardiac cycle is utilised, see section 1.10.1.2. The cardiac cycle
is estimated by taking the average time between peaks in the three ROIs.

Figure 2.13. The steps for fUS processing. A 4 point moving average is applied in the time
dimension on the SVD filtered power Doppler images followed by a min/max normalisation.
A binary mask is created by manually delineating the zebrafish brain. The signal for each
pixel is cross-correlated with a sequence of time-delayed versions of the visual stimulation
pattern resulting in a cross-correlated activation map. A 6 × 6 ROI is manually selected
based on the neural activation map. The fUS signal is obtained by computing the mean
intensity within the pixels of the ROI for each time point.
After identifying brain tissue, activation maps are created by computing the pixel-
wise cross-correlation between the power Doppler signal and the visual stimulation
pattern as described in section 1.8.
Due to a possible delay between neural activation and a resulting hemodynamic re-
sponse, the cross-correlation is implemented with time-shifted stimulation patterns
==PAGE:63==
2.4 Imaging Implementation
43
with delays ranging from 0 s to 5 s in steps of 0.5 s. The delayed stimulation pattern
yielding the maximum cross-correlation value is chosen.
A threshold of two times the standard deviation σ over all cross-correlation scores
is applied on the neuronal activation map. All cross-correlation scores lower than σ
are set to zero. The thresholded activation map is used to manually identify a 6 × 6
ROI within the zebrafish brain, where a peak in blood volume appears to happen
simultaneously with applied visual stimulation. The fUS signal is computed as the
mean intensity within the ROI for each time instance.

Discussion
The key outcomes of this thesis are the development of an experimental setup for
ultrasound imaging of adult zebrafish and an investigation of the adult zebrafish
anatomy and vasculature using ultrafast ultrasound. To the best of our knowledge,
this is the first study to obtain images of the vasculature within the adult zebrafish
brain using ultrasound.
Within the last three decades, the use of zebrafish as an animal model for preclinical
studies within biomedical research has seen a rapid and still ongoing increase [1][4].
The zebrafish has proven useful for human disease modelling, and especially the trans-
parency of the zebrafish embryos and larvae has made the zebrafish an ideal model
for developmental studies using optic microscopy imaging techniques[1][8]. However,
when it comes to imaging the opaque adult zebrafish, we find that the amount of
research is more sparse. Studying organ function and its impairment in fully devel-
oped animals will undoubtedly create new research opportunities in biology [1]. The
advantage of this study is the focus on imaging adult zebrafish individuals creating
images to expand the understating of their anatomical structure and their vasculature.
Additionally, this study shows the possibility to characterise adult zebrafish cardiac
physiology and takes the initial steps towards monitoring neural activity within the
adult zebrafish brain indirectly via the neurovascular coupling.
A critical point of performing ultrafast ultrasound Doppler imaging is the ability
to successfully discriminate between blood and tissue signals which can be accom-
plished through SVD clutter filtering. However, separating blood signals from clutter
and noise remains a challenge [29]. In this study, the sub-spaces of the similarity ma-
trix are rarely perfectly separable, and the exact number of spatial vectors to exclude
becomes a manual assessment. The hypothesis that tissue and blood have perfectly
separable spatial distributions might not hold in all cases, which could explain the
blurred borders between sub-spaces. Baranger et al. noted that for high-frequency
imaging of blood in small animals, which the zebrafish is, the vasculature might cover
a large portion of the field of view resulting in spatial singular vectors to be more corre-
lated between sub-spaces [29]. Furthermore, isolating blood signal proved challenging
in frames containing the gills, which are naturally moving targets. Considering that
blood and tissue signal is not perfectly separated, the outline of vessels within the
zebrafish are detectable.
==PAGE:80==
60
Discussion
An advantage of this study is the use of zebrafish as an animal model for Doppler
ultrasound imaging. The adult zebrafish is much smaller than mammalian animal
models, such as rodents, which pushes the abilities of spatial ultrafast ultrasound
resolution. Additionally, the size of the zebrafish allows the imaging of multiple or-
gans simultaneously. This study showed that it is possible in the sagittal imaging
plane to capture blood vessels within the brain and the zebrafish heart in a single
imaging frame. Another advantage of this study is that the zebrafish skull is thinner
than, e.g. rodent skulls, which in theory should reduce the limitations of transcranial
imaging [48]. However, some artefacts were seen in the cranial area of the zebrafish.
Slight horizontal smearing above the skull interface is observed along with a parabolic
shaped artefact covering the zebrafish, which could be explained by the shape of the
skull interface and the relatively high impedance mismatch between bone and soft
tissue.
For ultrafast Doppler imaging, the zebrafish needs to be alive, which necessitates oxy-
genated water surrounding the zebrafish gills [4]. Additionally, as ultrafast Doppler
imaging tracks moving structures, anything except blood cells should be kept as
static as possible [29]. Ultrafast ultrasound imaging of a living zebrafish completely
submerged in water proved a challenge as bright spots with descending trajectories
appeared in the reconstructed images in areas outside the zebrafish. Micro air bub-
bles being pushed down by the pressure from the emitted ultrasound waves could
explain the bright spots. The acoustic impedance mismatch between air and water
is high, resulting in air bubbles appearing bright in the ultrasound images [9]. Fur-
thermore, bright spots also appeared randomly within the zebrafish, which could be
caused by reverberation causing air bubbles to bounce between the zebrafish skull and
the transducer resulting in the signal mistakenly being reconstructed as originating
from within the zebrafish. The aforementioned proved an issue, especially for func-
tional ultrasound studies within the brain. The issue was solved by only submerging
the lower half of the fish containing gills in water and using ultrasonic contact gel
between the zebrafish head and the transducer, which both kept the zebrafish alive
while eliminating moving air bubbles.
To limit animal movement, zebrafish are anaesthetised, which is a possible limita-
tion of this study. Zebrafish vary in size since no requirement is put on the genotype
or sex for this study. As a result, the necessary anaesthesia dosage differs between
animals. A too low dosage causes movement artefacts in the images and spikes in
the continuous functional ultrasound data. Moreover, for triggering light-evoked ac-
tion potentials anaesthetisation is a possible limitation as it reduces brain activity.
However, an advantage of this study is that by anaesthetising adult zebrafish, it
was possible to secure a steady imaging environment which allowed the successful
acquisition of ultrafast Doppler ultrasound data of blood flow in the zebrafish body,
including the heart and the brain.
==PAGE:81==
Discussion
61
We did not succeed in detecting significant neural activation through the neurovas-
cular coupling with the current visual stimulation paradigm. Several parameters of
the implemented stimulation protocol can explain the absence of activation. We use
flashing red light with a wavelength of 625∼630 nm, which has previously been used
to stimulate mice and zebrafish larvae in similar studies [14][49]. However, zebrafish
larvae studies show a higher density of neurons responding to UV or blue light [50].
Additionally, the distance between the retina and the flashing LED is several cm,
which might result in lower activation. The type of stimulus can also help explain the
lack of activation. Flashing lights have proven a beneficial stimulation in rodents, but
studies show that only a small subset of neurons fire in response to bright flashes for
zebrafish larvae [51][52]. Finally, in this study, the optic tectum is chosen as the tar-
get brain structure as most retinal ganglion cell axons have been shown to terminate
here. However, the outer part of the brain where the optic tectum is situated does
not appear to be heavily vascularised [51][53]. However, the aforementioned possible
limitations are mainly based on studies of the zebrafish larvae.
In the future, to enable imaging of brain activation in adult zebrafish using ultra-
fast Doppler imaging, the parameters for the visual stimulation protocol and target
brain area should be investigated further to tailor the stimulation to this specific
animal model. After consultation with the Kamermans Group at the Netherlands
Institute for Neuroscience, which studies vision in zebrafish, it is concluded that us-
ing white light with a slower flashing frequency and positioning the light closer to
the retina is likely to elicit a stronger response in the zebrafish brain. Other visual
stimulation paradigms, which trigger more natural pray capture or predator escaping
responses, could also be investigated. In addition, as most studies of the zebrafish
brain focus on zebrafish larvae, a better understanding of the adult zebrafish brain
structure and visual system is also needed.
This study shows the possibility of imaging blood flow in multiple major organs simul-
taneously using ultrasound, which is not possible in usual animal models or humans,
and the possibilities of this should be investigated further in future studies, such as
exploring a possible neuro-cardiac coupling.
With the continuing advances of volumetric ultrasound imaging using matrix array
transducers, the setup and imaging sequences implemented in this study could be ex-
panded to imaging of the adult zebrafish in 3D. An even larger area of the zebrafish
could be covered with a 3D transducer enabling imaging of most major organs with-
out the need of microscanning.
Even though there are limitations associated with this study, I succeeded in develop-
ing an experimental setup for ultrasound imaging of adult zebrafish and exploring its
structural and vascular anatomy. Furthermore, ultrafast ultrasound imaging of adult
zebrafish can potentially expand the usability of this preclinical animal model even
further, in order to study biological processes in fully developed specimen.

Conclusion
This study aimed to develop an experimental setup for ultrasound imaging of adult ze-
brafish and establish the capabilities of ultrasound imaging in this new animal model,
including an exploration of cardiac and brain imaging using ultrasound. These aims
were largely achieved by designing and building a setup facilitating ultrasound imag-
ing of adult zebrafish anatomy and physiology. The final experimental setup allows
for the acquisition of ultrasound images while keeping the zebrafish fixated and im-
mobilised. Several iterations of the imaging setup led to experimental conditions that
successfully minimise ultrasound artefacts.
Our results show that with the final setup, it is possible to obtain THI frames of
anatomical structures and power Doppler images revealing blood flow within the
adult zebrafish, including the heart and the brain. The images were, in turn, further
processed to reveal information about zebrafish physiology. In summary, this study
presents a versatile platform for the ultrasound exploration of intact adult zebrafish
in a laboratory context.
Future work is needed to develop stimulation paradigms that elicit functional re-
sponses in the adult zebrafish brain before brain activity in adult zebrafish can be
measured using ultrasound. In conclusion, ultrafast ultrasound imaging of adult ze-
brafish could expand the usability of this animal model within preclinical research
even further.

A.2.3
Attenuation
Attenuation is the loss of intensity a sound wave experiences due to a loss of en-
ergy as it travels through a medium [56][27][16]. Attenuation is caused by reflection,
refraction, scattering, and absorption and can be modeled as:
p(z) = p0e−αz.
(A.11)
α depends on the type of propagation medium and frequency:
α(f) = α0 + α1f.
(A.12)
The local particle oscillations increase with increasing frequencies of the propagat-
ing wave, and with increased oscillations follows a higher grade of absorption and
attenuation [16]. α0 and α1 are tissue dependent constants [16].
