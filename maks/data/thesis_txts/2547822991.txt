Abstract 
 
The relatively new concept of circular economy (CE) aims to establish a new economic system 
where the resources and materials are being circulated in a closed loop. This concept helps 
both the reduction of further extraction and waste of resources as well as the establishment 
of new business models for a shared economy. This thesis aims at evaluating two options for 
a recreational swimming and activity pool in Nyborg that will be at service throughout the 
year. A baseline option O1 provides a warm freshwater swimming pool and a seawater 
activity pool without heating. A circular alternative option O2 provides both swimming and 
activity pools which operate with warm seawater used in cooling process of a waste 
management plant in Nyborg. Therefore, the partnership between this company and Nyborg 
municipality sets an example of a business model stablished based on resource sharing.  
 
This study investigates the viability of the circular option O2 from 3 sustainability perspectives 
and attempts to find the challenges and opportunities compared to the baseline option O1 
over a project lifetime of 30 years. Cost-benefit analysis, multi-criteria decision analysis, and 
environmental impact assessment were used in this study to build a quantitative decision 
support system (DSS). Stakeholder assessment and RESolve framework were also utilized as 
supporting tools.  
  
The socio-economic evaluation made upon cost-benefit analysis shows that the circular 
option O2 is by 54%  better than the baseline option O1 comparing their net present values 
(-70.7 M DKK for O2 and-155.4 M DKK for O1). This is driven by the 60% lower operating cost 
compared to the baseline project, despite its 34% higher initial investment cost at the 
beginning. Moreover, the O2 project’s economic performance is better in regard to an 
amount of 17 M DKK energy savings each year from heating of the pools which is also the top 
gain of the project. Two thirds of the direct benefits (32 M DKK out of 48 M DKK) of the circular 
project are supporting the circular economy concept. Moreover, the labor needs are lower 
than the O1 baseline due to the simplicity of the operation. The assessment also highlighted 
the importance of the information exchange between the involved parties in the business 
model.  
 
In the multi-criteria decision analysis, the performance of 8 qualitative criteria were measured 
where 6 of them were directly related to the circular economy. The assessment supported a 
conclusion that the O2 circular was better than the O1 baseline in the transition to a circular 
economy. Public perception and accordingly reuse related end-user acceptance of the project 
remains as one of the major challenges. There are also opportunities such as the elimination 
of legislative barriers which would allow for subsequent water and heat reuse practice (i.e. in 
urban water systems).  
 
Environmental impact assessment has followed a quantitative assessment methodology to 
measure environmental externalities of the circular option O2 at the operation stage. Three 
categories were assessed from the list of impact categories: freshwater depletion (FD), 
climate change (CC), and marine eutrophication (ME). The results showed that the CE project 
has a 25% better environmental performance for FD and 64% for CC, while it is 60% worse for 
ME than the baseline. Again, the energy savings from the heating of the pools were the top 
benefit also for the environment.  Another important finding of this analysis is that the shower

1. Introduction   
As the resources are being depleted and the current industrial system is not regenerative, a 
call for new economic models has become inevitable. Circular economy - a new hope 
regarding the solution- is a concept based on three principles:  Design out waste and 
pollution, keep products and materials in use and regenerate natural systems. (Ellen Mac 
Arthur, 2019) Circular economy is the world’s new megatrend where the private sector is 
taking an active role in reducing the resources in use as well as developing a new sustainable 
production system.  Reusing, recycling, and recirculation of resources have become the 
defining character of these business models. Both public and private sectors are progressively 
introducing new strategies, policies, partnerships, and new business models. Moreover, 
cities, corporations and citizens have an increasing joint interest in elimination of waste, 
creation of well-established markets for used products and promotion of reuse as well as a 
sharing economy.  (Danish Cleantech Hub, 2019)  
Water and energy are the two most basic needs of modern civilization (Webber,2008) that 
are intertwined. More water requires more energy and vice versa if you need an abundant 
supply of water to produce energy (Schnoor, 2011). In a world where water is being looked 
at very traditionally in a linear fashion, the “Take-Use-Discharge” strategy is commonly 
adopted in the water sector. However, this unsustainable human-managed water use 
approach goes against all the three circular economy principles (Arup, white paper 2018). In 
the same manner, this is also a valid argument regarding the high energy consumption level 
of the industry.   
 
Several decision-support tools are being developed for the evaluation of circularity. There are 
a number of specific methods that supports strategy building and decision making in order to 
integrate and measure circularity of materials, products or even entire companies. Moreover, 
shifting to a sustainable circular economy necessitates assessment methods that can be 
applied in all levels from product design level to a larger systems as well as entire economies. 
(Haupt and Hellweg, 2019).  It is problematical that, circular economy is generally by default 
assumed as environmentally friendly with increased resource efficiency compared to a linear 
economy without an assessment and this may result in erroneous decisions. A sustainable 
circular economy decision system should adopt all three aspects of sustainability; 
environment, economic and social.  (Haupt and Hellweg, 2019).  
This MSc. study covers a research and an evaluation of a project idea that is grounded on a 
collaboration between a private waste management company, Fortum and Nyborg 
Municipality.   Nyborg municipality wants to make a recreational, all year around open, public 
swimming and activity pools to establish training facilities for citizens as well as to attract 
more people in the harbor area. According to the agreement, Fortum will give away clean and 
warm seawater that is being circulated in its cooling water system for further use in the pools. 
Therefore, collaboration between the municipality and the company will set an example of a 
circular business model that keeps the water and energy in loop via resource sharing.  
This research work investigates the public swimming pool project based on a resource reuse 
as a case study comparing with a baseline project that represents a traditional swimming pool 
system. The study not only evaluates the project in a holistic way by employing financial,

2. Background

2.1. 
Swimming pool systems  
A swimming pool or a paddling pool, which is simply known as pool, is a structure that is 
designed to hold water for swimming or other leisure activities. (Wikipedia, 2020a) Swimming 
pools can be in all shapes and sizes, but almost all of them from domestic type to the Olympic 
work in the same basic principle. A typical swimming pool has following components:  A pool 
basin, a circulation system, filtration, chlorine dosage and pH adjustment system as well as a 
heating system. (Figure 1). (Hansen, 2013). In any type of pool having more than 5 m3 of 
water, the water is pumped in a continual cycle from the pool through the filtration and 
chemical treatment and back to the pool again. The circulation is needed to keep the water 
free of dirt, debris and microorganisms. It is also possible to include heaters in the system. 
Moreover, it must be possible to add up the water lost by evaporation, dilution, backwash of 
the filters and bathers carrying the water out of the pool on their swimming suits. 
(PWTAG,2009).  
 
 
Figure 1.Schematic drawing of a typical swimming pool with water treatment system. (Hansen, 2013) 
Water circulation and treatment  
As a general operational practice, the water is withdrawn from the bottom of the pool 
through the drain outlets. These outlets are usually the lowest point in the pool and entire 
pool floor surface slopes towards them. Most of the pollution such as dirt and debris leave 
the pool from these outlets while some debris have to be vacuum cleaned. The surface water 
which mostly includes the floating materials such as hair and skin cells will leave the pool from 
the surface by surface water draw off system which takes the water at the top of the pool 
into the transfer channel transferred to a balance tank. (PWTAG,2009) Today 80% of the 
public pools are working with this principle which is called deck-level principle.  The water is 
drawn out from the pool outlets by circulation pumps and returned to the pool through the 
pool inlets after filtration and treatment systems. (PWTAG,2009)

Filtration 
During filtration process, the polluted water is pumped down through the filter bed and the 
insoluble pollutants that can be filtered are captured by the small sand particles. When the 
water flow slows down due to the debris collected in the sand, the pressure level increases 
on the inlet pipe gauge and this means it is time to backwash the filters and water from the 
pump pushes up the water through the sand. The dirty water flows out through the pipe at 
the top of the filter tank where it ends up in the sewer.  (PWTAG,2009) 
 
Disinfection and free chlorine 
Chemistry takes a role in order to keep the pool water safe and clean and thus the chemical 
disinfection takes place in the water treatment system. It is important to control the pool 
chemistry for several reasons such as pH adjustment and disinfection of disease-causing 
microorganisms such as bacteria and viruses contained in the untreated water.  It is also 
important to keep the balance of chemistry so that low chlorine and high pH does not lead to 
cloudy water as well as to establish the appropriate levels of chemical concentrations in the 
pool that are important for human health and to protect the pool equipment from damage. 
(PWTAG,2009)  
 
Danish legislation on swimming pool water quality  
The pool water chemistry must be appropriate for disinfection, safe for swimming and pool 
materials. Therefore, microorganisms, pH, alkalinity, hardness and dissolved solids are the 
important parameters to maintain and requires measurements regularly.  (PWTAG,2009) 
Depending on the pool and the country regulating the pool water standards, the residual free 
chlorine concentration in the pools varies.  
The regulation on the swimming water quality and hygienic conditions in freshwater and 
seawater swimming pools in Denmark is provided by Statuary order no. 918 where 
recirculation and turnover period, disinfection, filtration and the backwash water use have 
been regulated. According to this regulation, the low chlorination in the pools having 
temperature below 34 C means that the free chlorine should be in the range of 0,4 mg/L and 
0,8 mg/L. In the ordinary pools free chlorine is allowed to be as high as 1,5 mg/L. pH should 
be between 6,8 and 7,6. (Appendix 1 Table 1a)   
 
Hydraulic design of swimming pool systems is also an important consideration and it is 
designed based on turnover period and recirculation rates. These two parameters have also 
been limited with this regulation. Circulation rate and turnover period are used to dimension 
the pool water treatment system including pump size selection and piping and heat 
exchangers for the heating as well as chlorination and filtration capacity. Turnover periods for 
the allowed temperatures in the basins and the depth of basins and the maximum time that 
it takes to pass the entire water volume to the treatment unit was limited to 5 hours for pools 
with temperatures below 29 C. The circulation rate that should comply with the turnover 
period for the same temperature is given 2 m3/hour/person in the pool. (Appendix 1 Table

Water use  
Turnover period is the time that it takes for entire volume of water in the pool to pass through 
the filtration, treatment plant, heaters and back to the pool again. The shorter the turnover 
time, the more frequent is the water treatment. According to PWTAG, the turnover period 
for the public pools up to 25 m long with 1 meter shallow ending should be 2 hours 30 minutes 
to 3 hours (PWTAG, 2009) whereas according to Danish regulations the turnover rate is given 
as 0,4 hour, 2 hours and 5 hours for pools below 1,5 m depth, 0,5 to 1,5 m depth, and above 
1,5 m depth, respectively. (Statuary order 918, 2016) 
PWTAG provides the amount of water needed in the swimming pool systems. The unit 
consumption for dilution is recommended as 30 L/user in order to minimize the build-up of 
pollutants from bathers or by products from disinfection. Moreover, there is also water 
requirement for backwashing of the filters. According to PWTAG, 2006, backwash is 
recommended once a week, 30 m/h for 5 minutes.  The water displacement by the users of 
the pool adds up on that with 80L/user (PWTAG,2009).  In addition to the technical water, the 
water will be consumed for pre and after swim showers. Results from a research by 
Maglionico and Stojkov suggests that the amount of water used in the showers are found to 
be 44.6 L/user.day. (Maglionico and Stojkov 2015)  
 
Swimming pool temperatures and heating  
According to PWTAG, the pool water is recommended to be between 26 C and 28 C for 
training, competitive swimming, diving, and fitness swimming. It can be 1 or 2 centigrade 
degrees below these values; however, it is not recommended to exceed this range for some 
technical arguments including the fact that microorganisms can multiply faster, bathers can 
get hotter and sweat more. Consequently, the increase of perspiration can rise the urea and 
ammonia levels in water and form more chlorine by-products which increases the chlorine 
demand for disinfection, and all of these can lead to complications in the pool environment. 
(PWTAG, 2020)  
 
The heat balance of the outdoor pools depends on the weather conditions such as humidity, 
wind speed, air and sky temperature and solar radiation. (Dannemand et al., 2017) although 
there are only limited literature on year around warm outdoor swimming pool systems, 
according to a comprehensive study from Mousia and Dimoudi  on the energy performance 
of 27 different open air swimming pools in Greece, the average annual thermal energy 
consumption has been found 2.686 kWh/m2 for natural gas fueled system. (Mousia and 
Dimoudi, 2015) 
 
Alternative applications in the swimming pool systems  
The pool water systems where the recirculation and treatment of the pool water are deemed 
as standard applications can be designed and operated differently and for example the 
alternative flow types, water and heat sources can be utilized in the swimming pool basins.  
Pools and spas may use either seawater or potable water as the source water.  
 
The peak number of bathers and the total number of bathers that can use the pool in one day 
are governed by the area of pool and the turnover period of the water volume. Therefore, 
the seawater pools should be designed with special attention on the maximum bathing load 
in order to eliminate the health risks. In the non-disinfected pools, the microbial colonization

Construction and operation costs of the swimming pools and related concerns  
The budget for the construction of a swimming pool is an important consideration as well as 
the operational costs in terms of water, chemicals, labor and energy. A swimming pool is an 
expensive service due to the fact that a large volume of water needs to be continually 
pumped, filtered, backwashed  treated, and heated to 26-28 C ((Seneviratne 2007, PWTAG,

the many bathers attending the facility and water efficient measures are important towards 
water conservation in swimming pools while the minimization of overall water consumption 
results in the saving of this precious source as well as in energy use and costs.  (Pasuarella et. 
al. 2017). Hence, the entire life cycle costs are important before reaching a decision. 
 
2.2. 
Waste heat recovery and trends in urban water systems  
 
High amount of heat from industrial processes and from energy production is lost and it can 
be found in many forms such as hot water going into the sea or steam going into the air. (Alfa 
Laval, 2019) For many municipalities and energy companies use of waste heat or the excess 
heat are untapped opportunities. Nowadays, many of these losses can be captured and 
distributed by the district heating systems to heat the buildings. While the same fuel 
accomplishes to provide twice the work, the fuel efficiency also doubles and thus it 
demonstrates tremendous opportunities for other communities from an environmental as 
well as a financial point of view. (Alfa Laval, 2019) 
 
The current trend is the heat recovery from urban water systems. Due to the fact that 15% of 
the thermal energy supplied to buildings are lost through the urban sewer systems, there are 
several ongoing researches that investigate possibilities to recover the thermal energy from 
the sewer water as alternative source of heat. (Cipolla and Maglionico, 2014) Moreover, a 
recent study in 2019 by Hubech Graudal et al. investigated the potential of water supply 
systems as a low heat source in the district heating system for the city of Copenhagen. In the 
study the use of heat pumps for the extraction and delivery of energy from the water network 
to the district heating was analyzed.  (Hubeck Graudal et al, 2019)  
 
2.3. 
Circular economy  
 
Circular economy (CE) is a concept that is currently studied by many governments such as 
European Union, Denmark, Sweden, China as well as by several businesses and business 
development agencies around the world such as Ellen McArthur Foundation(EMAF), Danone, 
Carlsberg. It is defined as a “framework for an economy that is restorative and generative by 
design” by the EMAF who also identified design, business models, reverse cycles and enabling 
conditions to be the 4 building blocks for transition to a circular economy. (Ellen MacArthur, 
2020) 
 
The volume of annual economic gains from the transition to this circular type of economy 
were estimated as 600 million euros for manufacturing sector alone by the European 
Commission and  CE is still developing and its practices are almost exclusively being developed

This section is dedicated to the circular economy concept, its objectives, challenges and 
relation with the water cycle.  
 
The limits of linear consumption 
Companies extract materials, use energy and labor to manufacture a product and sell it to 
their customers who discards it when it no longer serves its purpose. This take-make-dispose 
pattern demonstrates a linear model of resource consumption that has not move beyond this 
fundamental manner since established in the early days of industrialization. Even though 
there have been great efforts to improve the resource efficiency, this system which is based 
on consumption rather than restorative use of resources entails significant losses all along 
the value chain. The resource prices have been increasing after the start of the new 
millennium which made the companies notice the risks of rising and less predictable resource 
market conditions. The price volatility especially in the metals, food, non-food and agricultural 
outputs in the first decade of 2000s was higher in any single decade in the 1900s. Together 
with the urbanization and growth in population, environmental costs have also increased 
because of the rise in the depletion of natural capital. In the search of a “better hedge” of the 
business leaders against this scenery, the circular economy has been offered as a new 
industrial model that decouples the sales revenues from material input (Ellen McArthur, 
2013)  
 
Circular economy as a system  
A circular economy is being conceptualized and studied by EMAF since 2010 which has a 
leading role in spreading the circular economy vision. The term circular economy stands for 
an industrial economy that is “restorative by intention and design” and this new economy 
concept is based on three principles, “design out waste and pollution, keep products and 
materials in use and regenerate natural systems” (Ellen Mc Arthur, 2020).  The main 
characteristics of the system are based on systems thinking, designing out waste or using it 
as food, embedding diversity, and running closed loop renewable energy systems. A circular 
economy would rebuild capital regardless of this being financial, manufactured, human, social 
or natural as the system diagram (Figure 2) illustrates the continuous technical and biological 
materials flow through the value circles. (Ellen Mc Arthur, 2020).

2.4. 
Decision support systems and tools  
 
A decision support system (DSS) is an information system to facilitate the decision-making 
activities of a business or an organization. DSS serves the decision makers that can be at 
management, operational and planning levels of an organization and (usually middle and 
higher management) to solve the decision problems that can-not be easily addressed. 
(Wikipedia, 2020b) DSS provides a sound basis for the involved methodologies and there are 
many different systems that covers various disciplines, policy context and specific user needs. 
A DSS can support a decision-making process more effective by making use of appropriate 
methodologies. (Barfod and Leleur, 2019). 
 
For the holistic evaluation of sustainability in the water sector, a combination of quantitative 
decision-making tools can used such as cost benefit analysis, multi-criteria decision analysis, 
environmental impact assessment, risk assessment as well as qualitative surveys such as such 
as stakeholder assessments or user perception analysis. (Rygaard et. al, 2014)  
 
2.4.1. Option analysis  
 
According to European Union CBA guidelines, in order to assess the technical, economic, and 
environmental aspects of a project, the other feasible options should be considered for 
comparison.   
Option analysis generally is based on a common baseline and starts from a more strategic 
viewpoint such as application of conventional vs state-of-art technologies and continues with 
assessment of differences for the type of technology selected. New technologies are also 
accompanied by thorough evaluation of their risks related with financial, managerial, climate 
as well as their environmental impacts. (European Commission, 2014)  
 
For a good option analysis practice, options should be compared using the same reference 
period. In the comparisons based on costs, unit investment costs, O&M and replacement 
costs should be transparent and given separately for each option. Unit consumption costs 
such as labor energy, etc. should be the same for all options. (European Commission, 2014)

2.4.3. Cost-benefit analysis  
 
Cost benefits analysis (CBA) is an analytical tool that is used for appraisal of an investment 
decisions. The purpose to use CBA in investment projects is to facilitate allocation of resources 
in a more efficient way and demonstration of the convenience of a particular intervention for 
society rather than the alternatives. (European Commission, 2014)  
 
According to OECD Guide to CBA (OECD,2006), the justifications for using CBA was listed by 6 
rationales.  
 
1. CBA provides rationality for the decision-making process.  This is mainly because in 
the world of politics, decisions are not always made by rational thinking of gains and 
losses and CBA in both spatial and temporal dimensions obligate the decision maker 
to look at the beneficiaries and losers of the action.   
2. CBA is clear that one project should be seen as one of a series of alternatives. 
Therefore, setting out options is very important for achieving goals and is a 
fundamental condition of CBA.  
3. CBA is a tool that makes the decision-makers include in the list of options variations 
in the scale of project. CBA has the capacity to determine the optimal scale of the 
project at the point where net benefits are maximized.  
4. Although it is usually not included in practice, when properly executed, CBA should 
show the costs and benefits that is distributed across social groups of beneficiaries 
and losers. Social concerns on differential impacts can be accounted by the use of 
distributional weights.  
5. In CBA, the time needs to be accounted for in a strict manner and this is done by the 
discounting process.  
6.  In CBA, individuals’ preferences are counted, and CBA is democratic. However, there 
are two types of preference: one that is made out of self-interest and those made 
when the individual expresses a preference as a citizen.  
 
Environmental cost-benefit analysis (CBA) is the application of CBA to projects or policies that 
might involve environmental improvements or actions which somehow affect the 
environment indirectly as a consequence. At the center of CBA lies the idea of externality as 
it applies to environmental issues.    (OECD, 2006)

2.5.1. The project context  
 
Nyborg municipality is a municipality located in the Southern Denmark region on the east 
coast of the island of Funen that covers an area of 278 km², and has a total population of 
32,032 (Wikipedia,2020c). Municipality focuses on the potential of reusing industrial water, 
wastewater and waste products for circular economy in order to find ways to develop new 
methods and inspire other industrial towns.  
 
The Nyborg Swimming pool project idea has been arisen from the needs of renewal in harbor 
and marina area in order to establish a recreational area for water sports and leisure 
activities. As in most harbors, the Nyborg seawater doesn’t meet the water quality standards 
for swimming in addition to having the disadvantage of frozen surface water in winter 
months, especially in January and February. Therefore, the idea for a year-around fresh 
seawater pool with good swimming water quality has been evaluated with a master plan. 
However, like in most harbors, the water quality was not optimal, and they looked for 
different alternatives for the establishment of this recreational area concept.  
 
Fortum is a Nordic company that is specialized in recycling and waste management. The 
company plant in Nyborg is located approximately 2 km away from the harbor. Fortum also 
has approximately 4000 m3/hour of surplus and potentially reusable seawater with a thermal 
capacity originating from its cooling processes and at the present stage they are circulating it 
back to the sea after the use.  Nyborg municipality having already a number of contracts with 
Fortum and other industries in the region for district heating, wants to carry these 
partnerships in order to become an interesting place that attracts new inhabitants as well as 
to demonstrate further circular innovations.   
 
Therefore, Nyborg having a great interest in a year-around public swimming pool in the 
harbor and Fortum having a great will for sharing their excess resources, the parties came 
together and agreed on a collaboration. The agreement was made upon the sharing of warm 
seawater that Fortum discharges back into the sea after utilizing the necessary heat in its 
cooling water process. In the business model Fortum is responsible from provision of 
seawater free of charge as the supplier party and Nyborg is the beneficiary party. The goal of 
the Nyborg swimming pool and training facilities project is “provision of swimming pool and 
training facilities with a good water quality that is available all year-around and to make use 
of the harbor as a recreational area for water sports”

3. Methodology 
 
I have established a quantitative decision support system (DSS) in order to evaluate Nyborg 
circular swimming pool project by comparing it to a baseline project that I designed 
conceptually. Cost benefit analysis was used for economic assessment, environmental impact 
assessment used for elaborating the environmental consequences and multi-criteria decision-
making tool was included to assess the other qualitative attributes of the project. The final 
evaluation was then based on the results of these assessments while also considering circular 
economy principles. Stakeholder analysis, and option analysis were used as supporting tools 
so was the ReSOLVE framework for evaluation.

3.3.1. Stakeholder Analysis 
 
In this study, I used stakeholder matrix as an instrument for analyzing stakeholders. As this 
research focuses on evaluation of the project of Nyborg municipality and Fortum, I conducted 
this analysis only for the circular O2. Therefore, this is the first of the tools in the DSS that was 
used as a supporting tool to identify Nyborg case more in detail.  
 
The analysis consisted of three steps.  First, I identified all the stakeholders of project O2. 
Second, I grouped them according to their influence and importance by use of stakeholder 
matrix.  Lastly, I identified key stakeholders and their roles. I. selected the top 3 stakeholders 
and analysed which were found the most influencing as well as the most important 
stakeholders of the project.  The details on methodology was given in Appendix 10.  
 
3.3.2. Cost-benefit analysis  
 
I conducted the CBA as a comparative process to evaluate O2 circular over O1 baseline. In 
order to calculate the present values, net present value method was used. The discount rate 
was set at 3% as it is recommended for most of the EU states (Florio., 2013). I selected the 
project calculation period with the assumptions that concrete structures approximately have 
a life 50 years and electro-mechanical equipment has a lifetime of 10 years. Therefore, the 
project lifetime was selected as 30 years, which is in between 10 and 50.   
 
The project indirect benefits such as health benefits and environmental impacts were not 
included as they introduce uncertainties. In this way, double counting was also avoided as 
they would be covered in multi-criteria decision analysis and environmental impact 
assessment.  
 
Calculations were done using the investment and operational costs for O1 and O2 as well as 
operational net benefits for O2 (in comparison to O1) taking the factors affecting costs and 
benefits into account. (Table4). These factors were then calculated by multiplying unit prices 
of each cost or benefit group with the yearly operational consumptions.  (Table 3)

Distribution of costs among stakeholders can be done in many ways to find out how much is

contracted, water utilities for freshwater use and wastewater discharge, energy companies

Evaluation of project’s economic outcomes with ReSOLVE Framework  
I used ReSOLVE framework for the economical evaluation of O2 project in a context of circular 
economy as this tool can assess how much of this project’s outcomes such as direct benefits 
could be an opportunity to capture circular economy benefits. I also aimed to find the other 
benefits of the project that conflicts with circular economy objectives and therefore do not 
fit into any action areas given in the RESOLVE framework.   
 
First, I listed all the direct benefits of O2, then I grouped and assessed them qualitatively 
towards these 6 action areas: Regenerate, Share, Optimize, Loop, Virtualize, Exchange. If the 
categories were matching one or more ReSOLVE actions they were considered as “Relevant 
to CE” even if it is not a direct benefit but a cost. If not, the uncategorized outputs from the 
assessment were not taken into the consideration and considered “Irrelevant to CE”. Finally, 
I calculated and visually showed the share of the relevant outcomes as well as the not relevant 
ones.

3.3.3. Multi-criteria decision analysis  
 
The overall focus of the MCDA is to measure the non-quantifiable impacts of the project that 
are not covered in CBA and EIA processes. I created three categories as economy, 
environment and society in order to evaluate project in regard to all three aspects of 
sustainability.   
 
I started with longlisting criteria relevant to the project and circular economy and selected 8 
criteria from the list with two objectives.(Appendix-8) First aim was to evaluate the project in 
a multi perspective framework while respecting the vision of the municipality for the project. 
I also aimed at choosing criteria that represent two indicators of a circular economy under 
each category. 6 criteria out of 8 were selected from the circular economy objectives. I added 
2 more criteria under the social domain to give a weight on the assessment of social 
externalities of the project which are health benefits and urban development.

3.3.4. Environmental impact assessment  
 
The main goal of the environmental impact assessment within the DSS is to identify the 
challenges and opportunities regarding the environmental performance of the options.  
Herein, I followed a simplified life cycle assessment (LCA) methodology with a scope that is 
limited to operational impacts. Therefore, the construction stage was not covered. Moreover, 
I selected only three most relevant impact categories for comparison: water use, carbon 
footprint, and marine eutrophication were quantified using characterization models. The 
other end point impacts such as thermal pollution and stratification in the recipient waters 
were reviewed using available literature from previous studies.  
 
O2 Circular model was assessed against O1 Baseline in a comparative manner like in the 
previous assessments as well as using external normalization references for interpretation. 
ISO 14044:2006 standard for Environmental Management & LCA was used as a guidance 
document.  At the evaluation stage, the environmental consequences of the swimming pool 
project in the harbor area was quantified and assessed by comparing the available options 
given as O1 Baseline and O2 Circular model as well as by use of sensitivity analysis to identify 
the risks with the options. 
 
More details on the application of EIA method are given in Appendix-10.

4.4.5. Discussion on Cost-benefit analysis  
 
In the CBA, the costs and benefits of the O2 Circular business model were evaluated while 
being compared with the O1 Baseline and the results were given in subchapters 4.1.3 and 
4.1.4.  
 
The net present value (Figure 17) for the investment was calculated as – 155.4 million DKK 
for the baseline and – 70.7 million DKK for the circular model. The NPV of the project’s 
operating costs were found 72.4 million DKK which is much lower in comparison to the 
baseline which was found 120.2 million DKK. And the direct impacts which were calculated 
compared to the baseline was 48.1 million DKK.  The benefits to costs (BCR) ratio was not 
greater than 1, which means that the project did not outweigh its costs and thus it is not 
feasible as a socio-economic project (Barfod and Leleur, 2019). However, as this thesis 
objective is to find the more feasible option, the circular project is viable compared to 
baseline. In order to balance the costs, the revenue could be introduced such as entrance fee 
applied to target pool users or taxation of citizens to generate income from the swimming 
pool services.

4.5.3. Discussion on Multi criteria decision analysis  
 
MCDA process and results  
The results from the comparative MCDA process which aimed to analyze the project 
outcomes in a circular economy implied that with Nyborg swimming pool project (O2) could 
be an interesting demonstration of a circular economy project.  
The main focus of the assessment was on the public perceptions and water and reuse 
potential in circular option.  Public perception criterion created a good assessment over a risk 
of optimism bias for the evaluation of the project. As the results highlighted, on one hand a 
change of score from 80% to 100% which means that public fully supports the project has an 
effect of 4% points on the overall results. On the other hand, the score of public perception 
was reduced to 20% from 80, the overall result is lowered by 12% points with circular project 
still being 4% being more attractive than baseline. 
 
Moreover, the baseline model seems to have more (+4% points) reuse possibility since it 
would end up in the sewer system and being discharged to recipient. The excess heat from 
the pools could also be captured in the district heating network than seawater being 
discharged or reused again after use in pools. Therefore, the scenarios helped to identify the 
opportunities and challenges with the water-heat reuse and the importance of elimination of 
legal barriers. Last but not least, in the Sce 3 showed that, if a more attention would be paid 
on the water and heat reuse together with legislations i.e. by establishing the enabling 
environment, than the project become much more beneficial.  
 
As the aim of value measurement is to generate numbers associated with each alternative in 
the evaluation (Barfod and Leleur, 2019), the process with involvement of Nyborg 
municipality for the value appraisal was finalized by finding two scores. Therefore, in general 
the results highlight the importance of public support, elimination of legal or technical 
barriers for reuse application.  
 
The results support a conclusion that O2 was a better option than the baseline in the 
transition to a circular economy from all three evaluation perspectives.   
 
Stakeholder preference  
The municipality participated in the MCDA process. First of all, they shared all the documents 
necessary, then expressed their vision on the project outcomes, and finally participated in the 
call with me to disclose their thoughts and preferences for the decision making process. The 
problem with stakeholder participation process was that with this approach not all the 
stakeholders were represented such as Fortum. Moreover, these results might be biased by 
wishful thinking since the criteria which were ranked as top three most important (urban 
development, public acceptance, health benefits) were rated also with scores above 80%. 
When the rating of public acceptance was lowered to 20% from 80%, the overall result was 
63% from 75% reduced to while still being a better option than baseline by 4% points.  
These kind of biases (which is not a case for Nyborg project) can be addressed by employing 
multiple experts with alternative viewpoints (Montibeller and Winterfeldt, 2015). 
Nevertheless, even though the technique requires a more involving approach as it helps

Circular economy challenge: public perceptions towards water reuse  
The first scenario in the sensitivity analysis analyzed the effects of the public support on the 
decision-making process of the project.  According to the assessment, if the public acceptance 
on this swimming pool project got the full support from the citizens, an increase from 80% to 
100%, changed the project’s score overall by 4%. However, if it would have been reduced to

As this project proposes a new business model that the citizens have not met before, 
understanding how people feel about this new swimming pool model with a warm, reuse

is a research center working closely with government and industry hosted a stakeholder event

significance of the public attitudes and acceptance towards a more circular outlook. (Cie-map, 
2017). Without a research, it is not possible to know how the public really would perceive this 
project idea. Therefore, public acceptance criterion was considered as a potential future 
challenge of circular economy projects by generalizing the impressions from this project 
assessment.  
Circular economy challenge and opportunity:  Regulative barriers and reuse of water and 
heat in low heat district heating  
The further water and waste reuse potential after being used once in the pools prompted an 
advanced assessment as the ranking of the environmental criteria that was low (6 and 7 out 
of 8) and the score given by the Nyborg municipality to this criteria was only “0”.  
 
The reason for adding the water and heat reuse potential as a criterion was to appraise one 
of the principles of circular economy “recapturing the value throughout the value chain which 
would otherwise be lost.” (Mc Kinsey, 2016). The sensitivity analysis results emphasized that, 
if regaining the maximum out of water and waste were given the highest weight in the order 
of importance and the maximum score (100%) simultaneously, then the overall value of the 
project would have increased from 75% to 85%.  
The reason that the reuse related criterion got initially 0% from the assessment was the 
barriers for reusing it with the current legislations as the Nyborg municipality presently is not 
allowed to recirculate this water since it would be a saline wastewater after use. This 
argumentation shows the challenge in the reuse potential due to legislative barriers however 
it can be turned to be an opportunity supported by the welcoming results from the MCDA 
process as well as new developments in Danish transition to low temperature district heating. 
The possibilities of reuse of low temperature (which indicates temperatures lower than 23C) 
heat in the district heating network are more realistic nowadays according to Danish 
experiences, (2015). There are also ongoing efforts by utilities for the collection of low 
temperature energy from wastewater and seawater that would be utilized in district heating 
(State of Green, 2019). Consequently, by the adoption of new heat pump technologies in the 
urban water and district heating networks and enabling the policy environment, the shift to 
a more circular economy would be smoother.

4.6.7. Impacts on the marine ecosystem by thermal pollution  
 
There is a study suggesting that direct thermal water discharge into the sea not only increases 
the seawater temperature but also stratifies the seawater. (Huang et al., 2019) The results 
from this study by Huang et al.  (2019) indicated that the thermal discharge influenced the 
sea within the 3 km from the outlet of the discharge while having little impact beyond the 3 
km zone. (Huang et al., 2019) In the same study, it was stated that a water temperature 
increase exceeding 1.5 ◦C might induce stratification and affect marine organisms. (Huang et 
al., 2019) . Moreover, the most significant stratification might occur at 0.5 km to 1.0 km from 
the discharge point.  
 
In my opinion, the results of this study could be relevant for the evaluation of thermal impacts 
in the O2 circular model. Thus, in this project there is a risk that with the assumption of an 
increased temperature of seawater by 2° C, the thermal discharge can lead to stratification in 
the harbor area and might affect the marine organisms in the harbors. Therefore, the harbor 
seawater with O2 circular model is at risk of temperature increase and marine ecosystem 
might be affected from this.  
 
On the other hand, one of the outcomes of O2 Circular model is, using Fortum’s warm water 
in the swimming pool facilities which prevents the amount of water being dumped into the 
seawater from Fortum plants. Without the O2 circular project, seawater at Fortum could be 
more harmful on the seawater body as it contains higher temperatures in a range of 8-10 °C 
accordingly. Therefore, using this water rather in the O2 circular model could be considered 
as a better practice. It can be concluded that, to evaluate the thermal impacts, there is a need 
for further endpoint impact assessment which would also assess trade-offs between the with 
and without project scenarios for O2 circular case.

5. Conclusion  
 
This thesis evaluated two options for establishing a year-around recreational swimming and 
activity pool in Nyborg.  A baseline option O1 was designed to provide a warm and freshwater 
filled swimming pool and a seawater filled activity pool without heating.  A circular alternative 
option O2 was to provide swimming and activity pool using warm seawater used in cooling 
process of a waste management plant in Nyborg. The thesis aimed at applying a decision-
support system for the evaluation of viability of O2 circular option from sustainability aspects. 
Another objective of the study was to identify challenges and opportunities of the established 
business model.  
 
The decision support system (DSS) was built to evaluate the project over the 30 years by 
employing three perspectives of sustainability; economic, environmental and social. 
Evaluation of each was done by using cost benefit analysis, environmental impact assessment 
and multi-criteria decision analysis. Supporting tools such as stakeholder analysis was used 
for identifying top 3 stakeholders as well as ReSOLVE framework used for matching the 
project outcomes with CE principles. The findings were compared with the theoretical O1 
baseline project that was created for this thesis to demonstrate the results for O2 circular 
option in a comparative manner.  
 
Results from the economic perspective indicates that a new paradigm like CE is promising for 
the sharing of the resources such as water use and energy in regard to long term cost savings 
(547K DKK from water and 875K DKK saving each year from natural gas heating). While the 
implementation costs are higher by 34% than the baseline model, the operational benefits of 
the circular concept have better standing in comparison to baseline (2.5 M DKK each year). 
Therefore, the results indicate that NPV of O2 circular (-70.7 M DKK) improves by 54% 
compared to the O1 baseline project (-155.4 M DKK) at the end of the project lifetime. 
However, identification and valuation of environmental externalities are still problematic as 
they consist of a lot of uncertainties and they were rather evaluated in the EIA. Resource 
sharing through partnerships is critical due to a lack of information and therefore increases 
the operational risks in the circular project O2. Accordingly, more transparency from the 
involved parties of this business model is needed in the evaluation of circular economy 
projects. Last but not least, the simplicity of the operation leads to less labor needs in the 
circular option O2, which represents 33% of the monetized project benefits. However, this is 
on the other hand, one of the major attributes that conflicts with the job creation promise of 
a circular economy. Yet, the O2 circular project can be demonstrated overall as a good 
practice with remarkably lower 65% energy consumption and 25% less water use in the 
showers. 
 
The results from the multi-criteria decision analysis were obtained by assessment of 8 criteria 
of which 6 of them were relevant to CE. Results showed that for the circular project O2, public 
acceptance is important, as it is one of the key criteria that affects the success of the project 
and is dependent on reuse related end-user perceptions. There are limitations to CE that 
should be eliminated such as need for improvements in the legislations. This limitation 
however can turn out to be an opportunity for the circular project by enabling further reuse 
of water or waste heat i.e. in the urban water systems.

The results on environmental impact assessment emphasized the importance of consumption 
behaviors in a circular economy. Shower water use in the facilities seems to be a key 
parameter that affects both the groundwater use and energy for shower water heating. 
Therefore, user behaviors are important against the freshwater use and climate change 
impacts. O1 leads to 64 % more carbon footprints than the O2 where the majority (61%) of 
the CO2 emissions originates from swimming pool heating.  Elimination of energy needed 
(390K Nm3/year) for heating up of the pools is the top gain of the O2 circular model which 
makes it an environmentally and economically more feasible option than O1 baseline. Last 
but not least, the seawater polluted with nitrogen and excess heat has the potential to lead 
to damage in the marine environment and this should therefore be a subject of further 
research.   
 
In the stakeholder assessment the top three stakeholders were identified as Nyborg 
municipality & Fortum as they are the final decision makers of the project; citizens and activity 
club members; as they are the final users of the pools and their behaviors and acceptance is 
very important; and Danish EPA as it is the national stakeholder that can be the problem 
solver in the legislative bottlenecks.  
 
Finally, DSS established for Nyborg swimming project helped finding out the potential 
challenges and opportunities in the application of Nyborg public swimming pool project. 
However, there is still an improvement need in the quantitative circular economy evaluation.  
A quantitative method that incorporates the major principles of circular economy is missing 
in order to find matching relevance with the project outcomes. The formulation of a baseline 
system is another future vision which would lead to a better assessment on the retained 
environmental value or the quantification of CE benefits while reducing the design related 
uncertainties.  
 
All in all, Nyborg year-around public swimming pool project O2 based on a resource sharing 
business model seems to be the more viable option considering all three aspects of 
sustainability.  Decision-makers of projects showcasing circular economy should evaluate this 
new concept carefully. Project evaluation in the light of a DSS may reduce the uncertainties 
of circular economy projects and help to be fully aware of opportunities and challenges while 
making the shift towards a circular economy.

6. Future Outlook  
 
Herein, I would like to express my impressions on circular economy projects and their 
evaluation based on the DSS I have established for the Nyborg Harbor swimming pool project.   
 
My case specific conclusion is that, circular economy projects will be very promising if they 
can engage with more opportunities in their surroundings. Therefore, states, companies, 
public institutions, and other entities can collaborate to improve overall system efficiencies 
by integrating the innovations and by making their projects communicate. For example, in 
order to retain some heat for further reuse applications, the seawater wasted in the 
swimming pool can be an input of another project being developed simultaneously.  
Therefore, early communication of these projects at the development stage is very important 
when it comes to capturing these opportunities.    
 
Lastly, for the evaluation of circular economy projects, further focus should be given on 
citizens (or end users) as they were found especially important key players in the Nyborg case.  
End users have the power of changing the project direction or project success by their 
consumption behaviors and perceptions on the projects.   
 
My impression is that, currently circular economy projects have more focus on food and 
waste and there is a research gap for the life cycle assessment of outcomes on the project 
level as well as a lack of assessment methods for water or energy circularity.  Last but not 
least, integration of the UN sustainable development goal indicators in the monitoring of the 
circular economy projects in water sector could also be a new research interest as both topics 
are currently lacking a scientific basis and there could be some synergies.

Appendix 5- Methodology   
 
Brief summary of methods used  
 
Firstly, I reviewed the design in the O2 circular model. I started with calculating the heat loss 
in the pipeline that brings the warm water from Fortum. As the 1 km of the pipeline is buried 
and the other 1 km is a subsea line both seawater and soil heat transfer were investigated.  
Methodology that was also followed in the paper from Hubeck-Graudal et. al. (2019) was used 
according to Nyborg specific data, seawater level at -25 m and soil characteristics. (Appendix 
5.3) 
 
 
Figure 25 Mass and energy balance for the heating of swimming pools (Source: Orecchini et al, 2017) 
 
Then, I analyzed the water and energy balance in the pools both for baseline O1 and circular 
O2. The water and energy balance is illustrated in Figure1. I used the method given by the study 
from Almanza & Lara (1994) to calculate the energy balance and evaporation losses from the 
outdoor pool surfaces throughout the year. In addition to water losses from evaporation, this 
analysis also helped me to find heating energy needed in the pools in O1 and O2. For O2, the 
calculated value was assumed to be the energy that would be needed to keep the pools warm at 
the temperatures if warm seawater was not supplied by Fortum (Appendix 5.1). This value also 
was used later in the CBA and EIA sensitivity assessment in order to identify the financial and 
environmental risks with the business model.  Finally, I studied water balance for both options. 
The filter backwash water and dilution requirements for O1 as well as swimmers’ water 
displacement out of the pools and shower water use both for O1 and O2 were calculated 
(Appendix 5.4, 5.5) 
 
To calculate the electrical energy use, I assumed a total efficiency of 0.8 which includes the 
pump and motor efficiency by assumption. The capacity of the pumps were selected by the 
flowrates calculated by the hydraulic criteria given in order no 918. (Appendix -1).

Herein, I provide the full version of the method statement for the DSS system including literature

8.1. 
Decision support systems and tools  
 
A decision support system (DSS) is an information system to facilitate the decision-making activities 
of a business or an organization. DSS serves the decision makers that can be at management, 
operational and planning levels of an organization and (usually middle and higher management) to 
solve the decision problems that can-not be easily addressed. (Wikipedia, 2020)  DSS provides a 
sound basis for the involved methodologies and there are many different systems that covers 
various disciplines, policy context and specific user needs. A DSS can support a decision making 
process more effective by making use of appropriate methodologies. (Barfod and Leleur, 2019). 
 
For the holistic evaluation of sustainability in the water sector, a combination of quantitative 
decision-making tools can used such as cost benefit analysis, multi-criteria decision analysis, 
environmental impact assessment, risk assessment as well as qualitative surveys such as such as 
stakeholder assessments or user perception analysis. (Rygaard et. al, 2014)  
 
8.1.1. Option analysis  
==PAGE:116==

According to European Union CBA guidelines, in order to assess the technical, economic, and 
environmental aspects of a project, the other feasible options should be considered for comparison.   
Option analysis generally is based on a common baseline and starts from a more strategic viewpoint 
such as application of conventional vs state-of-art technologies and continues with assessment of 
differences for the type of technology selected. New technologies are also accompanied by 
thorough evaluation of their risks related with financial, managerial, climate as well as their 
environmental impacts. (European commission, 2014)  
 
For a good option analysis practice, options should be compared using the same reference period. 
In the comparisons based on costs, unit investment costs, O&M and replacement costs should be 
transparent and given separately for each option. Unit consumption costs such as labor energy, etc. 
should be the same for all options. (European commission, 2014)  
 
8.1.2. Stakeholder Analysis  
 
Stakeholder analysis is a method for the identification and analysis of the key stakeholders in a 
project and planning their involvement. (Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan-Parker, 1998) 
Stakeholders are defined as “those affected by the outcome, negatively or positively” or those who 
can change the outcome of the proposed intervention.  Stakeholders can be any party that the 
project touches such as; national stakeholders, co-operating institutions, funding institutions, 
expert communities and local stakeholders. (Rietbergen-McCracken and Narayan-Parker, 1998) 
 
8.1.3. Cost-benefit  analysis  
 
Cost benefits analysis (CBA) is an analytical tool that is used for appraisal of an investment decisions. 
The purpose to use CBA in investment projects is to facilitate allocation of resources in a more 
efficient way and demonstration of the convenience of a particular intervention for society rather 
than the alternatives. (European commission, 2014)  
 
According to OECD Guide to CBA (OECD,2006), the justifications for using CBA was listed by 6 
rationales.  
 
7. CBA provides rationality for the decision-making process.  This is mainly because in the world 
of politics, decisions are not always made by rational thinking of gains and losses and CBA in 
both spatial and temporal dimensions obligate the decision maker to look at the 
beneficiaries and losers of the action.   
8. CBA is clear that one project should be seen as one of a series of alternatives. Therefore, 
setting out options is very important for achieving goals and is a fundamental condition of 
CBA.  
9. CBA is a tool that makes the decision-makers include in the list of options variations in the 
scale of project. CBA has the capacity to determine the optimal scale of the project at the 
point where net benefits are maximized.  
10. Although it is usually not included in practice, when properly executed, CBA should show the 
costs and benefits that is distributed across social groups of beneficiaries and losers. Social 
concerns on differential impacts can be accounted by the use of distributional weights.  
==PAGE:117==

26th of April 2020

11. In CBA, the time needs to be accounted for in a strict manner and this is done by the 
discounting process.  
12.  In CBA, individuals’ preferences are counted, and CBA is democratic. However, there are 
two types of preference: one that is made out of self-interest and those made when the 
individual expresses a preference as a citizen.  
 
Environmental cost-benefit analysis (CBA) is the application of CBA to projects or policies that might 
involve environmental improvements or actions which somehow affect the environment indirectly 
as a consequence. At the center of CBA lies the idea of externality as it applies to environmental 
issues.    (OECD, 2006)  
 
8.1.4. Multiple-Criteria Decision Analysis  
 
MCDA is a useful tool to determine weights among criteria as well as scores of options for the 
criteria. (Godskesen, 2012) While using the tool, the main challenge is in the MCDA process rather 
than the weighted sum model where each alternative’s scores on the criteria are multiplied by 
assigned weights and these weighted sums across the criteria are used to  get a total value for the 
alternatives. The MCDA challenges include some such as identifying the criteria, explaning the 
methods for scoring or weighting to the stakeholders, eliciting their preferences, and presenting 
and interpreting the results. Therefore MCDA, is a tool that utilizes stakeholders preferences in close 
cooperation with them in order to solve a decision problem in order to cover the social costs and 
benefits that are difficult to monetize in the CBA but should have an influence on the decision 
making process. (Thokala & Madhavan, 2018) 
Use of MCDA in the decision making process increases the productivity of the decision makers and 
leads to improvements not only in the satisfaction of the decision process but also in the quality of 
the decisions. Therefore, it can be seen as a tool to gather the several points of view and to take the 
priorities into account to produce a joint output. (Barfod and Leleur,  2019) 
 
Many different tools and techniques are being used under the MCDA umbrella. Main techniques 
that are relevant with this study are the Simple Multi-Attribute RaTing (SMART) and SMARTER 
(SMART Exploiting Ranks) Techniques.  
 
SMART and SMART(ER) techniques  
 
SMART method is a linear additive model that calculates an overall value of a given option as the 
total sum of value (performance score) of each attribute (criterion) multiplied by the weight of the 
relevant criterion.  
 
SMART Exploiting Ranks (SMARTER) is a simplified form of SMART technique that was suggested 
and by Edwards and Barron because of assessments of value functions and swing weights in SMART 
being sometimes a very difficult task and decision makers being not confident always about it. 
SMARTER model is used to rank the criteria in an importance order such as “Criterion 1 (C1) which 
is more important than Criterion 2 (C2), that is more important than Criterion 3 and so on. 
(C1>C2>C3>..CN)” SMARTER then assigns the surrogated Rank Order Distribution (ROD) weights 
that is given below in Figure 1 that are found mathematically for N=2 to N=10 . ). (Roberts and 
Goodwin, 2002.)

8.1.5. Environmental impact assessment  
 
The importance of environmental protection and awareness as well as the possible associated 
impacts of the products or systems has increased attention on developing methods to understand 
and address these impacts.  And life cycle assessment as one of the environmental management 
techniques can be used for this purpose. (ISO 14044, 2008)  
Environmental impact assessment using an LCA technique can be used as a decision-support tool in 
order for;  
- 
Identifying and improving environmental performances of the products at different stages 
in their lifecycle,  
- 
Informing decision-makers during for their acts like strategic planning, priority setting, etc.  
- 
For selection of indicators and measurement techniques of environmental performance  
- 
Marketing of products such as ecolabelling. (ISO 14044, 2008) 
There are four steps of an LCA study; the goal and scope definition, the inventory analysis, the 
impact assessment, and the interpretation. (ISO 14044, 2008)  
Environmental impact assessment is recognized as a useful tool to map the material resources and 
assess impacts for promoting circularity (Dieckman et al., 2020). EIAs can meet the need for 
assessment of environmental impacts in the circular economy projects (Yijun et al., 2011).

8.2.4. EIA Methodology  
 
Goal and Scope definition  
 
The main goal of the environmental impact assessment within the DSS is to identify the challenges 
and opportunities regarding the environmental performance of the options.  Herein, I followed a 
simplified life cycle assessment (LCA) methodology with a scope that is limited to operational 
impacts. Therefore, the construction stage was not covered. Moreover, I selected only three most 
relevant impact categories for comparison: water use, carbon footprint, and marine eutrophication 
were quantified using characterization models. The other end point impacts such as thermal 
pollution and stratification in the recipient waters were reviewed using available literature from 
previous studies.

Marine euthrophication 
 
The amount of nitrogen emitted into the pool water can lead to eutrophication in the activity pools 
and therefore this potential impact has to be assessed in  both of the  options. The reason I looked 
at marine eutrophication is because seawater pool water is not treated before being overflown into 
sea unlike the baseline freshwater model. In the baseline model the swimming pool water ends up 
in the sewer.  
 
O1 baseline activity pools and O2 Circular swimming and activity pools are designed to discharge 
into the sea through overflow weirs as the fresh seawater enters the pools. Therefore, first, I 
researched the sources of pollution in the pools and found out that amount of urea and sweat which 
are the main sources of nitrogen release in the swimming pools. In the previous studies, it has been 
observed that 25-77.5 ml of urine and  200-1000 ml of sweat may be released from each bather 
which leads to a contamination of 0.8-1.5 gr ureaN/bather. (de Laat et al, 2011). 
 
In both models, the seawater activity pools will be used by 1210 visitors from water sport clubs 
everyday who mainly exercise surfing, kayaking, etc. Since these are less water contacted sports 
compared to the swimming, the release of nitrogen from body liquids into the water assumed to be 
less than in the swimming pools. Therefore, the pollution factor was selected from the lower end of 
the range as 0.8 grN per bather. On the other side of the facility, swimming pool in the O2 circular 
model will be used by 385 visitors every day. As the swimming pool will be more polluted due to the 
physical activity, the pollution factor was selected as 1.5 grN per bather from the top end of the 
range. Also write about ammonium and the formulations (Appendix 9, Table 4)

b. Normalization of results 
 
I presented the results in person equivalents (PE) from the need for putting the figures in a 
perspective in order to support the decision-making process. One PE unit is interpreted as the 
average pollution load generated by one person in Europe or in Denmark in one year. Therefore, all 
the impacts were evaluated using the same unit.  
 
European normalization references related to the domestic inventory for climate change (9.2 ton 
kg CO2eq.) and marine eutrophication (17 kg Neq.) were used for the interpretation. (Benini et 
al.,2014) (Appendix 8, Table 3). For freshwater, the value was taken from Danish water model 
metrics provided as 127 m3eq  (Gejl et al. 2018.) Weighting of the impacts according to importance 
was excluded from the scope of this study.
