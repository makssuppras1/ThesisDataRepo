an explorative study of the case (public school) and the available energy data, a simulation

1  Introduction 
Across Europe, fossil-fueled boilers will have to be replaced with heat pumps in the coming 
years.  
A switch of the heating source changes the operating conditions of the heating system in 
the building, and the functioning of the heat pump itself is strongly influenced by the 
supply/return temperature of the heating system.  
Therefore, the importance of a correct replacement of the heat generator concerns the 
energy procurement (which is increasingly necessary) from renewable sources and a 
proper supply of heat to keep the rooms warm and liveable. 
It is now clear how much comfort affects the behavior and efficiency of the actions that the 
occupants carry out inside the building. For this reason, in the case taken into 
consideration, an adequate heating system will be decisive, because it is directly linked to 
students’ learning and to the best possible performance of employees and teachers. 
 
 
1.1 Background 
Today’s global context is characterized by a generally stable economic condition and 
ordinarily positive growth rates, along with countries that are going through great phases of 
development and an increasingly unsustainable lifestyle, which is also linked to levels of 
overpopulation that are hard to ignore. This worldwide situation makes the issues of 
energy consumption, the increase in greenhouse gas emissions and the consequent 
climate changes, even more urgent to address. 
Referring to energy consumption, it is known that buildings represent forty percent of the 
global energy consumption. This index also represents a constantly increasing trend due, 
as previously anticipated, to factors such as: population growth, technological 
development, the expansion of existing urban centers and the construction of new ones. 
Hence, it is clear that, nowadays, the building industry represents a key element for 
change, constituting a concrete possibility to reduce the extent and impact of one of the 
biggest factors driving climate change. 
For this reason, it is understandably necessary to also intervene on the vast existing 
building stock through the implementation of better solutions for energy saving, which in 
this case are also aimed at operating an energy transition for the utilization of electricity, a 
vector consisting of a percentage of renewable energy which is destined to grow over time, 
and which can also be acquired from the environment through photovoltaic panels or other 
systems.

1.2 Objective 
The purpose is to find the best way to low the potential supply and return temperatures to 
get a high-efficient heat pump conversion.  
To do so, it is necessary to identify the bottlenecks, e.g., radiator capacity and insulation 
level to pinpoint the potential supply/return temperatures, map the investment costs and 
provide advice on the optimal cost balance. 
 
Intervening on the existing building stock, the replacement of fossil fuel boilers with heat 
pumps requires a study of the buildings subject to intervention: as they are not new 
constructions, they will have heterogeneous characteristics and properties, due to the 
different eras in which they were built. 
Once the circumstances are known, it will be important to analyse the climatic context and 
the energy information available to build a model that has behaviours which are similar to 
those of the real buildings. This must be used to analyse the variations in performance 
related to the different interventions. 
 
1.3 Scope    
The scope is to work with public buildings in which people spend most of their time  
(especially schools), in Danish weather context, and to develop an efficient and reliable 
method to solve this kind of issue. 
The in-depth study regarding the comfort of the occupants does not fall within the 
framework of this project. In fact, the only objective of this project is that of ensuring an 
adequate replacement to the heat pumps, so that an optimal ratio is guaranteed between 
the maintenance of the internal temperature at comfortable levels and a reduced energy 
and economic expenditure.

information that can be found may be scarce or incomplete. This involves the necessary

whether the results of the investigation will confirm the validity of the hypotheses 
themselves. 
The increasingly large number of documents that has been accumulated over time can 
also highlight how some of them are in contrast with each other in the description of an 
aspect of a building: in addition to establishing a hierarchy of reliability of the information

aspects on which doubts continued to arise.

3  Theoretical background  
Energy savings can be usually achieved thanks to various solutions, which can be pursued 
with interventions on the building envelope through actions aimed at increasing the thermal 
resistance or at using technologies capable of preventing the spectrum of IR 
electromagnetic radiation, associated with heat, from exiting the building. 
Otherwise, the same purpose can be achieved through the installation of new components, 
the improvement and / or implementation of the various systems present inside the 
building, such as ventilation and air conditioning, water, or lighting, through the use of 
more efficient components, the use of systems for energy production from renewable 
sources, automatic management systems with intelligent control and other complementary 
techniques such as storage systems. 
The set of these devices and technical solutions interfaces with the building as a whole 
and characterizes its dynamic thermal behavior. 
 
The study of the composition of a building and its peculiarities represents the starting point 
for the study of the optimizations to be implemented in the same. 
Among the factors that mainly govern the dynamic thermal behavior of a building the 
following are identified 
1. Building geometry and orientation:  
Factors such as the amount of dispersing surface and the exposure of the building 
significantly affect the contributions due to external atmospheric phenomena such 
as solar radiation, the effect of which can be extremely important if related to the 
arrangement of the surfaces-stained glass windows.  
Similarly, the presence of shielding can significantly affect energy balances. 
 
2. Type of envelope:  
The choice of the type of vertical or roofing structures strongly affects the energy 
behavior of a building. For example, in the geographical areas characterized by low 
temperatures during the year, the choice of a very casing high performance can 
guarantee significant energy savings. The choices in this area are many, ranging 
from localized solutions, such as restoration of thermal bridges or the provision of 
screening systems on glass surfaces, to global solutions such as the creation of 
external coats and roofs or ventilated facades. 
 
3. Glazed surfaces: 
Glazed surfaces make a decisive contribution to the overall performance of the 
building envelope. The choice of an appropriate technical characterization (type of 
frame, type and stratigraphy of the glasses, type of gas in the insulating glass) can 
produce an energy saving per m2 of glazed surface able to reach 40%. Another 
possible solution could be to treat the internal glass surface with a coating 
containing metal oxides that retain more heat inside the environment.

4. Systems:  
Also in this case the possible solutions are multiple, and they depend not only on 
the function that the system will have to carry out, but also on the type of building 
and on the type of energy resource from which it is possible to draw on. 
This latter aspect will make cheaper, for example, to use one energy vector rather 
than another, depending on the project location. 
In general, for space heating and domestic hot water production it is preferable to 
use a heat pump or a condensing boiler (which through an additional heat 
exchanger can minimize energy waste) and often a single plant is used. 
On the other hand, regarding the cooling, using a system that produces chilled 
water, especially in large buildings, is much more efficient than using traditional air 
conditioners. 
An additional contribution to energy efficiency can be given using heat recovery 
systems, which are able to reduce the energy consumption of HVAC systems by 
exchanging energy to the incoming air with the hot or cold air of the exhaust 
ventilation 
 
5. Class of use and trend of the employment profile:  
The contribution energy of a person inside buildings varies according to their 
metabolic activity. This factor contributes significantly to the internal temperature 
trend. It also represents one of the main aspects on which it is possible to focus on 
the study of modern buildings or variable behavior over the user's time, not only in 
the long term but also in the short term (< 24 hours). 
 
6. Energy from renewable sources: 
the requirements indicated by the European EPBD directives make almost 
mandatory the integration of systems that can generate energy by using renewable 
sources such as photovoltaic panels, heat pumps, biomass boilers or solar thermal 
plants. Technological development has led to the creation of photovoltaic panels 
with increasingly high performances that nowadays make them the most widely 
used RES (Renewable Energy Source) solution. 
 
7. ICT, BEMS and home automatization 
Technologies currently available have now reached considerable potential and 
represent an upgrade to the normal system design and construction. 
The Information and Communication Technology (ICT) and the Building Energy 
Management System (BEMS) represent the set of hardware devices and their 
communication technologies installed in a building, with the purpose of controlling, 
managing and automating electronic equipment and systems in a smart way. 
By efficiently modulating their use, they are able to improve overall comfort 
conditions while reducing energy consumption.

Described the main factors that affect the thermodynamic behavior of a building (and 
therefore the possible solutions to reduce their energy consumption), the parameters used 
later in the modeling of buildings are now introduced: 
• 
λ - Thermal conductivity:  
measured in [W/mk], the thermal conductivity coefficient λ is defined as the amount 
of heat transmitted in the unit of time through the unit section of a material whose 
walls are placed at a unit distance and when the temperature difference between 
the walls is worth 1 ºC. 
This property depends on the nature of the material, but not on its shape. 
 
• 
R - Thermal resistance:  
Equivalent to the ratio between the thickness of a material and its thermal 
conductivity and measured in [m2k/W]. In physics the thermal resistance is defined 
as the difficulty of heat in crossing a solid, liquid or a gas. 
This parameter owes its use to the simplicity with which, through the electrical 
analogy, it is possible to establish a link between the physical phenomena that 
describe the real behavior of a building and those that describe the behavior of an 
electric circuit. 
In fact, it establishes the equivalence between the heat flow and the electric current 
flow, between the temperature difference between two surfaces and the potential 
difference, and between the thickness/thermal conductivity ratio and the electrical 
resistance. 
Therefore, analytically, the law of Ohm (Relectrical * i = ΔV) is reformulated as follows: 
Rthermal * q̇ = ΔT. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
• 
U - Thermal transmittance: 
The thermal transmittance, equivalent to the reciprocal thermal resistance and 
measured in [W/(m2k)], is a physical quantity that measures the amount of thermal 
power exchanged by a material or body per unit of surface area and unit of 
temperature difference when lapped on both sides by fluids.

• 
ρ - density: 
The density, measured in [kg/m 3], is a physical quantity indicating how much mass 
is present within 1 m³ of a substance and it is defined as the ratio of the mass to 
the volume of that substance. 
 
• 
c - specific heat: 
The specific heat, measured in [J/kgK] and defined as the heat capacity of a 
sample of the substance divided by the mass of the sample, corresponds to the 
amount of heat (or energy) necessary to raise, or decrease, the temperature of a 
unit mass of substance of 1 ºC. 
 
• 
C - thermal capacity: 
The heat capacity, measured in [J/K] and defined as the ratio of the heat 
exchanged between the body and the environment to the resulting temperature 
change, indicates the amount of heat to be supplied to a substance to raise its 
temperature by 1 ºC. 
A substance with a high thermal capacity can absorb so much heat by slightly 
raising its temperature: in buildings it represents the amount of thermal energy that 
can be stored inside a layer or inside an element in its entirety (for example a wall). 
It is an important feature for transient thermal behavior because the release of 
accumulated thermal energy can mitigate the temperature of indoor environments. 
 
• 
HDD - heating degree days: 
The heating degree days, measured in [day * K],  
are defined as the sum, extended to all days of a conventional annual heating 
period, of the only positive daily differences between the temperature of the 
environment, fixed at 17 (because it is assumed that the various free thermal gains 
of the building contribute to the achievement of the internal temperature of 21 ºC), 
and the average daily external temperature. 
Consequently, the greater the number of degrees of winter day ("heating degree 
days") in an interval of time, the colder the location taken under consideration.

Free thermal gains ("free energy gains"): 
Among the various thermal exchanges that affect the building, some of them 
involve free thermal gains because they guarantee energy supplies without a 
specific expenditure of resources and are measured in [W]. 
During the winter air conditioning period, in fact, factors such as the metabolic 
activity of people, the use of lighting and electrical equipment contribute to the 
increase of internal temperature (while the transmission of heat to the outside, the 
infiltrations and the air exchanges for ventilation constitute thermal dispersions).

The complex of buildings object of study has the intended use of school. 
Sengeløse Skole is a public school in the municipality of Hõje-Taastrup located in 
Spangåvej 10, in the region of Hovedstaden.

Documents of the municipality.

5  Method  
To pursue the objective of this project, 2 different methods have been developed to find, 
process, and analyse the data. Then, data must be compared to evaluate any possible 
correlation that can confirm the reliability of the methodologies used. 
Having established appropriate calibrations between these procedures, the intent will be 
that of identifying the workflow that enables the resolution of this interventions in the 
fastest way, while maintaining an acceptable level of approximation. 
The project ends with the exposition of the method that was used to map investment costs, 
identify the most efficient ones, and provide advice on their optimal balance. 
 
 
5.1 Preliminary steps 
The first step to implement manual calculation, which is essential to establish a period of 
comparison with the obtained results (in this method and through energy simulation), was 
to analyse energy consumption. 
To do so, it was necessary to find the available gas consumption and the respective 
climatic data. 
Eventually, the last element used in both methods for energy estimation was dispersant 
surfaces. 
 
 
5.1.1 Gas data importation 
Since the required gas consumption had numerous gaps in the data records, to proceed 
with the re-elaboration of the information, it was necessary to create and insert the 
aforementioned records of missing data within the databases. 
Considering the excessive number of data strings (recorded on a 15-minute basis, from 
25/04/2018 to 30/06/2021) and the great number of heterogeneously distributed missing 
readings, Python language was used (because of the impossibility of completing the entire 
data set manually). 
Missing consumption data were calculated through linear interpolation, given the reduced 
time of the various timesteps. 
In the place of consumption data that were not available in the databases (from 16:00 of 
30/06/2021 to 00:00 of 01/07/2021) but that were still essential to complete the observed 
annual interval (from 01/07 to 30/06), identical values to those of timestep, characterized 
by very similar temperatures, were used (from 16:00 of 27/06/2021 to 00:00 of 
28/06/2021).

5.3 Energy simulation 
Once the manual calculations that led (with the relative approximations) to obtain the GHC 
of each building have been carried out, the use of software for energy simulation aims to 
confirm the estimated consumption and reliability of the relative global heat coefficients. 
If the confirmation is successful, in order to predict the results of the various retrofit 
interventions, it will be possible to directly intervene on the summary tables shown in 
chapter 5.1.4, changing the values of thermal transmittance. 
In addition, unless the results of the energy analysis show a trend of hourly energy peaks 
which is inconsistent with that theorized by the GHC, it will be possible to calibrate them 
(for example by increasing or decreasing them by some percentage value). In this way, it 
will be possible to predict again the effects of retrofit interventions, as described above. 
This possibility, offered by the establishment of a correspondence between the GHC (of 
the manual calculation) and the results of the energy simulation, will allow to not having to 
calibrate an additional simulation for each intervention. 
 
For the development of this methodology, an energy analysis was conducted on building G 
and its results have been taken as indicative of the performance of all the other buildings 
of the school complex.

5.3.1 SketchUp & Legacy Openstudio   
These software have been used due to the way that Energy Plus performs the analysis. 
Developed by mathematicians and computer scientists, the calculation engine of this 
software makes energy balances through surfaces whose vertices coordinates are 
required, without the need for a geometric model. 
This computation procedure makes Energy Plus a "two-dimensional" software. In fact, any 
element of the building envelope will have to be modelled as a slab or a plane,  
then the relative thicknesses will be entered among the required parameters. 
Given this calculation mode, the SketchUp software has been chosen for the building 
modelling, thanks to its simplicity and its use of surfaces for the characterization of three-
dimensional objects (unlike programs such as AutoCAD that typically use solid).  For these 
reasons, this program is an appropriate tool for data creation that will be subsequently 
imported into Energy Plus. 
In modelling, the two-dimensional elements created were placed along the centre lines of 
the envelope thicknesses. 
 
Since Energy Plus can only read .idf files, modelling in SketchUp environment requires the 
Legacy Openstudio plugin. 
Through it, the data associated with the geometric model will be saved in the extension 
recognized by Energy Plus, making them compatible.

5.4 Retrofit interventions 
Having now a detailed overview of the energy performance of buildings, the last step of the 
project involves the analysis of two different scenarios regarding the conversion to heat 
pumps: the one in which the supply temperature is equal to 55 ºC and the one in which the 
supply temperature is equal to 70 ºC. 
It is assumed that all the buildings’ systems have been sized with a design temperature of 
-12 ºC. 
The change of this temperature will necessarily cause a decrease in the power emitted by 
the heating system, having been dimensioned for different temperatures (those of the old 
buildings for supply and return temperatures of 90/70, while those of the most recent 
buildings 70/40). 
In order to guarantee an internal temperature of 20 ºC, retrofit interventions will reduce the 
heat power demands of the buildings, adapting them to the new power of the systems. 
 
 
5.4.1 Heatpumps 
Key element of this project, the heat pumps chosen were the result of a selection of 
models produced by Metro Therm and Vølund, indicated by the Danish Energy Agency on 
electricity consumption, heat consumption and energy solutions for home and workplace 
(https://sparenergi.dk/) as the most efficient on the market. 
 
 
5.4.1.1 Value interpolations 
The values of Pout and Pin for each outdoor temperature were obtained by linear 
interpolation for each of the following ranges, having only 4 values declared by the 
manufacturer for 4 different external temperatures (-7,C, 2,C, 7,C, 12,C): 
• 
Temperature between -7°C and 2°C: linear interpolation between -7°C and 2°C 
• 
Temperature between 2°C and 7°C: linear interpolation between 2°C and 7°C 
• 
Temperature between 7°C and 12°C: linear interpolation between 7°C and 12°C 
Getting values of temperature out of these ranges through a linear prediction that follows 
the trend closest to them

5.4.1.3 Series connection 
The estimation of the Pout and Pin values of the combined heat pumps in series has been 
obtained assuming that the linear interpolation of Metrosaver UB 17, done as described 
above, could continue until reaching the external temperature value of 40 ºC. 
Since the data provided by the manufacturer refer to a supply temperature of 55 ºC, the 
increase of 15 ºC needed to reach it (the supply temperature of 55°C) would instead be 
added to the water temperature come out from the first heat pump. 
By doing this, the 15°C increment provided by the brine-to-water heat pump (Metrosaver 
UB 17), added to the previous 55°C reached thanks to the air-to-water heat pump 
(METROAIR I 20), would allow the achievement of a supply temperature of 70°C. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The values of Pout and Pin of the combination in series have been finally obtained as a 
simple sum of the values of Pout and Pin of the two heat pumps:

Metrosaver UB 17: Pout=18,58kW and Pin=3,28kW at Tout=40°C 
METROAIR I 20 + Metrosaver UB 17: Pout=9,97+18,58=28,55kW and 
Pin=3,79+3,28=7,07kW at Tout=-5°C

Molio Dataprice already provided the price per square meter for this intervention. 
 
• 
Attic floor insulation with mineral wool, 100/200/300mm thick

Molio Dataprice already provided the price per square meter for this intervention.

Windows replacement with low emissive double glazing and argon

5.4.2.1 Price calculation of heat pumps 
Consulting Molio Dataprice, the installation price of METROAIR I 20 (air-to-water heat 
pump) was obtained by recalculating only the price of the heat pump itself (leaving the 
other installation costs unchanged). Obtained the cost per single kW of the largest 
available air-to-water heat pump size (13kW), was multiplied by the rated heat output of 
METROAIR I 20 (12,4kW). 
For Metrosaver UB 17 was made the same calculation, considering the price of the largest 
available brine-to-water heat pump size (4-16kW) and converting it for a rated heat output 
of 20kW. 
 
 
 
5.4.3 Interventions planning 
In order to optimise costs and benefits, the different types of intervention are simulated one 
at a time and ordered in relation to the DKK/kWsaved ratio (in the case of replacement of 
radiators, DKK/kWearned). 
Trying to ensure that the heating power of the system (once set the new supply 
temperature, of 55 ºC or 70 ºC) can be greater or equal to that required by the building for 
an external temperature of -5°C, retrofit interventions are carried out in the most 
convenient order: DKK/kWsaved (for each square metre) or DKK/kWearned. 
If a building does not require further work, these are carried out only if the amount of kW 
saved allows to save an heat pump. 
The number of heat pumps was finally calculated by directly dividing the number of kW 
globally required by the school complex for those supplied by the single heat pump 
(always taking as a reference an external temperature of -5,0 ºC).

7  Discussion  
7.1 Main findings 
Having analysed the obtained data, it can be said that the results that have been 
presented in the previous chapter have allowed to adopt the most efficient design choices 
for the case study. 
First, it was possible to show that almost all the hours belonging to the considered range 
have recorded an external temperature equal to -5 °C or higher. 
This aspect allowed to establish how this was the best reference temperature for the 
design of all future interventions. 
 
In this way, having established that design temperature has allowed to not overestimate 
the needs of the buildings. This has made it possible to optimize the investments and 
performance of the heat pumps, as well as energy efficiency. 
In fact, if rare circumstances that require more power than the guaranteed one should 
occur, the use of integration systems as electrical resistances (despite their poor 
efficiency), would ensure a more economically and energetically advantageous solution, 
considered their low use. 
 
In the same way that the average hourly temperatures influenced the development of the 
project, the measured gas consumption was also decisive for the correct estimation of 
each building energy demand. This is an aspect of fundamental importance to determine 
the goal that must be achieved for the convergence of energy simulation values. 
Regarding this aspect, it is important to notice that the year 2020/2021, due to COVID-19 
pandemic, has recorded abnormal consumption in terms of entity as trend. 
For this reason, the gas consumption taken as a reference, were those for the years 
2018/2019 and 2019/2020. 
 
Later, the estimated GHCs provided an overview of the buildings’ performance, also 
highlighting which ones were more energy-intensive by comparing the required power per 
single square meter of surface area. The buildings with a more pronounced slope turned 
out to be the most inefficient: therefore, their need for intervention is much more urgent. 
 
The comparison with the energy simulation shows that GHC trends have proved to be 
reliable. For this reason, it was possible to use them in the prediction of the effects of 
retrofit interventions. 
 
In the calculation of the latter, it became clear that the older buildings, due to their bad 
thermal transmittance values, have benefited from the insulation or the replacement of the 
windows. 
In most recent buildings, on the contrary, the already good energy performance of the 
building envelopes, have allowed a less reduction in the flow of transmitted heat, making 
the interventions less effective.

7.2 Discussion of results in relation to the matters of concern 
By comparing and evaluating the results obtained, it can be argued that the considerations 
expressed in chapter 2 "matters of concern" were plausible. 
The graphs regarding the hourly outdoor temperatures as well as those of the gas 
consumption have not evidenced, although the interpolations have filled different intervals 
during the various years, the onset of anomalous or not coherent trends. 
For this reason, these interpolations didn’t had a sufficient influence to affect the reliability 
of the results. 
Considering the coherence between the results of the manual calculation and those of the 
energy simulation (supported by the technical reference values of the Danish Standard 
and the considerations made during the inspection), it can be assumed that the doubts 
expressed initially did not had significant consequences, thus proving the reliability of the 
decisions taken. 
The constructions’ composition, the absence of mechanical ventilation in the old buildings 
and the correspondence between the nomenclature of the buildings in the energy labelling 
and in the documents of the municipality were therefore reasonably verified. 
 
 
7.3 Critical evaluation 
The aim of this project was to develop a method for analysing buildings and heating 
systems for optimal conversion to heat pumps, applying it to the case study. 
Therefore, making a critical evaluation, it is important to distinguish the aspects strictly 
related to the buildings analysed and those related to the developed method, which has a 
general value. 
With regard to the first, it is possible to reduce the relative uncertainty of the results 
obtained by having more detailed information on the current situation of the buildings. 
In addition to more a detailed documentation, actions such as the installation of 
temperature sensors that can monitor supply and return temperatures as well as the use of 
specific tools for estimating the thermal properties of building envelopes (such as a heat 
flow meter) would be able to significantly reduce uncertainties. 
With regard to the method itself, extending the energy simulation (possibly more detailed) 
to a larger sample of buildings (or directly to all) would allow more rigorous verification of 
the GHC reliability of each building, and then eventually calibrate them individually. 
In addition to this, the accuracy of the estimation of the running time and performance of 
heat pumps could be increased by improving the interpolation methodology of the data 
provided by the manufacturer and the procedure followed to obtain those data in case of 
series combination of more heat pumps (using for example the software Coolpack). 
Despite these possible improvements, the realized project provides a general methodology 
that can be applied in other cases, undoubtedly implementable, but which is in any case 
an effective working method.

8  Conclusion  
Observing the results, it is clear that by lowering the supply temperature to 55ºC, despite 
the fact that all retrofit operations have been used, even with the maximum available 
insulation thicknesses, the school complex cannot guarantee an internal temperature of 
20°C, since the condition "Heating power of the system ≥ Heating power needed" is not 
satisfied for each building. 
Nevertheless, if all the interventions had led to the fulfilment of this requirement, given the 
huge cost of some interventions it is logical to assume that the investment, even with the 
consequent reduction of consumption, would not have been profitable. 
 
The second scenario shows that with a supply temperature of 70ºC only the insulation of 
the walls of the older buildings is necessary to ensure an adequate heating even at -5ºC. 
The more recent buildings, having already been designed from the beginning for the set of 
temperatures 70/40/20, have not required any intervention.  
In addiction, even combining them all together has not been possible to save the use of 2 
heat pumps connected in series. 
 
 
9  Future works  
Demonstrated how the retrofit interventions analysed can have a significant impact on the 
demand of the complex, the study on the installation of domotic systems for further energy 
efficiency would be an extremely useful and interesting activity. 
Moreover, considering the possible conversion of the school to heat pumps, a survey 
focusing on the possibility of integrating systems for the generation of energy from 
renewable sources would represent another project of great interest. 
In this way, the economic and energy costs of the heat pumps’ running cost could be 
reduced.  
Any possible excess of energy generation could then be stored temporarily in order to be 
used as needed, or if the cost per kWh were to be higher within a time slot.
