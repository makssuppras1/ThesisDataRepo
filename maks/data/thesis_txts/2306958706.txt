Abstract
With the increasing social awareness of renewable energy and the support from govern-
mental energy subsidy, residential PV has become popular in recent years. In order to
maximize the solar self-consumption, pairing a battery storage to the solar panel becomes
an hot issue. Many companies has started residential battery business in these years, some
popular products such as Tesla Powerwall, Samsung SDI, LG Chem, Nissan xStorage are
well known. In this study a comparison is made in between residential battery and cen-
tralized battery on improving solar self-consumption and minimizing energy payment of
users.
An mathematical model of a solar battery system is developed for maximizing the
battery utilization. The model is interpreted into the linear programming software GAMS
and solved as mix integer liner programming (MILP) problem. Eight scenarios consisting
of four electricity prices and two optimizing methods are simulated and the results of the
battery behaviours are presented and discussed in two selected days with high and low
solar radiance.
The battery number of cycles, calendric lifetime and payback period are calculated
based on battery investment cost and the result from the simulation. The best battery
solution within the battery calendirc lifetime for this study is found to be daily optimized
centralized battery operating under ﬁxed electricity price. Furthermore, sensitivity study
of a single residential battery’s rated payback period which is related to the PV penetra-
tion, battery capacity and feed-in tariﬀis analyzed. An optimal battery capacity of yearly
optimized single residential battery is found to be 5.76 kWh. It is found that paring a
battery storage can increase the yearly solar self-consumption by 50% to 60%, and the
community battery has more advantage than home battery in the studied case, due to the
shorter payback period and longer calendric lifetime.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The power system has traditionally been developed according to a top down approach,
where all the power production is handled by a relatively few number of large power
plants. However, recently this has started to change with wind and photovoltaic (PV)
power production being the main drivers. In recent years, smaller PV plants have been
installed in many countries on household roofs. Only a few PV installations worldwide are
at a power nameplate size comparable to conventional power plants, such as Solar Star in
US and Longyangxia Dam Solar Park in China [1]. This shifts the power sector towards a
multi-level system, where consumption, generation and storage are conducted on all levels
of the power system [2].
In recent years, many companies start with their business on residential battery. Tesla
announced the Tesla Powerwall in 2015, which follows by Samsung [3], Panasonic [4]
and LG Chem [5] also announced similar product after that. Power storage facilities for
residential use is still a niche market and home battery storage products such as Tesla
Powerwall might not change this at ﬁrst. Despite that, economies of scale on the battery
production side might drive prices down the coming years and lead to future growth.
1.1
Motivation
Most of the renewable energy are intermittent energy which are diﬃcult to forecast pre-
cisely and diﬃcult to store. For example, in Denmark wind is in general stronger in the
night where the energy consumption is low, leading to transmission of excess wind energy
to other countries with fairly low price or potential renewable energy is unexploited. Sav-
ing excess renewable energy locally has become a very hot issue on achieving the fossil free
future. The saved renewable energy can be distributed to following hours, and so saved
renewable energy can be released during peak load to relief the grid congestion, or it can
be used for supplying grid services such as primary and secondary reserve.
By increasing the utilization of renewable energy, less power will be provided from
conventional power plants. Conventional power plants are a symbol of high CO2-emission,
and so reducing the conventional power plant production is a great improvement on low-
ering this emission. Furthermore, with higher renewable penetration, conventional power
plants may be totally substituted by renewable energy production combined with battery
storage or other more advanced storage techniques in the future.

ment and applications. In 2012 a very attractive renewable energy subsidy on small scale

1.2.1
Ancillary Service
In most countries, including Denmark, ancillary service is still provided by conventional
generators. It is secure but expensive and environmental unfriendly. Large scale energy
storage in the grid can perform very precise in fast regulation with a high renewable pene-
tration. It does not only enhance the security of supply, but it is also more environmental
friendly and cheaper than conventional generators [12]. The start-up and ramp-up time
for battery storage systems is in the range of milliseconds, while the gas power plant has
around 10 minutes response time [12]. Figure 1.3 illustrates the performance comparision
between battery and conventional generator in providing grid services. An example of
large scale energy storage is seen in Doha, Qatar, where the battery manufacturer com-
pany BYD Co.
Ltd.
has launched a 250 kW and a 500 kWh Li-ion battery at Qatar
Science & Technology Park. The Li-ion battery is charged by solar panels and back-up
diesel generators. The battery system is used for providing several ancillary services, such
as voltage support, frequency regulation, and black start [14].

1.2.2
Supply Shifting and Energy Output Smoothing
High renewable energy integration creates a lot of ﬂuctuation and uncertainty to the
power system. The ﬂuctuation power inject to the grid will cause voltage ﬂuctuation at
the distribution network. It can further aﬀect the stability in the whole power system if
the ﬂuctuation is too extreme; for example if the wind or sun suddenly stops powering a
wind farm or a large PV power plant. In ﬁgure 1.4 an example of how the solar energy
output is smoothed by battery on Hawaiian islands. Smoothing power can only mitigate
the small scale voltage ﬂuctuation problem. Another example is seen in New Mexico, US,
a 1 MWh lead-acid battery is installed to provide 500 kW power rating of smoothing and
250 kW of energy shifting potential for a 500 kW PV power plant [17].
1.2.3
Islanded System
Another example where energy storage can be beneﬁcial is in oﬀ-grid systems as distant
islands. Oﬀ-grid systems are in general supported by conventional generators, such as
diesel generator. For the reason of security of supply, the size of the conventional generator
in an island has to meet the peak demand. Normally to keep a generator running, it needs
to operate at least 30% of capacity [16]. Besides, ramp up frequently and power variation
cause more fuel consumption and emissions than normal operation [12]. In ﬁgure 1.5 is
shown a case study of the oﬀ-grid system on Barbados, Latin America.
Here battery
storage, besides improving the characters of peak load supply by conventional generator
such as faster ramp-up rate, is also optimizing the use of renewable energy available.
Figure 1.5a shows the normal supply by diﬀerent generators with 15% renewable energy
penetration in the system, while Figure 1.5b shows that with a 134 MWh battery installed,
the base and shoulder load from diesel generator has been signiﬁcantly decrease. The wind
penetration increased by 1 GWh. It also reduces carbon dioxide emissions by 1423 metric
tonnes per day [19].

1.2.4
Residential PV
During a year most of the time solar production proﬁle does not meet the residential
demand proﬁle.
As mentioned in section 1.1 the solar production is out of sync with
residential demand, leading to possibilities of optimizing the use of battery storage to
increase the utilization of solar energy. So far there are many diﬀerent studies related
to solar battery system in residential distribution grids. Most of them are taking both
technical and economical terms of the system into account. Some studies goes deeper into
speciﬁc considerations as life cycle, depth of discharge, degradation, electricity market,
etc. For small-scale battery, optimizing solar battery system sizes according to the cost
for single residence [20] and cost analysis of battery in single residential solar system
[21] are commonly focused. For large-scale battery, optimizing solar battery system sizes
according to the cost for greater load or aggregated load [22], and operate diﬀerent grid
services and supply the residential load simultaneously [11, 23, 24], are more popular in
research.
1.3
Purpose of this Project
Most previous researches classifying the study on small scaled battery is for end users
at distribution level. Large scaled battery is for providing grid service or supply shifting
at transmission level or supply side. In this project the aim is on having small scaled
and large scaled batteries operate as solar battery at distribution level, and compare the
utilization and economics of these two solutions.
A considerable amount of residences in a community has installed solar panels. In order
to increase the solar self-consumption, pairing an energy storage to the PV is considered.
Assuming the policy allows residences in a community to own a large scaled energy storage
and share the utility. By collecting the existing price data of batteries and electricity, a
comparison between install home battery for each household and install a community
battery at the grid transformer secondary side is needed. With diﬀerent initial investment
cost, operation and maintenance cost users receive diﬀerent payback time with diﬀerent
choices. Several scenarios of diﬀerent electricity price schemes based on current electricity
prices and future renewable energy subsidy expectation are discussed. Based on the result,
a sensitivity analysis will be made by adjusting PV penetration, battery capacity and solar
feed-in tariﬀ.
The objective in this project is listed below:
• Formulate optimization problem on energy cost with diﬀerent market price and battery
solutions.
• Economic and technical comparison on centralized and distributed batteries.
1.4
Methodology and Tool
The solar battery system in this project is formulated in a mathematical model with
several constraints and an objective function.
The objective function is based on the
energy payment of the users, whereas the constraints are formed by diﬀerent technical
limitations of the battery system. The mathematical model is interpreted into the linear
programming software General Algebraic Modeling System (GAMS 2014) as a mix integer
==PAGE:18==

linear programming (MILP) problem which contains continuous real variables, integer
variables, and binary variables. A number of input parameters such as electricity retail
price, residential demand, PV production, feed-in tariﬀwill be used to simulate the battery
behaviors and return the optimal objective result back. The script can be seen in Appendix
A.

2.1
Data Collection
This section introduces the data used in home battery scenario of this chapter. The four
main input data are household demand proﬁle, solar energy production proﬁle, electricity
retail price, and excess solar energy selling price. In order to keep the input data consistent,
all data used in this study are obtained from Denmark.
The household load proﬁle is from a single apartment without electric heating. Hourly
consumption has been normalized by the total consumption of 2012 as per unit value. An
estimated yearly consumption of a single house without electric heating in Denmark is
around 3700 kWh. This energy value is multiplied to the normalized hourly consumption
in per unit. The calculated hourly consumption in energy unit is the ﬁnal input that
have been used in this study and is shown in ﬁgure 2.3a. However, this calculated dataset
is only for one single user. In order to have several diﬀerent load proﬁles for diﬀerent
residence, a normal distribution sampling, N(µ, σ2), is applied to the single load on time
period. Here the expectation value µ is the input single household demand, while the

Chapter 3
Community Battery
With the decreasing lithium-ion battery price, more and more business cases with battery
applications are developed. For stationary batteries, large scaled is getting increasingly
popular since the price on each kWh is likely to be lower than small scaled batteries. Using
battery to run diﬀerent grid services instead of conventional generator has already been
realized in many countries as mentioned in section 1.2. However, this chapter focus on
the use of a large scale battery to maximize the utilization of solar energy. The principle
behind the optimization problem is the same as in the home battery case in chapter 2,
and is here reformed to the case of one single community battery instead of several home
batteries.
The community is the same as in chapter 2: The community consist of 50 residence,
where it is assumed 20% of the residence as PV installed on their rooftop. However now,
instead of installing a home battery in each residence with a PV, one large scale battery,
located somewhere in the community area, is shared between the residences with PVs.
Figure 3.1 illustrate the simpliﬁed topology of community battery scenario.

electricity price and feed-in tariﬀ, the charging and discharging of the battery is related
to the aggregated electricity demand and solar energy production of all the PV users.
Aggregated demand is basically the sum of each individual household demand by hour.

Battery Size
In order to have community battery size equivalent to the sum of home batteries in chapter
2, a simple method is to set the community battery capacity and power equal to the
accumulated capacity and power of the home batteries. This is reasonable since normally
large scale lithium-ion battery is a combination of many small lithium-ion storage modules.
Thus we will assume each storage module to be equivalent to a Tesla Powerwall. No matter
how these modules are connected, the capacity and rated power are only inﬂuenced by the
number of modules. However, the diﬀerent ways of connecting the batteries will eﬀect the
voltage and current, which inﬂuence the services it can provide. In this study, the battery
is only used for storage of solar energy and energy arbitrage, so the speciﬁc connection of
the modules will not be considered.

Chapter 4
Comparison
This chapter describes a comparison between home battery and community battery, men-
tioned as one of the objectives in section 1.3. The comparison includes several main cash
ﬂows in this study, which include battery investment cost, inverter cost, installation cost
and solar battery system operation cost, together with life cycles and payback period.
4.1
Battery Cost
As discussed in section 2.2 Tesla PowerWall is chosen as the home battery product for all
PV users. On Tesla’s website oﬃcial price for a product of 6.4 kWh capacity and 3.3 kW
power is 3000 USD. Since it is assumed that 10 out of a community of 50 households
are buying home batteries, the investment cost in total is 30 000 USD, corresponding to
198 000 DKK by the currency exchange rate 6.6 DKK/USD.
In section 3.2 it is assumed that a community battery is a combination of several Tesla
Powerwall modules. Hence the investment price of the community battery is the same as
the total battery cost in home battery case, i.e. again 198000 DKK.
4.2
Inverter Cost
Besides the battery, another important component in a solar battery system is the inverter.
An inverter price is found by van Sark et al. in 2014, and is shown as function of DC
input power in ﬁgure 4.1 [34] – here the inverter price is seen to decrease exponentially
with DC input power. However the data in ﬁgure 4.1 is collected in 2012, and today’s
inverter prices is expected to be less. Nevertheless the exponential decreasing relation
between inverter price and size is still expected.
Small size inverters are more expensive than large size ones: For size smaller than 1 kW
the average price is 0.6 EUR/Wp; for inverters greater than 10 kW the price is lower than
0.3 EUR/Wp [34]. With 3.3 kW DC input power, the inverter price is around 0.4 EUR/Wp,
and thus the investment price for one inverter is 1320 EUR, corresponding to 9900 DKK.
In the home battery case each household has an inverter for the battery, and the total
cost of inverters becomes 99 000 DKK.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Outlook
In order to reach 100% renewable energy in the future, maximizing the renewable self-
consumption becomes an important issue. In this study two battery storage solutions
used for increasing the solar self-consumption of residential PVs are being compared. The
comparison includes initial investment cost of the battery system, energy payment saving
of two solutions, battery cycles and battery calendric lifetime. Two study objects has been
solved in chapter 2 and 3 for home batteries and a shared large community battery, and
is compared in chapter4. A further sensitivity study of several uncertain variables in the
project is analyzed in chapter 5.
Maximizing the solar self-consumption in a PV battery system can be realized by
minimizing the energy payment of users, and is done by setting up a mix integer linear
programming (MILP) optimization problem, which in this study is solved using the Gen-
eral Algebraic Modeling System (GAMS). An extensive GAMS program for solving the
optimization problem is developed in this project and can be seen in Appendix A. The
more solar energy being used, the less energy buying from the grid and the lower the elec-
tricity payment will be. In energy payment optimization, input data in the community
battery case are almost the same with the aggregated home battery case, and therefore the
results in battery operation part are very similar. Pairing with a yearly optimized battery,
solar self-consumption increased by 65% compare to no battery, where the daily optimized
battery enhance the solar self-consumption by 51% only. The real battery operation will
be more close to the daily optimization case since the forecast on data can not be perfect
and as long as one year. Energy payment with battery has an approximately 62% and
50% decrease respect to yearly and daily optimization when comparing with the energy
payment without battery. The higher solar production and the higher the diﬀerences be-
tween electricity retail price and solar feed-in tariﬀ, the more energy payment saving users
can have. The more accurate of input data forecast, the higher battery utilization will be.
Initial investment cost in this study consists of battery cost, inverter cost and installa-
tion cost. An conservative assumption of battery cost is linearly increased with the battery
capacity for both cases. Inverter cost of the centralized solution is half of the price of total
inverter cost in the residential battery case. Installation cost is diﬃcult to deﬁne in this
study due to unknown labour cost, transportation cost, etc., and hence it is not included
in the payback period calculation. Realizing the project in a known local area, the instal-
lation cost will be included. Since inverter cost is one third of the initial investment cost,
the centralized battery investment cost is around 15% less than in the home battery case.
Centralized battery has general less payback period comparing to residential battery. Ac-

ﬁxed price has around one ﬁfth more operation time after the pay back, which will returns
the most proﬁt within the estimated operation period.
In this study rated payback period is used as the evaluating standard to measure
the sensitivity of variables. PV penetration, battery capacity and feed-in tariﬀs are the
three variables analyzed in chapter 5 based on a single home battery. Rated payback
period decrease with the increase of PV capacity, the decrease in battery capacity in daily
optimization and the lowering of feed-in tariﬀ. In battery capacity analysis with yearly
optimization the lowest rated payback period is 5.76 kWh. Sensitivities of rated payback
period is decreasing with the increasing of PV capacity, whereas it has positive increase
relation with battery capacity and feed-in tariﬀs. In general battery operating with ﬁxed
price is more sensitive to the rated payback period than variable price.
As a ﬁnal conclusion, in the case of the 50 residence community considered with 20%
PV penetration, a community battery has more advantage than home batteries based
on shorter payback period and longer calendrical lifetime. Paring a battery storage can
improve the solar self-consumption of users, however the solar self-consumption does not
increase linearly with the increase of battery capacity. Over a certain ratio between the
PV capacity and battery capacity, solar self-consumption will not increase in both the
home battery and community battery case.
6.1
Outlook
Further study based on the current project to improve the accuracy of the result can focus
on several aspects:
In this study the model is running on hourly discretized data. This leads to the power
in each hour being an average power. In real the power within each hour may vary with
high peaks, and thus it would be interesting to investigate the impact of increasing the
resolution of the data used in the model.
However, this requires more detailed data.
Besides the data sampling method can be improved according to diﬀerent lifestyles and
diversity factor.
On the model itself one can include system characteristics such as eﬃciency degra-
dation, self-discharge and energy losses in transmission into the model in order to move
the model closer to reality. Once including these factors the energy payment saving will
decrease year by year, which means that the payback period of battery storage will be
longer than found calculated in this report. However the driven down initial investment
cost today may balanced out the payback period. In the economic analysis more detailed
sensitivity study can be based on battery charging and discharging eﬃciency, self-discharge
and battery investment cost in order to determine the variable which aﬀect the payback
period the most.
One can also investigate the impact of changing the forecast period from one day to

several days. The result of energy payment is expected to decrease with the increasing
of forecast period. However if the battery capacity is too low to the daily solar energy
production, the increase of forecast period will not change the result. Furthermore, one
could work on adding uncertainties to the forecast and create several forecast scenarios
with diﬀerent probabilities. The stochastic linear programming will return more realistic
result in the optimization problem.
Apart from the two battery storage cases that has been studied, another case can
be made based on centralized solution study which is all users in the community have
excess to the battery no matter they installed PV or not. This case may ﬁt better to
the real residential community situation. The increasing of battery users leads to larger
battery capacity, while solar self-consumption and battery utilization may increase due to
the increasing of demand. Determine the optimal size of centralized battery to balanc-
ing the solar production and demand for such a community will be an interesting topic.
Furthermore if a relation between the number of households and the centralized battery
size can be found, the study can be brought into consideration when dimensionalizing real
projects.
