ABSTRACT
This thesis is looking into the implementation of a hydrogen production plant, in order to
facilitate a change from fossil fuel buses to hydrogen buses. The resulting impact on the
power grid is investigated by performing an AC optimal power ﬂow analysis using GAMS.
The GAMS model is based on the existing infrastructure on the Danish island Bornholm,
as most infrastructure may be reused in the new case, e.g. electricity grid and roads, but it
will be necessary to implement new infrastructure for refueling the ﬂeet of buses. The size,
placement and setup of the refueling stations will be investigated, through optimization
in the GAMS model. Furthermore, a technology review is made of a hydrogen refueling
station.
About the model: the AC optimal power ﬂow is calculating the power ﬂows and voltages in
the 60 kV network containing 20 busbars. The hydrogen refueling station, consisting of an
electrolyzer and a storage, is assumed to be placed near the busbar located at Vesthavnen.
The model is solved for a week for each of four seasons, using time steps of one hour. It is
assumed that the simulation year is 2025.
Input data for production from renewable sources, demand consumption and public bus
demand is real data from 2018. The base case is applying an Alkaline electrolyzer and
steel storage tanks at the refueling station. The demand for hydrogen was estimated from
the driving pattern of Bornholm’s public bus company’s, BAT, current driving pattern.
The model and therein the economic impact, capacity of electrolyzer and size of storage is
tested using a base case and a range of scenarios, including but not limited to: diﬀerent
economical parameters for the electrolyzer, alternative location of the refueling station,
diﬀerent refueling pattern for the buses, and interconnection to Sweden as non-operational.
In the base case the optimal capacity for the electrolyzer was found as 3,73 MW with a
storage capacity of 89 MWh. Through all scenarios using the same demand for hydrogen,
the optimal Alkaline electrolyzer size was found to be in the range 3,73 - 4,14 MW and
the storage capacity was found between 46 - 93,3 MWh.
If the demand is increased to twice the demand of the current buses, considering sale of
hydrogen to other purposes, then the electrolyzer capacity was found to be 7,46 MW with
a storage capacity of 177 MWh, both being twice the size of the base case.
The electrolyzer was in general not found to be leading to overload of the 60kV network
with regards to voltages and power ﬂows.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
With elevated focus on sustainability and low CO2 energy production in society today, a
range of new issues emerge such as "how do we manage a power system primarily supplied
by uncontrollable renewable sources?". Three of the critical issues when dealing with
renewable energy, which will be addressed in this paper are: low to zero CO2 emitting
public transport, energy storage and the possibilities of having a large controllable load
capable of consuming when energy production is high.
The investigation in this report is using the Danish island Bornholm as reference for
investigations based on the ambitious goals and high amount of CO2-neutral energy already
implemented in the power system.
In 2015 a strategical energy plan for 2015-2025[1] was released by Energy Innovation
Bornholm in collaboration with the utility companies (today collectively called BEOF),
the public bus company, BAT, and other public authorities. This plan describes the vision,
challenges, and the solutions that will be pursued. The overall goal is to achieve to be
CO2 neutral by 2025. This leads to the issue of storing energy and thereby utilizing the
installed renewable energy sources such as wind and solar power when production is higher
than consumption.
In ’Strategisk Energiplan 2015-2025’[1] page 10 it is stated that “Until we have learned to
store and exploit wind and solar energy to a greater extent than we are able to today, we
are forced to conserve our energy resources” (Translated by author). It furthermore states
that: “With an increasing amount of electricity from wind and solar power the production
will be more ﬂuctuating. This is easier handled in an integrated system, which is able to
deliver power and heat, but also will be able to store it”.
Both of these quotes are mentioning storage as a solution to the issues of ﬂuctuating energy
production.
Furthermore, is it broadly described in the plan that the goal for the transport sector is
to switch to CO2 neutral technologies when they are mature. There is no mentioning of
which technologies will mainly be in focus.
This study will look into a possible solution for the public transport sector on Bornholm,
which is based on bus transport. The investigated solution, introduction of hydrogen
fuelled buses, will to some degree support the three aforementioned issues. The proposed
solution will introduce emission free transport that can be supplied with fuel produced
mainly on excess renewable power. In addition, the production of fuel will be able to help
balancing the system in terms that it can be produced during time where the demand is
low, or during periods of high power production from wind and solar power.
The decision to investigate hydrogen fueled buses instead of battery driven electric buses,

is due to both the ability to consume electric power when needed and the refueling time of
buses. Introduction of battery electric buses would mainly be able to take advantage of the
low consumption during night, and would e.g. in most cases not be able to take advantage
of solar power. As hydrogen is produced and stored, the production pattern is able to be
very ﬂexible and utilize the uncontrollable production from wind and solar power.
This project will examine the optimal placement, size and production pattern of the
hydrogen refueling station, based on the consumption pattern of the buses and production
pattern of the surrounding grid.
The simulation will therefore look into both the hydrogen refueling station and how it
impacts the power system.
1.2
Formulation of the problem
The problems that will be investigated can be quantiﬁed by the following research questions:
• What is the optimal strategy of developing hydrogen refueling stations for Bornholm?
– How much hydrogen is needed for covering the replacement of the current buses?
– What is the optimal size and placement of the hydrogen refueling stations?
• How is the power system inﬂuenced by the new load pattern emerging from the
electrolyzer in the refueling station?
– Investigate the inﬂuence on the distribution network on Bornholm.
1.3
Methodology, scope and conditions
As the project is simulating a scenario where all buses on Bornholm has been exchanged
with buses fueled by hydrogen, the simulation year was chosen to be 2025. This year
was chosen as a realistic time frame for implementing the infrastructure for refueling and,
maybe optimistically, exchange most of the current buses to hydrogen fueled buses. Also,
this is the year by which Bornholm should be CO2 neutral according to the energy plan.
The simulation created in the project consist of two sub-systems. The main part is an
AC optimal power ﬂow simulation, representing the ﬂows and limits in the power system.
Build into this part is the optimal dispatch of the two existing power plants and options for
load shedding and curtailment of wind and solar power. The other part is an optimization
of the storage and electrolyzer capacities along with optimization of hydrogen production.
The scope is to run an AC optimal power ﬂow simulation of the 60 kV electricity grid on
Bornholm, including the interconnection to Sweden and hydrogen refueling station. The
simulation is run for a week in one hour time steps for four diﬀerent seasons.
All simulations are run under normal grid conditions, meaning that no faults, outtakes or
extreme weather conditions will occur during the simulation.
The optimal power ﬂow calculation is validated using two steps. Both steps compare
the results from the model build in this thesis with a representation of the same grid in
PowerFactory.
A sensitivity analysis is used to investigate the inﬂuence of diﬀerent production patterns,
placement of the hydrogen refueling station and a number of other parameters in the
model.

TECHNOLOGY REVIEW: HYDROGEN
REFUELING STATION
This chapter is a technology review describing the parts of a hydrogen refueling station
(HRS). The review is based on information from Hydrogen Europe [2], an interview with an
employee from Everfuel [3] and descriptions from the Danish Energy Agency’s publication
of Technology data [4].
The review is made from a general perspective and some of the options/statements may
not valid for the case of Bornholm, but is valid for other locations in Denmark.
A hydrogen refueling station can be generalized to consist of six parts, the parts can
deviate and some parts may be left out altogether, based on the applied technology and
the settings of the HRS. The six parts are:
• Electrolyzer
• Low/medium pressure storage
• Compressor
• High-pressure storage
• Pre-cooling
• Dispenser
The six parts are visualised in ﬁgure 2.1 and a description of each part is presented in the
following sections. The product catalogue of NEL hydrogen, a producer of hardware for

the fact that the interviewee from Everfuel was a former employee of NEL Hydrogen and

2. Technology review: Hydrogen Refueling Station
2.1
Electrolyzer
The electrolyzer is the component that is converting water to hydrogen using electrical
energy. The technology is based on an electro-chemical reaction that splits water molecules
into oxygen and hydrogen.
Multiple electrolyzer technologies are being researched and developed, but the scope of
this project is not on the technology of the hydrogen production but on the impact of the
use. An in depth description of the technologies is left out and the technologies are merely
mentioned with primary characteristics and key parameters are presented.
Three technologies that should be mentioned are Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM),
Solid Oxide Electrolyzer Cell (SOEC) and Alkaline Electrolyzer Cell (AEC). These are
the most mature technologies that are currently available on the marked and both PEM
and AEC can be found in NEL hydrogen’s catalogue.
All three technologies are build up by a stack of electrolysis cells that are making up the
production unit. The input to these cells are mainly water and electric power. For SOEC,
heat is also needed as the electrolysis cells are working on steam opposed to the other two
technologies which is working on ﬂuid water.
One advantage that was mentioned during the interview, is the AEC technology’s ability
to rapidly change production levels and thereby being highly suited for balancing of the
grid. It was mentioned that the electrolyzer is able to increase production from 10% to
100% in 10 min. In the Technology Data catalogue by the Danish Energy Agency [4] the
ramping time to and from full load for AEC in 2020 is stated to be 8 min. The technology
Data catalogue is stating that also PEM is having fast response times. In the description
of the technology is it stated that PEM has a startup time of 5 min. and a ramp up time
of 1 min. from minimum to maximum load. It is further stated that for 2020 the startup
time and ramping to full load should be just above 30 seconds.
For a hydrogen refueling station, the electrolyzer can either be part of the plant or be
a centralised component. The electrolyzer is the most expensive part of the HRS, but
it becomes more economically feasible at larger sizes e.g. 10 MW[3]. An ideal solution
for dealing with this, is to place the electrolyzer where the heaviest load is (where the
highest amount of buses are refueled) and then introducing decentralised refueling stations
without electrolyzers. This is more applicable for larger areas than Bornholm, e.g. on
Zealand or Jutland where multiple bus garages might be found. The issues and solutions
of a centralised electrolyzer are explained in the following section
2.2
Low/medium pressure storage
The output of the electrolyzer is fed to a low or medium pressure storage unit, in which
the hydrogen is compressed to 100-200 bar for large scale storage. The reason for the
storage at low to medium pressure is due to the cost of compressor and storage tanks.
The components can be bought as standardised components for pressures of less than
200 bar whereas higher pressure would demand custom-made - and thus more expensive -
components. See Figure 2.2 for an overview of the storage setup.
As earlier mentioned, the placement of the electrolyzer is ﬂexible and can either be a part
of the station or be a centralised component. The latter option will need some form of
transportation of the hydrogen from the electrolyzer to the refueling station. Two solutions

are currently utilized, namely having a ﬁxed storage that is reﬁlled by truck or having a
movable storage.
The option with a ﬁxed storage at the decentralized HRS, is functioning much like a
gasoline or diesel refueling station, in terms that a truck is moving fuel from the storage at
the production facility to a storage at the refueling station. Since hydrogen is in a gaseous
state, it is moved by having a higher pressure at the origin. In this case will the origin at
ﬁrst be the electrolyzer storage and then the truck. Each of these exchanges are involved
with a loss of energy.
The other option addresses these losses of energy, by simply producing directly into a
storage that can be moved as the trailer of a truck, and plugged directly into the refueling
station serving as low pressure storage. This is eliminating the two transfers of hydrogen;
to and from the transport tank.
The option used in the simulation is to place the electrolyzer directly at the bus garage,
and thus entirely removing the need for transportation.

2.3
Hydrogen station
NEL hydrogen has coupled the compressor, high pressure storage, and pre-cooling in a
single unit, which is called the ’Hydrogen Station’. The Hydrogen Station is shown in
Figure 2.3.
The compressor is moving hydrogen from the low pressure storage to the high pressure
storage, compressing it to 700-1000 bar depending on the type of consumer.
The high pressure storage is intended for storing the hydrogen that is at the correct pressure
for refueling of vehicles, as the refueling is based on achieving a higher pressure than the
vehicle’s tank.
Pre-cooling is implemented for safety reasons and in order to live up to the current standard
for refueling using light gasses "SAE J2601-1 v2016"[5]. This standard is setting limits for
the fuel delivery temperature as well as maximum fuel ﬂow and limits for pressure. One
example of a limit stated in the standard is the pre-cooling which is specifying that the
hydrogen may not heat up to above 85 ◦C.

Three diﬀerent setups of hydrogen refueling stations using gaseous hydrogen are shown in
the summary-report by NewBusFuel [6] page 15. The ﬁrst is similar to the setup described
previously. This setup is including a low pressure storage and compressor but on the high

The second setup, is as well using a low pressure storage and compressor but on the high

hydraulics to keep the pressure constant during refueling.
The third setup is using a compressor to input hydrogen from the electrolyzer a low pressure
storage, and then it is using a booster compressor after the low pressure storage to supply
the necessary pressure for refueling.
2.4
Dispenser
The dispenser is the connection between the high pressure storage and the vehicle. It is
designed much like the dispenser used to refuel gasoline and diesel cars, and has some of
the same functionalities. The dispenser is equipped with measuring equipment to monitor
the progress of the refueling. Refueling is stopped when the desired pressure is reached in
the customer’s vehicle.
Figure 2.4 is showing NEL hydrogen’s dispenser for cars, and is showing some of the
similarities towards gasoline/diesel car refueling stations. It is stated in the description of
the Hydrogen Station description, that it is possible to connect multiple dispensers to one
stations in order to refuel multiple types of vehicles and multiple vehicles at a time.

The Hydrogen Refueling Station in the model
The hydrogen refueling station is modelled as a storage facility with a number of losses
associated with the production and use of hydrogen, based on key numbers from DEA [4]
and supplemented with numbers obtained during the interview [3].
The model is considering the losses in the refueling station as a total eﬃciency considering
the combined use of energy for production and refueling of hydrogen. The remaining energy
after losses is stored in the storage and then exposed to the losses in fuel consumption by
the buses when satisfying the demand. The energy path, losses and variable names used in
the model is shown in Figure 2.5.

MODELLING
This chapter gives a description of the model developed and executed in GAMS. The grid
on Bornholm is modelled as a multi period optimal AC load ﬂow, using actual grid data
and an additional load from production of hydrogen corresponding to the demand of bus
transport through the day.
The implementation of hydrogen buses on Bornholm has multiple stakeholders e.g. the
future owner of the hydrogen refueling station, the public bus company BAT, and trans-
mission system operator BEOF.
In order to consider all stakeholder, a holistic approach was chosen. The holistic model is
containing an optimal power ﬂow calculation; taking into account the optimal dispatch of
production units, interconnection to Sweden and the limitations of the electrical power

3. Modelling
Equation (3.1) is the objective function consisting of fuel cost for the conventional power
plants, cost of load shedding, cost of wind and PV curtailment, investments for the hydrogen
refueling station, and the traded import/export of power to Sweden. The calculation of cost
is for a whole year, meaning that the cost for each week of the season is multiplied by 13 to
count for the full season. The investment is annualized. The interconnection to Sweden is
modelled as seen from Bornholm, and the power price for importing and exporting power
is therefore the power price of the Swedish power market. The price for curtailment is a
subject for discussion, the model should always try to include as much renewable power as
possible, since there is no cost for producing this power. The curtailment price is set to be
the price of power market DK2. The role of this price is to simulate the wear/tear and
operating cost of curtailing renewables.
Equation (3.2) is the active power balance consisting of power from conventional power
plants, wind power, PV power, the load from conventional demands and hereof load
shedding, for bus 14 the load from the electrolyzer, and for bus 20 the power from the
interconnection.
On the Right-Hand-Side is the active power ﬂow through the lines
connected to the bus.
Equation (3.3) is the reactive power balance. This is consisting of contribution from the
conventional power plants, load shedding and consumption from the loads. Right-Hand-
Side is the reactive power ﬂow through the lines. Reactive load shedding was not included
in the GAMS model, as the loads are deﬁned as purely active power consumers.
Equation (3.4) and (3.5) respectively, are the active and reactive power ﬂow through the
lines of the system, modelled by the voltages at each end of the lines.
Equation (3.6) and (3.7) are setting the ramp up and ramp down limits for the conventional
units.
Equation (3.8) is setting the apparent power limitation for the conventional power plants.
Equation (3.9) and (3.10) are determining the level of curtailment for wind and PV. This
is done by relating the amount of power available with the amount that is used, the
cost of curtailment is included in the objective function. The actual physical limits to
curtailing renewables is not included, but should be noted, since there is a limit for the
degree of freedom in the control of curtailment. Finding the actual limits would include an
investigation of the amount of turbines/panels for each renewable power plant, and their
control span of curtailment.
Equation (3.11) is the simulation of the storage. The storage simulation is constructed as
a circular relation, such that the last time step is before the ﬁrst for each season. Equation
(3.12) is setting the last time step in each season equal to the ﬁrst time step in the next
season.
Equation (3.13) is the eﬃciency of the electrolyzer. The eﬃciency is assumed to be constant
for all loads.

3.2
Validation
As mathematician George E.P. Box stated "All models are wrong, but some are useful".
To make sure that and the model developed in GAMS is part of the pool of useful models,
validation of the model was undergone.
The validation of the power ﬂow model was performed using DIgSILENT PowerFactory in
two stages; an initial stage in which a single load, line and conventional power plant was
tested and then a more detailed stage testing a grid.
Each network was inserted in both GAMS and PowerFactory and the results were compared
and evaluated.
All other parts of the model was as minimum tested on its own before being implemented
to the complete model. This test was in most cases performed in a two bus system, where
the new function was implemented under controlled conditions, such that the outcome
could be predicted and the result therefore could be validated.

3.2.2
Power ﬂow: Grid model Validation
Validation of the grid model is based on a simpliﬁed grid using the layout of the 60 kV
grid on Bornholm. The choice to replicate the 60 kV grid, but simplify lines, loads and the
power plant serves two purposes. Firstly, the model in GAMS does not have any visual
representation of the network that is being calculated, therefore, replicating the network in
PowerFactory based on the data that is used in GAMS will catch any mistakes or missing
information in the data. Secondly, the simpliﬁcation is done in order to make the results
easier to predict by the user.
Another reason for using a simpliﬁed grid for validation, and not directly the PowerFactory
model of the power system on Bornholm, is due to the high level of detail. The PowerFactory
model contain the full grid including 10,5 kV and 0,4 kV grid. Further it contains details on
the exact build-up of the power plants. This is beyond the level of detail that is included
in the GAMS model for this project, since the main scope is to evaluate the overall impact
on the power system.
As for the initial validation, the grid validation was only conducted for one time period.
The grid was ﬁrst solved in GAMS in order to get the optimal dispatch of the power plants.
This dispatch was then input to the PowerFactory model and the results were compared.
The two calculations reached the same results concerning power ﬂows, voltage and angle
for all buses. The results are shown in the Appendix B.

3.2.3
Other validation
The functions for active load shedding, wind curtailment and PV curtailment were tested
in the simple two bus network containing a load at bus 2 and varying power production on
bus 1. Slack bus was set to be bus 1 with ﬁxed voltage at 1 PU.
The functions were tested through four tests, with the following control options:
1. Load shedding
2. Load shedding and wind power curtailment
3. Load shedding and PV power curtailment
4. Load shedding, wind and PV power curtailment
All results of the tests are summarized in Table 3.2.
First test: The production capacity of the power plant was set to 100 MW and 100 Mvar,
and the load was set to 150 MW and 50 Mvar. Load shedding was therefore the only
option for achieving the remaining amount of power.
The simulation was solved and showed to be optimal with load shedding of 63,37 MW
equal to shedding the 50 MW load that can not be covered and 13,37 MW which was due
to grid losses. The function is therefore seen to be working.
Second test: The load was lowered to 100 MW, 50 Mvar and 200 MW wind power was
implemented on bus 1. The conventional power plant is kept with the same production
capacity of 100 MVA and 100 Mvar in this and the two following tests.
The full load is now able to be supplied, with wind power to spare. The wind power is
therefore curtailed by 82,37 MW, thereby showing that the curtailment is functioning.
Third test: The load was kept the same as for second test and 200 MW PV power was
implemented on bus 1. The solution for this test was the same as for wind power. PV
curtailment is therefore shown to be working.
Fourth test: In the last test, both wind and PV is installed with a capacity of 200 MW
each, in order to test the response when all three control options are implemented. The
load is kept at 100 MW, 50 Mvar. The result is that both of the renewable sources are
curtailing, but not to the same degree. The optimal solution found by GAMS is the ﬁrst
optimal solution encountered by the software, and a solution where the curtailment is
equal could therefore also be optimal. The curtailment is happening close to equally for
the two sources because the price of curtailment for both is the same.

4.1
Hydrogen buses
The impact that hydrogen buses have on the electricity grid is solely due to the additional
power demand from local production of hydrogen based on electrolyzer technology. The
ﬁrst step in determining the additional demand was to determine the driving pattern of
the current buses aggregated to kilometers per hour over a week.
This bus demand was then exposed to eﬃciency of fuel consumption of the hydrogen buses
and to losses in the production of hydrogen yielding the actual added demand in [MWh].
The procedure for the above mentioned steps are explained in detail in the next sections.
4.1.1
Bus demand
Hourly or daily driving distances of the buses were not directly available from BAT. Instead,
the demand pattern for the regional busses on Bornholm was deduces from BAT’s time
schedule as available via their webpage[9]. The schedules are composed of departure times,
routes and deviations from the routes. The departure times were used as the time stamp
for when the demand is due.
Google Maps was used to ﬁnd the length of each of the bus routes. This was accomplished
by comparing the map of the routes and listed destinations from BAT with routes registered
by Google using the public transport function. The routes from Google was in general
found to be more detailed and was therefore used as basis for measuring. Each route was
reproduced to the same detail level using the "by car" function as given for public transport,
as illustrated in Figure 4.2. This procedure was required since it was not possible to get
information about how long the routes registered for public transport were.

The optimization model in this project is focusing on the electric power system on Bornholm,
which is part of the Danish power market DK2. It is therefore assumed that all power
imported or exported is going out of or in to the Swedish market SW4, and therefore sold
or bought at the market price of this Swedish power market.
It is furthermore assumed that the Swedish market always is willing to sell or buy the
amount of power as bid by the model, with no ramping constraints. It is consequently
possible for the model to sell at full capacity for one hour and buy at full capacity in the
next. This behavior might not be a true possibility of the real interconnection and market
trading, that is probably having some limits to the rate of change and amount of power
that is sold. The data for production and consumption is real life measurements from the
power system, with the most sudden changes taking place in wind power production and
demand. The need for high rates of change is therefore not very likely. Moreover, the
simulation is running on an hourly basis, and this makes room for some ramping between
the bids.

4.6
Scenarios
The implementation of demand for hydrogen used in fuel-cell buses will be tested through
diﬀerent scenarios consisting of a base case, scenarios for diﬀerent refueling strategies and
scenarios that might occur during operation of the hydrogen refueling station.
All scenarios are tested through a full week in each season. The main focus in the evaluation
of the impact of the scenarios will be change in the objective function, loading of the
electric grid, and optimal sizing of the electrolyzer and storage of hydrogen. Furthermore,
any change in strategy for production of hydrogen will be analysed.
Base case analysis
The purpose of the base case is to compare the results from the optimal power ﬂow with

The power system is simulated to be in full island mode in this scenario, as the
interconnection is disconnected, meaning that no power is send to or received from

normally lead to curtailment in case of more production from wind and PV than can be
consumed by conventional demand, but with the electrolyzer installed, the curtailment
should be minimized. The amount of curtailment is therefore, in case of overproduction,

for the simulation by reaching this voltage, even though this is the most optimal for the
real system.

CONCLUSION
In order to supply fuel to a ﬂeet of hydrogen buses replacing the current busses used for
public transportation at Bornholm, a yearly amount of hydrogen equal to 440,9 tons need
to be produced. This is equivalent to 14,6 GWh of hydrogen.
For the simulation of reaching the goal of emission free public transportation in 2025,
the technology for fuel production was chosen to be Alkaline Electrolyzer. The size of
the electrolyzer was in the base case found to be 3,73 MW, whereas during the scenarios
supplying an identical sized demand, the capacity deviated between 3,33 MW and 4,15 MW.
The implemented capacities are expressed as the amount of power which the electrolyzer
is able to consume. The capacity of 3,33 MW was found in scenario 3 - future technology
using Solid Oxide Electrolyzer as technology for fuel production. If the electrolyzer was
replaced with an Alkaline Electrolyzer having the same hydrogen production capacity,
then this plant would be 4,15 MW and one of the largest capacities for the electrolyzer in
this study. The lowest hydrogen production capacity was found as the capacity for the
base case and scenario 1 - distributed refueling during the day.
Both base case and distributed refueling are implementing an electrolyzer that is exactly
large enough to cover the maximum demand during summer. This strategy is leaving no
room for scheduled or unscheduled maintenance during summer, and might therefore be
too small for a real life case.
It is seen from all other scenarios that a larger electrolyzer is implemented in order to be
able to produce hydrogen at the most optimal times. The hydrogen is mainly produced
during night or when production from wind is high.
The capacity of the storage was for the base case found to be 89 MWh, deviating between
45,99 MWh and 93,30 MWh during the scenarios with an equally sized demand for
hydrogen. For most scenarios a smaller storage was implemented compared to base case.
The exception being when the hydrogen refueling station is placed in Hasle, for which
the storage was slightly larger, due to that the production is highly depending on the
interconnection to Sweden. The smallest storage was seen in Scenario 1 - Distributed
refueling of buses, which was expected, as the demand is distributed over the course of the
day.
The size of the electrolyzer and storage was seen to double when doubling the demand for
hydrogen in Scenario 5 - Sale of hydrogen to companies. The electrolyzer reaching a size
of 7,46 MW and a storage capacity of 177,39 MWh.
By comparing the base case and scenario 1, it is deducted that the number of refueling
periods had almost no inﬂuence on the electrolyzer capacity and production pattern.
However, the storage capacity was reduced to half the size.
In order to minimize the storage size and since hourly refueling or refueling when needed

might not be an option, the best refueling pattern could be a mix of base case and scenario
1; by scheduling refueling of buses during two or more periods distributed through the day.
Two placements of the hydrogen refueling station was explored, at Vesthavnen in Rønne
and in Hasle. The locations were tested against each other and Vesthavnen was found to
be the most optimal placement. Vesthavnen had in particular two advantages over Hasle:
ﬁrstly, it was seen to be the most logical placement when considering the bus routes of
regional buses and secondly, the objective function was lowest for Vesthavnen. Furthermore,
Vesthavnen is where BAT’s buses garage is located, it is close to the main power plant,
and far enough away from the interconnection such that it has minimal inﬂuence on the
power trade with Sweden.

export/import of power to Sweden and voltages.

seen to be 5,33 MWh in the base case without hydrogen production. When implementing
the electrolyzer in the base case and testing the inﬂuence in scenario 1 through 5, the load
shedding was at the same level and occurring on the same buses. In scenario 6 - oﬄine
interconnection and scenario 7 - worst case, the increase in load shedding was not due to
the electrolyzer, but had root cause in the other changes introduced to the power system.
Some inﬂuence is seen in the amount of power being imported from and exported to Sweden
when implementing the hydrogen refueling station. The inﬂuence is dominating during
scenario 2 - electrolyzer placed in Hasle and scenario 5 - sale of hydrogen to companies.
The main cause for the electrolyzer to inﬂuence power trade with Sweden, is a result of
that the model is trying to sell as much power as possible to Sweden, therefore less power
can be sold when additional power is consumed on Bornholm.
The overall voltages are seen to not be inﬂuenced by the electrolyzer. Only during scenario
6 - interconnection oﬀ, and scenario 7 - worst case, are the voltages changing a noteworthy
amount. As for load shedding, the change in voltage for scenario 6 and 7 is not due to the
electrolyzer.
Furthermore, all voltages and power ﬂows are limited in the optimization, to always stay
within the allowed limits with voltages being the most restricting for the current model.
The result of the simulation for implementing a hydrogen plant for enabling the public
buses to be emission free shows that the yearly cost for fuel and investment in refueling
station is estimated to approximately €1 billion for driving 3,4 billion km. Additionally
the simulation shows, that the electrolyzer can be implemented without any signiﬁcant
impacts to the current power grid.
6.1
Perspectives
It is likely that the demand for hydrogen will increase in the future as partially investigated
in Scenario 5 - Sale of hydrogen to companies. The introduction of a hydrogen refueling
station on Bornholm could lead the way for other land based transport, and encourage a
larger shift to hydrogen fueled transport.
The Danish magazine ’Ingeniøren’ published an article on the 20th of December 2019[26]
announcing that the Danish energy company Ørsted is building a 2 MW electrolyzer close to

the power plant ’Avedøreværket’ in Copenhagen. The intention for this plant is to produce
hydrogen based on two oﬀshore windmills providing fuel for 20 to 30 buses. The project is
remarkably similar to the project investigated in this thesis, but the electrolyzer capacity
is smaller than the capacity found as optimal in this project. The choice of electrolyzer
size in Ørsteds project is based on, the assumption that the hydrogen production is only
based on power from the two windmills, which each has a capacity of 3,6 MW. The size is
therefore likely to be based on the expected average production from the two windmills. It
is stated in the article, that the plant is estimated to produce 600 kg of hydrogen per day.
It was found that the amount needed to fuel the public buses on Bornholm, would require
a production capacity of 1,21 ton or double the expected production capacity of the plant
being build at Avedøreværket.
An article in Børsen from the 19th of December 2019[27], published that the government
in Denmark is funding hydrogen projects in the years 2020-2025 with a total amount of
128 million DKK. Some of the funding is already allocated to a large scale project in
Fredericia stated to become the largest production facility in all of Europe. The time
perspective in the thesis is suitable for applying for funding from this pool set forward by
the government. This would enhance the possibilities for implementing a suﬃciently large
electrolyzer, which is able to cover the demand and utilize ﬂuctuating renewable energy at
Bornholm.
6.2
Future work
In order to achieve a simulation that is more true to the behavior of the system on Bornholm
a number of new control options could be implemented in the model.
The system is in need of additional reactive power or voltage control, as mentioned in
section 5.3.7. This could be implemented by letting the model use a speciﬁed amount of
renewable production capacity for reactive power control. Another solution is to include
the 60/10 kV transformers, assuming these have a tap-changing capability, thereby setting
the limit of 0,9 - 1,1 PU on the 10 kV level.
Furthermore, to introduce a behavior in the simulation of reaching as close to 1 PU as
possible, a reward could be introduced when this is accomplished or a cost whenever the
voltage is not 1 PU. The behavior of the interconnection should be investigated, as it seems
that it is too much of an advantage for the system to export power to Sweden. This is
either due to the cost of power production, mainly in the main power plant, or due to the
lack of trading costs, taxes etc.
The actual placement of 60 kV connection point at Vesthavnen should be investigated in
order to determine whether it is optimal to connect directly to this point. If it is found
that the busbar is too far from the bus garage or the bus routes, then the 10 kV grid should
be examined for optimal placements. The impact on the 10 kV grid should be examined, if
the connection is changed to this system.
A further option to sell hydrogen for additional land based transport or ferry transport
could be exploited, as mentioned in section 4.6.6. The amount of additional potential
was chosen arbitrarily in this thesis, and the actual magnitude of additional sale should
therefore be investigated.

==PAGE:74==
6. Conclusion
Hydrogen could either be sold as pure hydrogen or reprocessed to ammonia for use by
ferries. If the choice would fall on selling ammonia then the reprocessing plant should be
investigated.
An additional possibility for a hydrogen project on Bornholm, could be to exploit the
by-product of hydrogen production. The by-product of hydrogen production is oxygen
that can either be emitted directly to the atmosphere or collected in tanks and sold. The
latter option is an additional option for gaining revenue from the electrolyzer with the
extra advantage that the oxygen is produced based on CO2 neutral sources.
Another area that could be investigated is the option for power to gas to power, i.e. using
the hydrogen storage partly as a battery for power support. The eﬃciency of a reversible
plant should be found as well as any additional costs or investments related to the power
production capability of the plant.
Investigation of the actual changes in the bus demand during the seasons for a year could
be performed. During the time of this thesis, only the same bus schedule was available
from BAT’s webpage, and material might need to be supplied from BAT.
