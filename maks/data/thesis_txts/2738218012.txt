Response calculation for boat access to a floating wind turbine
Abstract
Floating wind turbines have been gaining traction among the offshore wind energy technologies over the
last decade. Yet, their maintenance involves several aspects that are different to bottom fixed structures,
particularly their accessibility in adverse sea states. Until now, wave height (Hs) and peak period (Tp) have
been the go-to criteria when it comes to planning offshore operations for wind turbines. However, these
criteria might inadequately capture the complex interactions between the moving floater and the vessel. The
present work defines an alternative approach to quantify floater accessibility, relying on floater and Crew
Transfer Vessel (CTV) motions and relative forces, from a probabilistic point of view. The floater and CTV
motions in an irregular sea state are first computed to understand their dynamic interactions. Subsequently,
by imposing connection constraints that involve the simultaneous motions of the two bodies, the relative
forces acting between them can be determined. Given a certain transfer operation duration, the required
thrust force that needs to be delivered by the vessel to ensure that it sticks to the floater can then be prob-
abilistically estimated. Extension to second order forces is then discussed since their contribution can be
significant for low frequency motions. Hereby, by shifting the focus from conventional parameters (Hs and
Tp) to dynamic forces and motions in a simple framework, this study contributes to the development of more
comprehensive strategies for the maintenance schedule of floating offshore wind farms.

1
State of the art for maintenance methods on a floating wind farm
1.1
Floating Wind Industry today
Over the past few years, floating wind turbines have been gaining traction, primarily due to their capacity
for installation in deep waters, where wind resources are more important. Indeed, according to Wind Europe
[30], around 80% of the offshore wind potential lies in European seas at depths exceeding 60 meters, where
the conventional approach of employing bottom-fixed foundations becomes economically unviable. When
it comes to floating wind installed capacity, Europe has about 200 MW in 2024 but this number is expected
to rapidly grow. Indeed, France has tendered a 250 MW floating wind farm in south Brittany, won by a
consortium of BayWare and Elicio in May 2024, and will tender an additional 500 MW in the Mediterranean
sea. Other countries also have huge ambitions like the UK which has already tendered 15 GW of floating
wind development areas [31]. DNV estimates that floating wind turbines should represent 15% of the
offshore wind power generation in 2050 with an installed capacity of 264 GW [12]. However, this appealing
technology requires a heavy supply chain, important engineering capabilities and port readiness to scale it
up from demonstration to commercial projects. The next section will focus on the new challenges when it
comes to Operation and Maintenance (O&M) for floating wind turbines.
1.2
New O&M challenges
Floating wind technology introduces complexities in both the installation process and offshore O&M [10].
Notably, the safe transfer of crew members from the vessel, generally Crew Transfer Vessels (CTV) and
Offshore Service Vessels (OSV), to the floater is challenged as the dynamic behavior of the two structures
is complex. Indeed, both the floating foundation and the vessel are moving, contrary to bottom-fixed foun-
dations where only the vessel needs to cope with the sea state. As such, in order to deal with adverse sea
states, towing the turbines back to port to perform the reparation onshore is possible and less risky from a
technical point of view. This was for instance the case for one of the turbines at Kincardine floating wind
farm which faced a technical failure in May 2023 [27]. However, this process takes a lot of time as shown
in Figure 1 with three AHTS mobilized during 100 vessel days and would result in an important loss re-
garding the electricity production while the turbine is disconnected. Hence the need to come up with a more
robust solution through offshore maintenance. Recently, in June 2024, a new project, developed by differ-
ent french academic and industry players, has been launched to optimize heavy maintenance of Floating
Offshore Wind Turbines [7]. This project will focus on Major Component Replacement (MCR) offshore to
reduce maintenance costs. It is thus clear that the floating wind industry is actively engaging on the offshore
O&M topic. The next section will discuss the possible transfer methods when dealing with CTV.

1.3
Transfer methods with CTV
Two main methods exist to access a turbine from a CTV, the "Bump and Jump" and the "Walk to work"
methods, which are explained respectively in subsubsection 1.3.1 and subsubsection 1.3.2. Other accessi-
bility methods exist using OSVs or helicopters but are not included in the scope of this Masterâ€™s Thesis.
1.3.1
"Bump and Jump" method
The "Bump and Jump" method is so far the most commonly used to access a turbine with a CTV (both
for bottom-fixed and floating wind farms). The CTV approaches the foundation and pushes its fender to
the structure so that both bodies can stick to each other, due to friction, as shown in Figure 2. Therefore,
the vessel is required to deliver a sufficient thrust from its propeller to remain stationary during the crew
transfer operation, which is often quantified by the bollard push capacity of the vessel. Most transfers are
limited to 1.5 m of significant wave height (Hs) as a rule of thumb [2]. However, BW Ideol, which operates
the Floatgen wind turbine (floating wind farm demonstration project in France), has carried out offshore
operations with Hs up to 2.3 m [6]. Therefore, bottom-fixed industry standards might not be applicable
to the newly emerging floating industry, due to the many different floater designs, in comparison to the
standard monopile design. This study tries to overcome this general limitation by introducing a simple
method to derive accessibility limits, based on floater and vessel motions as well as relative forces between
the two bodies.

1.3.2
"Walk to work" method
Even though "Bump and Jump" is the most commonly used method for CTV, "Walk to work" systems, more
commonly used on OSV, are now being developed on CTV. It consists in walking on a motion-compensated
bridging system between the CTV and the foundation, enabling the crew to access the platform, hence the
name of the method. However, these systems require bigger CTVs (and most commonly OSVs), and, as a
result, are not compatible with most CTVs on the market [2]. For instance, the CTV shown in Figure 3 has
a length overall of 41 m, while most of them do not exceed 30 m in length.

1.4
Recent research on floater accessibility
Floater accessibility has become one of the major topics in the O&M discussions over the past few years.
This section presents methodologies and studies dealing with floater accessibility.
Barthelemy has computed quantitative Hs limitations for berthing operations on different structures [4]. He
calculated the tangential and normal forces that occur when doing this operation and, by comparing to a
friction coefficient, derived a maximum Hs allowed for berthing. Results from his study shows that floater
accessibility is made easier than monopile accessibility due to the larger dimensions of the floater. As such,
the floater masks the CTV from the waves, therefore limiting the excitation of the vessel.
A great source of inspiration for this study also comes from [5] which introduces a constrain matrix to
link the floater and vessel motions. A probabilistic approach based on the motion limitation is undertaken
through a Rayleigh distribution of peaks to determine a maximum displacement allowance. Sensitivity anal-
ysis on the wave heading is performed and shows that for short wave periods, better results are obtained for
beam seas (waves coming from the bow or starboard of the vessel), and, on the contrary, longer wave periods
advantage head seas, as shown in Figure 4. However, influence of second-order loads is not quantified and
mentioned as possible future work while they might have a significant impact on the floater displacement.
Figure 4: Max wave height allowance for regular waves in during a berthing operation from [5]. Radial
coordinate represents the wave peak period.
Another major contribution in the O&M topic comes from CoreWind which published a paper dealing with
Floating Wind O&M Strategies [11]. They present conditions that need to be met to ensure a safe crew
transfer operation from the vessel to the floater. In their study, CoreWind suggests to check that both the
relative motions of the two bodies and the no-slip condition between them is ensured. They also use the
constrain matrix mentioned in [5] to link the two bodies, and additionally recommend that influence of the

not been considered and the control system requires heavier computational capabilities.

probabilistic assessment for wave elevation, relying on a Rayleigh distribution of peaks to define a max Hs

two bodies (floater and vessel), and then to the relative forces occurring between them during the berthing
operation. The next section will present the floater and vessel models used for this study.

This section dives into the description of the CTV chosen for the study and the preparation of the input

2.2.1
CTV description
CTVs are commonly used to access offshore wind farms. These vessels are used for light maintenance at
the wind farm, with ability to carry a few passengers (usually up to 12) [2]. They can also transport small
amounts of equipment, with a load capacity of around 30 tonnes [15]. A study from Offshore Renewable
Energy (ORE) Catapult [26], a leading innovation centre for offshore renewable energy, assessed the real-
world capabilities of CTV at Gwynt y MÃ´r offshore wind farm, located 15 km off the North Coast of Wales,
which has bottom-fixed foundations. They showed that access using CTVs was possible for 88% of the
time with wave heights up to Hs = 1.8 m. They also found that the largest vessels were not necessarily
performing better from a motion point of view, but also that both the fender configuration and the skipper
had an important role in the crew transfer process.
When it comes to floating offshore wind farm transfer operations, little data is available and limiting criteria
are not well known, given that the industry has not reached a commercial stage yet. Indeed, Hywind Tampen,
the biggest floating wind farm, has a capacity of 88 MW and is made of only 11 floating turbines [13],
while the biggest bottom-fixed offshore wind farm in terms of capacity reaches 1.32 GW with 165 turbines
[33]. Recommendations on accessibility limits for floating wind farms have been given by CoreWind [11],
which, in addition to rely on a max. value of Hs = 4 m, gives recommendations on motions. For a CTV, the
maximum relative rotations of 2.5Â° in roll, 10Â° in pitch and 2.5Â° in yaw should be considered, as well as a
transfer window length (time interval when the crew transfer operation is feasible) of 15 s, and a minimum
percentage of transfer time of 50%.

vessel model does not originate from a marine industry company nor from academia, but serves the purpose

Input file preparation
The geometry provided by Offshore Wind Learning has first been imported to BEMRosetta to extract the
geometry below waterline, in order to process it in WAMIT.

3
Single body motion
The first part of the work carried out for this study consists in computing the motions of the 15MW Univer-
sity of Maine floater and the CTV independently. Even though these results have not been used as such for
the rest of the study, it enables to understand the behaviour of the two structures, before accounting for the
two bodies together. Besides, in this section, only first-order loads that induce motions at a specific wave
frequency have been taken into account, and lead to the computation of the transfer function of the system.
Second-order loads will be discussed in section 8. It is important to mention that aerodynamic loads have
been neglected since the turbine is not spinning during offshore operations.

3.2
WAMIT and the linear radiation-diffraction theory
3.2.1
Potential flow theory and radiation-diffraction problem
WAMIT is a computational tool developed by the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) which ap-
plies the potential flow theory to derive the added mass, damping and hydrostatic stiffness matrices, as
well as the wave excitation force, as shown in Equation 4. One can refer to [24] for a detailed review on
potential flow theory. The hydrodynamic problem can be decomposed into diffraction and radiation parts.
The diffraction consists in assuming that the body is fixed and exposed to incoming waves with an angular
frequency Ï‰. When it comes to the radiation, the body oscillates with frequency Ï‰ in still water, producing
waves that radiate away from it [22]. A visualization of the radiation-diffraction problem from [17] can be
found in Figure 12. The set of discrete frequencies Ï‰ used are given as inputs to the problem. Both solutions
from the radiation and diffraction problems are then added to deduce the wave excitation force on the body.
The next section will dive into WAMIT parametrisation for the single body problem.

3.2.2
Setting up WAMIT for the single body problem
WAMIT relies on various input files to compute the different elements presented in subsection 3.1. One can
refer to [29] for more detailed information on the different input files but below are given the main elements
needed to run a WAMIT simulation:
â€¢ A configuration file .cfg, in which general information is mentioned such as the form of the other
input files, units for the frequencies, outputs that need to be computed.
â€¢ A potential control file .pot in which the user indicates specifically the set of discrete frequencies Ï‰,
the list of wave headings, as well as the coordinates of the body in the WAMIT referential. Waves
propagating in the X-direction as shown in Figure 8 were considered for the main results of this study.
Influence of the wave heading on the floater-vessel motions will be discussed in subsection 4.4. The
frequency range chosen goes from 0.01 Hz to 0.3 Hz to capture both the low frequency response
(around the natural frequency of the floater) and the frequencies closer to the wave spectrum chosen,
which will be developed in subsection 3.5.
â€¢ A force control file .frc which includes the method used to compute the forces, the center of gravity
of the floater, in addition to the mass, damping and stiffness matrices. However, it is also possible not
to indicate these matrices and rather to manually include them afterward in MATLAB when solving
the equation of motions, which is what was done for this study. For the floater, the forces have been
computed by pressure integration of the diffraction potential.
â€¢ A geometry file representing the body on which the radiation-diffraction process is applied. It is
important to be coherent in the body positioning both in the geometry file and in WAMIT to avoid
computing response at the wrong location and create inconsistencies across the different quantities
derived.
An example of the potential and force control input files is presented in Appendix subsection A.3 (obtained
from the Github repository mentioned in [3]). WAMIT runs sequentially the two subprograms POTEN and
FORCE. POTEN derives the radiation-diffraction velocity potentials based on the inputs given in the .pot
file while FORCE computes the hydrodynamic coefficients and the force on the body [29]. The output data
from the simulation is then collected in an output file which is imported in MATLAB for post-processing
operations, to derive the RAO of the floater. Other quantities are computed outside WAMIT, be they the
mass matrix, the viscous damping matrix and the mooring stiffness matrix. They are introduced in the next
section.

6.2.6
Limitations of the probabilistic approach
In this section, the limitations that should be considered when implementing the probabilistic formulation
of the problem are discussed. As such, the ones from the floater-vessel modelling (for instance the different
matrices derived) are not mentioned here, as they are independent from the method employed to compute the
waiting time. One of the major drawbacks of the method comes from employing the Poisson distribution.
Indeed, as shown in subsubsection 6.2.2, the Poisson distribution does not perfectly match with the actual
distribution of the peaks, especially for small values of force. Moreover, the probabilistic approach, in its
current framework, does not enable to add any other criteria, whether it be on relative motions or wave
height. Therefore, one should first check that the motions are below given thresholds and then compute
what the minimum propeller force must be to enable a safe crew transfer operation.
6.2.7
Influence of the wave direction
One can wonder how the wave direction impacts the required propeller force. At first glance, one could
think that waves coming from the bow or the starboard side would result in a higher propeller force needed
from the vessel, due to important induced sway motions. However, in this study, no significant differences in
terms of required propeller force were found when a sensitivity analysis was performed on the wave heading.
This is due to the fact that the major force to counterbalance originates from the heave motion restriction
(as observed in Figure 27). As a result, it would be interesting as a future work to derive a probabilistic
formulation that combines both the propeller force and the vessel motions threshold into a unique framework
to better assess accessibility for different wave directions. Indeed, in the case followed for this study, the
wave heading considered was 0Â°, and as observed in section 4, the relative rotations between the two bodies
were not the limiting criteria for the berthing operation, which was rather the propeller force delivered by
the vessel. However, in case the waves are coming from the bow or starboard side, the limiting criteria
to perform the crew transfer operation might come from the rotations as shown Figure 25, instead of the
propeller force. This appears as a limitation of the study.

6.3
Conclusion on the two waiting time definitions
Two methods were defined in the previous sections to quantitatively assess the waiting time to perform an
offshore operation on a floating turbine. The first one evaluates the time before a sufficient weather window
appears. The second one approaches the problem from a different angle by evaluating the time available
before encountering another slip condition that prevents the realization of the offshore operation. Both
methods offer an understanding of the floater accessibility and present different advantages and drawbacks
that are elaborated on below.
When it comes to the advantages of each, the first method evaluates when the transfer operation can or
cannot be started based on the contact between the two bodies, and, as such, can be extended to add criteria
on motions. This would result in a multi criteria analysis that permits to understand if the limiting criteria is
based on the sea state (Hs and Tp), the motions or the contact force. On the other hand, the second method
dives into the expected waiting time before the contact between the vessel and floater is lost. Its strength
lies in the probability theory applied, which offers a simple formulation to derive the necessary thrust force
that needs to be delivered by the vessel.
The drawback of one method is the strength of the other. Indeed, the granularity provided by the time series
approach of the first method enables to add different criteria that can be both on motions, forces and sea
states but requires more computational time and efforts since, at every time step, all the given criteria must
be verified. On the other hand, the simple formulation found through Equation 19 provides a straightforward
approach but cannot be overlaid with other criteria. Indeed, it would be interesting to add a probabilistic
framework in the relative motions between the floater and the vessel so that both criteria on motions and
forces can be applied. However, this has not been included in the scope of the thesis but could be relevant
as a further study.
However, regardless of the method used, one can ask what would the consequence of such an additional
force on the motion of the floater be when it comes to the equation of motion. Would the floater translate in
surge on 1 m or 10 m? This question is answered in subsection 6.4. Another point is to include the previous
work in a more practical framework, for instance by computing the force at a specific offshore location,
using previous metocean data. This will be the purpose of section 7.

Conclusion and limitations
This Masterâ€™s Thesis project presented another approach to the classic and widely used Hs âˆ’Tp statistics.
Indeed, offshore wind developers often use these two parameters to assess the viability of their offshore
operation, however not accounting on the motions and forces between the vessel and the foundation.
First, the floater and the vesselâ€™s responses have been computed through RAOs in a single body configura-
tion, for a 0Â° wave heading. This enabled to derive each bodyâ€™s motions given a certain sea state defined
through a JONSWAP spectrum. A statistical approach relying on Rayleigh distributions of peaks has then
been defined to quantify the maximum allowed motions, given a certain safety level. Since the previous
work disregarded the influence of the CTV approaching the floater and therefore, their relative interactions,
it has been extended to a double body configuration. Results have shown that the presence of the vessel did
not impact the floater motions, due to its relatively small size compared to the floating foundation. However,
since the vessel is masked by the floater, its motions were slightly reduced in surge and pitch.
Since the floater-vessel motion analysis did not enable to understand the force interaction between the two
bodies, the system has been constrained to ensure motion synchronicity. This can be achieved by adding
an additional thrust force delivered by the vessel. Due to the friction occurring between the two bodies,
motions are also limited in the Y and Z-direction, even though the force delivered by the vessel is along the
X-direction. If this force is large enough, the no-slip condition can occur between the two bodies, enabling
a safe transfer operation. This condition has been incorporated into a probabilistic framework to estimate
the force that must be delivered by the vessel, taking into account the crew transfer operation duration, the
sea state, and a specified safety level. This has been made possible by assuming that the slip condition
encountered follows a Poisson distribution. The previous framework has then been applied to a specific
offshore location, relying on hourly metocean data. Results show that the force method follows the same
trend as the Hs one, however, it offers a more complete approach, since it includes for instance the transfer
operation duration.
Extension to second-order forces has been included to the previous framework and has shed the light on
additional second-order motions in surge that were not captured in the previous linear analysis. However,
these motions slightly impact the force that must be applied by the floater to ensure a no-slip condition.
Therefore, under the specific assumptions considered (for instance the absence of free surface forcing con-
tributions) the probabilistic approach remained valid despite the additional second-order force.
However, the framework applied in this study comes with certain limitations. First, the modelling could
be improved by continuing the preliminary work carried out on second order loads, notably by including
the free surface forcing contribution or the drag loads. The easiest way to include them would be to solve
the equation of motions in the time domain as in each time step, the structural velocity can be determined.
Moreover, experiments could be undertaken to benchmark the results of this analytical study with real-life
data. Besides, the waves could be modelled using spreading functions as the modelling applied in this work
only relies on a JONSWAP spectrum with wave propagating in a single direction. Finally, the work carried
out did not account for wind forcing. Even when the turbine is not spinning, the wind still exerts a force on
the structure, which could lead to additional motions that need to be quantified.

Future work
This statistical approach to evaluate the floater accessibility with a CTV paves the way for further studies.
In addition to the modelling improvements mentioned in the limitations in section 9, possible future work is
mentioned below.
First, the scope of the study can for instance be extended. Indeed, one could also add some limitations for
water on deck, which can be derived from the RAO, to see if that could be a limiting factor as well for
the berthing operation. Another idea is to include a full trip from shore to the floating wind farm in CTV,
applying some motion limitations during transit with a statistical approach as well as the force methodol-
ogy during the offshore operation itself in order to better support offshore operation planning. What would
be particularly interesting to study is the combination of different criteria under the same probabilistic ap-
proach. This would enable to take into account the motions, the forces and the sea state and derive an
expected probabilistic waiting time for the offshore operation.
Then, this framework could be applied to other types of offshore operations. For instance, one could dive
into a probabilistic framework for waiting time estimation of a walk-to-work operation, from an OSV to a
floater, based on motion limitations. This would most likely involve a controller with a feedback loop since
the gangway is already motion-compensated. The same goes for a major component replacement (blade re-
placement for instance) with a crane on a heavy lift vessel, which would require additional force modelling
due to the load attached to the hook.
Besides, this methodology could enable to benchmark different floaters and vessels from an O&M perspec-
tive since this method relies both on the floater and vessel RAOs. As such, floating wind developers could
easily improve their floater selection process.
Finally, the results of this approach could be used as inputs for a further technico-economic study, measuring
the impact of this new accessibility windows on floating wind farm OPEX.
