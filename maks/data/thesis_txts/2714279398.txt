Introduction
Engineered timber has in recent decades become an appealing construction material due
to its mechanical properties, aesthetic qualities, and low embodied CO2 emissions in com-
parison with more conventional materials. Engineered timber consists of modified timber
products that are manufactured to increase the strength and stiffness of the elements.
Cross Laminated Timber, LVL and Glulam are types of engineered timber which, when
combined, are referred to as ‘heavy timber frame construction’. The advantage of heavy
timber frame construction compared to light timber frame construction is its structural fire
performance [1]. While the fire performance of individual timber members is generally well
understood, wood joints (or connections) are not as easily understood in fire and require
a more in depth investigation.
It has been shown that the greater the metal area exposed to high temperatures, the worse
the connection performance in fire [1] [2]. Therefore, the common practise for protecting
metal connectors is to embed them between the adjoining engineered timber members, so
that they are insulated by the surrounding wood and char layer. However, ensuring that
the joint is completely tight is not always possible since wood is a natural material and
due to construct-ability, tolerance gaps in the joints remain unavoidable. These tolerance
gaps typically range from 1 to 10mm and are used where two timber members meet at a
joint since the two members might have different moisture content and therefore swell or
shrink slightly uniquely depending on the season [3], [4].
The literature on fire resistance of wood joints for heavy timber frame construction is
mainly subdivided into two categories: timber-to-timber- and steel-to-timber-connections.
While the fire performance of these joints has been studied for steel-timber connectors,
there is a lack of information on the behaviour of aluminium connectors, which are nev-
ertheless widely available. For steel connectors, the predominant failure mode is a loss
of embedding strength in the timber surrounding the steel [5]. However, aluminium loses
significant strength 1 at much lower temperatures than steel [7], so exposure to high tem-
peratures could potentially lead to earlier failure of the aluminium connector itself.
Since aluminum connections will continue to be commercially available due to their excel-
lence in malleability, understanding to which extent a char layer with a certain tolerance
gap can reduce the temperature evolution of the aluminum connection is worth an inves-
tigation to understand how the connections fire resistance may be prolonged.
1.1
Objective and Aim
The objective of this master thesis is to investigate the effect of two different tolerance
gaps on aluminium connections in a wood joint, during fire exposure, as some convec-
tion of heat is expected when gaps are introduced, but the effect is unknown. The report
presents the results of small-scale experiments, including the temperature evolution in
such connections, char depths, and an estimation of the strength reduction of the alu-
minum brackets during fire. The aim of the project was to determine if any additional fire
protection measures need to be implemented in the tolerance gaps.

Theoretical Background
2.1
Tolerance gaps
According to a study performed at The Swiss Federal Institute of Technology in Zürich,
ETH, having a larger tolerance gap between the beam and the column, reducing the
spacing between dowels or including a reinforced self-drilling screw showed to have a
negative influence on the fire resistance of beam to column shear connections [8]. This
article, Fire resistance tests on timber beam-to-column shear connections [8], presented
the results of an extensive experimental program on the fire behavior of timber shear
connections were a total of nine different connection typologies were tested. Of those
nine, two were commercially available connections, the other seven were custom made.
All the connections were steel dowel connections except one of the two commercially
available connections, which was an aluminum dovetail connection, composed of two
interlocking parts that were separately screwed into each member. The commercially
available aluminum connector tested in the study is similar to the one tested in this project
except in the study the aluminum was fully exposed and not embedded into the timber
member.
Each members was subjected to 30 percent of its ambient shear capacity during the fire
testing. When the tolerance gap of the steel dowel connection in Figure 2.1 was increased
from a 10 mm to 20 mm the reduction in fire resistance was reported as eleven minutes.
The reduction in fire resistance between the 0mm and 10mm case was not as severe, but
was reported as three minutes for the steel-to-timber typology [8]. While three minutes
might not seem like a long duration it needs to be compared to the total fire resistance
of the connection. The average of the two tests completed for typology A.1 in the study
was 41 minutes, thus three minutes are 7,3 percent of the total fire resistance of the
connection.
Figure 2.1: steel dowel connection [8].

2.5
Char layer and pyrolysis zone
The environment in which timber burns plays a significant role in determining its burning
rate [9]. The burning behavior of timber is a complicated process. Up until the onset of
pyrolysis3, which occurs at around 280°C carbon dioxide, acetic acid, and water are given
off. Whereas during pyrolysis, light tars4 and methyl alcohol are given off, and a char layer
forms [11].
The formation of the insulating char layer alters the heat transfer into the timber, resulting
in a decreased pyrolysis rate of the timber section. The decrease in heat transfer is be-
cause the char has lower effective thermal conductivity, thereby reducing the conductive
heat transfer to the pyrolysis zone [9]. Figure 2.7 shows the order of the different layers
and zones.

The two simplified methods, RCS and RPM, assume a constant charring rate for simplifi-
cation, however, the charring rate depends on the specific heat and thermal conductivity
as seen by Equation 2.8. Therefore, when mechanical metal connectors are included at
the end of the timber member, intuitively it makes sense that the charring of the member
at that location would increases since the thermal conductivity of the end of the member
has increased.
The influence of metal connectors transferring heat into the timber member has been
studied by number of researchers. Thus, a number of researchers argue that the charring
rate at a connection should be increased to 0.81-0.88mm/min from the 0.7mm/min value
from EC5 for a glulam [14] [2]. However, it should be noted that those values are based
on mechanical steel connectors.
In order to comprehend and predict the structural performance of the connector in fire, or
which failure modes occur first a deep understanding of how the char layer and strength
properties of the materials are influenced by fire is necessary. This is a challenging and
complex matter due to the multiple number of failure modes, in addition to the change of
properties of several parameters when exposed to fire. The following section, 2.7, is a

2.7

2.7.2
Additional fire protection and detailing of timber connections
As previously mention the possible additional fire protection of connection are protec-
tion with fire-rated board systems or protection using timber as an insulator. The latter
one being more appealing to architects, but require a higher attention to detailing of the
connection. In chapter 8.7.2 in Fire Safe Use of Wood in Buildings[4] the importance of
connection detailing is addressed. For example by sealing tolerance gaps to prevent hot
gases to flow through the connection.
The fire sealants used to seal construction joints are available from multiple suppliers (for
example 3M, Temat, Rothoblaas, Promat, Hilti). Most, but not all fire sealants intumesce10
under heating. The point at which the sealant intumescents is referred to as the activation
temperature and varies between products. The activation temperature is in some cases
found in the technical sheet for the specific sealant.
Currently, there is no guidance or regulation on how these sealants should be used to
close tolerance gaps between the timber interfaces. However, the common practise11 is
to install the fire sealant in the timber factory and deliver the timber parts on-site with the
sealants already in place. The main reason is that on-site installation are more likely to
be inaccurate. Additionally, applying the fire seal on-site is more time consuming, thus
slows down the construction time.
The most applicable fire sealants for factory installation are fire stripes, typically ranging
in thickness from 0.25mm to 2mm but are 10mm to 50mm wide. Upon activation most of
these strips intumescent approximately 10 times their thickness 12. While the temperature

2.8
Fire development
The fire development is often divided into two periods, pre-flashover and post-flashover.
Prior to flashover there is sufficient oxygen in the system and the fire is said to be fuel
controlled. If a fire grows to flashover (i.e. reaches the steady state) the fire is called
a fully developed fire. Generally, the fire development is very different and a simplified
approach is to describe it using a simple design fire as shown in Figure 2.15 [17].
Figure 2.15: fire development [17].

a growth phase, a steady phase and a decay phase as a function of energy release rate

about the intensity at which the fire releases energy. The growth phase is defined using

Procedures
Prior to turning on the furnace it was ensured that no debris4 was in the furnace or on the
concrete interface around the heat exposure zone. Then the furnace was placed under
an industrial vacuum system5 for smoke control.
The furnace was pre-heated to 650°C for approximately 30 minutes without the sepa-
ration plate. After the pre-heating the separation plate was placed back and the top of
the chamber was cooled down to less than 40°C as per ISO 834 test requirements. A
fan was used to speed up this process. Once the furnace was sufficiently cooled to less
than 40°C the test specimen was placed on top of the furnace and the separation plate
removed again. The opening of the separation plate was then closed with non-flammable
insulation as shown in Figure 3.15.
Figure 3.15: shows the industrial vaccum hood and the insulation in the opening for the separation plate.

the pressure at 20Pa.

the edge of the furnace, to measure the oxygen concentration. The oxygen concentration
inside the furnace was only recorded manually every five minutes in test 2 (sample A)
using an emission analyser from Servomax.

5
Discussion
This chapter discusses the key findings from both phases of the project. In both phases,
an aluminum connection with a timber cover distance of 60mm and an edge distance of
53mm was exposed to ISO834 standard fire. In phase one, two different tolerance gaps,
1mm and 6mm, were tested and compared. In phase two, two fire stripe cases were
tested in a 6mm tolerance gap. Both cases included a 20mm intumescent fire stripe from
Rothoblaas with an edge distance of 30mm from all edges of the beam. The temperature
of the aluminum bracket was measured throughout the fire test and compared for all four
cases. Furthermore, the corresponding reduced capacity of the aluminum bracket in fire
was calculated using the reduction factor from EN 755-2 for aluminum alloy AW6005A T6.
The results in phase 1 showed a clear and consistent impact of the connection gap size
on the evolved temperatures of the aluminum brackets. For the larger 6mm gap, the
temperature of the bracket increased much faster, and reached 286,4 ± 35,6 °C after
80 minutes of exposure in all tests, at which time the bracket with a 1mm gap had only
reached 97,4 ± 0,8 °C as shown in Figure 4.5.
Based on the results presented in Figure 4.4 it is observed that the temperature of the alu-
minum bracket for the larger 6mm gap was on average 35-40°C higher than for the 1mm
gap until 45 minutes of fire exposure. After 45 minutes the heating rate of the aluminum
bracket for the 6mm gap changed from 0,99°C/min to 5,04°C/min.
The temperature-time curve for the aluminum brackets when an additional fire protec-
tion was used in the 6mm gap followed the same trajectory as the 6mm gap until forty
minutes had passed of ISO834 exposure, as shown in Figure 4.13. After forty minutes
had passed, cases I and II with additional fire protection deviated from the trajectory and
reached a maximum temperature of 160,5 °C and 169,2 °C, respectively after 77minutes
of fire exposure. The heating rate of the aluminum brackets for case I and case II were
1,3 °C/min and 1,34 °C/min respectively.
The time at which the fire stripe had closed the 6mm tolerance gap, thus happened prior
to forty minutes of fire exposure. Thermocouples L1, R1, B1 and T1 in phase two were
placed within the parameter of the fire stripe to compare the gas temperature within the
fire stripe to the gas temperature outside the parameter to thermocouples L2, R2, B2, and
T2. Figures 4.14 and 4.15 show the difference in gas temperature of thermocouples L1,
L2, R1 and R2. After ten minutes the gas temperature measured by L1 and R1 started
to decrease as observed in the Figures 4.14 and 4.15. Thus, it is estimated that the fire
stripe had closed the gap on the right and left side of the beam after approximately 10
minutes. When B1 and T1 are compared to B2 and T2 in Figures B.3 and B.4 the gas
temperature within the parameter of the fire stripe does not start to decrease until after
15-20 minutes. More tests need to be conducted to investigate when the fire stripe has
closed the gap completely, but from these results, it is suspected that the fire stripe had
closed the gap on all sides after approximately 20 minutes.
The fire stripe did prevent the wood behind the aluminum brackets from charring which
is observed in Figures 4.26 compared to when no additional fire protection occupied the
6mm gap as shown in Figure 4.9b and 4.10b. Charring behind the bracket influences
the embedment strength of the connector, which for steel connectors is the pre-dominant
failure mode during fire [15].

6
Conclusion
The objective of this master thesis was to investigate and compare the effect of 1 mm and
6 mm tolerance gaps on aluminum brackets in a wood joint, during fire. Some convection
of heat was expected to pass through the joint when the gaps are introduced, but the
effect was unknown. The aim was to determine if additional fire protection measure for
the 6mm gap was needed.
The results showed a clear and consistent impact of the connection tolerance gap size
on the evolved temperature of the aluminum brackets. The temperature of the aluminum
bracket for the larger 6mm gap was on average 35-40°C higher than for the 1mm gap until
45 minutes of fire exposure. After 45 minutes the heating rate of the aluminum bracket
for the 6mm gap changed from 0,99°C/min to 5,04°C/min. Thus, the temperature of the
bracket reached 286,4 ± 35,6°C after 80 minutes on of exposure in all tests for the 6mm
gap without additional fire protection, at which time the bracket with a 1 mm gap had only
reached 97,4 ± 0,8°C.
By introducing an additional fire measure in the 6 mm tolerance gap, the temperature of
the aluminum brackets did reduce from the case of a 6mm gap without a fire stripe. How-
ever, the impact of the additional fire stripe on the temperature of the aluminum brackets
was not detected until 40 minutes had passed. The temperature of the aluminum brackets
after 60 minutes was 118,9°C for case I and 119,9°C for case II, which is a reduction of
42°C from the 6mm case without a fire stripe (161,2°C). Therefore, it was concluded that
adding a fire stripe in the 6mm tolerance gap did not yield any additional benefits for the
aluminum bracket until after 40 minutes of fire exposure.
6.1
Future Work
To further investigate the fire resistance of the aluminum bracket with a 6mm tolerance
gap the mechanical behaviour of the connector needs to be assessed. Therefore, the
project could be extended to phase 3 where the specimen is exposed to standard fire
and subjected to a constant lateral load until failure. Figure 6.1 shows a scheme of the
suggested future work where the fire resistance of the joint could be compared for the case
without or with a 20mm wide and 1mm thick fire stripe with a cover distance of 30mm.
Furthermore, the experimental work could be compared to or used for computational fi-
nite element work. For example modelling the joint and comparing it to the experimental
results or using the results for the 1mm and 6mm gaps to predict the temperature of the
aluminum for tolerance gaps between 1mm and 6mm.
Another interesting study is to investigate the effect of aging on the fire stripes or even the
effect of water on the activation temperature of intumescent.

•	 Screws with the same length must be used in all holes, separately for each 
side of the connector. It is possible to use screws of different length in the 
two connectors, main element side and secondary beam side.
•	 The connector must always be fully fastened using all the holes.
•	 The pre-drill is not required for screws on main or secondary beam, with 
characteristic density ρk≤420 kg/m3. The pre-drill is mandatory on main or 
secondary beam with characteristic density ρk> 420 kg/m3.
•	 For screws on column, pre-drilling is always mandatory.
•	 For the LOCKTFLOOR135 connector installed on CLT panels no pre-drilling 
hole is required.

APPLICATION

OTHER MOTOR VEHICLES
  
Clean as recommended above and apply a 1 to 3 mm 
thick layer around the hole. Cut a steel sheet rein-
forced from a suitable material, e.g. a tin can, and 
fasten this with exhaust clamps or similar. Make sure 
the reinforcement is tight and filled with the wet 
product. Let dry for a couple of hours. When starting 
the engine, let it idle for some minutes and drive slow 
and carefully the first hour. A slight smell can occur 
during the first heat up.

reinforcement for smaller damages.
