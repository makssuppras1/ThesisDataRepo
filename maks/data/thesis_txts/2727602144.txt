Abstract
This thesis is part of the ReHyb project. The thesis focuses on detecting cognitive stress,
fatigue and emotional states using HR, EDA and facial sEMG signals. The aim has been
to evaluate the Empatica E4 and emteqPRO wearables for recording physiological signals
and create stress, fatigue and emotion machine learning models, that can later be inte-
grated into the Human Digital Twin in the ReHyb project. Datasets from previous research
projects as well as recorded signals during our own experiments have been used. The re-
sults show that it is possible to predict cognitive stress, fatigue and emotional states using
the mentioned signals and sensors, and that the sEMG and EDA signals are of good qual-
ity. The recorded HR signals from the Empatica E4 were of dubious quality. Especially
the emotion classifiers performed very well, with most models achieving between 80 and
90% accuracy. There were some difficulties related to measuring cognitive fatigue during
the experiments, however, when combining the experiment data with datasets from previ-
ous research, the fatigue models achieved up to 76-80% accuracy. The varied results of
merging datasets from different research projects underlines the challenges of acquiring
enough data for achieving generalizable models within cognitive state detection. Lastly,
incremental learning has successfully been used for updating models to perform better
on unseen data from a new experiment setting.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Stroke is the second most common cause of death in Europe and a leading cause of
disability in adults. Stroke affects approximately 1.1 million citizens and causes 440 000
deaths each year in Europe [1]. A stroke occurs when the blood supply to the brain is
restricted or stopped, leading to brain cell death (necrosis). The majority of strokes, 85%,
are ischaemic, which means that they are caused by a blood clot that blocks the flow of
blood to the brain. The remaining stroke incidents are caused by a burst in a blood vessel
that supplies the brain. The most important contributor to the risk of having a stroke is
age, and most stroke patients are above 65 years [2], but other variables such as genetics,
smoking and obesity are also risk factors. [3]
The proportion of older people in the European population is expected to increase in the
coming years, due to increasing life expectancy [4]. People over the age of 70 years
made up 17% of the European population in 2017 and will expectantly increase to 23%
in 2047. The number of stroke events is expected to rise by 3% by 2047, corresponding
to an additional 40 000 stroke incidents every year [1].
Patients experience a variety of physiological complications after suffering a stroke and
50% of the patients, who have had a stroke, acquire a disability that makes them depen-
dent on medical care or family members for activities for daily living (ADLs) [5]. Upper
limb impairment affects most patients during the stroke and causes difficulties for 50-75
% of patients for an extended time after the stroke. Therefore, stroke patients often have
to undergo rehabilitation to regain as much as possible of their lost abilities [6].
Technology can support physical therapy to make rehabilitation efforts more effective and
comfortable for the patient. One way to assist the patient is by using an exoskeleton, i.e.
a mechanical device that offers support to the muscles and joints of the body. Moreover,
recent research has explored the effect of another promising technology: a Human Dig-
ital Twin (HDT), which is a virtual representation of a human being [7] [8]. The HDT can
be used to predict mental states by collecting data such as pulse, sweat activity, blood
pressure and other information from the patient. By using an HDT, it will ideally be pos-
sible to gain insight into the cognitive state of the patient during rehabilitation sessions.
Thereby, rehabilitation exercises could be adjusted accordingly to suit each patient and
their challenges.
The European ReHyb1 project is an international project that aims to develop an exoskele-
ton and an HDT for assisting stroke patients. Specifically, the ReHyb project is focused on
creating an upper-limb exoskeleton, that will assist the patient as needed during games
used for physical rehabilitation, also known as serious-gaming. Together with electrical
stimulation and virtual and augmented reality, the exoskeleton will optimize training effi-
ciency and give the patient a comfortable experience with rehabilitation. [9]
This thesis is part of the ReHyb project. The thesis aims to investigate methods for de-
tecting cognitive stress, fatigue and emotional states using physiological sensor data, and
create machine learning models that can predict these cognitive and emotional states.
The goal is that these models can be integrated into the HDT at a later point in the ReHyb
project.

Many stroke patients report that they experience high levels of stress during their recovery
process, which can lead to less efficient rehabilitation outcomes [10]. Therefore, it is im-
portant to detect cognitive stress during stroke rehabilitation sessions. Furthermore, cog-
nitive fatigue is one of the most common symptoms after a stroke [11] [12], and can have
negative consequences for the patient’s rehabilitation [13]. Moreover, recognizing the
emotional state of the patient is important in physical rehabilitation. Several researchers
within the field of physiotherapy have stressed that it is significant that therapists have
good social skills and high levels of emotional intelligence [14] [15] [16]. Patients per-
ceive empathy as a personal characteristic of the physiotherapist that helps facilitate a
good therapeutic relationship [15]. Furthermore, the acknowledgment of the patient’s
emotions may result in positive treatment outcomes for the patient [14].
If the HDT can detect the cognitive and emotional states of the patient, the serious-games
should then adapt to the needs and abilities of the user. This could be done by changing
the difficulty level, offering words of encouragement, or suggesting the user takes a
break. In this way, the serious-game can keep the user motivated as well as properly
challenged during the entire gaming session.

2
Theory
This thesis aims to detect cognitive stress, cognitive fatigue and emotional states. There-
fore, a thorough understanding of these concepts, and how to measure them, is needed.
This theoretical chapter begins with a description of the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)
and then follows theories of cognitive stress, fatigue and emotions. Afterward, facial and
bodily measurements used for detecting these specific mental states are reviewed. The
last part of this chapter describes signal processing and machine learning methods rele-
vant to this thesis.
2.1
Autonomic Nervous System
The branch of the nervous system called the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) controls
the functions of the viscera, i.e. the internal organs in the body, such as the smooth
muscles in the intestines, the cardiac muscles and the glands. ANS also plays a role in
regulating other essential body functions such as the lung and skin functions. The ANS al-
lows the body to adapt to the environment by being sensitive to stressors and challenges.
It has also been found that ANS takes part in regulating the immune system response,
metabolism and inflammation. As the name suggests, the ANS operates largely involun-
tarily, with a few exceptions. The ANS can be divided into three different nervous systems:
the sympathetic (SNS), the parasympathetic (PNS) and the enteric (ENS). While the SNS
regulates the fight or flight response in emergencies, the PNS controls and maintains rest
and digest activities. The ENS is a neuron network within the gut. [17] [18]
2.2
What is cognitive stress, fatigue and emotions?
According to the Handbook of Neuroengineering [19], a cognitive state is the state of a
person’s cognitive processes. It reflects the processes that are related to many differ-
ent cognitions, such as attention, evaluation, reasoning, memory, problem-solving and
comprehension. In the following sections, cognitive stress and fatigue are described. Af-
terwards, theories related to emotions are presented.
2.2.1
Cognitive load and stress
According to Paas et al. [20], cognitive load represents the load imposed on the cog-
nitive system of a subject when performing a task. The cognitive load of a particular
task depends on the characteristics of the task, e.g. time pressure, novelty, rewards and
the environment. Novel tasks, tasks carried out under time pressure or tasks performed
in uncomfortable physical environments may increase cognitive load. Furthermore, the
characteristics of the subject, such as his/her cognitive skills, can also affect the cognitive
load level [20].
According to the Appraisal theory by Lazurus et al. [21], cognitive stress can occur when
the cognitive load of a task reaches a certain level and the individual believes that the re-
quirements of the task exceeds his or her abilities. In general, the events and conditions
that trigger stress are called stressors, which can be either physical, social or emotional
[22]. The stressors that are most relevant for this project are the mental or task-related
stressors that can be caused by high task demands compared to the individual’s capabil-
ities as well as conflicting or changing task instructions.
The state of stress can be described by the stress response that refers to the bodily
changes induced by events and conditions in the environment [22]. When a person expe-
riences stress, the ANS is activated along with the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA)

leads to increased heart rate, breath rate, sweat gland activity and muscle activity [25].

Stress can be categorized as either acute or chronic, depending on the duration and cop-
ing strategies of the individual. Acute stress has a limited time frame and is often caused
by a change in the environment. On the other hand, chronic stress can last considerably
longer and is not caused by a sudden stressor in the environment. Chronic stress can be
caused by repeated periods of acute stress over a long period and is very damaging to
mental and physical health [21]. Acute stress can also have unfortunate consequences,
such as an impaired ability to learn and remember, depending on the intensity of the
stressful episode [26]. This project focuses on detecting acute stress.
2.2.2
Cognitive fatigue
Cognitive fatigue can be defined as a decrease in cognitive resources due to sustained
cognitive demands independently of sleepiness [27]. Cognitive fatigue can be classified
as either acute or chronic, just like stress. Acute fatigue generally disappears when the
subject takes a rest or if the underlying illness is treated, which is in contrast to general
sleepiness that can only be relieved by sleep. Chronic fatigue is often defined as fatigue
that lasts longer than 6 months and does not disappear after rest. [28][29] [30]
Some diseases and disabilities increase the likelihood that the patient will experience
fatigue. For instance, cognitive fatigue is a common symptom for stroke patients [31].
There are several theories regarding the causes of cognitive fatigue. Some researchers
highlight that emotions such as anxiety as well as utilizing the working memory plays a vital
role in causing cognitive fatigue [32]. Other researchers underline that a prolonged state

of stress can lead to a malfunctioning regulation of the stress response, which causes
cognitive fatigue [27]. Some researchers have found that a long involvement in a cognitive
task can cause cognitive fatigue, without a stress component [33] [34]. This is also known
as Time on Task (ToT) effects [34]. Other researchers have found that cognitive load
induced by time pressure is responsible for the onset of cognitive fatigue [28].
Cognitive fatigue induced by a prolonged cognitive load is associated with decreased ac-
tivity in the PNS and increased relative SNS activity in adults [35]. This phenomenon is
true for both acute and chronic fatigue [30]. Similarly to stress, sustained cognitive fatigue
is associated with serious health problems, both mental and physical [36]. Cognitive fa-
tigue also leads to less efficient performance, a higher error rate and ultimately inability
to do the task at hand [19]. Cognitive fatigue also causes increasing reaction times [37].
For these reasons, it is important to prevent prolonged instances of cognitive fatigue.
2.2.3
Emotion
There is no consensus on an exact definition of emotions, but the influential psychologist
Paul Ekman has defined emotions as a set of psychological changes and emotional be-
haviors that automatically arise when a person senses that something important is hap-
pening to the state of his/her welfare [38]. Emotions can also be defined as complex
behavioral schemes that are characterized by an activation of the Autonomous Nervous
System (ANS) and by particular types of subjective experiences [39].
This project intents to detect if the user is experiencing either positive, negative or neutral
emotions. Positive and negative emotions have different effects on the body. Positive
emotions, and an overall sense of well-being, are generally associated with a decreased
risk of mortality and better cardiovascular health [40] [41], while negative emotions are
linked to worse self-reported physical health, such as higher degrees of chronic conditions
like diabetes [42] [43]. Several studies have shown that emotions affect learning and
memory [44] [45] [46]. For instance, events that induce an emotional response are more
likely to be remembered clearly over time [46].
Two different models are often used to differentiate between emotions: the basic emotions
theory and the valence-arousal model. The basic emotions theory dates back to Darwin
and states that all humans have a limited set of emotions and that every emotional ex-
pression is a combination of these basic emotions [47]. In more recent times, Ekman et al.
have proposed that these basic emotions stem from different neural pathways and have
different physiological expressions [48]. The majority of contemporary scientists that en-
dorse the theory of the basic emotions agree that there is empirical support for five basic
emotions: anger, fear, happiness, sadness, and disgust [49]. The basic emotions theory
is closely related to the method for measuring facial movements called FACS, which is
described in section 2.3.2.
The other commonly applied method to describe cognitive states and emotions is a model
with two dimensions: valence and arousal. The valence dimension relates to the pleas-
antness of emotion and is a continuum that spans from unpleasant to pleasant. The
arousal dimension describes the level of mobilization or activation and ranges from acti-
vated/excited to deactivated/calm [50] [51]. Figure 2.2 shows an example of how different
emotions can be placed inside a two-dimensional model. For instance, the state of being
relaxed is located towards deactivation and is closer to pleasant than unpleasant. Since
the valence and arousal model is continuous along both axes, many more emotions than
those depicted in figure 2.2 can be added along the perimeter of the circle.
Since the two dimensions are continuous, certain emotions or cognitive states overlap in

2.3
Facial expression measurements
This section describes methods that are often used to measure cognitive states and emo-
tions from facial expressions. First, the facial muscles that are involved in facial expres-
sion and the influential Facial Action Coding System (FACS) are introduced. The last
section reviews the theory of Electromyography (EMG).
2.3.1
Muscles of facial expression
There are over twenty individual muscles on both sides of the human face, linking the skull
bones to the skin of the face. Facial muscles can be divided into two different groups,
according to their primary functions: muscles used for facial expressions and those used
mastication, i.e. chewing food [54]. The muscles controlling facial expression include the
occipitofrontalis muscle which raises the eyebrows, the orbicularis oculi, which control
the eyelids, the zygomaticus major and minor muscles that pull the mouth upward and
outward, the corrugator supercilii which forms vertical lines between the eyebrows as well
as other muscles for controlling the mouth, nose and other parts of the face [55]. Figure
2.3 show the placement and names of many of the facial muscles.
The zygomaticus major muscle is often found to be correlated with positive valence, due
to its role in smiling, while the corrugator muscle plays an important role in frowning and is
therefore connected to negative valence [56]. There is a substantial difference between
spontaneous and posed expressions of emotions. For instance, the facial muscle con-
tractions peak simultaneously for a spontaneous expression, whereas this is not the case
for posed facial expressions [57].

2.3.2
Facial Action Coding System
In the late 1970s Ekman et al. developed a technique for measuring facial behavior called
the Facial Action Coding System (FACS) [59]. An essential part of FACS are the 46
different action units (AU) that each corresponds to a specific muscle configuration of the
face. Each action unit is identified by a number, an action name and the combinations
of muscles that cause the specific action [60]. Using FACS, any complex facial behavior
can be decomposed into a combination of action units by a person trained to do so [61].
FACS was intended to be a method for distinguishing between all visible movements of
the face [61]. The visibility constraint was introduced since Ekman et al. were primarily
interested in facial behaviors that could be detected by other people and, therefore, could
have social consequences. As a consequence of the movement constraint, changes in
muscle activity or tension, which do not lead to visible movements, are not measured [61].
Appendix D shows the name and physical appearances of seven frequently used action
units, along with further details of FACS.
2.3.3
Electromyography
Electromyography (EMG) is a method for measuring the electrical activity of muscles. It
can, for instance, be used for detecting muscle fatigue, diagnosing neuromuscular disor-
ders and controlling hand prosthetics [62] [63] [64]. Facial EMG is the measurement of
the activity of facial muscles and it can be used to detect cognitive states. In this project,
EMG is used in the experiments, and therefore the theory behind it is described in some
detail.
Neurons
The brain and the nervous system are made up of a type of cell called neurons. Neurons
send and receive signals that allow us to interact with the external world, including moving

==PAGE:20==
muscles, feeling heat and cold and making memories [65]. The brain is comprised of
numerous different types of neurons and these have not all been classified according to
their functions yet. However, the spinal cord has three types of neurons: the sensory
neurons, the motor neurons and the interneurons. The motor neurons control all muscle
movements. There are two kinds of motor neurons; the upper motor neurons that transmit
signals between the brain and spinal cord and the lower motor neurons that transmit
signals from the spinal cord to the muscles [65].
A neuron consists of dendrites, a cell body (or soma) and an axon. The dendrites receive
inputs from other neurons. The cell body contains the cell nucleus and the axon is a
long structure that creates and transmits electrical messages. These electrical messages
are called action potentials and they travel along the axon and are then converted into
chemical signals at the end of the axon. In this way, a neuron can communicate with
other neurons or cells, e.g. muscle fibers [66].

Motor units and muscle contractions
A motor unit, as defined by Sherrington [68] in 1925, is the basic unit of muscle contraction.
It consists of a single motor neuron and all the muscle fibers which it innervates, i.e.
supplies nerves to, as is shown in figure 2.4. There are different numbers of motor units
in each muscle, for instance, the platysma muscle in the neck has around 1000 motor
units while the first dorsal interosseus muscle in the hand has around 100 motor units
[69]. Similarly, the number of muscle fibers within a motor unit varies depending on the
muscle.
The muscle fibers that belong to the same motor unit always contract together [70]. In
more detail, a muscle contraction occurs when a motor neuron transmits an action po-
tential through its axon to the neuromuscular junction. Here, the neurotransmitter acetyl-
choline is released and binds to receptors on the muscle fiber. This causes an action
potential to propagate in both directions in the muscle fiber. The action potential in turn
initiates the muscle contraction [71]. A very small muscular activity can recruit only a sin-
gle motor unit. When a person makes a stronger muscle contraction, more motor units
are recruited [72].

How does EMG work
Electromyographic measurements give information about the activation of muscle fibers
by the motor neurons. When an action potential travels through the muscle fibers in the
process of muscle contraction, an electrical charge is emitted. This charge is measured
with electromyography. The amplitude of the EMG signal increases with the number of
muscle fibers used for the specific contraction. Depending on where the recording elec-
trodes are positioned, the EMG can be composed of recordings originating from a single
motor unit or, typically, from many different motor units [73]. Figure 2.5 shows how a
single EMG signal can consist of recordings stemming from different motor unit action
potentials.
Figure 2.5: The raw EMG signal can be decomposed into signals stemming from individual motor
unit action potentials. From [74].
Surface vs intramuscular EMG
EMG can be recorded using electrodes that are either positioned inside the muscle, also
called intramuscular EMG, or on the surface of the skin, which is called surface EMG
(sEMG). Surface EMG is easier to use than intramuscular EMG, however, surface EMG
is primarily used for measuring superficial muscle activity and the signals are obfuscated
by the thickness of the tissue underneath the skin (subcutaneous tissue). Furthermore,
the electrodes may pick up unwanted signals from other muscles close to the one being
monitored, which is called cross-talk. Using intramuscular EMG minimizes cross-talk.
The disadvantages of intramuscular EMG is the invasive nature of the procedure and that
it requires a trained professional to insert the needles [75]. In this thesis, surface EMG is
used.
EMG signal filtering
Farfán et al. [76] states: ”Raw EMG offers valuable information in a particularly useless
form.”. In other words, the information in EMG signals is only useful if it can be properly
processed and quantified [76]. Raw sEMG signals of the facial muscles can be contami-
nated by the movement of the subject, also called motion artifacts, as well as eye blinks,
brain activity, salivary gland activity and other effects stemming from facial activity. Fig-
ure 2.6 shows an example of a power spectrum for an EMG signal, that has an artifact.
A power spectrum is a representation of the distribution of signal frequency components
[77].
EMG artifacts are usually limited to the low-frequency region of the EMG power spec-
trum, i.e. in the 0–20 Hz region, and they can be removed by applying a high-pass filter.
However, the EMG power spectrum of the facial muscles has relatively large components

measured signals. Due to these extrinsic and intrinsic factors, it is difficult to interpret the

The emteqPRO mask
The emteqPRO mask [84] is used in this thesis for recording facial surface EMG. It is pic-
tured in figure 2.7. The emteqPRO device has seven facial stainless steel EMG electrodes
that do not require adhesives or electrode conductive gel. One electrode is placed on the
corrugator muscle and the remaining electrodes are placed on the left and right frontalis,
orbicularis and zygomaticus muscles. The emteqPRO mask also records heart-related
signals such as mean heart rate (HR) and inter-beat-intervals (IBI) every 10 seconds.
The emteqPRO also outputs estimated valence and arousal levels. The output contains
two different measures of valence, one variable called Valence, which can be either -1

of arousal: a categorical Arousal feature (values: -1 (low), 0 (medium), or 1 (high)), as
well as the continuous variable FacialActivation.
2.4
Body measurements
Barrett et al. [86] and Mullins et al. [87] have underlined that facial behavior alone is in-
sufficient to infer cognitive states and emotions, and must be complemented with obser-
vations of physiological signals from the body. This section describes some commonly
used body measurements for cognitive and emotional detection. Particular attention is
given to the methods used in this thesis, namely Electrodermal Activity (EDA) and heart
rate (HR).
2.4.1
Electroencephalography
Electroencephalography (EEG) is a noninvasive method for recording the brain’s electrical
activity [88]. EEG signals are recorded by placing multiple electrodes on the scalp and
measuring the differences in potential between two of these electrodes. EEG can either
be carried out by measuring the potential difference between each electrode on the scalp
and a reference electrode, or by recording the potential difference between two electrodes
on the scalp. These differences in potentials stem from changes in the flow of electrical
currents caused by the processing of information in the brain. [89]

Measurement
For the past many years, Electrocardiogram (ECG) has been the primary technique for
measuring heart rate in medical settings [96] [97] and has also often been used within
stress and fatigue detection studies [94] [95]. An ECG is a recording of the change in the
heart’s electrical activity as action potentials move through the heart during each heart-
beat cycle (cardiac cycle) [98]. For recording ECG, at least three electrodes are placed
in different locations on the body [99]. Another, newer method for measuring heart ac-
tivity is Photoplethysmography (PPG). A typical PPG device consists of a light source
and a photodetector. The light source emits light towards to skin and the photodetector
then measures the reflected light, which is proportional to variations in blood volume [99].
Wearables for detecting heart rate with PPG often provide the Blood Volume Pressure
(BVP) signal, from which both the HR and IBI signals can be extracted.

Heart rate recordings often include artifacts, i.e. changes in the signal that do not stem
from the signal source [100]. When recording heart rate via a wrist-worn wearable, the
IBI signals are susceptible to motion artifacts [101]. Such artifacts cause inaccurate or
missing beat detection, leading to poor data quality. It is important to handle missing data
before extracting features. This can, for instance, be done by estimating the values of
the missing beats, or by discarding incomplete time windows [92]. The python package

Feature Extraction
Clinicians and researchers extract frequency-domain, time-domain and non-linear HRV
features from IBI signals. The power spectrum of a signal is very relevant for understand-
ing frequency-domain features. Figure 2.8 shows two different power spectrums for HR
signals, one in which the subject is in a normal state (left) and one where the person is
meditating (right). The normal state power spectrum decreases with frequency, while the
meditation power spectrum has large peaks around 0.1 Hz. This is an example of how a
person’s mental state can affect the power distribution in the different frequency bands.
Usually, frequency-domain HRV features estimate the distribution of signal power in four
specific frequency bands: ultra-low-frequency below 0.003 Hz (ULF), very-low-frequency
between 0.003–0.04 Hz (VLF), low-frequency between 0.04–0.15 Hz (LF), and high-
frequency between 0.15–0.40 Hz (HF). The ratio between the low and high-frequency
power (LF/HF) may be an estimate of the ratio between the sympathetic and parasympa-
thetic nervous systems [91].

Time-domain features quantify the variability in measurements of the IBI. Examples are
the standard deviation of the IBI signal (SDNN) and the difference between the highest
and lowest heart rates during a fixed interval. Time-domain features are strongly affected
by the recording length, e.g. a 24 hour measurement of SDNN can predict heart attack
risk, while a 5 min recording cannot. Finally, non-linear HRV features measure the com-

Approximate Entropy (ApEn), the correlation dimension (D2) and the short and long-term
detrended fluctuation analysis measures (DFA1 and DFA2). From the heart rate itself, sta-

2.4.3
Electrodermal Activity
Electrodermal Activity (EDA) is a term used to describe electrical phenomena in the skin
and it is strongly correlated to sweat gland activity [103]. EDA is a measure of the brain’s
influence on the body and is thus a biomarker of cognitive and emotional states. EDA is
particularly useful for measuring stress, anxiety and fear since these states cause sweat-
ing, sometimes called psychological or emotional sweating [104]. EDA is also used as a
marker of various mental disorders like depression and schizophrenia [22][105]. In this
thesis, EDA measurements will be used to detect cognitive stress.
Sweat glands
Humans have approximately 2-4 million sweat glands that can be found on hairy and non-
hairy skin. The sweat glands are unevenly distributed across the skin surface, with some
areas having higher sweat gland density than others [106]. Sweat glands are categorized
as either apocrine or eccrine. Eccrine sweat glands are the most common ones and have
a high density in the palms, soles and forehead. These sweat glands serve as the primary
temperature regulation in humans and they are also innervated by the SNS [107]. The
control of the sweat gland activity by the SNS is also known as the sudomotor function.
EDA signals represent electrical phenomena in the skin that stem from activity in the
eccrine sweat glands, and hence indirectly from the SNS [108]. By applying a current
or voltage to the skin, it is possible to detect fluctuations in either the conductance or
resistance that stem from the amount of sweat and the sweat conductivity [109].
Measurement
Two different approaches can be used for recording EDA signals: the endosomatic ap-
proach, which measures potential differences that originate from the skin (SP), and the
exosomatic approach that measures changes in an externally applied current or voltage.
If the exosomatic approach is applied, it is most common to use direct current (DC) with
either constant voltage or constant current. With constant voltage, one can measure the
Skin Conductance Level (SCL) or Response (SCR), which are measures for change in
conductance. Using a constant current, it is possible to measure the Skin Resistance
Level (SRL) or Response (SRR), where changes in resistance are measured [109]. The
term Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) is sometimes used instead of SCR.
EDA signals can be decomposed into tonic and phasic measures.
The tonic aspect
records fluctuations in EDA over longer periods, as well as spontaneous changes in EDA
stemming from internal thoughts and emotional states, as opposed to external stimuli.
The SCL and SRL are tonic measures and they usually reside in the frequency area of 0
to 0.05 Hz [109]. On the other hand, SCR and SRR are phasic measures that correspond
to short-lived external and identifiable stimuli and the frequency typically varies between
0.05 and 1 Hz [110]. Figure 2.9 shows the difference between phasic and tonic EDA
measures.

Signal processing and feature extraction
EDA signals can be affected by artifacts caused by the recording procedure or by physi-
ological signals stemming from outside of the electrodermal system [113]. For instance,
EDA is sensitive to temperature and electrode placement [108]. In his book on Electroder-
mal Activity, Boucsein [100] states that EDA artifacts should be detected by doing a visual
inspection of the signal data. However, this is very time-consuming for large amounts of
data. An alternative approach for reducing the impact of artifacts is low-pass filtering,
where only the frequencies below a certain threshold are kept [112] [113]. The low-pass
filter is often set to 0.5 Hz [92]. A band-pass filter, i.e. applying both a high-pass and a
low-pass filter, is often used in studies that focus only on the SCR component, since the
band-pass filter both removes artifacts, but also the SCL component. The high-pass filter
is usually set around 0.05 Hz [110].
Both time- and frequency domain features can be extracted from the SCR and SCL com-
ponents. Previous research has found that time-domain features best describe the SCR
and SCL signals recorded from wearable devices [114] [115]. These features include the
number of SCR peaks and the maximum, standard deviation and mean values of SCL
and SCR [110]. Frequency domain features, such as the skewness or kurtosis of the fre-
quency domain signals, provide information about the transient behavior of the sudomotor
activity [92]. Transient behavior means any sudden change from a steady state that dies
out over time [116].

2.5
Machine Learning
Machine learning is an automated method of data analysis. An important difference be-
tween machine learning and data analysis is that machine learning is data-driven, and
works without prior knowledge of underlying patterns in the data, while conventional data
analysis and statistics are model-driven [122]. Machine learning methods detect patterns
in data and subsequently use these to make predictions regarding new, unseen data.
Usually, the dataset used for learning patterns is called the training set and a dataset
called the test set is used for estimating how well the model makes predictions for new
data. Machine learning is usually divided into three types; supervised, unsupervised and
reinforcement learning.
Supervised learning is the most widely used approach and the goal is to learn a mapping
between input and output variables, given labeled input-output pairs. The input consists
of a design matrix, X, with dimensions N times D, where N is the number of input-output
examples, or observations, and D is the number of features in each observation. A fea-
ture is for instance some numerical property that can be measured, such as the height,
weight or blood pressure of a person. The output variable is also called the response or
target variable. The true value of the target variable is called the label, however ’target’
and ’label’ will be used interchangeably in this thesis. When the target variable is dis-
crete, the machine learning problem is called classification and when the target variable
is continuous, it is called regression. In unsupervised learning, only the input variables
are given, and the aim is to discover useful or interesting patterns in the data. Reinforce-
ment learning is an interactive method, where the algorithm is provided with rewards to
encourage correct behavior or punish wrong ones.[123]

K-Nearest Neighbours
The K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) classifier is a simple model. For each test input x, the
algorithm finds the K closest points in the training set and then counts how many of these
K points belong in each class. Then, the algorithm predicts that x belongs to the mode,
i.e. the class that most of the K neighbor points belong to. There are many functions
for calculating the distance between points, but the most widely used is the Euclidean
distance. In general, the KNN classifier can achieve good results, but it does not work
well for high-dimensional data, i.e. data where there are many features compared to
observations [123]. Figure 2.12 (a) illustrates how the KNN classifier works.
Random Forest
A Classification and Regression Tree (CART) - or decision tree - consists of a root node,
branches, internal nodes and leaf nodes. The model attempts to predict the target value
of a given observation by learning decision rules derived from the data features. In the
case of classification, the training data is split into two at the root node, according to the

first feature and threshold value that best divides the datasets into distinct classes. The
data is split again in the same manner, at every subsequent internal node. The final tree
has leaf nodes at the bottom, that decide the final classes. [123] [128]
Figure 2.13 shows an example of a CART model. Using a single CART model is unstable,
meaning that small changes in the input data can lead to very different predictions. A
Random Forest (RF) consists of a group of CARTs, each trained on a random subset of
the original features and a random subset of the observations. The final prediction for
each test input is then made according to majority voting among the trees. This leads to
better performance and lower result variance [123]. This approach of combining several
simple models is called an ensemble technique [129].

eXtreme Gradient Boosting
Boosting refers to a way of sequentially adding new models, also called base-learners,
to an ensemble. In every iteration, a new base-learner is trained to mitigate the error
of the entire ensemble so far, and this model is then added to the ensemble. Figure
2.14 illustrates how every new base-learner is dependent on the performance of all its
predecessors. Each new base-learner can be created using so-called gradient boosting.
Gradient boosting works by constructing new base-learners by using a gradient descent
optimization algorithm. This entails that each new base-learner is created to be maximally
correlated with the negative gradient of the loss function. eXtreme Gradient Boosting
(XGB) is a gradient boosting algorithm that has proved to be faster than other gradient
boosting methods. The success of the XGB algorithm is partly due to the implementation
of parallel computing, which is a type of computing where many calculations are carried
out simultaneously. [129] [131]
Histogram-based Gradient Boosting Classifier
Like XGB, a Histogram-based Gradient Boosting Classifier (HGBC) is an ensemble model
that uses gradient boosting. The HGBC algorithm discretizes the values of the continuous
features used by the base-learners into smaller bins. Afterward, histograms that represent
the distribution of the feature values are created. Using these histograms, the algorithm
can quickly determine the optimal split points used for training the base learner. In this
manner, the algorithm does not need to scan the whole ranges of features, which reduces
the computation time. The histogram-based approach is also less susceptible to noise.
[132]

Support Vector Machines (SVM) is an algorithm that can be used for binary or multi-class
classification. Here, the version used for binary classification is described. The SVM

2.5.2
Imbalanced datasets
Class imbalance refers to the case where one or more of the classes in a classification
dataset are underrepresented. An imbalanced dataset can lead to worse model perfor-
mances, since many algorithms assume that the training data is balanced. This assump-
tion leads to worse results when classifying observations from the minority class compared
to the majority class [136]. The collected data can be modified to remedy the class imbal-
ance problem, either by generating more samples from the minority groups, which is also
called oversampling, or by removing observations from the majority groups, also called
undersampling [137].
When using oversampling, there is a risk that the model will overfit to the training data
due to repeated values stemming from the minority class[138]. Overfitting means that
the model has learned irrelevant noise from the training set and, thus, performs worse on

new, unseen data [134]. Undersampling is an alternative to oversampling, however, this
can cause a loss of potentially valuable information [138].
The Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique (SMOTE) is a common oversampling
method that was first presented by Chawla et al. [139]. SMOTE is also used in this
thesis. With SMOTE, new samples are generated by interpolation. First, the algorithm
picks a random sample,xi, and then finds the K nearest neighbor samples from the same
class. Out of these K neighbors, a single sample is randomly selected, xzi, and the new
sample, xnew, is calculated as follows:

2.5.3
Model evaluation
The most commonly used metrics for evaluating and comparing the performances of ma-
chine learning models are accuracy, precision, recall and the f1 score. The equations
for calculating them for a binary classification problem are shown in appendix J.2. The
accuracy score measures how many observations were correctly classified out of the to-
tal observations. The precision measures what proportion of positive classifications was
correct, while recall determines how many of the true positive observations were cor-
rectly classified. Finally, the f1 score is the harmonic mean of recall and precision. When
choosing an evaluation metric, the problem being modeled must be considered. [140]
2.5.4
Cross Validation
Cross-validation is a method for evaluating the performance of a model and compare
models with each other. The most common form is the K-fold cross-validation method,
that divides the entire dataset into K randomly created groups. In each of the K iterations,
one of these groups is used as the test set, while the remaining groups are used for
training the model. For each iteration, the model performance is calculated from a chosen
performance metric, and the final result is usually summarized by taking the mean of these
scores. [141]
2.5.5
Hyperparameter optimization
All machine learning systems have hyperparameters that control the learning process.
For instance, the number of trees is a hyperparameter for the RF algorithm. Optimizing
hyperparameters means finding the set of hyperparameters that leads to the best model,
usually in terms of a pre-defined loss function and given a specific dataset. In this thesis,
hyperparameter optimization is used for tuning the machine learning models, and there-
fore some commonly used hyperparameter optimization methods are described in the
following sections.
Grid and Random search
Grid search is a widely used method for finding the best set of hyperparameters. The
user specifies a set of values for each hyperparameter and the grid search algorithm tries
all combinations of these values. With grid search, the required number of evaluations
grows exponentially with every added hyperparameter. An alternative method is the so-
called random search, where the hyperparameters are sampled randomly from a specified
grid. Figure E.1 in the appendix illustrates grid and random search. The grid and random
search algorithms are relatively slow methods, especially when the dataset is large and
there are many hyperparameters. The Optuna optimization algorithm described below
outperforms these two approaches. [142]

Optuna
Optuna is a program for automatizing hyperparameter optimization. Optuna searches for
the optimal hyperparameter values by trial and error. In every trial, the algorithm takes
note of the results stemming from a set of hyperparameter values and then estimates a
new, more promising region in the hyperparameter space. [143]
In more detail, Optuna uses a Bayesian optimization algorithm called Tree-structured
Parzen Estimator (TPE). The first step of the TPE algorithm entails randomly sampling
hyperparameters to approximate which regions of the hyperparameter search space lead
to well-performing models. Then, the search space is split into a ”good” region, or distribu-
tion l(x), and a ”bad” distribution g(x), using a threshold between 0 and 1. If the threshold
is set to 0.15, then the l(x) distribution contains hyperparameter combinations that result
in models that perform in the top 15% of all the models created until now. The g(x) dis-
tribution contains the remaining hyperparameter combinations. In each of the following
iterations, the next best set of hyperparameters to try are the values that maximize the
fraction l(x)/g(x). This process is repeated for a set number of trials, as defined by the
user [144]. An illustration of how the Optuna algorithm works is shown in section 4.6.1.
2.5.6
Incremental learning
With incremental learning, a model can continuously learn from new data observations
and retain most of the knowledge it has already learned [145]. Incremental learning is an
alternative to training the model on the complete dataset whenever new observations ar-
rive, which reduces the computational time. The incremental approach can also favorably
be used if there are limited memory resources, since it removes the need for storing the
entire dataset [146].
Incremental learning approaches must be able to handle concept drift within the data [147]
[146]. Concept drift refers to the statistical changes of data over time, meaning a change
in the data distribution. A change in the input distribution is a type of concept drift called
covariate shift. When there are changes in the underlying function that maps input and
output variables, it is called real concept drift [146]. An example of a real concept drift is
an introduction of a new class similar to one of the original classes. Real concept drifts
negatively affect the model performance [146]. The prior-probability shift is another kind
of concept drift, namely a change in the distribution of classes. A prior-probability shift
can also affect the prediction performance if the change is significant [148].
2.5.7
Principal Component Analysis
The dimensionality of a dataset refers to the number of features in it. Dimensionality
reduction can be used for removing noisy or redundant features. Principal Component
Analysis (PCA) is a technique for reducing the dimensionality of a dataset, while retain-
ing as much of the variance, or information, from the original dataset as possible. PCA
works by creating new features that are linear combinations of the original features. Each
new feature, also called a principal component, successively maximizes the variance of
the dataset and is uncorrelated with the remaining principal components. PCA can also
be used for visualizing the data. When the features have been reduced, the data can
be plotted on a 2D plot using the first two principal components. This can be used for
gaining new insights into the dataset and inspecting how well the different classes can be
separated using the information in the data. [149] [150]

3.1
Stress Detection
Inducing stress
In a recent review on cognitive stress detection, Giannakakis et al. [22] mention various
methods for inducing stress, with some of the most common being the Stroop task, men-
tal arithmetic (i.e. solving math problems in the head) and tasks under time pressure. All
these tasks can be used in a controlled research setting. Other methods are less con-
venient, such as various driving tasks [22]. Another frequently used method is the Trier
Social Stress Test [151], where the participant is asked to pretend to be a job applicant
and present a speech to a group of people as if he/she were at a job interview. This task
is very reliable for inducing stress [152], however, it is also time and resource-consuming.
Signals and features
Healy et al. [114] and Wijsman et al. [153] used EMG measurements of the trapezius
muscle, i.e. a muscle in the upper back, for measuring stress. The disadvantage of
this method is that it requires positioning electrodes on the back of the participant, which
may hinder his/her movements. Recently, Vos et al. [24] have made a review of the
current state of stress detection using machine learning and data recorded from wearable
devices. They found that many stress-related studies include HR and EDA signals in
their machine learning prediction models. Healey et al. [114] have found that EDA and
HR features are most correlated with stress levels, among various other physiological
signals. In particular, two EDA components have been used to measure stress levels:
the amplitude of the SCRs and the absolute increase in the SCLs [109]. A study by Van
der Mee et al. [154] found that the SCR component was more strongly associated with
cognitive load and stress fluctuations when compared to the SCL component.
A commonly used way to measure cognitive load is by using wearable devices, such as
the Empatica E4 wristband, which primarily provides EDA and HR signals [155] [156].
The Empatica E4 is less intrusive compared to using a chest band or glove, which has
previously been used to measure EDA [157]. Furthermore, the Empatica E4 does not
cause much discomfort and does not hinder natural movement. Schuurmans et al. [158]
have found that the HR data from the E4 device was of good quality. Milstein et al. [118]
also found that the Empatica E4 gives heart rate data of good quality, however, they found
that the Empatica E4 does not always produce reliable EDA data.
Tran et al. [52] investigated the relationship between stress and valence and arousal
by analyzing annotated video clips. They found a clear negative correlation between
stress and valence, indicating that high stress levels are strongly connected to negative
emotions. They also found that stress is positively correlated with arousal. These two
relationships are shown in figure C.1 in the Appendix. A very similar conclusion was
reached in another study by Liapis et al. [53]. These findings suggest that stress might be
predicted by measuring valence and arousal levels, for instance with the facial emteqPRO
mask.
Establishing ground truth
There are not many different ways to establish the ground truth, of a person’s stress level,
i.e. the objectively true stress level [22], [155]. The self-assessment approach is often
used, where the individual is asked to fill out a questionnaire such as the NASA Task
Load Index (NASA-TLX) [159] for measuring workload or the Dundee Stress State Ques-
tionnaire [160]. Such questionnaires provide an insight into the subjective experience of
22
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
==PAGE:35==
stress, but they are also affected by measurement errors such as response bias. Re-
sponse bias refers to the tendency to respond in a way that is inaccurate or false, for
instance, to appear more favorably [22]. Another disadvantage of using questionnaires is
that they are applied after the stressful event has occurred, which might lead to a less ac-
curate stress assessment. Ground truth stress can also be established by using biomark-
ers that are thought to be very reliable for assessing the stress level, such as the cortisol
concentration in saliva. This method is however quite cumbersome.
3.2
Fatigue Detection
Inducing fatigue
In the recent research by Matuz et al. [93], they induced cognitive fatigue by exposing the
participants to cognitive loads over a long period. This is also called a Time-on-Task (ToT)
approach [28], as mentioned in section 2.2.2. The participants were exposed to three
fatigue-inducing activities over a total of 1.2-1.5 hours. Matuz et al. achieved good results
with this approach and found that the subjectively assessed fatigue level was significantly
higher after the experiment than before, for each of the three tasks. The disadvantage of
the ToT method is the long duration of the fatigue tasks, which sets a limit on the number
of test persons.
Another approach for fatigue induction is to manipulate the difficulty or complexity of the
tasks. For example, Borragán et al. [28] showed that by maintaining a higher cognitive
load, it is possible to induce mental fatigue in 16 min using a so-called TloadDback cog-
nitive test. The TloadDback is a type of task-switching game, where the participant must
continuously switch between two assignments. Recently, Kalanadhabhatta et al. [161]
exposed their participants to another task-switching game for 15 min. and they were
successful in inducing fatigue in their participants with this method. O’Keefe et al. [162]
made a review about fatigue induction methods and found that it is unnecessary to induce
fatigue for longer than 30 min using tasks with high cognitive loads.
Although most fatigue-inducing experiments consist of a single task, Matuz et al. [93]
recently used three tests, included a task-switching game, an N-back test and a version of
the Stroop test. The ideas behind the N-back and Stroop tests are described in appendix
H.3. Matuz et al. [93] theorized that their prediction models would generalize better, i.e.
perform well on unseen data, when they used data from three different fatigue-inducing
tasks instead of only one. They found that their models performed at a similar level as
previous studies that used only a single task for fatigue induction.
Signals and features
Tuncer et al. [163] and Monteiro et al. [164] have used EEG signals for training models
that can classify fatigue and non-fatigue states with high accuracy (between 80-90%).
Yet, the many electrodes of EEG are time-consuming to set up properly and they are
somewhat intrusive and inconvenient for physical activities [93][165]. EEG signals are
also prone to noise and motion artifacts [93].
An alternative to EEG signals is using heart rate variability (HRV) features, which are cor-
related to cognitive fatigue [166] [35]. Matuz et al. [93] used HRV features stemming from
ECG recordings. One of the disadvantages of using ECG is that, typically, at least three
electrodes must be placed at certain locations on the body, which limits the user’s mobility
[99]. Other studies have utilized PPG sensors that use simpler hardware implementations
and have lower costs than ECG. Furthermore, it is only necessary to place a single PPG
sensor on the body, usually either on the earlobe, fingertip or wrist [99]. Lee et al. [167]
found that while the ECG signals led to a slightly better fatigue classification performance
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
23
==PAGE:36==
in drivers, the PPG signal also performed relatively well and was a good and less intru-
sive alternative to ECG. Just like for stress detection, the Empatica E4 wristband and
other wearables that measure PPG signals have also been used for detecting cognitive
fatigue [95].
There is a consensus that HRV features calculated over longer periods of recordings
are more reliable and lead to better performances for fatigue detection [168] [93]. The
convention is to calculate HRV features for at least 5 min [169] but Matuz et al. [93] found
that their prediction models still performed relatively well (accuracy of 74.4%) when using
a shorter window of 2 min. This suggests that it can be considered to use smaller time
windows if it is required.
Li et al. [170] used wearable eye-tracking technology for measuring cognitive fatigue in
construction workers. Eye tracking and other eye measures such as pupil diameter are
sensitive to lighting and are therefore difficult to carry out outside of a laboratory setting.
Recording of eye activity can be done using a Virtual Reality (VR) headset such as the
HTC Vive Pro from emteqlabs [84]. Garcia et al. [32] have stressed that a person’s
emotional and motivational states are significant components of cognitive fatigue. No
studies were found that explicitly included features correlated to emotion. However, it
might be beneficial to include features that convey information about the emotional state
of the participant, such as valence and arousal levels, which is proposed by Luo et al.
[171].
Establishing ground truth
The most used method for assessing the ground truth fatigue level is by self-assessment
questionnaires [32]. Tseng et al. [172] have found that the very commonly applied Visual
Analogue Scale (VAS) can give reliable fatigue levels for stroke patients. Furthermore,
Borragán et al. [28] used a VAS for detecting cognitive fatigue levels and found a signifi-
cant correlation between the participant’s subjective VAS scoring and his/her performance
in the fatigue-inducing task. Luo et al. [171] also used a VAS for establishing the ground
truth fatigue level in their participants, and then trained their models on physiological data
stemming from a wearable device. However, they found that it was more difficult to pre-
dict the fatigue intensity than the presence of fatigue episodes. They attribute this to the
subjectivity of the VAS measurements.
Another method for establishing the ground truth is using reaction times, since cognitive
fatigue can lead to longer reaction time, as mentioned in section 2.2.2. For instance,
Kalanadhabhatta et al. [161] assessed the fatigue level before and after every fatigue-
inducing task by recording reaction times and by making the participants report their level
of fatigue on a VAS. The advantages of using reaction time are that it is a quantitative
method that can be measured concurrently with the fatigue-inducing task, and it does not
rely on the subjective reporting from the participant. On the other hand, there does not
seem to be a consensus on how to map increasing reaction time with different fatigue
levels. For instance, Jaydari et al. [173] and Langner et al. [174] found that the reaction
time increased by approximately 20–30 ms after a fatigue-inducing session, while Peters
et al. [37] only found an increase of around 7 ms. These differences can possibly be
attributed to the varying lengths and tasks used in fatigue-inducing sessions.
3.3
Emotion Detection
As mentioned in section 2.2.3, there are overall two different approaches for quantifying
emotions: the basic-emotion view and the valence-arousal model. The theory of basic
emotions offers a simple method for describing emotions, which is also easy to interpret.
On the other hand, proponents of the valence and arousal model argue that this model
24
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
==PAGE:37==
is more capable of distinguishing between subtle differences in emotions and is better
suited for describing all naturally occurring emotions [175] [176]. The disadvantage of the
valence and arousal model is the added complexity that comes with mapping emotions
onto the two continuous dimensions. For a further discussion of these two models, see
Appendix B.2.
Inducing emotions
Siedlecka et al. [177] have reviewed methods for inducing emotions and conclude that
there are five different types: visual stimuli, music, recalling memories, situational proce-
dures where the participant is put in a context that should create certain emotions and
lastly, imagery, where the participant is asked to imagine a specific emotional situation.
Out of these five, they conclude that visual stimuli is the most effective method for inducing
a wide range of emotions, while the other four methods only succeed in inducing certain
emotions. Quite a few studies have indeed used visual stimuli such as still images [178]
[179] or videos [135] [180] for inducing emotions.
Signals and features
EEG signals have often been used for detecting emotion in experiment settings [181] [182]
[183] [180]. However, it has been found that it can be difficult to collect reliable EEG data
[85], and EEG signals are vulnerable to artifacts, especially outside of a lab setting [184].
The very influential Facial Action Coding System (FACS) has been used for measuring
facial behavior and emotions within fields such as neuroscience [185], consumer analysis
[186] and social studies [187]. FACS is well-validated for the detection of emotion, how-
ever, it takes several months to train and get a certification in the AU coding system [188],
and since the FACS analysis is done by hand, it is not feasible to interpret all facial ex-
pressions in real-time. There are computer programs, like the Noldus FaceReader, which
can be used for automatic face analysis based on FACS [189] [188]. The FaceReader
is more accurate and faster than doing FACS by hand. However, the software requires
good lighting conditions and video quality. To get good measurements, the participant
must also sit close to the computer screen and not make head movements away from the
camera [188].
Another technique, facial sEMG, has been used extensively to measure emotional states
[190], [86]. In contrast to FACS, facial sEMG can measure microexpressions, i.e. acti-
vations of the muscles due to emotions that are not visible to the human eye. The main
disadvantages of using sEMG are that the signals are prone to noise and that it can re-
quire some effort to properly place the electrodes on the face [188]. The current versions
of facial sEMG recording wearables, like the emteqPRO, are also somewhat intrusive.
EMG recordings of especially the corrugator and zygomaticus muscles have been used
to get insight into the valence and arousal of emotional states [191] [192]. In a recent
study, Gjoreski et al. [56] showed different videos of positive, negative and neutral content
to 38 participants, who were wearing an emteqPRO sEMG mask. The chosen videos
had previously been validated to induce these emotions in two other studies. Gjoreski
et al. found that there was a statistically significant correlation between the activation of
certain facial muscles and both the valence and arousal levels of the video content. More
specifically, negative videos were correlated with increasing corrugator activity while the
activation of the zygomaticus and orbicularis muscles increased during periods of positive
valence. It was also found that overall muscle activation was increased when participants
watched high-arousal videos, except for the corrugator muscle.
Establishing ground truth
Self-reported ratings are often used for establishing the ground truth emotional state in
experiments [135] [56] [179], because it is a simple, fast and cheap method. However,

labeling methods [188]. Park et al. [193] used external observers for judging the emotional
states of the test persons, in addition to the subjective responses. This, however, requires
that the observer has detailed knowledge of how emotions are displayed in the face.
3.4
Datasets and Machine Learning
Most of the studies mentioned above have used machine learning methods to derive in-
formation from the sensor data. As described in section 2.5, successful machine learning
models rely on the availability of sufficiently large datasets. In the recent review of the
current state of stress detection by Vos et al. [24], they found that most stress experi-
ments included less than 30 participants. Vos et al. conclude that given the small number
of subjects used in the reviewed experiments, it is doubtful whether the resulting models
can generalize well on new, unseen data. In a follow-up study, Vos et al. [194] merged
four smaller stress datasets into one, and found that models trained on this larger dataset
generalize better to new, unseen datasets.
Nkurikiyeyezu et al. [195] used another approach, where they first made a ’generic’ model
that was trained on data from many people, and then calibrated the model with new data
from a single person, through incremental learning. They made 40 personalized models
and achieved high accuracies (approx. 90-100%) for all participants by calibrating with
relatively few samples (<100) from the same person. This approach is more demand-
ing compared to the method Vos et al. used, since one new model is made for every
participant.
Using RF, SVM, KNN and Gradient Boosting (GB) algorithms for stress prediction is quite
common. Out of these, the RF and GB usually lead to the best performances for binary
classification [24]. RF, KNN and especially SVM are also commonly used for fatigue clas-
sification [95] [196]. In recent years, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) have been
used for classifying both basic emotions, valence and arousal states, and cognitive fa-
tigue. For instance, Santamaria-Granados et al. [197] achieved better accuracy and
f1-scores (approx. 80%) when training a CNN on a publicly available emotion dataset,
compared to KNN, RF and other machine learning algorithms. Recently, Chui et al. [135]
also used a 1D CNN for recognizing emotions using features from ECG and EDA signals
which also led to better results (approx. 78% accuracy) compared with KNN, RF and SVM
models.

As mentioned in section 4.1, HR and EDA signals as well as valence and arousal fea-
tures will be used for the stress detection models. A list of some publicly available stress
datasets is located in appendix F.1. Most of the available datasets have categorical (ordi-
nal) target values, such as low-stress/medium stress/high stress. Several of the available
datasets stem from recordings of stress during driving, such as the AffectiveROAD [200],
DrivingStress [114] and DrivingWork [201] datasets. The majority of stress datasets have
relatively few participants, usually between 10-25. However, Vos et al. [194] have recently
combined several stress datasets into one dataset consisting of 99 subjects. This com-
bined dataset is called StressData [194] and consists of the four smaller datasets NEURO
[202], WESAD [203], SWELL [204] and UBFC-Phys [205]. The StressData is the largest
available stress dataset and will be included in the final, merged stress dataset in this
thesis. It was not possible to find any stress datasets that included valence and arousal
features.
The final merged dataset
One final, merged stress dataset is made in this project, called Stress Dataset 1, as shown
in table 4.1. The Stress Dataset 1 consists of the StressData and DrivingStress datasets.
Both of these datasets contain HR and EDA recordings. It is not stated in the study by
Vos et al. [194] what kind of EDA signal is present in StressData. However, it is probably
either the raw, total EDA signal or the phasic component SCR, since SCR is often used for
measuring stress levels, as mentioned in section 2.4.3. Vos et al. also do not mention how
they processed the HR or EDA signals before creating features, but they have probably
filtered the signals and removed artifacts. The final Stress Dataset 1 will contain 10 simple
statistical features that Vos et al. found were useful for stress detection. We will also use
the same window size for calculating features as Vos et al., namely 25s.

Creating Emotions Dataset 2
The Emotions Dataset 2 is merged from both the EMGDataVR [56] and PME4 [209]

mimic the six basic emotions upon instruction, and therefore the emotional responses are
posed instead of spontaneous. This will be discussed further in the Discussion section
6.4.
The PME4 dataset was processed by following the procedure that Gjoreski et al [56] had
used for their EMGDatVR dataset. The data was first filtered with a high-pass filter of 20-
30 Hz (depending on the muscle) and a low-pass filter of 500 Hz. Afterwards, the signal
amplitudes were extracted using the Root Mean Square (RMS). Figure 4.2 shows the raw
EMG signal, and how the EMG signal looks after applying a band-pass filter and RMS
amplitude extraction. The code used to apply the band-pass filter is shown in figure F.2 in
the Appendix. Then, insignificant components and amplitude bursts were removed from
the EMG signal by setting those observations that had a lower value than the 5th lowest
percentile to 0, and all data points with a value higher than the 95th percentile were set
to the value of the 95th percentile. Finally, the signals were normalized using a min-max
normalization algorithm and then downsampled from 5 kHz to 1 Hz by averaging over the
values.
The signal amplitudes from the zygomaticus and corrugator muscles and the average
amplitude for these muscles were used in Emotion dataset 2, since the PME4 dataset
only includes these. Furthermore, Gjoreski et al.[56] have found that these two muscle
groups were most effective for differentiating between positive and negative emotions.
The target values were made by grouping the basic emotions from the PME4 dataset into
three categories, in such a way that the emotion ’happy’ was labeled as ’Positive’, the
emotions ’sad’, ’angry’, ’fear’ and ’disgust’ as ’Negative’ and neutral as ’Neutral’. The full
code used to create the Emotion Datasets 1 and 2 can be found in the python notebook
Emotions Dataset 1_2.ipynb.

4.4
Experiments
This section describes the general experiment process and the procedures for measuring
the various signals. Then, the stress, fatigue and emotions session are described in
further detail. A list of the materials used for the experiments is located in appendix H.1.
As mentioned in section 4.1, the Empatica E4 is used for recording HR, EDA and IBI
signals and the emteqPRO mask will be used for recording HR, IBI, valence and arousal
features and facial sEMG signals in the experiments.
Overall process
Every experiment was carried out inside a room with closed door and blinds to remove
distractions. The participant and the experiment organizer were alone in the room and the
participant was seated facing a computer on a table. Before every experiment began, the
emteqPRO mask and Empatica E4 wristband were cleaned with alcohol wipes. Likewise,
the participant cleaned his/her forehead, both sides of the face, and the wrist to prevent
contamination between participants and ensure the best possible signal. If the participant
was right-handed, the wristband was placed on the left wrist, and vice versa, so that the
participant had the dominant hand free to control the computer mouse, without disturbing
the recordings from the wristband. The E4 Real-time app [210] was used to start and
end each recording via Bluetooth. Then, the emteqPRO was placed on the head of the
participant and we checked the Emteq Demos Desktop app [211] to make sure there was
good contact between the mask and face. If not, the mask was adjusted accordingly.
Thereafter, the emteqPRO mask was calibrated using the calibration tool in the Emteq
Demos Desktop app [211].
The built-in laptop camera was turned on and filmed the entire experiment for subsequent
eye activity analysis. However, even with a desk lamp, achieving sufficient lighting and
video quality was difficult. In particular, we found that it was hard to see the pupils clearly
in the videos. After the initial setup, three different sessions followed: a stress session
followed by an emotion session and finally a fatigue session, as illustrated in figure 4.3.
The order of the sessions was chosen to create the least possible interference between
the sessions. Further elaboration is found in appendix H.2. An online stopwatch [212] was
used to get timestamps on the games, tasks and videos. The entire experiment lasted
approximately 40-45 min. Figure 4.4 shows the experiment setup.

Stress questionnaires
The participant’s stress level was assessed using a self-assessment questionnaire, since
this is the most used method for detecting stress and since there are not many other feasi-

Load Index (NASA-TLX), which is a widely used and reliable questionnaire for assessing
task load and stress levels [216] [217]. In six questions, the participant assessed the men-
tal workload required for a specific task. The questions of the NASA-TLX questionnaire
are shown in appendix H.6.
The first five participants were also told to fill out 10 of the questions in the Short Stress
State Questionnaire (SSSQ) [218] before and after the stress session. These questions
can be seen in appendix H.4. Some of the benefits of the SSSQ are its short length and its
proven sensitivity to different aspects of stress, including distress and task engagement
[219]. However, the SSSQ was dropped from the subsequent experiments, since it was
deemed that the NASA-TLX was better suited for determining the stress level that the
participant felt during the session.
4.4.2
Fatigue session
Throughout the fatigue session, the participant had to carry out a primary, fatigue-inducing
task, and a secondary task for measuring their reaction time. As mentioned in section 3.2,
cognitive fatigue causes increasing reaction time. Therefore, it might be possible to use
reaction time as a quantitative measurement of the fatigue level. At the beginning and
end of the fatigue session the participant answered a VAFS questionnaire. The fatigue-
inducing task lasted approximately seven minutes for the first 16 participants. After these
initial experiments, it was found that seven minutes was not a long enough time to get

a consistent trend in the reaction times. This is elaborated further in section 5.3.2. For
this reason, the duration of the task-switching game was increased to 25 minutes in the
following experiments. The primary and secondary tasks and the VAFS questionnaire are

Fatigue games
Primary task (Task-switching):
For the first two participants, we used three different
fatigue-inducing tasks: a Task-switching game, an N-back task and the Stroop task. How-
ever, we found that the action of transitioning between these three games took up too
much time in the fatigue session and gave the participant time to recover from the in-
duced fatigue. For this reason, it was decided to only include one primary game, namely
the Task-switching game. As mentioned in section 3.2, it has been found that a Task-

located in the python notebook beep_sound.ipynb.
Fatigue questionnaire
The participants were asked to rate their fatigue level on a scale from 1-10 on a Visual
Analogue Fatigue Scale (VAFS) since prior work suggests that this is a reliable method
for assessing cognitive fatigue levels, as mentioned in section 3.2. The scale used in the
experiments is shown in appendix H.5. The VAFS was primarily intended for evaluating

4.5.1
Empatica E4 data
The function flirt.reader from the python package flirt [92] was used for reading the raw
HR and EDA data files from the Empatica E4 into data frames. The HR files contain the
estimated heart rate every second. The statistical features, i.e. mean, variance, std. de-
viation etc. were calculated over non-overlapping windows of 25 seconds, using a similar
function as the one used for the Stress Dataset 1 in section 4.3.1. The EDA files contain
raw EDA signals in MicroSiemens sampled at 4Hz. The raw signals were processed and
split into their phasic and tonic components using the function get-eda-features from the
python package flirt [92], just like when creating Stress Dataset 1. We then used the pha-
sic component of the EDA signal for calculating features. The features were calculated
for non-overlapping windows of 25 seconds, also with the function get-eda-features.

Experiment Stress Dataset
Three different experiment stress datasets were made. The first two, Exp. Stress Dataset
1 and Exp. Stress Dataset 2 both contain two stress categories, i.e. ’No stress’ and
’Stress’. The difference between them is that Exp. Stress Dataset 2 contains valence
and arousal features, while Exp. Stress Dataset 1 does not. This was done since we
wanted to test the importance of these features. We used the average scoring from the
NASA-TLX questionnaires to assign stress levels to each stress-inducing game. NASA-
TLX scores between 0-50 were set to ’No stress’ and between 50-100 to ’Stress’.
The third dataset, Exp. Stress Dataset 3 has four stress categories: ’No stress’, ’Low
stress’, ’Medium stress’ and ’High stress’. For this dataset, an average NASA-TLX score
of 0-10 was set to ’No stress’, of 10-40 as ’Low stress’, of 40-60 as ’Medium stress’ and
a score of 60-100 as ’High stress’. These levels are somewhat arbitrary since it was
not possible to find any literature that described how to divide the NASA-TLX scores into
categories.
Experiment Fatigue Dataset
As will be described in the Result section 5.2.1, the IBI signals from the Empatica E4 wrist-
band were of poor quality. Therefore, we used the IBI data recorded by the emteqPRO
instead. Four fatigue datasets were made, where the HRV features were calculated us-
ing non-overlapping windows of 2,3,4 and 5 min, respectively. This was done since the
fatigue dataset consisted of few observations. Therefore, we used smaller windows to
get datasets with more observations.
Initially, it was planned was to use reaction times for assigning fatigue labels. However,
this was not possible since we could not measure an increase in reaction time during the
fatigue session, as is elaborated further in the Result section 5.3.2. Appendix I.4 explains
how we had planned to label the fatigue data with the reaction times. Instead, the last
4 minutes of the fatigue session were labeled as ’Fatigue’ and the remaining part of the
fatigue session as ’No Fatigue’ for each participant. This decision was based on the fact
that the VAFS scores reported subjective feelings of increased fatigue after the fatigue
session. Two datasets were made: Exp. Fatigue Dataset 1 without valence and arousal
features and Exp. Fatigue Dataset 2 including them.

4.6
Machine Learning models
The general idea for creating the stress, fatigue and emotion machine learning models
is to first train and optimize models on the data from previous research projects. Then,
we test how well these models perform when classifying the experiment data from this
project. In the final step, the models are further trained using the experiment data. This is
also called incremental learning as described in section 2.5.6. The reason for doing this is
that the classification performance of a given machine learning model generally increases
with larger sample sizes, as mentioned in section 2.5.1.
As we have seen, the publicly available datasets within the field of stress, fatigue and
emotion include recordings from 10-25 subjects, and during this project, it is only feasible
to record data from a similar number of test persons. We hypothesize that by training
the models on data from other projects and then on our experiment data, we will achieve
robust models that can accurately classify new data. This is important since cognitive
state prediction modules used during physical rehabilitation will encounter many different
40
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
==PAGE:53==
patients and settings. In this thesis, models that are trained on our experiment data only
will also be developed, in order to compare these models with the incrementally trained
models.
4.6.1
General machine learning procedure
The procedures described in this subsection relate to both stress, fatigue and emotion
modeling.
The chosen Machine Learning models
Six different machine learning algorithms are used in this thesis. In the following sections,
a ”Modelset” refers to a set containing one of each of the six machine learning algorithms
mentioned here. The python notebook ML_Functions.ipynb and CNN.ipynb contain the
functions for implementing the six algorithms described below.
• eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGB):
The first algorithm is XGB, which was also used by Vos et al. [194] when they made
a stress model based on their StressData dataset. In this project, the XGBClassifier
from the python package xgboost is used [228].
• Convolutional Neural Network (CNN):
The second algorithm is a CNN inspired by Chui et al. [135] who used such a model
to classify biosignals for emotion detection. The CNN is built using Keras [229] and
the layers in the model are very similar to those used by Chui et al. The layers are
shown in the python notebook CNN.ipynb.
• Support Vector Machines (SVM):
The third algorithm is the SVM, which has often been used for emotion classification
of EMG signal data [230] [180]. The sklearn function SDGClassifier [231] is used
for implementing it.
• Hist Gradient Boost Classifier (HGBC):
The fourth algorithm is an HGBC which is a faster version of the very popular Gradi-
ent Boosting method for classification. We will use the version implemented in the
sklearn package [232].
• K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN):
The fifth algorithm is a KNN. Even though KNN models do not support incremental
learning, it is included to serve as a baseline model for comparison with the others.
The KNN has performed well in emotion classification [209] and with EMG signals
[233]. The model is implemented with the function KNeighborsClassifier from the
python package sklearn [234].
• Random Forest (RF):
The sixth algorithm is an RF, since this is a classic machine learning model that is
often used for classification. In this project, the RandomForestClassifier function
from sklearn [235] is used.
The target values in the datasets used in this project are ordinal variables, meaning that
they can be ordered. It is possible to make classification models specifically for ordinal
target values, where the order of the target values is considered [236]. However, it was
not possible to find convincing examples of this in previous studies on stress, fatigue or
emotion detection. Therefore, ”standard” classifiers are used instead.
Train-test split
A train-test split of 80-20 was used for all datasets. The same train and test datasets were
used for all models in a Modelset. After splitting the dataset into train and test sets, the

StandardScaler function from sklearn [237] was used for those models that are sensitive
to the scale of the features and require standardized data, i.e. the KNN and SVM models.
Oversampling datasets
As is shown in the Result section 5.4, some of the experiment datasets are imbalanced.
This pertains especially to the datasets including four categories, namely the Exp. Stress
Data 3 and Exp. Emotions Data 2. Therefore, the training sets of these datasets were
over-sampled using the python package SMOTE [127] [139]. Oversampling was chosen
since the datasets from this project are relatively small and undersampling would lead to
a loss of valuable information.
Hyperparameter optimization
Initially, a grid search method was used for finding optimal hyperparameters for all the
mentioned machine learning algorithms. However, the grid search method was very slow
for the larger datasets and, therefore, we decided to use the Optuna package instead.
The hyperparameters were found by maximizing the negative log-loss1 for the training
data using a 3-fold cross-validation. This process was repeated 200 times for each model.
Figure 4.11 illustrates how the Optuna algorithm works. The figure shows the contour plot
of the negative log-loss depending on two of the hyperparameters used in the Random
Forest algorithm. It is visible that Optuna tries a wide range of values, but intensifies the
search in the lower left corner, where the negative log-loss is highest.
It was not feasible to optimize the CNN models using Optuna within a reasonable time-
frame. Instead, the CNN algorithm hyperparameters were optimized more manually by
visually inspecting loss graphs during training, and by stopping the training if there was
no improvement, to avoid overfitting. The notebook Hyperparameter_optimizer.ipynb
includes the functions used for optimizing hyperparameters.

Test score metrics
Accuracy and f1 scores are often used for measuring the performance of machine learning
models within emotion and cognitive state detection [179] [135] [238]. We used a balanced
accuracy score for calculating the test scores for both the stress, fatigue and emotion
models, which gives a good performance estimate for imbalanced datasets. We also
calculated the weighted f1 score, which is a version of the f1 score that can be used for
both multiclass and binary classification. This version of the f1 score can also be used for
imbalanced datasets.
Incremental learning
Incremental learning is applied to three of the six machine learning models, namely CNN,
SVM and XGB. The remaining algorithms could not be updated incrementally within the
scope of this project. For the SVM model, the sklearn function partial_fit was used for
implementing incremental learning. A similar function was used for the XGB model. For
the CNN model, we froze the weights of the original model to not destroy the already
achieved knowledge. Then, new trainable layers were added to the model and the model
was then trained on the new data.

6
Discussion
In this chapter, the results of this thesis are discussed. Then follows a summation of how
these results contribute to the research questions regarding the detection of cognitive
stress (RQ1), cognitive fatigue (RQ2) and emotional states (RQ3).
6.1
Sensor data quality
The signals recorded by the Empatica E4 wristband during the experiments were of vary-
ing quality. The BVP and IBI signals had poor quality, with gaps, artifacts and uneven
sample frequencies. The recorded HR signal was more consistent and without gaps. The
reason for this might be that the HR is reported no matter whether the BVP signal has ar-
tifacts or not. In places where the BVP signal is poor, the HR signal is estimated by the
Empatica’s algorithm instead of being directly calculated from the BVP signal. The EDA
signals were nicely recorded and without many artifacts and gaps. These results con-
tradict findings in previous research, where the HR signals from the Empatica E4 were
of good quality, while the EDA signals were not always reliable. It is possible that the
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
57
==PAGE:70==
placement of the wristband could have led to the gaps in the recorded BVP and IBI data,
since the data quality varied from participant to participant.
The IBI, HR, sEMG, valence and arousal signals from the emteqPRO mask had good
quality, i.e. without many gaps and artifacts. The recorded FacialActivation scores were
heavily skewed toward values below 3, on a scale of 1 to 9. This may be due to the
experiment’s nature and the participants’ emotional arousal level.
6.2
Stress detection with EDA and HR sensors
The models trained on the dataset merged from publicly available data, Stress Dataset 1,
had accuracy and f1 scores of around 70%. The best algorithms achieved almost 80%.
The large majority of the Stress Dataset 1 originates from a dataset that has led to good
stress classification results in previous research [194]. Therefore, it is not that surprising
that the models trained on the Stress Dataset 1 performed relatively well.
Testing these models on the experiment data led to poor results no better than chance
for many of the algorithms. Thus, the models trained on Stress Dataset 1 could not gen-
eralize to the data from the stress experiment. This result could be caused by different
data recording and processing techniques, leading to two different data populations. For
instance, in the experiment data, the majority of observations belonged to the ’Stress’
category, while the Stress Dataset 1 consists of more ’No Stress’ observations. This is
also known as a prior-probability shift as mentioned in section 2.5.6. However, when the
models were updated by incremental learning using the experiment data, they achieved
better results, with the XGB model reaching an accuracy of 82%. This result is in line with
findings from previous research.
There was a large performance variance in the models trained on the experiment stress
data. Four out of six models trained on the dataset including valence and arousal features
performed better than when trained on the data without these two features. This result
suggests that there is a benefit in including these two features in stress detection, which
is in line with previous findings that have shown a correlation between stress and valence
and arousal. The XGB and HGBC models were the best performing models when using
the experiment stress data, reaching accuracy and f1 scores of 85 and 86%.
The models trained on the experiment data with four stress categories had relatively poor
results and the best performing model achieved 65% accuracy. The reason for this is
probably that the dataset was imbalanced, with far more ’High Stress’ and ’Medium Stress’
observations than ’No stress’ and ’Low stress’ ones. In general, both the models trained
on publicly available data and on the experiment data had a clear tendency to misclas-
sify the minority targets more often than the majority category, even though the SMOTE
algorithm was used on the imbalanced training datasets.
The results also suggest that both the HR and EDA signals contributed to the classification
results for all stress detection algorithms. However, the EDA signals had higher feature
importance than the HR signals.
6.3
Fatigue detection with HRV features
During the fatigue sessions, the recorded reaction times had no specific trend over time,
even though all participants reported higher levels of fatigue after the fatigue session
than before. The fluctuating nature of the reaction times could have been caused by the
participant sometimes anticipating the beep tone and other times being too involved with
the primary task to react promptly to the noise. It is also possible that the act of pressing
a keyboard button was too inconsistent for measuring reaction time.
58
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
==PAGE:71==
All models trained on the merged Fatigue Dataset 1 consisting of three publicly available
datasets had accuracy and f1 scores between 60 and 65%. The models especially strug-
gled with classifying the ’Fatigue’ observations correctly, which suggests that the Fatigue
Dataset 1 contains unwanted noise. The noise could be caused by different data process-
ing methods, since the data set made by Matuz et al. [93] had already been processed
when we downloaded it. Furthermore, the PCA plots of the Fatigue Dataset 1 suggest
that there could be wrongly labeled observations. We suspect that by changing the fatigue
labels of the FatigueSet and Gamer’s dataset from continuous to categorical, we could
have unintentionally created wrongly labeled observations.
Training new models on a subset of the Fatigue Dataset 1, namely the dataset by Matuz
et al. [93], led to better results for the majority of algorithms. The XGB and HGBC models
achieved 73% accuracy. These models were tested on the experiment data which led to
bad results. By instead using incremental learning with the experiment data, the models’
accuracy scores improved, with the XGB model achieving 73% accuracy.
None of the models trained solely on the experiment data had very good performance,
since most achieved accuracy and f1 scores below 60%. The poor performance of the
models is probably both due to the labeling procedure based on the self-reported VAFS
and the very small (<100) dataset sizes of the experiment fatigue data. When a larger
dataset was used, namely the Full Data consisting of both the experiment data and the
data by Matuz et al., the SVM, KNN and XGB algorithms all reached accuracy scores
between 76 and 80%.
6.4
Emotions detection with facial sEMG sensors
The best performing emotion classifiers were those trained on the Emotion Dataset 1
consisting of a single publicly available dataset from previous research. Most of these
models achieved accuracy and f1 scores above 92%. Similarly to the stress and fatigue
detection processes, the models trained on Emotion Dataset 1 were poor at classifying
the experiment data. With incremental learning, though, the results improved and the
XGB achieved 85% accuracy,
On the other hand, the models trained on the Emotion dataset 2, which is merged from
two different publicly available datasets, had poor results. These models all got accuracy
and f1 scores between 53 and 58%. The reason for this is probably that the Emotion
Dataset 2 does not include EMG amplitudes from the orbicularis muscles and valence
and arousal features. In addition, the PME4 dataset, which is a subset of the Emotion
Dataset 2, contains data recorded from posed emotions, instead of spontaneous. As
mentioned in section 2.3.1, there is a substantial difference between spontaneous and
posed expressions of emotions. This could have led to poor performance of the models
trained on Emotion Dataset 2.
When training models only on the experiment data, the algorithms performed well, with
the XGB and HGBC achieving 83% accuracy. The signals from the corrugator and or-
bicularis muscles had the highest feature importance for the best-performing algorithm
(XGB). This is probably because these two muscle groups are active in frowning and
smiling, respectively, which are two facial behaviors closely tied to emotion. The valence
and arousal features had the lowest importance.
Including a ’Other’-category in the experiment data led to slightly worse classification re-
sults, with XGB and HGBC getting accuracy scores of 75 and 77%. This is probably
because the dataset was heavily imbalanced, with far more ’Other’-observations than
’Positive’, ’Negative’ and ’Neutral’ ones. Furthermore, the ’Other’-observations stem from

those parts of the experiment where the emotional state of the participants were not
recorded or induced in any way. Therefore, it is possible that some of these observa-
tions should have been labeled as either ’Positive’, ’Negative’ or ’Neutral’. This could
have created wrongly labeled observations and thereby noise in the dataset.
6.5
Implications for research questions
In relation to RQ1, it has been found that it is feasible to detect stress using HR and
EDA features and binary classification models, and that especially the XGB and HGBC
algorithms perform well for this purpose. Therefore, both HR and EDA signals are suitable
for the detection of cognitive stress, which confirms the findings of previous research
projects. Most of the stress classifiers performed better when trained on the experiment
dataset including valence and arousal features, compared to the dataset without these
features. Therefore, these two features may also be suitable for stress detection.
It has also bee found that the Empatica E4 wristband records good quality EDA signals.
Yet, since the HR signals from the Empatica E4 stem from poor BVP signals, the Empatica
E4 device is not optimal for recording HR signals. A significant advantage of the Empatica
E4 is that it can be used for recording data in a rehabilitation setting without hindering the
patient’s mobility or distracting him/her from the serious-games. Future versions of the
wristband, such as the newer EmbracePlus [240] that is smaller than the Empatica E4,
will potentially be easier to fit on the wrist and thus yield more consistent results. The
emteqPRO could also possibly be used to record HR data for stress detection in future
research projects, since it records good-quality HR signals.
With respect to RQ2, the results showed that the IBI signals from the emteqPRO were
complete and without many artifacts. However, since the fatigue experiment datasets
were quite small, the usability of the emteqPRO for fatigue detection must be investigated
further in future studies. The less-than-ideal labeling process used for the experiment
fatigue data and the small number of fatigue experiments have impacted the quality of
the fatigue experiment datasets and the resulting classification models. Yet, the results
suggest that HRV features can be used for fatigue detection, given a sufficiently large
dataset. It cannot be concluded whether valence and arousal features are appropriate for
fatigue detection, since half of the algorithms performed better when the dataset included
these features, while some algorithms performed worse.
Regarding RQ3, the results confirm that sEMG and valence and arousal signals from the
emteqPRO mask have good quality. Moreover, most emotion classifiers reached high
accuracy scores and, therefore, these signals are suitable for emotion detection, as also
found in previous research. The emotion classifiers with four classes also achieved good
accuracy and f1 scores, which suggests that it is also feasible to attain good results with
more emotion categories. This could be valuable in a rehabilitation setting where the
patient is likely to experience a range of emotional states.
The main downside of the emteqPRO device is that it is quite intrusive in its current form
and it is not possible to wear glasses concurrently. The emotion classification results
suggest that it could be possible in future studies, and in a rehabilitation setting, to use
signals stemming only from around the eyes and forehead, since the corrugator and orbic-
ularis muscles had high importance. This could lead to a less intrusive experience with
recording sEMG. Another challenge related to the usability of the emteqPRO mask in
stroke rehabilitation is that stroke patients may experience an impaired ability to produce
facial emotional expressions, depending on the location of the stroke [241]. Hence, the
emteqPRO mask may not be directly usable for all stroke patients during rehabilitation.

Some of the findings from this thesis have implications for both stress, fatigue and emotion
detection. The most important finding concerns the size of the dataset. When merging
the datasets from previous research projects, a dilemma presented itself: On the one
hand, it is beneficial to have as much data as possible for achieving models with good
generalizability, and on the other hand, the dataset can become noisy, if several smaller
datasets are merged into one, just like the Fatigue Dataset 1. In future studies, care
should be taken to not create noise in merged datasets.
The results also indicate that it is possible to use incremental learning to update a classifi-
cation model and improve its performance on new data from a different experiment setting.
This is in line with previous research that has found that models trained on datasets stem-
ming from different experiment setting are better able to generalize to new data. Lastly, we
found that most of the created classifiers achieved worse results for the minority classes
compared to the majority classes. This highlights the need for good ways to mitigate the
effects of imbalanced datasets.
6.6
Limitations
This section reviews some limitations that affect the interpretation of the findings described
above and their applicability in stroke rehabilitation.
6.6.1
Stroke patients and rehabilitation setting
The experiments in this thesis have only included people who have not suffered a stroke,
since the primary aim was to investigate how to detect stress, fatigue and emotion and
which signals and sensors were most appropriate for this aim. Participants were mainly in
the age range of 23 to 27 years. Furthermore, during this thesis, it has been presumed that
it is possible to gain knowledge of emotional states based on facial expressions. However,
this assumption does not apply to some stroke patients with physical impairments that
affect their ability to control facial expressions. For these reasons, the final models created
in this project might not generalize well for all stroke patients.
The experiments were carried out in a controlled environment, where each participant
was seated on a chair during the experiment. The experiments were designed in this way
to control variables that could affect the recorded signals, such as physical movement.
For the serious-gaming stroke rehabilitation sessions, the patient will be more physically
active, which impacts both the heart rate and level of sweating. The final models de-
veloped during this thesis may not be able to distinguish between heart rate and sweat
activity caused by cognitive states versus physical activation. Therefore, it is necessary
to account for physical activation in future studies, for instance by including a feature that
monitors the level of physical activity the participant is engaging in.
6.6.2
Labeling experiment data
The measured reaction times could not be used for labeling the fatigue observations
from the experiments. Instead, the target values were created using the subjective VAFS
scores of the participant before and after the fatigue-inducing session. While there was
an increase in the VAFS scores for all the participants, the VAFS score is subjective and
also measured after the fatigue session. This means that some fatigue labels could be
inaccurate. Therefore, the fatigue detection model might not generalize well to another
experiment or rehabiliation setting.
The recorded data from the emotion experiments were labeled using the labels from the
shown videos, namely ’Positive’, ’Negative’ or ’Neutral’. Nevertheless, the participants’
personal experiences might have differed from the video labels. For instance, the ’Pos-
itive’ videos consisted of funny video clips, but some participants may have found them
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
61
==PAGE:74==
boring, etc. This could lead to incorrectly labeled data and affected the quality of the
dataset and the resulting machine learning models. In future experiments, this could be
remedied by asking the participants to rate their subjective experiences after watching
each video.
6.6.3
Dataset sizes
Some of the datasets used in the modeling process during this thesis are relatively small
compared to the usual machine learning standards. The sizes of the merged datasets
from previous research projects were limited by the number and quality of the publicly
available datasets. For example, the merged Fatigue Dataset 1 consisting of data origi-
nating from three publicly available datasets led to poor classification results. In addition,
the sizes of some of the experiment datasets were small, especially those that included
valence and arousal features. Some of the findings discussed may have been affected
by these limitations of the data. For example, some of the stress and fatigue models
performed better when trained on datasets that included valence and arousal features. It
should be investigated in future research projects whether the classifiers’ performances
will change if trained on larger datasets.
7
Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated how to detect cognitive stress, cognitive fatigue and emotional
states using various physiological signals. The aim has been to create well-performing
machine learning models, test the usability of the signals recorded by the Empatica E4
and emteqPRO wearables and estimate how feasible it is to use these wearables in stroke
rehabilitation. The choice of signals and sensors used have been found in collaboration
with the ReHyb project and by investigating the findings from other research projects.
The results suggest that HR and EDA signals can be used for cognitive stress detection.
Furthermore, the Empatica E4 wristband can potentially be used in a rehabilitation setting
without hindering the patient’s movements. However, due to the lacking data quality, it
might be prudent to use other wearables for recording the HR signal.
It has also been found that sEMG signals are appropriate for emotion detection. In addi-
tion, judging from the results of the fatigue modeling process, it is possible to create well-
performing fatigue classifiers based on HRV features, given a sufficiently large dataset.
Due to the somewhat intrusive nature of the emteqPRO mask, it may not be feasible to
use the current version of the mask in stroke rehabilitation. However, the results sug-
gest that a smaller, future sEMG device that can record signals from around the eyes and
forehead might also lead to good emotion classification.
For cognitive stress detection, there seems to be a benefit in including valence and arousal
features in the dataset. The results from the fatigue classification were more inconclusive.
These results must be investigated further in future studies since the used datasets in-
cluding valence and arousal features were relatively small.
Another key finding was that the use of incremental learning improved the ability of the
models to classify new, unseen data from a different experiment setting. This is true for
both stress, fatigue and emotion detection, especially when using the XGB algorithm.
The incremental learning method could therefore be a way to achieve better performance
results on a new group of subjects, potentially from a new rehabilitation setting, without
having to retrain the model on the entire dataset.

7.2
Future Work
Integration into the ReHyb setting
Future studies should investigate how the stress, fatigue and emotion models must be
changed to accommodate stroke patients, before the models can be integrated into the
Human Digital Twin in the ReHyb project. It will also be necessary to test whether the
detection models will function in the serious-gaming setting, which will involve more phys-
ical activity than in our experiments. For these reasons, it might be beneficial to collect
new data in an experiment setting that involves stroke patients and games from the stroke
rehabilitation setting. The resulting data from these experiments could then be integrated
into the models created in this thesis, either by incremental learning or by retraining the
models on the complete dataset.
In future work, it could also be interesting to create models that are trained with person-
specific data, by collecting data for only one person, over a longer period. Perhaps it
would then be possible to create one machine learning model for each stroke patient, by
using incremental learning.
When integrated into a stroke rehabilitation setting, the models must be able to recognize
cognitive states in real time. Therefore, future work could focus on processing the EMG,
HR and EDA data without lag. The code developed in this project can probably be used
for this aim. If the models were integrated into the Human Digital Twin (HDT), the system
could react appropriately to the patient’s current state. For instance, if the HDT detected
stress, it could lower the difficulty level of the serious-game or increase the assistance
from the upper-limb exoskeleton. Likewise, if the HDT detected fatigue, the system could
suggest that the patient should take a break from the activities which would help to prevent
the negative consequences of high cognitive load. Furthermore, if the HDT detected a
negative emotional state or a neutral state over a longer period, the system could offer
words of encouragement, praise the patient’s efforts or suggest that the patient changes
the current activity. The models could also be used for letting the therapist or clinician
know the patient’s current cognitive and emotional states.
Fatigue experiments
In this thesis project, reaction times were measured in the fatigue experiments by making
the participant react to a specific noise by pressing a button. No reaction time increases
could be registered, and therefore the fatigue level could not be measured using this
approach. In future studies, it could be beneficial to investigate how to establish the fatigue
level in real time.
Datasets and models
The biggest challenge of this thesis was to acquire enough data for making the machine
learning models, without creating noisy datasets. Therefore, future work could focus on
making larger stress, fatigue and emotion datasets while taking care not to create un-
wanted noise in the data. The experiment datasets from this project could contribute to
this task.
Three separate models for detecting the different cognitive states were made in this thesis.
However, both the cognitive load and fatigue models are sensitive to heart rate fluctua-
tions. For this reason, some stress experiences might be wrongly classified as fatigue
and the other way around. As mentioned in the Theory section, cognitive stress and fa-
tigue both stem from increasing levels of cognitive load. Therefore, it could be interesting
to create a single model that could detect both stress and fatigue.

B.1
Valence and arousal
The valence and arousal dimensions were originally presumed to be independent of each
other, meaning that the degree of valence is not dependent on the level of arousal and vice
versa [50] [51]. However, some have argued that valence and arousal levels are indeed
dependent [242]. Kuppens et al. have found that a symmetric V-shape best describes the
relation between the two dimensions. In other words, emotions that are either strongly
pleasant or unpleasant are accompanied by high levels of activation [243]. While Kuppens
et al. determined that the V-shape was universal in the people they investigated, they also
discovered that the steepness of the V-shape was dependant on the home country of the
subject and on personality factors such as introversion/extroversion. The two different
relationships between valence and arousal, i.e. the independent and the V-shaped one,
are pictured in figure B.1.
Figure B.1: Two different proposed relationships between valence and arousal. Left: an inde-
pendent relationship. Right: a V-shaped relationship. Made with inspiration from [243].
B.2
Basic Emotions vs Valence and Arousal
One of the largest critiques of the basic-emotion view is that it cannot adequately explain
the variability of emotions since any particular emotion, for instance sadness, varies sig-
nificantly in how it looks and feels across individuals, cultures and time [244]. And hence,
the idea that each basic emotion stems from a unique neural pathway can be questioned
[176]. While the basic emotion and valence-arousal models represent emotions in very
different ways, they can both be useful. In a survey from 2016, Ekman finds that the
majority of scientists that study emotion are of the opinion that both the discrete and di-
mensional views are useful for their research [49]. Furthermore, Liu et al. have attempted
to consolidate the two models by trying to correlate facial expressions of basic emotions
with expressions of valence and arousal. They found that each of the basic emotions
expressions consisted of specific and separable combinations of valence and arousal re-
lated facial movements [245]. This suggests a connection between emotional categories
and dimensions.

D.1
EMFACS
The FACS method is purely descriptive, hence it does not include theories of the possible
emotions behind facial behaviours. For coupling the objective description of facial move-
ments with specific emotions, different methods that build on top of FACS have been de-
veloped, such as Emotional FACS (EMFACS) [248]. EMFACS is an application of FACS
scoring which only considers behaviours that have emotional significance and includes
an emotional dictionary which can be used to classify facial behavior into emotions [248].
This is done by mapping combinations of AU’s to the basic emotion categories by using a
limited set of rules. For example, happy is characterized as a display of the AU’s 6 and 12
(as can be seen in table D.1 these are the Cheek raiser and Lip corner puller respectively)
or only AU 12 [247].

H.2
Order of experiment sessions
The stress session came first because we wanted the participant to be as alert and awake
as possible during this session. And the fatigue session was put last to make sure that the
emotions and stress sessions were not influenced by fatigue. One of the downsides of this
particular order is that the emotion session could have been influenced by the stressful
tasks. Furthermore, the initial fatigue level at the start of the fatigue session is probably
higher than it would have been without the previous stress and emotion sessions.
H.3
N-back and Stroop tasks
N-back task
In an N-back task, different items are displayed one after the other. These can either be
letters, numbers or pictograms to name some. The objective at any given time is to recall
whether the currently displayed item was also displayed N-times ago, hence the name.
In this project, both a 2-back task and a 3-back task are used. This was done, since the
2-back task might be too easy for some participants, and likewise the 3-back task could
be too difficult. The 2-back task version used that was considered to be included in this
project is taken from Psytoolkit.org [254] [220] [221]. In this version, the displayed items
are letters, and the participant presses the ’m’ button on the keyboard if he/she believes
they saw the same item two times ago. The 3-back task would be taken from [255] and
uses pictograms.

Stroop task
The Stroop test is a widely used task for inducing both cognitive stress and fatigue. The
version that would have been used in this experiment is taken from Psytoolkit.org [256]
[220] [221]. In this version, the participant has to respond to the print color of a word
and press either button ’r’,’g’,’b’ or ’y’ for red, green, blue and yellow respectively. This
is repeated for a set period. The task is complicated by the fact that the meaning of the
word itself is also color. Most of the time, the print color and the meaning of the word
are not the same. Figure H.1 shows three examples of words that can appear during the
Stroop task. When the top word in figure H.1 appears, the correct answer is red, since
the printed color is red. For the middle and bottom words, the correct answers are green
and and yellow, respectively.

I.4
Labelling fatigue datasets using reaction time
We planned to label the fatigue data from our own experiments using reaction time. As
mentioned in the Method section (4.5.3) this was dropped due to the fact that we did not
observe increasing reaction times over the course of the fatigue sessions. But the plan
Cognitive State Prediction in Rehabilitation of Stroke Patients
99
==PAGE:112==
we would have ideally used with reaction times is described here:
The first reaction time measurement will be used as a baseline value. For the rest of
the fatigue session, the increase/decrease in the reaction time compared to the initial
reaction time measurement will be used to label the observations. As mentioned in the
State of Research section (3.2), previous research have found that cognitive fatigue leads
to different relative and absolute increases in reaction times. Hower, the majority of studies
that we found had measured absolute increases of 20-30 ms. For this reason, we choose
to label our own observations as follows:
• Absolute increase in reaction time of [0-5[ ms: No fatigue
• Absolute increasein reaction time of [5-15[ ms: Pre-fatigue
• Absolute increase in reaction time of [15-30[ ms: Fatigue

J.1
Emotion prediction modelling process
Figure J.1: The process for making the emotion prediction models. First, two sets of classification
models are trained on the data stemming from previous research projects (two blue datasets),
called Emotion Modelset 1 and Emotion Modelset 2. The models in Emotion Modelset 1 are
then further incrementally trained and tested on the experiment data stemming from this project.
Concurrently, the models in Emotion Modelset 3 are trained on the experiment data from this
project. Lastly, Emotion Modelset 4 consists of models trained and tested on the combined Full
Data consisting of both Emotion Dataset 1 and the experiment data.
