Abstract
The purpose of this Project is based on extended research of the physics,
governing the glorious topic of outdoor sound propagation. Mathematical tools
that are designed to solve numerical computational problems will be presented.
Its focus is on the influence of weather conditions on a monopole’s sound propa-
gation. An accurate transfer function of the atmosphere would be characterized
as an impossible mission before the collaboration of acoustic engineers and me-
teorologists. The steps that have been followed were to study and to evaluate
the performance of the models after using different simulation based scenar-
ios. Moreover, the methods designed and employed proved that the initially
assumptions made (i.e Research Objectives) end up being true, with the results
presented later in this report describing how and why this statement applies.
Finally, all settings are explained in detail and in accurate manner, in this Mas-
ter Thesis report, especially for the new students that will feel as enthusiastic
as the author, regarding this excelsior subject under research.
Last but not
least, the contribution of this project is the provision of the implemented sound
propagation model as online repositories.

1.3
Brief comparison between Harmonoise and
Nord2000
Both of them predict the sound pressure level at the receiver position in 1/3 octave
bands. The two models treat the influence of the atmospheric refraction in radically
different ways. Although Nord2000 replaces the straight rays in the model with curved
rays for a homogeneous atmosphere, thus simulating the real refraction phenomenon,
Harmonoise uses straight rays but curves the ground to simulate the same effect using
conformal mapping. Additionally the curvature is structured differently. Harmonoise
calculates the curvature radius based on the maximum curve height, while Nord2000
uses a much more complicated approach to measure the curve length and its angle
relative to the ground.
Harmonoise and Nord2000 also introduce a coherence factor, which relies on a number
of elements to compensate for incoherent and averaging effects. Harmonoise’s total
coherence factor denotes the loss of coherence due to frequency band averaging and
turbulence. As in Harmonoise, Nord2000’s coherence factor accounts for effects from
frequency band averaging and turbulence, but also effects resulting from fluctuating
refraction, surface roughness and scattering areas.
Both models use the term Fresnel-zones to measure the influence of the terrain prop-
erties on the reflected sound. This is an approximate method used to estimate the
propagation of sound over flat terrain, with various surface properties. It is assumed
that the sound field at the receiver is determined by the surface conditions in a region
around the point of reflection, the Fresnel-zone.

Nord2000 and Harmonoise have been There are quite a few numerical methods that

pensation approaches have been investigated in [6] to quantify the underestimation
of the atmosphere’s influence, observed between full and scale models.

2.1
Acoustics in Atmospheric Surface Layer
The Atmospheric Surface Layer (ASL) is the bottomost 10% of the Atmospheric
Boundary Layer (ABL). The surface layer extends over 50–100 m under typical day-
time conditions while during nocturnal conditions its thickness decreases. The heat
flux from heated ground to the cold air causes convection and as a result high turbu-
lence intensity is yielded. Environmental phenomena that occur over a wide range
of temporal and spatial scales influence outdoor sound waves. Turbulent eddies and
gravity waves range from centimeters to kilometers in the reduced atmosphere, which
scatter sound. The propagation is also affected by weather phenomena on much big-
ger scales. When the wind is directed from the source to receiver, meaning that the
wind direction is in favor of the sound propagation, then the sound propagation is
characterized as downwind. On the contrary upwind sound propagation is against
the wind.
Atmospheric conditions are described by neutrality when there is balance between
the upward and downward heat flux between the ground surface and the atmospheric
boundary layer. This phenomenon happens for a short duration during the sunset
and the sunrise. When the propagation is considered as short range (distances below
3km), the surface layer (layer of the atmosphere between 50 and 100 m) will have
an effect on the propagation of sound. Usually the mean wind direction in this layer
exhibits short fluctuations [7]. The compressions and expansions occurring on sound
waves in free air are defined as adiabatic processes. This ensures that heat energy is
not a contributing factor and adjustments are made to the air temperature. That is
because air itself is a fairly weak thermal conductor. If air were a strong conductor
of thermal energy, the cycle would become isothermal. [8]

2.2.1
Geometric Spreading
The sound intensity reduces as the surface of the wavefront expands, regarding the
travelling wave that originates from a source with a radius that increases in space
and time. If a point source is to be considered then the spreading is spherical and
the decrease of the sound level exhibits a slope of 6 dB per doubling of the distance.
The resulting attenuation depends on the range of propagation, but is independent
of the frequency.

Influence of the ground
For typical outdoor sound propagation cases, with the source and the receiver close
to the ground, there is a complex interaction of the incident sound waves. Reflection
coefficient for a plane wave on a locally reacting surface. Insert equation The addition
of a ground surface bounds the atmosphere. The difference from the free field sound
pressure level may be represented by the quantity ∆L. In the frequencies below 500
Hz, the properties related to an acoustics point of view play a substantial role. Thus
importance has to be focused on the particular ground of interest. A vast amount of
ground models have been developed over the years.The most straightforward of these
models is the one where only one parameter is used, which is named the effective flow
resistivity. According to [9] these models are inferior than the physically admissible
models. The reason for that Ground surfaces that have low porosity are characterized
as acoustically hard, according to ISO 9613-2 [10]. Any kind of vegetation like grass or
trees on the other hand may be considered acoustically as soft. Although this might
be an adequate representation in some circumstances, it is an oversimplification of the
considerable range of properties and resulting effects. For example, different types of
grassland (i.e long or short) can yield significantly different ground effects. Ordinary
values regarding the roughness length for grass covered surfaces is between 0.001m
and 0.1m. The Delany-Bazley equations cannot be applied to all types of porous
materials, and won’t work well if the frequency is too low.
Simple relaxation models are also used and for a detailed analysis the reader may
refer to [11].
When the ground is not even, a combination of plane domains connected succesively
may be employed.
The estimation of the impedance of fibrous absorbing materi-
als with the empirical model of Delany and Bazley (used broadly in the field of
Architectural Acoustics) is also valid when the ground of interest may be character-
ized as natural (i.e grassland). Among others Attenborough and Zwikker & Kosten
have presented to the scientific community theoretical models to calculate the ground
impedance.

Different methods for outdoor sound propagation modelling in complex environments

field in which the sound field is calculated at discrete intervals along the propagation
route. Inside the vast literature regarding the subject of outdoor sound propagation

3.3
Absorption Layer
The concept of placing an absorbing layer at the top of the computation grid comes
from the fact that the PE was initially designed as an underwater propagation code
where the ocean bottom exhibits absorptive characteristics so that the rays going
downward will gradually attenuate. In the case of the atmosphere it is important to
guarantee that the reflections from the top of the computation space are vanished
accordingly. The size (or height) of this layer changes according the frequency and
the meteorological phenomena that are present at the particular case. The height of
the layer should be 50 times the wavelength of the simulated frequency.

3.9
State of the art
Popular methods have been coupled to the parabolic method as shown in 3.2 in an
attempt to characterize the field of interest under the presence of complex terrain but
not neglecting the environmental influence. The transition point between PE and the
full wave based methods depends on the particular application. A noise engineering
method and the 2.5D boundary element method has been developed to overcome the
burden of complex objects [34]. A three-dimensional PE model for low frequency
sound propagation in irregular urban canyons has also been implemented, with the
computational cost kept at low levels [35]. One of the most recent advancements is
the extended pseudospectral time domain methods [36] where the linearized Euler
equations are solved. The developed technique is much faster than the 2nd accurate
FDTD solution. An optimized six-stage Runge-Kutta method is employed for the
time iteration. When complex meteorology and straight rigid boundary surfaces are
present in the investigated case this method seems ideal. The software is built using
the open source components, Blender, Numpy and Python. The most recent paper
that makes clear that parabolic equation formulations still emerge, may be found in
[37].

Discussion
It was assumed prior to the derivation of the results that the outputs of the PE
formulations and the FFP should give negligible differences. Sometimes what is im-
portant is not the number of samples but how that compares to the complexity of
the phenomenon. It is crucial to notice where ray tracings occur, the FFP method
and likewise every linear method, produces unreliable results. The imperfection of
the sensors becomes evident by the differences in wind speed between the plastic cup
anemometer and the sonic corresponding one.
In the results section one of the most important parameters that was affecting dra-
matically the performance of the numerical models were the absorption layer char-
acteristics. Both the thickness and the height affected the model’s behavior. The
techniques described in [30] for improved results provoked slight difference. It is very
possible due to the fact that in this particular paper the remedies had to deal with
specific frequencies (10, 100, 1000 Hz). An enclosed loudspeaker may be considered
as a dipole [10] while all these models assume a monopole source. The fact that the
propagation is in the line of sight makes though the analysis realistic. Regarding the
ground during the days the experimental measurements were carried out, long grass
was evident and the surface was extremely wet because of previous rainfalls.
For the ground measurement the technique described in [39] that suits to long
range experiments was attempted but no meaningful results were obtained. Reasons
for that may be due to the atmospheric conditions during that particular set of mea-
surement. The thesis of my colleague Giulio Fauri (fauri2019a) that we collaborated
during the first year of my master studies for the Roskilde course covered the topic
of ground impedance measurements in the same field although with the aim being
in higher frequencies. To estimate the ground’s acoustic impedance, sound pressure
levels had to be measured also in viscinity of the source, where the meteorological
effects would be regarded as negligible. This means that the technique described in
[39] can be applied and the results may be compared to the results of his work. At
short time scales, the stochastic turbulence has noticeable impact (at least for the
higher frequencies) so these measurements could be revealing as far as the frequency
threshold where turbulence starts to have a drastic impact. In the literature there is
an ambiguity but this is most commonly mentioned to be around 80-100 Hz which
is right in the middle part of the subwoofer’s bandwidth. The turbulence scale is
usually mentioned in the bibliography as correlation length.
Mean relative sound pressure levels over 10 min can be determined over the same

5.1
Extension to a realistic concert scenario
An arbitrary waveform like a random music signal could not be the desirable excitation
signal for numerical methods like the ones described in this report.
If an entire
sound reinforcement system would be analyzed, not only one source in the ground
is to be modelled.
Multiple line arrays are installed to cover the audience.
This
automatically means that the interference between the sources changes a lot the
resulting phase response over the area of interest. The setup that could be realized
in order to quantify the atmospheric influence on the outdoor wave propagation is
the installation of vertical microphone arrays to identify the decay of the surface
wave over height with corresponding weather sensors installed in a moderately large
distance of the stage. One interesting location to setup weather sensors would be
in locations where the physical characteristics of the wave-fronts regarding the flown
arrays change.
These distances change according to the size of the line array for
each frequency but regarding the low frequency spectrum these distances are very
close to the sources. This implies that tethered weather balloons could be used in a
practical way so that the sampling of the atmosphere can be realized in more than
one locations. If this concert would last more than one days then every day ground
impedance measurements have to take place in order to observe any changes in the
ground impedance due to the change in terms of porosity or the grain shape factor.
Regarding the GFPE model it is considered to be of great importance because of the
large scalability that can be introduced. If a PE model would be accurate enough
which according to the author can be only when implemented as described in [33]
then even a neural network can automatically predict the magnitude response of the
system under investigation extending to higher frequencies and applying turbulence
which has detrimental consequences in distinct downward refraction cases. Since the
model is two dimensional, horizontal subwoofer arrays cannot be computed in a simple
manner. But a vertically subwoofer array can be computed. Essentially, what changes
is the radiation pattern which is a direct consequence of the directivity that makes its
appearance due to the change of the total size. When a vertical array is used, then
the size of the array in the vertical domain is big compared to the size of the array
in the horizontal domain. Holding that in our minds, the directivity pattern will
be narrow in the vertical domain and broad in the horizontal domain. On the other
hand when a horizontal subwoofer array is used, then the in the horizontal domain the
directivty pattern will be narrow and vice versa in the vertical domain. This exactly
is the reason why the sound reinforcement business applies beaforming techniques in
==PAGE:67==
5.2 Meteorological Classification
53
the horizontal arrays (by using incremental delay towards the ends of the array) to
broaden the desired coverage. It has to be mentioned for the reader to visualize it
that when delay is applied to an array the acoustic center of the arrangement moves
backwards and this should be taken into consideration for every subsequent analysis.
Additionally, an inattentive person would hypothesize that flown arrays are com-
posed by steady sources, fixed in space. Nevertheless, the truth is that they still move,
introducing additional errors to the subsequent analysis. In case the sound reinforce-
ment system is installed inside an urban area, then the surrounding buildings along
with the low frequency noise produced by the tires of cars (that can be interpreted
as a line source) will contaminate measurements and the frequency responses will
be contaminated with additional contributions that need to be examined thoroughly.
This analysis applies only to flat terrains as previously mentioned. For example in
the area where Roskilde festival is held a short hill is present and a GTPE model
would be more appropriate. Even this small sized hill in addition to the presence of
the people has an influence in the low frequencies response.
Last but not least, the influence of the people cannot be safely considered as
negligible. According to [40] the decline of the Sound Pressure Level (SPL) with the
distance is lower when the sound venue is occupied by the audience than in the free
field case and the effect tends to increase along with the crowd’s density. In that
particular survey people were modelled as BEM cylinders. According to the authors
the interference pattern revealed by their simulations may be due to reflections from
the rear “boundary” crowd surface or from a surface wave propagating on the listeners.
Weather measurements were not conducted though to support their point of view but
the author tends to agree with their way of thinking.
Although the presented methods above are operating in the frequency domain,
the analysis of low frequency - long duration events may be benefited by using low
frequency (high scale) wavelets that are long duration functions and the time fre-
quency resolution may be adjusted as needed. A crossover filter would change the
phase response and introduce additional errors in a full scale situation.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions and
Future Work
In this Project, the complexity of the studied topic was revealed from a spherical
point of view. This report aimed to offer a condensed description of well established
techniques that still are in common use in various formulations. Special effort and fo-
cus has been given to develop a GFPE model that would match the widely established
CNPE model. Three test case scenarios were chosen for a long distance propagation
and the results obtained seemed satisfactory for a logarithmic sound speed profile.
Since this profile applies only for neutral atmospheric conditions, before the compari-
son with real measurements, a logarithmic- linear profile has also been introduced to
describe the sound speed profile. The CNPE algorithm produced results that tried to
mimic the atmospheric behavior in a more accurate way than the GFPE correspond-
ing one. This was expected since in the literature the GFPE is usually preferred for
its speed performance and the CNPE is mainly used as a reference. Nevertheless
these two should give negligible differences so it is concluded that the GFPE model
needs additional tuning and the results for the GFPE model did not seem to follow
the reality behind the data measurements. Nevertheless since the main concern of
this study was to see if these PE models can be used for sound field control purposes,
their characteristic of the underlying monochromatic behaviour demands additional
attention to the plethora of parameters involved.
