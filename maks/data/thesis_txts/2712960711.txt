Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate what challenges organizations need to be
aware of when adopting different technologies for ocean data and resource management. The
reason for this research is to provide possible solutions in using innovative technologies that
can potentially present a practical strategy in achieving the SDG 14 targets that promote the
conservation and sustainable use of ocean resources, and improve overall ocean prosperity.
This study designed a framework based on a combination of the Technology Ac-
ceptance Model (TAM) and the Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) frameworks,
where challenge areas identified through a systematic literature review (SLR) in technology
adoption within organizations were implemented. The framework is then evaluated by ex-
pert interviews that work in various organizations associated with ocean data and marine
resources to correlate challenges from the literature and additional challenges from experts’
experiences within that specific industry. As a result, the framework is updated according
to these findings and future research is recommended.
Blockchain Technology (BCT), Internet of Things (IoT), Cloud Computing (CC)
and Artificial Intelligence (AI) were identified as the main innovative technologies from the
SLR and interview results that contributed to the sustainable management of ocean data
and resource management.
In order to facilitate the successful adoption and utilization
of these technologies within organizations, multi-stakeholder collaborations were identified
as a fundamental requirement for technology interoperability, as it enhances sustainable
development and management amongst organizations.
The resulting framework provides an overview of challenges and possible solutions
with technology adoption for organizations involved with management of ocean data and
marine resources. Lastly, the framework can be used as a foundation for future researchers
to study the challenges of innovative technology adoption in different types of industries,
especially organizations in ocean data and marine resources.

Introduction
1
Introduction
More than 70% of the Earth’s surface is covered by the oceans.
They play an
essential role in human and planetary health, in climate and food security, in local jobs as
well as in the global economy. However, the health of the oceans are threatened due to the
numerous different pressures that add up and contribute to rapid and unpredictable changes
in ocean ecosystems. The United Nation’s Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 14: Life
Below Water provide specific targets, which generally aim to conserve and sustainably use
the oceans, seas and marine resources for sustainable development (United Nations, 2017).
There are, however, as of yet no strategies established that focus on achieving any of these
targets. This means that countries and organizations lack the tools necessary to help the
implementation process and track the progress by collecting, analyzing and distributing data
between multiple stakeholders. Adopting and using innovative technologies that have the
potential to improve the sustainable management of ocean data and marine resources will
consequently enhance the conservation and sustainable use of ocean resources. By integrating
such technologies, they can help change the current downward trajectory of ocean health,
producing positive outcomes for both people and nature, and help the world achieve all of
the SDGs, not only SDG 14.
This project aims to explore the challenges of adopting various innovative tech-
nologies by organizations involved with ocean data and marine resources, specifically by
investigating what challenges organizations need to be aware of when implementing such
technologies. Currently, there is no existing literature that researches technology adoption
challenges particularly for ocean data and resource management in organizations.
Therefore, this study’s objective is to provide an overview of various technology
adoption challenges in organizations by conducting a systematic literature review that iden-
tifies the challenge areas within different innovative technology adoption. Once these chal-
lenges are identified, they are correlated to a framework that utilizes a combination of the
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE)
frameworks. The challenge areas and framework are evaluated based on interviews with
experts that work in different types of organizations associated with ocean data and marine
resources. As a result of the interviews, the framework is then updated with insights of
challenge areas found both in the literature and current organizations related to ocean data
and marine resources.
The intention of this project is to not only function as an overview of technology
adoption challenges for organizations involved with ocean data and marine resources, but
to provide possible solutions in using innovative technologies that potentially can improve
ocean data and resource management within organizations.
Thus, proposing a practical
strategy that promotes protecting and sustainably using ocean resources, which would be
one step closer in achieving the SDG 14 targets and overall ocean prosperity.

2.1
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
The United Nations’ (UN) 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development was adopted in
September 2015 and constructed by 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). They were
established with the aim of ending poverty, protecting the planet and ensuring prosperity for
all (United Nations, 2017), and cover all aspects of sustainable development, including ending
poverty and hunger, ensuring quality education and gender equality, reducing inequalities
and ensuring sustainable business practices and protecting the environment (Yiu & Saner,
2014; Saner, Yiu & Nguyen, 2020). They aim to empower people to understand the world
in a fundamentally new way, and invite people to work together and bridge the complex
differences that define human life.

goals reflect a common concern for the people (Goals 1-5), the planet (Goals 6, 12-15),
prosperity (Goals 7-11), peace (Goal 16) and partnerships (Goal 17). As part of the agenda,

type of data should be collected?
As a first step towards achieving the SDGs, a total of 169 targets were identified and
approved for each corresponding goal. These targets were then further subdivided into a set
of 232 quantified indicators for data collection purposes, and time-bound to be achieved by
2030 (United Nations Sustainable Development Solutions Network, 2014; Saner et al., 2020).
There is as of yet no consensus within the UN on how to organize the monitoring
and review of the target indicators, which includes the methodologies that should be used.
The term ‘review’ was chosen by the UN instead of ‘evaluation’ to avoid potential concerns
and resistance from the countries involved. This partially explains the lack of commitment
to a more strict definition of the monitoring and reviewing methodologies in the United
Nations General Assembly in the Resolution 70/1, titled “Transforming our world: the 2030
Agenda for Sustainable Development”. Reviewing, monitoring, and reporting have all been
mentioned as potential methodologies. However, in many cases monitoring is turned into
a mini-review, rather than a continuous process of assessing implementation, as it is not
intended to be only used for data collection in reviews (Saner et al., 2020).
On the other hand, evaluation is a useful practice in itself as it seeks to provide deeper
engagement. For evaluation to be truly effective, it needs to be complemented by a monitor-
ing process that includes both micro- and macro-monitoring practices. A combination of the
two using various sources of data that integrate the use of new Information, Communication
and Technology (ICT) methods and tools, would improve the ability to analyse data and
provide statistical analysis. These methods and tools should then be re-assessed as innov-
ations in data-collection techniques, analytical tools and data storage continuously evolve.
Providing countries with the appropriate ICT tools for rigorous monitoring and evaluation,
will allow them to conduct more comprehensive analysis of their data, and thereby improve
their sustainable development work. This will help countries track their implementation
progress based on the target indicators of the SDGs. Furthermore, implementing a more
modern, technological infrastructure for collecting, analyzing and distributing data between
multiple relevant stakeholders should be part of the approach for each country.
As the process of implementation progresses, it is certain that a common and stand-
ard communication template is required in order to compare and share data across local,
national and international boundaries. The scope and systemic nature of the 2030 Agenda
and the urgency of its challenges need to be addressed. This requires an extensive variation of
ICT tools and science-based analysis, in order to navigate the complexity of it (International
Council for Science, 2019). Furthermore, there should be a clear strategy to determine how
each country can be held accountable in regard to their implementation of the SDGs (Saner
et al., 2020).
In conclusion, tracking the progress towards implementing and achieving the SDGs is
an important but difficult task. The SDGs are interrelated, however it should be suggested
that each goal or target may require its own specific type of monitoring and evaluation,
beyond what is currently defined by the UN (United Nations, 2017). This means that more
work would be required by the international community (International Council for Science,
2019; Saner et al., 2020).

2.1.1
SDG 14 Assessment & Other SDGs
Oceans cover more than 70% of the Earth’s surface and contribute to humanity in a
variety of different ways (see figure 13 in appendix A.1 for a more detailed view). They play
an important role in Earth’s resilience and provision of essential ecosystem services. The
state of the ocean and several of its resources and functions have deteriorated over the past
century. Oceans, seas and coastal areas are exposed to the effects of pollution, overfishing
and exploitation, and impacts of climate change such as warming, coastal erosion, sea-level
rise, ocean acidification and oxygen depletion (Schmidt et al., 2017).
SDG 14: Life Below Water is focused on the interaction of humans with the ocean,
seas, and marine resources, which is supported by 7 targets associated with their conser-
vation, sustainable use, capacity building and ocean governance. Marine resources include
materials and attributes found in the ocean that are considered to have intrinsic value. These
include biological diversity, fish and seafood supplies, oil and gas, minerals, sand and gravel,
renewable energy resources, tourism potential, and unique ecosystems such as coral reefs,
amongst many other valuable resources. The way these resources are managed and used
is therefore of great importance. Ocean data is an fundamental element that needs to be
collected, analysed and distributed on a global scale, in order to sustainably manage and use
marine resources. Ocean data points refers to quantifiable information and data that can
be gathered from marine resources. Therefore, improving management of ocean data will
inevitably enhance the conservation and appropriate use of marine resources, which is a step
closer in achieving the SDG 14 goal.
The targets mentioned below are aimed at an urgent need to transform human
behaviour toward sustainable practices when exploiting marine resources, in addition to
taking action in preserving productive and resilient oceans.
The 2030 Agenda puts use
and conservation of the ocean and its resources into the wider sustainable development
context for the first time. Generally, the ocean and SDG 14 in particular have a converging
role in the 2030 Agenda, where SDG 14 interacts with all other 16 SDGs.
The nature
and intensity of these interactions is highly context-specific and differs across the SDGs and
their associated targets (Schmidt et al., 2017). This is seen to be necessary in order to devise
integrated strategies and policies that foster synergies across different goals and explicitly
address tensions and potential trade-offs amongst them (Le Blanc et al., 2017).
Below are the seven target goals of SDG 14: Life Below Water, ‘Conserve and
sustainably use the ocean, seas and marine resources for sustainable development’:
1. By 2025, prevent and significantly reduce marine pollution of all kinds, in particular
from land-based activities, including marine debris and nutrient pollution.
2. By 2020, sustainably manage and protect marine and coastal ecosystems to avoid
significant adverse impacts, including by strengthening their resilience, and take action
for their restoration in order to achieve healthy and productive oceans
3. Minimize and address the impacts of ocean acidification, including through enhanced
scientific cooperation at all levels

exceptional since they depart significantly from previous practices and/or have a more pro-

2.2.1
Incremental Technological Innovation
Incremental innovations are a set of small improvements or upgrades to a company’s
existing products, services, or technological systems. These improvements do not bring a new
product to market, yet offer improvements to the product which help further differentiate it
from the competition while building on current offerings. The aim is to continuously improve
on existing products, knowledge, techniques, technologies, or processes within the same
market (see figure 3). This is triggered either by internal interests, such as the company’s
own desire for optimization or by external influences, such as changes in legislation that have
to be met (Elia, 2020).

2.2

Background
Incremental innovation (also known as sustaining innovation) is one of the most
common forms of innovation observed. Most ideas and innovations lie within the field of
incremental innovation compared to disruptive or radical innovation, which are less common.
An example of incremental innovation would be the iPhone created by Apple. Since
2007, neither the form nor the function of the iPhone has changed, yet upgrades such as
Face ID, camera improvements, and retina display have improved the existing product. This
means that a 15 year old innovation still remains as a cutting-edge luxury smartphone that
it was at its first inception, by updating its features instead of altering the original product
(Elia, 2020). Other real-life examples of incremental innovation include Gillette, Coca-Cola,
and Cadbury.
2.2.2
Disruptive Technological Innovation
Disruptive innovation refers to a process in which new competitive technologies suc-
cessfully challenge incumbent firms, often in spite of inferior resources.
It is when new
technologies and products are created to serve an existing market (seen in figure 3). This
type of innovation is enabled by new technology that provides a more efficient and accessible
alternative to what already exists (Dieffenbacher, 2022). Businesses apply disruptive innov-
ation to serve the evolving needs of their consumer base, creating entirely new value streams
and service offerings that did not exist before. Contrary to the lore of disruption, it is not
solely about a single genius idea and its technology, but more often it is a process that sits
at the intersection of technology and business model innovation (Elia, 2020).
A perfect example of disruptive innovation can be seen in the introduction of music
streaming platforms such as Spotify or Apple Music, which replaced compact discs (CDs) us-
ing advanced technological innovations within internet streaming services. Netflix is another
great example. With new technology that made streaming accessible to a broader market,
they were able to grow their business by offering thousands of movies and TV shows to
stream for a fraction of the price of a trip to the movie theater (Elia, 2020). Other examples
include Amazon that utilized the existing market for books but changed the way they were
sold, delivered, and experienced.
2.2.3
Architectural Technological Innovation
Architectural innovation is the modification of existing solutions and technologies
directed for an entirely new market (seen in figure 3) (Dieffenbacher, 2022). This type of
innovation re-configures the overall design of a system or the way its components interact
from various processes and previously existing technology and apply them in a different
manner. Thus, components that may not be innovative alone or as part of another product
or system can be combined into a new product, service, or system that is innovative. A key
component of architectural innovations is that they destroy the usefulness of a company’s
architectural knowledge, but preserve the usefulness of their individual components. For
example, a physically distinctive portion of a certain technology, which represents an essential
design concept and performs a specific function is reaped of its physical components, in order
to create a brand new concept as part of a modernized version of an innovative technology

(Elia, 2020).
An example of architectural innovation would be memory foam which originally
came from NASA aiming to fix the safety of aircraft cushions with a new type of foam that
would spring back slowly. This was initially marketed as medical equipment before mattress
manufacturers repurposed it so that memory foam would be used in bedrooms all over the
world. An additional example is multi-core processors which now power every computer,
tablet, and smart phone. Chip makers ran into roadblocks with single-core processors be-

to redesign the chip, these manufactures reconfigured their circuits so that more than two
chips could operate and connect to each other within the same circuit. This ensured that
computing devices would have the processing power needed to handle maximal complexity
(Elia, 2020). Architectural innovations are successful because they are a low-risk high-reward
strategy. They have tremendous power to transform markets since they allow organizations
to dismantle what does not work and transform what does work into a commodity that has
the potential to change aspects of people’s lives.
2.2.4
Radical Technological Innovation
Radical innovation is spurred by the creation of new knowledge and the commercial-
ization of novel ideas, products, or services that usually replace an existing idea, product, or
service. This type of innovation is rare and relies on organizational capabilities and human
capital considering that it focuses on displacing existing technologies, creating new product
categories, or changing the relationship between consumers, businesses, and suppliers. These
types of breakthroughs rely on both human and organizational ingenuity which thrive when
there is a productive and symbiotic relationship between innovators and their environments
(Elia, 2020). Radical innovation can be summarized as a combination of new, revolution-
ary technology and new ways of conducting certain processes and businesses, that have the
potential to transform entire markets (seen in figure 3).
Radical innovation is a high-risk strategy. It not only relies on concocting unique
technology but also depends on that technology being such a remarkable force that it will
forever change consumer behavior, create a new status-quo, and obliterate existing markets.
A change of this magnitude never happens on a whim and even the best-laid plans for
this radical change can fail. Thus, the level of risk involved in radical innovation demands
a proportional level of caution.
However, with the proper amount of courage, patience,
and vision, these innovations will reap the greatest reward. The best example of radical
innovation was the invention of the airplane. This radical new technology opened up a new
form of travel, invented an industry and a whole new market.

in using a technology. These two constructs are prime determinants of technology use and
user acceptance, which the author validates through his research.

Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE)
The Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) framework was originally pro-
posed by Depietro, Wiarda and Fleischer (1990). The framework analyzes the process by
which an organization adopts and implements technological innovations influenced by tech-
nological, organizational, and environmental contexts. The technological context refers to
the internal and external technologies such as equipment and processes that are relevant and
available to an organization. The organizational context includes the characteristics and
resources of an organization such as company size, degree of centralization, degree of form-

Methodology
In order to examine the factors influencing the adoption and use of innovative tech-
nologies in organizations for ocean data and resource management, a combined framework
of TAM and TOE was implemented as described in the previous section. Based on the
findings of other researchers who have studied technology acceptance, a similar approach of
the TAM and TOE frameworks was used as the foundation of the various factors influen-
cing technology acceptance. TAM was used as the basis of the framework, where external
factors affecting perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use were classified in terms of
technological, organizational and environmental contexts, which originates from the TOE
framework. Hence, the first step was to systematically review the literature to identify the
factors influencing the adoption of innovative technologies.
The following subsections present the systematic approach applied to this study to
ensure valid and reliable results that address the aim of the project, in addition to, the
designed framework, interviews & evaluation, and results & discussion.
3.1
Systematic Literature Review Process
A systematic literature review (SLR) was conducted with the primary objective of
summarizing challenges and solutions that can be identified in the literature. Therefore, the
review process is concentrated on addressing the following questions:
• What type of challenges have been identified when adopting innovative technologies
within organizations?
• What kind of solutions can solve these challenges?
In recent years, the most commonly used systematic review framework in manage-
ment studies has been PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and
Meta-Analyses), which was initially developed by Moher et al. (2014). PRISMA is a tool
used as a set of methods to systematically search papers and literature for review based
studies. A checklist was utilized for the literature review approach of this study, which can
be found on PRISMA’s official website. It is based on the formulated inclusion and exclu-
sion criteria for any type of study that systematically assesses the quality of chosen papers
(Majumder, 2019).

3.1.3
Screening Process
Once the studies were collected based on the search terms and strategy proposed,
a screening process was performed to remove any studies that did not satisfy the inclusion
criteria. The first screening sequence excluded all studies irrelevant to innovative technologies
by examining the titles, abstracts, and keywords. However, other parts of certain studies
were inspected closer, in order to review whether or not they would be approved or rejected
as other selection criteria that contributed in addressing the research questions.
The second screening sequence excluded studies that did not include sustainable
data management as the main focus of the study. For example, if a study mentioned that
improvement in data management is a contributing factor in breach defences of a company’s
data infrastructure, but does not research or explore how to improve data management,
then the study was omitted. As part of this screening sequence, titles, abstracts, keywords,
introductions, and conclusions were reviewed.
The final screening sequence excluded studies that did not include a technology
adoption in an organization. The search term (“organization”) did not limit the resulting
studies, since it would include various types of organizations such as companies, businesses,
startups, agencies, associations, university institutions etc. The purpose of this study is to
examine the challenges and solutions in adopting innovative technologies within organizations
regardless of the type of industry or size. This final sequence analyzed the same parts of
studies mentioned above.

3.2
Framework Design
As mentioned in section 2.3, some studies used a combined framework of TAM and
TOE that evaluated technology adoption. This hybrid form of TAM and TOE can easily be
adapted to most circumstances involving technology adoption. Thus, the same approach was
applied in this study to determine the challenges and possible solutions involved in adopting
innovative technologies in organizations.
In order to demonstrate the challenges concerned with innovative technology adop-
tion, a combined framework design of TAM and TOE was used, where the challenge areas
identified and concluded from the SLR were added as part of the external factors affecting the
perceived usefulness (PU) and perceived ease of use (PEOU) (section 2.3.1). These external
factors are divided into three distinct contexts (section 2.3.2): technological, organizational,
and environmental. The external factors comprised of the challenge areas from the three
contexts are then correlated with either PU or PEOU to determine the attitude towards
using innovative technologies, and consider a positive usage of innovative technologies.
3.3
Expert Interviews & Evaluation
Based on the framework developed, an interview procedure was conducted to assess
whether certain organizations within the industry of ocean data and resource management
perceive and experience the same challenges identified in the literature review. Interview
questions were generated based on the external factors and the three contexts of TOE,
which can be seen in appendix A.2.
Experts within their respective fields were interviewed from various companies, semi-
governmental associations, and research institutions. None of the organizations’ and experts’
names are revealed due to signed non-disclosure agreements (NDAs). All experts interviewed
were associated with at least two technology types identified in the SLR that improve ocean
data and resource management. The interviewed experts were either tech solution providers
that implemented technological solutions within other organizations, or experts who were
in the process of adopting or already have adopted technologies in organizations working
with ocean data and marine resources.
The experts provided contextual knowledge and
experience related to the aim of this study. The initial part of the interviews involved an
introduction to the expert’s profession and their individual position within the organization
that they were employed at. Thereafter, a more generalised question was asked about the
different challenges that they personally experience within their respective organizations in
the adoption of innovative technologies. The purpose of this was to start an open discussion
with the interviewees, giving them the opportunity to contemplate about challenges that
they experienced as part of their work processes, without concern to any biases that they
may have. This was important as it could give indication to new challenges which might not
have been a part of the literature.
Once that discussion had been expended, several questions in regards to the tech-
nological, organizational, and environmental contexts were asked. This was done in such a
way to lead the expert in going into further details about other challenges that they may not
have considered within those specific contexts, without revealing the challenges identified in

The five innovative technologies identified from the SLR (i.e. BCT, IoT, CC, I4.0,
AI) require more context on what they actually are and what type of challenges could be
associated with each. As mentioned in section 2.2, there is a difference in the various types

For example, BCT is a radical technological innovation, which renders established
structural systems and processes obsolete accompanied by new functions that reorganize
business models and markets of any industry (section 2.2.4).
IoT provides unpreceden-

existing industry, and at the same time, presenting a new field for businesses to develop
alternative business models to capture market share. Hence, it is considered to be both

the existing technology foundation of virtualization, which is widely applied in data center
technology markets. However, CC adds automation, self-service, and flexibility as new tech-
nological functions to these existing markets and solutions. Therefore, CC is incremental
and disruptive technological innovation (sections 2.2.1 & 2.2.2). AI is considered to be such
a revolutionising technological tool that builds upon existing software with machine learning
and can be applied in almost any existing industry, especially ICT organizations, whilst also

4.2.1
Blockchain Technology (BCT)
Blockchain is a shared, immutable ledger database that facilitates the process of
recording transactions, tracking, storing, and trading information in a digital format. This
information can either be a tangible asset (e.g. cash, physical objects, production, resources,
animals) or an intangible asset (e.g. intellectual property, copyrights, patents, accounts).
A key difference between a typical database and a blockchain is how the data is structured
and stored. A blockchain collects information in groups, known as ‘blocks’, that hold sets of
information. These ‘blocks’ have certain storage capacities, and once filled, they are closed
and linked to the previously filled block, forming a chain of data known as the blockchain.
All new information that follows is added by compiling a newly formed block that will then
continue the blockchain once filled. This data and information structure inherently creates
an irreversible timeline when implemented in a decentralized nature. When a block is filled,
it is permanently stored and becomes a part of this timeline. Each block in the chain is
given an exact time stamp when it is added to the chain (IBM, 2022).
This is ideal for delivering information because it provides immediate, shared and
completely transparent information stored on an immutable ledger that can only be accessed
by users who have permission to for particular network or it could be entirely public accessed
network by anyone with an internet connection. Since users share a single view of authen-
ticated information, including all the details of a transaction from end-to-end, this would
give great confidence in security and reliability of information, as well as new efficiencies
and opportunities in performance, productivity and capability. The innovation with BCT is
that it guarantees the fidelity in a record of data and generates trust without the need of
approval from a mediator (Hayes, 2022).
AlShamsi et al. (2022) and Rizal et al. (2018) conducted a systematic review on BCT
adoption in literature utilizing both the TAM and TOE frameworks to identify the main
challenges and opportunities across different sectors. The study deduced that most research
examining the adoption of BCT is perceived from the lens of an organizational level, which
in retrospect is an important aspect of BCT adoption. However, little attention was given
to the individual level. In addition, the study revealed that supply chain management is the
most common domain in which BCT applications were adopted. This indicated that trust,
BCT knowledge, perceived cost, top management support, stakeholders collaborations and
government policies were the significant determinants influencing BCT application. This
gives the opportunity to explore how BCT adoption within the context of ocean data and
resource management can be improved and implemented in organizations that are associated
with ocean data and marine resources by utilizing a combined framework of TAM and TOE.
4.2.2
Internet of Things (IoT)
The Internet of Things (IoT) is described as a network of physical objects (“things”)
that are embedded with sensors, software, and other technologies for the purpose of connect-
ing and exchanging data with other devices and systems over the internet (Oracle, 2019).
These devices range from ordinary household objects to sophisticated industrial tools. It cre-
ates a seamless communication possibility between people, processes and “things” by using

low-cost cloud computing, big data, analytics, and mobile technologies, physical “things” can
share and collect data with minimal human intervention. This means that digital systems
can record, monitor, and adjust each interaction between connected “things”.
Recent advances in numerous technologies has made IoT technology adoption pos-
sible for many organizations. These include:
• Access to affordable, reliable, low-power sensor technology
• Availability of cloud platforms that enable scalability and management
• Improvements in connected network protocols that effortlessly connect sensors to the
cloud and other “things” for efficient data transfer
• Advances in machine learning and analytics gather information faster and easier
• Artificial intelligence (AI) brought natural-language processing (NLP) to IoT devices
which makes it more affordable and viable
Olushola (2019), Hsu and Yeh (2017), and Patil (2016) analyzed the various factors
affecting IoT adoption in different industries such as ICT, retail and logistics, respectively,
using TAM and TOE frameworks. It was concluded that IoT readiness, compatibility, com-
plexity, top management support, firm size, security, competitive advantage, stakeholder
collaboration and regulatory support were the critical success components within the con-
text levels of technology, organization and environment that influenced IoT adoption. This
encourages research to study the integration of IoT technology in organizations working with
ocean data and marine resources incorporating the TAM and TOE frameworks.
4.2.3
Cloud Computing (CC)
Cloud computing (CC) is on-demand access and delivery of different services via
the Internet.
These services include tools and applications like data storage, computing
resource applications, servers (physical servers and virtual servers), databases, development
tools, networking capabilities, and software. Cloud-based storage makes it possible to save
data and information to a remote database center, rather than keeping files on a proprietary
hard-drive or local storage device. As long as the electronic device has access to the web,
then users who have been given permission can access the data (Vennam, 2020).
CC is a much popular option compared to the traditional on-premises information
technology (IT) (i.e. software and technology that is located within physical confines of an
organization), due to a number of reasons including cost savings, increased productivity,
scalability, speed and efficiency, performance, and security.
Services of CC can be both
public and private, where public services are provided online for a certain fee, while private
services are hosted on a network to specific users.
Chiniah et al. (2019), Gangwar et al. (2015) and Novais et al. (2019) were similar
studies that were conducted using a hybrid TAM and TOE framework, which helped identify
the challenges and determinants of cloud computing adoption. The studies found that top
management commitment, stakeholder collaboration, training & education, compatibility,

4.2.4
Artificial Intelligence (AI)
Artificial intelligence (AI) utilizes computers and machines to mimic the problem-
solving and decision-making capabilities of the human mind. In its simplest form, AI is
a field that combines computer science and robust datasets, to enable problem-solving. It
also encompasses sub-fields of machine learning and deep learning, which are frequently
mentioned in conjunction with AI. These disciplines are comprised of AI algorithms which
seek to create expert systems which make predictions or classifications based on input data,
and has the capacity to take decisions (IBM, 2020). Tech developers use artificial intelligence
to more efficiently perform tasks that are otherwise done manually, connect with users,
identify patterns, and solve problems. McCarthy (2004) offers the following definition of
AI: “It is the science and engineering of making intelligent machines, especially intelligent
computer programs.
It is related to the similar task of using computers to understand
human intelligence, but AI does not have to confine itself to methods that are biologically
observable.”
Radhakrishnan and Chattopadhyay (2020) is a study that combined the TOE and
Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) frameworks to study the
technological adoption of AI. In this study, a systematic literature review was conducted
that evaluated articles and other reports pertaining to AI adoption and the factors that
facilitate the hindrance of AI adoption. It gives context for challenges and determinants
of adoption at the individual level and the organizational level. The study concluded that
factors influencing adoption at the individual level were related to trust, security, cost,
and user acceptance, whereas at the organizational level, it was related to the technical
competencies, top management support and the digital maturity of the organization. The
study provides enough credibility to examine how AI can be implemented in organizations by
identifying the challenge areas and potential solutions related to its adoption with the TAM
and TOE frameworks, and its capabilities to improve ocean data and resource management.
4.2.5
Industry 4.0 (I4.0)
Industry 4.0 (I4.0) is referred to as the fourth Industrial Revolution, which is powered
by a combination of new disruptive smart technologies such as BCT, IoT, CC, AI, 3D print-
ing, and smart sensors, just to name a few of the most essential. I4.0 is mainly controlled
by IoT technology and cyber-physical systems (CPS), which are smart, autonomous sys-
tems that use computer-based algorithms to monitor and control physical objects such as
machinery, robots, and vehicles. I4.0 is usually utilized in smart supply chains, starting
from smart manufacturing and factories to smart warehousing and logistics. However, it
also interconnects with back-end systems such as enterprise resource planning (ERP) to give
organizations an unprecedented level of transparency and control over all their resources.

Ultimately, I4.0 is a major part of an organization’s digital transformation (SAP, 2020).
Prause (2019) and Masood and Sonntag (2020) are studies that conducted large
reviews of literature to identify adoption challenges and opportunities with I4.0 mainly in
small- and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) utilizing the TAM and TOE frameworks. The
studies found that flexibility, cost effectiveness, efficiency, quality, and competitive advant-

constraints, market uncertainty, top management support were the key challenges identified

such, the framework designed in this study takes into account the challenge areas identified
for the adoption of I4.0 whilst using the TAM and TOE frameworks to further develop the
possible solutions in organizations associated with ocean data and marine resources.

4.3.1
Technical Integration
Technical integration can be defined as challenges arising when attempting to in-
tegrate a specific technology either due to technological immaturity, insufficient technical
implementation, and/or a lack of other physical/non-physical materials (e.g. machines with
sensors, cyber-infrastructure, internet connection).
Within the BCT studies, technical integration was an issue due to insufficient control
mechanisms that could avoid possible errors in the process of manual data entry ((Tiscini et
al., 2020; White et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021)). The IoT focused studies found that the lack
of other physical cyber-infrastructures such as smart devices and smart sensors prevented
the integration of IoT ((Haddud et al., 2017; Ahmad & Zhang, 2021)), which was the same
case for I4.0 technology ((Yang et al., 2018; Buyukozkan & Gocer, 2018; Popa et al., 2017)).
Lastly, CC ((Jayeola et al., 2022)) and AI ((Zhang et al., 2021)) studies concluded that
the technologies were still in their early development phases which had potential to cause
security and reliability issues, especially with AI technology.
Potential solutions to these challenges were identified within the studies, where re-
searchers suggested that over time more resources will be used to improve these technologies
(Haddud et al., 2017). In addition, the increasing development of machine learning (Ahmad
& Zhang, 2021), collaborations with other tech-orientated companies (White et al., 2021),
and governmental support (Yang et al., 2018) will mitigate the challenges related to technical
integration. This shows that other challenge areas could also influence technical integration
and vice-versa.
4.3.2
Value Creation
According to some studies, several industries overlook the value captured and cre-
ated by certain technologies, meaning they are unaware of the potential in adopting such
technologies for creating and capturing value through monitoring, control, optimization, and
autonomous functions of their processes ((Tsolakis et al., 2021; Maciuliene & Skarzauskiene,
2021; Haddud et al., 2017; Kohtamaki et al., 2019; Chohan et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2018)).
The lack of recognition of value creation through monitoring and control was related
closely to IoT (Haddud et al., 2017; Kohtamaki et al., 2019; Chohan et al., 2021) and CC
(Luo et al., 2018). In contrast, the recognition of value creation through optimization and
autonomy was closely related to BCT (Tsolakis et al., 2021; Maciuliene & Skarzauskiene,
2021).
Several solutions were proposed on how to influence industries in acknowledging the
value created by these technologies. Studies suggested that developing value co-creation
where multiple stakeholders enrich collaborative practices at different levels of the industry
ecosystem (Maciuliene & Skarzauskiene, 2021; Luo et al., 2018), and improving inconsist-
encies of standardization in data structures (Tsolakis et al., 2021; Haddud et al., 2017) will
enhance relationships between interrelated firms (Kohtamaki et al., 2019), thus, lowering
future costs and engaging users in value creation (Chohan et al., 2021).
This supports
the idea that improving data standardization and collaboration with other partners and/or
governments can have a positive impact on value creation.

4.3.3
Costs
Several studies specified that the costs of acquiring the necessary hardware for IoT
implementation such as smart devices were too high to consider adopting this technology
(Solima et al., 2016; Kohtamaki et al., 2019; Sott et al., 2021).
Additionally, the high
cost for development of software for AI (Zhang et al., 2021), and maintaining CC services
(Gozman & Willcocks, 2019; Subramanian et al., 2014; Qasem et al., 2021) were similarly
discouraging. However, as previously mentioned in the technical integration challenge, costs
of these technologies will drop over time as they become more researched and developed
(Kohtamaki et al., 2019; Sott et al., 2021; Qasem et al., 2021).
Based on findings of high cost challenges from these studies, it can be deduced that
industries and organizations that decide not to adopt these technologies and save on costs
instead, will limit their potential to improve on work processes and experience technical
integration issues as technology constantly develops. This in turn, will cause a decrease in
future revenue and profits since competition will outperform them on a technological level.
4.3.4
Privacy & Security Risks
Privacy and security concerns was the most prominent challenge area identified
within the studies ((Tiscini et al., 2020; Malik et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Haddud et
al., 2017; D’Amico et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2018; Ahmad & Zhang, 2021; Chohan et al.,
2021; Sott et al., 2021; Aliero et al., 2022; Costa et al., 2022; Popa et al., 2017; Gozman &
Willcocks, 2019; Chen et al., 2018; Subramanian et al., 2014; X. Yang et al., 2020; Tong et
al., 2022)). Studies that reviewed IoT as a technology type had the highest rate of addressing
devices, network and data security risks and vulnerabilities involved with implementation of
IoT (Haddud et al., 2017; D’Amico et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2018; Ahmad & Zhang, 2021;
Chohan et al., 2021; Sott et al., 2021; Aliero et al., 2022). The same concerns were reflected
in studies with the adoption of I4.0 technologies (Costa et al., 2022; Popa et al., 2017).
Furthermore, some studies revealed that adopting CC (Gozman & Willcocks, 2019; Chen et
al., 2018; Subramanian et al., 2014) and AI (X. Yang et al., 2020; Tong et al., 2022) tech-
nologies without any consideration in integrating other security technologies and software
developments will leave sensitive data and information vulnerable and easily accessible.
In the same studies, it was suggested that integrating other security technologies such
as BCT with IoT, I4.0, CC, and AI can mitigate and avoid malicious data/cyber attacks,
and provide a layer of privacy and security that is effective (Haddud et al., 2017; D’Amico et
al., 2020; Aliero et al., 2022; Popa et al., 2017). However, while BCT studies (Tiscini et al.,
2020; Malik et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021) do acknowledge and solve problems in information
reliability and system security, privacy disclosure were highlighted as the main issue since
any user can be given access to the specific blockchain database. Nonetheless, information
and records witnessed by a user within the blockchain cannot be overwritten as it is stored
on an immutable ledger, so transparency, accountability and verification are assured by the
blockchain protocol for every piece of information and record (Tiscini et al., 2020). One of
the studies even proposed that the role of top management support in organizations, and
government support in other certain cases, should provide the necessary resources and be

4.3.5
Lack of Standardization
A lack of standardization was mostly shown in studies that researched IoT and I4.0
implementation ((D’Amico et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2018; Ahmad & Zhang, 2021; Inge-
marsdotter et al., 2021; Aliero et al., 2022; Costa et al., 2022; Ebinger & Omondi, 2020;
Scuderi et al., 2022; Buyukozkan & Gocer, 2018; Popa et al., 2017)).
In terms of IoT technology, a lack of data standardization made it difficult for data
storage and device interoperability (D’Amico et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2018; Ahmad & Zhang,
2021; Ingemarsdotter et al., 2021). For I4.0 case situations, a lack of industry standardization
for smart sensors and hardware devices caused low quality of data collection since the needs
of each stakeholder and partners in a supply chain were different (Costa et al., 2022; Ebinger
& Omondi, 2020; Scuderi et al., 2022; Buyukozkan & Gocer, 2018; Popa et al., 2017).
The potential solutions recommended by some studies included having an organiz-
ation’s internal management set standards during the development of these technologies so
that hardware, software and data is sustainably appropriated for each given use case within
a specific department (Ahmad & Zhang, 2021; Ingemarsdotter et al., 2021). Other stud-
ies proposed a framework that permits managers and researchers in evaluating the selected
devices based on their technological process capabilities (Buyukozkan & Gocer, 2018; Popa
et al., 2017). Lastly, the involvement of larger tech organizations such as Microsoft or Apple
could set a global standardization guide for all other organizations to follow (Lim et al., 2018;
Costa et al., 2022), or having government regulations and policies set in place for certain use
cases of data standardization (Scuderi et al., 2022; D’Amico et al., 2020).
With the support of top management and stakeholder collaborations, resolving chal-
lenges related to data standardization would decrease time and effort spent on implementing
technological solutions, which in turn, would minimize costs and resources used for organiz-
ations.

Fernando et al., 2021; Lohmer et al., 2022; Paul et al., 2021; Maciuliene & Skarzauskiene,
2021; Zhu et al., 2020)). A similar case was found in AI (Tong et al., 2022) and IoT (Chohan
et al., 2021) technologies where government policymakers and agencies scrutinized the use of
AI in combination with IoT since the technologies were still underdeveloped and presented

However, several solutions were presented for this challenge where education and

continuation in using these technologies so that digital accumulation of data in most systems
in the long run will improve living standards for their citizens. Government support and
stakeholder collaboration is crucial in adopting these technologies within organizations as it
assists and encourages sustainable monitoring, management, and distribution of resources.
4.3.7
Education & Training
Education and training involves challenges associated with the level of skills and
knowledge capabilities of employees in organizations, especially for I4.0 (Shakaib et al., 2018;
Yang et al., 2018; Hughes et al., 2022) and CC (Jayeola et al., 2022; Bologa et al., 2017)
adoption. In addition, BCT (White et al., 2021) and AI (Tong et al., 2022) studies address
skill shortages in AI- and BCT-related occupations.
Studies supported solutions involving top management intervention and support by
developing strategies and education programs that build strong organizational structures
in staff training and skill improvements (Shakaib et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2018; Jayeola
et al., 2022). Re-education and retraining aid employees to transition and adapt to new
technological processes within an organization (Hughes et al., 2022; Bologa et al., 2017).
It can be argued that that this would add constraints and increase costs of an
organization’s resources, however, developing practical guidance and showcasing the value
of technology through improved education and training programs is vital for building a
competitive edge, as well as, enhancing automation, productivity, and effectively supporting
the desired levels of expertise and necessary skills for development (White et al., 2021; Tong
et al., 2022).

4.3.9
Stakeholder Collaboration
In certain cases, the lack of collaboration between other stakeholders was revealed
to have caused the adoption of a technology not to be accepted. This is however critical
when it comes to implementing BCT in the Thai fish industry, for example, as a high-level
of collaboration requirements is necessary to discontinue inconsistencies in data structures
across distributed data sources (Tsolakis et al., 2021). The same was revealed in AI and IoT
based business ecosystems for a case involving a Chinese fish farming industry (X. Yang et
al., 2020), where small-scale aquaculture farms lacked collaboration and networking between
other feed enterprises, fish farms, distributors, retailers, and financing institutions.
Improving on collaboration efforts between organizations and other enterprises (e.g.
suppliers, governmental agencies, financial institutions, researchers, environmental associ-
ations etc.) is a fundamental propriety in the adoption and interoperability of the technology
types, as it enhances performance and development in sustainable production, management,
and trustworthiness amongst organizations (Ebinger & Omondi, 2020; Tiscini et al., 2020;
Vovchenko et al., 2022; Aliero et al., 2022; Hughes et al., 2022; Bologa et al., 2017).
4.3.10
Top Management Support
Top management support was proven to be an integral part of an organization’s
decision to adopt new technology. The lack of leadership support reduced the chances of
adopting an innovation such as BCT (Malik et al., 2021; Fernando et al., 2021; Lohmer et
al., 2022) and CC (Jayeola et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2018). Studies found that when top man-
agement provides adequate leadership and required resources such as education and training
programs, then there was a much higher likelihood of technology implementation (Fernando
et al., 2021; Lohmer et al., 2022; Jayeola et al., 2022). Similar cases were displayed with IoT
(Ingemarsdotter et al., 2021) and I4.0 adoption (Shakaib et al., 2018) where knowledge and
maintenance management in digitalization of information and data from top management
were seen as integral to implementing these technology types.

Competitiveness
Challenges related to competitiveness are defined as a lack of competitive pressure
for adopting a certain technology from other organizations within the same field, and how
implementing a new technology enables a competitive advantage or not. BCT studies found
that cryptocurrency payment adoption offers practical implications for tourism stakeholders
towards supporting small- and medium enterprises competitiveness (Nuryyev et al., 2020),
which pressures other tourism enterprises to develop new service concepts with technological
innovation and adoption (Yang et al., 2018). However, in other industries such as AI and IoT
adoption in the fish farming business, there is a lack of competitive pressure to implement
such technologies (X. Yang et al., 2020), which causes a hindrance in technological develop-
ment and decreases the opportunities for sustainable management of resources within that
industry (Shakaib et al., 2018).
As mentioned previously, as technologies mature and evolve through time, there will
be an upsurge of technological adoption (Valeri & Baggio, 2021; Maciuliene & Skarzauskiene,
2021). In addition, support from top management and governments with implementation
of standardized data policies along with sustainable environmental regulations, and new
education and training programs will foster more competitive pressure (Scuderi et al., 2022;
Hughes et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2018).
Thus, in the future more organizations will be
pressured to adopt new innovative technologies such as BCT, IoT, I4.0, CC, and AI.

factors influencing a users’ and organizations’ intention to actually use the five innovative
technologies. Using a combination of the TAM and TOE frameworks is beneficial since they
effectively compliment each others strengths and disadvantages, whilst they also contribute
in analyzing and providing deeper clarification into the adoption challenges of innovative
technologies within organizations.

Interviewed Experts

7.3
Implications
BCT, IoT, CC and AI are relatively new, groundbreaking technologies that have had
tremendous implications in various industries within the ICT domain, and will continue to
evolve and disrupt a variety of industries. As such, this study contributed to research in the
adoption of these innovative technologies by reviewing challenge areas in current literature
related to different types of organizations. A framework was created utilizing a hybrid version
of the TAM and TOE frameworks to structure the challenge areas identified in the SLR in
a validated and consistent manner. In terms of theoretical implications, no other study has
used a combination of the TAM and TOE frameworks related to the challenges in adopting
innovative technologies with such a wide range of various industries. The designed framework
can be applied to a broad spectrum of innovative technologies adoption, which can serve as a
basis for other academic research to expand upon new challenge areas to specific industries,
or a wider range of industry implementation.
A series of interviews was then conducted with experts working in organizations
operating within the field of ocean data and resource management, in order to identify
common areas in implementation challenges that can be compared with the framework. The
framework was evaluated and validated by the experts, which was then updated based on
their feedback and interview results. The updated framework was then compared to other
literature investigating challenges and opportunities in collectively utilizing BCT, IoT, CC
and AI technologies to promote the conservation and advancement in ocean data and resource
management.
This was done to ascertain whether these challenges could be minimized
and possible solutions can be determined for the purpose of encouraging further adoption
of these technologies in organizations for the sustainable management of ocean data and
marine resources. In terms of practical implications, the updated framework and information
provided above, should serve as an overview for the possible challenges and solutions in
adopting innovative technologies within organizations to improve the sustainability of ocean
data and resource management. Additionally, the purpose would be for future organizations

7.4
Limitations
Limitations of this study include bias in the screening process and findings of the
systematic literature review, and the limited extent of information and results collected
through the interview procedure. The SLR was conducted solely using the Web of Science
database system which only incorporated articles transcribed in English and within the social
sciences citation index (SSCI). As a result, more challenge areas could have potentially been
identified by using more database systems such as Scopus and ScienceDirect, in addition to,
utilizing other languages and citation indexes.
In respect to the information and results gathered from the interviews, the experts
did stem from different organizations such as two tech solution providers from individual
private companies, two oceanographers from the same research institute, and an auditor
from a semi-governmental marine stewardship association. This extended the possibilities
of identifying various challenges and solutions in technology adoption from and by several
organizations, however, due to the limited number of expert interviewees, the information
collected was more qualitative than quantitative. The small sample size and experts’ extens-
ive opinions on the challenges that they experienced are more prone to bias, due to the fact
that the results of the challenges identified might be directly correlated with the challenge
areas from the framework, which could cause unintentional confirmation bias.
Furthermore, all the organizations that were involved are located in either Denmark
or Cyprus, which meant that the geographical locations of other international organizations
for instance in Oceania, America and/or Asia, would have had a larger influence on this study
based on the challenges and solutions in adopting technologies for the different ocean data
and marine resource scenarios compared to only within the continent of Europe. For example,
the government support challenge could have been discussed differently with experts in
Australia since their fishing laws, data regulations and policies are unique compared to the
Cypriot government standards. Therefore, a geographical bias can be suggested as limitation
in the interview results. Likewise, the challenge areas that are context specific and require
further validation can be perceived as another limitation due to the lack of data from other
international sources to confirm or reject their validity.
Lastly, an additional limitation is that further information and data is required
to confirm possible solutions to the challenge areas presented in technology adoption for
ocean data and resource management throughout this study. Insufficient research diminished
the practical insights of solutions resolving the challenges present in organizations adopting
technologies for the sustainable management of ocean data and marine resources.

methods. One method would be to improve on the limitations mentioned above, especially
in collecting and validating additional information for possible solutions to the challenge
areas identified in organizations implementing innovative technologies for ocean data and

Moreover, gathering quantitative data with the utilization of in-

Another approach would be to apply other various technology adoption models and
frameworks that would strengthen the effectiveness and legitimacy of different challenge areas
that could be identified compared to the combined frameworks of TAM and TOE utilized
in this study. For example, the FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable and Reusable)
framework introduced by Wilkinson et al. (2016) and improved by Jacobsen et al. (2020)
can be aimed to address issues in innovative technology adoption and facilitate solutions for
open and free exchange of data in oceans and marine resource management. The main FAIR
principles emphasize technology actions (i.e. the capacity of information and communica-
tion technology systems that find, collect, access, interoperate, distribute, and reuse data
autonomously) without human intervention, which could be directly associated with the use
of BCT, IoT, CC and AI technologies in organizations for ocean data management, and thus
enhance the conservation and sustainable use of ocean resources.
Additionally, the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT)
proposed by Venkatesh et al. (2003), combines various theories, models and frameworks
including TAM and TOE. UTAUT is more focused on elements such as performance expect-
ancy, effort expectancy and social influence that affect an individual’s behavioural intention
and user acceptance of utilizing technologies. This would be useful in analyzing and provid-
ing insights to a specific user’s perception and behaviour of innovative technology adoption
in organizations.
The framework presented in this study is centered around the overall challenge areas
of innovative technology adoption in various organizations, specifically for BCT, IoT, CC
and AI. The main intention however was to identify corresponding challenges in technology
adoption within organizations working with ocean data and resource management, thus facil-
itating potential solutions in the technology adoption process to improve on the sustainable
management of ocean data and marine resources. In turn, this would provide a more effective
strategy in achieving the SDG 14 target goals (section 2.1.1) and enhance the conservation
of the oceans, seas and marine ecosystems. However, in order to accomplish the targets of
SDG 14, a multi-stakeholder collaboration is essential to realise and mitigate the challenges
presented in technology adoption for ocean data and resource management. This is also
reflected in SDG 17 which aims to strengthen the means of technology implementation in
global partnerships for sustainable development. Finally, it would be beneficial for future
research to focus on how these technologies can directly improve on the targets of SDG 14 by
being implemented in different organizations and analyzing their potential solutions within
the field of sustainable management of ocean data and marine resources.

Conclusion
This study has identified challenges involved with adoption of innovative technolo-
gies in various organizations through a systematic literature review. On that premise, a
framework was designed based on a combination of the TAM and TOE frameworks, the
identified challenge areas and their respective technology types from the SLR, which were
BCT, CC, IoT and AI.
A series of five semi-structured interviews was carried out examining the opinions and
experiences of experts that are directly associated with ocean data and resource management
within their respective organizations. Information and data in regards to technology adoption
challenges and solutions was gathered from the resulting interviews, which was compared to
the framework to determine common and additional challenge areas within the industry of
ocean resources.
The framework was then updated based on the interview results and correlated with
other literature to identify possible solutions in adopting innovative technologies for the
sustainable management of ocean data and marine resources. The updated framework es-
tablishes a foundation that can be utilized for other innovative technology adoption research
that investigates relevant challenges in various industries, especially those affiliated with
ocean and marine resources.
However, more research is required to validate the existing challenge areas within
the framework, in order to support successful technology adoption and offer solutions to
organizations that aim to improve ocean data and resource management. As a result, an
effective strategy can be provided to the UN’s SDG 14 targets that promote the protection,
conservation and sustainable development and use of oceans, seas and marine resources.
Lastly, utilizing BCT, CC, IoT and AI technologies in an integrated infrastructure
for the sustainable management of ocean data and resources can only be achieved if multiple
stakeholders collaborate and decide to implement these technologies as part of their indi-
vidualistic organizations. The question that now arises is how to achieve multi-stakeholder
collaborations that would accelerate conservation and sustainable management of ocean data
and marine resources.

A.3
Discussion
A.3.1
Framework for Sustainable Management of Ocean Resources
Since this study is investigating the use of innovative technologies for the sustainable
management of ocean data and marine resources, an additional framework is presented that
collectively uses the four technology types explored in the SLR that have the potential
to be implemented in different organizations, and improve the sustainable management of
ocean resources, thus, possibly achieving an effective ocean protection strategy, sustainable
production, and equitable prosperity.
The framework designed for improving the sustainable management of ocean re-
sources incorporates the combined implementation of four innovative technologies types (i.e.
BCT, IoT, CC, AI), which construct a digital approach for data management systems that
can be adopted by organizations that directly operate with ocean resources (e.g. fish farms,
marine ship ports, research institutes, environmental protection agencies, government asso-
ciations etc.). The goal with this framework is to incentivize the use of these technologies
within these industries and coordinate efforts of various stakeholders through an integrated
ocean management perspective that provides information and data on ocean conditions and
resource use. With the use of this information and data, the intention is to promote an
effective protection strategy for the ocean, seas, and marine ecosystems, in addition to, im-
proving sustainable production and equitable prosperity. This addresses and creates a more
target-orientated strategy for the SDG 14 goals (mentioned in section 2.1.1).
This framework utilized models and frameworks from studies researched in the SLR
(Ebinger & Omondi, 2020; Saner et al., 2019; Tsolakis et al., 2021; X. Yang et al., 2020;
Nuryyev et al., 2020; Buyukozkan & Gocer, 2018; Lohmer et al., 2022; White et al., 2021;
Costa et al., 2022), in conjunction with articles from sources related to ocean conservation
such as the High Level Panel for a Sustainable Ocean Economy (Ocean Panel) (Leape et al.,
2020; Stuchtey et al., 2020; Winther et al., 2020), Oceana (Oceana, 2021; Sherrington et al.,
2022), The Ocean Conservancy (Ocean Conservancy, 2020; Lopez, 2021; Merwin et al., 2020),
amongst other study publications (Bushnell et al., 2018; Hermes et al., 2018; Horstmann et
al., 2021; Pearlman et al., 2021; Spalding et al., 2016; Van Stavel et al., 2020). The four
technologies identified through the SLR provided a technological foundation in which they
can be integrated with each other to give a holistic view on how ocean information and data
resources could be collected, managed, and shared. Industry 4.0 (I4.0) was not included as
part of the integrated technologies since it already involves the use of BCT, IoT, CC and AI
for manufacturing processes and supply chain management.
