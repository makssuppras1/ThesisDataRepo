INTRODUCTION 
Antimicrobial resistance is nowadays considered one of the top 10 global health threats by the 
World Health Organisation (WHO), having caused more than 4 million deaths around the world 
in 2019 (WHO, 2022). This, not only creates a high pressure on healthcare systems as it results 
in longer hospitalizations, but also creates an economic burden to the affected countries (Christaki 
et al., 2020). 
 
Antimicrobial misuse in human treatments, agriculture and farming are the main causes that 
contribute to antimicrobial resistance prevalence and spread (Christaki et al., 2020). In recent 
years, the launch of several surveillance programmes, such as GLASS by WHO and One Health 
by EFSA, have provided a strong background on the dissemination and prevalence of resistance 
genes and antimicrobial consumption and use (European Food Safety Authority & European 
Centre for Disease Prevention and Control, 2022a; WHO, 2022).  Thus, to be able to design novel 
strategies to treat these infections, it is key to understand and unveil the resistance mechanisms 
that these bacteria harbour.  
ENTEROBACTERIACEAE 
Enterobacteriaceae are a family of ubiquitous Gram-negative bacteria found in a wide range of 
different environments, such as vegetables, soil, water and as part of the normal flora of humans 
and animals (Jenkins et al., 2017; Landraud & Brisse, 2010). Taxonomically, Enterobacteriaceae 
belong to order Enterobacterales and class Gamma-proteobacteria, which is part of the phylum 
Proteobacteria (Jenkins et al., 2017).   
 
The Enterobacteriaceae is a large and diverse family. It contains more than 50 different bacterial 
genres and more than 200 species, which makes the definition of distinctive properties difficult 
(Landraud & Brisse, 2010). Principally, they are characterized for being Gram-negative non-spore 
forming bacilli (Donnenberg, 2015; Jenkins et al., 2017). When exhibiting motility, it is mostly 
due to the use of flagella, otherwise are non-motile (Donnenberg, 2015). They are easily 
culturable in the laboratory both under aerobic and anaerobic conditions, with an optimal 
temperature growth of 37ºC (Donnenberg, 2015; Jenkins et al., 2017). Apart from several

exceptions, they can ferment glucose and reduce nitrates to nitrites and most of them are oxidase-
negative and catalase-positive bacteria (Jenkins et al., 2017). 
 
The Enterobacteriaceae family is widely known for causing a wide range of both health-care 
acquired and community infections (Logan & Weinstein, 2017; Rodríguez-Baño et al., 2018). 
The most common Enterobacteriaceae implicated in human infections are Escherichia coli, 
Citrobacter spp., Shigella spp., Salmonella enterica, Yersinia spp., Enterobacter spp., Klebsiella 
spp. and Serratia spp., among others (Jenkins et al., 2017).  
 
In recent years, multi-drug resistant (MDR) and extensively drug resistant (XDR) 
Enterobacteriaceae have become a major health issue (Rodríguez-Baño et al., 2018), harbouring 
b-lactam resistance mechanisms as its main driving force (Logan & Weinstein, 2017). As b-
lactams are the main line of treatment of both health-care acquired and community infections, it 
is becoming highly challenging to fight these infections (Rodríguez-Baño et al., 2018). 
b-LACTAMS 
b-lactams are an antibiotic family with a bactericidal action due to the inhibition of the cell wall 
synthesis both in Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria (Capelo-Martínez & Igrejas, 2020). 
The bacterial cell wall is an outer membrane structure that is essential for the growth and the 
development of bacteria as it confers rigidity and protection (Donnenberg, 2015; Lima et al., 
2020). Its main component is peptidoglycan, an heteropolymer formed by the cross-linking of 
two aminoglycoside chains, N-acetylglucosamine and N-acetylmuramic acid (Heijenoort, 2010).  
The bacterial cell wall synthesis involves more than 30 enzymes, where penicillin binding 
proteins (PBPs) stand out (Lima et al., 2020; Typas et al., 2012). 
 
PBPs catalyse the last step of the cell wall synthesis, a transpeptidation reaction that crosslinks 
the peptide subunit of one N-acetylmuramic acid chain with the peptide subunit of another N-
acetylmuramic acid chain (Heijenoort, 2010). The peptidic part of N-acetylmuramic acid is 
usually a pentapeptide with the following composition: L-Alanine-D-Glutamine-X-D-Alanine-
D-Alanine6, where X is L-lysine in most Gram-positive bacteria and meso-diaminopimelic acid 
in most Gram-negative bacteria (Capelo-Martínez & Igrejas, 2020). PBPs interact with the fourth 
and fifth amino acid of one pentapeptide chain, which are D-Alanine-D-Alanine, and transfer the 
peptidic bond of the fourth amino acid to a free group of another peptide chain (Capelo-Martínez 
==PAGE:9==
 
5 
& Igrejas, 2020; Heijenoort, 2010). The outcome of the process is the linkage of the two peptide 
chains and the release of the fifth residue, D-Alanine (Lima et al., 2020).

b-lactams inhibit PBPs action by covalently binding to their active site and irreversibly avoiding 
the interaction of the enzyme with its natural substrate, D-Alanine-D-Alanine (Lima et al., 2020). 
The b-lactam interaction with the enzyme is due to the structural and stereochemical similarities 
between the pharmacoactive core of the antibiotic and the substrate of the enzyme (Capelo-
Martínez & Igrejas, 2020; Lima et al., 2020). As a result of the covalent inhibition of PBPs, the 
cell wall synthesis process is blocked and the structural integrity of the bacteria is compromised, 
leading to cell death (Capelo-Martínez & Igrejas, 2020; Heijenoort, 2010; Lima et al., 2020).  
 
Structurally, b-lactams are characterized for containing a b-lactam ring, which 
confers the pharmacokinetic action (Smith et al., 2018). Depending on the 
chemical structures fused to the b-lactam ring; b-lactams can be classified as 
penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, penems and monobactams (Lima et 
al., 2020).

Penicillins 
Penicillin G, the first b-lactam identified by Alexander Fleming in 1928, 
is the antibiotic that gives name to this group. The antibiotics in this group 
are characterized by containing a bicyclic core structure and are 
differentiated by the radical found in the side chain of the 6-amino group 
(Capelo-Martínez & Igrejas, 2020).  
 
This group can be subclassified in natural and semisynthetic penicillins (Capelo-Martínez & 
Igrejas, 2020). The most representative examples are penicillin, oxacillin,  temocillin, ampicillin 
and amoxicillin, among others (Bush & Bradford, 2016). 
Cephalosporins 
Cephalosporins appeared around the 1950s through the discovery of 
cephalosporin C, a natural antibiotic stable against the presence of 
penicillin resistant bacteria (Abraham, 1979; Newton & Abraham, 1955). 
Structurally, cephalosporins are characterized by the cephem scaffold, a 
6-membered sulphur ring that is fused to the b-lactam core (Lima et al., 
2020).  
 
The presence of this sulphur ring confers a higher resistance to b-lactamases and a broader 
spectrum of action than penicillins (Capelo-Martínez & Igrejas, 2020; Lima et al., 2020).  
Cephalosporins are classified in five different generations depending on their antibacterial activity 
(Capelo-Martínez & Igrejas, 2020). From this group cefotaxime, ceftazidime and cefoxitin stand 
out (Bush & Bradford, 2016). 
Carbapenems 
The precursor for this class of b-lactams, thienamycin, was first identified 
in the mid 70s (J. S. Kahan et al., 1979). This class of antibiotics is 
characterized by its broad-spectrum, both against Gram-positive and 
Gram-negative bacteria, as well as for its resistance to hydrolysis mediated 
by b-lactamases (Papp-Wallace et al., 2011).

Structurally, they differ from the other b-lactam antibiotics by the presence of 5-membered ring 
fused to the b-lactam ring that has a double bond between C-2 and C-3 (Lima et al., 2020; Papp-
Wallace et al., 2011), and the presence of a carbon atom instead of a sulphur or oxygen atom in 
the b-lactam ring (Papp-Wallace et al., 2011).  The most representative examples from this group 
are imipenem, meropenem and ertapenem (Bush & Bradford, 2016).  
Penems 
Penems are a group of synthetic antibiotics, as they do not occur naturally. 
Their core structure is very similar to the carbapenems, as they also have 
a 5-membered ring fused to the b-lactam ring that has a double bond 
between C-2 and C-3 (Lima et al., 2020). The main difference with 
carbapenems is the presence of the sulphur atom in the b-lactam ring 
(Sammes, 1982). Faropenem and sulopenem stand out as representatives 
of this group (Lima et al., 2020). 
Monobactams 
The first detected monobactam was sulfazecin, firstly described in a 
Japanese patent of 1979 (Imada et al., 1981). Monobactams are b-lactams 
that have no activity against anaerobic nor Gram-positive bacteria 
(Capelo-Martínez & Igrejas, 2020).  Their main distinctive feature is that 
they are monocyclic antibiotics, unlike all the other b-lactams which are 
bicyclic (Lima et al., 2020).  Aztreonam is the main representative of this 
group (Bush & Bradford, 2016).

In the last decade, the spread and number of b-lactam resistant Enterobacteriaceae strains has 
increased, becoming a major health threat (Rodríguez-Baño et al., 2018). The main b-lactam 
resistance mechanisms in Enterobacteriaceae are production of b-lactamases, reduced membrane 
permeability, modification of PBPs and increased efflux (Darby et al., 2023).

Reduced membrane permeability 
Gram-negative bacteria, including Enterobacteriaceae, have a complex outer membrane that b-
lactams need to cross in order to exert their action (Darby et al., 2023). The outer membrane 
contains porins, which are b-barrel structured protein channels that allow the diffusion of 
molecules with a molecular weight less than 600Da, including many b-lactams (Darby et al., 
2023; Vergalli et al., 2020). The expression levels of porins is up to 106 copies per cell, but it can 
be regulated in response to environmental stimuli such as presence of antibiotics (Dam et al., 
2018; Fernández & Hancock, 2012).

In the last decade, porins have been unveiled as great contributors of antimicrobial resistance 
(Darby et al., 2023). The number, presence, and type of porins widely determine the susceptibility 
and permeability of bacteria to antibiotics (Delcour, 2009; Fernández & Hancock, 2012) (Table 
1). Consequently, mutations altering porin expression or/and function have a high impact on 
antimicrobial bacterial response (Darby et al., 2023).  
 
The most common effect of these mutations is the modification of the size of the porin channel, 
down-expression of porins or even porin loss (Dupont et al., 2016; Sun et al., 2017). Eventually, 
this translates into avoiding or reducing the diffusion of the b-lactam antibiotics into the cell and, 
as consequence, a reduced effect of the antibiotic. It should be noted that mutations have been 
identified both in genes encoding for porins and in regulatory proteins that control the expression 
of these, such as ompR (Jaffe et al., 1982).  
 
These types of mutations are highly prevalent in both E. coli and K. pneumoniae isolates with a 
b-lactam resistance phenotypes, and have been shown to increase resistance to a wide range of  
b-lactams such as cefoxitin, ceftazidime and meropenem, among others (Choi & Lee, 2019; Jaffe 
et al., 1982; Shi et al., 2013)

Efflux pumps 
Efflux pump are energy-dependent transmembrane proteins which export toxic compounds 
outside of the cell, such as antibiotics (Darby et al., 2023; Fernández & Hancock, 2012). Efflux 
pumps can be divided in several families, the most well-characterized and prevalent in Gram-
negative bacteria being the Resistance-Nodulation-Division (RND) family (Darby et al., 2023).  
 
RND pumps have a complex structure as they constitute a tripartite system with an inner 
membrane pump protein, a transmembrane fusion protein and an outer membrane protein  (Alav 
et al., 2021).  All in all, RND pumps can export a wide range of substances ranging from bile salts 
and dyes to toxic fatty acids and antibiotics (Darby et al., 2023). In fact, non-specificity of efflux 
pumps plays an important role in MDR isolates, as they are substrate unspecific and can transport 
several antibiotics (Fernández & Hancock, 2012).  
 
However, there is little known on the effect of efflux pumps in b-lactam resistance, as generally 
other resistance mechanisms such as the production of b-lactamases and low antibiotic 
penetration due to porin alteration have been described to be more significant in 
Enterobacteriaceae (Nikaido & Pagès, 2012). In Enterobacteriaceae only efflux pumps AcrAB-
TolC and AcrEF-TolC are well known to provide b-lactam resistance (Ma et al., 1994; Pérez et 
al., 2007). Additionally, efflux pumps and porins share several regulators (Aono et al., 1998). 
Thus, altered regulation of efflux pumps also affects porins, usually increasing the expression 
levels of the former and decreasing the levels of the latter (Li & Park, 1999) . 
Modification of PBPs 
PBPs are transpeptidases that crosslink the peptides in the peptidoglycan synthesis of the cell wall 
(Typas et al., 2012).  PBPs are divided in three main groups: class A, class B and monofunctional 
PBPs (Typas et al., 2012).  As mentioned, b-lactams covalently bind to the active centre of these 
enzymes inhibiting them (Lima et al., 2020). Thus, modification of PBPs relies in the strategy of 
altering the primary target with chemical modifications, in order to protect it from the effect of 
the antibiotic (Darby et al., 2023).  
 
This is a very well-studied mechanism in Gram-positive bacteria and specially relevant in 
methicillin resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) (Chambers, 1999). Despite not being as 
relevant as other mechanisms, it is present in Enterobacteriaceae bacteria. Recently, mutations in

PBP3 in E. coli with the ability to provide resistance to aztreonam and avibactam were identified 
(Periasamy et al., 2020). 
b-lactamases 
b-lactamases are the most important and widespread resistance mechanism against b-lactams 
(Bush & Bradford, 2019). b-lactamases are enzymes that confer resistance to b-lactams by 
breaking down the amide bond in the b-lactam ring, inactivating the pharmacokinetic effect of 
the antibiotic (Darby et al., 2023).  The main catalytic element of the enzyme can be either a serine 
residue or a divalent zinc atom, dividing them in serine b-lactamases or metallo-b-lactamases 
(MBLs), respectively (Bush & Bradford, 2019). 
 
The first described b-lactamase was identified in 1940, and it is suspected it appeared as a natural 
protection mechanism against organisms producing b-lactams naturally (Abraham & Chain, 
1940).

the Ambler and the Bush-Jacoby-Medeiros classification (Bush & Bradford, 2019).  
 
The Ambler system classifies the different enzymes based on the differences in their amino acid 
sequences into four different classes (A, B, C and D) (Bush & Bradford, 2019; Darby et al., 2023). 
The Bush-Jacoby-Medeiros (BJM) system classifies the different enzymes based on their 
functional aspects; their hydrolysis profile and the response to b-lactamase inhibitors (Bush & 
Bradford, 2019; Darby et al., 2023). The BJM system has three main categories; 1, 2 and 3, where 
category 2 is divided into seven subgroups (2a, 2b, 2br, 2d, 2be, 2c, and 2f) (Bush & Bradford, 
2019). A comparison of the two different classification systems is provided in Table 2.  
 
The Ambler class A group is the largest, as it comprises 6 out of 7 subgroups of BJM class 2. 
Penicillinases that belong to group 2a have a very narrow spectrum and are inhibited by 
clavulanate and avibactam, as well as other substances (Bush & Bradford, 2019). Class 2b 
penicillinases have a more extended spectrum, as they show hydrolysis towards both penicillins 
and to several cephalosporins (Bush & Bradford, 2019). The presence of class 2b b-lactamases in 
Enterobacteriaceae, such as blaTEM-1 and blaSHV-1 which confer resistance to ampicillin, is highly 
common (A. Philippon et al., 2019).

Class 2be are extended-spectrum b-lactamases (ESBLs) and belong to Ambler class A (Bush & 
Bradford, 2019). These enzymes are capable of hydrolysing penicillins, monobactams and narrow 
and extended-spectrum cephalosporins, and are inhibited by clavulanic acid (Rupp & Fey, 2003). 
CTX-M b-lactamases are highly disseminated and very common in Enterobacteriaceae, 
especially in E. coli and K. pneumoniae (Cantón et al., 2012). 
 
Inhibitor-resistant b-lactamases are classified in the BJM 2br functional group and Ambler class 
A. These enzymes are unaffected by clavulanic acid and sulbactam, two common b-lactamase 
inhibitors (Bush & Bradford, 2019). Their spectrum of hydrolysis is mainly focused on 
penicillins, as the mutations that provide the resistance to the inhibitors also cause a decrease in 
the hydrolysis of other b-lactams (Cantón et al., 2008). Most of the enzymes in this group are 
variants of TEM-1 (Cantón et al., 2008). 
 
BJM group 2c is still a part of Ambler class A classification system. This group shows a narrow 
spectrum of hydrolysis as penicillins are their substrate of preference (Bush & Bradford, 2019). 
The characteristic that differentiates them from other groups is their ability to hydrolyse 
carbenicillin, a carboxypenicillin or 4th generation penicillin (Bush et al., 1995). Representative 
enzymes from this group are PSE-1, PSE-3 and PSE-4 (Bush et al., 1995).

Serine carbapenamases are defined as BJM group 2f and Ambler class A. Due to their wide 
spectrum of hydrolysis; penicillins, cephalosporins and carbapenems; these enzymes are 
considered a major health threat (Bush & Bradford, 2019; Kazmierczak et al., 2016). KPC 
enzymes are the most representative example due to their large spread both in many species and 
territories (Kazmierczak et al., 2016).  
 
Metallo-b-lactamases (MBLs) are Ambler class B b-lactamases and also class 3 b-lactamases of 
the BJM classification.  Unlike all other groups, MBLs are characterized by the presence of Zn2+ 
in their active site (Garau et al., 2005). Their spectrum of hydrolysis includes most b-lactams, 
except for monobactams (Bush & Bradford, 2019). This group is further divided into three 
subclasses (B1, B2, B3), based on differences in structure, sequence, hydrolysis spectrum and 
zinc stoichiometry, among other characteristics (Boyd et al., 2020). The most well-known 
subgroup is B1, as the most relevant MBLs fall into this group (Boyd et al., 2020). The most 
characteristic MBLs are IMP, VIM and NDM, all identified in several Enterobacteriaceae species 
(2019, Karen Bush).  
 
Serine cephalosporinases are regarded as group 1 and class C for the BJM and Ambler 
classification, respectively. Also known as AmpC enzymes, they are able to inactivate 
cephalosporins of different generations as well as penicillins, in the absence of b-lactamase 
inhibitors such as clavulanic acid (A. Philippon et al., 2022). Some of them also hydrolyse 
monobactams to a lesser extent (Bush & Bradford, 2019). The most representative enzymes of 
this group are the DHA, MIR and ACT families (Bush & Bradford, 2019; A. Philippon et al., 
2022). 
 
Ambler class D or BJM group 2d comprises families, the most important one being the OXA 
family (Yoon & Jeong, 2021a). Despite sharing the ability to hydrolyse oxacillin, this family 
contains a wide range of enzymes with high variability both in sequence and in hydrolytic 
spectrum (Evans & Amyes, 2014).

ABSTRACT 
OXA-1 and OXA-10 β-lactamases have frequently been reported as extended spectrum β-
lactamases (ESBLs), especially as some of their variants exhibit ESBL or carbapenemase (CPE) 
activity. Due to their high prevalence and dissemination globally, it is important to elucidate their 
capacity to hydrolyse extended spectrum β-lactams and carbapenem antibiotics. The aim of this 
study was to clone blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 genes derived from Escherichia coli isolates exhibiting 
narrow spectrum or ESBL phenotypes with or without their natural promoters and examine the 
phenotypes of the resulting transformants. Nineteen E. coli isolates from food and food-producing 
animals, harbouring blaOXA-1 or blaOXA-10 genes, alone or in combination with narrow-spectrum β-
lactam genes, were collected. The isolates were genomically characterised, and their minimum 
inhibitory concentrations (MICs) determined by the broth microdilution method. E. coli TOP 10 
transformants harbouring blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 with their natural promoter and/or under the 
control of T7 promoter were generated. Transformants were whole-genome sequenced and their 
MICs determined. The transferability of both genes was assessed by conjugation experiments. 
Most isolates harboured blaOXA-1 on class 1 integrons with similar genetic contexts. When cloned 
with its natural promoter, OXA-1 maintained the antimicrobial resistance phenotype of the isolate 
of origin (narrow spectrum or ESBL), while under the control of the strong T7 promoter, the 
transformants exhibited ESBL (for blaOXA-1) or CPE (for blaOXA-10) phenotypes. However, OXA-
10 did not show CPE activity when tested with the modified carbapenem inactivation method. 
Moreover, neither of the genes were transferred by conjugation. The results revealed that blaOXA-
1 and blaOXA-10 genes should not be referred as ESBLs or carbapenemases and that carbapenemase 
or ESBL activity is more dependent on the gene copy number and/or the promoter for expression 
of the blaOXA genes. Other unknown resistance mechanism explaining ESBL or carbapenemase 
phenotypes in blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10-harbouring isolates as sole resistance determinants could not 
also be excluded.

INTRODUCTION 
OXA b-lactamases, named after their ability to hydrolyse oxacillin, are a heterogeneous family 
of class D b-lactamases with more than 1000 species identified (Evans & Amyes, 2014; Yoon & 
Jeong, 2021b). The family is divided into more than 25 different subfamilies based on the 
similarity in the aminoacidic sequence between enzymes (Yoon & Jeong, 2021b). Within these 
subfamilies, several representatives stand out, such as OXA-48, for its carbapenemase activity 
(Poirel et al., 2004), OXA-655 and OXA-656 variants of OXA-10 having a single amino acid 
substitution and conferring carbapenem resistance (Kotsakis et al., 2019). Among them, OXA-1 
and OXA-10 b-lactamases have been frequently reported as having extended spectrum b-
lactamase (ESBL) phenotype (BLDB, 2023; Boyle et al., 2011; Ute Wolff Sönksen, 2021).  
 
A large-scale screening performed in the European Nucleotide Archive (ENA) among the 
published sequences until 28 November 2018 showed that 12,680 and 921 isolates harboured 
blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 genes, respectively, regardless of the presence of other AMR genes 
(unpublished data). The most common bacterial species harbouring blaOXA-1 were Klebsiella 
pneumoniae, Escherichia coli, Shigella flexeri and the dominant ones for blaOXA-10 were Klebsiella 
pneumoniae, Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Escherichia coli (Figure 1).  These genes were found 
to be vastly disseminated globally, with blaOXA-1 being the most frequently detected in the UK and 
USA, while blaOXA-10 was most prevalent in Germany, China, Pakistan, among other countries 
(Figure 1). However, association with country or bacterial species is biased towards countries that 
sequence more bacterial isolates or regions where these bacteria are endemic.   
 
blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 were first identified in the 1980s (A. M. Philippon et al., 1983), however, 
the resistance phenotype conferred by both genes has still not been unambiguously defined. Both 
genes have been referenced as ESBLs by antimicrobial resistance gene (AMR) databases, such 
as the Beta-Lactamase Database (BLDB) (Naas et al., 2017), as well as in the literature (Boyle et 
al., 2011; El-Badawy et al., 2017; Malathi & Ramaiah, 2016) due to the classification of some 
closely related b-lactamases, such as OXA-11 and OXA-10-like b-lactamases as ESBLs (Hall et 
al., 1993). Moreover, in the 2021 DANMAP report, two E. coli isolates were described as 
harbouring blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 as a sole b-lactam resistance genes and reported as ESBLs (Ute 
==PAGE:20==
 
16 
Wolff Sönksen, 2021). Later, it was found that these isolates also carried extended spectrum b-
lactam resistance genes.

Therefore, due to the above-stated reasons, the objective of this study was to unambiguously 
elucidate the OXA-1 and OXA-10 hydrolysis capacity and resistance towards b-lactams and to 
clarify their role in conferring an ESBL phenotype in E. coli. To accomplish the objective, 
nineteen E. coli isolates recovered from food and food-producing animals in Europe and 
harbouring blaOXA-1 or blaOXA-10 alone or in combination with narrow-spectrum b-lactam resistance 
genes (e.g., blaTEM) were collected. Nine isolates with available sequencing and MIC data were 
chosen for further genomic characterisation and cloning experiments.

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
Bacterial isolates  
Nineteen blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10-harbouring E. coli strains isolated from caeca and meat from 
broilers, pigs, cattle and turkeys were used in this study (Table 1). The isolates were collected 
between 2015 and 2021 as part of i) the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) and the 
European Union Reference Laboratory for Antimicrobial Resistance (EURL-AR) confirmatory 
testing, ii) the “One Day in Denmark” project (Rebelo et al., 2022),  iii) the surveillance programs 
of the Netherlands (National Institute for Public Health and the Environment, RIVM), Norway 
(Norwegian Surveillance System for Antimicrobial Drug Resistance, NORM) and Germany 
(Federal Institute for Risk Assessment, BfR). All isolates harboured blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 genes 
as the only b-lactam resistance gene, except four isolates that carried additionally blaTEM (Table 
1, Table 2).  
Minimum inhibitory concentration determination 
The minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) was performed by the broth microdilution method 
using the EUVSEC3 and EUVSEC2 plates (Sensititre System, Thermo Fisher Scientific, 
Denmark). Isolates grown on blood agar (BA, SSI Diagnostica, Denmark) were checked for purity 
and several colonies were suspended in demineralized water to obtain optical density (OD) of 0.5 
McFarland standard. Ten µL of the adjusted solution were transferred into cation-adjusted 
Mueller Hinton broth and 50 µL of the solution dispensed into each well of the EUVSEC2 and 
EUVSEC3 plates using Sensititre AIM Automated Inoculation Delivery System (Sensititre 
System, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Denmark). Plates were incubated at 37ºC for 18 hours and the 
MIC values recorded. As a quality control, 1 µL of each bacterial suspension was streaked onto 
BA plates.

with bbtools v36.49 (Bushnell B., 2014) and assembled by SPAdes v3.15.3 (Prjibelski et al.,

the contigs were aligned in Geneious Prime 2023.0.4 using MAFFT v7.490 and annotated to study 
the genetic contexts of the genes. MOB-suite v3.1.0 was used for plasmid typing, to reconstruct 
plasmids from the short-read sequencing data and to associate plasmid types with blaOXA-1 and 
blaOXA-10 genes (Robertson et al., 2020; Robertson & Nash, 2018) .  
 
SNP-based analysis of the isolates was performed by CSI phylogeny (Kaas et al., 2014) and a 
phylogenetic tree inferred by IQ-TREE v1.16.12 and visualized with Geneious Prime. Promoter 
regions of the blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 genes were predicted by ProPr: Prokaryote Promoter 
Prediction v2.0 (http://ppp.molgenrug.nl ). Genomic and/or plasmid DNA from the transformants 
and transconjugants were extracted by Monarch Genomic DNA Purification kit and Monarch 
Plasmid Miniprep kit (BioNordika, Denmark), respectively. Genomic libraries were prepared 
using the Nextera XT DNA Library Preparation Kit (Illumina) and sequenced with a NextSeq 
500/550 Mid Output v2.5 Kit (Illumina) on a NextSeq 500 platform (Illumina). The copy numbers 
of the OXA genes in the original isolates and transformants were estimated by ccne v1.1.2 
(https://github.com/biojiang/ccne) using trimmed fastq files as input. The sequences of the blaOXA-
1 and blaOXA-10 genes with their promoter regions in the transformants were aligned to the pGBW-
m4046841 plasmid sequence using MAFFT to identify potential SNPs. The blaOXA-1 or blaOXA-10-
harbouring contigs were annotated by Prokka (Seemann, 2014) and the .gbf were used as input in 
clinker for pairwise alignments, gene cluster similarity and visualisation (Gilchrist & Chooi, 
2021). 
Cloning of blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 genes and their promoter regions  
Genomic DNA was extracted by the DNeasy Blood and Tissue Kit (Qiagen, Denmark). blaOXA-1 
and blaOXA-10 genes were amplified using PCR primers OXA-1-LLMF-F and OXA-1-LLMF-R, 
and OXA-10-LLMF-F and OXA-10-LLMF-R, respectively (Table S1). Additional primers OXA-
1-P-LLMF-F and OXA-1-P-LLMF-R (Table S1) were used for PCR amplification of blaOXA-1 
with its natural promoter. PCR products blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 were digested and cloned into the 
EarI and AgeI sites of the previously digested pGBW-m4046841 (CmR) plasmid, a gift from 
Ginkgo Bioworks & Benjie Chen (Addgene plasmid #145578; http://n2t.net/addgene:145578; 
RRID:Addgene_145578).  PCR product of blaOXA-1 with its natural promoter was digested with 
==PAGE:23==
 
19 
NdeI and AgeI and cloned into the AseI and AgeI sites of the previously digested pGBW-
m4046841 (CmR). The digested plasmid was treated with FastAP Thermosensitive Alkaline 
Phosphatase (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Denmark), following the manufacturer instructions, 
before ligation with T4 DNA Ligase (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Denmark).  
 
DNA gel electrophoresis was used to verify the correct digestion of the vector and amplification 
of insert before ligation. The resulting constructs P-OXA-TAPA, P-OXA-JAE, T7-OXA-1, T7-
OXA-10 were used to chemically transform One Shot TOP10 Chemically Competent E. coli 
(Thermo Fisher Scientific, Denmark), following the manufacturers recommendations. The 
transformants were selected on Luria-Bertani (LB) agar plates supplemented with 25 µg/mL 
chloramphenicol and 30 µg/mL ampicillin. The presence of the insert in the transformants was 
determined by a colony PCR using the above-mentioned primers. Primers pGBW-Cm-F and 
pGBW-Cm-R were used for detection of the plasmid pGBW-m4046841. The MICs of the 
resulting clones were determined by the broth microdilution method as previously described.  
Conjugation for transferability of blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 genes 
Conjugation experiments were carried out to assess whether the phenotype conferred by blaOXA-1 
and blaOXA-10 genes was transferrable. E. coli K12 J62-2 (RifR, lactose negative) and E. coli K12 
20R764 (RifR) were used as recipient strains. As donor strains E. coli OXA-TAPA (carrying 
blaOXA-1 on a plasmid) and E. coli OXA-2IDSA (carrying blaOXA-10 on a plasmid) were used. 
Overnight cultures of the recipient and donor strains were prepared in LB (Luria Bertani) broth 
supplemented with rifampicin (50 and 100 µg/ml) or ampicillin (30 µg/ml), respectively. Fresh 
cultures of the donor strains were prepared, and conjugation was performed when the donor 
strains reached the exponential phase, which was determined by OD measurements at 600 nm. 
Several washing steps were performed, and the donor and the recipient strains were placed on a 
0.45 µm filter and incubated at 37ºC for 2 h on LB agar. Filters were removed and rinsed, and the 
final solutions were plated on MacConkey agar plates supplemented with rifampicin (50 and 100 
µg/ml) and ampicillin (30 µg/ml) to select the transconjugants. 
Modified Carbapenem Inactivation test 
The transformant E. coli TOP10 T7-OXA-10, which showed decreased susceptibility to 
meropenem and ertapenem, was tested by the modified carbapenem inactivation method (mCIM) 
(James S. Lewis II, 2023) to study the capacity of the cloned blaOXA-10 gene to hydrolyse 
carbapenems. Briefly, 1 µL loopful biomass of E. coli TOP10 T7-OXA-10 was incubated with 
10 µg meropenem and 10 µg ertapenem disks at 35ºC for 4 h. A 0.5 McFarland suspension of E. 
==PAGE:24==
 
20 
coli ATCC 25922 was spread evenly over the surface of Mueller Hinton Agar (MHA) II plates 
by swabbing in three directions and the disks were placed on the agar surface. Plates were 
incubated at 35ºC for 18 h and inhibition zones were measured and interpreted by the CLSI 
guidelines (James S. Lewis II, 2023). As a positive control strain E. coli ESBL_20_0779, which 
harboured the blaVIM-1 gene was used. 
RESULTS 
Phenotypic characterisation of the blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 -harbouring strains  
Nineteen E. coli isolates harbouring blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 as only b-lactam resistance genes or in 
combination with blaTEM-1 gene were collected (Table 1). All isolates displayed carbapenem MIC 
values below the epidemiological cut-offs (ECOFFs) and showed high resistance to ampicillin 
(MIC > 64) (data not shown).  E. coli OXA-2IDSA, the only isolate harbouring blaOXA-10, showed 
no resistance to cephalosporins, except for elevated cefoxitin MIC value, which was close to the 
ECOFF and varied between 8 and 16 mg/L. Of the remaining isolates, E. coli OXA-TAPA 
showed a clear ESBL phenotype according to the EFSA guidelines (European Food Safety 
Authority & European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control, 2022b). This strain harboured 
both blaOXA-1 and  blaTEM-1B as the only detected b-lactam resistance gene.  
 
None of the isolates from the “One Day in Denmark” project showed resistance to ceftazidime 
nor cefotaxime, but two of them, E. coli OXA-D33 and E. coli OXA-D42, were resistant to 
cefoxitin (Table 1). Isolates E. coli OXA-523, E. coli OXA-867, E. coli OXA-1126, E. coli  OXA-
JAE, E. coli  OXA-U14, E. coli OXA-297, E. coli  OXA-1947, E. coli  OXA-2412, E. coli  OXA-
1274  and E. coli OXA-2114 were cefotaxime resistant,with MIC = 0.5 in all cases, except for E. 
coli OXA-1126 (MIC = 1 mg/L),  and cefoxitin susceptible (MIC ≤ 8 mg/L). All above-mentioned 
isolates, except E. coli OXA-2412 and E. coli OXA-JAE, exhibited synergy for cefotaxime-
clavulanic acid (MIC ≤ 0.06/4 mg/L). E. coli OXA-233 and E. coli OXA-31.41 were resistant to 
cefotaxime with MIC = 2 mg/L and MIC = 1 mg/L, respectively, and susceptible to cefoxitin. 
Both isolates showed no synergy of cefotaxime with clavulanic acid, MIC = 0.5 mg/L and MIC 
= 0.25 mg/L, respectively. All isolates harbouring blaOXA-1, regardless of the blaTEM presence, 
showed resistance to cefepime with MIC values ranging between 0.5 mg/L and 8 mg/L, except 
E. coli OXA-D33 and E. coli OXA-D42 (Table 1).

Specific mutations in the ampC promoter have previously been associated with increased 
cephalosporin resistance (Caroff et al., 1999). In this study, E. coli OXA-D33 and E. coli OXA-
D42 harboured two nucleotide changes, G28A and A12T. In the rest of the isolates, except E. coli

G18A were detected (Table 2 and Figure 3). The ampC promoter in E. coli OXA-TAPA and E. 
coli OXA-17FP strains were identical to the reference sequence (CP037449.1). Strains OXA-
2IDSA, OXA-31.41, OXA-523, OXA-867, OXA-D267 and OXA-JAE had three out of the four 
mutations described by Olsson et al. (1982) in their ampC promoter regions, in nucleotide 
positions −18, −1 and +58 (Figure 3).

E. coli OXA-TAPA and E. coli OXA-2IDSA were subjected to conjugation experiments, as they 
were the only two strains that harboured blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 genes, respectively, with high 
probability to be located on plasmids (Table 2).  Conjugation experiments were performed with 
rifampicin concentrations of 50 and 100 µg/ml to avoid acquiring resistance to rifampicin due to 
spontaneous mutations in the donor strains. Colonies growing in both rifampicin and ampicillin 
were subjected to MIC experiments and whole-genome sequencing (Table S4). The 
rep_cluster_658, associated with blaOXA-10 in E. coli OXA-2IDSA was predicted to be mobilizable 
but not conjugative, could not be transferred in two conjugation attempts (Table 2). The IncK2/Z 
plasmid, associated with blaOXA-1 in E. coli OXA-TAPA, was predicted to be conjugative. 
However, despite the two conjugation attempts, blaOXA-1 also could not be transferred. Only the 
conjugative plasmid IncFIA/IncFIB/IncFIC, harbouring blaTEM1-B, was transferred (Table 2, Table 
S4).

DISCUSSION 
The project was initiated with 18 E. coli isolates harbouring blaOXA-1 as the only b-lactam 
resistance gene, except for the narrow spectrum blaTEM genes in some of the isolates. Among these 
isolates, E. coli OXA-TAPA, harbouring blaOXA-1, was the only isolate that exhibited an ESBL 
phenotype (Table 1). Additionally, 13 of the isolates showed resistance to cefotaxime, in most of 
them the resistance was borderline (MIC = 0.5 mg/L), while exhibiting synergy with clavulanic 
acid (Table 1). Resistance to ceftazidime and cefoxitin was not common among the isolates, but 
decreased susceptibility to cefepime was observed in all of them (Table 1). 
 
Despite common traits in the antimicrobial susceptibility phenotype, the performed phylogenetic 
study showed that there was a high genetic diversity between the isolates (Figure 2). E. coli OXA-
D33 and E. coli OXA-D42 were the most phylogenetically similar isolates, in which identical 
AMR and plasmid replicon genes were detected (Table 2) and identical blaOXA-1 genetic 
environment with no similarity to the other phenotypes was identified (Figure 4). Additionally, 
these were the only isolates exhibiting cefoxitin resistance, explained by the detected unknown 
mutations in the ampC promoter in the attenuator region, previously associated with increased 
cefoxitin resistance (Figure 3) (Caroff et al., 1999). 
 
For most blaOXA-1 harbouring isolates, several plasmid replicon genes were detected but blaOXA-1 
location could not be associated with a plasmid for any of them (Table 2). This was supported by 
the NCBI BLASTn results, for which correlation between NCBI results and the in silico 
prediction of chromosomal or plasmid location were obtained for most isolates (Table S3).  
Among the  blaOXA-1 harbouring isolates, only E. coli OXA-TAPA was predicted to be located on 
plasmid IncK2/Z (Table 2). IncK2/Z is a low copy plasmid commonly containing mobile-genetic 
elements and AMR genes (Zhang et al., 2019). According to the estimated copy number of blaOXA-
1 in the isolates analysed in this study, the predicted number of copies of the gene was always 
approximately one copy, regardless of plasmid or chromosomal location.   
 
Except E. coli OXA-D33 and E. coli OXA-D42, the other strains exhibited a similar blaOXA-1 
genetic environment regardless of their association with plasmid or chromosome. In all cases 
blaOXA-1 was located on an integron class 1 and as a part of a resistance gene cassette (Figure 4). 
The presence of blaOXA-1 in class 1 integrons is not uncommon, as similar genetic environments 
have been previously described (Mo et al., 2022; Wyrsch et al., 2022).  
  
==PAGE:36==
 
32 
The possible effect of the promoter on the blaOXA-1 expression was examined for all isolates. All 
of them harboured a Pc promoter, located within the Int1 coding sequence, and a P2 promoter 
(Figure 5). The Pc (P1) promoter has been previously described to have a weak expression (PcW) 
(Jové et al., 2010). Despite the presence of both promoters, the expression of genes controlled by 
this combination of promoters has been described to be mainly driven by P2 (Jové et al., 2010), 
in which two SNPs among the isolates were identified.  All isolates harboured intact triplet GGG 
downstream of the P2 promoter. The presence of this triplet has been described to increase the 
expression of the downstream gene, in this case blaOXA-1 (Boyle et al., 2011). Additionally, it was 
reported that when the triplet GGG was associated with blaOXA-1, it leads to increased resistance 
to cefepime (Boyle et al., 2011), which is in line with the antimicrobial susceptibility profiles of 
the isolates in this study (Table 1). 1). The only isolates that did not exhibit high cefepime 
resistance (OXA-D33 and E. coli OXA-D42) had different blaOXA-1 gene regulation. 
 
Two SNPs were detected within the P2 promoter sequence in the strains in this study (Figure 5).  
E. coli OXA-TAPA, E. coli OXA-JAE and E. coli OXA-31.41 were selected for the cloning 
experiments, as each of them harboured a different SNP in the P2 promoter region. However, 
only transformants harbouring E. coli OXA-TAPA and E. coli OXA-JAE blaOXA-1 promoter 
regions were obtained. An additional transformant harbouring blaOXA-1 under the control of the 
strong T7 promoter was also obtained. Despite having identical blaOXA-1 genes, it was noted that 
the different promoters had a high effect on the antimicrobial susceptibility phenotypes of the 
transformants (Table 3). The transformant P-OXA-JAE exhibited susceptibility to all 
cephalosporins, including cefepime despite having the triplet GGG (one two-fold below ECOFF) 
(Table 3).  
 
Meanwhile both transformants T7-OXA-1 and P-OXA-TAPA exhibited an ESBL phenotype due 
to cefotaxime resistance and synergy with clavulanic acid (Table 3), which was in line with the 
initial phenotypic characterization (Table 1), as cefotaxime resistance was more prevalent than 
ceftazidime resistance among the isolates. P-OXA-TAPA and P-OXA-JAE harboured an 
identical blaOXA-1 gene cloned in the same high copy plasmid. Both transformants exhibited an 
identical promoter region, except for a SNP in nucleotide position 151 (Figure 5). We 
hypothesised that this SNP difference would cause a change in the strength of the promoter, 
having a strong promoter for the P-OXA-TAPA transformant while a weak promoter for the P-
OXA-JAE transformant. These results correlate with the  blaOXA-1 phenotypic profile when cloned 
under the strong T7 promoter. In fact, overexpression effects under the strong T7 promoter have 
been described to increase resistance to previously susceptible antibiotics in other OXA b-
lactamases, such as OXA-23 (Colquhoun et al., 2021) . In other cases, such as for OXA-82, 
overexpression was mediated by insertion elements  (Zander et al., 2013) 
==PAGE:37==
 
33 
 
The transferability of the ESBL phenotype in E. coli OXA-TAPA harbouring blaOXA-1 by 
conjugation of the IncK2/Z plasmid was unsuccessful despite the two conjugation attempts. Only 
the blaTEM1-B gene along with sul2 and other resistance genes were transferred (Table S4). The 
transferability of blaTEM-1B gene solely was due to its association with a plasmid, 
IncFIA/IncFIC/IncFIB, IncFIC, rep_cluster_224, different from the one associated with blaOXA-1 
gene (Table 2).  Results on the transferability of the ESBL phenotype in E. coli OXA-TAPA could 
have provided more information on the effect of the plasmidic location in blaOXA-1 expression. 
 
Regarding the blaOXA-10 gene, the original isolate harbouring the gene did not show resistance to 
any cephalosporins, except borderline resistance to cefoxitin (Table 1), which could be explained 
by the detected mutations in the ampC promoter (Caroff et al., 1999) (Figure 3). blaOXA-10 was 
unequivocally associated with a plasmid, which was demonstrated by its co-location with a 
plasmid replicon gene on the same contig (Figure 6, Table 2). The in-silico prediction associated 
the gene to the plasmid rep_cluster_658, which was predicted to be mobilizable and with an 
estimated copy number of two (Table 2, Table 4). The attempt to transfer blaOXA-10 gene by 
classical conjugation was not successful and no transconjugants were obtained, which was in line 
with the in-silico prediction of the plasmid transferability (Table S4). 
 
When blaOXA-10 gene was cloned on a high copy vector with the strong T7 promoter, it led to 
overexpression of the gene. Under the control of a strong promoter and a high copy plasmid (Table 
4), blaOXA-10 exhibited resistance to ceftazidime and cefotaxime with no synergy (Table 3). 
Surprisingly, it also exhibited a carbapenemase phenotype (Table 3). Similar results about 
carbapenems were obtained for the closely related blaOXA-656 and blaOXA-10, when cloned under the 
pTetOL1 promoter using the high copy plasmid PZE21 (Kotsakis et al., 2019). In both cases, the 
genes were overexpressed and similar increased MIC values for meropenem, ertapenem and 
imipenem were obtained. Despite this, the hydrolysis studies showed low efficiency for 
carbapenem hydrolysis in both OXA-10 and OXA-656 (Kotsakis et al., 2019), which supports the 
results obtained in the mCIM test in our study (Figure 7). Moreover, though as a poor substrate, 
cefotaxime hydrolysis by blaOXA-10 was detectable (Kotsakis et al., 2019), supporting the above-
mentioned MIC values in this study (Table 3).  
 
In conclusion, the MICs results of the transformants clearly showed that the phenotypes 
exhibited by blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10  genes are dependent on the gene copy number and the level of 
expression of the genes. When blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 are present in multiple copies and are under 
the control of a strong promoter can exhibit ESBL and carbapenemase activity, respectively. 
However, ESBL and carbapenem antibiotics are not easily hydrolysed by these enzymes in 
==PAGE:38==
 
34 
normal conditions; only when they are overexpressed and a high ratio of enzyme vs. substrate is 
generated. This magnifies the low hydrolytic effect of the OXA-1 and OXA-10 that is seen 
towards these antibiotics, generating an antimicrobial resistant phenotype towards them. It should 
be noted that enzyme overexpression for blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 is not commonly found in nature.  
Furthermore, as there is no experimental evidence in the published literature that OXA-1 and 
OXA-10 can be classified as ESBL or carbapenemases and therefore they should be regarded as 
b-lactamases with a narrow spectrum activity.  
FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
To provide further strength the hypothesis of the role of blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 in providing an 
ESBL phenotype, several experiments could be performed. The cloning of the above-mentioned 
genes under the control of a weak promoter and the study of the antimicrobial susceptibility 
phenotype of the transformants would provide a complete overview of the effect of the strength 
of the promoter on these genes. Additionally, both low and high copy plasmids could be used. 
Moreover, microarrays analysis to study the level of expression of blaOXA-1 and blaOXA-10 of both 
the original isolates and the obtained transformants could complement and support the already 
obtained evidence. Finally, enzymology studies could be performed to determine the hydrolytic 
activity of the genes to the b-lactams of interest.

ABSTRACT 
The lack of porins and/or their malfunction is one of the main b-lactams resistance mechanisms 
in Enterobacteriaceae, particularly to cephalosporins such as cefoxitin. The objective of this 
study was to determine if porin malfunction was the resistance mechanism behind the increased 
cefoxitin resistance in ampicillin susceptible Salmonella and E. coli strains with no b-lactam 
resistance genes detected. Eighteen Salmonella and two E. coli isolates exhibiting this unusual  b-
lactam resistant phenotype were included in this study. An extensive bioinformatic analysis of the 
porin and porin-related genes was performed along with cloning experiments of the main porin 
genes, ompC and ompF, of the E. coli isolates. Antimicrobial susceptibility testing, SEM imaging 
and growth studies were performed on the generated transformants. The analysis revealed that 
both E. coli isolates harboured a high number of non-synonymous mutations in both ompC and 
ompF genes, including a premature stop codon in the ompF gene in one of the E. coli isolates. 
Antimicrobial susceptibility studies on the resulting transformants revealed that OmpF 
dysfunctionality/absence was related to cefoxitin but not to ampicillin resistance, showing that 
ompF is the main route of entry of cefoxitin in the bacterial cell and its absence or alteration is 
most likely to cause the unusual antimicrobial resistance phenotype of the E. coli isolates.

INTRODUCTION 
The outer membrane (OM) of Gram-negative bacteria constitutes a barrier for many toxic 
compounds, such as antibiotics (Vergalli et al., 2020). The OM has a complex composition, 
including lipoproteins, phospholipids, lipopolysaccharides and porins (Delcour, 2009). Porins are 
outer membrane proteins found in Gram-negative bacteria which allow the diffusion of small 
hydrophilic solutes with a molecular weight less than 600 Da (Delcour, 2009; Vergalli et al., 
2020). To permit the diffusion of solutes, porins adopt a b-barrel structure formed by antiparallel 
b-strands formed by hydrophobic and hydrophilic residues (Delcour, 2009; Fernández & 
Hancock, 2012; Vergalli et al., 2020). These b-strands are combined with several a-helix loops, 
where the most important is the loop 3 (L3) which helps creating a selective electrostatic field in 
the middle of the porin channel (Delcour, 2009; Fernández & Hancock, 2012; Vergalli et al., 
2020).   
 
Figure 1. 3D model of E. coli ATCC 25922 OmpC generated with SWISS-MODEL. Red colour represents a-helix 
structures, yellow represents b-strand structures and green represents coil structures. (A) Vertical vision of 3D model 
OmpC of E. coli ATCC 25922. (B) Transversal vision of 3D model of OmpC of E. coli ATCC 25922. 
 
Porins constitute highly abundant proteins in the cell membrane with a complex regulation 
affected by elements such as chemical stress and osmolarity (Dam et al., 2018). Variation in their 
levels of expression impacts various aspects in the bacterial cell, such as virulence, antimicrobial 
susceptibility, growth potential, outer membrane integrity, among others (Camprubí-Font et al., 
2019; Choi & Lee, 2019).  
 
==PAGE:45==
 
41 
Porin malfunction or lack of porins have shown to provide resistance to b-lactams in 
Enterobacteriaceae, including Klebsiella pneumoniae (Shi et al., 2013), Escherichia coli 
(Ziervogel & Roux, 2013) and Enterobacter aerogenes (Yigit et al., 2002). This has been 
previously described to increase the resistance to cephalosporin in E. coli, especially cefoxitin, 
while still exhibiting ampicillin susceptibility (Jaffe et al., 1982).  
 
In the recent years, there has been an emergence of Salmonella and E. coli strains isolated from 
food and food producing animals in Europe associated with cefoxitin resistance and ampicillin 
susceptibility that lack any known b-lactam resistance genes. The objective of this project was to 
characterise the genomes of eighteen Salmonella and two E. coli strains that exhibit such 
phenotype with the objective to determine if porin malfunction was the antimicrobial resistance 
mechanism behind this uncommon phenotype. To accomplish the objective, we initially 
performed an extensive bioinformatic study of the porin genes and further cloning experiments 
to study the effects of porin mutations on the antimicrobial susceptibility, growth potential and 
membrane integrity of the affected strains.  
MATERIALS AND METHODS 
Bacterial isolates 
Two E. coli  and eighteen Salmonella strains from caeca and meat broilers, pigs, cattle and turkeys 
were used for this study. The isolates were collected between 2014 to 2020 and analysed at the 
European Union Reference Laboratory for Antimicrobial Resistance (EURL-AR) in the context 
of the confirmatory testing held by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) that consist of 
MIC determination by broth microdilution and whole-genome sequencing (WGS) by Illumina. 
An additional Salmonella Orion strain exhibiting ampicillin susceptibility and cefoxitin MIC > 8 
mg/L was also included.  
Minimum inhibitory concentration determination 
The minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) was performed by the broth microdilution method 
using the EUVSEC3 and EUVSEC2 plates (Sensititre System, Thermo Fisher Scientific, 
Denmark). Isolates grown on blood agar (BA, SSI Diagnostica, Denmark) were checked for purity 
and several colonies were suspended in demineralized water to obtain optical density of 0.5 
McFarland Standard. 10 µL of the adjusted solution were transferred into cation-adjusted Mueller 
==PAGE:46==
 
42 
Hinton broth and 50 µL of the solution dispensed into each well of the EUVSEC2 and EUVSEC3 
plates using Sensititre AIM™ Automated Inoculation Delivery System (Sensitire System, 
Thermo Fisher Scientific, Denmark). Plates were incubated at 37ºC for 18 hours for further 
reading of the results. As a quality control, 1 uL of each bacterial suspension was streaked onto 
BA plates.  
Genomic characterization of the E. coli and Salmonella isolates 
The raw sequencing data were subjected to quality check by FastQC v.0.11.5 (Andrews, S., 2010), 
trimming with bbtools v36.49 (Bushnell B., 2014) and assembly by SPAdes v3.15.3 (Prjibelski 
et

all

v1.6 
(https://bitbucket.org/genomicepidemiology/foodqcpipeline/src/master/). Assemblies quality 
was evaluated by Quast v5.0.2 (Gurevich et al., 2013). MLST profiles were determined using 
mlst (https://github.com/tseemann/mlst). ResFinder v4.1 (Bortolaia et al., 2020) and  CARD 
(Alcock et al., 2023) databases were used to screen the isolates for acquired antibiotic resistance 
genes and plasmids with default minimum identification and coverage thresholds (90% and 60%, 
respectively) using ABRicate (https://github.com/tseemann/abricate). AMRFinderPlus v3.10.42 
was also used to identify AMR genes and point mutations (Feldgarden et al., 2019). 
 
Porin encoding and regulating gene sequences from E. coli and Salmonella isolates, were 
extracted from the fasta files by getfasta in bedtools v2.30.0 (Quinlan & Hall, 2010) and aligned 
with MAFFT v1.5.0 in Geneious Prime 2023.0.4. Escherichia coli ATTC 25922 (GenBank 
accession number NC_000913.3) and Salmonella enterica LT2 (GenBank accession number 
NC_003197.2) were used as references. EMBOSS (Rice et al., 2000) in Geneious Prime 2023.0.4 
was used to predict the secondary structure of porin genes. to generate 3D models of E. coli OmpC 
and OmpF porins, SWISS-MODEL was used (Waterhouse et al., 2018). Salmonella serotypes 
were determined using SeqSero-1.2 (https://github.com/denglab/SeqSero ). 
Preparation of electrocompetent porin deficient E. coli strains 
E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) DompF, E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) DompC and E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) 
DompCF were a gift from Jack Leo (Addgene plasmid #102259, Addgene plasmid #102258, 
Addgene plasmid #102265). Overnight cultures (16-h) of strains E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) 
DompF, E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) DompC and E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) DompCF were prepared 
in Luria-Bertani low-salt (LB-LS) broth. Fresh cultures were prepared by inoculating 2 mL 
overnight culture in 200 mL LB-LS broth and incubated until it reaches OD600 = 0.4-0.6. Cells 
were centrifuged at 4°C, 4500 rpm for 10 min, supernatant removed, and pellets resuspended in 
==PAGE:47==
 
43 
cold 10% glycerol followed by centrifugation. Glycerol washes were performed three times, on 
ice and finally the pellets were combined and resuspended in 1 ml 10% glycerol. Aliquots of 50 
µL electrocompetent cells were flashed frozen with liquid nitrogen and stored at -80ºC.  
Cloning of ompC and ompF genes and their promoter regions  
Genomic DNA was extracted either by DNeasy Blood and Tissue Kit (Qiagen, Denmark) or 
Monarch HMW DNA Extraction Kit for Tissue (New England Biolabs, Denmark), following the 
manufacturers recommendations. The genes ompC, ompF and ompCF were amplified from 
strains E. coli AMP-ZTA18, E. coli AMP-19F and the E. coli ATCC  25922 by PCR with primers 
shown in Table S1. pGBW-m4046841 was amplified by PCR for assembly of ompC, ompF and 
both ompC and ompF using the primers in Table S1. Plasmid pGBW-m4046841 was a gift from 
Ginkgo Bioworks & Benjie Chen (Addgene plasmid #145578; http://n2t.net/addgene:145578; 
RRID:Addgene_145578). All PCR assays were performed using Q5® High-Fidelity DNA 
Polymerase (New England Biolabs, Denmark), followed by a two-step PCR for the amplification 
of gDNA fragments and a three-step PCR for the amplification of the plasmid fragments (Table 
S2).  
 
PCR products were purified using PureLink™ PCR Purification Kit (Thermofisher, Denmark) 
and the amplified fragments were assembled using the Gibson Assembly Master Mix (New 
England Biolabs, Denmark) following the manufacturers protocol. pGMW-OmpC, pGMW-
OmpF and pGMW-OmpCF were the final constructs obtained for each strain (Figure S1, Table 
S3). Gel electrophoresis was used to verify the correct amplification of the fragments and the size 
of the final constructs. The final constructs were transformed by electroporation into 
electrocompetent strains E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) DompF, E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) DompC and 
E. coli BL21 Gold (DE3) DompCF. Transformants were selected on LB-LS agar plates, 
supplemented with 25 µg/mL chloramphenicol. The MICs of the resulting transformants were 
obtained using the broth microdilution method (Sensititre System) as previously described. The 
presence of the cloned ompC and ompF genes in the transformants was verified by PCR using the 
appropriate primers (Table S1).

Genomic characterisation of the E. coli and Salmonella strains of the study 
E. coli AMP-ZTA18 was identified as sequence type (ST) 115 (Table 2). E. coli AMP-19F was 
not typeable due to a novel allele of the adk gene (Online Supplementary Material). No 
antimicrobial resistance genes were detected for the isolate E. coli AMP-ZTA18 and only tet(A)

The alignment of the porin-related genes in the Salmonella strains revealed several single 
nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in all isolates (Table S5, Table S6). Most of the detected SNPs 
were synonymous (Table S6). As for the main porins OmpC and OmpF, all isolates had a 
substitution leading to G173D in the OmpF, while only isolate, Salmonella AMP-S-SAL, had 
substitutions leading to N153D, A154E, R155H, P107K and S105N in OmpC (Table 3). Due to 
the few non-synonymous mutations in the porin genes in the 18 Salmonella isolates and the lack 
of correlation between mutations in ompC and ompF and the observed phenotypes, it was unlikely 
that mutations in these genes would be responsible for the observed cefoxitin, cefotaxime and 
ceftazidime resistance in the Salmonella isolates.

Growth analysis of E. coli isolates, porin deficient strains and transformants 
The effect of lack of porins and porin mutations in the fitness of the isolates was studied by growth 
analysis. The exponential phase values (µ) were similar for all isolates both in LB and LB-LS 
media (Table S8, Table S9). The lag phase (l) values were higher for the isolate grown in LB 
than in LB-LS. The stationary phase values (A) were lower for the isolates grown in LB than in 
LB-LS. Growth potential (AUC) was higher for all strains in LB-LS media (Table S8, Table S9). 
 
The growth potential of E. coli ATCC and E. coli ZTA18 was comparable, indicating that the 
fitness of the isolate is not affected (Table 8). As for the isolate E. coli 19F, the AUC values show 
a slight reduction compared to the reference strain, suggesting that its growth is less efficient. 
Growth potential values of porin deficient strains were akin and displayed little difference 
between the strains with deletion of only one or both porins. When compared to the reference 
strain values, the growth potential of E. coli ∆ompC, ∆ompF and ∆ompCF were clearly reduced, 
showing the fitness cost of the absence of the above-mentioned proteins (Table 8).

SEM Imaging of the E. coli isolates, porin deficient strains and transformants 
The size and shape of the different E. coli isolates, porin deficient strains and transformants were 
studied with scanning electron microscopy (SEM). E. coli is a rod-shaped gram-negative bacteria 
that in usual conditions is between 1 to 2µm long (National Research Council (US) Steering 
Group for the Workshop on Size Limits of Very Small Microorganisms, 1999). The SEM images 
generated in this study revealed a difference in the size between E. coli ATCC 25922 harbouring 
the wild-type OmpF and OmpC porins and isolates E. coli AMP-19F and E. coli AMP-ZTA18 
with non-wild type OmpF and OmpC porins (Figure 8). E. coli ATCC had an average length size 
of 2 µm, ranging from cells of 1.5 µm to 4 µm, the last ones possibly being in a cell division.

E. coli AMP-19F and E. coli AMP-ZTA18 displayed half of the size of E. coli ATCC 25922, with

DISCUSSION 
The project involved two E. coli and eighteen Salmonella isolates displaying ampicillin 
susceptibility and cefoxitin resistance (Table 1). No b-lactam resistance genes, except for blaTEM 
in one Salmonella isolate, were detected (Table 2). No known point mutations in ampC were 
detected in any of the isolates (Table 2). Thus, these results indicated that the reason behind these 
antimicrobial susceptibility phenotypes was not a known antimicrobial resistance gene or a point 
mutation. Due to the previous correlation of porin malfunction with providing resistance to b-
lactams (Jaffe et al., 1982; Shi et al., 2013), the porin and porin-related genes were studied. The 
alignment of porin regulatory and porin genes for Salmonella strains showed almost no non-
synonymous mutations and when they did, most of them were shared between the isolates (Table 
3, Table S6). Additionally, 15 Salmonella Infantis were the same ST, and 13 of them shared the 
same AMR gene profile (Table 2). This indicated that there might be a clonal relationship between 
them. This added to the fact that a low number of mutations were detected in ompC and ompF 
genes (Table 3), lead to the conclusion that it was highly unlikely that porin malfunction was 
behind the antimicrobial susceptibility phenotype of the strains. Thus, the Salmonella strains were 
not subjected to further cloning experiments.  
 
On the other hand, both E. coli strains exhibited multiple mutations in the porin regulatory genes 
(Table 4). The number of mutations detected in these genes are highly unlikely to generate a non-
functional protein, but some might have an effect on the porin regulation if, for example, are 
located in e.g. a DNA binding site. Despite that, porins follow a complex regulation where 
different actors take place (Dam et al., 2018). Therefore, if the mutations in the porin regulatory 
genes have an effect on porin expression, it might be slight. 
 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
 
60 
When examining the main porin genes, ompC and ompF, a high number of non-synonymous 
mutations were detected for both genes, including deletions (Figure 2, Figure 3). The most 
relevant mutation was premature the stop codon detected in position 358 of the ompF gene in E. 
coli AMP-19F (Figure 3).  Most of the mutations were shared between the two strains (Figure 2, 
Figure 3). The high number of detected mutations indicated a high probability of protein 
malfunction, therefore this was further studied with the prediction of the secondary structure and 
creating a 3D model of the proteins. Prediction of the secondary structure provided some 
information (Figure 4, Figure 6), but it does not consider interactions between amino acids that 
are located away in the sequence which might interact when adopting the quaternary structure. 
Therefore, 3D models were generated. 
 
The obtained 3D models of OmpC of E. coli AMP-19F and E. coli AMP-ZTA18 clearly showed 
that both proteins have a high chance of being unfunctional, as they cannot adopt the enclosed b-
barrel structure (Figure 5). Therefore, this indicates it is highly unlikely that these proteins are 
expressed in the cell membrane.  
 
3D models for OmpF showed higher variation (Figure 7). For the isolate E. coli AMP-19F it was 
clear that the protein might not be expressed in the cell membrane due to the premature stop codon 
(Figure 3, Figure 7), as the predicted 3D model adopted an opened b-barrel structure. For the 
isolate E. coli AMP-ZTA18, the model was a grosso modo highly similar to the wild type porin 
(Figure 7). Despite this, E. coli AMP-ZTA18 OmpF is missing two b-strand structures and has 
different orientation of b-strands and a-helix structures located in the centre of the porin channel 
(Figure 7). This orientation change might cause changes on the electrostatic field and size of the 
pore (Vergalli et al., 2020). Therefore, it is possible that the protein is expressed but its 
functionality might be altered.   
 
Unfortunately, the antimicrobial susceptibility phenotypes did not reveal anything about the 
functionality of the above-mentioned proteins (Table 5, Table 6, Table 7). In all of cases, when 
cloning the wild-type porins, the wild-type phenotype was not restored (Table 5, Table 6, Table 
7). This led to the conclusion that the proteins were not expressed or that the level of expression 
was too low. Despite this, the antimicrobial susceptibility studies of the porin deficient strains 
revealed that the malfunctioning OmpF was probably the reason behind elevated cefoxitin MIC 
values (Table 5, Table 6, Table 7) as only when OmpF is functional, the porin deficient strains 
were susceptible to all b-lactams (Table 6). OmpC seemed to have no effect on b-lactam 
resistance, not even having a synergistic effect as the antimicrobial resistance was not increased 
(Table 5, Table 7).  
==PAGE:65==
 
 
 
61 
 
Jaffe et. al. and Ziervogel and Roux respectively demonstrated that both cefoxitin and ertapenem 
might interact with OmpF to enter into the cell (Jaffe et al., 1982; Ziervogel & Roux, 2013). 
Similar results were obtained by Choi & Lee, that observed an increase in cefoxitin and 
ceftazidime resistance when generating and OmpF deficient strain (Choi & Lee, 2019).  These 
correlates with the antimicrobial susceptibility phenotypes seen both in the original strains (Table 
1), as well as in the OmpF porin deficient strains (Table 5, Table 7). It has also been demonstrated 
that ampicillin interacts with OmpF to enter into the cell (Ghai et al., 2018; Ziervogel & Roux, 
2013), but constant susceptibility indicates that it has an alternative mechanism of entry. In fact, 
Kojima & Nikaido, demonstrated that ampicillin can follow a spontaneous passive diffusion 
kinetics, suggesting it does not need to specifically interact with a protein (Kojima & Nikaido, 
2013).  
 
The growth analysis of the strains indicated that E. coli AMP-19F showed a reduced growth 
potential compared to E. coli AMP-ZTA18 and E. coli ATCC (Table 8). This is in line with the 
obtained results in the 3D models of OmpC and OmpF (Figure 5, Figure 7), which showed an 
aberrant structure for both proteins, supporting the idea that neither of them might be expressed. 
For E. coli AMP-ZTA18 the growth potential was unaltered (Table 8), but again the 3D model 
for OmpF showed it was able to adopt the b-barrel structure (Figure 7). This supports the idea 
that the protein might be expressed in the cell membrane but showing an altered functionality. 
Results of growth potential of the transformants did not provide information about the expression 
of the cloned porins, only that harbouring the constructs implied a fitness cost for the cell (Table 
9, Table 10, Table 11).  
 
SEM images showed a 1µm size reduction in strains lacking porin genes in respect to the reference 
strain, E. coli ATCC (Figure 8). Both isolates, E. coli AMP-19F and E. coli AMP-ZTA18 also 
showed size reduction (Figure 8), supporting the idea of lack of porin expression. It should be 
noted that no difference between lacking OmpC, OmpF or both porins could be extracted from 
the results, therefore, E. coli AMP-ZTA18 could still express OmpF with altered function, and 
still show reduced size if OmpC was not expressed. When analysing the transformant results, a 
range of sizes could be detected (Figure S2) but was not conclusive enough to state whether the 
cloned porins were expressed or not. Most probably, they were expressed only for some of them 
that showed increased size (Figure S2), explaining why the wild-type antimicrobial susceptibility 
phenotype was not restored in the transformants harbouring the wild-type porins (Table 5, Table 
6, Table 7).  
 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
 
62 
Finally, the results in this project revealed that OmpC and OmpF dysfunctionality may not be the 
resistance mechanism behind the cefoxitin resistance in the studied Salmonella strains due to the 
few mutations detected in the porin genes. However, the genomic characterisation of the porins 
and the corresponding protein structures in the E. coli isolates clearly showed a possible lack of 
functionality of both ompF and ompC. Despite the possible dysfunctionality of OmpC in both E. 
coli strains, the results showed that it did not affect b-lactam susceptibility. However, OmpF 
dysfunctionality increased cefoxitin, but also ertapenem and meropenem resistance. In the case 
of E. coli AMP-19F, the resistance mechanism behind the cefoxitin resistance was, most likely,  
the lack of expression of OmpF due to the premature stop codon; while in E. coli AMP-ZTA18, 
it was possibly due to an altered OmpF functionality. Ampicillin susceptibility in the ompF 
mutants implied that this antibiotic might have an alternative way of entry into the cell, possibly 
by passive diffusion. 
FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
To further study the role of the OmpF and OmpC porins in the E. coli isolates, the ompF and 
ompC porin genes could be cloned with a strong promoter (such as T7) to ensure high gene 
expression for further analysis using SDS-PAGE experiments or using His-Tag to enable protein 
purification using affinity chromatography. Antibiotic influx and efflux studies could be 
performed to study the alternative mechanisms of entry of ampicillin. Regarding the Salmonella 
strains, it would be interesting to further examine the sequences of other porin genes, such as 
nmpC and to perform a phylogenetic study of the strains.
