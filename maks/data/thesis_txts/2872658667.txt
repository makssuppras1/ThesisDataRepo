Abstract
CO electrolysis (CO2E) is gaining increasing attention in the scientific community as it ad-
dresses the challenges of sustainable fuel production and climate change. Among the various
device configurations developed for CO2E, zero-gap anion exchange membrane (AEM) cells
show a lot of promise. One of the critical challenges that hinders the large-scale deployment
of these systems is the complex relationship between membrane selectivity, water transport,
and morphological characteristics that are closely related to the size of ionic channels and the
membrane’s hydration level [3] [4].
In this work, we investigate these relationships as a foundation for the future design
of AEMs for high-performance CO2E. Using radiation-grafted NH+
4 -functionalized ETFE
and LDPE backbones, we examine the impact of backbone chemistry and degree of graft-
ing (DoG) on the internal nanostructure, water uptake, swelling, and ion exchange capacity
(IEC). Structural features were characterized by Small-Angle X-ray Scattering (SAXS) under
dry, hydrated, and electrolyte-saturated conditions, showing D-spacing values in the 27–38 nm
range and distinct morphological trends between ETFE and LDPE. SAXS analysis revealed
anisotropic features and peaks broadening at high DoG, suggesting hydration-induced disor-
der. Electrochemical performance was evaluated in a custom-built zero-gap single cell. Cell
tests confirmed CO and H2 production with Faradaic efficiencies up to 80%. To support SAXS
interpretation, an intuitive Python-based analysis tool was developed and validated on ref-
erence materials. It features 1D and 2D analysis, Background subtraction, Gaussian fitting,
and sector-specific mapping, offering a reproducible and accessible framework for polymer
nanostructure analysis.

Introduction
Amid the green transition, the recycling of CO2 represents a crucial step toward achieving
climate neutrality by 2050, as outlined in the European Green Deal [5]. To significantly con-
tribute to reducing greenhouse gas emissions, technologies for CO2 electrochemical reduction
must advance to the stage of industrial scalability. Scalable systems will enable the sustain-
able production of valuable chemical feedstocks such as carbon monoxide, methane, formic
acid, ethanol, and ethylene, offering both environmental and economic benefits. To meet the
requirements for industrial adoption, these devices must operate at current densities exceeding
200 mA/cm2 and at cell voltages below 3 V, ensuring sufficient efficiency, selectivity, stability,
and economic viability [4]. Emerging Power-to-X conversion technologies depend heavily on
the interaction between ions, water, and gas transport, all of which are intrinsically linked
to cell architecture and membrane selection. Understanding and optimizing these transport
phenomena are essential to overcoming current limitations, as highlighted in recent advances
within the field [3] [1] [6].
Electrochemical CO2 reduction (CO2E) systems are classified into three reactor configu-
rations: H-cells, zero-gap reactors, and hybrid reactors. H-cells are batch-type systems suited
for catalyst studies due to the possibility of easily placing a reference electrode close to the
cathode. On the other hand, they are affected by the low CO2 solubility in aqueous solution,
limiting the current densities, and also by the high ohmic loss due to the distance between
the electrodes. Zero-gap reactors are the most industrially promising configuration, and they
are also called flow reactors due to the continuous circulation of cathodic and anodic feed-
stocks. In this setup, the anode and cathode gas diffusion electrodes (GDEs) are pressed
directly against an ion exchange membrane (IEM), forming a membrane-electrode assembly
(MEA). This design minimizes the distance between electrodes, enhancing mass transport
and reducing ohmic losses, thus enabling higher current densities and efficiency. Due to all
these reasons, a zero-gap CO2E will be used in this study. Hybrid reactors incorporate a
circulating electrolyte layer between the cathode and the membrane, which allows better pH
control but suffers from increased resistance.
The types of ion exchange membranes used in CO2E are cation exchange membranes
(CEMs), anion exchange membranes (AEMs), and bipolar membranes (BPMs). While CEMs
like Nafion are common and exchanging protons reach high ionic conductivity, they often cre-
ate acidic environments that favor the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER), thereby reducing
CO2E selectivity. AEMs are currently considered the most promising for CO2 electrolysis, es-
pecially in flow reactors. They enable operation in alkaline conditions, which suppresses HER
and improves product selectivity. Furthermore, they allow the use of less expensive catalysts
and cell materials. Advanced AEMs such as Sustainion, PiperION, and Aemion(CHECK IF I
HAVE TO PUT SYMBOLS FOR USING THE COMMERCIAL NAME) have demonstrated

high current densities (>500 mA/cm2) [7], and excellent selectivity for C1 and C2 products,
while effectively suppressing the competing hydrogen evolution reaction (HER). Their ability
to facilitate CO2E in high-pH environments while minimizing energy losses makes them lead-
ing candidates for scalable, efficient CO2 conversion systems. Due to all these reasons, this
study will focus on new types of AEMs. BPMs combine CEM and AEM layers and are useful
in niche applications but present added complexity. For these reasons, AEMs in zero-gap
configurations have been selected as the core focus of this research.
AEMs are widely used in recent CO2E reactors, building on their proven success in wa-
ter electrolysis. These membranes are typically composed of hydrophobic polymer backbones
with hydrophilic cationic functional groups, forming hydrophilic/hydrophobic phase-separated
morphologies. This structure enables the formation of ion- and water-conducting channels
essential for ion transport. However, transferring AEM technology directly from water elec-
trolysis to CO2E presents significant challenges, which currently hinder their commercial
viability.
One major issue is membrane stability. The highly alkaline environment of CO2E systems
leads to nucleophilic attack by hydroxide ions, which degrade the membrane over time. Even
Sustainion, the most stable commercially available AEM, fails after approximately 1000 hours,
whereas a minimum of 4200 hours is required for economic viability [6]. Swollen hydrated
channels facilitate such degradation by increasing the exposure of vulnerable sites to hydroxide
ions.
Product and reactant crossover is another critical challenge. Negatively charged products,
such as HCOO-, migrate toward the positively charged anode, while neutral species like
ethanol diffuse through the membrane. In addition, carbonation of OH- ions due to reaction
with CO2 feed generates carbonate and bicarbonate ions, which can diffuse to the anode.
There, coupled with the oxygen evolution reaction (OER), they are converted back to CO2,
undermining the cathodic reduction process. Carbonation also lowers membrane conductivity,
due to the larger size and lower mobility of carbonate ions compared to hydroxide, and
acidifies the anode electrolyte, increasing the OER overpotential. These issues are closely tied
to membrane morphology, as an overly swollen or poorly structured phase can disrupt ion
transport and enhance undesired crossover by enlarging or disconnecting conductive channels.
Given the limitations currently hindering the commercial viability of AEM-based CO2
electrolysis systems, this work aims to contribute to a deeper understanding of the structure-
property relationships that govern membrane performance. While the majority of research
in CO2 electrolysis has focused on catalyst development, relatively few studies have investi-
gated the role of membrane properties, despite their critical influence on overall cell efficiency
and stability. Specifically, the objective is to examine the morphology of newly developed
radiation-grafted AEMs and elucidate how it influences key transport properties such as wa-
ter uptake, swelling, ion conductivity, and product selectivity.

Theoretical Background
This section outlines the theoretical knowledge necessary to understand the results presented
in this work, as well as the rationale behind the selection of characterization techniques
for the AEMs.
It includes a description of the CO2 electrolyser (CO2E) reactor used for
electrochemical and gas chromatography (GC) testing, a review of the state of the art AEMs,
and fundamental principles behind the techniques used to characterize them, namely, SAXS,
WU, swelling ratio, and IEC.
4.1
Zero-gap CO2 Electrolyzer
Flow reactors can operate with either liquid or gaseous feedstocks, which continuously circu-
late through flow plates in direct contact with the porous cathode and anode. Performance
can be optimized by adjusting operational parameters such as flow rate, temperature, and
feedstock pressure. The cathodic output strongly depends on the catalyst: Ag typically yields
C1 products like CO or CH4, while Cu also produces C2 products, e.g., C2H4, and longer-
chain Cx carbon species. On the anode side, a KOH or KHCO3 solution is introduced, and
the outlet stream contains O2 and dissolved K2CO3 [6].
Membrane-based CO2E reactors are typically designed in two configurations: zero-gap
and hybrid, as schematically illustrated in Fig.2. In zero-gap reactors, conductive cathode
and anode flow plates tightly sandwich a membrane-electrode assembly (MEA). The MEA
consists of a cathodic GDE and an anode positioned on opposite sides of the AEM, with
their catalyst layers (CLs) in direct contact with the membrane. This compact arrangement
facilitates efficient transport of reactants to the catalytic sites and significantly reduces ohmic
losses by minimizing the distance between electrodes. Hybrid reactors include a circulating
catholyte layer between the IEM and the cathode [1]. As this study focuses exclusively on
the influence of membrane morphology on CO2E performance, this configuration, introducing
additional ohmic resistance, was not employed.

4.2
Anion Exchange Membrane
The key functions of an AEM are to act as both a physical and electronic barrier, preventing
the passage of electrons and unwanted species between electrodes while allowing selective ion
transport between the catholyte and anolyte compartments. Unwanted species may include
cations or CO2 reduction products, and they will be discussed in more detail later in this
section. AEMs are designed to allow the passage of anions while blocking the cations, and
consist of a polymer backbone functionalized with cationic groups. The ionic species involved
in charge transfer are OH−, HCO−
3 , and CO2−
3 . Comparing the ionic mobility of hydroxide
and carbonates, it can be seen that carbonates have a 65-77% lower mobility hydroxide [9]. A
similar trend is followed by the conductivity of those species in aqueous solution. The usage
of OH- ions ensures a more alkaline environment, which enables the use of non-noble catalysts
for OER [6].
CEMs are historically more developed than AEMs, but recent advances in AEM-based
zero-gap CO2E cells have demonstrated significant improvements in performance metrics. A
poly(aryl piperidinium)-based membrane (PiperION) paired with a tailored electrolyzer de-
sign achieved unprecedented partial current densities exceeding 1.0 A/cm2, with high CO
selectivity up to 90%, conversion between 20–45%, and low cell voltages ranging from 2.6
to 3.4 V [10]. Another study using a next-generation Aemion+ membrane reported a cell
voltage of 3.17 V at 200 mA/cm2 with faradaic efficiencies above 90%, alongside stable oper-
ation at 100 mA/cm2 for 100 hours [11]. Furthermore, radiation-grafted AEMs with tailored
thickness and headgroup chemistries exhibited balanced properties, achieving CO selectivities
up to 80%, stable operation over 200 hours at 150 mA/cm2, and low cell potentials around
3.1 V even at elevated temperatures above 60°C [12]. Radiation-grafted AEMs are gaining
attention as a promising technology for CO2E due to their high ionic conductivity and chem-
ical stability also at elevated temperatures ( 60°C) [13].
The radiation grafting technique
provides versatility by allowing relatively easy modification of both the polymer backbone
and the functional quaternary ammonium groups, enabling tailored membrane chemistries to
optimize ion transport and selectivity for specific electrochemical applications.
4.2.1
Structure and Transport mechanisms
Building on the general structure and ion-conducting role of AEMs, this section explores fur-
ther how the morphology and hydration influence ionic transport mechanisms. The polymer
backbone provides the membrane with both mechanical and chemical robustness, enabling it
to withstand the highly alkaline environment, pressure, and temperature during operation. It
is typically hydrophobic, whereas the functional groups are hydrophilic. Upon hydration, this
contrast creates a two-phase structure with water-filled channels that facilitate ion transport,
driven by the positively charged groups promoting anion migration toward the anode.

OH- ions in AEM can move through a structure diffusion mechanism (e.g. Grotthus) or
vehicular and exchange site-based hopping mechanism, as bigger anions such as HCO3- or
CO32- do. Grotthus’s mechanism consists of highly hydrated OH- ions being transported by
the creation and disruption of hydrogen bonds between neighboring hydration shells. Ve-
hicular mechanism consists of the anion moving by diffusion with its solvation shell(vehicle).
Exchange site-based hopping mechanism is when the ions jump from one fixed site, the cationic
functional group in our case, to another without carrying the environment with them(no ve-
hicle) [3]. The contribution of each mechanism to the transport of OH- is a function of the
hydration level. It is clear that the ability of a membrane to promote ion transport, hence its
ionic conductivity, is strictly related to the number of water molecules in its pores.
The ionic conductivity(σ) is the ability of the membrane to transport a specific ion under
electric field conditions. It is derived from the resistance measurement in a CO2E through
the following formula:
σ =
Lmem
Rmem · A ∝µion · cion
(8)
where Lmem is the membrane thickness, Rmem is the membrane resistance in the through
plane direction, A is the active contant area, µion is the ion mobility, and cion is the ion
concentration.
It has to be considered that Lmem, µion, and cion are all functions of the
hydration level and IEC of the AEM.
4.2.2
Membrane related issue in CO2E
AEMs can compete with CEMs, despite the higher conductivity of protons over hydroxide and
carbonate ions, thanks to the high pH at the cathode. This alkaline environment suppresses
HER as a competing reaction, which is due to the dependency of the activation overpotential
on the pH. [1].
The alkaline environment moves the standard potential needed for HER
towards more negative values, making CO2RRs more favorable. In addition, the limiting step
of CO2R is a non-proton-coupled electron transfer(n-PCET); therefore, it is not affected by
the strong alkali solution, while the HER limiting step is a PCET and is almost completely
inhibited at high pH [3]. While these electrochemical advantages make AEMs attractive for
CO2 reduction, their operation in alkaline environments introduces new challenges, as detailed
in the following section.
The high pH at the cathode, while beneficial for CO2Eselectivity, also promotes carbona-
tion within the AEM through secondary reactions involving hydroxide ions:

This process, known as CO2 pumping, significantly reduces the FE of CO2Eproducts. Al-
though increasing ion conductivity is expected to have a positive impact on FE, it also inten-
sifies CO2 pumping, highlighting the inherent challenges of operating such devices.
Another side effect of the carbonation is the neutralization of the anolyte. At the anode
H+ and CO32- react together and stabilize the pH around 7.9, while at the cathode, it is
likely to be around 14. This pH difference not only acts as the driving force for CO32- transfer
through the membrane, but also causes a Nernstian thermodynamic loss of 354 mV at room
temperature. A method to prevent this effect is to regenerate the KOH anolyte in a caustic
recovery loop, which is highly energy demanding. In addition, HCO32- and CO3- ions are
bigger than OH- , leading to lower conductivity [3].
Ideally, an AEM should be permeable to anions and impermeable to cations. However, in
real cells, cation crossover is necessary to maintain the electric field at the electrodes to per-
form CO2R.The issue is that cations at the cathode side may react with carbonates and cause
salt precipitation at the GDE if the surroundings are not hydrated enough. The transport
occurs through two processes: diffusion, when the driving force is the difference in concentra-
tion at the two electrodes; and migration, when the driving force is the electrical potential
gradient. The cation crossover can be minimized by modifying the operating conditions of
the electrolyzer or the chemistry of the AEM. For instance, using Cs+ instead of K+ can
help due to its larger size; additionally, a higher concentration of cationic groups in the mem-
brane channels can hinder cation diffusion, but this also increases water uptake, which reduces
electrostatic repulsion, highlighting the need for a careful trade-off [3].
Product crossover may also be an issue unless they are recollected at the anolyte side. Ionic
products such as formate(HCOO−) and acetate(CH3COO−) may migrate if high current
densities are achieved, which depletes carbonate species and drives their migration to maintain
electroneutrality in the cell.
AEMs’ ability to perform in a strong alkaline environment makes them subject to hydrox-
ide’s nucleophilic attack, which can degrade vulnerable sites such as functional groups or weak
spots in the polymer backbone, particularly when electrophilic groups lack steric protection,
examples of these mechanisms are reported in the schematic in Fig. 3. Maintaining an optimal

and an example is reported in Fig. 6. The appearance of a characteristic peak at 3375 cm-1,
attributed to N-H stretching, indicates successful quaternization [18]. The resulting AEMs

Jupyter Notebook for SAXS Data Analysis
The code was developed to be user-friendly, aiming to broaden accessibility for users per-
forming SAXS characterization of membranes. The whole code is designed to allow user to
conduct data analysis of raw SAXS scattering data with minimal intervention on the code.
The first widget allows users to select a dataset along with the corresponding background file,
and to edit metadata if discrepancies are present between the file and the code’s expected
units. An interactive I map is also displayed, enabling the user to define the optimal trim

the SAXS intensity map, where each bin is assigned specific polar coordinates. This grid
will be used later in the code tp to generate an intensity versus scattering vector (q) plot
by performing azimuthal integration. Within each bin, the intensity values detected by the
detector are collected and averaged, with each pixel weighted based on the number of signals
it contributes. The result is a single representative intensity value per bin prior to integration.

parameters: q, which defines the radial distance from the center of the map to its edge (not
to be confused with the scattering vector q), and θ, which sets the angular range. Once these

tern and generates the I vs q plot using only the non-masked intensity values. Two additional
features are integrated into the widget: outlier removal and background subtraction. Outlier
removal helps eliminate artifacts or anomalous scattering peaks that may interfere with peak
identification. For background subtraction, a scaling factor is computed as the ratio between
the average of the first five values in the intensity arrays of both the sample and the back-

6.3.2
Code Validation and Optimization
The reliability of the code was validated through two independent tests. First, a measurement
was conducted on the standard material Silver Behenate (AgBeh), which possesses a well-
characterized lamellar structure and generates a scattering pattern with well-separated, sharp
peaks. In our SAXS region of interest, in the q range between 0.05 and 2.5 nm−1, the first
two peaks observed for AgBeh were located at 1.068 and 2.137nm−1. These values closely
match the literature-reported positions of 1.076 and 2.152 nm−1, corresponding to relative
errors of 0.74% and 0.7%, respectively. A second validation was performed on Nafion in the
fully hydrated state to verify the robustness of both the methodology and the code, also for
samples in solution. As a reference, the work by Kreuer K.D. et al. [25] reports an ionomer
peak at 1.4 nm for Nafion in its acidic form. In this study, a comparable value of 1.5 nm was
obtained under the same conditions, confirming the reliability of the SAXS setup and data
analysis procedure.
A series of preliminary tests was then carried out, systematically varying one parameter at
a time, specifically the number of membrane layers, measurement duration, and counter-ion
type, to identify optimal measurement conditions. These adjustments aimed to maximize
peak sharpness and improve the reliability of peak fitting and q-value extraction. Hydrated
June 30, 2025
Page 26 of 40
==PAGE:28==

Master Thesis
SAXS profiles were recorded for stacks composed of 1, 5, and 10 membrane layers.
The
five-layer configuration yielded the most pronounced scattering peak, offering the best signal-
to-noise ratio. The thickness of individual hydrated membrane layers ranged from 18 to 27 µm.
Consequently, the total thickness of the multi-layered samples used for SAXS measurements
was estimated to be between 90 and 135 µm. Multiple layers of anisotropic material lead
to a less defined anisotropic shape in the scattering pattern due to the different orientations
of the layers.
This makes it more difficult to use the Python code to zoom in on well-
defined high-intensity regions, which leads to less accurate results. It can be seen in Fig. ??

seconds, the signal was insufficient for reliable analysis. At 300 seconds, peaks were detectable
and q-values could be extracted with acceptable precision. For higher accuracy and better
statistical reliability, a duration of 1800 seconds was ideal. Nonetheless, to balance the number
of measurements and data quality, 300-second acquisitions were adopted throughout this
study to allow measurements under multiple conditions (dry, fully hydrated, and immersed
in electrolyte solution) for several samples with varying backbone chemistries, DoG, and
radiation doses.
To optimize contrast between hydrophilic and hydrophobic phases, counter-ion selection
was also explored. Nafion was initially exchanged with Cs+ under the assumption that heav-
ier atoms would increase the electron density of the hydrophilic phase, thereby enhancing
contrast. However, the relative electron densities of the phases were not definitively known,
and this approach ultimately reduced contrast. In fact, SAXS profiles showed complete deple-
tion of Nafion’s characteristic peak upon Cs+ exchange. A similar observation was reported
in [16], where substitution of OH−with Br−in an AEM resulted in diminished SAXS signals.
One other possible explanation for this behavior is that heavier counter-ions generally exhibit
lower hydration energy, causing them to associate more closely with the fixed ionic groups and
reside near the edge of the hydrated domains. In contrast, lighter ions tend to remain more
homogeneously distributed within the water-rich regions, producing better electron density
contrast [16,26].

ETFE D-sp values are consistent with those reported by Willson T.R. [13] for similar
samples under both dry and hydrated conditions. In that work, a lamellar-stacked backbone
polymer structure is proposed to describe the hydrophobic domains, while a rod-like con-
figuration is associated with the ionic channels. This interpretation is based on scattering
theory, which correlates regions of the SAXS profile exhibiting a q−1 slope with these specific
morphological features (rod-like). Our SAXS profiles exhibit similar characteristics, making
it reasonable to assume a comparable structural arrangement. It is important to clarify that
the D-spacing derived from the shoulder of the SAXS profile represents the average distance
between the centers of two adjacent hydrophilic domains. It does not correspond to the actual
size of the rod-like structures within the membrane.
An interesting description of the nanostructure of ETFE-based radiation-grafted AEMs
is provided by Zhao Y. in [27], where distinct morphologies are proposed for membranes
under low and high water content conditions. Although the AEMs investigated in that study
has different functional groups compared to those used in the present work, making a direct
comparison unreliable, the underlying conceptual framework remains relevant. Specifically,
Zhao suggests that at low hydration levels, the hydrophilic domains form narrow channels
embedded within a lamellarl packed polymer backbone. In contrast, at higher water contents,
the polymer backbones are proposed to aggregate into micelle-like structures, surrounded
by a continuous hydrophilic phase. While further validation is required, this morphological
transition concept may also be applicable to the membranes examined in this study.
The D-spacing values observed for LDPE-based membranes fall in a range close to the
range reported by Biancolli A.L.G. [23], supporting consistency in structural features. In
the 0.1 to 0.4 nm−1 region, the SAXS profiles exhibit trends similar to those of ETFE-based

such as channels or domains, are predominantly oriented in the same direction.
6.4
Spraying
Manual spraying of the CCM on ETFE samples resulted challenging due to the membrane’s
limited thickness, which caused significant swelling during the process. Despite small amount
of catalyst ink sprayed to the 1x1 cm2 area, the membrane expanded beyond the boundaries
of the PTFE mask. The continuous and repetitive mechanical stress associated with spraying
likely induced surface defects, such as micro-scratches, particularly near the edges of the coated
area, coinciding with the locations where damage was later observed following cell testing.
In contrast, the spraying process for the GDEs was more straightforward and resulted in a
seemingly homogeneous and well-distributed catalyst layer on the carbon paper substrate.

initially dropped upon operation, it stabilized after approximately 800 s, as shown in Fig.
12(a).
This initial voltage drop is attributed to the activation process of the membrane,
which was pre-conditioned in the OH−form and subsequently operated in a 0.1 M KHCO3
electrolyte. The transition from hydroxide to bicarbonate as the predominant charge carrier
requires the establishment of a new ionic equilibrium within the membrane. As a result, a
higher potential is initially needed to drive the reaction until a steady-state distribution of
ionic species is achieved. The chronopotentiometric step at 50 mA/cm2 was extended until
1800 s to ensure proper membrane activation. Following this, the chronopotentiometry at 100
mA/cm2 was successfully conducted without further complications. The test was successfully
repeated for ETFE 100 kGy 54.1% DoG, while LDPE 30 kGy 38% DoG was short-circuited.
In Fig. 12(a), ETFE exhibited a faster voltage stabilization in response to current changes,
reaching a steady-state value in less than 100 s, whereas LDPE required nearly 800 s to
stabilize, even following the transition to a higher current density. This more rapid response
of ETFE may be attributed to its lower thickness, which facilitates ion transport and enables
quicker adaptation to variations in current.
Aside from the different stabilization times,
both membranes showed comparable performance at 50 mA/cm2. At 100 mA/cm2, LDPE
demonstrated slightly improved efficiency, reaching a cell voltage of 3.03 V compared to 3.18
V for ETFE.

7
Conclusions
SAXS is a powerful technique to investigate nanostructures in amorphous polymers, and the
in-house instrumentation allows flexible adaptation of the experimental setup. In this work,
SAXS successfully resolved nanostructural features in both ETFE- and LDPE-based AEMs
under dry, hydrated, and electrolyte-saturated conditions. The characteristic D-spacing values
ranged from 27 nm to 38 nm, depending on membrane type and hydration level, enabling
quantitative analysis of domain swelling and structural ordering.
SAXS offers direct insights into the internal nanostructure, allowing for correlations be-
tween the domain separation peak and the ionomer peak with bulk hydration properties.
Structural features revealed by SAXS, as the appearance and broadening of ionomer peaks,
were found to be consistent with increasing degrees of grafting and water uptake in LDPE
membranes.
In addition, ETFE membranes exhibited a clear linear relationship between
D-spacing values of the domain separation peak and degree of grafting.
A user-friendly Python notebook was developed to facilitate SAXS data analysis and
will be made freely available to support researchers in the field.
The notebook includes
interactive widgets for sector-specific 1D and 2D analyses, automatic background subtraction,
and Gaussian peak fitting. It was validated using standard materials such as Silver Behenate
(relative error ¡1%) and hydrated Nafion (ionomer peak at 1.5 nm vs. 1.4 nm reported in
literature). By minimizing the need for manual code adjustments, the tool lowers the entry
barrier for researchers unfamiliar with SAXS processing, while maintaining reproducibility
and precision in nanostructure quantification.
