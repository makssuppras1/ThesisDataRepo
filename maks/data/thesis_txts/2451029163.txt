Abstract
This project presents a heuristic approach to solve network design problems. Network design prob-
lems are broadly used to model diverse real-life situation, particularly in the transportation area. One
of the simplest and most fundamental of those model is the Capacitated Multi-commodity Network
Design Problem (MCND). This well-studied NP-Hard problem deﬁnes a core structure and a base set
of constraint shared by various real-life transportation problem. Hence it makes an excellent ﬁrst
step when developing a heuristic to solve network design problems.
The approach proposed is an iterative linear programming (ILP) procedure that aims to foster the Lin-
ear Programming (LP) model solved to converge towards Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) solutions.
The suggested procedure solves the LP relaxation of the MCND iteratively, modifying the objective
functions, of the solved models, to push the variables toward integers artiﬁcially.
The fostering ILP procedure has been tested using instances widely used across the literature and
revealed promising results. Moreover, the analysis of the execution of the tests showed that the
proposed method get good results in a small amount of time and experience more difficulties to
improve afterward. Therefore, the fostering ILP could form a suitable starting method to generate
initial solutions and be combined with other approaches.
The thesis also proposes an extended model for the MCND, inspired by the liner shipping context,
that takes into account ﬂow constraints upon edges. This model is more suited for vehicle-based
transportation.
The fostering ILP procedure has been adapted to solve this model by adding a recovery process.
The recovery process transforms LP-feasible solutions into MIP-feasible ones. This simple recovery
process cannot always provide MIP-feasible solutions, but with all the iterations of the fostering ILP,
solutions have always been found. The suggested procedure has been tested, and computational
results reveal interesting results, especially over the largest instances used.

Chapter 1: Introduction
”Globalization refers to all those processes by which the peoples of the world are incorporated into
a single world society, global society” Martin Albrow, Elizabeth King (1990) [23]
Several deﬁnitions have been proposed to describe the globalization, this ongoing phenomenon that
everyone experiments in its daily life. The ﬁrst occurrences of this term date back to the 30s but the
use of this word is skyrocketing since the 80s. In their book ”Globalization, Knowledge and Society”
[23], Martin Albrow, Elizabeth King link it with all the processes making the world closer and more
global. Transportation and its evolution are one of those.
Between 40000 and 45000 thousand years ago, humans used the ﬁrst reals boats to ship to Aus-
tralia and to settle there. Transportation kept evolving after this point, with the invention of the
wheel and the domestication of animals, for instance. This evolution speeded up since the industrial
revolution and James Watt steam engine. Tremendous improvements have been made in propulsion
engines, aviation, and shipping areas. The transportation got both cheaper and faster, creating new
trading possibilities and making peoples closer. For example in maritime shipping, since the end of
the XV IITH century, ship capacity have been multiplied by 150 in term of volume, 400 in term of
capacity, the number of crew member have been divided by almost 10 and ships sails more than 10
times faster, as shown in the ﬁgure 1.1 on the following page.
The rising transportation capacities and possibilities created new trade opportunities, which led to an
explosion of global shipping demand. Nowadays, merchandise travel all over the world, incorporating
always more all the peoples in a global market and a more global world as described by Albrow and
King (1990) [23]. The value of merchandise export skyrocketing evolution, in the past 50 years,
illustrates this trend, as depicted in ﬁgure 1.2 on the next page.
Cheaper and faster shipping opportunities, industrialization and globalization are factors that can
explain the steady growth and demand in international shipping but increasing the vehicle’s capacity
is not the only answer nor a sufficient one. The growth of the market and the demand also led to a
rising number of ports and potential delivery locations, which combined with the rising quantities to
ship have made shipping organizations become very complex systems.
To respond to this challenging situation, shipping companies have to ﬁnd planning, schedules, and
routes that allow them to meet the demand while optimizing costs and time. Moreover, each trans-

I. Problem introduction
”The sciences do not try to explain, they hardly even try to interpret, they mainly make models. By a
model is meant a mathematical construct which, with the addition of certain verbal interpretations,
describes observed phenomena. The justiﬁcation of such a mathematical construct is solely and
precisely that it is expected to work — that is, correctly to describe phenomena from a reasonably
wide area. Furthermore, it must satisfy certain esthetic criteria — that is, in relation to how much it
describes, it must be rather simple.”, John von Neumann (1955) [18]
John von Neumann was a Hungarian American scientist, who worked in numerous pure and applied
scientiﬁc ﬁelds in mathematics, physics and computer science, and was considered as one of the
leading mathematicians of his time. By these words, he illustrates how building and using model is
critical to understand our world, as well as to develop tools and technique to make the world ours.
In this transportation context, the models used are often networks. They are a graph G = (V, E)
composed of a set V of vertices and E a set of edges. Vertices represent speciﬁc locations such as
ports in a liner shipping context, airports in airborne context, stations for rail industry or warehouse
and distribution points. The edges are the routes between those points. The edges often have
capacities, costs both ﬁxed - for opening and operating the route - and variable - proportional to the
quantity transported -, a time necessary to travel the route and other parameters.
In logistic terms, the deliveries, also named commodities, are OD pairs (Origin-Destination) with an
origin vertex, named the source, and the destination one called sink. The commodities have a quan-
tity to transport. They can have a time window to respect, eventually an associated proﬁt and a
penalty cost for late arrival or no delivery.
The goal is to design a transportation network corresponding to the cheapest or most proﬁtable way
to deliver the commodities.
The core and simplest version of the problem can be expressed as a Capacitated Multi-Commodity
Network Design Problem (MCND): The graph G = (V, E) contains a set of vertices V as stated
previously and a set E of edges with capacities, ﬁxed and variable costs. The commodities only

have origin-destination and a quantity to ship. The objective is to minimize the cost of conveying
the commodities from their origin to their destination through the design graph.
Upon this core MCND model, it is possible to add speciﬁc constraints to meet the requirements and
stick to the reality of speciﬁc real-life contexts.
The capacitated multi-commodity network design problem (MCND) is a well-studied problem in op-
erations research. It has been proven NP-Hard as describe in Magnanti and Wong (1984) article [38]
and several exact or heuristic approaches have been proposed. Nevertheless, many large instances
still remain unsolved to optimality.
As the MCND is a core model and shares its structure with numerous real-life problems, ﬁnding effi-
cient methods to solve or to ﬁnd good feasible solutions to this problem can have a massive impact
over a wide range of real-life problem in the transportation area particularly.

MCND problems consist in designing a network of routes that ensures commodity delivery minimiz-
ing the cost of such a delivery schedule. The set of routes opened and the quantities transported
amongst those routes compose a ”ﬂow”. The cost of the ﬂow (i.e., the objective function) is the sum
of the transportation costs and the ﬁxed costs. The transportation cost (also named variable cost)
corresponds to the product of the unit costs, and the quantity conveyed over each edge, and the
ﬁxed cost of an edge (also named design cost) correspond to the cost of opening this speciﬁc edge.
Several constraints bound the ﬂow on the MCND problem:
• Flow constraints on nodes: For each commodity, from its origin to its destination, the con-
tinuity of the ﬂow has to be ensured. This means that the difference between the quantity
conveyed over the edges entering one node and over the edges leaving this node has to be
equal to:
– the demand if this node is the origin
– the opposite of the demand if this node is the destination
– null for all the other nodes, ensuring all the incoming ﬂow is transmitted and routed out.
This also ensures that all the commodities are routed from their origin to their destination
• The ﬂow over one edge cannot exceed the capacity of this edge
• There cannot be any quantity conveyed over a not opened arc
Generally, two main formulations of this problem are used: an edge-based one and a path-based one.

II. Literature review
As Magnanti and Wong (1984) [38] explains, network design is a crucial problem transportation area.
It is used for decision making at strategic, tactical, and operational levels - from the infrastructure
design, the planning and ﬂeet constitution to the maintenance management. The network design
problem is a central problem in the transportation area but also in communication system planning,
water resource planning or distribution planning, for example. Multi-Commodity Flow Problems are
a core formulation to those problems, but it needs to be adapted to meet speciﬁc context and to be
effective. To have a realistic model, time frame, the impact of external actors, whether the user or
the organization should be the primary beneﬁciary of the network (i.e., whether the model should
be customer satisfaction oriented, cost or proﬁt based ...) are factors to take into account to have a
realistic model.
The Capacitated Multi-Commodity Flow Problem remains a very theoretical model but is the root of
many other models. Hence a great approach to this problem might lead to a breakthrough in various
domains. Thus, many different methods have been proposed. This problem is NP-Hard as recalled in
Magnanti and Wong (1984) article [38], and despite the high number of contributions and proposed
approaches, for numerous instances and even for small ones, optimal solutions have not been found
or not been proven optimal. Crainic at al. (2001) [32] describe 43 instances that are commonly used
in the literature to test and compare the methods. Amongst those instances, 16 are not solved to
optimality including one with only 20 nodes, 230 edges and 200 commodities.
To be more concrete and to connect this to real-life contexts, the lines operated by CGA-CGM (the
third largest shipping company in the world [39]), inside the mediterranean sea cover 60 ports (i.e.,
nodes) and uses 172 directed edges between those ports [9]. The overall network design problem
for CGA-CGM contains certainly far more edges. Moreover, the CGA-CGM Mediterranean network is
not isolated and is part of a more global network. Therefore, this network design problem is also
impacted by the side effects of other networks. Furthermore, the liner shipping network design is a
more complex problem than the Capacitated Multi-Commodity Flow Problem with more constraints
and speciﬁcities inherent to the liner shipping context. Real life instances are often too large and

II.1 Exact methods
1.a Benders-based approaches:
Benders decomposition is a way to split signiﬁcant problems into smaller, easier to solve, ones fol-
lowing a divide-and-conquer strategy, as described in Benders (1962) [19]. The idea is to separate
the variables into two groups, let us use x and y to deﬁne those groups. Then two problems are
iteratively solved. The ﬁrst one ﬁxes a group of variables (i.e., y = ¯y) and gives an upper bound and
cuts for the second problem. Also, the second one uses the y variable group and the cuts generated
by the ﬁrst model to generate a lower bound and a new ¯y group of variables for the next iteration.
The procedure stops when the lower and upper bounds are close enough.
Costa et al. (2009) [2] explore the inequalities of Benders decomposition and how they can simplify
and help to solve the MCND problem. In Benders decomposition, the ﬁrst problem with a part of the
variable ﬁxed is often solved using the dual problem. Dual theory ensures that the primal model
converges if and only if its dual converges also. The principle is to use the feasible domain of the
dual problem to generate cuts that can strengthen the formulation. The feasible domain of the dual
problem is a polyhedral cone, and every solution is called a ray. The dual problem converges only if
it is bounded. Hence, dual bounds can generate cuts. Costa et al. (2009) [2] distinguish three types
of cuts:
• Trivial benders inequalities that can be expressed using any ray and that ensures that the
problem is bounded
• Extreme benders inequalities that are special cases of the trivial ones. The Minkowski theorem
ensures that every ray can be expressed as a linear combination of a limited set of extreme
rays. The extreme benders inequalities are the trivial ones on this restricted set of extreme
rays.
• Metric inequalities that are obtained tightening the benders ones using the speciﬁcities of the
problem considered.
Then Costa et al. (2009) [2], try three versions of the benders decomposition generating cuts corre-
sponding to three types of inequalities. To improve and accelerate the benders procedure, Costa et al.

1.b Cutting-plane approaches:
Chouman et al. (2017) [26] investigates cut and cut-set generations. The procedure proposed start
by solving the LP relaxation of the problem and iteratively add cuts to the problem until the solu-

commodity the origin and the destination are separated. Also, ﬁnally a ”Metaheuristic-inspired sets”,
that construct a partition of the nodes, and use the build sets are used to create the cuts, a local
search procedure is then modifying the sets.
1.c Column-Generation approaches:
Other approaches are based on column generation. Gendron and Larose (2014) [4], proposed a
branch and price and cutting plane procedure. The described process starts with the arc-based for-

commodity, an artiﬁcial edge between the source and the sink, with unlimited capacity and a high
transport cost, is added. This ensures that the restricted master problem remains feasible. At each
iteration, pricing sub-problems are solved, generating edges to add to the restricted master problem.
Simultaneously, for each commodity, strong inequality cuts are added, helping the procedure to con-
verge to integer solutions. The proposed Branch-and-Price-and-Cut algorithm also includes variable
ﬁxing and branching.

II.2 Heuristic methods
2.a Heuristics using TABU search
TABU searches are a particular type of local search metaheuristics. It starts from a given feasible
solution an explore a neighborhood of solution around this one to ﬁnd the best one. The speciﬁcity
15 of 95
==PAGE:28==
Chapter 2: Capacitated Multi-Commodity Flow Problem
of the TABU search is that the moves done from one solution to another, at each iteration, are stored
to avoid doing them again backward. This way, it tends to diversify the search, avoiding to re-explore
already examined solutions and to escape local optimum attraction, which is a signiﬁcant problem
for local search procedure.
Crainic et al. (2000) [36] explains a TABU procedure to solve the MCND problem. This procedure
uses two different neighborhoods. A ﬁrst one composed of extreme points that can be reached from
the current one by one simplex pivot., The second one is made of the solutions found only using a
restricted set of paths corresponding to the current solution.
The same TABU search approach is implemented in Crainic and Gendron (2002) [34]. The neigh-
borhood is constituted with solutions found using simplex pivot. The difference with Crainic et al.
(2000) [36] lies in the base solution found. In the 2000 article, simplex pivots are operated on the
best solution found so far, whereas the 2002 article uses a cooperative parallel approach. Five dif-
ferent threads performing simplex TABU search are used, and the results are stored in a central pool
of solutions. The threads are fed selecting solutions in the pool. One thread uses the best solution.
Another uses a probabilistic selection, solutions with an excellent objective value have good prob-
abilities of being chosen, and bad solutions low probabilities. A third thread also uses probabilities,
but a solution a more chance to be selected if it is better than the solution added to the pool since
its last selection or entry. The two last threads are also fed through probabilistic selection. A sub-
set of the best solutions is built, a pattern is constructed using those best solution. In on thread,
the probability for a solution to be selected is linked to how ”close” (using Hamming distance) this
solution is from the pattern, on the other thread the probability to be selected is based on how ”far”
the solution is from the pattern. This parallel cooperative procedure aims to diversify this simplex
pivot-based TABU search.
Another TABU procedure is proposed in Ghamlouche et al. (2003) [15]. The neighborhood, used
in this procedure, is constructed using the cycles present in the solution after an iteration. If the
design network contains two or more distinct paths P1 and P2 to go from node A to node B, then the
network contains a cycle. Ghamlouche et al. (2003) neighborhood contains solutions build combining
P1 and P2 to redistribute the ﬂow, deviating all the ﬂow from P1 to P2, for example. Enumerating
all the pairs of nodes and all the paths between those nodes to ﬁnd cycles would be excessively
computationally demanding, hence to ﬁnd the interesting moves, Ghamlouche et al. (2003) uses
the residual graph (the network obtained deducing the arc capacity used by the current ﬂow), and
heuristically ﬁnds the shortest path between the nodes of the residual graph. With the shortest path
found and the real ﬂow between the origin and the end of this path, the procedure found a cycle.

2.b Other local search heuristics
Crainic and Gendron (2007) [35] describes a scatter search heuristic to solve the MCND. The base idea
of the scatter search is the same as genetic algorithms one: the best solutions to the problem share
the same attributes. Hence, the scatter search aims to recognize those attributes and ﬁnd solutions
using those. A candidate set is build using the neighborhood used in Ghamlouche et al. (2003) [15].
Then for each arc, a desirability factor is computed. This factor corresponds to the probability for a
solution in the candidate list to use the corresponding arc. Arcs with a high desirability factor are
then opened and arc with a low one closed. The problem is solved with those new settings giving
new solutions.
2.c Branching heuristic
Rodriguez-Martin and Salazar-Gonzalez (2010) [16] explained a solution based on local branching.
A MIP solver is used as a black box, and an initial solution ¯x0 is provided.
The research domain is then split in two part using a parameter k: the solutions that are close to ¯x0
(i.e. ∆(x, ¯x0) ≤k, ∆corresponding to the Hamming distance) and the others. The problem is solved
over the solutions that are close to ¯x0. If a no better solution is found, then ¯x0 is saved, and the
problem is solved over the solution far from ¯x0: ∆(x, ¯x0) ≥k + 1. Otherwise If a better solution ¯x1
is found close to ¯x0 then the problem is solve over the solution far from ¯x0 but close from ¯x1. The
procedure stops when one solution ¯xn is found better than both the ones close and far from itself.
If all the MIP solving are realized until optimality is found, this process is an exact method. To make
this problem faster, the key is choosing a value k that allows exploring large regions at a reasonable
time.
The method proposed by Rodriguez-Martin and Salazar-Gonzalez (2010) is a heuristic version of
this process. MIP solving have a time limit. Hence the ¯x are not always optimal. To intensify the
process, if no improvement is found within the MIP solving time limit, the k value can be reduced. At
the opposite, if improvements are found before the time limit, k could be increased to diversify the
search. TABU constraint ∆(x, ¯x) could also be implemented.
2.d Mixed heuristic
Hewitt et al. [27] proposed a procedure combining exact and heuristic approaches. Two models are
solved iteratively. A ﬁrst one is solving the edge-based formulation as a MIP over a restricted set of
edges. The second one in a column generation approach, solve the LP relaxation of the path-based
formulation.

2.e Iterative Linear Programming Methods
The iterative linear programming procedures are procedures that solve the LP relaxation of the orig-
inal MCND problem or modiﬁed version of this MCND problem iteratively. The key is to ﬁnd ways
to modify the model in order to make the process converges towards feasible MIP solutions. In the
MCND problem, one major drawback of the LP relaxations is that they tend to underestimate the
ﬁxed cost and make solutions that use many routes with a small ﬂow to lower the variable costs.
From an LP solution, it is always possible to build a MIP solution replacing by 1 all the decision vari-
ables that are not null. Nonetheless, the solution obtained by such a transformation from an LP
optimal one is not necessarily MIP optimal.
Kim and Pardalos (1997) [10] describe a ﬁrst slope scaling procedure. The dynamic method proposed
changes, at each iteration, the parameters of the problem to reﬂect the MIP reality. For example,
after an iteration, if the variable ye, representing whether edge e is used or not, has a value of 0, 2
in the LP optimal solution, then the ﬁxed cost for this edge is multiplied by 5 in the next iteration.
Then if the same solution comes up the ﬁxed cost corresponding to this edge in the LP relaxation
correspond to the ﬁxed cost in the original MIP problem and not only 20% of it. This Iterative Linear
Procedure stop when the solutions generated converges.
Katayama et al. (2009) [30] proposed another scaling method. Instead of modifying the costs, the
procedure modiﬁes the capacities of the arcs. Hence a 0, 2 arc use in one iteration becomes a 1 arc
use in the next one as the capacity of this arc has been divided by 5, reﬂecting the real ﬁxed cost.
To prevent rapid jumping, Kim and Pardalos (1997) [10] and Katayama et al. (2009) [30], use smooth-
ing parameters between 0 and 1 for the updates. Thus, ﬁxed costs and the capacities are not harshly
modiﬁed, and the convergence is not too fast. This way, the procedure is more diversiﬁed.
Those methods are heuristics, and the solutions they provide are not always optimal. Local optima
can be an issue. To tackle this problem, Crainic et al. (2004) [33] and Gendron et al. (2018) [5]
proposed different methods to escape local optima and explore more potential solutions. Crainic et
al. (2004) article uses the dual variables to add a perturbation in the objective function diversifying
the procedure, while Gendron et al. (2018) adds pseudo-cuts to tighten the LP relaxation.

III. Iterative Procedure
As explained in the section II. , computing and using efficiently the distance between a repulsion
point α and boolean values such as ye in one linear programming is not an effective approach for
the MCND problem. Instead of focusing on this distance, one could try to devise another method
with this ”0-1 push effect”. Solving the LP relaxation of the MCND problem is really fast, hence an
iterative procedure, similar to the method described in Kim and Pardalos (1999) [10] or Katayama
et al. (2009) [30], could use the solution provided solving the LP relaxation to change the model to
solve and have this ”0-1 push effect” aimed.
Kim and Pardalos (1999) [10] is modifying the ﬁxed cost to change the ye whereas Katayama et al.
(2009) [30] is modifying the capacities. The approach proposed, here uses, the same mechanism as
the Lagrangian relaxation presented in section II. : the ”0-1 push effect” is ensured by adding terms
to the objective value.
At the start, we consider the simple LP relaxation. Then after each iteration, ye are compared to a
repulsion point α. If ye > α (resp. ye < α) a term to ”push” ye to 1 (resp. to 0) is added to the
objective function.
In this procedure, α is a parameter that has to be tuned to solve the MCND problem efficiently. The
main issue with the classic MCND is the low values of ye. This procedure aims to distinguish between
those edges the ones that are necessary and need to be ”pushed” to 1 from the ones that are not
required and that can be ”pushed” to 0. Pursuing this goal, values from 5% to 50% have been tested.
Parameter tuning results are shown in section V. on page 36.

IV. Diversiﬁcation and Intensiﬁcation extensions
The ﬁrst experimentations with this raw algorithm showed that this method tends to converge
within a small number of iterations (∼20 iterations) towards local optima and gets stuck, around
those optima. To improve this algorithm, one ﬁrst idea could be to add destruction methods in order
to escape the local optimum attraction. The destruction methods used are detailed in section IV.2
. To be effective, it is crucial to have stop criteria able to identify when the process reaches a local
optimum or a dead-end and when to use a destruction method. Section IV.1 explains the criteria
implemented. Section IV.3 speciﬁes intensiﬁcation processes that have also been implemented.
IV.1 Stop and acceptance criteria
Several stop criteria have been implemented and tested:
• Integer Solution: If the solution found is an integer then no further update on the multipliers
will give a better integer solution. Indeed π multipliers are made to penalize non-integrality.
So when an integer solution is found, the process is stopped.
• Optimality Pruning: If the LP objective value is worst than the best MIP objective value found
than modifying the objective to converge to an integer solution might not give a better integer
solution than the best one already found. Indeed the ”0-1 push effect” tends to get closer to
the real ﬁxed cost and then to make the LP and the MIP objective values closer, then the LP
objective value will grow, and the MIP objective value will be bigger than the LP objective
value.
• TABU multipliers: If at one iteration the multipliers, have already been tested at a previous
iteration, test them again end with the same result and form a loop. Then the procedure should
be stopped. The drawback of this technique is that it requires to use a large memory space
and to implement a fast search method inside this list.
• No improvement limit: When the process reaches a local optimum, the next iteration might
not lead to any improvement, and the procedure will get stuck. Hence when no improvement is
found after a certain number of iterations, we consider that a local optimum has been reached
and the process is stopped. After some preliminary tests, we have seen that a limit of 15
iterations is sufficient to be sure that the value reached correspond to a local optimum.
In a heuristic version of the algorithm (see section IV.4 on page 34), including destruction methods
(see section IV.2 on page 31), acceptance criteria have also been tested. The principle of this accep-
tance criteria is to ”accept” a ”good enough” solution and stop the process after when this solution

IV.2 Diversiﬁcation extensions
Tests show that this procedure tends to converge easily toward local optima. A simple way to tackle
this issue is to use a destruction method when the stop criterion is reached and to resume the process
as in a Large Neighborhood Search.
Three different destruction methods have been implemented:
• Random destruction: Reset randomly a portion rate of the multipliers to 0. the destroy rate
is a parameter that had to be tuned
• High destruction: Destroys all the multipliers that are bigger than a certain value. In practice,
all the multipliers π that are greater than m · Π are reset to 0.
• Low destruction: Destroys all the multipliers that are smaller than a certain value. In practice,
all the multipliers π who are lower than m · Π are reset to 0.
The rate parameter of the random destruction and the m parameter of the other destruction methods
have to be tuned to have an efficient procedure. The results of this tuning are available in section V.
on page 36.
A mixed version of those destroy method has also been implemented. Some destroy method could be
more interesting at some step of the procedure and some other at the end, for instance. Adaptative
Large Neighborhood metaheuristics use a stochastic destroy method selection. A similar one has
been implemented. Let ∆be the set of destroying methods. Here ∆is composed of the Low, High
and Random destruction methods.
This ALNS-like destruction method selection is conceived using three sets of variables.
A ﬁrst set (probδ)δ∈∆corresponds to the probability of method δ to be the next destruction method
selected. The probabilities are updated every 10 destruction method selections. This allows the
procedure to compare several destructions and prevent the probabilities from converging too fast.
Indeed updating the penalties too often would favor the ﬁrst method that leads to improvement and
disadvantages the others.

and update consequently the probabilities. (countδ)δ∈∆is counting the number of time destruction
method δ has been used between two update. (weightδ)δ∈∆is storing a value corresponding to

V. Parameter Tuning
V.1 List of the parameters to tune
The methods described above rely on parameters that need to be tuned to make the procedure the
most efficient it can be.
First of all, the procedure proposed is based on a repulsion point α and multipliers that are iteratively
updated using a Π parameter. Those two parameters have to be tuned to come up with an efficient
method. The repulsion point objective is to reject arcs that are used for small quantities, really small
compared to the capacity of those arcs. Hence values between 5% and 50% have been tried for α,
but preliminary tests showed that α ∈{0.2, 0.25} give the best results. By deﬁnition of the Π
parameter (see section III.1 on page 25), Π correspond to a proportion of the ﬁxed cost that should
be added to the objective function in order to perform the ’0-1 push effect’. Hence theoretically
Π could take any value within ]0, 1] but ﬁrst tests showed that 0.4 and 0.45 were giving the best
results.
Other parameters correspond to the destroy methods: on one side the ”Low Destroy” and the ”High
Destroy” and on the other side the ”Random Destroy”. The two ﬁrst destroy methods cited uses a
parameter m to set a limit determining the multipliers that should be destroyed or not. This limit is

V.2 Tests
Crainic at al. (2001) [32] presented a test set that is widely used across the literature. This test set
is composed of 43 instances that are publicly available at http://www.di.unipi.it/optimize/
Data/MMCF.html#Canad. Those instances are generated using three parameters for |V |, |E| and
|K|. Then the parameters, capacities, costs, quantities, sources, and sinks of the MCND instance are
randomly generated. Two ratios describe the generated instance: the capacity ratio and the ﬁxed
cost ratio. Those ratios characterize respectively, how tight are the capacities compared the quan-
tities to route and whether the ﬁxed or variable costs are predominant. Not all of those instances
have been solved to optimality, but the best lower bound found are available in Gendron et al. (2018)
article [5].

the arc capacities are loose.

the arc capacities are tight.

V.3 Analysis and comments
To select the best parameters for each method, it is essential to choose parameters that give good
results for both of the set of instances (C42 and C51 vs. C44 and C49), to avoid overﬁtting. The aver-
age gap found and the standard deviation are the two indicators that help measure how interesting
is a set of parameters. The average gap gives an overview of the gap we could expect and how small
it is while the standard deviation indicates the capability of those parameters - their ability to give
similar results every time. A set of parameters that can give outstanding solutions as well as awful
ones with no regularity is less attractive than parameters giving good solutions every time.

VI.2 Convergence Study
One other interesting point to take into consideration when designing heuristics is how fast those
methods converge or ﬁnd good solutions. Indeed a method that can produce, in a reasonable amount

of time, solutions with a low optimality gap can be more interesting in real life contexts, than methods
that need days to produce good solutions even if those solutions are excellent.
To visualize the pace of the convergence of the fostering ILP procedure proposed, 400s tests have
been run over the four instances (C42, C44, C49, and C51) used in the parameter tuning (see sec-
tion V. on page 36). Every 10 seconds, the optimality gap of the current solution so far is logged.
Using this data, a convergence graph has been built. The ﬁgure 3.1 depicts the average optimality
gap observed over the four instances as a function of the execution time for the different conﬁgu-
ration of the algorithm considered (i.e., ALNS-like destroy or Random destroy, simple or accelerated
multipliers update and with or without TABU list).

From ﬁgure 3.1, we can see that the method tends to ﬁnd most of the improvements within 3 min-
utes. It is possible to see a ﬁrst plateau after 50 seconds and a second one more stable after 190
seconds. This observation has to be nuanced by the fact that those tests have been run on only four
instances, and those timings only correspond to those instances. Larger instances might need more
time. Nevertheless, it highlights that interesting solutions are found in a relatively short amount of
time, and it gets harder to ﬁnd better ones and requires more time and resources.
This ﬁgure also shows that the random destroy method tends to ﬁnd better solutions than the ALNS-
like destruction selection. The average optimality gap curves corresponding to the random destruc-
tion methods (brown curve for example) have ”stairs shape” alternating signiﬁcant steps and vast
plateaus whereas the ”stairs shape” of the ALNS-like destruction selection (yellow curve for exam-

than in the random destroy methods. This points up that High and Low destructions help for the

the approach describe in Hewitt et al. [27], needs initial solutions. The fostering procedure could be
used for a small amount of time to provide initial solutions, and for the Hewitt et al. [27] approach,
it could also ﬁll the initial sets of edges to uses and paths.
VII. Conclusion on the fostering iterative linear programming
method for the MCND
This chapter presents an iterative linear programming procedure to solve the Capacitated Multi-
Commodity network design problem. This method is inspired by Lagrangian relaxation method and
other iterative procedure such as the ones presented in Kim and Pardalos (1999) [10], Katayama et
al. (2009) [30] or Gendron et al. (2018) [5]. This procedure uses the fact that the LP relaxation of the
MCND problem can be solved efficiently using commercial solvers. Hence it is possible to solve the
LP relaxation iteratively, modifying its objective function dynamically to make the solution closer
the MIP ones.
The method proposed is composed of:
• A core MCND model using π multipliers in the objective function to make the solutions found
closer to MIP ones. (see 3.15 on page 27)
• A π multipliers update procedure (see algorithm 1 on page 26 for the simple update or sub-
section 3.b on page 33 for the accelerated update)
• A destruction method to diversify the search.
• A tabu list of π multipliers not to reuse can also be implemented to intensify the search
In the end, eight conﬁgurations of the fostering ILP procedure have been tested over the 36 Canad
instances described in Crainic et al. (2001) [32]:
• With Random Destruction or with ALNS-like destroy method
• With or without TABU list of multipliers not to reuse
44 of 95
==PAGE:57==
• With a simple multiplier update or with an accelerated one designed to intensify the research.
The tests showed that the fostering ILP procedure using the random destruction, a TABU list, and
the accelerate multiplier update gives interesting results comparable with state of the art method.
In the rest of the thesis, this conﬁguration is the only one use.
As chapter 4 explains, the MCND is only a theoretical problem that contains the core structure of
numerous other real-life based problems. Then to see if the proposed fostering ILP method could be
interesting in industry-related contexts, we should see how this method can be adapted to face side
constraints representing real contexts.

I. From theoretical MCND to real life models
Magnanti and Wong (1984) [38] describes a core version of the network design problems, but also
provides a surrounding context and links it to real life cases. Network design problems are a way to
model various decision-making situation that occurs in the transportation area but also in communi-
cation systems, resource distribution (gas and water, for instance) and many more. Those problems
are an efficient way to approach those hard combinatorial problems mathematically. However, each
of those contexts has its speciﬁcities. The Multicommodity Capacitated Network Design Problem is
only a simpliﬁed theoretical model that is the root of numerous real-life situation, but that does not
entirely ﬁt their complexities and particularities. This model has to be adapted to any real situation
to be effective.
The Multicommodity Capacitated Network Design Problem is already an NP-hard problem that shares
its structure with numerous real-life model. Hence, it is then reasonable to think that an effective
method that provides good solutions to the Multicommodity Capacitated Network Design Problem
can be adapted to solve models closer to industrial reality. The MCND is a good start model to develop
new approaches over simpler models before testing them over real-life based problems. The MCND
problem is a well-studied problem, and the corresponding literature is considerably more extensive
than the ones of all the side models based upon it. It can constitute an interesting basis for a proof-
of-concept.

In the shipping area, one ﬁrst difference with the raw MCND model is that the transport is ensured
by vehicles (planes, boats, truck, trains ...). Therefore the vehicle ﬂeet management has to be con-
sidered: replacement, capacity, maintenance, for example. The vehicles are, by nature, indivisible
(wagons in trains might constitute an exception). Consequently, when they enter a port, they need
to leave it and vice versa. For now, vehicles ensuring transport are most often driven by humans and
may require additional crew members. The law or union agreements regulate the use of employees.
Government legislation might also restrain the use of particular types of vehicles in certain areas.
Companies might also have preferences in the network they want to have (weekly schedule, using
hub nodes ...)
This chapter is an introduction to a real-life context: the liner shipping industry, its properties, and
how to model them. Section II. contains an overview of the liner shipping industry and the section III.
provides a literature review on the models built for this speciﬁc context. The section IV. detailed an
extended version of the MCND model closer to the liner shipping reality, including side constraints.
II. Introduction to the liner shipping industry
Optimization in the seaborne cargo shipping industry and its evolution is explained in a series of ar-
ticles published approximately every ten years: Ronen (1983) [11], Ronen (1993) [12], Christiansen
et al. (2004) [25] and Christiansen et al. (2013) [24].
The seaborne shipping industry is divided into three different main modes of operations: industrial,
liner, and tramp shipping.
• In industrial shipping, the company that owns the cargo to convey also owns the ships. The
objective is then to minimize the delivery cost. This type of shipping can be found, for example,
in raw material shipping and is comparable to private ﬂeet transportation.
• Liner vessels operate over predeﬁned routes and published schedules. They can load and
unload merchandises at every port on their route. Those merchandises are often containerized,
simplifying loading and unloading operations as well as their storage. Large companies such
as Maersk or MSC operate those services, and their goal is to maximize their proﬁt. This type
of operations can be compared to bus-services.
• Tramp shipping corresponds to vessels following available cargoes to maximize proﬁt. This
type of service is similar to taxi-cab.
Each of those shipping strategies can have speciﬁc niches with their characteristics. Tramp and In-
dustrial shipping are quite similar and often deal with a limited number of cargos on route with a
48 of 95
==PAGE:61==
small number of stops, vessels can respond punctually to demand and be chartered at some other
period. They do not operate using pre-established schedules and does not have to use a complete
network with predeﬁned services. At this opposite, the liner shipping industry operates upon pe-
riodic schedules, adding repositioning and ﬂeet management problematics to the network design
process.
The focus of this chapter is to extend the capacitated multicommodity ﬂow network design problem,
and add constraints to reﬂect more the reality of industry problems and in this case, the liner shipping
industry.
Agarwal and Ergun (2008) [31] explains that the decision-making process of the liner shipping in-
dustry, involves solving multiple problems at strategic, tactical and operational stages as presented
in scheme 4.1. At a strategic level, a shipping company has to decide which resource to acquire, what
its ﬂeet would look like and estimate the global demand. At a tactical level, the service network in-
cluding the route and time-schedule has to be designed, the vessel operating the routes have to be
chosen, and the schedules have to be published to book time-slots and teams on the ports to load
and unload containers and for clients to organize their cargos. Ships to operate those routes also
have to be selected. At the operational level, the decision of whether to accept or reject cargo has
to be made, and the accepted cargo have to be routed. In seaborne shipping, numerous factors can
impact a vessel during is trip. Bad weather, mechanical incidents, strikes, eventually pirates in some
areas of the globe might slow down the vessel. To face this, liner shipping companies also have to
do disruption management to reroute or reschedule their trips while operating them.

III. Liner shipping network and literature review
In the literature, the tactical level problems are often handled simultaneously and treated using
”Liner Shipping Network Design and Scheduling Problems” (LNSDSP). Knowing the periodic cargo
demands, the LNSDSP gives the routes to operate, the ships to uses, their speed and how the cargos
should be routed. The LNSDSP is an NP-Hard problem, and several versions have been discussed in
the literature. Unlike the MCND, most of the approaches described on the LNSDSP use path-based
formulations.

In the liner shipping context, the commodities to ship are most often a quantity of standardized
containers with an origin, a destination, and a delivery time. The containers can have speciﬁcities
that might add particular constraints to their transportation. Some can be refrigerated, containing
dangerous material or having speciﬁc dimensions.
The vessel capacity is often expressed in TEU (twenty-foot equivalent unit): the number of standard
twenty foot long containers they can carry. Small vessels used on small routes are recalled ”feed-
ers” and usually links major international ports (hubs) to smaller regional ones. The smallest ones
have a capacity of around 500 to 1000 TEU. Largest feeders can go up to 3000 TEU. Larger vessels
are generally used to link together major international ports with more signiﬁcant demand, and the
largest ships can carry more than 20000 TEU.
The transportation is operated by vessels, that are deﬁned by capacity and three different speed: a
minimum one, a maximum one and a design one corresponding to the one that the vessel designer
speciﬁcally conceived the vessel for. Vessels dimensions also impact where they can operate: large
vessels need large canals and deep ports. The liner shipping companies have a ﬂeet of vessel they
can use. As shown in ﬁgure 4.1 on page 49, modifying the ﬂeet of vessels is a strategic decision for
a liner shipping company. Every vessel represent a considerable investment and enormous operating
costs. Agarwal and Ergun (2008) [31] estimates that the idling cost of a vessel with a capacity of
around 2000 TEU is between $20000 and $25000 per day. This cost corresponds to the operating
cost of the vessel when it is not used and when it is waiting between two tasks.
It is not mandatory for a container to be shipped using only one unique vessel from its origin to
its destination. Containers can be transshipped: a ﬁrst vessel can load a container at its origin and
unload it in an intermediary location where another vessel will load it and transport it somewhere
else. Transshipment adds costs to the transport for the loading, the unloading, and the storage in
the intermediary location. Often, cargos between to regional ports from different regions are at
least transshipped once in an international intermediary hub. Using transshipments, different styles
of networks can be implemented as explained in Brouer et al. (2014) [7].
The ﬁgure 4.3 on the next page is showing different types of structure a liner shipping network can
contain: either end-to-end connections where a vessel is making round trips between two ports 4.3a,
or ports integrated into bundled lines where a vessel is serving a series of ports in a deﬁned sequence
4.3b. Those simple ports connections can be combined in larger structures like the Hub and Spoke
one 4.3c where larger ports (hubs) are linked together and serve smaller ones (spokes) or hybrid
structures 4.3d with a main bundled service linking hub ports and several feeder services serving
smaller ports. Primary services operated between large international hubs are often operated with
large vessels and the smaller regional services linking smaller ports are operated by feeders. One
constant in all the service design is that the vessels operate close circuits allowing them to use
periodic schedules.

IV.1 MCND-LNSDSP comparison
The liner shipping network design and scheduling problem contains two major components that the
Capacitated Multicommodity Flow Network Design problem described in chapter 2 does not take into
consideration:
• Time: liner shipping industry has to build schedules for the vessels, containing the sailing time
and port time. Then the company can book a time slot and teams at a port to load and unload
containers, and adjust the boat speed and gas consumption between the ports.
• Vessels use: As the liner shipping industry uses vessels to ensure the deliveries, the network
solution should ensure that the ﬂow is ensured by indivisible vessels that operates in round
routes. This means that if a vessel enters a port it also as to leave, ﬂow conservation on the
vessels has to be respected.

using time-spaced graphs. This section goal is to ﬁnd a side constraint to add to the MCND problem
to represent this vessel use. The following chapter detail how the fostering ILP can be adapted to

Some factors may make the recovery problem unfeasible:
• One solution to this problem can exist only if the sum of all ”y-degrees” is null. For the recovery
problem seen as a transportation problem, a positive sum of the ”y-degrees” means that there
is more quantity to ship than the demand. On the other hand, a negative sum signiﬁes that
the demand is higher than the quantity to ship. Therefore, if the sum of the ”y-degrees” is not
null, it is not possible to balance the demand and the offer.
• It is also possible that the ”not used” residual subgraph does not contain enough edges to
balance the degrees.
Running tests, this problem occurred multiple time, particularly in the ﬁrst iterations of the overall
process where the LP solution provided are still highly fractional. However, in the full process with
many iterations, the transformation has always been possible at some point, a MIP solution has
always been found.

Chapter 6: Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to build a heuristic to solve Capacitated Multi-commodity Flow Network
Design problems and similar network design problems.
The core Capacitated Multi-commodity Flow Network Design problem (MCND) is a well-studied prob-
lem on which numerous approaches have been proposed. The MCND is an NP-Hard problem, and its
structure is the root of many real situation based model. Therefore, the MCND constitutes a perfect
basis for proof-of-contexts, and an excellent ﬁrst step to build heuristics that can efficiently solve
real-life based network design problems.
In that optic, an iterative linear programming procedure to solve the MCND has been proposed. This
procedure aims to foster the convergence of LP relaxations towards integer solutions. The fostering
process (”0-1 push effect”) is ensured by modifying the objective functions of the models solved
iteratively.
This iterative procedure has been included in a global heuristic using destruction methods to in-
crease the diversity of the solution found and several intensiﬁcation procedures. In the end, 16
conﬁgurations of the heuristic have been proposed, tuned, and tested. To perform those tests, a set
of instances commonly used across the literature have been employed. Using those instances also
allows comparing the values obtained with state of the art approaches.
The tests performed shows that the procedure using a random destruction method, implementing a
TABU list and using an intensifying method to update the objective function (i.e., accelerated mul-
tipliers update), gives the best results. The results obtained are promising. The average optimality
gap observed is comparable with state of the art approaches. The analysis of how the optimality
gap evolves while using this fostering ILP procedure revealed that the bulk of the improvements is
found at the start of the procedure and that it struggles to ﬁnd new improving solution afterward.
Hence, this procedure could also be used as a starter for other polishing approaches requiring good
initial solutions.
As mentioned before, the MCND is a root model that can be used as a ﬁrst step when developing
heuristics to solve more complex problems. In this case, the proposed fostering has been adapted to
see if it could be an efficient heuristic to solve Multi-commodity Network Design Problem that also
required to use vehicles and the to have continuity constraints upon those vehicles.
79
==PAGE:92==
Chapter 6: Conclusion
An extended model has been built using the liner shipping example to include those vehicle-based
ﬂow constraints to the MCND problem. A recovery method had been added to the fostering ILP
procedure to make it able to solve this extended model. Tests have been run upon this extended
model and offered exciting results. Commercial solvers are very efficient in small instances, but
the procedure gives better results when the instances get bigger. Extending the model made the
procedure less efficient, but the optimality gaps remain relatively low.
More tests would help to have a better understanding of the behavior of the proposed heuristic.
Notably, it would be very interesting to test the fostering ILP procedure over very large instances of
the extended MCND that would more resemble the liner shipping network design problems.
The recovery procedure, added to the fostering ILP method, tends to use a large memory space
even if it cannot operate the LP-MIP transformation every time. The recovery procedure solves an
LP problem every time it is used. One could try to ﬁnd a heuristic that could ensure this recovery
process using less memory and less time.
Another idea to improve this process could be to solve a min-cost ﬂow problem once the edges added
have been chosen, this way the solution provided would have the least expensive ﬂow possible given
the set of arcs used.
As the results revealed for both the MCND and the extended MCND, the fostering ILP approach is
very interesting at the start but lacks intensiﬁcation to go further. It would be interesting to try to
combine it with a polishing algorithm to ﬁnd better results.
For both of the methods, the parameter tuning has been realized using 30 seconds tests over a re-
stricted set of instances. This allows testing various conﬁgurations of the procedure with numerous
different parameters. The parameters found seem relatively big to compensate the shortness of
the tests: Π = 0, 45 for the classic MCND model. On short tests, it might help to explore new solu-
tions rapidly, but more extended tests would probably beneﬁt from using smaller parameters, as it
could lead to a smoother and more in-depth exploration. The set of instances used was somewhat
small (only four instances). This reduces the number of tests to perform, but it also increases the
possibility of overﬁtting. Even if the instances have been separated into two groups to reduce this
likeliness, a broader set of instances to tune the parameters would have been desirable. Overall, the
fostering ILP method could beneﬁt from a more in-depth parameter tuning for both the MCND and
the extended MCND.
This approach has been developed for the MCND and adapted to face vehicle-continuity constraints.
However, both the MCND and the extended MCND are theoretical model somehow different from
problem modeling real-life situation. Hence, the next step in the development of such a procedure
is to adapt it to solve even more complex model if not real situation-based models.
