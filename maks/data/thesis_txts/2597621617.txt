Abstract
In 2009-2019 investments in Renewable energy has doubled. To evaluate investments especially Variable
Renewable Energy (VRE) in a system increasingly dominated by variable production there is need for precise
projection of electricity prices and variation. In this study day ahead electricity prices were simulated for the
years 2025, 2035 and 2045 using the BALMOREL framework. In contrast to the common approach of using
historical variance from only one year, this study simulated day ahead prices using 5 years of historically derived
data (2015-2019) concerning production patterns for VRE technology and demand patterns for power. These
were utilised in three different scenarios to generate a total of 15 outcomes of price for each of the 3 years. The
model space was a number of European countries with the aim of simulating day ahead prices in Denmark while
catching interactions from other European countries. The three scenarios were made using the rolling horizon
model of BALMOREL to discover optimal investment under three different og sets of conditions. All scenarios
utilised standard investment set in BALMOREL but also had enabled investments in wind turbines specialising
in low wind conditions. The scenarios explored two further aspects of the future power market, power to heat
investments and increased transmission investments. One scenario was not allowed to invest in Power to heat
technologies while one was not allowed to invest in transmission network. The last scenario as allowed to
invest in all of the aforementioned technologies. The scenarios are named for their allowed investments, LW
denoting Low wind, TRA denoting Transmission and P2H denoting Power to heat. The three scenarios were
thus LW_TRA_P2H, LW_NOTRA_P2H and LW_TRA_NOP2H.
The three scenarios were assumed equally likely future outcomes for the power system and thus used to calculate
the average prices including the deviation. The resulting simulations generated average prices of 35.25, 35.77
and 39.79 Euro/MWh for the Danish power market in 2025, 2035 and 2045 respectively. The standard deviation
of the results were calculated as 3.39, 2.89 and 7.8 for 2025, 2035 and 2045 respectively.
To combine the results for day ahead prices with simulation of wind turbine cases, ERA5 reanalysis data was
tested for use at generating production levels for wind turbines. The approach of simulation production at hourly
resolution for individual parks was validated using hourly production data for Norwegian onshore wind parks for
the year 2015-2019. On average annual results were 6.8% higher than historical data.
ERA5 was also used in combination with calculated day ahead prices in a comparison of performance of a V100
and a V164 wind turbine at location in Zealand. Using both the simulated day ahead prices and production
levels on hourly levels. Distributions of capture prices and production levels on annual levels were calculated for
both wind turbines for all simulated scenarios. Using the calculated distributions Monte Carlo simulations were
performed under the assumption that each scenario was equally likely as outcome for the future. Results from
the Monte Carlo results show that the smaller V100 wind turbine on average generates revenue 48.05% higher
than the V164 wind turbine per MW installed effect, with 0.151 Meuro vs 0.102 Meuro for the v100 and v164
respectively. Furthermore the V100 displayed a tighter distribution in the iterated Monte Carlo results with an
average CV value of 12.8% compared to 15.4% for the V164.

Introduction
The Paris Agreement was ofﬁcially signed in 2016. The agreement was signed by 195 countries, with a global
ambition to keep global warming well below 2 degrees. This inevitably requires a transition from our conven-
tional fossil fuel driven power sector, to predominantly renewable. Renewable energy (RE) power production
has seen a signiﬁcant surge in investments, and more than doubling in the 10 year period of 2009-2019 totalling
282.2 billion $ in 2019[1]. With both cumulative installed capacity and new installed capacity, we increasingly
have to anticipate a power market more dominated by RE power with numerous new projects each year.
The electricity commodity market is a unique market with constant balance of supply and demand. Although
electricity is one of most important commodities for our society and almost always in demand, it is also a
highly volatile market. It posses volatility measured well beyond other well studied ﬁnancial markets[2]. This
is evidently clear when looking at most arbitrary days, which display a quickly shifting market with prices
increasing in relatively up to 1000% from one hour to the next. An example of hourly day ahead prices are
displayed in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Day ahead electricity prices from Nordpool DK1 bidding zone at date 27/7/2020 [3]
This makes precise evaluation of revenue for production units complicated, especially for Variable Renewable
Energy (VRE) project without subsidy support. As renewable energy projects are long term projects with time
horizons in the range of 15 - 35 years, depending on the technology, there is need for good projections of the
future market.

Forecasts of electricity prices and capture prices are produced by a multitude of companies. Produced us-
ing modelling approaches such as operations research, AI, statistical modelling and more. These models
commonly use a combination of historical data and expectations of the future market, to investigate how the
future electricity market will look and which prices it will generate.

The forecast for average electricity prices displayed in Figure 2 show projections of how the average electricity
price will evolve in the future under different scenarios of the future. It is very evident that the expectations for
the future market we put into our scenario of the future, can have high impact on the resulting forecast. Although
some outcomes of the future are more likely than others, it is ultimately unknown how the future will pan out.
Therefore multiple scenario or outcomes for the future are often explored.
Figure 2 displays the importance of assessing the uncertainty of the future. As the smooth graph suggest however,
the fact that even the same scenario can contain signiﬁcant variation is not investigated. Looking at average
electricity prices for 5 most recent years 2015-2019 as displayed in Figure 3, it is evident that electricity prices,
even on an average yearly level in a similar system highly varies.

In many modelling approaches, the historical input is limited, and thus the results do not capture the ﬂuctuations
that semi random factors such as weather and demand impose on resulting prices. To better explore the variability
of electricity prices, the historical input should be increased in existing power forecasting models.
While much historical data is readily available for many aspects of the energy market, others are not. Speciﬁc
wind speeds at wind turbine sites or solar irradiation for solar projects, are often not readily available. There is
often no way to get direct data on these measures for any project, as only a limited amount of weather stations
exist. Although measurements on site rarely exists, satellites have enabled observations of a multitude of on
site measurements from orbit. Although they do not directly measure the wind speed or irradiation, the data
they gather enable re-analysis data sets at high resolution of the desired data. The value from these data sets
can be immensely valuable both as tools for assessing site or entire areas for production potential. Combining
reanalyzed data sets and power modelling forecasts, business cases and Geographical information system (GIS)
based economic evaluations can be performed for otherwise highly variable projects.
In this study, state of the art power market model BALMOREL will be used with a large selection of historical
data to investigate how variations in the power market affects electricity prices, capture prices and case studies
of speciﬁc wind turbines.

Methodology
This thesis aims to investigate how uncertainty in both scenarios of the future and historical input affect the
resulting electricity prices, and ultimately expected revenues for RE projects. This is done in a few different
steps, most notable modelling of future electricity prices, using the modelling framework BALMOREL, and
modelling of production from RE projects corresponding to the historical input in the model using satellite
derived data sets.
5
BALMOREL
BALMOREL is a partial equilibrium model written in the programming language GAMS. The model was
originally released in 2001 by Hans Ravn et Al. [6], as a tool for analysing the energy systems in the Baltic sea
area. The model uses a bottom-up approach focusing on electricity and heating sector.
BALMOREL optimizes generation, transmission and consumption of electricity and heat in the power market,
under the assumption of perfect competition in the market[6]. The model optimizes the system to supply, then
deﬁned demand, maximizing social welfare minus producer and system costs. This optimization is subject to
technical, physical or regulatory constraints, which can include minimum load on power plants, environmental
regulation or capacity of transmission lines. The decision on these constraints are part of the process of forming
the scenarios of the system the modeller wishes to simulate[7].

5.1.1
Spatial
BALMOREL operates on three levels of geographical entities; Countries, Regions and Areas. These are included
such that each Country contains one or more regions, which in turn contains one or more Areas. It should be
noted that each Region or Area belongs to purely one Country or Region. Countries, regions and areas may
be interconnected such that electricity or district heat can ﬂow between them depending on the constraints as
displayed in Figure 5.

In theory, there is no limit to how many countries or regions that can be included. In return for the added
geography the complexity of the solution will logically increase which leads to increased computational time and
size of the solution space. Therefore included countries are usually limited to the most important to the context
of the study. The exercise of including or excluding countries should however always be given considerable
consideration, since the reality is that the European electricity market is heavily interconnected and countries
and areas can have effect on other countries even far away.
The scope of this study will mainly focus on electricity prices in the danish bidding zones. The chosen countries
for the model are displayed in Figure 6.

5.1.2
Temporal resolution
BALMOREL contains 3 temporal levels; years, season and terms. Each year contains an equal number of
seasons, which in turn contains an equal number of terms. Commonly, seasons will be used to divide the year
into either 52 weeks or 364 days, with 168 and 24 terms respectively. Both methods totals a total of 8736 time
steps per year. In the context of this study, we will simulate electricity prices for the years, 2025, 2035 and 2045
with 364 season and 24 terms, corresponding to days and hours respectively. It should be noted that to depict
uniformity of days and months each year, each season 1 will correspond to a Monday and each term 1 to hour
0:00-1:00 [6].

The models should not be seen as competing models, but rather complementary sub models all part of the
BALMOREL framework. They each fulﬁll a separate role and produce results that can be fed into other
sub-versions of the framework, such that resolution slowly can be increased. This is with the exception of BB4,

and will be described more in detail in section 5.2.1.
The purpose of each of the other Balbases is shortly covered here. When simulating the future power system, the
initial step is to investigate which investments in transmission, generation etc. is feasible. This is done using the
BB2 sub model. As stated in Table 1 BB2 has enabled investment optimization and seasonal optimization. As
such it will optimize the solution such that the model invests in the optimal mix of technologies for one year at a

With BB2 done, optimal investments are determined. These can be loaded into either a BB1 or a BB3 model.

5.2.1
The rolling horizon model BB4
All model runs in this thesis are performed in the BB4 model of the BALMOREL framework. As mentioned in
subsection 5.2 BB4 contains the functionality of all of the other Balbases, depending on the settings and add-ons.
The differentiation is the rolling horizon functionality. Rolling horizon refers to the concept that rolls through
either years or seasons, optimizing two at a time, but only keep the initial. The model then rolls on to the next
two years or seasons, again only keep the ﬁrst. The concept is displayed in Figure 7. This addresses the concept,
that the prior models have been unable to simulate, the fact that investments are made with some foresight of the
future. For example, i would not invest in a coal power plant today, if i knew that it would be illegal or heavily
taxed in 10 years. Also as is evident, we do make projections of the future, and take them into account, as such
so should the model.
Figure 7: Illustration of the used rolling horizon in the BB4 submodels, both displayed for the year based rolling horizon
(full year and investment model and daily rolling horizon (Day ahead model).
The results have been obtained using three versions of the BB4. An investment model, a full year model and
ﬁnally a day ahead model. The 3 models each do their part in creating a detailed look of how the power system
evolves. The submodels feed results into each other, step by step increasing resolution. How the model link
together is displayed in Figure 8

In the initial investment mode, BALMOREL simulates the power market at lower resolution while allowing
for optimization of investments. The purpose of this submodel is purely to obtain the optimal investments in
capacities such as transmission, generation and storage. With the optimal investments established, we need to
run a full-year model to establish optimal behaviour of cross seasonal activities such as storage optimization.
Although the investment model does has seasonal optimization, the temporal resolution is not ideal for proceeding
using the obtained values in that run. The full year uses higher temporal resolution to simulate two years with
seasonal optimization, using the investment obtained in the investment run. Although it might seem we would be
ﬁnished after the full-year run, we are computationally limited to using a relaxed mixed integer solver to avoid
computational overload. It can also be discussed whether 2 years of perfect foresight on market behaviour is
ideal for simulating a power market that in reality has imperfect foresight.
A ﬁnal run is performed to get a more accurate picture of market behaviour. The ﬁnal submodel, the day ahead
model, named for its purpose of simulating the real day ahead market. It uses an optimization period of 2 seasons
(days), each time keeping the results of the ﬁrst of the two days, while proceeding with the next two. The same
procedure used for the rolling horizon in the typical BB4 model, but on the seasonal temporal level, rather than
the yearly temporal level. It should be noted that due to the short time resolution that the day ahead model uses
it utilizes seasonal optimization results from the fullyear model. Each mode is summarised in Table 2.

5.2.2
Investment Scenarios
As illustrated in Figure 2 with the electricity price outlook from Dansk Energi[4], the scenario of the future can
have signiﬁcant impact on the resulting power market. This is both in terms of the investments and resulting
prices. To get an estimation of the uncertainty of future electricity prices, the uncertainty, in this context the
investments, needs to be valuated. For this purpose three investment scenarios have been made using the balbase
4 investment submodel. It should be noted that the investment runs were not performed by the author of this
thesis, but rather staff at DTU Management. The results are however utilised in this thesis to proceed with the
fullyear and dayahead sub models to obtain electricity price results.
The scenarios contain 3 distinctive investment features. Besides investing in the conventional technologies found
in the BALMOREL framework, all of the scenarios allow for investment in low wind speed turbines. These
turbines posses higher efﬁciencies at lower wind speeds, generally sacriﬁcing nominal power and/or performance
at higher wind speeds. Second distinctive feature is whether the investments in transmission capacity between
countries are allowed. Interconnection highly affects power market behaviour and investment, especially in
relation to Variable Renewable Energy production (VRE). In BALMOREL study by Tveten et al, 2016 [9]
assuming purely the planned increases in transmission capacity (of 2016), resulted in a 3.3 increase in total
VRE revenues and VRE electricity generation increased by 3.7 TWh [9] compared to no expansion. Finally
Power2heat (P2H) investments can be either allowed or not. As described in detail by Kirkerud et al. 2017 [10]
higher shares of renewables tend to lower spot prices at abundant times. This lowers market values of electricity
generated from renewables. Kirkerud demonstrated that power to heat investments can be used to increase VRE
integration and increase market value of the generated electricity[10].

5.3
Historical input
As mentioned earlier, the BALMOREL framework utilizes a large amount of input data. Most of these are dated
information and projections, such as expected CAPEX costs of technologies both presently and in the future.
Some of them however, are historically derived data. Most of the historical data concern the variable power
generation, for technologies such as wind and solar, but also demand patterns for heating and electricity. In the
current framework, the year of 2012 is used as a reference year from which all the data has been collected. In
this study, investments were made with the standard framework using 2012 as reference year. Seasonal storage
optimization and day ahead prices will however be simulated using the data derived from 2015, 2016, 2017,
2018 and 2019. The aim is to identify, how big the effect of the variable input has on the resulting electricity
prices. Thus for each investment scenario, 5 different versions of the full-year and day ahead model are run to
asses electricity prices of that scenario for each of the 5 years of variation. The model ﬂow is illustrated for
scenario LW_TRA_P2h in Figure 9.

LW_TRA_P2H.

As can be seen in Table 9, ERA5 and MERRA 2 produced by ECMWF and NASA respectively are the only
analysis currently available in 1 hour resolution. This has made these two data sets popular for researchers
and industry alike to use them for wind power based modelling[14]. A main distinguishing factor between the
two are their data assimilation strategies. Data assimilation strategies describe the process of how observations
distributed in time and space are merged with the numerical weather model. Initially weather models had deﬁned
initial conditions and as the observations became available, they provided correction. The MERRA 2 set utilizes
a 3D-FGAT (ﬁrst guess at time) var assimilation scheme, while ERA5 utilises a 4D VAR assimilation scheme.
To understand how the 3D-FGAT compares to the 4D-var we must ﬁrst understand how the a usual 3D-var
compares to the 4D-var scheme. 3D var corrects the simulation using observed data in this case in a 6 hour
window, it does however not keep track of the temporal distribution of time. Rather it uses there nearest t=+3
and t=-3 and treats them as if observed at t=0. Thus initial conditions are corrected and the model continues.
4D-var uses all the data points in the time window t=+6 to calculate the suitable corrections corresponding to the

7.1
Using satellite data for wind production siting and estimation
ERA5 has had recent success at modelling wind production levels both on national and single wind turbine
levels.
Although Grid spacing is low in ERA5 datasets, there is always going to be distance between the nearest data
point and the desired location. In this case interpolation is necessary to obtain a good as possible data. In general
three approaches are common in literature.
• Nearest neighbour approach: The nearest neighbor approach, simply takes the value of the nearest available
gridpoint. This is generally recognised as a suitable to catch high spatiotemporal variability in variables
such as wind[24] [25].
• The Nine-point average is commonly used to get smoother data results by averaging the nine nearest point.
• Billenear interpolation: Billinear interpolation is commonly used to regrid the grid using billinear in-
terpolation. This method has been used in several studies to re-grid wind ﬁelds in higher detail [26]
[27].
In the context of this study the nearest neighbour approach has been chosen as we desire representation of the
spatiotemporal variability of the wind speeds. It can be argued that a Bilinear interpolation would provide better
results, but it usually used to re-grid data-sets rather than provide single point data. It was decided that this
approach would be unnecessary complex and require signiﬁcantly more computational power.
With the nearest neighbour approach, we can handle the spacing in regards to coordinates. That leaves us with
the second spatial aspect to handle, the height. As ERA5 is only available in 10 m and 100 m heights, data needs
to be interpolated to get at better ﬁt at the desired height. Following the approach in described by Ramon et al.
in [22] winds speeds at 100m can be adjusted to the desired height using Equation 11

8.5.2
Monte Carlo simulations of revenue
With multiple outcomes of production, capture prices calculated for multiple possible futures it is evident that
a variety of outcomes for revenue is possible. When dealing with multiple distributions of random variables,
Monte Carlo simulations are a typical approach. Monte Carlo simulation consists of initially determining random
variables and their distribution. In this case study we are using three variables.
• Production from our wind turbine
• The obtained capture price
• The resulting scenario
Both the production and capture price are assumed normally distributed. In this case we assume that each
scenario outcome is equally likely and thus the distribution of the scenario variable follows a uniform distribution.
It could however just as easily be the case that we assumed that some scenarios are more likely that others, or
that we use some scenarios to represent some aspect that is uncertain to the range of which it will happen. It is
generally highly unlikely to imagine that the future electricity system would not expand transmission networks at
all. Using the Monte Carlo approach one can however build in the implications of lower than expected expansion
by assigning a small chance of a no transmission.
With the distributions types determined, the distribution of outcomes for each variable is made. The normal
distributions are made using the calculated STD and mean for each simulated year and scenario in the case of
40
==PAGE:47==
DTU Management
August 17, 2020
the capture price. The distribution for production is made from the simulated production values made from
2015-2019 data. This therefore assumes that wind conditions will be constant through the WTs lifetime. The
distribution of the outcome of scenario is simple made by as a random number in the interval of 1-3. From the
generated distributions the Monte Carlo results can be produced. The process is illustrated in Figure 30.
Figure 30: illustration of Monte Carlo approach
The function that is utilized is very straight forward in our case, and can be described as:
Annualproduction · CP(Y,S) = revenue.
(15)
CP denotes the capture price for the given year Y and given scenario S. The distributions are calculated using
using the calculated means and standard deviations described in the tables in subsubsection 8.5.1. Using the
given randomly chosen variables from the distributions, the resulting revenue is calculated. The process is
iterated for a total number of 6000 times for each year. The resulting distribution can be seen in Figure 31.

9
Conclusion
Historically derived data was successfully implemented into the BALMOREL framework to increase the
representation of variability in weather and demand and its effect on electricity prices. The 3 scenarios were
simulated using 5 different sets of input data. The resulting prices for the danish market were 35.25. 35.77 and
39.79 Eur/MWh with corresponding CV values of 9.641, 8.1 and 19.61 % respectively.
The generated hourly day-ahead prices allowed for calculation of multiple possible capture prices for the major
VRE technologies, On shore wind, offshore wind and solar. The results indicate that variation in the resulting
capture prices differed signiﬁcantly more compared to the average electricity prices. With average CV values of
19.88, 17.12 and 12.12 for onshore- , offshore wind and solar respectively. In relation to the historically observed
CV values of 26.2, 25.67 and 23.91 it does indicate that even though only the pattern of VRE production
was changed and not the total produced amount, the approach was still successfully in capturing comparable
variation.
The day-ahead prices were used to evaluate performance of a large (7MW) V164 vestas wind turbine vs a
smaller (3.6) V100 wind turbine. Using ERA5 data to generate matching production proﬁles to the historical
variation proﬁle of the model allowed for calculations of capture prices speciﬁc to each turbine and site. The
approach demonstrated that the smaller V100 wind turbine on average obtained 4.9% higher capture prices in
the simulations but also 10 % lower CV value. The production also revealed both 33% higher production per
installed capacity and 25% lower CV value.
Monte Carlo simulation, enabled from the multiple scenarios and historical variations of the simulation, showed
signiﬁcant differentiation in performances for the 2 wind turbines. The resulting distribution had the V100
generating on average 48% revenue per installed capacity over the three simulated years. The resulting
distribution was also signiﬁcantly tighter.
This illustrates the potential of getting better evaluation of speciﬁc projects or potentially entire areas by
combining reanalysis weather data and power system forecasting.

Although production levels could in theory be used directly as input in BALMOREL to determine variation in

ease comparisons of countries and years. Capacity factors are calculated using Equation 16.
CFt = Pt

technology G. This results in a dimensionless factor, usually between 0 and 1. It should be noted that due to
inconsistencies between reporting on installed capacities and produced power factors above 1 were observed.
