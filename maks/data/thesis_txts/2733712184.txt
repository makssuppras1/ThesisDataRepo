Abstract
To address the climate issues of today large, investments have been made into clean energy. With a potential
future increase in renewable energy, hydrogen production has become an area of interest as itis an energy
dense and low-emissions fuel source that can be produced from electricity. Furthermore, hydrogen sees use
in laboratory synthesis and industrial manufacturing. The hydrogen can be produced from the electrolysis
of water which is a technology that can be configured in different ways. The most mature technology is
the low temperature alkaline water electrolysis (AWE) which utilises cheap non-noble materials to produce
hydrogen. AWE suffers from drawbacks related to low current densities, which negatively affect system size
and cost. A less commercialised technology is the low temperature proton exchange membrane water elec-
trolysis (PEMWE) that improves on the troublesome areas of AWE. There is however a trade-off as PEMWE
utilises the scarce and expensive platinum group metals (PGMs) Pt and Ir making it a difficult technology
to implement on a large scale. The last prominent option is solid oxide water electrolysis (SOWE) which in
contrast to AWE and PEMWE is a high temperature technology that is able to reach high current densities
while utilising non-noble metals and ceramics. SOWE is still not a commercialised technology, as it suffers
from stability issues because of the high operating temperatures.
The goal of the thesis was to conduct a life cycle assessment (LCA) to identify the most impactful technology
and the biggest contributing elements for each technology. The technological elements were compared based
on their ability to produce hydrogen as to ensure a fair comparison. The results indicated that AWE and
PEMWE were very similar in their environmental impact across multiple environmental issues, while SOWE
was about 30-40% less impactful. The most burdensome element was by far the electricity consumption for
all of the technologies. While certain cell components had visible impacts for specific environmental issues,
there is no doubt that in order to noticeably reduce the impact of water electrolysis the electricity consump-
tion has to be lowered. An additional comparison was made, this time only with the cell components, and
here the most environmentally critical materials were identified. Stainless steel for all technologies and IrO2
specifically for PEMWE was shown to be the major contributors to the environmental burdens.
Future work should be focused on finding very accurate data for the electricity consumptions, as well as
constructing scenarios that showcase the effect material choice has on the electricity consumption.

Introduction
Hydrogen has the potential to play an important part in addressing the environmental concerns of the future.
With the increasing investment into clean energy [1] it opens up the prospect of producing "green" hydrogen
which is when renewables are used entirely for the production. The hydrogen is produced through water
electrolysis where electricity is utilized to split water into hydrogen and oxygen [2]. Hydrogen has properties
that currently makes it an often used reactant in laboratory synthesis and industrial manufacturing. Its high
energy density also makes it a viable low-emissions fuel source for transportation. Furthermore, it is possible
to store hydrogen as a liquid or a gas on a large scale in underground aquifers, salt caverns or simply storage
tanks. Today, less than 4% of the total hydrogen production today stems from electrolysis so green hydrogen
production needs to be ramped up significantly if the potential environmental benefits are to be realized [3, 4].
When it comes to water electrolysis there are three well known technologies of varying maturity: 1) alkaline
water electrolysis (AWE), 2) proton exchange membrane water electrolysis (PEMWE), and 3) solid oxide
water electrolysis (SOWE). AWE and PEMWE are the more conceptualized technologies that both operate
at low temperatures compared to the high temperature SOWE technology that still is under development
[3]. AWE is performed under alkaline conditions which makes it a relatively durable and cheap technology
as non-noble metals are driving the hydrogen production. But inefficiencies in areas like current density and
operating pressure increase system size and costs. PEMWE is a less mature technology that is performed
under acidic conditions that expose system components to harsh corrosion. This increases system costs as
noble metals like platinum (Pt) must be used. But still, PEMWE offers advantages such as high power and
current density and provision of pressurized pure hydrogen. SOWE is not yet commercialized but it has been
demonstrated on the lab-scale. It uses an ion conducting ceramic to perform the electrolysis and operates
at very high temperatures (650-1000°C). The main advantage of SOWE is the potential for high electrical
efficiency and low material cost, however, the durability of the system is challenged at the high operating
temperatures [5].
While the potential for hydrogen to decarbonize certain industrial sectors is promising, it is important to
assess the entire life cycle of hydrogen production, and especially also outside of carbon emissions. This can
be done with a life cycle assessment (LCA) which provides comprehensive coverage of several environmental
issues for products or services.
As LCA is quantitative in nature it can be an assisting tool in product
development, policy formulation and other contexts where decisions are affected by environmental issues [6].
Currently, there has only really been done one comprehensive LCA by Zhao et al. 2020 [7] that includes all
three technologies, while there exists LCAs focusing on either AWE [8] or PEMWE [9]. In Zhao et al. 2020
the basis for comparison was the production of 1 m2 of each type of cell. They concluded that PEMWE had
the highest potential impacts in terms of global warming potential and resource scarcity. Furthermore, they
4

identified critical materials for each technology that were: nickel (Ni) for AWE, platinum and iridium (Ir)
for PEMWE, and finally stainless steel and nickel for SOWE.
The lack of comprehensive LCAs in the field of water electrolysis has motivated the topic of this thesis. In

be conducted and presented. The research questions for the thesis are:
1. What are the environmental impacts of the most prominent water electrolysis technologies, and how
do they compare to each other?
2. For each technology, what are the main contributors to the environmental impacts?

2.1
Water electrolysis
Electrolysis is an electrochemical process in which an electrical current is utilised to induce a chemical trans-
formation. In its simplest form, the electrochemical cell consists of two conductive objects (electrodes) that
are in contact with an ionically conductive liquid or solid (electrolyte). Once a voltage is applied to the
electrodes they will become electrically charged as electrons move from one electrode to another. At the
negatively charged electrode (cathode) reducing reactions take place, while oxidizing reactions take place at
the positively charged electrode (anode) [10].
During water electrolysis, water is split into hydrogen and oxygen. The negatively charged cathode will
attract hydrogen atoms and reduce it to hydrogen gas, while the positively charged anode will attract oxygen
atoms and oxidize it to oxygen gas (see figure 1). These reactions are known as the hydrogen evolution
reaction (HER) and oxygen evolution reaction (OER). The combined reactions result in the formula:
H2O + electric current −−→H2 + 1
2 O2
(1)
Figure 1: Principle sketch of water electrolysis. It shows the flow of electrons, as well as the orientation of
water molecules due to the charged cathode and anode. Adapted from Cavaliere 2023 [11].
Water splitting is not a spontaneous process. At standard atmosphere pressure and temperature the equi-
librium cell potential is 1.23 V regardless of pH [10]. This means that the reaction will not occur below
this threshhold. In industrial plants the voltage will be higher (around 1.8-2.4 V). This difference between
the theoretically determined potential and the actual potential is known as overpotential [12]. Some com-
mon causes of overpotential that are relevant within water electrolysis are ohmic losses due to conductivity,
6

electrode distance and gas bubble effect as well as activation overvoltage which depends on the electrode
material. There is an interest in designing electrolyzer cells as to minimize overpotential so that electricity
consumption gets reduced [3].
Figure 1 shows the electrolysis as it is with pure water, although this is not how it is performed in real-
ity. Water on its own is a poor conductor of electricity and therefore the electrolyte is often a solution of
an ionic compound such as KOH or a ion conducting solid such as Nafion®[13]. Different types of water
electrolyzers are in part categorized based on their electrolyte. There are three types of electrolytes: acidic,
neutral and alkaline. The nature of the electrolyte has influence on the mobility of ions within the solution,
as cations are more mobile under acidic conditions while anions are more mobile under alkaline conditions [14].

The three most commercialized and conceptualized technologies are alkaline water electrolysis (AWE), pro-
ton exchange membrane electrolysis (PEMWE) and solid oxide water electrolysis (SOWE). AWE is currently
the most mature technology which has seen the largest commercialization. PEMWE is an intensely studied
technology, but due to instability issues it is still not employed on as large a scale as AWE. SOWE is the
least developed technology as it needs to go from lab-scale demonstrations to small-scale commercialisation
[5]. Table 1 provides a simple overview of major specifications for each technology.

2.2
Alkaline water electrolysis
Under alkaline conditions the water splitting has specific reactions at each of the electrodes:
7
==PAGE:9==
SECTION 2
Anode : 4 OH−−−→O2 + 2 H2O + 4 e−
(2)
Cathode : 2 H2O + 2 e−−−→2 OH−+ 2 H2
(3)
In both half cell reactions hydroxide ions are present. To ensure high ionic conductivity, a sufficient amount
of charge carriers (in the form of hydroxide ions) must be provided by a strong base [11]. In alkaline water
electrolysis (AWE) the employed electrolyte is typically a solution of KOH between 20 wt.% and 30 wt.%,
which is range where the highest specific conductivity has been observed. A cheaper option is a solution
of NaOH between 15 wt.% and 25 wt.%, however, this solution comes with a 40-50% decrease in specific
conductivity [3].
Extensive research has been conducted to find the best electrodes for the different water electrolysis tech-
nologies. The goal is find materials that catalyze the HER and the OER while remaining stable over long
periods of time in the highly corrosive alkaline conditions. Better efficiency, stability and durability leads to
reduced costs in the form of lower electricity and resource consumption [11].
The electrodes in AWE typically consists of a porous metal framework such as solid meshes, perforated
metal plates, powder electrodes or metal foams. The electrode can either work as a catalyst on its own,
or electrocatalysts can be deposited onto the structure to drive the water splitting reactions [16]. Platinum
group metals (PGMs) like Pt, Ru and Ir have shown excellent catalytic ability for both the HER and OER,
but their large-scale usage is hindered by both cost and scarcity [17]. Furthermore, the cheaper and more
abundant iron group metals Fe, Co and Ni show higher stability whilst showing catalytic activity specifically
in alkaline media [16]. Ni in the form of nickel foam (NF) is often used as a substrate because it itself shows
great catalytic ability for the HER. When it comes to the OER there are some issues with stability and
overpotential due to the complexity of the reaction mechanism, but NF can still be used [18].
An essential part of the alkaline water electrolyzer is the separator set between the two electrodes that pre-
vents hydrogen and oxygen gas from mixing [3]. The most commercialized material is Zirfon®Perl UTP 500,
which is a 85% zirconia, 15% polysulphone diaphragm with desirable properties such as good conductivity
and wettability [19]. Alkaline electrolysers can be configured in two ways. Traditional electrolyzers employ
solid electrodes that are placed a certain distance away from the separator, but this set-up suffers from lower
current densities and lower efficiencies. Another type of set-up utilizes a zero-gap configuration where porous
electrodes are pressed directly onto the separator. This compacts the design, reduces ohmic loss caused by
the electrode gap and results in higher efficiencies [13]. Figure 2 shows the principal difference between the
traditional configuration and the zero gap configuration. The traditional set-up will not be further detailed
as this is not relevant for the environmental assessment in upcoming sections.

The remaining components of the zero-gap alkaline electrolysis cell are the bipolar plates, current collectors
and sealings. When many cells are connected in a series they form the electrolysis stack, and here the bipolar
plates serve as a separator between cells. As the name suggests, the bipolar plate is positively charged in
one cell and negatively charged in the adjacent cell. Furthermore, channels engraved in the bipolar plates
supply the cell with electrolyte as well as transport product and electrolyte from the cell. Ideally the bipolar
plate are made of cheap materials that are resistant in alkaline conditions such as Ni or stainless steel [16, 20].
Figure 3: Figure showing the structure of the AWE cell in 2-dimensions. Not to scale. Adapted from
Schalenbech et al. 2018 [16].
Current collectors are placed between the bipolar plates and the electrodes to ensure electrical contact be-
tween the two. They are typically only employed if the electrode has weak in-plane conductivity which rarely
is the case for electrodes in AWE. If a current collector is needed, it must be a porous and electron-conducting

structure such as carbon or metal foams. Lastly, there is sealings between the separator and the bipolar plates
to eliminate leaks [7, 16].
All these elements combine to make the electrolysis cell, also known as the single repeating unit (SRU) when
connected to form an electrolysis stack. Figure 3 and 4 provide a visual overview of the SRU structure in 2
and 3 dimensions. Stack designs can vary in active cell area (where electrolysis occurs) and number of SRUs

2.3
Proton exchange membrane water electrolysis
Proton exchange membrane water electrolysis (PEMWE) is carried out in an acidic environment where the
half cell reactions at the anode and cathode are:
Anode : H2O + 2 H+ + 1
2 O2 + 2 e−
(4)
Cathode : 2 H+ + 2 e−−−→2 H2
(5)
PEMWE is also a low-temperature technology as it operates best in the temperature range of 20-80°C, and it
is currently the most mature technological counterpart to AWE. The shift from alkaline to acidic conditions
means that the highly reactive protons are the charge carriers in the process. This makes the environment
more corrosive which in turn significantly decreases the amount of commercially viable materials [21].
The electrolyte in PEMWE needs to be stable, proton-conducting, gas-impermeable and electrically isolating.
The most widely used electrolyte is the solid polymer eletrolyte (SPE) perfluoro-sulfonic acid (PFSA), also
known as Nafion®. SPEs consist of ionisable functional groups, such as -SO3H in sulfonic acids, that are
embedded into the polymer matrix. These groups are hydrophilic so they can uptake water and provide the
protons necessary to facility the electrolysis via mechanisms such as Grothuss transport and diffusion [16, 22].
10
==PAGE:12==
SECTION 2
The electrodes and electrocatalysts in acidic media typically consists of noble metals as they are the among the
only metals that exhibit sufficient catalytic activity and stability. Metals like Ni and Co have been reported as
being unstable under acidic conditions and are therefore not used. The state-of-the-art commercial catalysts
are different for the HER and the OER [16] because of the differing reaction mechanisms. The catalytic
ability of several metals in 0.1 M H2SO4 at 80°C can be seen below [23]:
For HER: Pd > Pt > Rh > Ir > Re > Os > Ru > Ni
For OER: Ir ≈Ru > Pd > Rh > Pt > Au > Nb
Even though Pd shows higher catalytic ability for the HER, catalysts based on Pt give the best results indus-
trially. Carbon supported platinum (Pt/C) is a standard commercial choice. The performance and properties
of Pt/C changes depending on weight percentage as well as production methods. When it comes to the OER,
Ru has the highest catalytic ability but is heavily corroded in acidic media [21]. Due to its superb stability,
Ir is the commercial choice although not when in its metallic form due to durability. Instead, the metal oxide
IrO2 provides satisfactory performance. The major problems of these catalysts are their scarcity and cost
which is why a large amount of research is still being done to increase efficiency and stability [24].
Figure 5: Figure showing the structure of the PEMWE cell in 2-dimensions. Not to scale. Adapted from
Cavaliere 2023 [21].
A typical PEMWE cell (or SRU) has a structure that is very akin to the AWE cell. Figure 5 shows the cell
components as well as the flow of reactants/products while the 3D structure is similar to the one portrayed
in 4. It consists of a SPE with a cathode/anode catalyst layer (CL) and porous transport layer (PTL) on
either side sandwiched between two bipolar plates. The PTL and bipolar plates provide the same function
in PEMWE as in AWE. Namely, the PTL provides electrical contact between the CL and the bipolar plate,
and the bipolar plates with the engraved flow channels separate the cells, deliver current to the system and
11
==PAGE:13==
SECTION 2
transports reactants and products [24]. The SPE, CLs and PTLs make up the membrane electrode assem-
bly (MEA) and it has a great impact on the performance of the cell as a whole. The MEA can either be
manufactured by depositing the CL onto the PTL or the SPE [25]. Typical catalyst loadings range are 1-2
mg/cm2 on the anode and about 1 mg/cm2 on the cathode [21].
The harsh conditions also affect the material choice for the PTLs and the bipolar plates. The anodic side
experience more corrosion which further restrains usable materials. In general Ti, stainless steel and their
alloys seem to be the most investigated and viable metals. Ti is often used for the PTL in porous structures
such as meshes or foams, while both Ti and stainless steel can be used for the bipolar plates. On the cathodic
side, it is possible to use porous carbon structure for the PTL [25]. There are also sealings that prevent
leakages and these typically are made out of silicone rubber [7].
2.4
Solid oxide water electrolysis
Solid oxide water electrolysis (SOWE) is a high temperature electrolysis operating between 650°C and 1000°C.
SOWE utilizes typical solid oxide cells (SOCs) in their electrolysis mode (solid oxide electrolysis cell, SOEC)
[26]. The working principle at the anode and cathode are [27]:
Anode : 2 O2−−−→O2 + 4 e−
(6)
Cathode : H2O + 2 e−−−→H2 + O2−
(7)
SOECs can work in the "reverse" fuel cell mode (SOFC), but this function will not be further detailed.
The increase in operating temperature has the big benefit of reducing the overpotential needed to achieve
acceptable current densities. The energy needed to drive the water splitting reaction is lower in the gas phase
when compared to the liquid phase, and the additional energy from the heat improves the reaction kinetics
resulting in higher efficiencies [28].
The SOEC consists of a dense electrolyte with porous electrodes, current collectors and interconnects on
either side. The "sandwich" structure is similar to both AWE and PEMWE as can be seen in figure 6.
As can be seen, water (steam) is fed in at the cathode and the HER occurs at the interface between the
electrolyte and the cathode. The OER then occurs after oxygen-ions reach the anode-electrolyte interface.
On both sides this area is known as the triple-phase boundary (TPB) [27].

==PAGE:14==
SECTION 2
Figure 6: Figure showing the structure of the SOWE cell in 2-dimensions. Not to scale. Adapted from
Cavaliere 2023 [27].
The state-of-the-art material for the electrolyte is the ceramic yttria-stabilised zirconia (YSZ) which show-
cases good oxygen-ion conductivity aswell as superior stability during operation when compared to higher
ion-conducting materials such as doped ceria. The electrodes need to exhibit catalytic capability aswell as
thermal and chemical stability. The state-of-the-art cathode material is Ni composites which show good
catalytic ability for the the HER as previously discussed in AWE. Typically a Ni/YSZ composite is utilized,
but the Ni often sinters and agglomerates into coarse structures at 800°C-1000°C which reduces the size of
the TPB [26, 27].
The anode needs to support the OER for which state-of-the-art materials are lanthanum strontium mangan-
ite (LSM) or lanthanum strontium cobalt ferrite (LCSF). LSM outperforms LCSF at higher temperatures
(800°C-900°C) while LCSF is an excellent choice for the lower temperatures. LCSF can work as both an
electron- and ion-conductor, while LSM needs to have an ion-conductor added to ensure a sufficiently large
TPB. This is typically is also done by having a LSM/YSZ composite [26, 29].
Just as with AWE and PEMWE, the current collectors provide even electrical contact from the interconnects
as well as help evenly distribute the gas flow. A typical choice for the cathodic current collector is simply
a Ni mesh as due to excellent stability, electrical conductivity and cheap cost. The anodic current collector
is most likely to just be an additional layer of the underlying anode material, so if the anode consists of
LSM/YSZ an additional layer of LSM will make up the current collector [26].
The interconnect provides the electrical connection to the cells much like the bipolar plates. The interconnects
therefore also need to have high electronic conductivity and thermal stability. Furthermore, it needs a similar
13
==PAGE:15==
SECTION 2
thermal expansion coefficients to the electrolyte, electrodes and current collectors [30]. Stainless steel is a
great option and is one of the most commonly used materials, however, they do experience enough corrosion
at temperatures above 800°C to affect their performance. Materials such as Ca- or Sr-doped LaCrO3 can
work as interconnects between 900°C and 1000°C [31]. The sealing and frame of the cell are typically made of
glass ceramics (e.g. from the BaO-Al2O3-SiO2 system) as they soften at the operating temperatures, while
the frame is typically made of stainless steel [26].
The SRU needs a mechanically supporting element to make sure the functional layers are manageable, easy
to manufacture, transportable and integrable into mutli-celled stacks. In SOECs this support can be attained
from 5 different configurations[26]:
1. Electrolyte-supported cell (ESC) with a thick electrolyte
2. Anode-supported cell (ASC) with a thick anode
3. Cathode-supported cell (CSC) with a thick cathode
4. Metal-supported cell (MSC) with an extra electron-conducting metal phase
5. Inert-supported cell (ISC) with an extra fully inert material purely for support
Figure 7 shows the principle behind the different types of supported cells. In the review by Lenser et al.
2020 [26] it is clear that the CSC (ASC if in fuel cell mode) is the most researched type of SOC. These
configurations typically run at around 700°C but this is mostly in cases where it was run with one SRU.

2.5
Balance-of-Plant
The previous sections have explored the design of the water electrolysis cells, but this is only part of the
electrolysis plant. The other half of the plant consists of operational equipment that controls temperature,
heat exchanges, water supply of adequate purity, gas separation and gas purification. This equipment is
known as the balance-of-plant (BoP). Figure 8 represents an AWE plant with a zero-gap configuration. For
the low-temperature electrolysis (LTE) technologies AWE and PEMWE, the BoP is similar and so the figure
14
==PAGE:16==
SECTION 2
also to a certain extent represents the PEMWE BoP. Water is fed into the system, which in the case of
AWE contains KOH, after which it gets circulated around using pumps. Two flows exit the cell stack: one
containing oxygen-loaded water and one containing hydrogen-loaded water. The gasses are separated, after
which the oxygen is typically vented off. Hydrogen gets purified and sometimes pressurised so that it can be
stored [15, 16].
Figure 8: Balance-of-plant for the LTE technologies. Not included in the graphic are potential heat ex-
changers and the water purification unit. Adapted from Schalenbech et al. 2018 [16].
For the high-temperature electrolysis (HTE) SOWE the BoP looks quite different. As can be seen in figure
9 the main components consists of two counter-flow heat exchangers. Water for the system is fed into the
first heat exchanger recovers waste heat post-electrolysis. It then travels to the second heat exchanger which
increases the temperature of the water (steam) to operating conditions. The resulting hydrogen, oxygen and
steam feed into the first heat exchanger to cool down, after which the water condenses and separates from
the hydrogen [32].

2.6
Life cycle assessment
Life cycle assessment (LCA) is a science-based quantitative assessment that covers a broad range of environ-
mental issues. It is an excellent tool for environmentally assessing products and systems both individually
and comparatively in an unbiased manner. The methodology is subject to a few limitations such as the in-
ability to consider rare problematic events such as oil spills. Furthermore, it is a relative assessment method
in the sense that it can indicate which product systems are better than others, and not if they are absolutely
sustainable [6].
The LCA methological framework consists of 4 phases as laid out by the ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 standards
[33, 34]: goal and scope definiton, inventory analysis, impact assessment and interpretation (see figure 10).
The goal and scope definition are meant to explain which products and systems are assessed, how they will
get assessed and why the LCA is being performed. Both the goal and scope are taken into consideration
when the interpretation is performed because they shape how the data is collected and how the systems are
modelled. The inventory analysis collects detailed information on the input and out flows of all processes
within the product system (e.g. materials, products and emissions). In the end, the life cycle inventory (LCI)

SECTION 3
3
Goal and Scope
3.1
Goal
This life cycle assessment is intended to provide details on the environmental impacts of the most promi-
nent water electrolysis technologies in relation to hydrogen production. The most mature technologies will
be compared relative to each other and a detailed hot-spot analysis will be made for each technology. A
sensitivity and scenario analysis will also be performed on each technology.
As water electrolysis is an area of great interest, currently there are many different ways that the varying
technologies can be configured, which all perform slightly different. When deciding which configurations to
assess others will inevitably be left out, and so the comparisons of the technologies must be interpreted with
this in mind.
This study is being conducted because water electrolysis has the possiblity of playing a major role in reducing
environmental impacts for several systems. A study looking into the utilization of captured CO2 by Chris-
tensen and Bisinella 2021 [35] showed that hydrogen production from water electrolysis had relatively large
environmental burdens with regard to climate change. An LCA of the technologies could provide context
to the result as to figure out which areas bring about the largest burdens. Furthermore, it is important to
investigate if there are any environmental trade-offs when changing configurations so that the sole focus does
not lie on e.g. climate change. The decision context for the study is C1 as defined in the ISO standards as
the results will not amount to any decisions being made, but the study will interact with other system models.
The target audience is any party interested in an in depth life cycle assessment of current water electrolysis
technologies, be it experts in water electrolysis, students, professors etc. Finally, this study will not be issued
to the public but it may become available on the DTU Research Database (DTU Orbit).
3.2
Scope
The study will provide a detailed documentation of the Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) results and Life Cycle
Impact Assessment (LCIA) results, which will be presented in the thesis.
Each of the technologies utilize electrolysis to split water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen at their elec-
trodes. The technologies split water at varying pH levels and temperatures which affects the material choice
and electricity usage. With relation to the goal of the study the main function is the hydrogen production
at a certain purity. It’s possible to consider oxygen production as a secondary function of the system but for
this study it will be disregarded because the produced oxygen is effectively the same for each technology per
unit of H2. Furthermore it is often vented off as described in the background.
18
==PAGE:20==
SECTION 3
The functional unit has been defined as:
Production of 1 Nm3 H2 at a purity of >99% in a plant with a lifetime of 90.000h in Europe
This definition allows for fair comparison between the technologies as it takes into account the production
rate as well as the lifetime, which are some of the most influential factors in relation to resource use.
In the goal definition it was determined that the decision context for the study is C1 and this affects the way
multifunctionality within the system is handled. Seeing as water electrolysis also produces oxygen it would
normally be necessary to define how we handle this secondary function, but as previously described it will
be disregarded during modelling. As mentioned earlier, this LCA aims to look at the current state of water
electrolysis and as such the LCI modelling framework will be attributional. This means that the modelling
will be based on average background processes that for example represent the current status of the electricity
mix in Europe.
All processes from cradle-to-grave apart from the waste management are analysed. This includes the extrac-
tion of the raw materials like metals and fossil fuels, the production processes for the different components
such as stainless steel and metal foams and finally the use phase. Due to the limited knowledge regarding
the end-of-life of the electrolysers this part of the life cycle will not be included. The system boundaries are
illustrated in figure 11.

been combined in the illustration.

representativeness in the LCI data. The ecoinvent 3.10 database has been used to model the three systems
in the LCA software EASETECH. The process system model was chosen to be the allocation at the point

For the comparative assessment and the hot spot analysis the environmental footprint (EF) framework es-

Uncertainties are likely to affect the results of the comparison. First, some technologies will have better
representativeness as more accurate processes will be available. Second, the nature of the comparison is
heavily dependent on the reviewed literature and so it has to be clearly stated how the technologies have
been modelled and why. Finally, without having real examples with experimental data it is unlikely that the
stated parameters will match exactly with one another. In the end, all uncertainties affecting the comparison
will be taken into account to ensure a fair comparison.

Interpretation
6.1
Significant issues
When looking at the results of the LCIA the most glaring issue is obvious: electricity consumption. As
indicated by the hot spot analysis it is by far the biggest contributor and seems to largely dictate the results
across all impact scores. During modelling the electricity consumption was set highest to lowest in the order
of AWE, PEMWE and SOWE [5], which is the biggest reason as to why AWE has the largest impact scores.
This would indicate that any improvements made towards reducing electricity consumption is currently the
most efficient strategy for reducing the environmental burden of the technologies.
Looking beyond the electricity consumption, there are a few other components/elements that had noticeable
impact scores in the hot spot analysis, namely the AWE bipolar plates, the PEMWE anode and electrolyte
and the SOWE water consumption. The presence of the bipolar plates is most probably because of the
large combined mass stemming from it being a thick component. When it comes to the PEMWE anode
and electrolyte they score high in their respective impact categories mostly because of the material choice.
It needs to be stated that the IrO2 anode had to be modelled by the approximate material Rh, and the
electrolyte was only modelled after tetrafluoroethylene and without sulfonic acid due to lack of data. With
these results it might be of interest to look into modelling these components more precisely. Finally, the
water consumption is more impactful in SOWE compared to the other technologies. This indicates that the
process of superheating steam is more demanding than the process of purifying water, but still this increased
demand is environmentally beneficial as a result of the lower electricity consumption.
6.2
Sensitivity analysis
To supplement the results of the hot spot analysis, a sensitivty analysis was performed. Table 8 shows the
sensitivity ratios for the highest scoring parameters: electricity consumption at both the stack and BoP level.
This was to be expected after looking at the hot spot analysis. The sensitivity ratios indicate that for a
10% increase in electricity consumption of either type the total impact score will increase with 40-50%. The
only other noticeable observation found from this sensitivity check is for the electrical efficiency. The results
for electrical efficiency can be found in appendix A.2, table 18. Here it can be seen that a higher efficiency
would slightly increase the impact scores of AWE and PEMWE, but decrease the score of SOWE. A higher
efficiency results in lower water consumption, so it makes sense that SOWE with the more impacting water
consumption process would experience greater benefits.
The sensitivity analysis in conjunction with the hot spot analysis can be used to evaluate if certain assump-
tions and modelling choices should be reconsidered. A minor assumption was made with regards to the input
process for electricity consumption. It was decided that medium voltage would be used for modelling of all

proposed thicknesses were also different to a certain extent. Second, they had access to more accurate data
as they were collaborating with scientists and industrial partners, and so their processes are likely to have
been more representative. Other factors such as methodological approach and assumptions made will also
have contributed to the difference in the results. All things considered, the likeness of the impact scores adds

SECTION 7
7
Discussion
Electricity consumption can be improved if the cell efficiencies are improved, which in turn would lower the
environmental impacts of the technolgoies. In Schmidt et al. 2017 [5] it was mentioned that the industry is
not necessarily focused on improving cell efficiency for two reason. First, because of the relatively low cost of
electricity compared to materials and second, because the efficiencies are maximised at low current densities
resulting in research being focused on improving current densities instead. While this focus is mainly driven
by the high costs of the scarce materials like Pt and Ir, it would still produce environmental benefits because
of the reduced consumption. Reducing consumption in general is quite sought after with PGMs, as their
supply consistently falls behind the demand in recent years [52].
An potential point of interest for water electrolysis that is not considered in this LCA is the presence of
impurities within the water. Impurities affect the performance and durability of the LTE technologies, as
some impurities like the cation Fe3+ increase the activity of the OER. Other impurities such as Cd2+ poison
the catalyst, which effectively deactivate it, while Cl−increase corrosion on nickel electrodes [53]. Another
researched aspect is if would be possible to achieve hydrogen production when using seawater but here the
main problem is that the potential for chlorine to be formed at the anode.
Several solutions have been
proposed such as operation at low current density or use of special catalysts [54] and with those come trade-
offs in the environmental impact when compared to purified freshwater. Potential scenario analyses could be
carried out once more research is done on water impurities or seawater within water electrolysis.
7.1
Future work
If this work were to be continued there is one key point that needs to be addressed. The modelling of the
electricity consumption should be redone as to achieve the highest possible data quality. With it being the
highest sensitivity parameters it is crucial that the most accurate data are used and that they allow fair
comparison between the technologies. Second, it would be interesting to look into the aforementioned affect
of impurities in the water as well as the potential for using seawater for electrolysis.

SECTION 8
8
Conclusion
In this thesis the most prominent water electrolysis technologies have been elucidated and a life cycle assess-
ment (LCA) was conducted. The chosen technologies consists of the 2 low temperature technologies alkaline
water electrolysis (AWE) and proton exchange membrane water electrolysis (PEMWE), along with the high
temperature solid oxide water electrolysis (SOWE). A life cycle inventory (LCI) was pieced together from
several journal articles, data sheets and technical reports where the cell specifications thickness, surface area
and density are provided. While water electrolyzers can be manufactured in many varying configurations
with different performance, the LCI just describes one configuration per technology. The quality of the ac-
quired data was qualitatively rated and was regarded as being low in uncertainty. Some components of the
alkaline AWE had high uncertainty whilst several PEMWE components received a medium uncertainty rating.
The life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) provided characterised results of the 16 impact categories found in
the Environmental Footprint (EF) impact method. The characterised results showed that AWE narrowly had
larger impact scores for a majority of the categories, with the difference rarely being more than 5%. SOWE
was by far the least impactful technology as it was scored 30-40% lower in all categories. The following hot
spot analysis revealed that the results were heavily determined by the electricity consumption at both the
stack and balance-of-plant (BoP) level. In the 3 categories where PEMWE had a higher impact score it was
due to either the material choice for the anode or electrolyte, indicating that the PEMWE cell contained en-
vironmentally burdensome materials. The water consumption was also noticeable more impactful for SOWE,
because it uses steam rather than purified water. The sensitivity analysis quantified the hot spot results
further to show that the electricity consumptions by far were the most sensitive parameters. As such, none
of the assumptions were categorised as having significantly affected the results.
Finally, the results were recalculated with a new impact method (ReCiPe 2016) and an alternative functional
unit (1 m2 of cell). This was done to compare the results of the cell components to the LCA in Zhao et al.
2020. The results were within one order of magnitude for AWE and PEMWE, while it often was two orders
of magnitude lower for SOWE. The identified critical materials were stainless steel for AWE, PEMWE and
SOWE because of the bipolar plates and interconnects. PEMWE was mostly affected by the IrO2 in the
anode.
For any future work, the primary focus should lie on the electricity consumption as it is the main environmen-
tal burden. The data needs to be as accurate as possible and perhaps several scenarios should be included
where the effect of material choice of electricity consumption is explored.
