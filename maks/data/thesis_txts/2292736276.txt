Abstract
The purpose of this study was to investigate the frequency dependent material properties of the vis-
coelastic material, glass ﬁber reinforced vinylester, which potentially can be used to construct T shaped
power pylon composite structures. This was done by performing two types of experiments. A numerical
imitation of the viscoelastic material properties was furthermore performed with the two rheological
models, the Kelvin Voigt model and the Zener model.
Tensile dynamic tests were performed for rectangular specimens oriented in three directions with respect
to the lay-up orientation of the laminate. The frequency dependency of the material stiﬀness and damping
has been derived from the experimental results. It was from these investigations found that the stiﬀness
increased as the frequency increased for all three specimen orientations. The damping was found to reach
a maximum value at diﬀerent frequencies depending on the specimen orientation.
Impulse tests were performed on a cantilever beam with material properties equivalent to the tensile
specimens, and a geometry that can be related to the arms of the power pylon composite structures.
Based on these tests, the natural frequency and damping ratio was determined for the beam.
By deriving the governing diﬀerential equations for the Kelvin Voigt and the Zener model, these were
implemented in the Matlab-based FE program MaxiFrame and calibrated to ﬁt the tensile test results.
The ability of the numerical models to imitate the experimental results for both the cantilever beam and
tensile tests has been evaluated in detail.
Based on the ﬁndings of this study it is possible to estimate what the ideal natural frequency of the
ﬁnal power pylon structure should be in order to obtain maximum damping, assuming that the actual
structure will be made by a viscoelastic material with properties similar to the glass ﬁber reinforced
vinylester tested in this thesis.

Resumé
Formålet med dette studie var at undersøge de frekvensafhængige materialeegenskaber, der ﬁndes i det
viskoelastiske materiale glasﬁber forstærket vinylester, som kan blive brugt til at lave T formet komposit
højspændingsmaster. Dette er gjort ud fra to typer af eksperimenter. En numerisk gengivelse af de
eksperimentelle resultater blev lavet, med de to rheologiske modeller, Kelvin Voigt modellen og Zener
modellen.
Dynamiske træk test blev udført for testemner orienteret i tre forskellige retninger i forhold til den prede-
ﬁnerede orientering af laminatlagene i det undersøgte glasﬁber forstærkede kompositmateriale. Frekven-
safhængigheden for materialestivheden of dæmpningen i materialet blev udledt fra eksperimenternes re-
sultater. Fra disse undersøgelser sås det at materialestivheden steg, når frekvensen steg samt at materialet
opnåede maksimal dæmpning ved forskellige frekvenser afhængigt af prøvelegemernes orientering.
Efterfølgende blev impuls laste påført en udkravet bjælke, lavet af samme materiale som prøvelegemerne
lastet af dynamiske træk laste, med en geometri der minder om de udkravede arme som på toppen af
komposit højspændingsmasten. Ud fra disse den eksperimentelle data blev egenfrekvensen og dæmpn-
ingsforholdet for bjælken fundet.
Ved at udlede de styrende diﬀerentialligninger for Kelvin Voigt og Zener modellen, blev den numeriske
implementering i det Matlab-baserede FE program Maxiframe udført og kalibreret i forhold til test
resultaterne fundet fra træk testene. De numeriske modeller blev efterfølgende evalueret, ved at undersøge
med hvor god en præcision, disse gengav de eksperimentelle resultater fundet for både træk testene og
bjælke testene.
Fra resultaterne fundet i dette studie, er det, under forudsætning af at den endelige højspændingsmast
bliver lavet af et materiale med lignende egenskaber, som de eksperimentelt testede prøvelegemer, muligt
at give et approksimativt bud på den ideelle egenfrekvens hvor maksimal dæmpning af den faktiske
konstruktion kan blive fundet.

1
Introduction
Conventional transmission towers are today mainly made of steel. The high electric conductivity of this
material results in large structures, since it needs to be ensured that the distance between the power lines
is suﬃcient. A recent alternative power pylon design proposal, made by Bystrup Architects, has sug-
gested using the low conducting composite material, glass ﬁber reinforced polymer instead. Approximate
estimations suggest that using this will lead to a reduction of the structural weight by 70%, faster instal-
lation time and a compact design, which only is half the size of comparable conventional transmission
towers, as seen in ﬁgure 1.1 [5].
Figure 1.1: Comparison of conventional transmission tower, and suggested composite power pylon design.
Power pylons are designed to withstand harsh environmental conditions, since wind induced vibrations
can result in galloping vibrations of the transmission line that can cause catastrophic failure of both
the lines and tower. However by optimizing the design of the power pylon, it is possible to ensure that
free response vibrations of the structure are related to the frequency where maximum damping of the
composite material is found, which can help prevent critical conditions to occur. Bystrup Architechts
have for this reason included The Technical University of Denmark in the design phase of the power
pylons, in order to ensure that the damping properties of the power pylon are accounted for. The scope
of this thesis is to investigate the damping properties of a relevant type glass ﬁber reinforced polymer
material through experiments and try to imitate these properties by numerical calculations. Two types of
experiments will be conducted. The ﬁrst type contains tensile tests of small scale specimens, performed
in order to investigate the properties of the material. These results will be used investigate the frequency
where maximum damping is found for the material, and to calibrate the numerical models. Afterwards
tests will be performed on a cantilever beam, which is designed with a geometry that can be related to
the arms of the power pylon connected to the transmission lines. By performing impulse tests on the
cantilever beam, the ﬁrst natural vibration is determined, and comparable results are derived from the
numerical models, in order to investigate the how well these models can imitate the actual properties of
the cantilever beam.

2.1
Glass-reinforced plastic
A Composite material is a minimum of two materials that combined have improved properties compared
to the individual materials.
An example of such a material is glass ﬁber reinforced plastics.
The
most common form of glass ﬁber reinforced plastics use epoxy, vinylester or polyester as the polymer,
reinforced by E-glass ﬁbers [24].
The reinforcing glass ﬁbers can either be mixed with the resin as
discontinuous randomly oriented ﬁbers or as systematically oriented continuous ﬁbers. Since the strength
and stiﬀness is higher for continuous ﬁbers, these can be advantageous compared with random ﬁbers
when used for structural purposes. Continuous ﬁbers can be split up in three groups; unidirectional,
cloth or roving ﬁbers. Since the scope of the current analysis is to compare numerical and experimental
results of specimens compounded of multiple laminas with unidirectional continuous ﬁbers, the latter
two continuous ﬁber types of continuous ﬁber reinforcement will not be mentioned any further. Glass-
reinforced plastics, with unidirectional ﬁbers have anisotropic material properties, meaning that both the
stiﬀness and strength depends on the load direction. When multiple unidirectional laminas are stacked
in varies angles the lay-up is called a laminate. The strength and stiﬀness of laminates can be designed
conveniently with respect to the applied load in order to minimize the necessary number of unidirectional
layers or maximize the stiﬀness of the composite material [6].

dependent. Two methods, that can be used to determine the damping properties of glass ﬁber rein-
forced materials, by free response analysis respectively harmonic loading, are presented in the following

2.5
Viscoelastic properties described by rheological models
Because the properties of viscoelastic materials are time dependent, the classic linear relation σ =
Eε, which can be related to Hookes law, can not be used to describe the time dependent stress-strain
properties. However time dependent stress-strain relations can be obtained by including viscous dampers,
in either series and/or parallel with the spring. The ﬁrst who developed models that could be used to
describe viscoelastic properties, were the Scottish physicist James Clerk Maxwell (1831 −1879) who
developed the Maxwell model and Lord Kelvin (1824 −1907) who developed the Kelvin Voigt model
[4]. These models provide the foundation of rheological models used to represent time dependent stress-
strain relations through springs and viscous dashpots. For both the Kelvin Voigt model and the Maxwell
model, the time dependent properties are included by implementing a viscous damper in either parallel
or series with the spring, as illustrated in ﬁgure 2.5 [20].

Experimental work
The damping properties of glass ﬁber reinforced vinylester have been analyzed through two types of
experiments
• Dynamic tensile tests of rectangular glass ﬁber reinforced vinylester specimens
• Dynamic bending tests of a cantilever glass ﬁber reinforced vinylester beam
The main purpose of both tests are to determine the damping properties in terms of the loss factor.
The damping properties are for the tensile test specimens determined by measuring the delay in the
displacement response, when applying a load. Based on the delay, the phase shift can be found, which
is used to determine the loss factor, as described in detail in Section 2.3. By calculating the loss factor
a diﬀerent frequencies, an overview of the frequency dependency for the viscoelastic material can be
obtained, which provides information that can be related to considerations in the design process of the
power pylon composite structure.
The cantilever beam is tested by applying impulse loads. By recording the acceleration history from the
impulse load, the damping ratio and loss factor of the beam is estimated from the damping decrement as
described in section 2.3.1. Furthermore fast fourier transformations (FFT) is used to identify the natural
frequency related the ﬁrst lateral vibration mode of the beam.
Each of the two types of glass ﬁber composite specimens, were produced in a version made by hand
laminated processing and another made by vacuum infusion processing. To make the hand laminated
specimens, polyester was used used as resin. To make the vacuum infused test specimens, vinylester was
used as resin. Hand lay-up is an open molding process, where vacuum infusion processing is a closed
molding process, performed with lower than atmospheric pressure in the mold cavity, which makes it
possible to obtain very high resin to glass ratios [1]. Due to the pressure diﬀerence for the two production
methods, diﬀerent thicknesses were obtained, even through the lay-ups were identical. According to Tuco
Marine Group, the expected thickness of the laminates, were prior production given to be as shown in
table 3.1.
It should be emphasized that only experimental analysis of the glass ﬁber reinforced vinylester specimens
made by vacuum infused processing is performed in this study.

3.1.1
Design of test specimens
In order to extract the tensile test specimens from the composite plate, a specimen design and cut out
method had to be chosen. To determine the specimen dimensions, the ASTM D4065-12 [2] standard was
used. This is made as a guide to determine the dynamic mechanical properties of plastics depending on
the testing technique, frequency and mode of oscillation. Since the specimens were loaded in tension,
the mode of oscillation closest related to the current analysis was assumed to be the cantilever specimen
loaded in tension. The suggested dimensions for such a specimen is in ASTM D4065-12 given to be;
thickness (t) = 0.025 −1.0 mm, width (b) = 12.7 mm and length (L) = 63.5 mm. However since the
ﬁber reinforced plate was signiﬁcantly thicker than the suggested thickness of maximum 1.0 mm, the
plate dimensions were scaled with respect to the upper limit of the suggested thickness; t = 5.5 mm,
b = 63.5 mm, L = 381 mm, in order to obtain specimen dimensions closer related to the suggestion of
the specimen dimensions.
Another aspect which needed to be considered, before the ﬁnal specimen dimensions could be chosen,
was the statistical signiﬁcance of the results. According to the ASTM3039 standard [3], it is seen that
a minimum of 5 specimens is recommended to be used, when determining tensile properties of polymer
matrix composite materials.

w = 30 mm and L = 240 mm. As presented in ﬁgure 3.1 this provided a total of 12 specimens, of
which four were oriented in each of the three directions; 0 degrees, 45 degrees and 90 degrees. The
specimens that have been cut out in the 0 degree orientation have a lay up which in the longitudinal
specimen direction corresponds the lay-up deﬁned in table 3.1. This is assumed to be an acceptable
compromise when considering both the ratio between the length, thickness and width, and the total

test has been suﬃciently long for the loss factor to reach a steady level. The reason for the increasing
loss factor is expected to be caused by the viscoelastic properties of the glass ﬁber composite. Since the
strain is set to oscillate about the ε = 0.2% instead of 0.0%, the measured test data will be eﬀected by
the stress relaxation in the material. With reference to the loss factor calculation, presented in equation
2.17, this means storage modulus, E′, decreases by time, which will cause the phase shift and loss factor
to increase, until the eﬀect of the stress relaxation is insigniﬁcant. Having this in mind, the loss factors
estimated closest to the end of the tests are expected to provide the best representation of the actual
loss factor for the material.

compounds; resin, hardener and ﬁller material. The resin/hardener ratio was 4/1. After mixing the two
compounds, the ﬁller material, consisting of glass bobbles, was ﬁlled into the mixture until the viscosity
was suﬃciently high, and the mixture could be poured into the empty space inside the supporting steel
proﬁles. The ﬁller material works as a reinforcing compound, and will for this reason strengthen the
epoxy, compared to a mixture only consisting of resin and hardener.

Conclusion
To understand the damping and stiﬀness properties of the glass ﬁber reinforced vinylester material inves-
tigated, two types of experiments were conducted. To imitate the experimentally determined properties,
the governing diﬀerential equations for the two rheological models; the Kelvin Voigt and the Zener model
were derived, and implemented in the existing Matlab-based FE program MaxiFrame.
The experiments were made on specimens delivered by Tuco, consisting of a quadratic plate measuring
550x550x5 mm and a cantilever beam, which was connected to a supporting back plate (see ﬁgure 3.10
a). Both specimens were made by the same production method, and had identical lamina lay-up, as
shown in see table 3.1, which meant that the material properties in the to specimen types theoretically
should be identical.
To obtain a general understanding of the damping and stiﬀness properties of the viscoelastic material, the
rectangular test specimens with dimensions 30x240x5 mm, were designed and cut out of the quadratic
plate. This provided a total of 12 test specimens, split in three groups of four specimens with diﬀerent
orientation, as presented in ﬁgure 3.1. The mutual orientation of the specimens meant that it was possible
to extract material properties representing the 0, 45 respectively 90 degree orientations, with respect to
the laminate lay-up orientation. By testing the specimens, the recorded stress-strain data were used to
derive properties regarding the material stiﬀness and damping at the six test frequencies, ranging from
0.1 to 10.0 Hz, for each of the three specimen orientations. From these results it was seen that the
stiﬀness increased when the frequency increased, and that the damping peaked at a frequency near 1.0
Hz for the 0 and 45 degree oriented specimens, and at a frequency near 0.5 Hz for the 90 degree oriented
specimens. Average values of the stiﬀnesses and loss factors, for each of the specimen orientations and
test frequencies, are presented in table 3.4 and 3.6.
The experimental tests performed on the cantilever beam, with a total length of 1.2 m, and cross section
geometry as shown in ﬁgure 3.10 b, were afterwards conducted by applying an impulse load to the free end
of the beam and record the free response acceleration history. The natural frequency and damping ratio
were from this test found to ω1,EXP = 16.72 Hz and ζ1,EXP = 0.29%. Since the beam was asymmetric
about the axis transverse to the load application direction, a misalignment of the shear center and center
of gravity could have inﬂuenced the natural frequency due to eﬀects of coupled torsion-bending vibration
mode. Another parameter which may have inﬂuenced the measured natural frequency, is the rigidity of
the ﬁxed end, which is expected to be less rigid than a fully ﬁxed beam end. Both of these observations
have for this reason been taken into consideration, when comparing the results with similar numerical
results. Because it originally was planned to test the cantilever beam with a driven actuator at the
free end, a mass consisting of hardened epoxy and a steel proﬁle had been attached. The weight of the
attached mass was estimated to be ∆m = 4.82 kg. The experimentally determined natural frequency
does for this reason not represent the natural frequency of the beam alone, since it is aﬀected by the
additional mass.
From the stiﬀness and damping properties found for the tensile tests in the 0 degree direction, which
is identical to the ﬁber orientation in the beam, the model parameters used to determine the time
dependent stress-strain relations for the two rheological models were calibrated and implemented in
the numerical calculations.
Furthermore the geometry of the cantilever beam was implemented, by
calculating the constitutive stiﬀnesses of the cross section geometry by the Matlab-based BeamSec
program, which due to the changing geometry along the beam was updated for every element in the

numerical model. Based on the properties implemented for the cantilever beam, the natural frequency
was found to ω1,NUM = 20.86 Hz in the numerical calculations. This corresponds a deviation of 24.8%
compared to the experimentally determined natural frequency. Since the natural frequency is inﬂuenced
by multiple factors such as the weight lumped mass attached the free beam end, the constitutive stiﬀness
of the beam and the tensile stiﬀness of the material, it was diﬃcult to identify exact source for the
deviation.
The ability of the rheological models to imitate the damping properties of the viscoelastic material, was
afterwards investigated. The damping ratio for the cantilever beam was by the Kelvin Voigt model found
to ζ1,KV = 11.1% and by the Zener model found to ζ1,ZEN = 0.07%. Both damping ratios are considered
related to the numerically determined natural frequency of 20.86 Hz.
Since the peak damping was identiﬁed to occur at frequencies near 1.0 Hz for the 0 degree orientated
tensile test specimens, the Kelvin Voigt model was calibrated to represent damping for frequencies below
1.0 Hz. With this in mind it is understandable that the linear damping-frequency relation for the Kelvin
Voigt model overestimated the damping of the beam, since the natural frequency is signiﬁcantly larger
than 1.0 Hz. Further investigations of the numerically implemented Kelvin Voigt model indicated that the
model was capable of providing a good approximation of the experimental results from the tensile tests
within the 0.5 Hz to 1.0 Hz range, but underestimated the loss factor found at 0.1 Hz and overestimated
loss factors found at frequencies higher than 1.0 Hz.
The damping ratio estimated by the Zener model was found to be only 24% of the experimentally deter-
mined loss factor for the cantilever beam. By investigating the damping-frequency relation for the Zener
model, it was found that the peak loss factor of 1.5% as expected occurred at 1.0 Hz. However compari-
son with the experimental results obtained from the tensile tests and the cantilever beam indicated that
Zener model underestimated the damping for very high and low frequencies, and overestimated damping
near the peak at 1.0 Hz as seen from ﬁgure 4.21.
Assuming that the power pylon structure will be made by a material with properties equivalent to the
experimentally tested glass ﬁber reinforced vinylester, it is from the ﬁndings of this study shown that
optimal damping properties of the structure are obtained, if the design leads to a natural frequency
of approximately 1.0 Hz. The numerically implemented Zener model is furthermore seen have great
potential to imitate the stiﬀness and damping properties, and is expected to be able to provide a good
approximation of the properties for the actual structure, if recalibration of the model parameters is
performed, based on a larger amount of experimental data.

STORAGE
To ensure maximum stability and maintain optimum resin properties, resins should be stored in closed containers 
at temperatures below 24°C/75°F and away from heat ignition sources and sunlight.  Resin should be warmed to 
at least 18°C/65°F prior to use in order to assure proper curing and handling.  All storage areas and containers 
should conform to local fire and building codes.  Copper or copper containing alloys should be avoided as 
containers.  Store separate from oxidizing materials, peroxides and metal salts.  Keep containers closed when not 
in use.  Inventory levels should be kept to a reasonable minimum with first-in, first-out stock rotation. 
Additional information on handling and storing unsaturated polyesters is available in Reichhold’s application 
bulletin “Bulk Storage and Handling of Unsaturated Polyester Resins.”  For information on other Reichhold resins
