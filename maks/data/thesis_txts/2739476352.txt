Abstract
In this Master’s Thesis, two types of adaptive sliding mode controllers are designed and
evaluated through simulations for reference tracking of a UR10e robot manipulator. Both
controllers are of the adaptive gain types, with one being a barrier­function type sliding
mode controller. The adaptive properties of the controllers enable rejection of unknown
disturbances and system dynamics, thereby resulting in a robust system that can maintain
high accuracy tracking. The controllers are also extended with an auxiliary safety­critical
controller for avoiding predefined unsafe sets. All controllers are simulated in various
scenarios with varying motions, disturbance models, and unsafe sets, with performance
comparison being made against a conventional controller type. The results show that the
barrier­function adaptive technique have great potential in both regular and safety­critical
control scenarios, which must be further explored.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
In the modern day, the industry becomes ever increasingly more complex. Fast, high
accuracy task completion with precise coordination between multiple pieces of equipment
is needed. Satisfactory control of the individual machines in these scenarios depend on
the proper closed­loop definitions.
A robot manipulator is one type of these tools found in industrial settings whether it be
pick and place, welding or other tasks. Besides the need of coordination between several
machines, a robot manipulator is in itself a highly complex tool with its multi­actuated
design for control of every limb. Therefore, several controllers are required for one single
robot manipulator for achieving high precision motion control.
Over time, wear and tear affects every mechanical system leading to changes in the nom­
inal model upon which the controller is defined. Therefore, unwanted performance degra­
dation sets in. Furthermore, external disturbances such as the gravitational force from an
object being carried by a manipulator adds extra uncertainty if the mass of said object is
not known exactly. While systems can be serviced and the controller revised in order to
mitigate said changes, it is costly and time consuming process. A much more desirable
to have a controller that is able to account for these changes on line.
Fortunately, this is possible by the use of adaptive type controllers. As suggested by
the name, such controllers have properties that allow them to adapt, thereby overcoming
uncertainties acting on the controlled system.
In the recent years, sliding mode controllers have been shown to be robust while maintain­
ing high accuracy in motion systems. Furthermore, their adaptive variants are capable of
handling disturbances and the phenomena of system degradation. As such, this controller
type is a great candidate for satisfying many of the problems seen in real­life scenarios of
the industry.
Besides the need to overcome unmodelled uncertainties, safety­critical control is also
a highly relevant topic, where some definable unsafe set is avoided by the controlled
system. In a highly dynamic environment such as the case of a robot manipulator moving
around within its reachable space, some small subset might suddenly become unsafe due
to external factors. Here, it is desirable to have a safety­critical controller to either take
full control or assist the nominal controller.
Based on the above, the overall goal of this study is to employ different adaptive slid­
ing mode controllers to a six degree of freedom (DOF) robot manipulator and compare
their performance against a conventional controller. Additionally, the controllers will be
extended to include a safety­critical controller for avoiding unsafe sets of the task space.

First, a literature review will be conducted on the relevant topics regarding sliding mode
control and safety­critical control. In order to control the 6­DOF robot manipulator, a first­

The adaptive sliding mode controllers and their safety­critical variants will lastly be eval­

1.3
Literature Review
In this section, a review is given of the methodologies relevant to the project. The re­
view will consist of the newest advances in the field, but it will also bring up the preceding
techniques that have lead to the current stage of sliding mode control. Therefore, regu­
lar sliding mode control will be described first, followed by adaptive sliding mode control
variants, and at last the use of control barrier functions for safety­critical control will be
studied.
1.3.1
Sliding Mode Control
Sliding Mode Control (SMC) has its origins in Russian literature in a sense of variable
structure control [1],[2], and [3]. Since then, SMC has been applied to numerous control
systems such as robot manipulators [4]. SMC is able to handle matched disturbances
without knowing them directly, whilst ensuring finite­time convergence. This, however,
comes at the cost of a chattering control signal stemming from its discontinuous high fre­
quency switching. Therefore, several techniques have been developed to reduce chat­
tering.
One of these is the continuous approximation via a saturation function [5], though this
does degrade the overall performance of the SMC. From the initial first­order SMC, vari­
ous higher order sliding mode control (HOSMC) have been implemented with one of the
most popular types being the super­twisting variant (STSMC). In STSMC, an integral com­
ponent is added to the control signal, leading to the super­twisting property, seen in [6]
for motion control systems.
1.3.2
Adaptive Sliding Mode Control
As indicated by the name, adaptive sliding mode controllers (ASMC) are SMC with added
adaptive capabilities. This family of SMC have adjustable parameters depending on vari­
ous changes to the controlled system. While adaptive SMC has its origins stretching back
to the 1960’s in for example [7], one of the earlier modern examples of ASMC with vary­
ing gain appears in [8]. Here, the gain dynamics depends directly on the sliding variable,
however, when entering sliding mode the gain remains constant despite the possibility of
2

==PAGE:15==
over­estimation with respect to the bounds of the uncertainties. Nonetheless, the control
structure allows the uncertainty bounds to be unknown.
A different ASMC control structure is proposed in [9] with the capability to decrease the
controller gains after reaching sliding mode. However, an alternative is presented in [10]
as it was shown that improper initial gain values could lead to instability. As such, un­
certainties could not be completely unknown, and the newly proposed control law would
therefore compromise with slightly degraded performance in favor of robustness towards
uncertainties.
Extension of STSMC to have adaptive gains can be seen in [11] and further improved in
the later paper [12]. As with the ASMC, the adaptive STSMC (ASTSMC) is also able to
handle uncertainties with unknown bounds. Also, upon reaching sliding mode, the gains
will start decreasing to prevent over­estimation. [13] proposes an ASTSMC for robot ma­
nipulators using a novel sliding manifold design with implementation on a 2­DOF robot.
General adaptive HOSMC have also been implemented in [14].
Within the last decade, a new type of ASMC has been introduced in [15] by using barrier
functions (BFASMC) on first­order SMC, which confines the sliding variable to a neighbor­
hood around zero. If the sliding variable approaches the barriers due to some perturba­
tion, the adaptive gain in turn approaches infinity. When initializing the system outside of
the barrier­function bounds, a control law aims to bring the system towards said bounds,
whereafter the control law changes to the BF­based control law. A method of prescribing
the time required to reach the bound was found in [16]. In [17] the barrier function adap­
tive method was then adapted to the STSMC (BFASTSMC).
In one of the newest additions to the area of BFASMC, the signed power­based controller
(BFASP) and bipowered controllers (BFAB) are introduced [18]. The latter constitutes a
dual barrier­function control law with adjustable exponents for increased robustness. By
setting the exponent of the barrier­function, the final set of the sliding variable can be
manipulated.
1.3.3
Control Barrier Functions in Safety­Critical Control
On the topic of safety­critical control using control barrier­functions (CBF), different meth­
ods have been developed. A CBF based quadratic program (QP) was applied to adaptive
cruise control [19]. This method was later later employed in robotic systems in [20] and
improved upon in [21] for both kinematic and dynamic control on a 6­DOF robot manip­
ulator. The CBF­QP method has also been applied as model­free control systems for
obstacle avoiding drones and quadruped robots [22]. Even newer is the implementation
of the CBF­QP with a SMC [23] for both perturbed and unperturbed systems with the latter
being based on the tunable input­to­state safe CBF from [24].
A recent paper explores the possibility of safe SMC by constructing a safe sliding man­
ifold (SSM) with a relative degree of one [25]. Here, the system avoids an unsafe set
both when converging to and in sliding mode by combining control Lyapunov functions

(CLF) and CBF’s. Unlike the CBF­SMC­QP, the CBF is integrated directly into the man­

presented a CLF­CBF merging strategy for stable, safe control of time­delayed systems
based on SMC theory. The idea of merging these function was proposed in for example
[27] for stabilization with guaranteed safety in nonlinear systems, which in turn is inspired

2.1
Introductory Robot Manipulator Theory
Robot manipulators are electro­mechanical machines consisting of one or more links with
each pair of links being connected by a joint with revolute or prismatic properties. For
each joint, a servo motor transforms electrical power into joint motion. The number of
extra link and joint pairs expand the reachable space, also called the task space, along
with adding additional joint configurations for obtaining the same position and orientation
at the manipulator tip. This tip is called the end­effector and it is either the tip of the last link
or the manipulator tool if one is attached. When talking about the position and orientation,
an inclusive term of both is pose.
With six revolute joints in a specific configuration, it is possible to have six DOF mean­
ing the manipulator can move the end­effector in all translational and rotational dimen­
sions. Thereby, the manipulator mimics the human arm by generally being able to attain
Adaptive Sliding Mode Control of Robotic Manipulator
5
==PAGE:18==
some pose in the taskspace through multiple joint configurations. In the case of Universal
Robots, all models have the 6­DOF property.
In terms of control of a robot manipulator, one can focus on either the task space or the
joint space. The former relates to the cartesian space in which the manipulator is located,
allowing for easily definable objectives for the manipulator end­effector, simply because
of the cartesian space itself. This, however, complicates the individual joint control. Con­
trarily, joint space control, defined by the vector of translational and angular displacement
of the individual links, gives easy control of the robot links at the cost of more complicated
end­effector task definitions.
As an example, take some spherical obstacle in the cartesian space that the end­effector
must circumvent. This sphere can easily be defined in the cartesian space, but seeing as
several joint configurations can lead to the same end­effector pose, the spherical obstacle
attains some abstruse shape that is complicated to model in the joint space. Therefore,
a mapping between the two is needed for achieving proper manipulator control.
When it comes to robot modeling, there are two approaches. The first is kinematics:
the study of the end­effector pose, velocity, and acceleration, based on the coupling of
robot links and joints. Logically, inverse kinematics relates to the opposite, which is more
complicated compared to the forward variant due to the already established possibility
of an end­effector pose being achievable through multiple joint configurations. Since the
kinematic aspect is not the primary focus of this project, it has been omitted from the main
report and can instead be found appendix A.
The second approach is dynamics modeling, a method that builds upon the kinematic
approach by joining together the robot manipulator in terms of torques and forces affecting
the links depending on their position and velocities. By assuming the torques and forces
as system inputs, the electrical aspect of the robot manipulator is disregarded, which is
the approach used in this thesis.

the friction model, fd, is of the following format

Basic Control Concepts
This chapter aims to give the reader an explanation of the basic theoretical concepts that
lay foundation for the control design in chapter 4. In order the sections govern, 3.1 SMC,
3.2 ASMC, and 3.3 safety­critical control. Furthermore, the section on SMC covers both
regular and the super­twisting variant, and the section on ASMC covers the basics of the
two types of adaptive gain SMC seen in chapter 4.
3.1
Sliding Mode Control
Generally, SMC relies on the definition of a so called sliding variable based on the sys­
tem to be controlled. When this variable is equal to zero, it resides on the sliding man­
ifold and the system is therefore in sliding mode. Here, a certain constraint based on
the equation defining the manifold is then imposed on the closed­loop system. These
constraints makes the system exhibit tracking accuracy and robustness against matched
disturbances with known bounds. Thus, a suitable choice of the sliding manifold, which
is defined in Rρ−1, can be critical. ρ is the relative degree of the system, defined by the
number of derivatives required of the controlled system state before the system input
appears.
If not in sliding mode, the controller drives the sliding variable towards the manifold de­
pending on the defined reaching law. Therefore, designing a SMC calls for the design
of both a sliding mode control law and a reaching law for achieving sliding mode. As an
example, consider the uncertain system given in (3.1).

control input nullifies the known system dynamics and lets the second term overcome the
uncertainties in order to reach and uphold sliding mode by proper selection of the gain

to higher­order SMC and the previously mentioned sign­approximation in (2.11). To fur­
ther reduce the risk of chattering, the sign function will also approximated for the SMC
implemented in chapter 4. Ultimately, an actual sliding mode controller implementation
can only achieve real sliding mode, where the sliding variable is bounded in some small
neighborhood around zero.

5.3
Discussion
By analyzing the presented results, some interesting conclusions can be drawn. In terms
of non­SCC, only the BFASMC satisfies the desired MAE requirements for all test scenar­
ios. However, this tracking accuracy comes at a cost of having a large spike in actuation
force to overcome the stiction of the dragging disturbance model. For a real life appli­
cation to a UR10e, it might not be feasible in terms of force sensor and joint actuation
capabilities.
Despite the adaptive capabilities of the ASTMC, it fails to mitigate the effects of stiction
fast enough, thereby failing the MAE requirement. For the more forgiving joint disturbance
model, the ASTSMC adapted quite nicely. Also, when tracking some reference with con­
stant derivatives, the ASTSMC gave oscillating control signals, which could be a sign of
either improper tuning or some precursor to the chattering phenomena. In case of the
latter being true, the reader should keep in mind that this happens despite utilizing sign
approximation for the sliding variable.
Another aspect that makes the BFASMC desirable is the fewer parameters to tune per
degree of freedom. Where the ASTSMC has seven parameters, the BFASMC only has
six with the reaching law consisting of two parameters. If one is certain of the external
disturbance being upper or lower bounded by one, then one of the BF exponents, αBF,1
or αBF,2, can be omitted thereby making the BFAB into the simpler BFASP variant. The
PID­controller is even more simple, but the simpleness leads to lackluster results when
facing highly nonlinear unknown disturbances.
For the SCC variants, it was possible to design controller extensions that ensured safety
for the designed scenarios, except for the extra case containing two unsafe sets. Here,
only the BFASMC variant managed to stay clear of both sets. This is not inherently neg­
ative for the ASTSMC as this scenario was designed on purpose to be extreme to see if
the controllers could ”thread the needle” and go inbetween the two sets. The reason for
the auxiliary SCC failing here is possibly the need for a conservative design due to the
disturbance bounds being unknown. As previously mentioned, this results in the tendency
of the controllers to have a big margin between themselves and the unsafe sets. When
forced inbetween the two sets, this margin is inevitably reduced, for which the SCC tries
to mitigate and therefore ends up overcompensating.
Regarding the set MAE requirement of the SCC­extended controller, none of the con­
trollers pass. The closest is the BFAMSC, which barely fails due to the average x1 MAE
being too high. With that said, the placement of the unsafe sets can greatly affect the
tracking performance and as such the MAE. Therefore, the systems have no guarantee
of fulfilling the MAE requirement in any tracking scenario with an arbitrarily placed unsafe
set. Also, the conservative design of the auxiliary SCC can also lead to both the BFASMC
and the ASTSMC having worse tracking performance under the established MAE defini­
tions as was seen in test case 17. The greatest concern regarding the SCC­variants is
Adaptive Sliding Mode Control of Robotic Manipulator

the resulting control signal, which in general are higher than their non­SCC counterparts,
but also tend to reach 500­1000 N at times.
Besides the successful implementation of the SCC­ASMC’s for the UR10e regarding stay­
ing safe, the main takeaway is the seemingly novel hybrid control law for the BFASMC,
which allows the control system to switch between the BF­law and the reaching law. As
stated, this SCC variant was the most successful, however, the current implementation of
the closed loop suffer from spiking in the input signal when switching between the two con­
trol laws. As such, a more refined switching law than the current hysteresis function must
be explored. Furthermore, the stability of the hybrid system seen in the test cases, does
not equate to actual guaranteed stability. Therefore, further exploration of this method
also calls for a stability proof.

6
Conclusion
The main objective of this thesis was to implement different adaptive sliding mode con­
trollers capable of mitigating the effect of unknown, bounded disturbances to ensure a
robust and accurate reference tracking performance for a robot manipulator end­effector.
Furthermore, the controllers were extended with a safety­critical control law to ensure
end­effector pose safety from some predefined set in the state space.
The two implemented ASMC includes a well established adaptive gain super­twisting
sliding mode controller and a new barrier­function adaptive sliding mode controller. The
BFASMC can more easily maintain a real sliding mode state and thereby a robust tracking
performance. This is also reflected by the results with only the BFASMC satisfying the
desired accuracy requirement.
Regarding safety­critical control, both safety­critical extended ASMC maintained safety in
all tested scenarios with the exception of the ASTSMC that fails in the special case that
involves two closely placed unsafe sets. Both of the controllers, however, fail to satisfy
the desired reference tracking accuracy, although the tracking accuracy is affected by
the placement of the unsafe sets in the state space and easiest path to circumvent it.
In order to combine the BFASMC with a SCC, a modification was made to the nominal
controller that transformed the system into a hybrid system. Despite the shortcomings in
terms of accuracy, the implemented BFASMC has shown great potential in both regular
and safety­critical scenarios.
6.1
Future Work
Several aspects of the material presented in this thesis can be expanded upon. The below
list includes the most interesting:
• Real system testing: The work in this thesis only governs simulations of a UR10e,
which only gives an indication of a proper real life implementation. Then, one must
deal with the challenges of sensor and actuation limits, sensor noise and sampling
limits. The latter is especially concerning for the BFASMC.
• Controller tuning: The implemented controllers are not optimally tuned given the
number of controller parameters present in the ASMC’s and the six DOF of the ma­
nipulator end­effector. As such, future work on this project calls for further controller
tuning.
• BFASMC refining and stability proving: The hybrid control system of the safety­
critical extended BFASMC does not have a smooth transition between the two con­
trol laws. Therefore, a more refined set of switching conditions is desired especially
if the system is to be implemented on a real life robot manipulator. Furthermore, the
stability of the hybrid control law has not been proven, which is also needed.

==PAGE:68==
• Improvement of the safety­critical controller extension: With the current imple­
mentation of the SCC extension, a very conservative design is used to compensate
for the lack of knowledge of the system disturbance bounds. While this keeps the
end­effector pose safe, the tracking performance can degrade as a result. There­
fore, another disturbance sensitive SCC could be explored, or one could try using
weights for penalizing the use of a specific state for keeping the system safe.
