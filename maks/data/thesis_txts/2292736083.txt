Abstract
In this project, the microstructural evolution of a centrifugally cast high temperature austenitic
alloy is investigated. It is the so called HPMA alloy which is Nb-Ti-Zr- modiﬁed and is used for
steam reformer tubes. These are tube furnaces for hydrogen production, containing a catalyst
through which steam is ﬂowing under pressures of about 10 bar at temperatures of about 900 ◦C. A
minimum lifetime of 10 years is expected this calls for the HPMA alloy that due to its dendritic
structure with a network of primary carbides is highly creep resistant.
Two as-cast samples and a series of samples from a failed steam reformer furnace tube have
been supplied by Haldor Topsoe, and two of the samples have received further heat treatment in
order to follow the microstructure at diﬀerent conditions. LOM and SEM are used to characterise
the visual changes in the microstructure, EDS is applied to analyse the compositional changes, and
XRD is utilised in order to directly follow the phase transformations happening.
The main focus of this project is on using the experimentally obtained information about the
microstructural changes to evaluate the accuracy and usefulness of a thermodynamical simulation
model that combines the Scheil solidiﬁcation model with equilibrium calculations. Dividing the
microstructure into two regions, the austenitic dendrite matrix and the carbides allow for a reﬁnement
of the results obtained by thermodynamical modelling. Making an equilibrium simulation using
the composition of the austenitic phase as found by the Scheil solidiﬁcation model after 10 %
solidiﬁcation provides a phase diagram that can evaluate the temperature stability regions of the
phases in the matrix. According to the Scheil solidiﬁcation simulation, the carbides start to form
at 50 % solidiﬁcation. An equilibrium calculation using the composition of the liquid at 50 %
solidiﬁcation as found by the Scheil simulation gives a phase diagram that describes the stability of
the primary carbides.
These two phase diagrams describe the microstructural evolution more accurately than a phase
diagram calculated using the base composition. An important result indicating this is the presence
of G-phase in the samples heat treated at 900 ◦C, this is not expected from the phase diagram for
the base composition, but the G-phase is stable at 900 ◦C. According to the phase diagram for the
primary carbide region.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The purpose of this project is to investigate the microstructural evolution in centrifugally cast high
temperature austenitic alloys, more speciﬁcally of the HPMA alloy. It contains about 25 wt% Cr,
35 wt% Ni, 0.5 wt% C and is then alloyed with about 1 % Nb and micro alloyed with Ti and Zr
with Fe as the balance. This alloy is used for tube furnaces in so called steam reformers which
are the primary source of hydrogen for reﬁning and many other process purposes. This alloy is
used due to its superior creep resistance that allows it to operate at approximately 900 ◦C with an
internal pressure of 10 bar for a minimum of 10 years.
Haldor Topsoe produces both the equipment for steam reforming and the catalysts aiding the
reactions in the process. Haldor Topsoe has supplied a number of samples from a steam reformer
furnace tube that has failed during operation. The samples are from diﬀerent regions of the tube
which have experienced diﬀerent temperatures during the lifetime of the tube. Haldor Topsoe has
further given the samples a systematic series of heat treatments. Microstructural characterisation
of some of these samples in combination with equilibrium calculations performed in Thermo-Calc is
the foundation of this project.
The microstructural features of these samples are characterised using Light Optical Microscopy
(LOM) and Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), the local compositions in the visible phases are
evaluated using Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS), and ﬁnally in order to determine
which phases are present, X-ray Diﬀraction (EDS) is utilized. These methods combined allows for an
investigation of the changes happening in the microstructure during exposure to high temperatures
for extended periods of time.
The aim of this project is to determine if a CALPHAD program as Thermo-Calc can help estimate
the temperature history of the HPMA alloy from a steam reformer furnace tube. By making an
equilibrium simulation based on the overall composition of the HPMA alloy, the thermodynamically
stable phases as a function of time can be found. This phase diagram would only describe the
situation in which the sample has reached equilibrium. As the reformer furnace tubes are produced
by centrifugal casing, the material is very far from equilibrium. During centrifugal casting, the melt
is poured into a rotating steel mould, resulting in a very fast cooling and thus a fast solidiﬁcation.
This gives rise to a systematic dendritic microstructure with carbides in between the dendrite
arms. During solidiﬁcation, a signiﬁcant segregation is happening, which means that the element
distribution is far from homogeneous, making the overall equilibrium calculation inadequate for
description of local microstructure evolution.
In this project, a reﬁnement of the Thermo-Calc equilibrium simulation has been attempted
so that it better describes the inhomogeneous microstructure of the centrifugally cast HPMA
reformer furnace tubes. This has been done by employing the Scheil-Gulliver solidiﬁcation model
to investigate in what order the phases form and to describe the segregation happening during
solidiﬁcation. In this way, the compositions of the diﬀerent phases can be followed as a function
of the fraction of solid. This can be used to create a geometric sectioning of the microstructure
into the dendritic matrix and the interdendritic area containing carbides. Making new equilibrium

simulations based on the composition in these two areas as found by the Scheil simulation, allows
for better estimates of the microstructural evolution during heat treatments.
By comparing the results from the microstructural characterisation with the predictions of the

this way of using Thermo-Calc in combination with a microstructural characterisation can help
piece together the history of temperatures that a reformer furnace tube has been exposed to.

2.1
Steam Reforming
The process of steam reforming is widely used for large scale manufacturing of hydrogen and
synthesis gases using catalytic reactions that convert hydrocarbons and steam into hydrogen and
carbon dioxide by the following reactions.

Reaction 2.1 is endothermic so that it requires heat (energy) to proceed, and reaction 2.2 is exothermic.
Using methane as a reactant would give the endothermic reaction CH4 + H2 →CO + 3H2 with an
enthalpy of −∆H0
298 = −206 kJ/mol. Together with reaction (2.2), this would give a total positive
enthalpy resulting in an overall endothermic reaction. To make the speed and eﬃciency feasible, the
reaction takes place on a catalyst. This is where the need of a reformer furnace arises, to contain
the catalyst and supply enough heat for the reaction to continuously proceed. [2]
The reformer furnace consists of columns of furnace tubes containing the catalyst. The heat is
supplied from radiant burners that ﬁre backwards towards the furnace walls so that the tube to some
extend avoids direct ﬁre and instead is heated by thermal radiation. The operating temperature is
typically around 900 ◦C and the pressure of the steam inside the tube is of the order of 10 bar.
These are very rough conditions for the reformer furnace tube; high pressure from the reactants
inside the tube, exposure to high temperatures for extended periods of time combined with the
corrosive eﬀects of the environment inside and outside the tube calls for extreme material properties.
When operating at homologous temperatures above 0.5 for a minimum lifetime of 100,000 hours,
the creep resistance of the alloy is an exceedingly important parameter. The corrosion properties
are also very important as especially the ﬂue gas from the fuel used to ﬁre the burners can have a
deteriorating eﬀects[3]. The aspect of corrosion will not be dealt with in this project though.
A big challenge of steam reforming is obtaining the overall stability of the plant that allows for
continuous operation for several years at a time without breakdowns or instabilities, as start-ups and
shut-downs can have detrimental eﬀects. It is of utmost importance to avoid temperature excursions
as just small increases in temperature can potentially speed up the creep damage signiﬁcantly.
2.2
Centrifugal Casting
The need for high temperature creep resistant steel tubes is especially pronounced in the chemical
and petrochemical industry, where a stable operation over many years is crucial for the eﬃciency.
These demands call for the use of centrifugally cast nickel-chromium austenitic alloys.[4]
Centrifugal casting is in practice performed by pouring the melt into a cylindrical mould spinning
with several hundred rpm, generating centrifugal forces of around a 140 times the gravitational
force. A sketch of the process of centrifugal casting is shown on Figure 2.1.

ﬂuctuations in composition from segment to segment.
2.3
Solidiﬁcation
The solidiﬁcation for a pure metal can be described by the Gibb’s free energy for a cluster of atoms,
a so called nucleus. The Gibb’s free energy for this nucleus consist of a volume and a surface term
depending on the radius cubed and the radius squared, respectively. This means that a critical
stable nucleus radius can be found, above which growth and thus solidiﬁcation starts. This is the
fundamental description of how a liquid can minimize its Gibb’s free energy by forming a solid
phase when the temperature is lowered.
The solidiﬁcation in a single-phase binary alloy can be described by a number of diﬀerent
one-dimensional models, the characteristics of some of them will be summarised in the following.
2.3.1
Lever Rule - Equilibrium Solidiﬁcation
A simple one-dimensional solid-liquid interface where heat is extracted only in one end, as shown in
Figure 2.4(a), is used for the solidiﬁcation description. In an equilibrium solidiﬁcation, the cooling
is taken as inﬁnitely slow which implies perfect solid state diﬀusion. This means that the solid
and liquid phases upon solidiﬁcation will have homogeneous compositions following the solidus and
liquidus lines of the phase diagram.

2.3.3
Cellular and Dendritic Solidiﬁcation
The segregation discussed above results in a varying solute concentration in the liquid ahead of the
solidiﬁcation front, which means that the equilibrium solidiﬁcation temperature can also vary in the
melt. This is the origin of the dendritic growth. Protrusions can grow into the melt if they stay
below the local liquidus temperature, which is changed due to segregation. This phenomenon is
called constitutional supercooling.

2.4
Creep Strength
Creep is deﬁned as the deformation of a material exposed to a static mechanical stress at elevated
temperatures. Creep begins to be a concern at a homologous temperature of about 0.4 (0.4Tm,
Tm = absolute melting temperature). The material properties with most inﬂuence on the creep
characteristics are the melting temperature, the elastic modulus, and the grain size. The higher
the melting temperature and elastic modulus, the better the creep performance. Large grains are
generally considered an advantage because less grain sliding can happen compared to a ﬁne grained
structure. Solid-solution alloying and precipitate phases enhance the creep strength[9].
The creep phenomenon is typically divided into the three stages, relevant at successive periods
of time, primary creep, secondary creep, and creep fracture. During primary creep new dislocations
are generated from the stress, and to some extent low-energy networks of sub-boundaries can also
form. In austenitic steels, the sub-boundary formation is very slow compared to ferritic steels
which can be ascribed to their low stacking fault energy. The secondary creep is characterised by a
minimum creep rate, so at this stage the least deformation should be observed. During the creep,
fracture cracks begin to nucleate, grow, and eventually this leads to ﬁnal fracture[10].
2.5
HP Alloys
The HP alloys are very speciﬁc and complex alloys within the huge family of high temperature
austenitic steels. There are several versions of the HP alloy modiﬁed for slightly diﬀerent purposes.
The base composition of the HP alloys is 25 wt% Cr, 35 wt% Ni, 0.5 wt% C, with Fe as the balance.
In this alloy, the strengthening and creep resisting properties come from M7C3 and M23C6 in the
form of primary eutectic carbides located in between the dendrites and secondary carbides dispersed
within the dendritic matrix. The demand for alloys able to withstand even higher temperatures
lead to the development of the HP modiﬁed alloy which had about the same composition as the
general HP alloys, but with the addition of about 1 wt% Nb. This addition introduces the MC type
carbide in the microstructure.
A further development of the HP alloy was the creation of the HP-micro-alloyed (HPMA)
material, again using the same base composition of micro additions of certain alloying elements
such as Ti, Zr, Ta or Tungsten. These elements are all strong nitride and carbide formers and can
thus produce carbides stable at higher temperatures[12]. This is the kind of alloy considered in this
project. Even more exotic variations also exist, using rare earth elements as modiﬁers, but these
will not be discussed in this work.
The macro/microstructure of the centrifugally cast HK and HP alloys has a characteristic
dendritic structure. The dendrites initiate at the outer surface of a tube, that is from the side
that solidiﬁes ﬁrst, and then grow inwards. This results in segregation of certain elements in the
liquid in front of the dendrites, in between the dendrites and between the secondary dendrite arms,
which allows for the network of primary carbides to be formed. In Figure 2.5(a) an example of the
typical microstructure of a Nb-modiﬁed HP40 alloy acquired by LOM by L.H. de Almeida et al.[1]
is shown, where the dendritic structure is clearly seen. The black areas are the primary carbides
formed in the areas between the dendrites during solidiﬁcation. The brighter areas are the dendrites
constituting the austenitic matrix.
On Figure 2.5(b), also from the work of L.H. de Almeida et al., a close-up of the microstructure
from Figure 2.5(a) is shown. It illustrates that the carbides consist of two types, one that appears
black and one that appears grey on the micrograph, together forming the network of primary
carbides that supplies the high temperature creep strength. High temperature exposure over long
periods of time tends to change the morphology of the carbides, often resulting in the coarsening of
the strengthening particles[11], thus minimizing the surface energy. The creep strength is therefore
partly determined by the stability of the carbides.

2.5.1
Relevant Phases
Several more or less exotic phases can be stable in the high temperature cast alloy considered in
this project. The most abundant phase is of cause the austenite consisting of mainly Fe, Ni, and
Cr in a composition close to the base composition of the HP alloy, but with a low concentration
of the alloying elements. Diﬀerent types of carbides are present in the microstructure of the high
temperature austenitic steels discussed in the previous sections. The types present depend on the
alloying elements, solidiﬁcation rate and heat treatment. In the following sections, the diﬀerent
types will be described.
2.5.1.1
Carbides
MX Carbides and Nitrides
can precipitate when strong carbide/nitride formers, such as Ti,
Nb, V, Zr, Ta, etc., are present in the alloy. During solidiﬁcation, they precipitate at the grain
boundaries, as shown on Figure 2.5(a), where Almeida has identiﬁed them to be NbC. But during
service, more MX carbides can also precipitate on dislocations within the austenitic matrix. In some
applications of austenitic stainless steels, the carbide/nitride formers are added to avoid Cr-carbides
forming at the grain boundaries as this can lead to a chromium depletion, potentially resulting in
intergranular corrosion. For the HPMA alloy used for steam reformer furnaces the purpose of the
carbide/nitride formers are to provide creep resistance via the secondary carbides formed during
operation. The MX carbides have a NaCl FCC structure. The stoichiometry in the MX structure
can vary quite a lot, but in this project variations of Nb carbides will be of the biggest interest[13].
M7C3
is the carbide forming during solidiﬁcation of the HPMA alloy. It has a higher stability
temperature than the M23C6.
M23C6
is primarily Cr23C6 but Ni, Mo and Fe can sometimes substitute some of the Cr atoms.
M23C6 is more stable than M7C3 and also nucleates very easily, thus it is often found during
precipitation. For the HPMA alloy, it is seen as the secondary carbides found in the matrix after
heat treatment, these would often have a plate like structure. M23C6 also precipitate on grain
boundaries, where it forms after heat treatments. The stoichiometry can vary depending on the
composition of the alloy[13].

Z-phase
is a carbonitride which can form when Nb is added to the alloy and N is present in
a relatively high amount. It has a tetragonal unit cell, consisting of Cr, Nb, and N with the
formula Cr2Nb2N2. As opposed to MX particles, it dissolves only small amounts of substitutional
elements[13].
G-phase
is a silicide that can form in austenitic stainless steels stabilised with Ti or Nb. The
stoichiometry of the G-phase is given by Ni16Nb6Si7Laigo2005.
σ-phase
can lead to embrittlement if precipitated on grain boundaries, but is considered harmless
if precipitated intragranularly.
2.6
Thermo-Calc Simulation of Phase Stability in the HPMA
Alloy
Thermo-Calc is a program developed for calculations of the stability of phases. It uses the CALPHAD
(CALculation of PHAse Diagrams) method which allows for multicomponent multi-phase behaviour
in complex systems. The program calculates the Gibbs free energy of the participating phases, and by
minimizing the calculated energies, the most stable phases can be found. The calculations buildson
an extensive database of material properties, experimentally determined data, and theoretical
information on phase equilibria[14][15].
The Scheil module in Thermo-Calc uses the Scheil-Gulliver equations mention in Section 2.3.2,
but can handle several elements. The requirement of linear solidus and liquidus lines in the phase
diagram is relaxed, as non-linear lines are more easily handled numerically.
The TCFE7.0 database seems to be the most obvious choice for phase stability simulations of
the HPMA alloy, but some of the element concentrations are not within the database speciﬁcations.
From the TCFE7 datasheet shown in Appendix A it can be seen that the database expects a
minimum of 50 wt% Fe and a minimum of 20 wt% Ni, which is not the case for the HPMA alloy,
but the rest of the elements are within the range of the database.

Chapter 3
Experimental Procedure
3.1
Light Optical Microscopy
The many samples from the reformer furnace tube received from Haldor Topsoe were already
embedded and polished. They had also been etched for Light Optical Microscopy (LOM), which
was done using electrolytic etching with oxalic acid at 3.5 V for approximately 10-20 seconds. This
etching makes the microstructural feature of the alloy clearly visible.
The light optical microscope used for the microstructural characterisation is a Olympus GX41
brightﬁeld microscope with Olympus Mpln objectices and a Leica DFC 450C camera attached. This
microscope allows for magiﬁcations of up to 500 X.
3.2
Scanning Electron Microscopy - Energy Dispersive X-ray
Spectroscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) has also been applied to reveal the microstructural features
of the samples. Two microscopes have been used, a JOEL JSM-5900 and a FEI Inspect. The JOEL

Both instruments have an Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectrometer (EDS) from Oxford Instruments
with a 50 mm2 X-Max silicon drift detector attached for compositional analysis. This feature is of
particular interest in this project so the samples are prepared for EDS. This preparation is not much
diﬀerent from the preparation for taking pictures with the SEM, the only thing done diﬀerently is
that the samples are polished again to remove the etched surface. The grinding and polishing steps
steps are shown in Table 3.1 The etching removes some material from the surface, especially at
the grain boundaries, so to get the most representative analysis using the EDS, the samples are
not etched. They are still carbon coated in order to remove electrons from the surface to avoid
charging. In this way, the actual surface composition can be revealed. It might also change the
images produced from the backscattered electrons since these obtain their contrast from diﬀerences
in atomic mass versus the secondary electrons that reveals the morphology.
The acceleration voltage used is stated on the individual images, but generally 20 kV has been
used. For the data acquisition of the EDS measurement, both the INCA and AZTEC software are
used.

4.2
Microstructural Characterisation
4.2.1
HPMA, As-cast
In order to obtain an understanding of the high temperature creep resistance of the HPMA alloy, the
microstructure of the as-cast alloy needs to be characterized. The HPMA alloy considered in this
report is a centrifugally cast tube, a production technique which of course aﬀects the microstructure.
In the process of centrifugal casting, the melt solidiﬁes from the side in contact with the mould, that
is the outside. Looking at sample 20100033-1, this can be seen from the characteristic uniformly
oriented dendritic structure, shown in Figures 4.2(a) and 4.2(d) of the outer and central part of
the tube. The dendrites are primarily growing from the outside (top of micrograph) and inwards.
The inner part of the tube, shown in Figure 4.2(g), also has the dendritic structure, but with a less
obvious directionality. The largest temperature gradient is found in the material in contact with
the mould which means that the secondary dendrite arm spacing should be smallest in this region.
This is conﬁrmed from the micrographs shown in Figure 4.2. Figures 4.2(b), 4.2(e) and 4.2(h) show
the same areas in larger magniﬁcation, revealing two types of particles in between the dendritic
matrix. These are the carbides described in the literature review in Section 2.5.1.1. One of them
appears light grey and the other dark grey. The light grey carbides seem to form in an irregular
laminar structure, indicating that a pool of melt might have been sealed oﬀso that the overall
melt can not act as an element exchange reservoir, resulting in the phase changing from carbide to
austenite several times within such a short distance. It appears that the two types of carbides do
not mix as they are clearly separated. It is also noted that the matrix is very homogeneous with no
precipitates in it, which is expected when the samples have not undergone heat treatment.
As mentioned before, Figures 4.2(b), 4.2(e) and 4.2(h) show micrographs of sample 2010033-1 at
the outer part, the centre, and the inner part, respectively and Figures 4.2(c), 4.2(f) and 4.2(i) show
the second as-cast sample (2009043) at the outer part, the centre and the inner part respectively.
The microstructure of the two as-cast samples is very similar. Two diﬀerent carbides are seen in
between the dendrites, the light grey carbides again seem to form a kind of laminar structure, but
more coarse than for sample 2010033-1.
4.2.1.1
SEM-EDS
Figure 4.3 shows a comparison of the microstructure of sample 2009043 as seen by light optical
microscopy and scanning electron microscopy. To be able to ﬁnd the same area in LOM and SEM,
the pictures are taken close to a square indent made in the sample. Figures 4.3(a) and 4.3(c)
are obtained by LOM, and Figures 4.3(b) and 4.3(d) are obtained by SEM with the backscatter
detector. As described earlier, the light optical micrographs have revealed two types of carbides in
the microstructure, one that appears white and one dark grey. In Figures 4.3(c) and 4.3(d), the
exact same carbides can be seen so that the white carbides appear dark grey or even black in SEM,
and the dark grey carbides can be identiﬁed as white in the SEM. This suggests that the dark grey
carbides, as seen with the LOM, contain the heaviest elements.
In Figure 4.4, the microstructure of the as-cast sample 2010033-1 is shown. The same character-
istics as for sample 2009043 (Figure 4.3(d)) are observed, that is two types of interdendritic carbides,
appearing black and white, and a completely homogeneous matrix with no visible secondary carbides.
The black carbides have a laminar structure whereas the white carbides are solid.
Using the EDS detector on the SEM, local compositions can be estimated. In Figure 4.3(d),
some points and an area, which have been examined with EDS have been marked. The composition
results can be seen in Table 4.3. Measurement 1 and 2 are done in the black carbides, with the
results showing a very high concentration of Cr, suggesting that these could be of the type M7C3 or
M23C6. The results for point 3 and 4 in the white carbides show a very high concentration of Nb,
suggesting Nb carbide perhaps of the type MX. A measurement has also been performed on a larger
area, marked 5 in Table 4.3, which shows a composition quite close to the average composition.

4.2.2
HPMA, Heat Treated
Some of the samples from the reformer furnace exposed to operation for eight years will be
characterised in this section. Four samples have been chosen, two samples from the hot end of
the reformer furnace exposed to approximately 844 ◦C and two from the colder end, exposed to
approximately 622 ◦C (the calculated temperature curve supplied by Haldor Topsoe is shown in
Appendix B). One sample from each section of the tube has then received a heat treatment at 900
◦C for 30 minutes and an additional 5 weeks. Haldor Topsoe, who have performed all the heat
treatments, have made samples exposed to only 30 minutes to examine short term heat excursions,
but this report will not be dealing with these. So when mentioning the heat treatments in this
section, the 5 weeks plus 30 minutes will be called just 5 weeks. The history of the samples is
summarised in Table 4.1.
In Figure 4.6, 4 micrographs taken with the backscatter detector on the SEM of the heat treated
samples are shown. The dendritic microstructure of the centrifugally cast HPMA alloy shown in
Section 4.2.1 is clearly seen on the heat treated samples as well. The micrographs show the dendrite
arms surrounded by two types of interdendritic carbides, as was the case for the as-cast samples.
But on the heat treated samples, the matrix is not homogeneous as it was for the as-cast samples.
Here some kind of secondary carbides has formed as is expected from the literature. So all in all
the microstructure only changes locally.
In Figure 4.7 is shown a close-up of the four samples, where the secondary carbides in the
dendrites can be more clearly seen. Samples 2010026-1 and 2010026-18 have a signiﬁcantly smaller
amount of the secondary carbides than samples 2010032-1 and 2010032-18, so the samples which
have experienced the highest temperature during operation have the most secondary carbides. The
secondary carbides are also markedly bigger for samples 2010032-1 and 2010032-18, creating a
needle-like structure. This means that the secondary carbides must be forming as interconnected
plates.
The micrographs in Figure 4.7 also reveal that the white primary carbides are undergoing a
transformation during heating. In Figure 4.7(a), the white carbides have become light grey with a
spongy look, suggesting a partial solid state transformation. The same is seen in Figure 4.7(b) from
the same section of the tube, but with the heat treatment at 900 ◦C. On the part of the tube exposed
to a higher temperature during operation, shown in Figure 4.7(c) and Figure 4.7(d), the white
carbides are behaving diﬀerently, in that they have not become spongy. But still a transformation
is taking place from the very white phase, still visible in the centre of some of the particles, to the
light grey particles now dominating.
The black primary carbides seem to remain homogeneous. The literature suggests that a phase
transformation would also have happened for the black particles. This will be examined using X-ray
Diﬀraction in the following section.
The microstructural description of the four samples from the scanning electron micrographs
is conﬁrmed by the light optical micrographs shown in Figure 4.8. For sample 2010026-1 and
2010026-18 from the least hot part of the tube shown on Figure 4.8(a) and Figure 4.8(b), the
same small secondary carbides are seen in the matrix. A lot more secondary carbides are actually
visible in the LOM, this is most probably due to the etching of the surface which mainly attacks
grain boundaries, enhancing the contrast produces from the small particles. For sample 2010032-1
and 2010032-18 shown in Figure 4.8(c) and Figure 4.8(d), the needle-like structure is seen again,
especially for sample 2010032-18.
It also seems that there is a region around the primary carbides on sample 2010026-1 and
2010026-18 with no secondary carbides, and that the concentration of secondary carbides increases
towards the middle of the dendrite. For sample 2010032-1 in Figure 4.8(c), the opposite trend is
seen, as it seems that the secondary carbide concentration is increasing towards the primary carbides.
For sample 2010032-18 in Figure 4.8(d), the secondary carbide distribution is quite homogeneous in
the dendritic matrix, except close to the white primary carbides, where a clean zone is visible. The
white particles in LOM are Cr carbides.

ThermoCalc
Using a CALPHAD program allows for calculations of phase stability in a given alloy. Assuming a
homogeneous distribution of the elements, Thermo-Calc can calculate which phases will be stable
at a given temperature. This can in relation to this project improve the understanding of the
microstructural evolution of the HPMA alloy at high temperatures by predicting which phases, that
are not necessarily formed during solidiﬁcation, can form because of a higher stability. Thermo-Calc
is also used as a tool to model the solidiﬁcation sequence and thus obtain an understanding of the
geometric distribution of the phases in the as-cast material.

4.3.2
Scheil Solidiﬁcation Model
Because of solidiﬁcation phenomena happening during casting, an overall equilibrium calculation
cannot predict much about the ﬁnal microstructure. The dendritic solidiﬁcation happening during
centrifugal casting results in composition gradients throughout the casting. These gradients will in
the following be estimated by a Scheil solidiﬁcation simulation. The segregation of certain elements
allows for phases to be formed, that would not be stable in a homogeneous system, but can be in a
local equilibrium due to the concentration gradients. The most obvious eﬀect of this is the carbides
forming in between the dendrites.
In order to get an understanding of the solidiﬁcation process during casting of the HPMA alloy,
a Scheil solidiﬁcation simulation has been performed in Thermo-Calc. The Scheil-Gulliver model is
based on the assumptions that the diﬀusion is inﬁnitely fast in the melt and that no diﬀusion is
allowed in the solid. At each step of the solidiﬁcation process, a local equilibrium is calculated for
a volume at the solidiﬁcation front. The system’s phase diagram determines which solid phase is
forming and thus which composition the solidiﬁed volume has. As no diﬀusion is occurring in the
solid phase, the composition is retained, which means that the composition of the remaining liquid
is also changed[17].
Interstitial elements such as C, N, O, and S have substantial diﬀusion coeﬃcients in the solid
phases at elevated temperatures. This is ignored in the classical Scheil solidiﬁcation model. But in
the numerical SCHEIL Module in Termo-Calc, some fast diﬀusion elements can be deﬁned. This
feature is quite essential since carbides and the properties they bring along aare the main interest
of the HPMA alloy.

attributed to the fundamentals of the Scheil solidiﬁcation model, that it is a stepwise equilibrium
calculation, and the centrifugal casting process is far from an equilibrium solidiﬁcation. It is also a
problem that the model assumes perfect mixing in the liquid. This is quite accurate throughout
most of the solidiﬁcation, but not towards the end where the segregation becomes extreme. This
is seen from the fact that even G-phase supposedly will form in the last few percent solidiﬁcation
according to the Scheil solidiﬁcation model. However this is not true.

Discussion
5.1
Solid State Transformation to G-Phase
As mentioned in the description of the SEM pictures in Section 4.2 the white phase visible in the
two as-cast samples becomes grey. In the case of sample 2010026-1 and 2010026-18, the phase
becomes spongy, in sample 2010032-1 it becomes uniformly grey, and in 201032-18 some of the grey
particles have a white core. Most of the EDS point scans in the grey particles in the four heat
treated samples have a higher Si content than the scans in the white particles in the two as-cast
samples. Looking in Table 4.4, point 2b, 3b, and 3d, have Si contents of 22.0, 22.5, and 22.5 at%,
respectively. These values are very close to the Si content of the G-phase (Ni16Nb6Si7), which has
24.1 at% Si. If assuming that the three phases are G-phase then it would make sense to ignore
the response from other elements. Normalising the remaining three concentrations to 100 gives
the values shown in Table 5.1. These three measured values are very close to the stoichiometric
composition of the G-phase. The interaction volume of the electrons while doing EDS is of the
order of 1 µm, so the other elements present according to the EDS measurement could be from the
surrounding phases. This could justify ignoring them.

The presence of G-phase proves that the solid state phase transformation from Nb carbide
to G-phase has had time to happen. The two samples in which the G-phase is conﬁrmed are

G-phase to have formed. So that an EDS point scan would only receive response from G-phase, not
from the surrounding phases. Sample 2010026-1 has been exposed to a much lower temperature for
the eight years, this could have resulted in a partial transformation from Nb carbide to G-phase,
because the diﬀusion is slow and will only happen over shorter distances. This could explain the

5.2
Secondary Precipitates
At all four heat-treated samples considerable amounts of secondary carbides have formed. In
Appendix D, a series of EDS point measurements, from which it is apparent that the interaction
volume of the electron beam is too big to only get a response from a single secondary carbide are
shown. The measurements of the points placed in the secondary carbides are most probably a
combination of matrix and carbide. What can be concluded though is that the visible carbides have
a signiﬁcantly higher Cr content than the surrounding matrix, and also a higher Si content.
The Thermo-Calc equilibrium calculations performed all show that M7C3 is not stable at the
temperatures that the samples have been exposed to. This is in agreement with the experimental
characterisation. The XRD measurement of the heat treated sample 2010032-18 concludes that
there is no M7C3 whereas M23C6 is deﬁnitely present. The equilibrium calculation for the dendritic
matrix predicts that only the M23C6 is stable in signiﬁcant amounts, except below 740 ◦C where
the SIGMA phase is also stable.
The secondary carbides are harder to see in the SEM than they are in LOM, this is clearly seen
when comparing ﬁgure 4.7 and ﬁgure 4.8. The explanation to this is that the SEM scans through
particles, so that the formed image is blurred by the surrounding matrix whereas the etching of the
surface attacks grain boundaries which makes contours that gives contrast in LOM.
Considering the phase diagram for the matrix region in Figure 4.15(a) shows that there is a
temperature interval from around 1170 ◦C to 1320 ◦C in which only the austenite is stable. This
suggests that the secondary precipitates will dissolve if the material is kept at this temperature.
This would greatly deteriorate the creep strength, as the secondary carbides play an important role
in the hindering dislocation movement.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Representative samples from the batch of samples supplied by Haldor Topsoe have been chosen for
investigation and for comparison with results from thermodynamical modelling software.
The dendritic structure, documented in the literature, of the HPMA alloy has been found from
visual inspection of the samples using LOM and SEM. The orientation of the dendritic structure is
found to be in the radial direction as is expected from the process of centrifugal casting. Two types
of primary carbides are observed in between the dendrite arms. For the as-cast samples, they appear
black and white by the backscatter detector on the SEM. This visualises the signiﬁcant segregation
happening during the fast cooling that allows for the big primary carbides to form. The white
and black carbides in the as-cast samples are identiﬁed as Nb carbides and Cr7C3, respectively,
by means of XRD and EDS compositional analysis. The dendritic matrix structure is completely
homogeneous for the as-cast samples.
For the four heat treated samples, a solid state phase transformation from Cr7C3 to Cr23C6
has been conﬁrmed using XRD. This transformation is also known in the literature and is a
direct consequence of the fact that Cr23C6 is more thermodynamically stable than Cr7C3 at the
temperatures that the samples are exposed to. The white Nb carbides have also been observed to
undergo a transformation, but the change in appearance is diﬀerent from sample to sample. For
the sample exposed to 622 ◦C during operation, the former white Nb carbide has become grey and
spongy with a lower Nb content. For the sample from the same section of the tube that in addition
to operation has been heat treated at 900 ◦C for 5 weeks, the grey spongy structure is still present,
but slightly more uniform grey areas are seen. For the sample exposed to 844 ◦C during operation
the former Nb carbides have become grey, but still uniform. In the sample from the same section
of the tube that has also been given a heat treatment at 900 ◦C for 5 weeks, the uniform grey
structure is still seen. EDS measurements on both samples that have been heat treated at 900 ◦C
show compositions indicating presence of G-phase. An EDS mapping of one of the spongy grey
particles has been used to analyse the transport of Si into the G-phase, showing how the Si has
travelled from the matrix into the outer edge of the grey particle.
Secondary precipitates are observed in the dendritic matrix for all the heat-treated samples
considered. For the samples exposed to about 622 ◦C during operation, the secondary carbides are
seen as small dots, whereas for the sample exposed to 844 ◦C, the carbides assumes the shape of
plates.
The proposed Thermo-Calc model based on the idea of using a Scheil solidiﬁcation simulation
to divide the microstructure into two characteristic sections, of which equilibrium simulations can
be performed, has proven useful for describing the microstructural evolution of the dendrites and
the primary carbides seperately. The main contribution from this model is the reﬁnement of the
temperature stability ranges that it oﬀers. This allows for better estimates of which phases can form
during exposure to elevated temperatures for extended periods of time. The potential application
of estimating the temperature history of a samples is of even greater interest. Observing a phase
and its location in the microstructure gives information about the temperature that the sample has

been exposed to, because the stability area is known from one of the reﬁned phase diagrams.
A direct conﬁrmation of the proposed model is the formation of G-phase during heat treatment
at 900 ◦C, from the phase diagram calculated using the base composition, G-phase should not be
stable at this temperature. Whereas the phase diagram calculated for the carbide region predicts
the stability of G-phase.

Chapter 7
Future Works
This project has been dealing with an exceedingly complex alloy, thus a rather speciﬁc approach
has been followed in this work, as a complete understanding of the alloy is way beyond the scope of
a Master thesis.
It would be of utmost interest for companies employing the HPMA alloy, like Haldor Topsoe, to
be able to characterise their products and then determine which conditions it has been exposed to.
For instance to determine if a reformer furnace tube has undergone any temperature excursions
that could have stabilised detrimental phases. So if a further improved thermodynamic model could
be developed being able to simulate the stability ranges and maybe even amounts of phases formed
it would be of great value .
One step towards this goal could be to try and implement a Moving boundary model to describe
the diﬀusion between diﬀerent pairs of phases present in the microstructure. This model could be
implemented in Dictra which is an extension to Thermo-Calc. It can also be used to simulate the
solidiﬁcation like the Scheil equation, but taking the cooling rate into account.
