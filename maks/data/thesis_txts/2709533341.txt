Abstract
In the current study, the examination of the thermal performance of solar collector fields
for different climatic conditions takes place after the validation of TRNSYS model with
reference point the Dronninglund Fjernvarmethe Solar Thermal Plant.
Dronninglund Fjernvarme is a solar district heating plant that utilizes 37,573 m2 of solar
panels, gas engines and biomass boilers and with combination of a pit heat storage for
the yearly production of hot water for the nearby city of Dronninglund. Using the TRNSYS
model, the solar field is first simulated in order to validate the thermal performance of the
solar field with the measured one. Next, the TRNSYS modeled solar field of Dronninglund
Fjernvarme is used to calculate the thermal performance in Greece and Romania. For
the realistic validation of the model as well as for the simulations in different conditions,
actual input data such as local weather data, solar radiation data (diffuse, direct and global
irradiance) and the technical specifications of the collectors are utilized.
The results show that there is a deviation between the measured and simulated data.
Possible errors during the simulation are discussed as well as recommendations in order
to improve the TRNSYS model for the design optimization of solar heating plants are
given.

Introduction
Since the age of industrialization, the need for energy to power houses and factories, as
well as space heating and cooling and domestic hot water has increased exponentially,
and thus the consumption of fossil fuels to meet those needs. Consequently, many issues
have arisen that are severely affecting daily life [1]. To tackle the rising problem of climate
change a lot of countries worldwide have started to integrate renewable ways into their
energy system in order to cut off fossil fuels. Wind and solar are the two main sources that
are used to produce clean energy. As for sunlight, it can be used as a means of generating
electricity through photovoltaics, but also hot water through solar panels. Solar panels are
mainly used in solar thermal plants in fields for producing hot water for the district heating
system [2].
District heating (DH) is a system for distributing heat produced in a central location to
meet various heating requirements, usually space heating and water heating. Heat can
be generated by burning fossil fuels; however, the amount of biomass used has increased
to reduce cost and decrease CO2 emissions. Therefore, these central district heating units
are optimized by the application of heat pumps, boilers, geothermal heating, and larger
storage spaces, such as seasonal storage [3].
For Solar district heating (SDH), solar panels are used to absorb solar radiation and heat
the fluid inside them, which is then transferred to the heat exchanger to heat up the water
that will be needed in the DH grid [4]. This process can occur either with the hot water be­
ing supplied directly to the DH system or indirectly where it can be stored in a large thermal
pit to be used based on the seasonal needs of the area. In recent years and with great
success, solar collectors have been introduced in the DH systems in Denmark, Germany,
Austria, Sweden, and other countries around the globe to reduce the CO2 emissions from
fossils fuels and biomass [5].
Solar collector fields are commonly used in centralized SDH systems and are installed
close to the main DH plant, hosting the auxiliary energy system. They are added to the
existing DH system to minimize the usage of conventional fuels (such as gas) in the main
plant. Europe’s market is dominated by the ground­mounted flat plate collectors (FPC) for
centralized SDH systems since they cover more than 90% of the total area [6]. Usually,
the solar field is installed in parallel with the auxiliary energy system, and in case of high
solar radiation, the field mainly provides the heat required by the DH network. On the other
hand, when there is no sufficient heat from the solar thermal (ST) system, the auxiliary
system supplies the extra energy needed to increase the temperature for the DH network.
The plant is also equipped with storage, which stores heat from the solar collectors and
the auxiliary system, and has a key role regarding the solar fraction that the system can
achieve [7].
The scope of this thesis is, by using as a reference the solar thermal plant at Dronninglund
Fjernvarme in Denmark, to study and analyze the measured and simulated thermal per­
formance of the solar collectors using TRNSYS model, as well as to estimate the thermal
performance of a solar collector field in different climatic conditions.
Before the simulations, the fundamental theory of solar collectors at Dronninglund Fjern­
varme will be presented, as well as the mathematical background on which the data were
calculated and how the model was created at TRNSYS.

Dronninglund solar thermal plant
2.1
General information about Dronninglund Fjernvarme
Located at Lunderbjerg, west of a small city called Dronninglund which is in the north part
of Jutland is one of the largest solar thermal plants. Dronninglund Fjernvarme (District
Heating) was a natural gas­driven thermal plant built in 1989 and was a consumer­owned
cooperative. After its commission, it became the first Danish district heating company to
install natural gas­driven engines to produce heat and power simultaneously [8]. However,
as climate change is a major problem to be addressed, the general assembly and the
board of Dronninglund Fjernvarme decided in 2005 that over time they would have to
replace the natural gas with a more sustainable source of energy [8].
Several Danish companies specialized in district heating had moved quickly and had al­
ready installed solar thermal plants that could cover 20% of the annual heat production.
In this case, however, Dronninglund Fjernvarme wanted to go a step further in the inte­
gration of solar energy in the system. The initial plan was to achieve 50% of coverage
in the yearly heat production via solar heat [8]. So, in 2007, Nordjyllands Vækstforum, a
forum for regional development in northern Jutland, subsidized a feasibility study to find
how the Dronninglund Fjernvarme could achieve its goal. The study showed that the so­
lar thermal plants that use seasonal storage could achieve the coverage of 50% of the
heat consumption through solar energy. In addition, the cost of heat production will not
increase, due to the subsidized investment. The study was approved, the subsidy was
granted, marking the beginning of a detailed design for long­term storage, piping, heat
exchangers, and the connection of the control system with the production units.
Even though Dronninglund Fjernvarme was originally built in 1989 to produce heat and
power using natural gas, in May 2014 became the largest solar thermal plant in Denmark
and the 3rd worldwide after the installation of solar panels. In 2006, the board decided to
replace two of the oil boilers, and that was one of the preliminary reasons for introducing
solar heating in the Dronninglund Fjernvarme [8]. The other reason was the gradual re­
placement of fossil fuels with alternative energy sources by 50% in the short term. In the
long term, the Danish government’s national goal was to use up to 100% of sustainable
sources for the production of heat by 2035, as the European goal is to produce energy
entirely from renewable sources.
In the plant, 4 gas engines of 3.6 MW power and 6.4 MW heat are operating. An emer­
gency gas boiler of 8 MW of heat and a bio­oil boiler with an associated absorption heat
pump of total output of 6 MW are also operating in the plant. Furthermore, one bio­oil hot
water boiler with an output of 10 MW, as well as solar panels of a total area of 37,573 m2
with heating production that could reach approximately 27 MW combined with a 62,000
m3 seasonal heat storage are included [8].

2.2.1
New thermal plant
The main components in the new production plant are a large solar thermal field and a pit
heat water storage connected to it. During summer, the solar thermal unit generates quite
more heat than the Dronninglund spends [8]. So, in the summer months, the surplus is
used to heat the pit thermal storage. In autumn, the water inside the storage pit is cooled
by adding water with a district heating return temperature (around 40°C) to the bottom of
the pit storage, and hot water is transferred from the top of the storage to the city. For
further utilization of the storage, the return temperature is cooled from 40°C to 10°C in
order to be used as a heat source for a heat pump. This will lead to the raise of the
storage capacity before next summer, reduction of the heat losses, and increase of the
solar panels’ energy production [8].
2.2.2
Solar thermal plant
Solar collectors transform solar radiation into heat. This heat can be used for space
heating or heating water in facility services. The main component of a solar collector is its
absorber, which is absorbing heat, to send it from the absorbers to the desired point. This
is achieved by a heat­transporting pipe filled with liquid that is connected to the absorber.
The liquid is a mixture of water and propylene glycol, with 35% of propylene glycol and
the rest water, to prevent freezing during colder months [9].
Solar collector’s efficiency is based on the capacity of the heat it can absorb in relation to
how much of that heat can be lost once it is absorbed. Figure 2.4 illustrates the principles
of energy flows in a solar collector [9].

2.2.3
Heat Storage Pit
Solar systems generally have the disadvantage that they produce the most heat when
the sun has the highest radiation, which is the time when the need for heat is at its lowest
possible level [11]. To compensate for this problem, large underground storage facilities
are installed to store thermal energy in hot water, which can be used from time to time for
greater heating needs. In addition, storage spaces can be combined with other energy
technologies, such as heat pumps and biomass units, to obtain a district heating system
with a higher share of renewable energy [12].
The available methods for the seasonal storage of solar thermal energy are mainly based
on sensible heat. Storage in the form of sensible heat is stored in a system where the
temperature varies internally in the system [13]. Owing to temperature fluctuations into
the system, storage heat losses occur over time. Losses are a function of storage time,
storage and ambient temperatures, storage volume, storage geometry, and thermal prop­
erties of the storage medium used, such as water.
The above described type of storage is called Underground thermal storage (UTES). The
Figure 2.7 [14] shows the main drawings of the 4 most promising warehouses. These are
aquifer thermal energy storage, drilling thermal energy storage, water tank storage, and
gravel water storage. To make storage as feasible as possible, the large volumes required
for storage must be cheap. Therefore, seasonal storage is placed in the ground [13].
Figure 2.7: The 4 types of underground thermal storage
In Dronninglund Fjernvarme, the Water­gravel Pit Storage is used. This method is nor­
mally fully buried in the ground and is insulated and waterproofed in the walls and the top
lid. Heat is stored and discharged out of the storage. The volume of the pit must be larger
compared to a normal heat storage tank with only water since the water­gravel mixture
has a lower specific heat capacity than just water [13].

The Water­gravel Pit Storage used in Dronninglund contains 60,000m3 water. Construc­
tion of the pit lasted 10 years(March 2003 ­ November 2013). The inlet and outlet pipes
are placed at the bottom, which is at the same level as the technical building. The Water­
gravel Pit is waterproofed with a 2.5 mm welded polyethylene liner. All the seams of the
waterproofing are checked after the completion. The guarantee of the polyethylene layer
is 20 years of lifetime if the temperature do not exceed 90°C. After the implementation of
the liner, the filling of the storage could begin. The lid is constructed by gluing a 2 mm
polyethylene coating on ground and gradually pulling it over the water. The insulation of
the lid is placed on top of the liner. Also, weight pipes (plastic pipes with concrete) are
placed inside and on top of the lid to guide and collect rainwater.
The top of the lid is a roof foil with vacuum vents removing moisture coming from the
storage through the polyethylene liner. Furthermore, ventilated roof sheets are placed
on top of the lid to remove the moisture that accumulates between the storage and the
lining [8].
2.2.4
Technical Building
The technical area includes heat exchangers and pumps. If in the winter, the solar panels
can produce water with temperatures above the temperature in the bottom area of storage,
the solar panel’s pump starts, and the storage heats up. In the summer, the solar pumps’
flow is adjusted to secure that the production temperature exceeds the district heating
flow temperature (almost 75°C) [8]. When the production is greater than the consumption
in the city, the storage is heated by adding hot water to the top of the hot water pit and
discharging cold water from the bottom.
Besides solar collectors, there is a bio­oil boiler at Søndervang in Dronninglund that sup­
plies heat in an absorption heat pump. The bio­oil boiler heats up the water to 160°C and
then the hot water operates the absorption heat pump. The heat pump utilizes the water
of the storage pit to produce district heating at flow temperature [8].

Validation of TRNSYS model for
Dronninglund thermal plant
TRNSYS is a graphics­based simulation software used to study the behavior of transition
systems and it is mainly used for the simulation of thermal and electrical energy systems
[15]. In this chapter, the analysis of the initial data of the Dronninglund Fjernvarme will be
presented as well as the initial values used for the model. Furthermore, the analysis of
how the solar thermal plant was build­up in TRNSYS to first validate the measurements
based on the data provided and secondly to utilize this model in observing the thermal
efficiency of solar panels at different climatic conditions.
The model in TRNSYS is focused on the two solar collector fields where the propylene
glycol is heated up with the help of the two counter­flow heat exchangers in order to
provide the necessary hot water to the plant.
3.1

To validate the model in TRNSYS, the actual yields of the solar collector fields must be
first calculated. The fields were analyzed separately so that the measurements be more
accurate. The methodology for calculating the thermal efficiencies of the solar collector
fields along with the necessary parameters and equations is presented in detail in the
following paragraphs.
Regarding the data used for the calculations, both the factory operating data and the data
for direct sunlight refer to the years 2015 and 2016. The data for the year of 2014 were
excluded from the present thesis due to the lack of recording for the months before May,
since that was the commission date of the plant.

5
Discussion
The first goal of the current thesis is to analyze the thermal performance of the Dron­
ninglund Fjernvarme thermal plant located in Denmark and validating those measure­
ments with simulations in the TRNSYS. The second goal is to use the model built in
TRNSYS and study the thermal efficiency of the solar collectors in countries with different
climatic conditions. The weather data for the calculation of the measured results were
taken by the site Solvarmedata [25], while the weather data that used in the simulation
were retrieved by the site SolarCast [26]. The reason that two different site were cho­
sen was because the first site did not provide the solar radiation data (diffuse and direct
irradiance) needed for the simulations.
Graphs for (a) the collector’s input solar radiation versus the energy output (Input/Out­
put), (b) the collector’s output energy throughout the year, and (c) the collector’s mean
temperature minus the ambient temperature (Tmean ­ Tamb, °C) versus the aggregate so­
lar radiation were created from the measured and simulated data for Denmark, as well as
using simulated data for Greece and Romania.
There was no good agreement between the measured and simulated results for Denmark.
There was a higher dispersion in regards to the measured data, which means that for the
same values of solar radiation there was a variety in energy output but also in the daily
Tmean­Tamb. The highest solar radiation and the highest energy output of the measured
data was almost 8 (kWh/m2/day) and 4 (kWh/m2/day), respectively. Nevertheless, the
modeled data presented quite higher values of 11 (kWh/m2/day) for solar radiation and
6­8 (kWh/m2/day) for energy output. A reason could be that the weather data used in
TRNSYS model were measured by satellites and therefore are less accurate than the field
measurements. A further explanation could be that the solar sensors had a low quality
reading, due to infrequent maintenance, leading again to inaccurate solar radiation and
temperature measurements.
More specifically, the differences between the measured and simulated data in the energy
output versus the total solar radiation could have derived from a possible fluctuation in the
collector’s fluid temperature during its operation as higher storage temperatures lead to
higher collector’s inlet temperature and therefore to lower useful energy output. Another
explanation could be the possible malfunctions in the system or the low ambient temper­
atures which affected the system and forced it to reverse the flow inside the collector field
to maintain the operational temperature.
Additionally, the measured data showed higher values of Tmean­Tamb than the simulated
data in the graphs of Tmean­Tamb versus the total solar radiation. The useful energy
output of simulated data was higher than the measured ones and therefore a lower value
of Tmean­Tamb occurs. The reasons for the different values of Tmean­Tamb presented
for same values of solar radiation could be:
(a) For the low values of total solar radiation and Tmean­Tamb, a possible explanation
could be that during winter in Denmark the temperature dropped below zero, and therefore
the difference between Tmean and Tamb was small. Another explanation could be that
the Tmean of the collector was low as well as the Tamb and that led again to a small
difference between them.
Thermal performance of solar collector fields for different climatic conditions

==PAGE:37==
(b) For low solar radiation but high Tmean­Tamb, a reason could be that the Tmean of the
collector was high and the Tamb quite low.
(c) For total solar radiation from 4 to 8 kWh/m2/d, the Tmean­Tamb varied from 30 to 45
°C. Even if the summer months the temperature reached 35°C, the Tmean values could
have been 70°C leading again to higher difference.
By summing the daily useful energy output, the annual values occurred based on simu­
lated data were 22216164.74 kWh/m2 (±4356841.31) for the year 2015 and 26505835.26
kWh/m2 (±61698.25) for the year of 2016, while based on measured data were 8,568,570.1
kWh/m2 (±664359.06) and 8089969.65 kWh/m2 (±304776.52), respectively. By comparing
the energy output, for the year of 2015, the simulated value was 2.6 times higher than the
measured one, whilst for the year of 2016 was 3.3 times higher. In addition, the total so­
lar radiation of the simulated data was almost double than the measured for both years.
Therefore, not surprisingly, the efficiency of the simulated data was significantly higher
than the measured data but also unrealistic since it was 63.8% in Field 2 for the year of
2015 and 65.8% (±0.1) for the year of 2016. The solar radiation (1050.8 kWh/m2/y for the
year of 2015 and 1112.9 kWh/m2/y for the year of 2016) as well as the efficiencies (37.7%
to 45.8%) of the measured data seemed reasonable as the average total solar radiation
in Denmark varied from 1100 to 1200 kWh/m2/year [21] and a typical thermal efficiency
of a solar district heating plant in Denmark is 40 ­ 50% [22].
By simulating the data for Greece and Romania, the results in respect to the energy output
and the Tmean­Tamb were quite similar with a distinct difference during the winter and
autumn months. Specifically, although during summer months both of them achieved
a useful energy output that ranged from 3 to 5 kWh/m2/day, during winter and autumn
months, Greece showed higher values than Romania (0.5­1.5 kWh/m2/day). The differ­
ence in these two countries was also observed in Tmean­Tamb, where Romania (25 to
40°C) presented higher values than Greece (20 to 25°C) when the solar radiation was in
low levels (1 to 3 kWh/m2/day).
By summing the daily useful energy output, Greece presented 1.22 times higher value
than Romania for both years. Correspondingly, the total solar radiation in Greece (2015.7

higher than in Romania (1687.2 kWh/m2/day for the year of 2015 and 1662.8 kWh/m2/day
for the year of 2016). The average total solar radiation in Greece [23] was around 1400
kW/m2/year and in Romania ranged between 700 and 1450 kWh/m2/year (3 ­ 4 kWh
/m2/year) [24], therefore the difference between the average and the simulated values
was considered significant (p<0.05). The efficiencies for both Romania and Greece varied
from 61.5% to 63.5% and could be considered unrealistic.

Conclusions
The main focus of the thesis was to study and analyze the thermal performance of the
solar collectors using as reference the Dronninglund Fjernvarme thermal plant, located in
Denmark, as well as to simulate and estimate the thermal performance of a solar collector
field in different climatic conditions.
First, based on the real measurements, the solar radiation and the useful energy output
of the collectors were calculated and the thermal performance of the plant was examined.
The monitored values were compared to the simulated values calculated by the TRNSYS
model. The results showed an overestimation of the simulated values compared to the
measured ones, with the energy output of simulated data be around 2.5 times higher in
2015 and 3 times higher in 2016. Also, the solar radiation of the simulated data was
almost double than the measured one, and therefore the thermal efficiency of the plant
based on the simulated data reached unrealistic values (63.8% ­ 65.9%).
Furthermore, the useful energy output, the solar radiation and the thermal efficiencies
were calculated for Greece and Romania based on TRNSYS simulation results. Over­
estimated values of the energy output and the total solar radiation occurred once again,
leading to extremely high efficiencies (61.5% ­ 63.5%).
6.1
Future Work
Detailed investigation is recommended in order to improve the TRNSYS model, but also
a more detailed selection of weather data entered into the program.
More specifically, a research could be conducted to determine if solar data such as diffuse
and direct radiation would need adjustment. In addition, the parameters such as the
intercept efficiency, the slope efficiency and the efficiency curvature within the collector
should be chosen after a detailed calculation in order to obtain more accurate values that
will best fit the collector model.
Additionally, it is recommended to obtain the solar data for the measurements and simu­
lations by solar sensors that record diffuse and direct radiation from the site or historical
weather data from meteorological stations and not from satellites.
