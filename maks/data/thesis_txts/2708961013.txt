Abstract
DTU Mechanical Engineering
M.Sc. Thesis
Simulation and Structural Optimization using the Updated Lagrangian
Approach
by Mads Kasper Hørsholt
This thesis describes the implementation of a geometrically non-linear ﬁnite element
method code using an updated Lagrangian approach. Subsequentially, level-set based
structural topology optimization using the cut element method is performed. Using
an updated Lagrangian approach is key, as the cut element method is an immersed
boundary method, which potentially will break down under large deformations. This
issue is alleviated with the updated Lagrangian method in which the structure is
always analysed in its deformed conﬁguration, i.e., using a geometric update of the
level-set ﬁeld. It is of great interest to develop this functionality, as it facilitates struc-
tural topology optimization of e.g. Fluid-Structure-Interaction (FSI) problems with
large deformations using the cut element method. Moreover, this approach allows
to fully decouple the analysis mesh from the design representation, i.e., an Arbitrary
Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) approach.
This thesis details the development and implementation of the aforementioned meth-
ods.
The proposed immersed boundary ALE method is then utilized in an end-
compliance topology optimization problem, as a proof of concept.
The proposed
method produces optimal designs on the level of standard level-set based Lagrangian
topology optimization. This shows the potential of the method for future development
and utilization in multi-physics problems.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In many engineering disciplines, numerical modelling and simulation of complex phys-
ical systems are of great value. This is also the case for mechanical engineering, where
numerical modelling is used to simulate the deformation of complex geometries among
other things. However, numerical modelling is also used in civil engineering, chemical
engineering, electrical engineering and numerous other disciplines.
Numerical modelling is the use of mathematical and numerical techniques and meth-
ods to obtain approximate solutions to physical problems. Using numerical modelling
to approximate the behaviour of complex physical systems are of great scientiﬁc and
engineering interest, as it allows relatively simple mathematical and numerical meth-
ods to solve complex problems where analytical solutions are non-existent.
Solving multi-physics problems, where diﬀerent physics and their interactions are sim-
ulated simultaneously, is even more complex than just structural simulation. Exam-
ples of multi-physics problems include electro-mechanical, thermo-mechanical, vibro-
acoustic, ﬂuid-structure and more. In all of these interaction problems, a crisp bound-
ary, meaning distinct zones of transition between the diﬀerent physics that are being
simulated, is pre-eminent to a high accuracy numerical solution. A speciﬁc example
could be the ﬂuid-structure interaction of insect wings, or a ﬂag waving in the wind,
Tian et al. 2014. Figure 1.1 shows a ﬂag waving in the wind.
Figure 1.1. Example of a geometrically non-linear Fluid-Structure
Interaction problem. A ﬂag waving in the wind.
Furthermore, optimization of physical systems is also valuable. Employing math-
ematical and numerical techniques to optimize a system for a chosen objective is
==PAGE:10==

very useful, and has many applications in engineering and science. Examples include
shape optimization of structures to achieve maximum stiﬀness in solid mechanics, or
shape optimization to maximize lift for an airplane wing in ﬂuid mechanics. In these
examples, the objective to be maximized is stiﬀness and lift, respectively. Combin-
ing multi-physics numerical modelling and optimization, the amount of interesting
physics to be explored is extensive.
Because crisp boundaries are crucial for successful structural optimization and multi-
physics problems in general, methods able to accurately model crisp boundaries are
desired. Furthermore, it is of academic interest to include geometric non-linearities
within such methods, as some multi-physics problems have large structural deforma-
tions, which cannot be represented with any degree of accuracy by linear elasticity.
1.2
Goals
This thesis aspires to develop a novel immersed boundary Arbitrary Lagrangian-
Eulerian (ALE) method, allowing for large deformations of simulated structures, us-
ing a cut element method. In addition, topology optimization is applied, using the
methods proposed in this work. The thesis will not cover any multi-physics, but aims
to develop methods facilitating such capabilities, allowing others to use it for that
purpose in the future.
A cut element method with geometrically non-linear modelling of structures is
of academic interest, because it could allow simulation of multi-physics problems
with large structural deformations, which previously has been notoriously diﬃcult,
Tian et al. 2014. An implementation of an updated Lagrangian element formula-
tion supporting large deformations implemented in the cut element method proposed
by Andreasen, Elingaard, and Aage 2020 will be presented in this thesis. Solving
geometrically non-linear coupled problems using an immersed boundary method is
often problematic, as the void-mesh easily becomes highly deformed, leading to non-
convergence. This thesis aspires to solve this issue by mapping the current solution
of a simulation onto a structured mesh when the current mesh becomes to distorted
to allow for a continuation of the simulation without changing the mesh, i.e. an ALE
approach. This is contrary to other methods where mesh updating is necessary, which
is very costly, Tezduyar et al. 2006.
In fewer words, the goal of this thesis is developing a novel immersed boundary, Ar-
bitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian method supporting structural geometric non-linearities.
Development of such a method could allow for numerically solving interesting, struc-
turally non-linear multi-physics problems.
1.3
Contributions
This work will contribute to the ﬁeld of multi-physics and numerical solid mechanics
with a novel method facilitating simulation and optimization of problems with large
structural deformations. Speciﬁcally, the contributions from the work of this thesis
are the following:
• An updated Lagrangian element formulation supporting geometric
non-linearities
– Implementation in a cut element method.

are Zienkiewicz and Taylor 2000, Bhatti 2005, Kim 2015, Reddy 2004, Belytschko

Implementation of a total and updated Lagrangian
element formulation
As mentioned in the goals of the thesis, a working implementation of an updated
Lagrangian element formulation is a prerequisite to everything else in this thesis.
Implementation details of a total Lagrangian formulation and an updated Lagrangian
formulation is described in this section. The total Lagrangian formulation is included
as a future benchmark to compare the updated Lagrangian formulation to, as well as
a stepping stone to implement the updated Lagrangian formulation upon. The total
Lagrangian formulation is signiﬁcantly better documented in the literature, and it is
therefore convenient to implement ﬁrst. Pseudo-code implementation details are also
included. The derivation of the equilibrium equations from the principle of virtual
work along with discretization details are presented in Appendix B.

Cut elements
This chapter present the theory of cut elements, as described in Andreasen, Elingaard,
and Aage 2020. The cut element method is a type of immersed boundary method.
As the name suggests, it refers to the boundary, in this case of a structure, which lies
immersed in a mesh. In the method described here, the boundary of a geometry is
implicitly deﬁned by a level-set.
The cut element method is an expanded ﬁnite element method, with the ability to
use cut elements, i.e.
elements which are part solid and part void.
Figure 3.1b
shows such an element. Figure 3.1a shows an example of a cantilever immersed in a
structured grid. Elements are cut in order to represent the boundary. This allows for
analysis of arbitrary geometrical shapes upon a structured mesh. For a standard ﬁnite
element method, the elements would have to be formed into the geometry which is
desired to analyse, i.e. body-ﬁtted. Therefore, anytime the geometry is changed, the
mesh must be discarded, and a new one must be made. Through an implicitly deﬁned
geometry by a level-set, no remeshing is necessary when geometry changes. Instead of
remeshing, a categorization of elements to ﬁnd the ones cut by the new boundary is all
that is required upon changes in the geometry. With an immersed boundary method,
it is important to know which nodes are solid, i.e. within a structure, and which
nodes are not, i.e. void. Through an indicator ﬁeld α(x) this can be achieved. The
indicator ﬁeld takes on the value α(x) = 1 inside the solid region, and α(x) = 10−9 in
void regions. Figure 3.1b illustrates this For the purposes of this thesis, two element
formulations are used concurrently, one for the void regions, and another for the solid
regions. The indicator ﬁeld is used when determining the stiﬀness (Young’s modulus)

Within a cut element method such as the one utilized in this thesis, there exists three

As simulating geometrically non-linear structural deformations is desired all the
solid elements must have a total or an updated Lagrangian formulation. The cut
elements consist of both a solid and a void portion. Further, to ensure numerical
stability, a void regularization strategy of using a linear element formulation on the
void elements is employed. What remains to be determined is how to treat the cut

4.1.1
Solid elements
For the solid elements, the updated Lagrangian formulation described in chapter 2
is used. The only diﬀerence is in the bookkeeping, ensuring only solid elements are
formulated using the updated Lagrangian formulation.
4.1.2
Void regularization
To ensure that the void region behaves properly, a void regularization strategy must
be implemented. The strategy used in this work, also the one employed by Wang
et al. 2014, is to use a linear element formulation for the void elements.
Linear
elasticity and its ﬁnite element implementation is assumed well-understood by the
reader, and details will not be discussed. The stiﬀness contribution from void regions
are calculated once and kept during the convergence iterations.
4.1.3
Cut element formulation
Potential issues with convergence and element stability inﬂuences the choice of what
element formulation to use for the void portion of a cut element. The solid portion
must have the relevant non-linear element formulation, ensuring equivalent stiﬀness
and behaviour as a standard ﬁnite element method. There are two options. Either the
void portions of the cut elements are treated as void elements, or they are given a non-
linear element formulation with the same void stiﬀness used in the void elements as
determined by the indicator ﬁeld 3.1. To quantify which option is preferable, a simple
cantilever subject to a lateral force is considered, which Figure 4.4a shows. Diﬀerences
in displacement ﬁelds, CPU time, convergence and cut element behaviour is relevant
metrics to determine which option is preferable.
Table 4.1 lists the quantitative
diﬀerences between the linear and non-linear void element formulations.
Displacement ﬁeld
Between the two options, there are no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the solution of the
displacement ﬁeld. The two relevant measures, maximum displacement and norm of
displacement ﬁeld show no signiﬁcant diﬀerences.
Computational time
With regard to CPU-time, there is a signiﬁcant increase in computational time for
the non-linear void portion formulation. This is because the stiﬀness of an element
with a non-linear formulation is updated for each equilibrium iteration, which is
not the case for a linear element. Thus, a linear element must only be integrated
once to determine its stiﬀness, whereas the stiﬀness of a non-linear element must
be determined again in each iteration. This is a clear disadvantage of using a non-
linear element formulation where it is not strictly necessary. While the diﬀerence
in computational time in the chosen example is small, it will scale with the amount
of cut elements, and compound when shape optimization is performed, which can
usually take hundreds of optimization iterations.
Convergence and cut element behaviour
For the test problem used, there is no diﬀerence in convergence. For the non-linear
formulation, void nodes of a cut element might behave poorly however. This problem
can mostly but not completely be alleviated when imposing an area constraint on cut

6.1
Lagrangian and Eulerian descriptions
It is pertinent to ﬁrst deﬁne what Lagrangian and Eulerian means. In this context,
both are ways to describe the state of objects in a mesh. A Lagrangian description
describe the motion of a material point by its initial position and its displacement.
Thus the current position of a material point is given by the initial position (reference)
added together with the displacement of the point away from that reference. This is
identical to the way position is deﬁned in the total Lagrangian formulation, as Figure
2.1a shows. In an Eulerian description, motion of material points are not explicitly
described. Rather, it is a spatial description, specifying if material is present at a
point. For an Eulerian mesh, the node coordinates are ﬁxed. For a Lagrangian mesh,
the nodes are coincident with material points, and will displace with the material.
Conspicuously, this means that a Lagrangian mesh will distort as deformation occurs.
As element accuracy will degrade as it becomes distorted, there is a limit to the mag-
nitude of deformation that can be modelled using a Lagrangian mesh. An Eulerian
mesh, by virtue of its ﬁxed node coordinates, does not become distorted as defor-
mation occurs. The diﬀerences between a Lagrangian and Eulerian mesh is easier to
grasp viewed in an illustration. Figure 6.1 illustrates a block in a Lagrangian and
Eulerian mesh subject to shear. The Lagrangian mesh elements are ﬁxed on material
points, and deforms along with the block. The Eulerian mesh is ﬁxed, and the block
deforms within the mesh. This necessitates that the Eulerian mesh is large enough
to enclose the material when it becomes deformed, in this case requiring additional
elements to encompass the material.

Eulerian step resets the mesh, and allows for accurate simulation of additional physics
once again. The Lagrangian and Eulerian steps can be repeated as necessary. It is
applicable to multi-physics problems with large deformations, especially pertinent to

6.2. Transfer of boundary

It is necessary to develop methods for mapping data from simulations resulting
in a deformed mesh onto a structured mesh. The level-set describing the void-solid
boundary and the deformation gradient information are the relevant quantities that
must be mapped. First the level-set implicitly deﬁning the boundary of the structure
must be mapped to the structured mesh. With this mapped level-set, relevant nodes
of the structure to which deformation gradient information also must be mapped
can be identiﬁed. This order of operations is important, because only relevant nodes
should be used when mapping deformation data. The reason is that void element
nodes degrade the mapping accuracy, especially if void elements overlap the solid
elements. Figure 6.4a shows such overlap of elements. The displacements and defor-
mation gradients of these distorted void elements carry no relevant deformation data
pertaining to the deformation of the solid. All of this combined is designated as the
Eulerian part within the Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE).
There are multiple eligible approaches for data transfer between meshes along with
multiple types of data. These are introduced here, and which method is more suitable
is assessed.
6.2
Transfer of boundary
A precise and robust way to transfer the deformed boundary to an undeformed mesh is
very important. If the boundary is transferred inaccurately, the subsequent mapping
of the deformation gradient ﬁeld, which might bring its own inaccuracies, will suﬀer.
Figure 6.2 shows an example mapping of the boundary from a deformed mesh to a

6.3
Mapping deformation gradient information
As Chapter 2 describes, the deformation gradient describe the state of deformation of
the structure being simulated. To make the ALE approach work, it is therefore nec-
essary to devise a method capable of accurately transferring the deformation gradient
information from the deformed mesh onto the structured mesh. This section concerns
itself with two approaches for mapping the deformation gradient information from a
deformed mesh to a structured mesh.
6.3.1
Nodal displacement method
One approach to mapping information about the deformation of the solid elements is
to transfer nodal displacements from the deformed mesh to the undeformed mesh, and
subsequently reconstructing the deformation gradient ﬁeld. This is done by bilinear
interpolation of displacements from relevant nodes on the deformed mesh onto rele-
vant nodes on the undeformed mesh. Relevant nodes are deﬁned as all nodes belonging
to solid and cut elements. Figure 6.9a shows the relevant nodes on both meshes. The
deformation gradient ﬁeld is subsequently reconstructed from the interpolated nodal
displacements. Figure 6.9b show the procedure of the nodal displacement method.

6.5
n-step ALE topology optimization
The n in n-step ALE refers to the number of Eulerian steps (mappings) that are
performed for each optimization iteration. Thus a zero-step ALE optimization is a
purely Lagrangian optimization, and a one-step ALE is an optimization with one
Eulerian step. A few hindrances must be solved to facilitate n-step ALE topology op-
timization. The hindrances are with the structural compliance calculation, sensitivity
calculations and structural loading after mapping.
6.5.1
Sensitivity mapping
In a n-step ALE topology optimization, the sensitivities are determined after the
geometry and deformation information are mapped onto a structured mesh n times.
To be able to optimize, the sensitivities must then be mapped backwards onto the
original conﬁguration, in order for the optimization algorithm to update the level-set.
A method that maps the sensitivity ﬁeld accurately is essential, as it is "direction the
optimizer goes". If the sensitivity ﬁeld is not mapped accurately, the optimizer will not
perform well. The method proposed in this thesis is a simple bilinear interpolation,
identical to the procedure for the mapping of the displacement ﬁeld onto a new,
structured mesh.
Relevant nodes on the old and new mesh are identiﬁed, and a
bilinear interpolation is performed to transfer the sensitivity ﬁeld from the new mesh
onto the old mesh. Please refer to section 6.3.1 for details. Figure 6.16 shows the
sensitivity ﬁelds of a structure for a zero-step ALE optimization, i.e. no mapping, and
a one-step ALE optimization along with a visualization of the absolute error. Figure
6.16a shows the sensitivity ﬁeld for no mapping. Figures 6.16b and 6.16c show the
sensitivity ﬁeld before and after backwards mapping to the original conﬁguration. The
absolute diﬀerence of the two sensitivity ﬁelds with and without mapping are shown
in Figure 6.16d. Curiously, the mapped sensitivity ﬁeld shown in Figure 6.16c, is quite
far from the directly determined sensitivity ﬁeld shown in Figure 6.16a, in magnitude.
However, qualitatively the sensitivity ﬁelds look very similar, as though the mapped
sensitivity ﬁeld is simply scaled down by some factor. Where this apparent scaling
comes from is hard to say.
While the objective functions of the Lagrangian and
ALE approach from which the sensitivities are scaled are not identical, they do not
diﬀer by a substantial amount.
The scaling is therefore not introduced there.
It
seems likely that the scaling stems from the adjoint method used to calculate the
sensitivities, described in Chapter 5. The Lagrangian multiplier vector {λ}, which is
a sort of pseudo-displacement ﬁeld determined from the end tangent-stiﬀness matrix,
will be substantially smaller in magnitude for the ALE approach. This is due to the
already deformed geometry, which will deform less than the original geometry will,
by the analogy that λ is a displacement ﬁeld. With regard to the mapping procedure,
some smoothing of the sensitivity ﬁeld appears to occur, see the magnitude of the
colorscales in Figures 6.16b and 6.16c. High values in individual nodes are diﬃcult
to accurately map between meshes, as the nodes do not coincide, thus resulting in
some information loss. This is hard to ﬁx as it is an inherent problem, or feature, of
interpolation.

7.2.2
ALE discussion
With these results, it was possible to successfully perform topology optimization
with a novel Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian immersed boundary method with multi-
ple mapping steps. The method is promising, and could lead to solving interesting
problems in the future. The method was supposed to alleviate issues with instability
and mesh distortion for large deformations. Using the method, the deformation of the
void mesh was signiﬁcantly reduced, as Figure 7.7d and Figure 7.7e show. Notice the
severe deformation of the void elements in the Figure 7.7d, compared to the structured
void elements of Figure 7.7e. The latter mesh is unloaded in Figure 7.7f, distorting
the void elements, in reverse. The implementation of the method in its current form
nonetheless leaves something to be desired. Many issues presented themselves while
performing the optimizations in problem 2. Issues include boundary collapse, split-
ting of boundaries into multiple separate structures, positioning of new loads after
mapping and boundary contraction. An obvious increase in computational time was
of course also evident the more steps were performed per optimization iteration.
==PAGE:64==
56
Chapter 7. Results
Boundary collapse
As the number of ALE steps went up, the chance of an unfortunate cutting of el-
ements leading to collapse increased. An example of this behaviour was when an
attempt was made to do optimization with n = 10 Eulerian steps. At some step
along the 10 steps, a bad cut of an element or an inaccurate deformation gradient re-
construction would cause a collapse of the boundary, which would then be attempted
mapped onto a structured mesh. The mapping of the level-set does not work with a
collapsed boundary, and the subsequent steps would be worthless, making the result-
ing sensitivity ﬁeld at the end of the Eulerian steps unusable. Because of the unusable
sensitivity ﬁeld, the optimizing algorithm could not identify where on the boundary
there was a problem, and the problem would often persist. Figure 7.7b shows an ex-
ample of boundary collapse. It is easy to imagine how mapping a collapsed geometry
is counterproductive.
Boundary separation
Depending on the ﬁlter radius and optimization penalty parameter c, a solid geometry
would sometimes separate when a region of the geometry became too thin. This would
sometimes lead to crashes of the code and at the very least unphysical structures. The
code would crash if a loaded part of the geometry totally separated from the main
structure, as there would be no elements with signiﬁcant stiﬀness holding it inside
the mesh domain. Thus, when applying an equivalent load, the position of the loaded
elements were no longer within the domain. In other cases a double cut of an element
is present. The separation has occurred, but there is non-physical stiﬀness holding
the two together. Figure 7.7a shows an example of the latter case.
Boundary contraction
If the initial conﬁguration was too far from the desired volume fraction, the optimiza-
tion algorithm would start indiscriminately contracting the boundary everywhere.
This could sometimes lead to the load position being nearly or completely without
contact to the structure. This leads to problems with positioning of the new load
after mapping, and often cause crashing and non-convergence. An example of this
behaviour is shown in Figure 7.7c. If this problem presented itself and there were
subsequent steps to be performed, the equivalent load position determined by inter-
polation was often outside the mesh, crashing the code.

Chapter 8
Discussion
While the work in this thesis has been fruitful, some challenges still remain. With
regard to the desired contributions described in the introduction,
8.1
Updated Lagrangian implementation
A working updated Lagrangian formulation has been developed and implemented in
an immersed boundary ﬁnite element method, speciﬁcally the cut element method
proposed by Andreasen, Elingaard, and Aage 2020. This contribution has been pro-
foundly successful, with an eﬀective implementation virtually identical to the more
well-described total Lagrangian formulation. It has been totally successful, and does
not warrant future work.
8.2
Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian method
Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian method functionality was also a key goal of the thesis.
While a decent amount of success on this point was accomplished, some issues still
remain. From most to least successful, they are
1. Level-set mapping.
• The method developed for mapping the geometry was entirely successful. If
any criticism is warranted, it is the numerical implementation which leaves
some computational eﬃciency to be desired, referring here to the brute
force part of the signed distance ﬁeld construction. Developing a method
for directly determining the distance of each node to the boundary might
be appealing, if computational speed becomes a priority. Furthermore, the
method used for determining which nodes are inside and which are outside
the boundaries only support single contours. To increase the complexity
of structures that can be simulated and optimized, such as the geometry
in Figure 7.1, support for multiple contours could be interesting.
2. Sensitivity ﬁeld mapping.
• The backwards mapping of the sensitivity ﬁeld was moderately successful.
In some cases, the sensitivity ﬁeld had too intricate details to be able
to be captured by the simple linear interpolation proposed in this thesis.
However, the ALE results produced had only slight discrepancies compared
to a purely Lagrangian optimization, on the order of a few percent at worst,
and in one case being few percent better. Other alternatives have not been
conceived however. Further deliberations are warranted to ensure that this
is actually the case.
==PAGE:68==
60
Chapter 8. Discussion
3. Deformation gradient ﬁeld mapping.
• This was the least successful, or more accurately the least satisfying, part
of the ALE method development. Originally, as is described in Section
6.3.2, it was the plan to directly interpolate the deformation gradient ﬁeld
onto the new, structured mesh. It was thoroughly unsuccessful, and it is
still not plausibly explained why this is the case. Instead, a method of
mapping the displacement ﬁeld to the new mesh, and reconstructing the
deformation gradient tensor ﬁeld from that was chosen. With this method,
an obvious information loss is apparent, with deformation gradient tensors
in a system being in greater numbers, and having more information about
the state of the solid itself than a displacement ﬁeld contains. Nevertheless,
this method was vastly superior to the alternative by virtue of it actually
working. Regrettably, the method has situations where it performs subop-
timally, and will distort the boundary slightly post mapping.
8.3
Topology optimization
Some interesting topology optimization results were produced in this thesis. It was
shown to be possible to perform an ALE structural topology optimization, which
was the ultimate goal of the thesis, with all the other goals being prerequisites to
that. While it was possible to produce a successful ALE end-compliance topology
optimization, multiple issues arose. Nevertheless, a large reason for developing an
immersed boundary ALE method was to ensure that there are no large deformations
of the void ﬁeld or poor void element behaviour.
This part has been completely
successful.
==PAGE:69==
61
Chapter 9
Future work
While the goals of the thesis has been achieved, there are some issues that would be
pertinent to resolve within the novel Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian method that has
been proposed. From most to least important.
First, more accurate deformation information mapping is desired. As Section 6.3.3
describes, there exist cases where the mapping is inaccurate and performs poorly.
Also, it is interesting to know why direct deformation gradient entity interpolation
does not work, if for nothing else but academic curiosity.
Second, alleviation of the issues encountered during structural topology optimization
should be prioritized. Whenever a new method is proposed, it is bound to encounter
unforeseen circumstances and initial roadblocks to success. The proposed method in
this work has been no diﬀerent. Many of the issues experiences are not necessarily
inherent to the ALE method, but at least partly inherent to level-set topology opti-
mization. Resolving these problems are of high priority.
Third, support for multiple boundaries in the brute-force signed distance ﬁeld is
desirable to broaden the type of structures possible to simulate and optimize. An
alternative algorithm for determining if a node is inside or outside a boundary must
be found or developed. As an aside, an alternative to the brute force part of the
calculation of distances should be rather straightforward to implement. Algorithms
for calculation distance from a point to constrained lines exist. Typical methods con-
sider the distance between a point and a line of inﬁnite length, which of course is
inapplicable here.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
The work set out in the goal of this thesis was successful. An updated Lagrangian
implementation within an immersed boundary method, more speciﬁcally a cut ele-
ment method, has been carried out. A novel Arbitrary-Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE)
method allowing for mapping of geometry and deformation data has been proposed
and implemented.
Finally, functionality of all developed tools has been shown by performing topology
optimization using said tools. To great satisfaction, it was possible to perform struc-
tural topology optimization the proposed ALE method. The method developed in
this thesis is promising for use in multi-physics problems where structural deforma-
tions with geometrical non-linearities are present. More speciﬁcally, it is not far from
being used for Fluid-Structure-Interaction (FSI) problems.
In closing, this thesis has provided a novel immersed boundary Arbitrary Lagrangian-
Eulerian method capable of structural topology optimization with geometric non-
linearities. In the authors opinion it is a signiﬁcant contribution to the academic
ﬁelds of topology optimization, numerical multi-physics and numerical solid mechan-
ics.
All goals have been achieved, all contributions made.
