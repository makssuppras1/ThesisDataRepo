Abstract
Studying free surface flows generated by a moving body is relevant to various fluid-
structure interaction applications. Such investigations are crucial for fields such as
ship and submarine design and wave energy devices, where understanding fluid dy-
namics and wave motion is essential. Although many problems involve bodies moving
at constant speed in water, wave generation by submerged structures remains a long-
standing challenge. Despite decades of research [1, 2, 3], progress in this area remains
limited.
Meanwhile, advances in high-performance computing have enabled CFD
methods of increasing fidelity for predicting flows. This thesis presents a new high-
order numerical solver capable of finding steady-state solutions to the incompressible
Navier-Stokes equations subject to various flow conditions, different submerged struc-
tures, and a free surface.
With this objective, the mathematical task is twofold:
determine i) the final shape of the free surface and ii) the flow quantities, including
velocity and pressure. The governing equations are handled within the open-source
parallel computational framework, Firedrake [4], where they are solved considering a
Galerkin-based numerical discretization. In particular, the weak form of the problem
is addressed using a spectral element method (SEM) [5, 6, 7]. Curvilinear elements
are introduced to enable the solver to accurately represent and handle more complex
geometries in the setting of a high-order spectral element method. Since the free
surface dynamically adapts to the pressure and velocity fields of the flow, an iterative
approach is employed to reach the steady-state profile. The proposed spectral ele-
ment solver is first validated by solving intermediate problem, then is subsequently
extended to account for free surface effects. The resulting steady wave patterns gen-
erated by a submerged moving body are analyzed under various flow conditions. The
numerical results are qualitatively compared with reference studies [1, 8, 9] and with
a solution obtained using a finite-volume CFD code (OpenFOAM) [10]. Finally, the
model is extended to three spatial dimensions in order to simulate scenarios that are
representative of real-world applications.
Keywords: Free surface flow, spectral element method (SEM), incompressible Navier-
Stokes, Firedrake, high-order numerical solver, steady-state flow.

Abstract in lingua
italiana
Lo studio dei flussi a superficie libera generati da un corpo in movimento è rilevante
per numerose applicazioni nell’ambito dell’interazione fluido-struttura. Tali indagini
sono fondamentali in settori come la progettazione di navi e sottomarini o dei dis-
positivi per il recupero dell’energia dalle onde, dove la comprensione della dinamica
dei fluidi e del moto ondoso è essenziale. Sebbene molti problemi coinvolgano corpi
sommersi che si muovono a velocità costante, lo studio delle onde da essi generate
rimane ancora oggi un problema complesso. Nonostante decenni di studi [1, 2, 3],
i recenti progressi in questo ambito sono stati limitati.
Tuttavia, lo sviluppo del
calcolo ad alte prestazioni ha reso accessibili metodi CFD più avanzati e accurati.
Questa tesi presenta un nuovo risolutore numerico ad alto ordine in grado di calcolare
soluzioni stazionarie delle equazioni di Navier-Stokes incomprimibili, considerando di-
verse condizioni di flusso, strutture sommerse e la presenza di una superficie libera.
L’obiettivo matematico si articola in due compiti principali: determinare i) la forma
finale della superficie libera e ii) le grandezze del flusso, come velocità e pressione.
Le equazioni di governo sono risolte all’interno del framework computazionale open-
source Firedrake [4], utilizzando una discretizzazione numerica basata sul metodo di
Galerkin. In particolare, la forma debole del problema viene affrontata mediante un
metodo agli elementi spettrali (SEM) [5, 6, 7]. Vengono introdotti elementi curvilinei
per poter rappresentare e gestire con precisione geometrie più complesse. Poiché la
superficie libera si adatta dinamicamente ai campi di pressione e velocità del flusso,
si adotta un approccio iterativo per raggiungere un profilo stazionario. Il risolutore
proposto viene dapprima validato risolvendo problemi intermedi; successivamente, è
esteso per tenere conto degli effetti di superficie libera. I profili d’onda generati da
un corpo in movimento sommerso sono esaminati sotto diverse condizioni di flusso. I
risultati numerici sono confrontati qualitativamente con studi di riferimento [1, 8, 9]
e con una soluzione ottenuta utilizzando un codice CFD a volumi finiti (OpenFOAM).
Infine, il modello è esteso a tre dimensioni al fine di simulare scenari più realistici.
Parole chiave: Flusso a superficie libera, metodo degli elementi spettrali (SEM),
equazioni di Navier-Stokes incomprimibili, Firedrake, flusso stazionario.

Expected prerequisites
and Notation
Expected prerequisites
This thesis is aimed at a Master’s level audience in mathematical modeling and com-
putational methods. The reader is expected to have a foundational understanding
of scientific computing, particularly partial differential equations (PDEs). This in-
cludes familiarity with Sobolev spaces, weak formulations, and standard numerical
methods such as the Backward Euler method for temporal integration. A working
knowledge of Galerkin discretization would be beneficial; however, relevant concepts
will be introduced throughout the thesis.
A fundamental understanding of Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is recom-
mended. Familiarity with the Navier-Stokes equations, their role in modeling fluid
dynamics, and an awareness of the behavior of free surfaces will be advantageous.
Although prior knowledge of free surface flow modeling is not a strict requirement,
understanding the challenges of CFD, particularly in the dynamic modeling of free
surfaces and the associated boundary conditions, will be beneficial to the reader’s
comprehension of the material presented in this thesis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The study of fluid flows has been a fundamental area of scientific inquiry since the
inception of the field of fluid mechanics.
The ancient Greek scientist Archimedes
began investigating the properties of fluids in the 3rd century BCE, formulating his
renowned Principle of Archimedes, which remains a cornerstone in fluid mechanics.
Since Archimedes’ time, fluid flows have continued to be a primary focus of scien-
tific research, with numerous advancements in theory, experimentation, and compu-
tational methods. Despite the vast range of flow problems that can be investigated
under various conditions, this thesis will specifically concentrate on free surface flows.
Studying free surface flows generated by a moving body is critical in fluid-structure in-
teraction, with wide-ranging applications across various engineering disciplines. The
ability to predict and understand the behavior of such flows is crucial in designing ma-
rine vessels, such as ships and submarines, and in developing wave energy conversion
devices. In these applications, accurate modeling of fluid dynamics and wave motion
is essential for optimizing performance, ensuring stability, and improving eﬀiciency.
The ability to simulate and predict how these bodies interact with the surrounding
fluid, and how they generate waves, provides invaluable insight into their behavior
under real-world conditions.
Despite significant advances in fluid dynamics, wave generation by submerged, mov-
ing bodies remains a longstanding and challenging problem. While the motion of
bodies at constant speed in water has been widely studied, the interaction between
submerged structures and the free surface continues to pose complex modeling and
analytical diﬀiculties. These interactions are governed by the incompressible Navier-
Stokes equations, which describe the flow of viscous fluids under the influence of
boundary conditions imposed by the free surface. The challenge in simulating such
flows lies in capturing the dynamic evolution of the free surface, which is influenced
by both the body’s motion and the flow characteristics.
Various numerical and experimental approaches have been developed to address this
problem.
Salvesen (1976) was among the first to compute steady nonlinear free-
surface waves generated by a two-dimensional hydrofoil, employing a finite difference
iterative method [11]. Bai (1978) introduced a localized finite element method based
on the Galerkin formulation [12]. Subsequently, Yeung and Bouger (1979) developed
a hybrid integral equation approach using Green’s theorem [13]. Duncan (1983) con-
ducted a series of experiments on a NACA0012 hydrofoil, varying the submergence
depth, angle of attack, and velocity to measure free-surface elevations and both break-
==PAGE:22==
2
1 Introduction
ing and non-breaking wave resistance [14].
Kennell and Plotkin (1984) addressed
the potential flow around a thin, two-dimensional hydrofoil advancing at constant
speed beneath the free surface [15]. Forbes (1985) proposed a method for computing
two-dimensional potential flows around wings with cusped trailing edges submerged
beneath a stream of infinite depth [16]. Coleman (1986) attempted to simulate the
wave breaking process through a finite difference method, incorporating an artificial
pressure distribution on the free surface and enforcing a Kutta condition at the trail-
ing edge [17]. Hino (1988) solved both the Euler and Navier–Stokes equations with
nonlinear free-surface boundary conditions, analyzing the lift and drag characteristics
of submerged hydrofoils [18]. Later, Kwag and Mori (1991) applied a Navier–Stokes
solver to examine three-dimensional free-surface flows around submerged hydrofoils
[19]. Hino (1993) also introduced a finite volume method on unstructured grids for the
simulation of free-surface flows [3]. Wu and Eatock Taylor (1995) compared the finite
element and boundary element methods for time-dependent, nonlinear simulations of
two-dimensional hydrofoils [20]. Bal (1999) formulated the hydrofoil problem using
Green’s function and Green’s theorem, employing Kelvin-type sources and normal
dipoles on the hydrofoil surface, while assuming a linearized free-surface boundary
condition [21]. More recently, Di Mascio et al. (2007) developed a Reynolds-Averaged
Navier–Stokes (RANS) solver to capture both breaking and non-breaking waves gen-
erated by submerged hydrofoils [22].
Despite decades of research into this phenomenon, as highlighted in the cited works,
progress has been slow, with relatively few breakthroughs in recent years. This can be
attributed to the complex nature of the fluid-structure interaction and the diﬀiculties
inherent in modeling the free surface accurately. However, rapid advances in compu-
tational methods have provided new opportunities for tackling these challenges. The
rise of high-performance computing (HPC) has enabled the use of state-of-the-art
CFD methods, which offer increasing fidelity in predicting complex fluid flow phe-
nomena. These advancements in computing power have significantly impacted the
engineering design process, allowing for more detailed simulations that inform design
decisions and improve the performance of various engineering systems.
This thesis presents a new high-order numerical solver which solves the steady in-
compressible Navier-Stokes equations subject to varying flow conditions, submerged
structures, and a free surface. The solver is capable of finding steady-state solutions
that accurately represent the fluid flow around moving bodies in a fluid domain. The
mathematical task can be broken down into two main objectives: first, to determine
the final shape of the free surface, and second, to compute the flow quantities, such
as velocity and pressure, throughout the fluid domain. The proposed solver addresses
these objectives by employing a high-order discretization method within the the open-
source computational finite elements framework, Firedrake [4].
To accurately represent curved boundaries within the computational domain, curvi-
linear elements are incorporated. This is achieved using a transfinite linear blending
technique based on the methodologies outlined in [23, 24, 25]. The approach enables
precise geometric modeling, which is crucial for maintaining spectral convergence that
can otherwise degrade due to geometric approximation errors near curved boundaries.

One of the key challenges in modeling free surface flows is the dynamic and a priori
unknown nature of the free surface, which must be determined as part of the solution.
As the fluid flows around the submerged body, the free surface continuously adapts
to the pressure and velocity fields in the fluid domain. To capture this behavior, an
iterative approach is employed, which involves adjusting the free surface boundary
at each pseudo-time step to converge toward a steady-state solution. This dynamic
process is crucial in obtaining an accurate representation of the steady state flow and
the wave pattern generated by the moving body.
To validate the proposed spectral element solver, a series of intermediate test cases
are solved, such as the lid-driven cavity flow, which is a well-known benchmark prob-
lem in computational fluid dynamics. This problem involves the flow of fluid in a
square cavity with a moving lid, and it serves as a useful test case for verifying the
accuracy and performance of the numerical solver [26]. Moreover, the well-known
benchmark problems of a NACA airfoil and a circular cylinder placed in a channel
are also considered.
The comparison with different benchmarks as [27, 28, 29] is
performed to confirm the correct behavior of the solver. Once the solver is validated,
the computational domain is extended to simulate more complex scenarios involving
submerged bodies in motion, which generate waves on the surface.
Initially, the flow over a bump located on the bottom boundary is examined. Sub-
sequently, attention is turned to the flow around a submerged NACA airfoil moving
at constant velocity, a configuration that is equivalent to a fixed airfoil subjected to
a uniform incoming flow. The numerical results obtained from these test cases are
discussed with reference to various literature works. However, a direct comparison
is not possible since the published studies, such as [1, 3, 8], consider different flow
regimes. To enable a meaningful comparison, the free surface flow around a NACA
profile is reproduced and simulated using the CFD finite volume code OpenFOAM [10].
While not intended as a full validation—since OpenFOAM involves a low-order solver
and handles two-phase flow—this comparison provides a useful reference to assess the
proposed approach’s ability to capture key features of free surface flow behavior.
The accurate simulation of free surface flows induced by moving bodies is essential for
numerous applications, including naval engineering and renewable energy technologies.
By advancing the state-of-the-art in numerical modeling of these flows, this work aims
to contribute to a better understanding of the underlying physical phenomena and
to provide a powerful tool for engineers and researchers working in this field. The
development of high-fidelity numerical solvers, such as the one presented in this thesis,
is essential for addressing the complex challenges posed by fluid-structure interactions
and for advancing the design of more eﬀicient and effective engineering systems.

1.1
Finite element software
To address the challenges outlined, we opted to employ Firedrake [4], an open-source
finite element framework specifically developed for the numerical solution of complex
Partial differential equations (PDEs). It is chosen for this project due to its flexibility,
high-order accuracy, and suitability for large-scale simulations.
One of the primary reasons for choosing Firedrake is its integration with PETSc,
which facilitates the eﬀicient handling of parallel computing [30].
This is crucial
for large-scale problems where computational resources need to be maximized to
solve complex systems in a reasonable time frame. PETSc allows Firedrake to scale
across multiple processors, providing the necessary infrastructure to solve problems
with thousands or even millions of degrees of freedom. This parallelization would be
particularly beneficial in HPC environments, ensuring that computational resources
are fully utilized, thus optimizing performance.
Firedrake supports quadrilateral and hexahedral elements and allows for high-order
polynomial basis functions, making it particularly suitable for capturing nonlinear
and variable physical phenomena with high accuracy and fewer elements. This con-
figuration is in full accordance with the prerequisites of the SEM and facilitated an
effective application of the method.
Another significant advantage of Firedrake is its use of the Unified form language
(UFL) for defining variational forms [31]. UFL simplifies the process of specifying
weak formulations, making it easier to implement complex mathematical models.
The combination of these features makes Firedrake an ideal choice for problems re-
quiring robust computational techniques, parallelism, high-order accuracy, and ease
of implementation. Additionally, the extensive documentation, coupled with an open,
supportive Slack channel, ensures that users can quickly resolve issues and stay up-
dated with the latest developments in the field. Recently, Visbech et al. [32] employed
this framework to develop a new parallel spectral element solver, FNPF-SEM, for
simulating linear and fully nonlinear potential-flow water waves and their interaction
with offshore structures. This work highlights both the advantages of using a SEM
and the flexibility of the Firedrake framework.

CHAPTER 2
Theory
This chapter outlines the theoretical foundation of the numerical strategy adopted
to solve the governing equations of the physical problem under investigation. The
selected approach is the SEM, a technique that combines the geometric flexibility
of the finite element method with the high accuracy of spectral methods. Owing to
its ability to deliver high-order approximations on complex geometries, SEM has be-
come a well-established tool in the numerical solution of partial differential equations,
particularly in fluid dynamics problems [7, 5, 33, 34].
The discussion includes the mathematical formulation of the method, its implemen-
tation in both mono- and multi-domain settings, and the analysis of convergence
and approximation errors, with special attention to the implications for the present
application.
2.1
Spectral element method
The SEM is a high-order numerical technique based on the approximation of the
solution using high-degree piecewise polynomial functions. It has been extensively
investigated in the literature; refers, for instance, to [5, 35, 7, 6]. The method offers a
favorable trade-off between accuracy and computational cost, especially when dealing
with smooth solutions and geometrically complex domains. This section introduces
the fundamental principles of the SEM and provides the key definitions and results
necessary for subsequent numerical developments.

There are many different ways to choose the discrete velocity and pressure spaces,
Vh and Qh, such that they are compatible with the discrete inf-sup condition (3.26).
The natural choice of using equal-degree polynomial spaces for both the velocity and
pressure fields typically leads to discrete function spaces that violate it. A distinction
must be made between triangular and quadrilateral elements, especially when adopt-
ing high-order methods such as the SEM. In the case of quadrilateral (or hexahedral
in 3D) elements, a common and effective choice is to use a staggered approximation,
where the velocity space Vh consists of continuous polynomials of degree p (e.g., Qp)
and the pressure space Qh consists of polynomials of degree p −2 (e.g., Qp−2). This
choice satisfies the discrete inf-sup condition for p ≥2 and helps to avoid spurious
pressure modes. The velocity degrees of freedom are associated with a grid of (p+1)2
GLL nodes, while for the pressure field, two interpolation strategies are typically em-
ployed: either the subset of (p −1)2 internal GLL nodes extracted from the (p + 1)2
grid, or a distinct set of (p −1)2 Gauss nodes. On the other hand, for triangular
elements, constructing compatible high-order spaces is more delicate and typically
requires the use of special mixed finite element pairs, such as the Taylor–Hood pair
Pk/Pk−1 for k ≥2, or other stable mixed formulations. It is important to distinguish
between the polynomial spaces Pk and Qk. The space Pk consists of all polynomials
of total degree less than or equal to k, and is typically used on triangular (or tetrahe-
dral) elements. In contrast, Qk includes all polynomials of degree at most k in each
coordinate direction separately (i.e., tensor-product polynomials), and is defined on
quadrilateral (or hexahedral) elements. As a consequence, Qk contains more degrees
of freedom than Pk for the same polynomial degree k. In the context of SEM, trian-
gular elements are less commonly used due to the diﬀiculty in constructing eﬀicient
and accurate high-degree polynomial bases on simplex domains. As a result, quadri-
lateral meshes are generally preferred for their tensor-product structure and superior
spectral accuracy.

Preliminary Validations
Before tackling the free-surface flow problem, it is crucial to validate the numerical
solver through a series of benchmark tests. This chapter presents a set of preliminary
cases designed to assess the accuracy and convergence properties of the SEM for
incompressible N-S equations.
The validation begins with a manufactured solution, confirming optimal convergence
rates in both L2 and H1 norms and demonstrating exponential accuracy with in-
creasing polynomial degree.
Subsequently, the lid-driven cavity problem is solved
and compared with reference benchmarks across various Reynolds numbers. Finally,
flow past a circular cylinder and an inclined NACA airfoil is analyzed. The pressure,
velocity, drag, and lift values in steady flow regimes are discussed. These tests estab-
lish confidence in the solver before applying it to more complex free-surface scenarios.

After validating the solver against a known analytical solution, as discussed in the

This is a well-established physical problem of fluid mechanics, where the N-S equations
are solved in a simplified domain without incorporating a free surface. Numerous
benchmark studies are available in the literature and some of them will be considered
to validate our results.
The reader must keep in mind that numerically stable steady solutions for lid-driven
cavity flows at high Reynolds numbers are notoriously diﬀicult to compute. This
diﬀiculty is primarily attributed to the challenge of resolving thin boundary layers and
the onset of flow oscillations, particularly when employing coarse meshes or low-order
discretization schemes. While several studies have successfully reported results up to
Re = 10,000, the work of Erturk et al. (2005) was among the first to present steady
solutions obtained using a finite difference method up to Re = 21,000 [46]. After
that, the limit was passed by Wahba (2012) who proposed a fourth-order accurate
central difference scheme and reported steady-state solutions for Reynolds numbers as
high as Re = 36,000 [26]. Nevertheless, obtaining reliable steady solutions at higher
Reynolds numbers remains a challenging and open problem in computational fluid
dynamics.

As anticipated, one of the key advantages of the SEM is its geometrical flexibility,
which enables accurate representation of complex domains. However, it is essential to
recognize that, when curvilinear boundaries are present, a second-order geometric ap-
proximation error may dominate if the geometry is represented using straight-edged
elements. As showed in Section 5.1, increasing the polynomial degree of the basis
functions can significantly reduce the approximation error (achieving spectral conver-
gence); however, this advantage is lost if the geometry is not accurately captured.
This chapter begins by presenting the transformation from non-curvilinear to curvi-
linear elements and describing the underlying theoretical framework. Subsequently,
numerical results for a steady flow around a cylinder are presented, with particular
focus on the drag and lift coeﬀicients. The associated errors are analyzed in relation
to CPU time, highlighting the advantages of high-order methods.
6.1

This section presents the methodology for constructing curvilinear elements in two di-
mensions using transfinite interpolation with linear blending, following the approaches
described in [23, 50]. For simplicity, the analysis will be presented for two-dimensional
triangular elements for a circular curved boundary. However, similar transformations
and analogous results can be extended to other parametrized curves, as well as to
three-dimensional or quadrilateral elements. The transformation of straight-sided el-
ements into curvilinear ones has been extensively discussed in the literature (see, e.g.,
[51, 24, 52, 25]); however, applications to free-surface flows are lacking. A novel con-
tribution of this thesis is therefore the use of curvilinear elements also in the context
of free-surface flow problems.

CHAPTER 7
Free Surface Problem
The free surface flow involves a fluid domain with a deformable boundary. In this
chapter, we present the mathematical formulation and numerical strategies employed
to simulate such flows, including the governing equations, relevant boundary condi-
tions, and discretization methods. As highlighted in the introduction of this thesis,
free surface problems have been extensively studied in the literature; nevertheless,
they remain a challenging topic in computational fluid dynamics due to their nonlin-
ear nature and the diﬀiculty in accurately capturing the interface dynamics. We begin
by introducing the governing equations and subsequently derive their weak formula-
tion. This is followed by a detailed description of the numerical procedure adopted
to solve the problem, including the mesh update algorithm. In the second part, a
comprehensive set of numerical results is presented and critically analyzed to evaluate
the performance and accuracy of the proposed method. The influence of key physical
and numerical parameters, such as the Froude and Reynolds numbers, as well as the
effect of damping, is thoroughly investigated. The steady free surface profile is then
compared with the solution obtained using a finite-volume CFD solver, showing good
agreement between the two and reinforcing the validity of the presented approach.

7.2
The complete numerical procedure
After having introduced the full set of equations and boundary conditions of the
system in this section the chosen numerical strategy is outlined. Several numerical
strategies can be employed to solve free surface problems. In this work, the interface
tracking approach has been adopted, which explicitly tracks the evolution of the free
surface over the iterations. This methodology has also been employed in previous
studies, such as [1, 3].
The objective of the numerical method is to find the steady-state solution of the prob-
lem. To determine when the steady state has been reached, a convergence criterion
based on a predefined tolerance is adopted. Specifically, the difference between two
successive solutions is evaluated at each iteration. Once this difference falls below the
prescribed threshold, the solution is considered to have reached steady state, and the
solver is terminated. The iterative procedure outlined below is repeated until this
convergence criterion is satisfied.
Considering that both the fluid and free surface domains are updated using pseudo
time-stepping, the numerical procedure can be outlined as follows:
1. Initialize the simulation with a flat free surface;
2. Solve the incompressible N-S equations within the fixed computational domain;
3. Extract the velocity field u = (u, v) along the free surface;
4. Solve the kinematic equation presented in (7.6) to find the free surface elevation
at the next pseudo-time step;
5. Update the computational domain and the entire mesh;
6. Redefine the surface boundary condition;
7. Verify whether convergence has been achieved. If not, return to Step 2 and
repeat the procedure; otherwise, terminate the algorithm.

ments can provide accurate representations of the surface while potentially reducing
the overall number of DOFs and the associated computational cost.
To enable this improvement, it is suﬀicient to adapt the numerical procedure intro-
duced in Section 7.2, taking into account that the DOFs must now also be repositioned.
In particular, step 4.1 of the algorithm — which originally involved constructing a
one-dimensional mesh along the free surface using the surface nodes — now builds a
high-order one-dimensional mesh that incorporates both the nodes and the internal
surface DOFs associated with the two-dimensional mesh.
Importantly, at each of
these points, the values of u, v, and η are known and can therefore be consistently
projected onto the new function space. Note that the pseudo-time step ∆t, defined
in 7.3, is reduced to prevent instabilities in the solution process.
With this modification, Equation (7.6) is solved within a high-order one-dimensional
function space. The resulting solution then contains the updated vertical position of
each DOFs located on the free surface.
The mesh update process, presented in Section 7.2.1, based on the binning strategy
remains unchanged, but it is now extended to include all DOFs, not just the nodes.
Consequently, a greater number of bins may be created for the same mesh, reflecting
the increased resolution introduced by the high-order representation. In Figure 7.4,
the non-equidistant nodes associated with a high-order curvilinear mesh are shown.
The figure illustrates the structure of a curvilinear discretization applied to a free
surface problem.

Before going further in the analysis of the results, it is important to recall that,
as shown in Section 7.3, high-order spectral element discretizations are capable of
accurately capturing the curvature of the free surface, even on relatively coarse meshes.
This is achieved by employing basis functions of higher polynomial degree, which
allow the representation of smooth curved boundaries without requiring a fine mesh
resolution.
To highlight this capability, Figure 7.14 shows a direct comparison between the results

nearly identical surface shapes. The differences become visible in Figure 7.15, which

of the free surface, while using a lower number of DOFs, potentially reducing the

7.4.2
Results for different Reynolds number
In order to investigate the effect of viscous forces on the free surface flow, a series
of numerical simulations is carried out by varying the Reynolds numbers, fixing the
Froude number to 0.5672. It is important to emphasize that lower Reynolds numbers
correspond to higher viscous forces, whereas for higher Reynolds numbers, the flow
is dominated by inertial forces. Moreover, it is well established in the literature that
the Froude number plays a crucial role in determining the behavior of free surface
flows. As reported in [55, 56], for Fr < 1, the flow is subcritical, while for Fr > 1, the
flow becomes supercritical. In the subcritical regime, the flow can be influenced by
both upstream and downstream conditions, and a train of periodic surface waves is
typically observed. Additionally, for this case, perturbations can propagate upstream.
On the other hand, in the supercritical regime, information travels only downstream,
and the free surface tends to follow the geometry of the objects [57].

7.4.3
Results for different Froude numbers
In order to investigate the influence of the Froude number on the flow characteristics,
different simulations are considered while keeping the Reynolds number fixed at Re =
1000, and varying the Froude number.
Considering that for Fr < 1 the flow is
classified as subcritical, it is particularly relevant to explore the distinct behaviors
corresponding to different Froude regimes.
Given the definition Fr = U/√gL, where U is the characteristic velocity, g the gravi-
tational acceleration, and L the characteristic length, it is evident that modifying the
Froude number—while holding u and L constant, is equivalent to altering the inten-
sity of the gravitational effects. Indeed, increasing the Froude number corresponds
to reducing the influence of gravity, while decreasing the Froude number enhances it.
Figure 7.20 and 7.21 show the free surface elevation profiles obtained for various
Froude numbers at a fixed Reynolds number Re = 1000.
Lower Froude numbers
are associated with smaller wave amplitudes and shorter wavelengths, resulting in
more frequent wave crests. In contrast, higher Froude numbers lead to waves with
greater amplitudes and longer wavelengths. These results are in agreement with those
presented in [56], where a linear analysis of steady water wave flows is carried out,
and in [55], where a fourth-order Runge–Kutta method is employed to solve the free
surface flow over an obstacle. Moreover, this behavior aligns with the interpretation
based on gravitational influence: a stronger gravitational effect (i.e., lower Fr) sup-
presses wave growth and promotes the formation of shorter, more frequent waves,
whereas a weaker gravitational influence (i.e., higher Fr) permits the development
of taller and more widely spaced waves. The reader should note that Figure 7.20
features a shorter computational domain compared to Figure 7.21. Specifically, in
the former, the horizontal extent is x ∈[−8.5, 11.5], whereas in the latter it spans
x ∈[−16.6, 27.5].
This observation makes it evident that the wavelength of the
free-surface waves is significantly larger for higher Fr numbers.

the surface flow as the depth increases. Specifically, when the airfoil is positioned
further from the free surface, its effect on the surface wave generation becomes less
significant, leading to lower wave amplitudes. Consider that the Reynolds number is
set to 4000 for both cases.

face waves. At the lower depth (d1), the wavelength is shorter, while at the higher
depth (d2), the wavelength is observed to be longer. This behavior is consistent with
theoretical expectations, where a shallower airfoil depth tends to produce a more pro-
nounced perturbation in the surface, resulting in shorter wavelengths. Conversely, as
the NACA airfoil moves further away from the surface, the magnitude of the pertur-
bation diminishes, leading to a corresponding increase in the wavelength.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
In this Master’s project, a new high-order spectral element solver is developed and
validated for the steady incompressible Navier–Stokes equations, with a focus on
free surface problems. The theoretical advantages of a high-order method are first
discussed, including a detailed analysis of its convergence properties and associated er-
ror estimates. The incompressible N-S equations are non-dimensionalized, their weak
form is derived and their mathematical properties are then briefly discussed. The
solver is implemented using Firedrake [4], an open-source framework that facilitates
the direct formulation and solution of variational (weak form) problems. Firedrake
automatically handles the construction and use of system matrices, and by selecting
high-order Lagrange basis functions, the implementation of a nodal SEM is made pos-
sible. A key contribution of this work is the development of a solver that incorporates
viscosity, improving upon previous inviscid approaches found in the literature [1, 3].
First, theoretical expectations regarding the convergence properties of the SEM are
confirmed through numerical experiments involving a problem with a known ana-
lytical solution. Then, prior to addressing free surface effects, the solver is validated
against several benchmark problems, including the lid-driven cavity, flow past a cylin-
der in a channel, and flow around a NACA airfoil in a fixed domain. Various physical
conditions are considered, such as different Reynolds numbers and angles of attack.
The results are validated numerically by comparison with those available in the liter-
ature [26, 47, 48, 29].
To accurately approximate curved boundaries within the computational domain, curvi-
linear elements are introduced. Building upon the methodologies presented in [23, 24,
50], a transfinite linear blending technique is employed to achieve a curvilinear ge-
ometric representation. The effectiveness of this approach is demonstrated through
numerical simulations of flow around a cylinder. The results include detailed analysis
of drag and lift coeﬀicients, illustrating how spectral convergence, compromised in
the presence of curved boundaries due to geometric approximation errors, is success-
fully restored. Furthermore, performance evaluations conducted on the DTU HPC
server highlight the computational advantages of employing high-order methods over
low-order schemes. These findings underscore the potential of curvilinear spectral el-
ement methods to deliver both high accuracy and eﬀiciency in simulating fluid flows
with complex geometries.
The second part of the thesis focuses on the analysis of free surface flows. The dy-
namic and kinematic boundary conditions which govern the free surface are derived
==PAGE:122==
102
9 Conclusion
and incorporated into the model. Since an iterative numerical approach is adopted,
a strategy for dynamically updating the mesh is implemented. The mesh nodes are
moved vertically, distributing the displacement of the free surface along the verti-
cal axis to preserve mesh quality.
While the initial test case addresses the flow
over a smooth bump, the primary investigation concerns the flow around an inclined
NACA airfoil. Several parameters are varied, including object depth, Froude number,
Reynolds number, and the presence of a damping zone. The results are presented, an-
alyzed, and qualitatively compared with reference studies available in the literature [1,
3, 9], confirming the expected flow behavior. The capabilities of the high-order solver
are demonstrated by showing how curvilinear elements can accurately approximate
the free surface. Indeed, the wave profile is well captured even with a very coarse
mesh, provided that a suﬀiciently high polynomial order is employed. This approach
represents an innovative aspect of the present work, as the combination of curvilinear
elements with free surface modeling has not been widely explored. Finally a compar-
ison is carried out against the solution computed using the open-source finite-volume
CFD toolbox OpenFOAM. A case with very similar flow conditions is solved, and the
results exhibit a clear alignment between the two solutions. Finally, the model is
extended to three dimensions to enable the simulation of more realistic scenarios.
Ultimately, the high-order methodology explored in this thesis opens avenues for
more accurate and eﬀicient simulation tools, supporting both academic research and
engineering practice in fluid mechanics.
By incorporating the viscous term, the solver represents an initial step toward simulat-
ing non-inviscid flows, demonstrating the potential of high-order methods combined
with curvilinear elements for free-surface flow modeling. This paves the way for vari-
ous future developments, which are outlined in the following section.
9.1
Future developments
The model presented in this thesis has some limitations, many of which are discussed
throughout the manuscript. In order to address certain gaps identified in the liter-
ature, additional assumptions have to be introduced. For instance, to account for
viscous effects, the analysis is restricted to low Reynolds number regimes.
There are several promising directions in which this work could be extended.
A
straightforward development would be to consider partially submerged bodies and
analyze the resulting free surface behavior in three dimensions.
The Wigley hull
case has been investigated by various researchers. For example, in [61], the authors
employ an Euler solver with an unstructured grid, while in [62], a stabilized semi-
implicit fractional step FEM is proposed.
Another valuable extension would be the incorporation of transient (i.e., time-dependent)
capabilities into the solver. This would enable the simulation of unsteady phenom-
ena. Moreover, if the goal is to simulate flows at higher Reynolds numbers, suitable
stabilization techniques should be considered. Among the various approaches, varia-
==PAGE:123==
9.1 Future developments
103
tional multiscale (VMS) methods offer a viable strategy, particularly when turbulence
modeling must be integrated with stabilization.
To accelerate convergence toward steady-state solutions, various numerical accelera-
tion techniques could be employed. For example, in [9], a method based on a quasi
free-surface condition is proposed, demonstrating that steady-state flow over a bump
can be achieved in only a few iterations.
