ABSTRACT 
 
 
Solar power has become a crucial part of the path towards the electrification of the devel-
oping world. The lack of national grid in certain regions and the weakness of the overall 
system, impel business owners to possess an additional generation scheme to cover their 
electricity needs. 
 
Due to the relevant role that the United Nations plays in developing countries, energy 
security is essential. The goal of the Green Energy Unit of the UNDP is to provide their 
Country Offices with specific solutions that will improve their current situation in relation 
to business as well as environment. 
 
This thesis is focused on four UNDP Country Offices, located in three different countries 
in Africa: Niger, Congo and Botswana. The project is developed in MATLAB. An energy 
assessment is carried out based on their current consumption pattern and suitable solar 
installations are designed based on the technical feasibility of the plant.

1   INTRODUCTION 
 
 
This chapter gives an outline of the background and motivation of the project. The main 
objective of the thesis is described, its scopes and limitations are also presented, along 
with the thesis layout. 
 
 
1.1 
Background and Motivation 
 
The United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) is the largest agency within the 
United Nations (UN). It works in nearly 160 countries and its main goal is to help the 
territories sustain development results. UNDP’s strength comes from having the trust of 
the developing countries and their governments, which is the reason why it is able to 
support governments to transform their renewable energy markets and implement poli-
cies that will promote green energy technologies. However, and precisely because one 
of UNDP’s most relevant targets is climate change, UNDP itself should lead by example. 
 
The importance of Environmental Sustainability has long been championed by the UN 
as reflected in Millennium Development Goals (MDG) [1] and the Sustainable Develop-
ment Goals (SDG) [2] agendas.  
 
In the wake of the Ebola crisis, the Country Office ICT Advisory Service (CIAS) unit 
leveraged its experience in implementing technical solutions in Country Offices (CO) to 
roll out solar solutions to the affected countries. Unreliable energy provision for the COs 
of Liberia, Guinea and Sierra Leone, due to grid outages and difficulties to transport fuel, 
became a major problem when coupled with increasing energy requirements to support 
the influx of additional crisis response staff.  
 
Due to the urgency of the matter, the CIAS partnered with a solar solution vendor to 
install solar systems. While these projects were hindered by the uncertainty and unprec-
edented nature of the crisis, it led to the creation of the Green Energy unit in order that 
any future project would follow a procedure that would ensure an optimal and feasible 
solution.

14 
The Green Energy unit bases its green energy project in the 7-step process shown in 
Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1: 7-step Solar Solution Process 
 
Out of the 157 UNDP Country Offices, 89 are enrolled in this project in which the final 
goal is to design an efficient solar system for the COs where one is required. Neverthe-
less, even the COs that choose to stay in the first step will have made an improvement, 
as they will be more aware of their energy consumption pattern. The Green Energy Unit 
supervises every single step, and is the sole responsible for the completion of the first 
two steps.  
 
The motivation and incentives behind this thesis are not solely economic or climate re-
lated. Being the UNDP a trusted global agency, the showcasing of these solutions could 
mean the beginning of new paths leading to technical and economic development of cer-
tain regions. 
1.2 
Project Objectives 
 
The main objectives of this thesis are: 
• Energy consumption analysis for the selected Cos 
 
 Consumption data is filtered to omit erroneous data 
 Frequency and duration of power outages are examined   
  
• Climate analysis for the different locations (irradiation, temperature, etc.) 
 
• Design of a technically feasible solar installation based on the previously obtained 
data and the equipment options provided by the Long-Term Agreement (LTA) 
vendors.

1.3 
Scopes and limitations 
 
The scope of the project are listed below, 
 
• The study is carried out for 4 significantly different scenarios based solely on the 
local conditions; which include client requirements, local weather, building char-
acteristics and consumption patterns. The selected system will remain operational 
if any of the aforementioned criteria vary. 
 
• Proposed installations consist of technology that is increasingly popular. Such 
systems will prevail in the near future when renewable energies become relevant 
energy producers. 
 
Although the research has reached its aims, there were some unavoidable limitations, 
 
• Consumption data retrieved from the monitoring device was recorded each hour, 
at minimum. Thus, depending on the circuit, minutes-lasting grid outages that 
may have occurred during that hour were not always clear. Besides, there is no 
way to know what generation source provided the load; hence, the daily or inter-
mittent use of the generator is hard to predict. 
 
• The optimal solution is found by performing a technical feasibility analysis, not 
considering the economical aspect of the project. 
 
• Significant part of the project was dependent on CO response; acquisition of ac-
curate data was not always possible. 
 
• Client (CO) requirements had to be met, even though other possible solutions 
may have been more optimal.

2 METHODOLOGY 
 
This chapter explains the process followed for every phase before determining the final 
system proposal. The study is developed solely in MATLAB. 
 
2.1 
Energy assessment 
 
Data on office energy consumption is collected from the Power Consumption Monitoring 
and Measuring (PCMM) device. These devices are installed inside the building’s breaker 
panel, the Current Transformers (CT) are clamped around the main incoming wires to 
provide the electrical reading of the amount of electricity the building, or the specific 
circuit, is using.  
Inside the breaker panel, the CTs are directly connected to a Measuring Transmitting Unit 
(MTU). The MTU sends the information to the data-receiving hub, which analyzes and 
stores real-time electricity data. This device can then be connected to any computer and 
the data can be accessed through an online dashboard. 
Recorded information corresponds to the energy consumed by the measured circuit, to-
gether with the date and time when the measurement is taken. Table 1 serves as an exam-

Knowledge of the length of the grid outages can help when designing a solar system. 
Depending on the circuit that is being measured, it may be clearer if there has been any 
loss of power or not. When the consumption drops significantly it can be assumed that 
the grid suffered an outage and that the generator is not able to cover all the needs.  
The average and maximum consumption per hour is calculated as well. If the consump-
tion patterns change considerably from one yearly season to another, the analysis is sep-
arated. As for the maximum values, the evaluation is done in order that random peaks that 
may have occurred are not considered. A maximum value is stored only when the con-
sumption of the following hour is at least 90% its value. This last calculation is used for 
comparing the daily consumption with the potential production of the designed installa-
tion per hour. 
 
2.2 
Design of the installation 
 
This section explains the calculation followed until deciding the optimal installation size 
and characteristics. 
 
2.2.1 
Optimal PV angle 
 
PV modules work best when their cells are perpendicular to the Sun’s incoming rays. The 
module intercepts the maximum amount of solar energy when it is maintained perpendic-
ular to the sunlight. Nevertheless, because of the constant shifts of the solar angle, keeping 
the module at right angles to the sun is not possible with static mounted PV panels.  
 
Perez et al. [3] studied the impact of the module angle in relation to the power output. In 
order to keep the panel perpendicular to the sun’s daily east to west motion, a tracking 
device is used. The use of this technology has been proved to increase the output from 
10% to 40% on a yearly basis. 
 
Otherwise, seasonal manual adjustments considering the sun’s north to south shift can 
also increase the output by up to 10%. 
 
Module angle is obtained based on the assumption that the panel will be at right angles to 
the solar rays at solar noon. The calculation method used involves two parameters, 
 
 Local latitude (L) 
 Declination of the sun (D)

2.2.2 
Irradiation 
 
Solar irradiation is a key parameter to consider when determining the size of a solar sys-
tem. It is the sun’s radiant energy incident on a surface of unit area, which is represented 
in units of Wh/m2 or kWh/m2. 
 
Whether a solar tracking device is used or not, it is most likely that the PV modules will 
be mounted at a certain angle with respect to the horizontal plane.  
 
HelioClim is the tool used to download irradiation data for the COs for a period of one 
year. The data is recorded hourly and specified for the corresponding latitude and longi-
tude coordinates.  
 
The exported values of irradiation correspond to the global irradiation measured for a 
horizontal plane. Thus, a conversion needs to be made in order that the accurate irradia-
tion levels for the selected module tilt are obtained. 
 
The solar radiation received at a tilted surface is formed by three components:  
 Direct radiation coming from the face of the Sun 
 Diffuse radiation derived from the sky 
 Reflected radiation coming from the surroundings

2.2.3 
Ambient Temperature 
 
One of the parameters that affect the efficiency of a solar panel is temperature. Once the 
irradiation on the modules is known, ambient temperature corresponding to the specified 
time frame needs to be determined as well. 
Nonetheless, available data this time is not as extend as for the irradiation. NASA pro-
vides information about the maximum and the minimum temperature related to each 
month. These values correspond to the Typical Meteorological Year (TMY), which is a 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
collection of selected weather data related to a specific location and deduced from a long 
data bank [10].  
Due to the limited amount of data available, a procedure that will estimate the develop-
ment over time [11] of the ambient temperature is followed. The estimate will be in in-
tervals of an hour, it will also assume that every day of the month will have the same 
development of the ambient temperature over time. In other words, it is assumed that the 
model that is followed below shows the development of one day of each month, and that 
this day will be used to represent that specific month. 
 
The assumptions of the model are as follows,

2.2.4 
System Design 
 
Once the external parameters affecting the efficiency of the solar system are calculated, 
the next step is to design and size an installation that will fulfil the clients’ requirements 
and that will be technically feasible.  
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
The details of the scenarios will be presented in the next Chapter. However, in order to 
explain the reason why the following approach was chosen, it must be said that the re-
quirements led to the objective of designing a system that would ensure that it would 
produce the amount of energy it was designed to produce, to not fall into over- or under-
production.  
Several aspects must be taken into account to ensure an optimal sizing and design: 
 Selection of PV panels and inverter. 
 Choice of PV panels configuration and size. 
 Compatibility between modules and inverter. 
 Calculation of the predicted performance of the installation. 
Inputs to this procedure are the consumption data obtained by following Section 2.2.1. 
Thus, the method will consist of trying to meet the consumption requirements. The coun-
tries where the selected COs are located do not have any tariffs or compensation/deduc-
tions for sending excess power back to the grid. On the one hand, if the system is over-
sized, the result would not be optimal since the surplus of energy would be wasted; on 
the other hand, if the system is too small, it will not be able to feed the required load. 
Hence, the objective of this scheme is to achieve a system that will have a relation of 
produced and consumed energy as close to 1 as possible.

3 RESULTS 
This chapter contains the different case studies analysed following the methodology ex-
plained in Chapter 2. The selected COs where the simulations have been carried out are: 
Niger, Congo (Brazzaville), Botswana (Kasane) and Botswana (Maun).  
 
3.1 
Niger 
3.1.1 
Current energy situation in Niger 
 
Ability to cover the energy demand is the essence of present-day civilization. The func-
tion of energy in the process of evolution of a country is well known and access to elec-
tricity is key for a sustainable development and declination of poverty.  
Niger is a continental West African country located south of the Sahara desert. It is the 
largest country in West Africa with an area of 1,267,000 km2 and a population of 17.138 
million. Climate in Niger is predominantly tropical, with one dry season from October to 
May and a rainy season for May to September.  
Niger has significant energy potential, rich and varied, that is weakly exploited. The re-
sources gather biomass (main source of households for cooking), uranium, mineral coal, 
oil, natural gas, hydroelectricity and solar energy [12]. Over 70% of Niger’s primary con-
sumption comes from biomass, being solar energy less than 1% of the mix. As for house-
hold’s consumption, 97% originates from biomass, only 2% have access to grid electricity 
and no solar energy is used. 
Despite this potential resources, access to electricity remains a challenge for the Nigerien 
government. Energy consumption per capita is one of the lowest of the world. This is due 
mainly to the limited access of Nigerien households to modern energy. 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
 
Figure 3: Distribution of Final Primary Energy consumption in Niger 
Evidently, the exclusive use of conventional energy coming from firewood constitutes an 
overexploitation of the resource. Regarding the renewable energy sector, there is a lack 
of specific regulation. Nevertheless, the government’s willingness to create optimal con-
ditions for the development of this sector covers the issue.  
3.1.2 
Energy assessment 
The UN House of Niger has, in close collaboration with CIAS Green Energy Solutions, 
taken steps towards implementing the path towards a solar solution. The promotion of 
sustainable development constitutes an essential responsibility for all UN premises, and 
opting for a solar system is a viable pathway to achieve this aim. 
Energy security is fundamental to ensuring business continuity, as well as crisis prepar-
edness. Niger CO currently relies on a diesel generator as a back-up power supply and 
the local grid as the main source of electricity, which suffers monthly outages that can 
last for days. These blackouts strongly occur during the hot season (from April to July) 
and can extend for periods from days to weeks. Niger depends on limiting countries; any 
disruption to the production of electricity in Nigeria is reflected in every region of Niger.  
The back-up generator is unable to continuously withstand electrical loads during periods 
of severe power outages. Niger is a country with significant sun and heat, especially dur-
ing the hot season, and the generator cannot be run constantly without a period of pause, 
to allow the cooling of the engine. Thus, during this break, the UNDP CO slows down its 
activities. On the other hand, although Niger is an oil-producing country, the price of 
diesel is of 538 fca (approximately 1 USD per liter), on average.

Optimal PV angle for Niger CO 
In order to maximize the energy generated by the system, one of the main factors to con-
sider is the PV angle. The procedure followed in this part is explained more in depth in 
Section 2.2.1. 
One of the assumptions used in this method is the fact that the panel will be at a right 
angle to the sun at solar noon. The two parameters needed are the local latitude and the 
declination of the sun. Local latitude in the Niger CO located in Niamey is 13.5178. The 
declination of the sun is obtained by equation (2.5), specified for each day of the year.  
Once these two parameters are determined, the optimal PV angle is found by means of 
equation (2.6), the result gives the angle between the horizontal plane and the panel and 
its value is 13.57°. The roof slope has been calculated to be of approximately 10° with 
respect to the horizontal plane, so in order to avoid installing mounting structures that 
would probably result in a higher angle than the optimal one, and since the difference is 
significantly small, it was decided to proceed with no structures. 
Hence, the chosen PV angle for the installation is 10°, which will be used in the following 
sections to define the final solution.

3.1.6 
Final system design 
Once the external parameters affecting the efficiency of the solar system are calculated, 
the next step is to design and size an installation that will fulfil the clients’ requirements 
and that will be technically feasible.  
The requirements of the representatives of Niger CO are that the system should supply 
electricity to the circuit of the data center. Since no economic analysis can be carried out, 
and the country does not have any tariffs or compensation/deductions for sending excess

2012 [19].

4 CONCLUSION 
This chapter serves as a summary and conclusion of the study. 
 
4.1 
Conclusions 
The objective of this thesis was to design solar PV installations for the selected UNDP 
Country Offices based on the technical feasibility aspect. In order to find the optimal 
solution, understanding of the consumption pattern and identification of potential issues 
such as power outages are essential.  
The factors and parameters used to determine the characteristics of the resulting system 
included, 
- 
Demand data from the specific Country Office, which was filtered and ana-
lyzed and was also used to identify grid instabilities that would affect the out-
come of the solar system. 
- 
Determination of the optimal PV angle in order to maximize the output of the 
modules. 
- 
Climate conditions such as ambient temperature and irradiation to the surface 
at the optimal PV angle established above. 
- 
Clients’ requirements. 
- 
Verification of the connection and operation possibility between the elements 
forming the solar system. 
The analyzed regions count with a high solar resource available, however, other deter-
mining factors such as available space or grid instability can hinder the outcome of the 
project. 
4.2 
Future work 
In a general perspective, and because the analyzed region is still in the path towards 
growth and development, business continuity and energy security are key to achieve this 
goal. Since the UNDP advocates for sustainable development and it has the power, the 
ability and probably the obligation as well of promoting access to clean energy, the next 
step would be that the agency leads by example in this matter. 
In relation to this study, next step would be the optimization of the system considering 
the economical aspect.
