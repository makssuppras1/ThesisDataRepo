ABSTRACT 
 
Denmark as an archipelago nation posseses many potential seaweed species to be utilized. One 
of them is Fucus vesiculosus, a brown seaweed species which is widely available and have 
been proven to contain high amount of phenolic compounds. These phenolic compounds are 
able to substitute synthetic unsustainable antioxidants and to fulfill consumers’ increasing 
demand to use only natural ingredients in food and personal care products. However, efficient 
extraction method is needed for these natural antioxidants in order to compete with the 
synthetic ones in terms of price and to use them in sustainable way. Pressurized liquid 
extraction (PLE) exhibits great extraction efficiency even when using environmental friendly 
solvents.  
 
This study investigated effect of several PLE parameters, i.e. extraction time [3-7 minutes], 
extraction temperature [125-140ºC] and ethanol concentration in solvent [40-80% v/v in 
water], on extraction yield and several antioxidant properties (total phenolic content/TPC, 
DPPH radical scavenging activity, ABTS radical inhibition and ferrous chelating ability). The 
effect of each parameter was studied by Box Behnken experimental design and modeled with 
response surface method. Mathematical models’ adequacies were evaluated using the 
coefficient of determination (R2) and lack-of-fit test with a 5% level of significance. 
 
Analysis of variance of the models showed that ethanol concentration was the most influential 
parameter for all antioxidant properties. The constructed models were statistically significant 
and had R2 between 0.817 and 0.890. Optimal condition to achieve highest amount of phenolic 
content with average antioxidant activities were found at 137.18ºC, 58.65% v/v ethanol and 
4.68 minutes. This optimum condition was validated with triplicate experiments which gave 
little error between actual and predicted values, thus showed the robustness and accuracy of 
the models. The obtained value for yield, TPC, DPPH inhibition at 0.5 mg/mL, ABTS 
inhibition at 0.5 mg/mL and metal chelating ability at 1.5 mg/mL were 31.16% dry basis, 3.69 
g GAE/100 g dry seaweed, 87.7%, 16.2% and 46.3%, respectively. This extract was observed 
to have higher yield and TPC, same radical scavenging activity and lower metal chelating 
ability compared to conventional solvent extraction with the same ethanol concentration 
despite much shorter extraction time and lower solvent requirement. Moreover, HPLC analysis 
identified gallic, protocatechuic and gentisic acid in the extract, while LC-DAD-ESI Q-ToF-
MS successfully detected one phlorotannin derivative with 4 phloroglucinol units.

1. Introduction 
According to Ferdouse et al. (2018), global seaweed production in 2015 reached 30.4 million 
tonnes. From this number, approximately 12 million tonnes were processed in seaweed 
industry with 85% of them became food products for human consumption. Nowadays, the main 
ingredients produced from seaweed are carrageenan, agar and alginates. However, seaweed 
also contain a lot of bioactive compounds such as proteins, lipids, pigments, iodine, 
halogenated compounds and phenols (Holdt and Kraan 2011). These compounds are very 
interesting to be exploited for its functionality in food or pharmaceutical products. 
 
Denmark as an archipelago nation between North and Baltic Sea has many potential seaweed 
species to be utilized. There are around 350-500 species in the northern area with high salinity 
to less than 50 species in the southern with low salinity (Ferdouse et al. 2018). One species that 
grows in Denmark sea area is Fucus vesiculosus which is categorized as brown seaweed 
species. Although there is no big scale cultivation and harvesting of this species recorded yet 
(Jacobsen et al. 2019), availability of this species is very high since it is the most common 
species for canopy forming in Baltic Sea (Wikström and Kautsky 2007). This species contains 
high amounts of phenolic compounds with antioxidant activity, with content varying from 
<0.4-12.2% of dry weight gallic acid equivalent (GAE) (Holdt and Kraan 2011).  
 
These phenolic compounds are possible alternatives for replacing synthetic antioxidants in 
delaying lipid oxidation and prolonging food shelf-life. Nowadays, natural antioxidants are 
more preferred than synthetic ones which are petroleum-based. Although these phenolic 
compounds could be as potent as synthetic ones, they exist amongst other compounds which 
might not be active in managing oxidation. Therefore, selective extraction of these phenolic 
compounds is needed to obtain antioxidant compounds with high activity. It is also very 
important to optimize the extraction to get as much antioxidant as possible because natural 
antioxidants are more expensive than the synthetic ones (Berdahl et al. 2010). 
 
Currently, main conventional technique used for extracting bioactive compounds from 
seaweed is solvent extraction (SE), where seaweed is exposed to organic solvents e.g. hexane, 
ether, chloroform, acetonitrile, benzene and ethanol. Afterwards, solvents can be separated 
using evaporation step (Joana Gil-Chávez et al. 2013). This traditional method has low 
efficiency and needs large volumes of environmentally unfriendly organic solvents (Saha et al. 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
2017). Not only toxic for the environment, these solvents are also toxic for humans 
consumption that is why separation is important if the products are used for food application. 
Despite many negative sides, SE method still has some superiorities in terms of low processing 
cost and ease of operation (Joana Gil-Chávez et al. 2013). 
 
In order to extract these antioxidant compounds, biorefinery concept can be applied to utilize 
all the valuable constituents in seaweed with economic feasibility and sustainable approach 
(Torres et al. 2019). Pressurized Liquid Extraction (PLE) offers as one of the alternative 
technique for the biorefinery steps with lower environmental impact as it reduces toxic and 
hazardous solvents usage (Jacobsen et al. 2019). PLE utilizes temperature and pressure above 
solvents’ boiling point and below critical point to change the physical properties dramatically, 
especially polarity. With PLE, water can extract hydrophobic secondary metabolites (Plaza and 
Marina 2019). Some generally recognized as safe (GRAS) solvents, such as ethanol, ethyl 
acetate, ethyl lactate, or D-limonene, can be mixed with water to change the polarity and 
increase the extraction yield but still considered as environmentally friendly (Alvarez-Rivera 
et al. 2020). PLE also gives faster process for extraction compared to traditional SE (Marcus 
2018). 
 
Until now, to the best of author’s knowledge there has been no publications on application of 
PLE for extracting phenolic compounds from F. vesiculosus. In this research, effect of some 
PLE parameters, i.e. extraction time, temperature and ratio of ethanol to water in solvent, on 
total phenolic compounds (TPC) value in extract will be investigated. Furthermore, PLE 
process will be optimized with response surface methodology based on the single parameters 
effect results to obtain highest yield of phenolic compounds with best antioxidant properties. 
Antioxidant properties that will be optimized are TPC, dry matter yield, radical scavenging 
activity 
(measured

condition will be validated by a triplicate experiment and compared with SE results in terms of 
all antioxidant properties. Finally, extracts from optimized condition will be characterized with 
High Pressure Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) for phenolic acids identification and Liquid 
Chromatography with Mass Spectrometer (LC/MS) for phlorotannin identification.

2.1 Fucus vesiculosus 
2.1.1 Properties of F. vesiculosus 
F. vesiculosus is a brown seaweed species which reproduces sexually. It is a dioecious species, 
meaning it has distinct male and female individual organisms; however, their gametes have a 
short life span. Vegetative reproduction by reattachment of adventitious branches is also 
possible but only on very low probability. This species is able to float for about 100 days due 
to enlarged air cavities in their vesicles as well as reproductive receptacles (Rothausler et al. 
2015). 
 
F. vesiculosus plants consist of holdfast (a root structure for adhesion to seabed), leaf-like 
blades with some air bladders, and stipe which connects holdfast with blades. There are also 
some receptacles with conceptacles at the tip of the blades with forms of swollen areas 
(Hermund 2016). This species has high moisture content, ranging from 68-87% (Holdt and 
Kraan 2011). The physiological appearance of the plants is illustrated on Figure 1 while

2.1.2 F. vesiculosus antioxidant compounds 
Since F. vesiculosus is classified as brown algae, it contains a lot of antioxidant compounds in 
many forms, such as phenolic compounds, carotenoids, fucoidans and laminarins. Carotenoids 
are linear polymers that are soluble in lipid which able to harvest energy or light. This group 
of compounds is located in the thylakoid membrane of seaweed together with chlorophylls. 
Fucoidans are a group ranging from typical fucoidans to heteropolysaccharide-like-fucans with 
low-sulfate content. They can be found within fibrillar cell walls and intercellular spaces on F. 
vesiculosus. Laminarins range from branched (soluble) and unbranched (insoluble) 
polysaccharide from β 1–3, β 1–6 glucan. They contain around 84-94% of sugar, 6-9% uronic 
acids and some mannitols. Laminarins act as energy reserve polysaccharides and are stored in 
vacuoles inside the cells of brown seaweeds (Jacobsen et al. 2019).

Phlorotannins are a group of polyphenolic compounds called tannins which are formed by 
phloroglucinol (1,3,5-trihydroxybenzene) polymerization (Martinez and Castaneda 2013). 
According to Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen research (2013), F. vesiculosus also contain simple 
phenolic acid compounds such as gallic acid, protocatechuic acid, gentisic acid, chlorogenic 
acid, vanillic acid and caffeic acid. Phlorotannins and phenolic compounds are accumulated in 
a membrane-bound cytoplasmic vesicles called physodes on the surface of this seaweed 
species. Phlorotannins are able to form a complex with alginic acid in the cell walls when 
physodes fuse with it. Most of physodes are located in the outer cortical layer. The highest 
concentration of phlorotannins was found in the basal part of F. vesiculosus, while the lowest 
was in mesocosm receptacles. These compounds are important for protection from UV and a 
chemical defence towards herbivores (Singh and Sidana 2013; Jacobsen et al. 2019). 
 
Characteristics of phlorotannins group are (a) more than 10% solubility in water; (b) molecular 
mass range from 250 to 1,738 Da; (c) have 8 aromatic rings per 1,000 mass units; (d) have 14 
to 24 phenolic hydroxyl groups per 1,000 mass units; and (e) polymeric structure formed from 
phenolic oxidative coupling (Martinez and Castaneda 2013). These compounds differ from 
each other either by the types of linkage between phloroglucinol units (PGU) or by the means 
of one or more additional phloroglucinol units (Pal Singh and Bharate 2006). Phlorotannins 
can contain short polymerized chain of PGUs (low molecular weight phlorotannins with 4 to 8 
PGUs) but also highly polymerized up to 16 PGUs (Kirke et al. 2017). There are three ways of 
polymerization of PGUs, i.e. phenyl linkage (C-C) in meta position (named fucols group); aryl-
ether linkage (C-O-C) in ortho, meta, or para position (named phloroethols group); or mixture 
of them (named fucophloroethols group) (Martinez and Castaneda 2013). 
 
Some types of phlorotannins own some additional functional group that differentiate them. 
Fuhalols have at least one additional OH-group in para or ortho position. If every 
phloroglucinol unit in fuhalol contains OH-group, it is clustered into special class called 
isofuhalol.

2.1.3 Characterization of phenolic compounds 
Due to the complex structure, identification of individual species of phlorotannin is difficult. 
However, in 2017 Kirke et al. succeeded to separate phlorotannins from purified and low 
molecular weight phlorotannins isolated from methanol extract of F. vesiculosus using 
Quadropole Mass Time-of-flight Mass Spectrometry (Q-Tof-MS). The spectrometer is 
operated with negative electrospray ion mode (ESI-) from molecules of 300 to 3000 m/z and 
equipped with Ultra Performance Liquid Chromatography/Tandem Quadrupole Mass 
Spectrometry (UPLC-TQD-MS/MS). They found out that F. vesiculosus contains 162 isomers 
of phlorotannins ranging from 3-16 PGUs (373 to 1986 Da). Most dominant phlorotannins 
present in it are polymers of 4 and 5 PGUs, accounting for 20 and 42% respectively of the total 
phlorotannin abundance. 
 
Heffernan et al. (2015) identified 144 isomers of phlorotannins between 3 and 14 PGUs in F. 
vesiculosus’ phlorotannin-enriched fraction. The significant amount of them are phlorotannins 
with 4 to 9 PGUs. Hermund et al. (2018) utilized Ultra High Performance Liquid 
Chromatography coupled to a Diode Array Detector and a Quadrupole Time of Flight Mass 
Spectrometer (UHPLC-DAD-QTOFMS) to observe phlorotannins in ethyl acetate fraction of 
F. vesiculosus. They managed to find 13 phlorotannin isomers ranging from 374 to 870 Da. 
 
The amount of phenolic compounds in extract can be quantified by Total Phenolic Compound 
(TPC) assay or usually called Folin-Ciocalteu assay. Folin-Ciocalteu reagent contains tungsten 
[W(VI)] and molybdenum [Mo(VI)] oxides which can be reduced in the presence of phenolic 
antioxidants forming a greenish-blue phosphate-tungsten [W(IV)] and phosphate-molybdenum 
[Mo(V)] and can be measured using a ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis) spectrophotometer (Stengel 
and Connan 2015). However, since non-phenolic compound with reducing capability can also 
react, this method is nonspecific and can lead to overestimation (Jacobsen et al. 2019). 
According to Connan et al. (2004), the amount of phenolic compounds in F. vesiculosus varies 
through the year in a pattern with the maximum content is achieved on summer months.  
 
Antioxidant property to inhibit lipid oxidation by scavenging radical can be measured with 
DPPH˙ (2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl radical) test. This assay measures radical scavenging 
activity of a stable radical (DPPH˙) with purple chromogen which could be reduced to the 
corresponding pale yellow hydrazine by antioxidants (Blois 1958). Sometimes radical 
scavenging capacity can be expressed as effective concentration (EC) for obtaining 50% 
==PAGE:14==
2 
 
inhibition, which shows the concentration of the antioxidant needed to get 50% inhibition 
(Jacobsen et al. 2019). Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen (2013) discovered that water extract of F. 
vesiculosus showed high DPPH radical scavenging activity although its total phenolic value 
was low. This fact suggests that nonphenolic antioxidant compounds were also extracted, such 
as small molecular weight polysaccharides, pigments, proteins, or peptides. 
 
Another assay to measure radical scavenging activity is using ABTS (2,2′-azino-bis(3-
ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid). The principle of this assay is by measuring 
decolouration of ABTS+ radical cation in the presence of antioxidant from blue/green colour. 
The ABTS+ ion can be generated by a reaction of ABTS and potassium persulfate. The 
advantage of this method is that ABTS cation is soluble in both aqueous and organic solvents 
so that it is capable of measuring both lipophilic and hydrophilic algal extracts (Stengel and 
Connan 2015). Díaz-Rubio et al. (2009) measured ABTS of an antioxidant extract from F. 
vesiculosus produced by two stages of SE, first with acidic methanol/water (50:50 v/v, pH 2) 
and then continued by acetone/water (70:30 v/v). Both extract solutions were mixed and gave 
ABTS EC50 value of 0.19±0.02 g/g. Another study by Ferreira et al. (2019) measured a value 
of ABTS EC50 of 0.134 mg/mL for rich phenolic extract obtained from water extraction of F. 
vesiculosus. 
 
In addition to electron transfers, antioxidant can inhibit oxidation of oil and fat by other 
mechanisms such as metal chelation. Transition metals such as iron and copper are the most 
important prooxidant in food system because they exist in essentially all biological tissues. 
Metal chelating antioxidants are able to control transition metals (Decker et al. 2010). One 
method to measure metal chelating power is by utilizing ferrozine (3-(2-pyridyl)-5,6-diphenyl-
1,2,4-triazine-4’,4”-disulfonic acid sodium salt).  Ferrozine can react specifically with ferrous 
ions to form a magenta coloured complex. This method measures difference of free ferrous 
ions amount available in solution in the presence of antioxidant and without antioxidant by 
measuring the absorbance of the solutions (Mladěnka et al. 2010).  The ferrous ions complex 
can be chelated by ferrous ion chelating antioxidant which reduces the colour intensity 
(Hermund 2016).

2.1.4 Application of F. vesiculosus phenolic compounds extract 
Commercial application of F. vesiculosus phenolic compounds extract as antioxidant has not 
been reported. However, there are already many experiments of application of the extract in 
laboratory scale. Hermund (2016) reported application of water extract and ethanol extract 
fractionated with ethyl acetate in milk with fish oil enrichment. At concentration of 2 g/kg, the 
ethanol extract was able to reduce the formation of volatile oxidation products during storage 
compared with the control. It also prevented the risk of tocopherol loss during storage. They 
also discovered when the extracts were used for mayonnaise made from milk and fish oil, water 
extract were more efficient than the ethanolic one. This conclusion was based on decrease of 
peroxide value, significant inhibition of certain volatile compounds formation from 
eicosapentaenoic and docosahexaenoic acid. 
 
Another experiment of the extract application is incorporation into granola bars (Karadağ et al. 
2017). They found out that ethanol extract and acetone extract were more efficient than water 
extract to reduce the formation of lipid hydroperoxides and prolonging formation of most 
volatile oxidation products. The extracts were also able to reduce presence of large unprotected 
oil pools inside granola bars which mean they had emulsifying properties. 
 
F. vesiculosus extracts were also once incorporated into cod mince as well as a 20% cod liver 
oil-in-water emulsion in a publication of Jónsdóttir et al. (2016). They applied ethyl acetate 
extracts which were proven to efficiently reduce some properties of lipid oxidation i.e. peroxide 
value, thiobarbituric acid reactive substances, protein carbonyls and rancid odour during 
storage in the cod mince experiment. However, they promoted oxidation in the emulsion 
system especially in forming rancid odour and peroxides, which they suggested were caused 
by Fe which is a strong prooxidant. 
 
The extracts has also been tested in a facial cream formulation (Hermund et al. 2018a). The 
results showed that ethanol extract could reduce the peroxide oxidation rate from the control 
after 42 days of storage. Water extracts had lower radical scavenging activity which leads to 
lower efficacy of oxidation inhibition.

2.2 Pressurized Liquid Extraction (PLE) 
2.2.1 PLE Principle 
In PLE, high pressure and temperature are applied to solvent in subcritical area (above the 
boiling point but below the critical point) to maintain solvent in liquid state. As illustration, 
phase diagram of water is shown in Figure 3. Water is in liquid state at temperature and 
pressure between the triple point and critical point. It will evaporate into vapor when the 
temperature is increased above boiling point (right side of the graph). At pressure and 
temperature above critical point, water will be in supercritical state. Subcritical area 
(highlighted by green colour on graph) is the area where high pressure is applied to maintain 
solvent in liquid state although it is above the normal boiling point of water at 1 atm (100°C).  
 
 
Figure 3. Phase diagram of water with subcritical area highlighted in green 
 
The most frequently used solvent for PLE is water due to its availability, nontoxic, non-
flammable and environmentally friendly properties. Below its boiling point, water is not 
suitable for extracting most of the hydrophobic secondary metabolites from their matrix 
because of its polarity. However, in subcritical area properties of water change drastically and 
==PAGE:20==
14 
 
become more favourable for recovering these hydrophobic compounds (Plaza and Marina 
2019). 
 
Water and ethanol at high temperature and pressure are able to dissolve less polar compound, 
since the hydrogen bonding which causes intermolecular interactions becomes less 
pronounced. The polarities of these solvents diminish with increasing temperature, and become 
more drastic near the critical point (Marcus 2018). This fact is caused by London dispersion 
forces (induced dipole-induced dipole forces) which gets more favourable than hydrogen 
bonding and thus water turns into less polar solvent. The viscosity of water also decreases at 
higher temperature following Arrhenius-type equation. Increasing temperature of water also 
decrease surface tension of water, for example at 200°C the surface tension is about 50% 
compared to at 25°C and keeps decreasing until reaching zero at critical point. The low surface 
tension at higher temperature improved sample wetting which creates larger contact area 
between solvent and sample matrix (Plaza and Turner 2017).  
 
Mechanism of extraction compounds from samples matrix during PLE is described in five 
steps: (1) extracting solvent moistens the matrix and then the compounds; (2) the analytes 
desorbs from the matrix (can include breakdown of chemical bonds or not); (3) the analytes 
dissolves in the extraction solvent; (4) dispersion of the compounds out from the matrix; (5) 
the compounds diffuse into the nearest solvent layer around the matrix and finally reach the 
bulk solvent (Alvarez-Rivera et al. 2020). 
 
During those steps, solvent properties at subcritical area can enhance the extraction efficiency 
through improving solubility, mass transfer effect and also increasing surface equilibria 
disruption. Modification of solvent properties at elevated temperatures increases capacity of 
fluid to solubilize hydrophobic compounds. Better diffusivity of solvent enables better 
penetration of solvent into matrix particles to reach the analytes to be extracted. The high 
temperature and pressure condition of PLE could disrupt surface equilibria. The thermal energy 
supplied can overcome the solute-matrix interaction by van der Waals forces, hydrogen 
bonding, dipole attraction of the analyte molecules or active sites in the matrix. Therefore, 
higher temperature will decrease cohesive force between solute molecules and adhesive force 
between solute and matrix molecules by lowering the requirement of activation energy to 
==PAGE:21==
15 
 
desorb. On the other hand, high temperature also facilitates convection transfer process from 
matrix to pressurized hot solvent besides of diffusion (Teo et al. 2010). 
 
The selection of solvent type is very important in PLE because the principle of extraction is 
that solvents will dissolve analytes with more or less the same polarity. Some aspects that can 
be considered for solvent selection are solubility characteristic of the desired analyte, the 
analytes diffusivity in the solvent and the characteristics of the sample. Solvent for extracting 
analytes at low concentrations must also be chosen carefully since the rate of extraction is not 
affected by the analyte concentration but rather by the rate of mass transfer. Therefore, the 
solvent need to have chemical properties that ensures solvation and the release of the analyte. 
Nowadays, green solvents are more preferred considering the economy, safety and 
sustainability aspects. These solvents are less toxic, non-harmful and easy to remove or 
recover. For example, simple alcohols (ethanol and methanol) or alkanes (heptane and hexane) 
are more sustainably preferable compared to dioxane, acetonitrile, acids, formaldehyde and 
tetrahydrofuran  (Mustafa and Turner 2011).   
 
Mixture of solvents is an option to enhance the extraction yields by improving the solubility of 
the desired analyte and increasing its interaction with the extraction solvent. In a dual mixture, 
one solvent could act to improve analyte solubility, while the other could enhance the analyte 
desorption. Water is usually used in the mixture as a matrix and also matrix-analyte (hydrogen) 
bond destroyer. Moreover, some studies suggest that solvent mixtures such as methanol–water 
or ethanol–water solutions are environmentally favourable compared to pure alcohol or 
propanol–water mixtures (Mustafa and Turner 2011). Adding ethanol in water also decrease 
the critical temperature and pressure of the solution (Marcus 2018). 
 
There are two types of equipment for PLE, i.e. dynamic (continuous flow) and static (batch) 
system. In dynamic system, solvent is pumped continuously at high pressure into an extraction 
vessel where the analytes matrix is located. The solvent is heated through a coil heating device 
before entering the vessel, and then collected in another vessel. The pressure inside the 
extraction vessel is controlled with a pressure restrictor. On the other hand, in static system 
solvents is pumped or transferred manually into an extraction vessel equipped with inlet and 
outlet valves. This vessel is heated using an oven, heating mantle, or heating tape. After 
extraction, outlet valve is opened and the solvent with extracted compounds is collected in 
another vial. The advantage of using static system is simplicity and easiness of operation, but 
==PAGE:22==
16 
 
the drawback is longer residence time which is dangerous for thermolabile compounds. There 
is also a risk of tube clogging in outlet tube of dynamic system since the solvent with extract 
compounds will be cooled down to certain temperature where the analytes can precipitate 
inside the tube (Plaza and Turner 2017). Difference between static and dynamic system for 
PLE is illustrated in Figure 4.

2.2.2 Parameters Affecting PLE  
2.2.2.1 Temperature 
Due to improved solvent properties, higher temperature could improve wetting of sample 
matrix, facilitating initial desorption of analytes from sample matrix by disruption of analyte-
matrix interactions (especially hydrogen bonds and other dipole-dipole forces), faster 
diffusivity, and altered solubility of analytes. However, there are three main drawbacks of using 
high temperature, namely decreasing selectivity of desired compounds (co-extraction of many 
undesired compounds), degradation of some thermolabile analytes, and unwanted chemical 
reaction in sample matrix. Degradation of some compounds at elevated temperature is 
sometimes unavoidable but can be minimized by optimization. Unwanted chemical reaction 
could form toxic and unwanted compounds. Therefore, optimization of operating temperature 
condition is very important to ensure high extraction yield with minimum unwanted products 
(Teo et al. 2010; Plaza and Turner 2017). 
==PAGE:23==
17 
 
2.2.2.2 Pressure 
Pressure is important to maintain the solvent in liquid state and might help to wet the sample 
matrix. High pressure can boost the analyte solubility and desorption kinetics from sample 
matrix by three ways: (1) forcing solvent within matrix pores to contact with analyte and extract 
them; (2) disrupting matrix which enhance mass transfer rate of analyte from sample to solvent; 
and (3) breaking air bubbles found within matrix that hinder the solvent to reach the analyte 
(Mustafa and Turner 2011). However, it often has low effect on the increasing extraction 
efficiency and water properties. Thus, a moderate pressure at 1.5 MPa to 8.5 MPa is typically 
used, even though higher pressure until 15 MPa also sometimes applied (Teo et al. 2010; Plaza 
and Turner 2017). 
 
 
2.2.2.3 Extraction Time 
In static mode, extraction time is important to be optimized to prohibit degradation of 
thermolabile compounds and undesirable chemical reactions. After reaching equilibrium 
between solvent and matrix, no more analytes will be extracted into the solvent anymore so the 
extraction process can be stopped. In a dynamic mode, this equilibrium can be avoided 
theoretically. Here extraction time is controlled with flow rate of solvent. Higher flow rate will 
improve the extraction yield per unit time if the extraction rate is controlled by solubility rate. 
However, if desorption rate control, extraction yield will be independent of flow rate. Main 
drawbacks of using high flow rate is low concentration extract solution and higher cost for 
removal of solvent (Alvarez-Rivera et al. 2020). 
 
 
2.2.2.4 Solvent to Sample Ratio 
Solvent to sample ratio is another important parameter since the higher the value is, more 
fraction of analytes can be extracted without replacing with fresh solvent. Nevertheless, higher 
value means more solvent to be heated and separated after extraction because the analyte will 
be less concentrated. During long concentrating step, the risk of compound degradation is 
higher in addition of longer whole process time. In conclusion, solvent to sample ratio needs 
to be optimized in order to get the highest possible extraction yield but at the same time should 
be as small as possible to reduce solvent usage, minimize energy application and get higher 
concentration extracts (Plaza and Turner 2017). This fact correlates with the partition-
==PAGE:24==
18 
 
equilibrium constant and solubility of compounds at elevated temperatures, which can prohibit 
highly concentrated samples or low solubility compounds to completely extracted in the limited 
volume of solvent used (Teo et al. 2010). 
 
 
2.2.3 Advantages and Disadvantages of PLE 
Compared to the conventional technique of solid liquid extraction, PLE has some advantages 
in terms of faster process, reduction of the amount of solvents used and lower cost for solvents. 
Moreover, replacement of organic solvents with water for extracting functional food or 
pharmaceutical ingredients is beneficial for health safety and also greener for the environment 
(Zakaria and Kamal 2016). However, Plaza and Marina (2019) suggested that environmental 
effect need to be identified quantitatively by Environmental, Health and Safety performance 
factor (EHS) or Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) as no studies has been done to calculate it. With 
these tools, the sustainability of whole process of PLE can be identified because even though 
water is the most environmentally sustainable solvent, sometimes at the end of extraction 
process clean up steps and/or water elimination are needed. 
 
Besides of all the advantages, PLE also owns some disadvantages in term of selectivity, 
degradation of thermolabile compounds, and undesirable chemical reactions. At higher 
temperature, diffusivity of contaminant compounds which are not desirable also become higher 
which means they will be co-extracted into solvents. Therefore, it might need further clean-up 
steps after extraction. Optimization is also needed to avoid thermolabile bioactive compounds 
degradation. At subcritical region, the dissociation constant (Kw) of water increases from 1.0 x 
10-14 at 25°C to a maximum value of 4.9 x 10-12 at 250°C, which mean the pH of water decreases 
from about 7.0 to 5.5. This implies to stronger ionic strength of hydronium (H3O+) and 
hydroxide (OH-) ions at elevated temperature. These ions are catalysts for some unwanted 
reactions, such as Maillard, caramelization, thermal oxidation, and hydrolysis of 
polysaccharides and proteins into smaller molecules. Stronger hydronium ions strength and 
higher pressure also increase corrosion rate, so corrosion resistant metal alloy need to be 
applied for instrumentation material if the temperature used is near the critical point (Plaza and 
Turner 2017; Plaza and Marina 2019). Another drawback of PLE is it requires expensive 
equipment to handle pressurized fluid. This high investment could be compensated if efficient 
processing could be accomplished (Mustafa and Turner 2011).

2.2.4 PLE Application for Phenolic Compounds Extraction 
Currently there has been no publications yet about application of pressurized liquid extraction 
for phenolic compounds from F. vesiculosus. Heavisides et al. (2018) used PLE for F. 
vesiculosus but they extracted bunch of bioactive compounds into crude mixture without any 
optimization for phenolic compounds. Nevertheless, they figured out that temperature gave 
significant positive effect on the extraction yield of the crude mixture. 
 
Tierney et al. (2014) did PLE to extract polyphenols from four species of macroalgae from 
Irish sea including Fucus spiralis, a quite closely related species with F. vesiculosus. They 
compared PLE with SE using ethanol, acetone and water as solvent. They concluded that PLE 
gave higher yield than traditional solvent technique. Water as solvent gave higher yield than 
other solvents proved that there are a lot of hydrophilic compounds in F. spiralis. In terms of 
TPC, the highest value was achieved by PLE with acetone solvent, followed by ethanol and 
water as the lowest one. However, in terms of DPPH radical scavenging activity, the value of 
all of the solvents were quite similar, even between the PLE and SE. 
 
In different experiment, Sánchez-Camargo et al. (2016a) tried to optimize PLE process from a 
brown seaweed species called Sargassum muticum in terms of phlorotannin and phenolic 
compounds yield, antioxidant activity and TPC. Ethanol concentration was the most significant 
variable to all the parameters, while temperature also had quite important significance except 
for antioxidant activity. Extraction yield increased with higher temperature and more water in 
the solvent. Despite the low yield, ethanol was more selective towards the extraction of active 
polyphenols which gave higher TPC and antioxidant activity. Thus, they concluded that the 
optimum parameters were 160°C and 95% ethanol. 
 
In another study, Sánchez-Camargo et al. (2016b) used different approach (Hansen solubility 
modelling) to optimize the best parameters to extract polyphenols from Cystoseira abies-
marina, a brown algae species. With this theoretical approach, they concluded that temperature 
does not give a strong effect on the solubility of phlorotannins with 3-14 PGUs. The most 
suitable solvent theoretically was pure ethanol at low temperature (25°C). However, validation 
work with experiment demonstrated that pure ethanol at 100°C and pressure of 10.3 MPa gave 
==PAGE:26==

the highest selectivity for extracting phlorotannins. They assumed that the difference came 
from unconsidered transport phenomena in the theoretical model.

3. Materials and Methods 
3.1 Raw Material  
Brown seaweed F. vesiculosus were harvested on May 2018 from Bellevue beach, 
(55º46’17.4"N 12º 35’48.4"E), north of Copenhagen, Denmark. The samples were frozen at 
-20ºC prior to experiment. For this experiment, the seaweed was freeze dried in Christ Freeze 
Dryer Beta 1-8 (Germany) for 2 days and then powdered with Waring blender (VWR) and 
sieved to remove large particles. The powdered seaweed was stored at -20ºC freezer after being 
purged with nitrogen to avoid oxidation. 
 
 
3.2 Phenolic Compounds Pressurized Liquid Extraction (PLE) 
3.2.1 Single Parameter Effects Screening 
Pressurized Liquid Extraction (PLE) were performed with Dionex ASE 350 Accelerated 
Solvent Extractor (Thermo Scientific). For each experiment, 1 g of dried F. vesiculosus powder 
and 1 g of Ottawa sand were mixed and loaded into a 10 mL extraction cell equipped with a 
cellulose filter at its bottom. Extractions were done by varying one parameter at a time in 
triplicate experiment at operating pressure of 10 bar. Variation in parameters started with 
temperature (75, 100, 125, 150 and 175°C) with keeping the other parameters constant (time 
extraction of 5 minutes and 80% v/v ethanol in water as solvent). Total phenolic compounds 
(TPC) of the extracts were measured with protocol mentioned in Section 3.3. Temperature 
which gave highest TPC value was chosen as constant parameter for extraction time variation 
(5, 10, 15 min). Lastly, temperature and extraction time which contributed to highest TPC were 
kept constant for ethanol concentration variation (40, 60, 80, 100% v/v in water). Each 
experiment used 50% rinsing volume, purge time of 100 s and 250 mL glass collection vial 
with septum on its cap. Samples were moved to 50 mL centrifuge tubes and stored at -20ºC 
freezer prior to characterization.

3.3 Total Phenolic Compound (TPC) Measurement 
250 µL of each PLE extract was diluted with ethanol 20 times (4.75 mL ethanol) and stored in 
-20ºC freezer prior to analysis. Since SE extracts were more diluted than PLE, they were diluted 
by 5 times with ethanol. An aliquot (100 µl) of the diluted extract was mixed with 750 µL of 
Folin–Ciocalteu reagent (10% v/v in water) in a plastic microcuvette. The mixture was 
incubated for 5 minutes and then 750 µL of sodium carbonate (Na2CO3, 7.5% w/v in water) 
was added into the mixture and stirred in order to mix them. This mixture was incubated at

4. Results and Discussions 
4.1 Single Parameter Effect Screening 
4.1.1 Effect of Temperature 
Effect of extraction temperature on the amount of phenolic compound in extract is shown in 
Figure 6. It appears that TPC value reached maximum at 125ºC (2.82±0.1 g GAE/100 g dry 
seaweed) with lower values at lower and higher temperature points tested. There has been no 
other publications yet on similar study, but Vo Dinh et al. (2018) observed the same trend when 
doing subcritical water extraction to another species of brown seaweed (Saccharina japonica). 
They found a maximum value at 200ºC after varying temperature from 100-250ºC with 25ºC 
increment. In agreement with that result, PLE with water on red seaweed Hypnea musciformis 
reached the highest TPC at 210ºC because at higher temperature, polyphenol compounds 
degrade into soluble ones and reversibly bonded phenolic compounds were broken because of 
protein precipitation (Pangestuti et al. 2019). The difference in optimum temperature on these 
two studies with this study might come from the different solvent used, where they utilized 
pure water and this study used water-ethanol mixture. The effect of interaction between 
temperature and ethanol concentration on TPC value will be explained further in Section 4.2.3.

In a conventional SE, optimal temperature for extracting phlorotannin from F. vesiculosus was 
found at 25ºC. The authors explained that this temperature was high enough to improve 
extraction yield but low enough to prevent degradation of the phenolic compounds (Catarino 
et al. 2019). However, the extraction time for PLE is far shorter than conventional SE, which 
in turn can elevate the optimum temperature since the contact time of phenolic compounds 
with high temperature is short enough to degrade them. Since the highest TPC was achieved at 
125ºC with lower value at lower and higher temperature tested, more optimization was done 
around this temperature level. Increment of 25ºC used in this single parameter effect was still 
too wide to determine the optimum temperature. Therefore, response surface optimization was 
done with low value of 110ºC, central point of 125ºC and high value of 140ºC. 
 
 
4.1.2 Effect of Extraction Time 
Longer extraction time appears to decrease phenolic content as seen in Figure 7Figure 7. The 
shortest extraction time which was 5 minutes gave higher TPC (2.82±0.1 g GAE/100 g dry 
seaweed) than 10 and 15 minutes. Ferreira et al. (2019) did a SE experiment at 120ºC to extract 
phlorotannins from F. vesiculosus and discovered that TPC value increased until 2 hours of 
extraction and continued to decrease after that. They suggested that after 2 hours, the rate of 
phenolic compounds extraction was surpassed by other substances such as polysaccharides. 
This fact was also strengthened by another study which conclude that 78% of the total phenolic 
content can be extracted in 1 hour (Koivikko et al. 2005). PLE utilized high pressure which 
enhance extraction efficiency by improving diffusivity of solvent into seaweed matrix particles. 
This high pressure could also disrupt cohesive force between solute molecules and adhesive 
force between solute and matrix molecules which increase extraction rate. Thus, maximum 
TPC value on PLE could be achieved within 5 minutes, compared to several hours on 
conventional SE.  
 
Ferreira et al. (2019) also thought that small variation of TPC value with time and temperature 
variation was caused by the size of phlorotannins in F. vesiculosus. Most of the phlorotannins 
in this species are less than 1.2 kDa which do not require long time to transfer to the extract. 
Phenolic compounds are also quite sensitive to degradation at prolonged time of exposure with 
high temperature. Thermal resistance of phenolic compounds decreases with an increasing 
degree of polymerization (De Paepe et al. 2014) which confirms that phlorotannins (polymer 
==PAGE:40==
34 
 
of phloroglucinol) are prone to degradation when extracted at longer time and thus decrease 
the TPC value. For that reason, shorter time than 5 minutes should be investigated for a 
hypothesis of giving higher TPC value due to less phenolic compounds degradation. In 
contrary, extraction time longer than 5 minutes should also be examined to observe whether 
the highest TPC had been achieved at this time. Based on these, low value of 3 minutes, central 
point of 5 minutes, and high value of 7 minutes were chosen as variation on response surface 
experiments.

4.1.3 Effect of Ethanol Concentration 
Figure 8 shows the effect of ethanol concentration in solvent to total phenolic amount in 
extracts. The highest value of TPC was gained at 60% v/v ethanol in water with value of 
5.17±0.28 g GAE/100 g dry seaweed. Different result was observed by Sánchez-Camargo et 
al. (2016a) where the response surface gave 95% ethanol as optimum for extracting phenolic 
compounds with PLE from brown seaweed species Sargassum muticum. The difference 
probably caused by different phenolic compounds composition between these two species. The 
maximum TPC value from S. muticum was 9.4 g GAE/100 g with total phlorotannin content 
of 0.5018 g PGE/100 g measured using 2,4-dimethoxybenzaldehyde (DMBA) assay. Total 
phlorotannin in F. vesiculosus from DMBA method analysed by Ferreira et al. (2019) was 0.56 
g PGE/100 g extract. It shows that S. muticum contains more phenolic compounds in other 
==PAGE:41==

forms than phlorotannin as also stated by Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen (2013). Another study 
mentioned that pure water is better for phlorotannin extraction with conventional SE than 80% 
ethanol in water (Koivikko et al. 2005).  However, it must be noted that ethanol physical 
properties change a lot in subcritical condition, and it behaves differently at high pressure and 
temperature. The physical properties of ethanol solution in subcritical area might be similar to 
the best solvent for SE of phenolic compounds from F. vesiculosus which was 70% acetone in 
water (Koivikko et al. 2005).

Another interesting result to be highlighted was that the TPC value at 60% ethanol 
concentration was much higher than other concentration (about two times). It might prove that

4.2 Response Surface for Extraction Optimization 
4.2.1 Statistical Analysis and Model Fitting 
Experiment results of antioxidant characteristics with independent variable variations are 
shown in Table 8. Overall analysis of variance (ANOVA) was done to screen the significance 
of each independent variable and their derivations for all models built as shown in Table 9. 
Using P value of 0.05 as limit of statistically significance, terms of (E*t), (T*T), (t*t) and (T*t) 
were not statistically significant to the models produced. The models were refitted without 
these effects to obtain more precise estimate of the model parameters. Although time was also 
not statistically significant, this variable was still included in the model to be optimized. This 
ANOVA analysis also shows that ethanol concentration was the most significant parameter 
affecting the overall studied antioxidant properties.  
 
Analysis of variance for the second-order polynomial models for all antioxidant properties are 
shown in Table 10. P-value for each model was lower than 0.05 which shows that all models 
were statistically significant. On the other hand, the small values of F-value for lack of fit in 
each property and their corresponding p-values which were higher than 0.05 showed that lack 
of fits, relative to pure error, were not significant. The coefficient of determination (R2) of the 
models were in range of 0.817 to 0.890. This fact indicated that the models produced were 
adequate to accurately predict all antioxidant properties (yield, TPC, DPPH, ABTS and metal 
chelating ability) with small errors.

4.2.2 Effect on Extraction Yield 
Response surface graph and pareto analysis of each independent variable to extraction yield is 
displayed in Figure 9. Highest extraction yield could be achieved by higher extraction 
temperature and lower ethanol concentration. High temperature can enhance yield by 
improving wetting of sample matrix, disrupting analyte-matrix interactions and boosting 
diffusivity. However, applying high temperature to achieve high yield could result in co-
extraction of some undesired compounds with the phenolic compounds (Plaza and Turner 
2017). Pareto analysis indicates that E*E term was the most significant factor affecting yield 
with negative effect, followed by E*T with negative effect and temperature with positive effect. 
The other parameters were not significantly affecting yield. Several studies on response surface 
observed the same phenomena. Linear, quadratic and cross-product forms of ethanol 
concentration and linear form of temperature were observed to be significantly affecting PLE 
yield of phenolic compounds from olive pomace (Pavez et al. 2019). Tripodo et al. (2018) 
reported increasing temperature gave significant positive effect and quadratic form of ethanol 
concentration was statistically significant with negative effect for PLE yield of phenolic 
compounds from goji berry. In another study, temperature was found to be the only significant 
parameter to PLE yield of phenolic compounds from jabuticaba skin when pressure, extraction 
time and temperature were varied (Santos et al. 2012).

4.2.3 Effect on Total Phenolic Content 
Three-dimensional response surface for the influence of each independent parameter on TPC 
value of extract together with its pareto analysis are depicted in Figure 10. In order to obtain 
highest TPC, high temperature and low ethanolic solution were preferred. Interaction between 
temperature and ethanol concentration was the most statistically significant parameter with 
negative effect. Hence, increasing temperature on low ethanol concentration can raise TPC 
value, while increasing temperature on high ethanol concentration will reduce TPC value. This 
fact was also discovered by Howard and Pandjaitan (2008) while studying PLE of flavonoid 
from spinach. They examined that TPC linearly increased in ethanolic extracts over the 
temperature range of 90 to 170ºC, and then declined from 170 to 190ºC. In water extracts, TPC 
kept increasing linearly over the temperature range of 110 to 190ºC. Quadratic form of ethanol 
concentration and temperature were also significant for affecting TPC. Extraction time was not 
a significant parameter as also observed by Luthria (2012).

4.2.4 Effect on DPPH Inhibition Activity 
As shown in Figure 11, the only significant parameters affecting DPPH scavenging capability 
are ethanol concentration with positive effect and temperature with negative effect. Therefore, 
maximum DPPH activity can be achieved when higher ethanol concentration and lower 
temperature were applied to the seaweed powder. This profile can be explained by lower 
selectivity of extraction in higher temperature at the temperature range of 110-140ºC and lower 
ethanol concentration in solvent. Although TPC value for these extracts were high, water in the 
solvent enabled co-extraction of some water-soluble compounds from the matrix. TPC assay 
might also detect other compounds than phenolic that have reducing capabilities such as 
laminarin and fucoidan. Laminarin and fucoidan capability of inhibiting DPPH radical was 
found to be lower than phenolic compounds extracted here, with inhibition of 1.09% for 
laminarin and 66.13% for fucoidan at a concentration of 1 mg/mL after 1 hour of reaction 
(Moroney et al. 2015). 
 
The same fact was discovered when extracting phenolic compounds from beetroot leaves 
where ethanol/water mixture gave less antioxidant reducing capacity than pure ethanol 
==PAGE:48==
42 
 
(Battistella Lasta et al. 2019). Reducing DPPH activity trend when escalating temperature was 
also observed by Kamali et al. (2018). When increasing temperature from 60-90ºC in PLE 
using water, DPPH EC50 value lowered from 0.491 to 0.302 mg/mL. The same trend was 
observed for increasing temperature from 50-70ºC in PLE with methanol as solvent, with 
DPPH EC50 reduction from 0.493 to 0.305 mg/mL. Raising temperature further than these 
values started to give a rise of DPPH EC50 value which mean less radical scavenging activity, 
with value of 0.451 mg/mL for PLE with water at 120ºC and 0.454 mg/mL for PLE with 
methanol at 90ºC. Variation of temperature employed in this study was quite narrow and might 
depict only the declining DPPH activity after the highest optimum point.

4.2.5 Effect on ABTS Inhibition Activity 
Effects of each independent variable on ABTS radical scavenging activity is represented in 
Figure 12 together with the pareto chart to show their significances. The pareto chart shows 
that ABTS was affected by all parameters significantly. Temperature gave negative effect on 
the ABTS inhibition activity. When Ferreira et al. (2019) extracted bioactive compounds from 
==PAGE:49==
43 
 
F. vesiculosus using SE with water as solvent, they also observed lower antioxidant activity for 
hot-water extracts. They concluded that the decreasing activity was caused by phlorotannin 
degradation because of high temperature and long extraction time.  
 
Comparison of ABTS and DPPH scavenging activity for the same extract in Table 8 showed 
that ABTS inhibition activity was always lower than DPPH inhibition activity. Imbs et al. 
(2014) also noticed the same profile while extracting fucose-containing sulfated 
polysaccharides from F. vesiculosus. The difference between ABTS and DPPH scavenging 
activity became larger on higher TPC value. For example, one of their sample with TPC value 
2.1 mg PGE/g had DPPH inhibition activity of 39.1% and ABTS inhibition activity of 10.1%. 
The possible reason for this was antioxidant compounds in the extracts were not very reactive 
towards ABTS radical cation.  Ilyasov et al. (2020) compared several antioxidant capabilities 
to reduce ABTS radical and found out that there is a huge variation even for compounds with 
a little different in chemical structure. They also stated that some antioxidants react 
incompletely on the 6 minutes of incubation, even some antioxidants still demonstrate gradual 
ABTS radical inhibition after 30 minutes.

4.2.6 Effect on Metal Chelating Ability 
Figure 13 shows three-dimensional response surface graph of effect of extraction time, ethanol 
concentration and temperature on metal chelating ability with the pareto plot of these variables. 
Ethanol concentration and interaction between ethanol concentration and temperature were 
significantly affecting metal chelating ability. Highest amount of metal chelating could be 
accomplished with lower temperature and lower ethanol concentration (more water in solvent). 
Water extract of F. vesiculosus was demonstrated by several studies before to have higher metal 
chelating ability than ethanolic extract (Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen 2013; Hermund et al. 
2018a). 
 
If Figure 13 is compared with Figure 10 and Figure 11, extract with high TPC value or DPPH 
scavenging activity was not a good metal chelating agent. This discovery was in match with 
studies of Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen (2013) and Wang et al. (2009) who could not correlate 
metal chelating capability with TPC and DPPH through principal component analysis. This 
analysis also indicated that phenolic compounds were not the major contributors to the metal 
chelating properties (Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen 2013). Phlorotannins were also confirmed 
to be a bad metal chelating agent by analysis on F. vesiculosus ethanolic extract fractions 
produced by partitioning (Wang et al. 2012). Hence, metal chelating abilities of the extracts 
produced in this study might come from co-extracted compounds in low ethanolic solvent. 
There were still contradictory information in literatures regarding metal chelating capacities of 
polyphenols because some studies reported high metal chelation by polyphenols (Wang et al. 
2009). A good metal chelator forms σ-bonds with metal which can reduce redox potential and 
thereby stabilizing the complex formed (Terpinc et al. 2012). Yuan et al. (2005) listed some 
functional groups that indicates a good metal chelating agent, i.e. –OH, –SH, –COOH, –PO3H2, 
C═O, –NR2, –S– and –O–. Phlorotannins have only hydroxyl groups among these potential 
functional groups which explains the low metal chelating ability.

4.2.7 Optimization with Pareto Front 
Pareto front method was chosen for optimization in order to pick the best solution from all set 
of potential solutions. These sets of solution were generated by JMP software which gave 
highest value of desirability for each parameter and can be seen in Appendix Table C.7. 
Potential solutions were selected from these solution sets based on mixture plots and parallel 
plot. Mixture plot uses greyscale to show top potential solutions across all evaluated weight 
objectives combinations. Horizontal axis of this plot is the weight of one criterion with a scale 
of zero to one. Darkest colour on the plot shows highest desirability for the selected weight of 
criteria set. On the other hand, parallel plot visually illustrates the trade-offs of one solution 
over another in terms of the scaled criteria values. Best values of each criterion are on the top 
of the plot. Flatter lines in the graph indicate less trade-off between the criteria. All potential 
solutions from these graphs were observed in the scatter plot of pareto front. For objectives of 
maximum value, the best solution is located on the upper right side of the graphs which 
indicates highest values for each parameter, while for minimum value it is plotted on the lower 
left sides. 
 
==PAGE:52==
46 
 
In this study, objectives of optimization were maximum values of TPC, DPPH and metal 
chelating ability. Yield and ABTS were not considered on the optimization since extraction 
yield had the same profile as TPC while ABTS profile was almost similar with DPPH activity. 
As the aim for this study was to extract maximum amount of phenolic compounds from F. 
vesiculosus, weight of this objective was emphasized more than other objectives. 
 
First mixture plot as shown in Figure 14 was produced with no emphasize in metal chelating 
(weight = 0). For maximum value of TPC without focusing on other parameters, weight value 
of one for TPC and zero for DPPH was selected. Solution set number 51 gave highest 
desirability and thus this set was chosen as the first potential solution. While more weight was 
put to DPPH (DPPH weight value of 0.4 and TPC weight value of 0.6), solution set number 43 
had highest desirability and could be considered as the second potential solution.

of TPC always had a trade-off of low DPPH values. Contrarily, high values of DPPH had a 
trade-off of low metal chelating activity. The best set of solution from this graph was indicated 
with the flattest gradients of line (red line) and hence this set was chosen as the third potential 
solution.

4.2.8 Validation of Optimized PLE Process 
Optimized condition obtained from the mathematical model was validated by performing 
triplicate PLE experiments. The results of this validation were represented in Table 12, as well 
as comparison with the predicted value from the models. The validation suggested that the 
mathematical models produced were quite accurate and robust to predict all antioxidant 
properties with little error. These errors might come from the low R2 of the models which show 
the uncertainty of the models. There was also limitation of the PLE equipment used where 
decimal values could not be inputted, consequently extractions were done at temperature of 
137ºC and time of 5 minutes. 
 
The PLE yield of this optimized condition (31.1% dry weight) was higher than polyphenols 
yield of PLE from Fucus spiralis using 80% ethanol in water (23.67% dry weight) (Tierney et 
al. 2013). The discrepancy might come from the different composition between different 
species and temperature that they used (100ºC) which was lower than the one used in this study. 
The amount of water in the solvent used in this study was also higher, which enabled co-
extraction of some water-soluble compounds. In comparison, solvent extraction with the same 
ethanol concentration yielded nearly the same amount of extract. This fact shows the 
superiority of PLE compared to SE in terms of yield because SE requires longer extraction time 
(48 hours for SE compared to 5 minutes for PLE) and higher solvent requirement (solvent to 
solid ratio of 50 mL:1 g for SE compared to 17 mL:1 g for PLE). Even though PLE requires 
energy to heat and pressurize the mixture, SE also requires energy to agitate the mixture for a 
long time. SE with 80% ethanol produced less extract than SE with lower ethanol at optimized 
concentration as also suggested by (Monteiro et al. 2020). 
 
Phenolic content of the extract was lower than the value that was reported by Hermund et al. 
(2018a) (16.5 g GAE100 g) who applied SE with 80% ethanol for extracting polyphenols from 
F. vesiculosus harvested from the same place as this study. The lower phenolic content can be 
explained by the difference of harvesting month of the seaweed, where this study used seaweed 
harvested in May whereas the former harvested the seaweed in September. This argument was 
also proved by TPC value obtained from SE experiment in this study with the same procedure 
as the reference which gave lower value. Connan et al. (2004) justified that TPC value of F. 
vesiculosus varied during the season with the highest value achieved in early autumn 
(September). On the other hand, SE using the same solvent as PLE gave nearly the same TPC 
value, again confirming the advantage of PLE.

Radical scavenging ability of the phenolic PLE extracts in this study were lower than some 
literatures. This statement was acquired by comparing EC50 value of both DPPH and ABTS.   
Hermund et al. (2018a) recorded DPPH EC50 value of 3.7 µg/mL for F. vesiculosus extract 
from SE with 80% ethanol, while Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen (2013) carried out SE with 
96% ethanol and got DPPH EC50 value of 9.9 µg/mL. For ABTS, Ferreira et al. (2019) 
obtained EC50 value of 0.134 mg/mL for extract produced by SE with water. This study 
reported higher EC50 value for both stable radicals which mean more antioxidant was needed 
to achieve the same inhibition level. The variation might be resulted from the lower selectivity 
of PLE compared to SE which was already shown by low DPPH inhibition activity for sample 
with high yield and TPC value. Tierney et al. (2013) observed higher DPPH EC50 value for 
PLE ethanolic extracts from F. spiralis compared to the SE ones, which was also noticed in 
ABTS EC50 value in this study.  
 
Extraction with pressurized liquid was also found to lower the metal chelating ability compared 
to SE. The EC50 value of extract from SE was reported to be 87.7 µg/mL for 80% ethanolic 
extract (Hermund et al. 2018a) which was quite similar to the result of the same experiment in 
this study. Moreover, SE extract using 58.65% ethanol (optimized ethanol concentration) was 
found as a worse metal chelator than the one extracted using 80% ethanol as observed in the 
EC50 value. Berdahl et al. (2010) mentioned that some good natural chelators are citric acid, 
phospholipids and amino acid/peptides/proteins. Phospholipids are best extracted using organic 
solvent with high polarity such as ethanol, acetone and ethyl acetate (Zhou and Rakariyatham 
2018). Therefore, SE extract using 80% ethanol had highest metal chelation ability because 
more phospholipids and peptides with similar polarity extracted. PLE samples might had low 
metal chelating ability because large molecules of phospholipids and peptides require longer 
times to be extracted. Compared to another F. vesiculosus extract obtained by PLE, metal 
chelating activity at concentration of 1.5 mg/mL in this study (34.6%) was higher than value 
reported by Hermund (2016) which was 7.5%. The discrepancy was caused by higher ethanol 
concentration used in the literature which extracted less polysaccharides and non-optimized 
parameters application.

4.3 Identification of Phenolic Compounds and Phlorotannin 
4.3.1 Identification of Phenolic Acids  
Quantification result of six species of phenolic acids in Fucus vesiculosus for different 
extraction method and solvent is shown in Figure 18. Chlorogenic, vanillic and caffeic acid 
were not detected in all samples. Sabeena Farvin and Jacobsen (2013) identified vanillic and 
caffeic acid only in extract produced by SE from F. vesiculosus using pure water. Chlorogenic 
acid was detected in ethanolic extract but in a much smaller amount than other phenolic acids 
which could be the reason of why this phenolic acid was not detected in this study. Their study 
also discovered that ethanolic extracts generally contained higher concentration of phenolic 
acids but lower number of different phenolic acids when compared to water extracts. This 
phenomenon was not found in this study due to smaller range of polarity difference between 
the solvent used.  
 
More gallic acid was extracted using SE with 58.65% ethanol compared to PLE with the same 
ethanol concentration and SE with 80% ethanol. This discovery showed that gallic acid polarity 
is closer to 58.65% ethanol solution rather than other solutions studied. Polarity of solvent in 
PLE decrease because of diminishing hydrogen bond strength in subcritical area (Marcus 
2018), which explained lower concentration of gallic acid in PLE. The polarity of 58.65% 
ethanol in PLE condition of this study might be similar to 80% ethanol in unpressurized 
condition, which was shown by similar amount of gallic acid extracted for these two conditions.

could represent fucol, phlorethol or fucophlorethol since the molecular formula of these classes

of phlorotannin from crude extract in LC/MS equipment.

5. Conclusions and Perspectives 
5.1 Conclusions 
This study succeeded to model effect of extraction temperature, extraction time and ethanol 
concentration in PLE from F. vesiculosus on extraction yield and several antioxidant 
properties. The models were constructed using response surface method with Box Behnken 
experimental design and proved to be statistically significant with ANOVA analysis and 
coefficient of determination (R2) in range of 0.817 to 0.890. These models can act as a 
cornerstone for different aims, either for maximizing TPC or maximizing antioxidant 
properties, with a little error. However, this study focused on maximizing the extraction of 
phenolics with average antioxidant activities. Optimum condition for reaching this condition 
was found to be temperature of 137.18ºC, ethanol concentration of 58.65% v/v and extraction 
time of 4.68 minutes. Under this optimized condition, the obtained value of extraction yield 
was 31.16% dry basis, TPC was 3.69 g GAE/100 g dry seaweed, DPPH scavenging activity at 
0.5 mg/mL was 87.7%, ABTS radical scavenging ability at 0.5 mg/mL was 16.2% and ferrous 
chelating ability at concentration of 1.5 mg/mL was 46.3%. These values were close to the 
predicted value from the models with little error which showed the robustness and accuracy of 
the mathematical models. This study also observed that PLE could extract phenolic compounds 
with higher yield and TPC compared to SE although SE required longer extraction time and 
higher solvent requirement. The extract obtained from PLE had the same radical scavenging 
ability and lower metal chelating ability in comparison with SE extract. Moreover, when the 
PLE extract was analysed with HPLC, gallic, protocatechuic and gentisic acid were detected. 
Analysis using LC-DAD-ESI Q-ToF-MS was successful to identify one phlorotannin 
derivative with 4 PGUs. 
 
 
5.2 Perspectives 
This study was one of the earliest to apply PLE for extracting phenolic compounds from F. 
vesiculosus. Therefore, many further studies are still needed to give better understanding 
especially in a larger scale considering filter blockage observed in this study due to water 
presence in solvent. It is interesting to study PLE as one stage of biorefinery to extract more 
than one product together with other extraction method, e.g. enzymatic assisted extraction, and 
observe the effect of this combination on the antioxidant properties. In order to justify the low 
selectivity of PLE, analysis of polysaccharides and proteins in the samples are needed. Finally, 
==PAGE:65==
59 
 
application of antioxidant extract from PLE in food or pharmaceutical products are needed to 
observe the effectivity of this extract to inhibit lipid oxidation. 
 
In terms of phenolic acids and phlorotannins identification, many studies are needed to improve 
the analysis. This study only focusses on six phenolic acids found in previous study of Sabeena 
Farvin and Jacobsen (2013). However, there are more peaks identified on the chromatogram 
which had much larger area than the six phenolic acids studied (see Figure E.2 in Appendix). 
Further study with coupling MS to the HPLC system might be able to identify these 
compounds. For phlotoratannins identification, optimization is required to give better 
separation of phlorotannin from crude extract in LC/MS equipment. Analysis using a UHPLC 
coupled with MS/MS might give more accurate mass spectra to identify phlorotannins from 
the fragmentation pattern.
