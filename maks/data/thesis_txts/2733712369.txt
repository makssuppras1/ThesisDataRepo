Abstract
In the context of Copenhagen’s evolving energy landscape, the imperative for sustainable district
heating systems is increasingly pronounced. This research navigates the landscape by developing
a Mixed Integer Linear Program model for the present district heating energy system to optimize
and explore the interplay between heat demand, electricity prices, heat production technologies
and heat storage units. The study extends its analysis to a future Case Study for year 2050, explor-
ing three scenarios: Reference, Power-to-X (PtX), and Carbon Capture (CC). Results indicate a
significant decrease in Waste-to-Energy (WtE) technology use in the 2050 scenario, driven by the
impact of increased CO2 quota prices, even as biomass continues to retain its role as one of the
major energy sources. The Reference scenario optimization for 2050 reveals a reduced average
marginal cost attributed to decreased emissions and a notable increase in heat pumps utilization
attributed to their incremented capacity. In the PtX scenario, waste heat generated by PtX facilities
contributes to additional cost savings and emissions reduction, operating under the assumption of a
zero marginal cost. The Carbon Capture scenario, with a compensation scheme for negative emis-
sions, presents a unique carbon-neutral scenario but raises practical viability concerns. Thermal
Energy Storage (TES) units significantly mitigate the impact of fluctuating electricity prices on
the system, minimizing overall heat production costs by storing heat from the most cost-effective
units according to the varying electricity prices. The study ultimately acknowledges the shifting
role of the district heating system from an electricity producer to a consumer, posing new demands
on the electricity system.

factors driving the study. Section 1.2 subsequently narrows down the focus, aligning the research

1.1
Motivation
To achieve the Paris Agreement’s goal of limiting temperature increases to no more than 1.5 de-
grees Celsius and to keep the global temperature rise well below 2 degrees Celsius, it is vital for
nations all over the world to adopt and implement proven sustainable technologies that can address
this issue. [1]. According to the scientific findings of the United Nations’ Intergovernmental Panel
on Climate Change (IPCC), it is imperative that greenhouse gas emissions reach their highest point
before 2025 and subsequently decrease by 43% before 2030 to remain within this 1.5°C threshold.
Exceeding this limit poses the danger of generating more severe consequences of climate change
[2]. These outcomes, presented in the technical report of the Global Stocktake, serve as a clear
and compelling reminder of the pressing need for immediate action. The stocktake will not make a
difference unless the world responds to it with greater ambition and faster action. In this global set-
ting, Denmark is recognized for its uncompromising commitment to renewable energy (RE) and its
ambitious climate policies. The nation’s objective is to decrease greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions
by 70% before 2030 compared to 1990 levels and achieve net-zero emissions by 2050, in accor-
dance with the Paris Agreement. These objectives are supported by a comprehensive strategy to
increase the dependence on renewable energy. Denmark’s goal for 2030 is to have renewable en-
ergy sources account for a minimum of 50% of the country’s overall energy consumption and fulfill
100% of its electricity requirements. Additionally, by 2030, the government intends to gradually
phase out the sale of gasoline and diesel vehicles, and it is projected that 90% of district heating
will originate from non-fossil fuels by the same year [3]. This evidence is particularly significant
considering that over 95% of the heat demand in Denmark’s four largest cities is met by district
heating, and 64.5% of Danish households are linked to the district energy network, as shown in
Figure 1.1. District heating is a crucial component of the solution and plays a role in creating a
competitive and environmentally friendly economy by efficiently utilizing limited resources.
In 2008, three major utilities in the capital area of Denmark, namely CTR, VEKS, and HOFOR,
collaborated to create the Varmeplan Hovedstaden 1 heating plan. This plan aimed to achieve a
balance between cost, efficiency, and supply reliability within the district energy sector. It un-
derwent two further phases [5], which involved developing plans to reduce carbon dioxide (CO2)
emissions by 2025 and combining district heating with a wind-powered electric system. Initiated
recently, the fourth phase [6], ”Future District Heating Supply in the Capital Area 2050” (FFH50),
which Vestforbraending has joined, has the objective to define a green district heating system by
2050. Aspects such as CO2 emissions, biomass, and waste utilization and the robustness of the
district heating system are among the strategic concerns that are addressed in this vision-driven
plan, which also coordinates actions and takes stakeholder interests into account. FFH50 is cur-
rently exploring different scenarios to support future decision-making in the four companies. This
involves evaluating the effects on competitiveness, climate targets, and future peak and reserve
loads.
This thesis takes inspiration from the FFH50 project and seeks to explore and evaluate the potential
design of Copenhagen’s upcoming district heating energy systems. It focuses on the role of such

1.2
Denmark's Challenges and Drivers
In recent years, Denmark’s district heating system underwent a significant transformation, mostly
shifting from fossil fuels to more green energy sources. The transition over the last ten years
has been marked by a growing reliance on biomass, as shown in Figure 1.2, which has taken
the role of natural gas in smaller districts and coal in central co-generation locations. Looking
ahead, an increased reliance on heat pumps and excess heat from businesses is expected to lessen
this dependency on biomass in district heating systems. Nonetheless, there are currently plans to
install Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) plants in Combined Heat and Power (CHP) facilities,
which could potentially prolong the operational life of these plants. As a result, the increased
attractiveness of large heat pumps, caused by falling electricity prices, will weaken the operational
viability of co-generation units, especially those that rely on natural gas. Given the declining
earnings in the energy market, CHP plants will likely cease operations once their existing contracts
and subsidies expire. The outcome of this process will then result in a move toward more cost-
effective heat-only units. Similarly, there is a concomitant debate regarding decommissioning
Waste-to-Energy (WtE) facilities, as development in thermal capacity expects a reduction in waste
incineration in accordance with the Climate Plan for a green waste sector and circular economy [7].
This includes decreases in Danish waste due to enhanced sorting and recycling, and a reduction in
imported waste owing to the sector’s competitive pressures [8]. The decreased waste availability
and altered waste composition will then lead to reduced district heating production from waste
incineration plants, requiring replacement by other renewable energy (RE) heat-producing units.
Additionally, the utilization of surplus heat will have a significant impact on the district heating in-

1.3
Literature Review
Energy system modeling is essential for analyzing and assessing the energy supply of a particu-
lar area, providing insights into future scenarios, technological integration, and economic feasi-
bility. Mathematical models allow for systematically examining the interplay between technical,
economic, environmental, and social factors and support decision-making in energy systems man-
agement. Various modeling tools have been employed, as described in a study that examined 54
distinct energy system tools [11]. Among these, relevant ones include Balmorel [12], EnergyPLAN
[13], energyPRO [14][15]. When utilizing these tools, two primary methodologies emerge: 1) opti-
mization approaches, represented by Linear Programming (LP) and Mixed Integer Linear Program-
ming (MILP) models or tools like Balmorel that encompass both, and 2) simulation approaches, as
employed in programs such as EnergyPLAN and energyPRO. Optimization models prioritize the
identification of the most optimal solution while complying with specific constraints. They excel
in accuracy and cost-effectiveness, making them particularly suitable for objectives such as cost
minimization. However, their rigidity can limit flexibility in dealing with a dynamic environment
where conditions are constantly changing. Simulation models, on the other hand, provide a more
exploratory approach by examining different scenarios in the absence of a pre-established ”opti-
mal” solution. Although it provides a holistic perspective on possible results, this approach might
not be as accurate as optimization models when identifying the most efficient solution [16].
Owing to Denmark’s highly developed district heating system, numerous studies on the energy
system dynamics and inter-sector coupling with the electricity market have been conducted in the
country’s major cities. Using the Balmorel model, Ben Amer et al. [17] identified P2H technol-
ogy, waste utilization, heat storage, and excess heat as crucial in the upcoming energy transition,
leading to a sharp decline in CO2 emissions from Greater Copenhagen’s district heating by 2025.
Employing the same tool, [18] examined the impact of renewable gases, such as hydrogen and bio-
gas, on the transition of electricity and district heating systems in North-Western Europe by 2050.
In a similar vein, Heinisch et al. [19] developed an LP optimization model to examine operations
and investment in Gothenburg’s urban energy system. The primary conclusions were that power-

specific optimizations that allow us to include and focus on unique constraints and objectives, such
as integrating new Thermal Energy Storage (TES) systems or specific PtX or Carbon Capture (CC)
plants, that are not covered by Balmorel. The present work contributes to the existing literature
by introducing this flexibility through the creation of a novel deterministic MILP model. This
resulting model is then used to perform a techno-economic optimization of Copenhagen’s district
heating energy system.
1.4
Research Questions and Contribution
The primary research questions this thesis project seeks to address are:
”What is the optimal combination of energy sources for Copenhagen’s future district heating system
to achieve the 2050 climate goals and ensure lower heat costs? How will future Power-to-X and
Carbon Capture technologies within the proposed energy system facilitate these achievements?
To answer these questions, a MILP model of Copenhagen’s District Heating (DH) energy system
will be developed, in which the operational schedule of the plants withing this system is optimized
with the objective of minimizing the cost associated with heat production. The primary focus is
on modeling the facilities’ operational constraints and related expenses using a variety of techno-
logical and financial parameters in order to compare outcomes between the present year and the
year 2050. Furthermore, all relevant data required for the model has been rigorously gathered,
consolidated, and reviewed to ensure an accurate representation of the operation of the DH produc-
tion, thereby allowing results that closely correlate with real-world circumstances. As a result, the
intricate aspects of heat distribution will be overlooked to focus mainly on the heat supply.
This thesis’s contributions can be summarized as follows:
1. DH Energy System model: A MILP model designed to calculate the economically optimal
unit commitment for the heat-producing plants in Greater Copenhagen and asses the system’s
marginal cost in 2050.
2. Integration of Future Technologies: Evaluation of the impact of integrating Power-to-X
and Carbon Capture technology on both the cost of heat and the consequent emissions of the
DH system.

Reader's Guide

Fundamentals of the DH system
This chapter begins by providing an overview of the current DH energy system in the Greater
Copenhagen area, detailing its key technologies in Section 2.1. Following that, Section 2.2 thor-
oughly explains the mechanisms governing the dispatch of heating units and provides insights into
the dynamics of the DH market.
2.1
Current Energy System
Today, Copenhagen’s district heating system consists of a 1,500 km double-piped network that
heats over 30,000 users, or about 562,000 people [26], with an annual production of 10,000 GWh
[4]. The system is a component of the Greater Copenhagen district heating system, renowned as
one of the world’s oldest, most extensive, and most highly successful district heating systems. It
provides the metropolitan area with cost-effective, reliable, and energy-efficient heat.
The system is fundamentally split into an interconnected transmission and distribution network.
The transmission system facilitates the transfer of heat from the major heat plants to the distribution
networks in the various municipalities of the Copenhagen metropolitan area. This is accomplished
through the utilization of pumps, pipes, and heat exchanger stations. Within these heat exchanger
stations, heat is transferred, and the heated district heating water is pumped into the distribution
networks, which then distribute the district heat to individual customers.
The companies CTR, VEKS, and Vestforbraending each own an individual transmission network,
all of which are hydraulically interconnected (Figure 2.1). Vestforbrænding has the capability to
provide heat to VEKS and CTR. However, VEKS and CTR cannot provide heat to Vestforbrænding,
as it operates at a higher temperature. Instead, Vestforbrænding relies on other peak load plants
to meet its heat supply needs. Contrarily, VEKS and CTR can transfer heat between themselves
through a 335 MJ/s connection at Damhussøen, creating a seamless network where heat generated
in one network can be supplied to the other.
The local district heating areas possess an independent distribution network through which individ-
ual customers are provided with their supply. Most of the heat in the distribution networks receives
its supply from the transmission network, while a small portion of the heat can be generated locally.
The transmission network differs from the distribution network in that it operates at higher pressure
and temperature levels, as it directly receives heat from large producers. The system relies on a
160 km transmission network as its main infrastructure [4], which operates within a temperature
range of 115°C (supply) to 55°C (return) and a pressure of 25 bar. In contrast, the distribution
network, responsible for delivering heat to individual customers, operates at lower temperatures of
around 90°C/50°C and pressures below 10 bars. On the user’s end, there is a temperature setting
of 75°C/45°C in the building. The interconnection between the two systems enables the extensive
district heating area to receive heat from numerous different production facilities. By adopting
this approach, the generation of district heating becomes economically viable since heat can be
consistently produced at the facilities with the lowest costs at the time of production.
The heat is generated at various large combined heat and power (CHP) plants that produce both heat
and electricity and at smaller plants that solely produce heat. Most of the heat produced is sent to the
transmission networks, while smaller heat-producing plants only provide heat to local distribution
networks. The remaining heat needed in the distribution network is then supplied by the heat
from the transmission system. Figure 2.2 illustrates that the majority of heat generation today is
attributed to CHP plants that operate using waste and biomass. These plants collectively contribute

2.1.1
Biomass CHP and WtE plants
A biomass or waste-fired Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plant functions by converting fuel into
electricity and thermal energy simultaneously within a unified system (Figure 2.3).
Figure 2.3: Main system of a CHP facility [28].
Various types of biomass and waste are utilized as a source of fuel. For biomass plants, this includes
wood chips, pellets, straw, and other residues. Waste-to-energy facilities utilize non-recyclable mu-
nicipal solid waste (MSW), commercial waste, and specific industrial and construction wastes. The
fuels are stored and subsequently subjected to furnace processing to generate high-temperature flue
gas. This gas produces superheated steam in a boiler, which is then directed to the turbine, where
the energy of the steam is converted into rotational energy. The rotational energy is transformed
into electricity through its connection to the generator. The temperature and pressure of the steam
drop as it drives the rotation of the turbine blades. The low-pressure steam is extracted from the tur-
bine and directed to the DH condensers, where it is adjusted to a suitable pressure and temperature
for the DH network.
The heat generated by condensation is transferred to the district heating network. The facility
employs specific technologies such as grate firing and fluidized beds, which are customized to
suit the type of fuel and the scale of operation. Flue Gas Treatment systems are used to mitigate
harmful emissions and ensure environmental compliance. Flue gas condensation systems improve
efficiency by extracting additional heat from the flue gas. The surplus condensate is processed for
industrial purposes, maximizing the efficient use of resources and reducing the negative effects on
the environment.
CHP plants can be categorized into two types based on their turbine configurations: backpressure
CHP and extraction CHP. Both options allow for the simultaneous production of electricity and
heat, which can be supplied to the district heating network using a backpressure mode. The back-

In contrast to other fuels used for energy generation, waste has a negative price and is received
as a gate fee. The main goal of a waste-to-energy facility is the treatment of waste. The gener-
ated energy can be seen as a valuable by-product, especially given its growing significance for the
future Danish energy system, which heavily relies on district heating and large-scale wind power
generation. WtE plants are typically operated as base load power plants because of their significant
initial investments. Additionally, the long-term storage of certain types of waste poses challenges,
necessitating continuous incineration. This also guarantees an uninterrupted supply of district heat-
ing. The financial viability of operating a WtE unit solely for power generation may be limited,
and frequently, CHP facilities are designed to prevent power-only operation due to the absence of
required cooling infrastructure.
Regarding emissions, the primary distinction from an economic standpoint between waste and
biomass used as fuels is that while both are subject to taxation, only Waste-to-Energy (WtE) facili-
ties are required to pay for CO2 quotas on their emissions. This is because biomass is classified as a
”carbon-neutral” energy source. On the contrary, waste is defined as a mix of CO2-neutral biomass
and fossil-derived products, the majority of which are plastics. On average, approximately 32%
±5% of the CO2 that is released comes from fossil sources, and the waste mixture currently being
incinerated in Denmark has a typical emission factor of 153 kg/MWh (LHV-basis) for fossil CO2
[30].

2.1.2
Heat Pumps
Heat pumps employ the same technology as refrigerators, transferring heat from a lower temper-
ature level to a higher temperature level. Heat pumps extract thermal energy from a heat source
(input heat) and convert it into higher-temperature heat (output heat) using a closed process (Fig-
ure 2.5). Depending on the type of heat pump, the Coefficient of Performance (COP), which ranges
from 2 to 6, defines the relationship between the energy input and the energy output. This metric
quantifies the heat pump’s efficiency by evaluating the ratio of heating or cooling output to energy
input. A higher COP denotes a system that is more efficient, as it produces a greater amount of
heating or cooling energy for each unit of electrical energy it consumes. There are various types
of heat pumps, broadly categorized into compression heat pumps (that use electricity or fuel) and
absorption heat pumps (that utilize heat from steam, hot water, or oil).
Figure 2.5: Schematic Representation of a Heat Pump System with Heat Source and Heat Sink
Connections [31].
The majority of heat pumps in the district heating system are electrically powered compression
heat pumps and are used at DH plants. These heat pumps can utilize various energy sources, such
as seawater, drinking water, wastewater, groundwater, air, geothermal heating, and excess heat
from industrial processes or data centers. Large-scale heat pumps are typically designed based
on the available heat source size and require a significant number of full load hours to justify
the high specific investment costs. Therefore, large-scale heat pumps are typically designed for
base load rather than peak load. For large heat pumps to be used efficiently and effectively, they
must be located in close proximity to the district heating network and a heat source. This, in turn,
necessitates reserving space in the capital area.
Nevertheless, it is anticipated that larger plants could be added as an additional source of power
to already established central power plants. This is possible because, for instance, large-scale heat
pumps, particularly those utilizing seawater, can be strategically installed at existing CHP plants,
leading to cost-effective advantages. The heat pump can utilize the pre-existing infrastructure
of the CHP plant, including its direct connections to the district heating system and power grid.
Integrating the heat pump into the existing CHP infrastructure can, in turn, substantially reduce
capital expenditures.

derived from renewable sources such as wind and solar, into X, where X is chemically-based en-

heat pumps. However, certain industrial productions, as well as long-distance transport, will

the incentive to locate PtX production near the district heating network.

Generating substantial quantities of products through PtX necessitates a considerable amount of
electricity. Thus, expanding solar and wind energy, specifically offshore wind and solar cells, is
crucial for the production of PtX in Denmark. The district heating sector’s willingness to pay for
surplus heat is determined by its quality and variability; lower variability leads to higher value,

a lower number of full load hours per year, typically around 1,000, and its availability coincides
with the period when district heating consumption is at its lowest. The surplus heat generated from
the expansion of solar will, therefore, have a reduced impact on district heating. This is because
the excess heat produced in the summer holds little to no value for district heating. Similarly, the
significant fluctuations in heat production during the winter can make it challenging to utilize it
effectively.
2.1.4
Carbon Capture
Carbon capture technologies (CC) are systems that extract CO2 from various sources such as com-
bustion processes, fossil fuel and biofuel refinement, industrial chemical processes (like cement
production), or directly from the atmosphere. Currently, CC technology is readily accessible and
utilized globally. However, it has not yet become financially viable in the power and heat sector.
The predominant application of CC technologies presently involves a separation process in which
CO2, methane, and hydrogen are extracted from pure natural gas. In Denmark today, the most
common use of CC is for upgrading biogas [35].
CC technologies can be implemented using a variety of systems, including Post-Combustion [36],
Pre-Combustion [36], Oxy-fuel combustion [36], Chemical Looping Combustion [37], and Direct
Air Capture (DAC) [38]. The first three system types are similar to more traditional power plant
solutions and have been proven on a larger scale, whereas chemical looping combustion is only a
demonstration scale and could be considered a special case of oxy-fuel combustion. DAC differs
significantly from the other four technologies in that its sole purpose is to capture CO2, not to limit
emissions from power and heat generation.

through the use of heat pumps.
2.2
The District Heating Market Design
The heat price varies across different Danish district heating areas, although the principles for
determining it remain consistent. The method for setting the heating price paid by the consumer
is set by law to counter monopoly pricing while making sure plants can cover all necessary costs
related to supplying heat. Consequently, according to Danish legislation, heat supply companies
are required to maintain a non-profit status. Heat plants cannot charge more for heat than the
actual expenses of producing and transmitting it to customers. It is crucial to highlight that these
costs encompass not only the depreciation of assets but also the expenses related to finance the
investments. This ensures that the heating companies can maintain their financial viability in both
the short and long run. In the CPH district heating system, load scheduling is performed based
on marginal production prices by a single central entity, ”Varmelast.dk” [40], while prices are
established through bilateral contracts. Thus, there is a specific market design for plant scheduling,
as well as one for pricing determination. The sections that follow will go into greater detail about
plant scheduling and price-setting mechanisms in the DH system.
2.2.1
Load Dispatching
The heat load dispatch in the Copenhagen metropolitan area is handled by Varmelast, a load man-
agement unit resulting from the collaboration between CTR, VEKS, HOFOR, and now also Vest-
forbrænding; however, it is still one single pool of optimal load dispatch. Varmelast calculates
where the heat can be produced for the lowest possible cost at any given period. The heating com-
panies have individual heat purchase agreements with each of the major producers. The purpose of
having a separate scheduling organization is to economically optimize heat production across all
plants while maintaining the necessary confidentiality regarding the CHP plants’ operation on the
electricity market. The negotiated variable heating price typically differs from the variable heat
production cost. The production cost is used for the scheduling of hourly load dispatch, while fixed
distribution ratios between the heat and power sections determine the agreed heating price. After
that, the heat producers provide a report on their operational capacity and offer their heat output to

2.2.2
Heat Price Determination
The pricing of heat in the Danish market is not determined by Varmelast. Instead, it is determined
through negotiations between district heating suppliers, i.e., VEKS, CTR, Vestforbrænding, and
HOFOR, and the combined heat and power as well as WtE facilities. The heat pricing mechanisms
comply with the cost-recovery strategy mandated by the Danish heat supply legislation, specifically
the Heat Supply Act, LBK no. 2068 of 16/11/2021 §20 [41]. This law stipulates that prices should
solely cover essential expenditures, such as capital investments, operating expenses, and salaries.
The pricing of heat production in waste incineration plants is regulated by either a price ceiling
or the essential cost of heat production, as specified in the Heat Supply Act. In 2016, the Danish
Energy Agency (DEA) [42] established a price ceiling of 306 DKK/MWh. This limit prevents
waste facilities from charging more than this amount for their heat. These plants must choose
the lower value between their production costs and the specified price ceiling. Additionally, these
plants are regulated by the substitution principle, which ensures that heat production is only carried
out if it is the most economically efficient choice. Varmelast follows this principle by prioritizing
lower-cost providers when scheduling their load. However, it is not necessarily contingent on the
actual heat pricing, as the heat sales price differs from the scheduling costs.
The confidential nature of bilateral agreements prevents public examination of the precise price
mechanisms utilized. Insights from industry dialogues reveal that heat sales prices are typically
split into two components: 1) a fixed annual fee encompassing investments and fixed operating
costs and 2) a variable element that compensates for fuel and operational expenses. Due to the
complexity of distributing fuel expenses and operational maintenance between electricity and heat
output, a fixed ratio is commonly used. The heat pricing from WtE plants includes fixed and vari-
able costs, reflecting their previous function as base-load units. Hence, the rates charged for heat
do not necessarily reflect the precise expenses of heat generation but rather result from negotiated
pricing strategies. In essence, the pricing structure within the Danish district heating system is
sculpted by the unique configuration of its market and regulatory environment, which dictates the
permissible components of heat pricing.

The decision to employ a Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) model as the main method is
based on its ability to effectively handle a combination of binary and continuous variables. These
variables are intrinsic to the operating and scheduling of heating plants, where discrete decisions,
such as turning units on or off, as well as start-ups and shut-downs, coexist with continuous en-
ergy output, such as heat and electricity production. MILP models are capable of addressing the
complicated nature of cost minimization and covering aspects such as fuel costs, emissions costs,
O&M expenses, and power income while managing the complex operational aspects of the system.
This includes static elements such as production capacities and emission rates, as well as dynamic
elements, like fluctuating heat demand and electricity prices. This aspect is crucial for assessing
the sensitivity of various decisions, thereby improving risk assessment and management.
The model’s ability to balance multiple parameters within the restrictions of a district heating net-
work is a significant strength. The deterministic approach of MILP systematically explores the solu-
tion space to find a globally optimal solution that satisfies both physical and operational constraints.
MILP facilitates advanced planning, scenario analysis, and well-informed decision-making in de-
ploying heating units. When compared to simulation or heuristic approaches, MILP offers clear
benefits. It guarantees optimality within linear constraints, which is not typically seen in heuristic
methods, and offers solutions that can be replicated.
While a MILP model provides a more intricate and accurate depiction of the system, a linear pro-
gramming (LP) model offers faster solutions for simpler models. Additionally, LP models allow
for optimization across longer time horizons compared to MILP models, which require iteration
over shorter time intervals, such as weekly or monthly cycles, within the same time period. For
this thesis, it was decided to prioritize the model’s level of detail over its simplicity and speed. A
high-performance computer was also used to increase the time horizon of the model allowed by
the machine’s computational power, reducing the difference in the periods allowed while adding
complexity to the model to better describe the mechanisms of heat dispatch. Comparably, the de-
cision to use a linear model over a non-linear one is mostly due to the considerable computational
needs of non-linear models. Such models would have been impracticable for optimizing an entire
year on an hourly basis. This factor is critical to ensuring feasibility while conducting a thorough
investigation within the specified timeframe. This decision successfully balances the need for a
detailed analysis and the limitations imposed by computing tractability.
Two main case studies were established and subsequently examined. One represents the present sta-
tus of the DH system as of the year 2023, while the other represents a hypothetical future scenario
for the system as of the year 2050. As noted in the Chapter 1, the choice of the year 2050 is based
on the country’s current sustainability goals, which establish 2050 as a deadline and demand action
to be taken before this year. The 2050 scenario is broken into three sub-scenarios, each building
upon the previous one. The first sub-scenario, which is the most conservative, involves changes
in capacities, demand, and pricing compared to the 2023 baseline. The second sub-scenario intro-
duces a new technology, namely PtX. The final sub-scenario further expands this by integrating
both PtX and CC technologies, representing a more advanced and forward-looking energy system.

The hourly heat demand utilized in the 2023 case study is based on FFH50’s baseline scenario

base load, accounted for 25% of the overall consumption. On the other hand, the degree days’
dependent consumption, which is allocated for space heating (SH), makes up 75% of the total
consumption.

Mathematical Model
3.3.1
Design and Simplifications
The model is formulated as a MILP with the objective of minimizing the hourly marginal cost of
the District Heating system, which the model itself represents. It takes as input all the parame-
ters described in Section 3.2, performs the optimization, and returns as the value of the objective
function the total production cost of all the plants within the system for the entire period under
analysis. Heat demand and electricity prices are the only input factors that vary hourly, while the
others remain constant over time. A schematic representation of the model’s functioning is de-
picted in the Figure 3.6. A comprehensive and detailed description of its constraints is provided in
Section 3.3.3.
This study covers two principal Case Studies, focusing on the years 2023 and 2050, with the lat-
ter divided into three sub-scenarios. Therefore, in principle, there are effectively four distinctive
models, with each model corresponding to one of these cases. In practice, there is one base model,
which was initially developed for the 2023 Case Study, that serves as the starting point for the
three subsequent scenarios of the 2050 Case Study. Thus, each subsequent scenario builds upon
the previous one, adding layers of complexity and new operational dimensions to the base model
outlined for the 2023 Case Study.
The four models represent the following:
1. 2023 Case Study: This is the initial model that forms the basis for future scenarios.
2. Reference Scenario of the 2050 Case Study: The first sub-scenario of Case Study 2050
employs an identical model structure to that of the 2023 Case Study. The main difference
resides in the updated input parameter values to reflect the conditions of the year 2050.
3. Power-to-X (PtX) Scenario of the 2050 Case Study: Building upon the Reference Sce-
nario, this second sub-scenario introduces additional constraints and input parameters spe-
cific to the operation of the Power-to-X (PtX) plan
4. Carbon Capture (CC) Scenario of the 2050 Case Study: The third and final sub-scenario
further extends the model used in the Power-to-X Scenario. It introduces new constraints
and necessary input parameters specific to the operation of a Carbon Capture (CC) plant.
The model constraints can be categorized into three main groups: fulfilling heat demand, plant
operations, and storage system dynamics. The design of the production plants and storage system
involves defining variables to track and process input parameters and store output values. The
variables are classified as continuous and binary variables. Continuous variables track heat and
32

electricity production, costs, revenues, and emissions. Binary variables are used to determine
whether the unit or storage is functioning or not during a specific time step. These variables are
defined for every time step and for each individual unit or storage system. In essence, each unit
receives a specific input, such as fuel or electricity, and produces an output, which can be heat or
power, along with emissions. On the other hand, storage systems function on the premise that heat
is both an input and an output. All of these actions require and generate costs or profits, which are
provided as outputs in the model. The time horizon for all the scenarios is one year, with a time
step of one hour.

Several simplifications are carried out in the designed model to ensure reasonable computational
times and feasibility. The model is deterministic and does not consider stochastic aspects. It as-
sumes perfect foresight of future inputs such as heat demand pattern and electricity pricing. More-
over, as outlined in Section 3.2.4, all input financial parameters, except the electricity price, are
time-independent and constant throughout the optimization process. This approach streamlines
both the market dynamics, which in the real world impact the hourly dispatch and operation of
the units and storages, and the computational complexity of the model. While functional, it may
not capture the full range of challenges encountered in dynamic and uncertain operational environ-
ments. In addition, for the CHP and WtE facilities, ramping up and down limitations have been
excluded, as the ramping conditions provided by the DEA for the specific technologies, asdescribed
in Section 3.2.1, are in MW/minute. Thus, when converted to hourly time steps, they exceed the
single plant’s production capacity in MW and, thus, are non-binding. Further simplifications made
to the operational design of the individual units will be described in Section 3.3.3.

4
Case Studies' Results
4.1
Premise to the Results
The time horizon was split into small intervals since using Mixed Integer Linear Programs (MILPs)
to accomplish year-long optimizations required substantial computing power. During this process,
critical variables such as the binary state of units and the level of charge in storage units were
sequentially transferred from one time interval to the next in a continuous loop. This approach
was essential to accurately represent the seasonal patterns of Pit Thermal Energy Storages (PTES),
which are designed for operation over extended periods (months), unlike Thermal Energy Storage
(TTES) systems which typically operate on a daily cycle. The goal was to minimize the number
of these periods as much as possible. However, the model could only be optimized for weekly
intervals (168 hours) due to limits in the processing power of an average laptop. This timeframe
proved insufficient for capturing the dynamics of PTES, as the model underutilized the PTES and
failed to reach its full capacity. The DTU High Performance Computing (HPC) cluster [65] was
used to overcome this limitation, providing increased computational power. Utilizing the HPC
cluster enabled the division of the year into eight periods, each spanning 45-46 days starting from
the first hour. This finer segmentation was critical to the PTES’s functional operation, ensuring it
worked effectively.
All the MILPs have been solved using the Gurobi Optimizer (version for linux64) in Python with
an optimality gap fixed at 0.01%. The computations were carried out on a cluster consisting of two
nodes, each equipped with an Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6142 CPU @ 2.60GHz. Each node features
32 physical cores and 32 logical processors, utilizing up to 32 threads. The computational times
for the individual 4 cases studied were:
1. Case Study 2023: 4 minutes and 16 seconds.
2. Reference Scenario of Case Study 2050: 11 minutes and 58 seconds.
3. Power-to-X (PtX) Scenario of Case Study 2050: 16 minutes and 29 seconds.
4. Carbon Capture (CC) Scenario of Case Study 2050: 23 minutes and 7 seconds.
4.2
Case Study 2023
This section presents and analyzes the results of the Case Study 2023 to provide a comprehensive
understanding of the primary drivers and operating mechanisms of the model. The study begins
by focusing on the heat cost, depicted in Figure 4.2, which represents the objective function of the
model. The heat cost, as explained in Section 2.2.2, serves as the foundation for determining the
heating price that the consumers of the district heating system will pay. The price of electricity
and the demand for heat are the two main factors impacting the cost of heating. Increased heat
demand requires the activation of the most expensive units to meet the demand, therefore raising
production costs. In contrast, the electricity price directly impacts the revenue generated by CHP
and WtE units, directly influencing the heat cost. Additionally, the electricity price determines the
expenditure of electricity-consuming units to produce heat.

the base load, comprising all the WtE plants represented in orange and biomass-CHP plants in
green. These plants, especially WtE facilities, operate continuously throughout the year as long as

4.5
Discussion of the Outcomes
This research has implemented and adapted the developed model across four distinct case studies.
The aim was twofold: first, to examine the implications of a future scenario in contrast to the
present, and second, to assess the model’s robustness by introducing variations in parameters and
incorporating additional layers of complexity.
The study conducted in this thesis relies mainly on two hourly time series parameters: heat de-
mand and electricity prices. Therefore, it is important to bear in mind how these two parameters
were determined in this study and the impact they have on the outcome of the models. The heat
demand profile is relatively consistent across historical data for Greater Copenhagen, where se-
lecting different years for the input data has a relatively minimal influence on the outcomes. This
consistency is attributed to the regular pattern dictated by seasonal variations. Hence, the impact
of heat demand on the findings of this study is comparable with similar research in the current
literature, documented in Section 1.3.
In contrast, electricity price patterns exhibit high year-to-year variations and pose challenges for
accurate hourly forecasting. The intricacy of forecasting electricity costs adds a considerable de-
gree of uncertainty, and as this chapter illustrates, the particular dataset used to represent these
costs can significantly influence the results of the analysis. Therefore, the focus of this study is
rather to establish and replicate the strong correlation between hourly heat production costs and

day-ahead electricity market prices at a reasonable price level.
The optimization approach also impacts the results, as shown in Section 4.3.3, which primarily
appears in the use of TES storage. Performing a single optimization for the entire year, rather than
cycling across it for eight periods, as done in this study, would have resulted in better exploitation
of the PTES units. Indeed, in [66], where the optimization is performed for a single period, the
PTES units perform fewer and larger cycles than the ones in this thesis. As a result, the segmented
optimization does not provide the most efficient unit dispatch for the Case Studies evaluated, which
would have required significantly more computer resources.
Starting with the present-day scenario, represented by the 2023 Case Study, the optimization con-
ducted finds an average marginal cost of 26.1 e/MWh for the district heating system in a year.
This is somewhat less than, but still quite near to, the socio-economic prices discovered in [27],
which used a Balmorel model to determine that the average heating price for 2023 would be 32.3
e/MWh. However, as the name suggests, the number obtained in the stated analysis represents a
price, whereas, in this study, the values obtained are marginal costs. The difference between these
two values is detailed in Section 2.2.2. The value of 32.3 e/MWh also takes into account fixed
operational expenses, investment contributions, and opportunity costs, which refer to the missed
electricity income from CHP and WtE plants. Consequently, this raises the average cost of heat-
ing. In addition, the model used in the aforementioned research also considers bottlenecks in the
district heating network. These bottlenecks cause certain areas to be more dependent on peak load
units fueled by natural gas, even when cheaper units are not in operation. As a result, the actual
marginal costs of the system are increased.
The study carried out in this thesis is simplified by additional assumptions, as shown in the Chap-
ter 2, which influences the outcome of the model optimization. It has been emphasized that imple-
menting fixed fuel prices may not accurately reflect market fluctuations during times of instability,
such as the current energy crisis. Similarly, applying constant efficiencies to specific units may
cause the model to diverge from the real behavior of these plants, as they may react differently to
changes in heat demand and the electricity spot market. This includes important parameters like
the Coefficient of Performance (COP) of heat pumps and the Heat-to-Power ratio (HPR) of CHP
and WtE plants.
Findings from ref. [67] reveal that the decision to use a fixed Coefficient of Performance (COP) or
a variable COP for the heat pumps has minimal influence on the outcomes. However, the choice of
heat source and whether the heat pumps are connected to the distribution or transmission network
significantly affects their COP and, consequently, their yearly production measured in full load
hours. This is due to the substantial temperature differences between the two types of networks.
In this analysis, all the heat pumps were considered to be connected to the transmission network
and assigned a Coefficient of Performance (COP) of 3, which is somewhat greater than the COP
of 2.6 reported in the previous study. Moreover, a variable HPR for CHP and WtE plants can also
influence the full load hours of these plants and, consequently, the cost of heat, as their participation
in the electricity market would be different.
In terms of CO2 emissions, the findings of the 2023 Case Study closely align with the results
reported in the literature. In ref. [17] it was calculated, through a Balmorel model, that 0.64 Mt
of CO2 were emitted in 2020. Similarly, the study done in this thesis research, as illustrated in the
Section 4.4 section, found a total of 0.65 Mt of CO2 emissions for the year 2023. Likewise, the
portion of biomass and waste used for generating heat, which constitutes the base load in the 2023
Case Study, matches the data reported in ref. [17] for the year 2020. The consistency of these
numbers, along with the observed average heat cost, align the results of the base model for the
2023 Case Study with the current literature. This alignment provides support for the validation of
the 2050 Case Study model, which is constructed based on the underlying model for 2023.

The 2050 Case Study, described in Section 4.4, shows a notable decrease in Waste-to-Energy (WtE)
technology use compared to the 2023 Case Study. This can be linked to the rise in the CO2 quota
price, which is projected to increase from 85 e/tonne in 2023 to 276 e/tonne in 2050. Given that
this latter value extends more than 25 years into the future, it introduces considerable uncertainty.
As evidenced by the fluctuating projections provided by the DEA over the past five years [68], these
forecasts exhibit substantial year-to-year variations. Consequently, they should be cautiously ap-
proached and utilized more as representative indicators of potential future energy regulations rather
than precise numerical values. Moreover, predictions on the expansion of capacity for certain unit
types, such as heat pumps and boilers, also see substantial uncertainty. In light of this variability,
the values chosen for this study should be considered as just one potential scenario among numer-
ous possibilities for the 2050 district heating system. Therefore, the main goal of this research was
rather to investigate how the current structure of the 2023 district heating system would respond to
a projection of its input parameters, represented by the Reference Scenario in the 2050 Case Study,
and the incorporation of future technologies, represented by the PtX and CC scenarios.
The optimization of the Reference scenario in the 2050 Case Study yields an average marginal
cost of 23.5 e/MWh for the District Heating (DH) system. As detailed in section Section 4.4, this
cost reduction is primarily attributed to decreased emissions from reduced waste utilization and
the increased capacity of heat pumps, significantly enhancing overall system efficiency. It is worth
mentioning that in the year 2050, electric boilers have not been utilized to a greater extent compared
to the year 2023, even though their capacity has more than doubled. This can be attributed to
a combination of factors, including the tax on electricity, which remains at 45% of the average
electricity prices in 2050 due to the frozen policy applied. Additionally, the electricity prices in
2050 have not significantly decreased compared to those in 2023. Consequently, heat pumps, being
three times more efficient due to their COP, are the preferred choice during low electricity price
conditions, together with the Combined Heat and Power (CHP) units, which instead are utilized
during periods of high electricity prices.
Undoubtedly, the model continues to show a significant influence from CHP facilities in 2050, pri-
marily because their earnings are very responsive to fluctuations in electricity prices. Therefore,
in both the Reference Scenario and the other two scenarios of Case Study 2050, heat generation
is mainly determined by the dynamic interplay between CHP units (due to a reduced contribu-
tion from WtE plants) and heat pumps. These two types of units alternate in being the primary
producers depending on the specific hourly day-ahead market price of electricity being above or
below the cutting price identified in Section 4.3.2. This is additionally influenced by the behav-
ior of Thermal Energy Storage (TES) units, seeking to be charged with the most cost-effective
heat, thereby prompting CHPs to overproduce, exceeding heat demand during periods of negative
marginal costs.
Nevertheless, it is important to note that in this model, the district heating system is optimized
as an individual and independent entity without considering its connection with the power grid.
In this simplified representation, CHP and Waste-to-Energy (WtE) facilities are assumed to be
capable of selling any volume of electricity at any time, independently of electricity demand. This
assumption, though, deviates from reality. In practice, CHP and WtE plants with higher marginal
costs than Renewable Energy Source (RES) units find themselves outside the merit order when
electricity demand is not significantly high, being their marginal costs beyond the market clearing
point. Consequently, during periods of lower electricity demand, CHP and WtE plants are not
scheduled to produce electricity. As a result, they are unable to sell power and, consequently,
cannot capitalize on the revenues from cogeneration during these situations. This explains the
lower cost observed in the 2050 reference scenario, contrasting with the average cost of 37 e/MWh
identified across seven scenarios in [69].

The Power-to-X Scenario is closely comparable with the Reference Scenario of Case Study 2050
in terms of outcomes. The sole divergence arises from the inclusion of heat extracted by the PtX
plant, which reduces the previous marginal cost of 23.5 e/MWh to 21.7 e/MWh. Considering that
there are no extra CO2 emissions resulting from the PtX integration, the total yearly CO2 emissions
in both the PtX scenario and the reference scenario amount to approximately 0.1 Mt. This aligns
with the findings of the analysis presented in [69] for the 2050 scenario without carbon capture
units. As described in section Section 4.4, the PtX scenario is considered an improved version of
the Reference scenario because it assumes that waste heat from the PtX plant is given basically at
no cost. This reduces the amount of heat that has to be generated by the most expensive plants.
Consequently, there is a reduction in both heat cost and CO2 emissions. Nevertheless, it should be
noted that this waste heat comes with a price, which in [70] was estimated to be around 27 e/MWh.
This cost factor would have an impact on the findings of the study done in this thesis. However, it
is important to consider that being the PtX plant designed to be fed by power generated by wind
and solar energy sources, the waste heat coming from this plant comes at the same time that it is
also most advantageous to use this power produced from RES directly in heat pumps and electric
boilers, namely when the electricity is cheapest, thus making the output heat from PtX less valuable
during these hours.
Although the PtX scenario only considers the installation of a single facility based on AEC tech-
nology, the future of the e-fuel industry will determine the specific products and processes that
would be carried out to these facilities; thus, there is a lot of room for uncertainty in projections
for several more PtX plants. Consequently, this would bring significant doubts regarding the quan-
tity and temperature characteristics of the waste heat produced. However, since the production of
hydrocarbons relies on carbon sources such as CO2 recovered from flue gas, the district heating
network in Copenhagen’s current combustion sites can play a crucial role in encouraging the place-
ment of PtX production facilities nearby. This would ensure a higher quality of heat in comparison
to building the plants far away or on an energy island.
In the Carbon Capture scenario for the year 2050, the average marginal cost is calculated at -0.4
e/MWh. This value is strongly dictated by the possibility of the Carbon Capture plant integrated
into the model to sell CO2 quota as compensation for the negative emission. The threefold increase
in CO2 quota prices from 2023 to 2050 further accentuates this dynamic. While the Carbon Capture
unit operates throughout the year, generating revenue and reducing the hourly marginal cost to zero
on average from an initial 23.5 e/MWh in the PtX Scenario, the overall annual costs for district
heating heat production are only diminished by 25% compared to the PtX Scenario. This reduction
is hindered by increased emissions from the ARC WtE unit due to heightened utilization of this
plant. While it may be unrealistic to achieve an average cost of -0.4 e/MWh in practice, this
scenario seeks to examine the effects of such a compensation scheme on load dispatching and
heat production costs in a future district heating system. When analyzing emissions, the Carbon
Capture scenario successfully captures 0.5 Mt of CO2. This puts it at level with four scenarios in
[69] that had negative CO2 emissions ranging from around 0.3 Mt to 0.5 Mt. Therefore, as positive
CO2 emissions are kept below 0.2 Mt, the Carbon Capture scenario emerges as the sole carbon-
neutral scenario. Nevertheless, the substantial investment costs and uncertainties in the regulatory
framework for Waste-to-Energy plants raise doubts about the viability of such solutions in reality.

5.1
Final Remarks
Following the research questions outlined in Section 1.4, the final remarks are put together.
What is the optimal combination of energy sources for Copenhagen’s future district heating system
to achieve the 2050 climate goals and ensure the lower heat costs?
Predetermining the ideal combination of energy sources for Copenhagen’s future district heating
system is not easily achievable due to multiple uncertainties related to both technologies and the
energy market, which hinder a definitive answer. To attempt to provide a response, one can assess
many possible scenarios and thoroughly analyze and evaluate their potential implications. The
Case Studies examined in this thesis research indicate that, just by enhancing the capacities of only
currently available technologies, along with a favorable political and regulatory energy framework
focused on carbon-neutral solutions, there is a potential solution to substantially reduce CO2 emis-
sions from the district heating system while still keeping heat costs low.
Electricity-consuming technologies, such as heat pumps and electric boilers, would greatly benefit
from a future shift towards increased electrification in the energy sector, where electricity becomes
the primary energy source. This transition would encourage the market to further invest in these
technologies, gradually phasing out units based on natural gas and waste incineration. Moreover,
if heat production becomes diversified, with a significant portion produced through biomass co-
generation and the use of multiple large heat pumps, there could be an opportunity to optimize
production based on hourly electricity prices. This would enhance the flexibility of the electricity
system.
Moreover, to establish a robust connection in sector coupling, it is crucial for district heating to en-
hance its flexibility also by increasing Thermal Energy Storage (TES) capacity. Heat storage will
be particularly valuable in a system with highly fluctuating power production driven by Renew-
able Energy Sources (RES), providing load balancing and grid support during periods of surplus
electricity generation.
Biomass combustion remains a significant contributor to the future district heating system inves-
tigated, accounting for slightly less than half of the total heat generation in conjunction with heat
pumps. Biomass consumption has been the key to a rapid phasing out of coal, and significant in-
vestments have been made to support this transition. However, the continual consumption of large
quantities of biomass is facing opposition from politicians and interest organizations who ques-
tion the availability of sustainable biomass and its climate effects, although biomass used today is
certified sustainable. Nevertheless, forcing an early phase out of biomass would not be feasible.
It would be costly for heat consumers to shut down non-depreciated CHP plants, and ensuring
security of supply would become challenging without viable alternatives.
How will future Power-to-X and Carbon Capture technologies within the proposed energy system
facilitate these achievements?
The significant uncertainty about the time horizon, potential energy quantities, temperatures, and
production curves in the PtX processes makes it challenging to accurately evaluate the district

59
==PAGE:69==
heating system’s willingness to pay for surplus heat from PtX facilities. Hence, this study did
not establish the actual and specific influence on heat costs. Instead, it focused on examining the
consequences of this excess heat on the whole energy system in relation to heat production. Results
from the PtX Scenario of Case Study 2050 revealed that integrating this technology into the district
heating energy system effectively reduces the amount of heat generated by the most expensive and
least sustainable plants by exploiting surplus electricity coming from RES, such as wind and solar.
Thus, if prices for this surplus heat prove to be competitive enough within the DH system, a future
reduction in emissions proportional to the PtX capacity installed can be expected.
Ultimately, the Carbon Capture technology exerted the most significant influence on the system’s
CO2 emissions, resulting in a fully carbon-neutral scenario with a surplus of negative emissions.
Here, the primary effect on the system is observed through emissions since the waste heat generated
is considerably less than in PtX facilities. In this study, the integration of this technology yielded
a marginal cost of 0; however, for these outcomes to be realistic, a comparable compensation
structure and a high CO2 quota price are imperative. In addition, in order for this technology to be
economically viable, it must be implemented on plants with a high number of operational hours,
which poses a limit on the number of suitable WtE or CHP plants these units can be coupled with.
The evolution of the district heating system denotes a shift from its role as an electricity producer
to a future where it will act as an electricity consumer, presenting entirely new demands on the
electricity system.
5.2
Future Research
The thorough analysis carried out in this master’s thesis establishes the foundation for possible
future investigations and improvements. Several possibilities for additional research and enhance-
ment of the model and its applications can be explored.
This study, which aims to predict the future district heating system for the year 2050, includes
various simplifications in the input parameters and the units modeling. Additional investigation
is necessary to improve our comprehension of how the district heating system adapts to changes
in the energy market. Specifically, investigating the effects of different taxation approaches, par-
ticularly regarding electricity, and various compensation systems for Carbon Capture units might
offer significant insights into the scheduling dynamics of different units.
Exploring alternative optimization methods or considering more detailed time-dependent optimiza-
tion, such as reducing the number of optimization cycles throughout the year, could lead to more
efficient utilization of resources.
The PtX Scenario proposed in this study assumes that waste heat from Power-to-X plants is pro-
vided at no cost. Further research should examine the economic implications of this assumption
by considering the actual cost associated with this waste heat. Furthermore, analyzing the possible
synergies between PtX facilities and the district heating network could yield significant insights.
The Carbon Capture Scenario offers an interesting chance for investigation, specifically about the
feasibility and viability of adopting carbon capture technology in the long run. Further research
should address uncertainties in the regulatory framework, investment costs, and the practicality of
achieving carbon-neutral scenarios by coupling these units with WtE or CHP plants.
Lastly, as this study only examines a single unit for both Power-to-X (PtX) technology and Carbon
Capture (CC) technology, a more thorough analysis might be conducted by expanding the model
to incorporate multiple units of each type. This extension would shed light on the implications
and effects of these emerging technologies on the overall production cost of heat and the emission
profile of a future district heating system.
