Abstract
The Major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I molecule is specialized in the pre­
sentation of foreign and self­antigens on the surface of every nucleated cell in the body,
where cytotoxic T­cells will interact with the compound. The TCR:pMHC interaction is
well studied and continuously tested in vitro to understand the immune system for de­
tecting possible cures for cancer and viral infections. Studies show that anticancer and
autoimmune T­cells TCRs have a low affinity to cognate antigens, making them difficult
to target. To overcome this problem, multimerization has been performed where mul­
tiple TCRs and pMHC complexes can interact simultaneously by using scaffolds like
tetramers and dextramers, resulting in an increase the avidity. To increase the production
of pMHC molecules, MHC­based technologies has improved over the years using ad­
vanced peptide­exchange technologies. The aim of this master thesis was to optimize a
MHC ELISA protocol by testing functional concentrations on different HLA types. These
optimal concentrations should then be tested using T­cells staining on fluorescently la­
belled pMHC­dextran multimers with BCs to investigate if the adjusted concentrations
reached the exact optimal stoichiometry of pMHCs for each tested HLA type. The de­
velopment of MHC ELISA using HRP­anti­β2m­Ab for the detection of correctly folded
MHC I molecules was successfully done for the HLA types HLA­A*02:01, HLA­A*03:01,
HLA­A*11:01, HLA­B*08:01, and HLA­B*15:01 testing different individual batches. Back­
calculated stock concentrations based on the values generated from the MHC ELISA was
used for the T­cell staining of fluorescently labelled pMHC­dex multimers on different BCs.
The performed T­cells staining of each HLA test batch indicated the optimal stoichiometry
ratios of used pMHC for the analyzed data suggest to be HLA dependent, with batches for
HLA­A*11:01 showing the best results. Additional optimizations for the MHC ELISA using
HRP­W6/32­Ab as the detection antibody, which indicated that the detection sensitivity
level was higher, resulting in lower back­calculated values. Further investigation of the
relation between the correctly folded MHC ELISA and the optimal stoichiometry of pMHC
multimers visualized by T­cell staining should be conducted.

1.1
Introduction to the Immune system
The body is constantly fighting threats of all kinds, and here the immune system plays
a vital role. The immune system protects us from infectious agents like viruses, bacte­
ria, parasites, etc. However, at the same time, it also monitors abnormalities such as
cancer by using a broad network of immune cells. The immune system is divided into
two linked systems called the innate and adaptive immune system, which together are
responsible for fighting foreign and internal invaders. The innate immune system is often
referred to as the unspecific with immediate fighting of foreign antigens. The immune cells
specific for the innate system are macrophages, dendritic cells (DCs), and monocytes.
More specifically, DCs and macrophages are defined as antigen­presenting cells (APCs),
which take up the foreign antigen to present to the adaptive immune system. The APCs
use pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) to recognize pathogen­associated molecular
patterns (PAMPs) and damage associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) on given target
cells. PAMPs are found on pathogens, whereas DAMPs are released from tissue or dying
cells [2]. When recognized the APCs engulf the foreign cell and travel to the lymph node
to activate the adaptive immune system. The adaptive immune system is specific towards
foreign threats and consists of B­ and T­cells. To activate the adaptive immune cells, the
APCs must present the foreign antigen via the major histocompatibility complex (MHC)
class I or II to the B­ and T­cells. Upon this interaction the B­ and T­cells become activated
and migrate to the site of inflammation to initiate activation of other immune cells. In this
project the CD8+ T­cells and MHC class I will be in focus.

1.2
Introduction to MHC­I molecule
1.2.1
Structure of MHC class I molecule
One of the most important cellular components for antigen presentation of viral and cancer
is the MHC class I. The MHC class I is a heterodimer consisting of two polypeptides
chains; the α­chain (45 kDa) and β­chain (12 kDa), as seen in fig. 1.1. The α­chain
comprise of three external domains (α1,α2 and α3), which together are defined as the
heavy chain, while the β­chain only comprise of one domain, the β2­microglobulin (β2m),
which are defined as the light chain. The α1 and α2 domain together creates the peptide­
binding cleaft, which can bind 8­10 amino acids, while the α3 domain binds to the CD8
co­receptor. Furthermore, the MHC molecules are known to be highly polymorphic, which
can be observed in the peptide­binding grove as it varies in formations[3].
The MHC molecules are known to be highly polymorphic, and between the different allelic
forms of MHC, it is often the peptide­binding groove that varies in formation. The peptide­
binding domain has the ability to bind 8­10 amino acids, and since the peptide­binding­
groove formations are specific to each allele, many different combinations of peptides can
be presented to T­cells.
Figure 1.1: Illustrations of the MHC­I complex. a) The structure of a MHC class I molecule
consisting of an α­chain (α1,α2 and α3) and β­chain. The α1 and α2 domain together form
the peptide binding groove responsable for binding target peptides. The α3 domain is in
charge of transmembrane anchoring of the MHC class I complex to the cells surface. The
β2­microglobulin is responsible for the stabilisation of the MHC complex and supports the
formations of the peptide­binding groove. b) A ribbon diagram of the MHC class I complex
is shown. Adapted from [3]
Each peptide has a specific set of amino acids residues at unique places in the peptide se­
quence. These amino acid residues interact with the groove of the MHC molecule, which
is called anchor residues. Every human or mouse cell each express many different MHC
I molecules, which all have specific criteria for how peptide­binding interacts with anchor
residues. The α3 domain is responsible for the transmembrane anchoring of the MHC
class I molecule to the cell surface. The β2m molecule is responsible for the stabilisation
of the MHC complex and supports the formation of the peptide­binding groove. The β2m
is also referred to as the light chain. All these components together make it possible to
fold the MHC­peptide complex displayed on the cell surface properly. [4]

1.2.2
MHC class I antigen presentation pathway
In fig. 1.2, the MHC I pathway is displayed. Proteins inside a cell are contently being
degraded and replaced with newly synthesized proteins. Proteins that are damaged or
misfolded inside the cells are tagged with ubiquitin by undergoing various enzymatic ac­
tivities. If a virus infiltrates a nucleated cell, the virus can take over the cells machinery
to generate virus endogenous proteins in the cytosol. In the process of replicating virus
peptides, translation errors of the virus peptides results in misfolding or damaging the en­
dogenous proteins. The misfolded or damaged proteins are then ubiquitin tagged. These
ubiquitin­tagged­viral peptides enter the proteasome, which degrades the proteins into
smaller fragments in the length of 9­15 amino acids (aa). These small molecules can then
enter the Endoplasmic reticulum (ER) through the TAP transporter, composed of TAP1
and TAP2. After entry, the fragments can be even further broken down to fragments of
8­10 aa by the enzyme endoplasmatic reticulum aminopeptidase (ERAP) within the ER.
In the ER, the ribosomes are responsible for making the MHC I complex by producing
the components α chain and β2­microglobulin. After newly synthesized MHC­I α­chains
enters the ER, calnexin and ERp57 works as a chaperone by binding to the α­chains to
stabilize the complex in a partly folded formation. Later, when β2m binds to the α chain of
the complex, calnexin is released and, at the same time allowing calreticulin and the TAP­
associated protein tapasin to binds to the complex. These proteins, calreticulin, tapasin
and ERP57, are responsible for the formation of a MHC class I peptide loading complex.
When peptide fragments of 8­10 aa interact with the peptide­binding groove, the MHC­I
complex is complete. Each allele of a MHC I complex has a specific set of variable re­
gions that only fits with a particular set of antigens. After the MHC complex has bound
to a peptide, transportation out to the cells surface is conducted. The MHC complex will
now leave the ER in an endosome where it will travel through the Golgi apparatus. When
expressed in the cell membrane surface, the MHC­peptide complex will then wait for the
interaction with cytotoxic T­cell specifically. [5],[6]

the CD8+ T­cells by an APC in the lymph node, the CD8 T­cells will migrate to the site of

1.3
Strategy to make MHC class I in vitro
Many studies examine the interaction between peptide­MHC­1 complexes for the use of
T cell detection technologies. In 1996 Altman et al. created MHC multimers based on
oligomers of MHC monomers that were loaded with antigenic peptides tagged with fluo­
rochromes. This creation of MHC multimer has become a primarily used platform for de­
tecting and analyzing antigen­specific T­cells using flow cytometry. However, since empty
MHC class I molecules are volatile, instant refolding with peptide was needed. Since the
generation of multimer complex’s requires, first the production of MHC I heavy chain and
β2­microglobulin in bacteria, refolding the complex with desired peptide, purification, bi­
otinylation ending with multimerization. To do this for each individual tested peptide­MHC
I complex could end up being very time­consuming and comprehensive.Therefore the
search began for the discovery of other peptide exchange technologies. Several tech­
niques have been explored over the years, and one method showed by Saini et al. 2013
which relies on the exchange of low­ for high­affinity peptides to stables the MHC I com­
plex. By using di­peptides containing a C­terminal side chain that binds to the F­pocket
of the MHC class I, showed to support the refolding of the MHC class I molecule. Since
the di­peptides are low­affinity binding towards the MHC­I molecules, the exchange reac­
tions facilitate their removal, making it possible for high­affinity peptide binders to interact
with the MHC­I molecules. Garstka et al. 2015 also demonstrated stabilization of MHC­
I molecules with low­affinity peptides replaceable with high­affinity peptides, but in their
case, the process was strongly dependent on temperature. The low­affinity peptides sta­
bilized the MHC­I complex at low temperatures and released when exposed to higher
temperatures making it possible for the high­affinity peptide of interest to bind. An al­

ternative method was shown by Rodenko et al. 2006 using a photocleavable peptide to
stabilize the MHC I monomer, which can be cleaved off by UV exposure, making space
for peptide loading of an epitope of choice. In fig. 1.4, the UV­exchange of peptides is
illustrated.

1.4
Detection of antigen specific T­cells
A explain in section 1.3 many production methods for pMHC has been investigated for
the detection and isolation of antigen­specific T­cells. These tools rely on the interaction
between TCR and the pMHC, but the TCR/pMHC interaction is known for its low affinity
Page 6
==PAGE:13==
with a fast off rate. Therefore over the years, multimerizations has been performed where
multiple TCRs and pMHC complexes interact simultaneously to increase the strength and
duration of interaction between the T­cell and the target cell. This multimerization will in­
crease the avidity. The first platform used for MHC multimers was the MHC tetramers
which combined biotinylated pMHC with fluorochrome­conjugated streptavidin. By cre­
ating this pMHC, tetramer T­cells from PBMCs were used to detect and phenotyping
antigen­specific T­cells. [10]. Tetramerazation is a good detection tool and has been
used in many studies, but Dolton et al. 2014 showed that pMHC­I tetramers often fail
to stain antigen­specific T­cells where the TCR­pMHC interaction is weak. This can be
a problem when screening for T­cells specific for self­derived peptides, which could be
anti­tumour and autoimmune T­cells. To overcome this problem, multimerization strate­
gies using dextramer backbones can be used instead. The dextramer contains multiple
binding sites for the pMHC, and many fluorophores can be attached, which can induce
the avidity to bind antigen­specific T­cells even for low­affinity receptors. [15], [16]
1.5
ELISA types and its function
In many studies in the scientific field the enzyme­linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) has
proven an invaluable research and diagnostic tool. Identification of immune responses
after vaccinations, expression of protein to performance of quality control testing of differ­
ent components etc. [17], [18], [19] An ELISA is a commonly used to measure biological
samples often antigens or antibodies in a combination with specific target proteins. Often
immunoassays, like the ELISA, the assay is depend on antibodies binding to a specific
target which generates a signal. Often the ELISA assay tests are conducted in a 96­well
plate which makes it possible to screen multiply experimental setups and samples at once.
[20] In fig. 1.6, an overview of the most commonly used ELISA types is shown.

In 1997 KATO et al. created a Sandwich ELISA where antigen was sandwiched between
rabbit antibody IgG bound onto glutaraldehyde­activated aminoalkylsilyl glass rods and
Fab’ fragments of rabbit antibody conjugated with β­D­galactosidase extracted by Es­
cherichia coli. In this project the Sandwich ELISA will be focused on.
The sandwich ELISA technique is based on two antibodies used to sandwich a target pro­
tein of interest. This design of this type of ELISA can either employ the direct or indirect
ELISA format but the difference is that the instead of non­specific binding of antigens that
binds the assay plate, the capture antibody is responsible for binding the tested proteins
of interest. To increase the antigen binding to the capture antibody, a Blocking detergent
called Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) is often used. BSA has the ability to bind to the
surface of ELISA plate increasing the protein of interest to bind to the capture antibody.
After the capture antibody is bound to the ELISA plate the addition of proteins of interest is
added which will bind to the capture antibody. The secound antibody used is called the de­
tection antibody which often is enzyme­conjugated. The most used enzymes conjugated
to the detection antibodies is the horseradish peroxidase (HRP) or alkaline phosphatase
(AP). By adding substrate optimized to react to the chosen enzyme, an enzyme­substrate
reaction occurs generating a colored product detectable for the ELISA reader. To ter­
minate the enzyme­substrate reaction at the right absorbance value a stop­solution is
applied specific for the chosen enzyme. The final step is to analyse the ELISA samples
by an ELISA reader which in this case measures absorbance.

3
Materials and Methods
For the development of a major histocompatibility complex Enzyme­linked immunosorbent
assay (MHC ELISA) various optimizations were conducted to analyse the different human
leukocyte antigen (HLA) types of functional concentrations. An ELISA reader was used
to detect the absorbance signal generated by the reagents for the use in the MHC ELISA
protocol. After establishing the MHC ELISA, back­calculated stock values were tested
using peptide­major histocompatibility complex (pmhc) multimers on dextran backbones
fluorescently labelled with either Allophycocyanin (APC) or Phycoerythrin (PE) . To de­
tect these fluorescently labelled pMHCmultimers complexes flow cytometry was used to
investigate if any relation between the MHC ELISA and T­cell staining could be conducted.
3.1
Production of MHC class I
The disulfide­stabilized­HLA (DS­HLA) monomers and wild type (wt)­HLA monomers were
used throughout this project. The production of DS­HLA monomers was based on the
disulfide­stabilized MHC­I technology as described by Saini et al. (2019).
To wt­HLA
monomers, UV­mediated peptide exchange technology was used, mixing HLA­specific
heavy chain inclusion body and β2m together HLA specific UV ligands as described
in Rodenko et al. 2006 and forms the conditional ligand­HLA­complexes (p*MHC). The
monomers was previously produced in the T Cell and Cancer Group.
3.2
NetMHCpan software 4.1 for the selection of viral
peptides
The selection of viral peptides used for the MHC ELISA and T­cell staining with pmhc­
dextramers were performed by using the NetMHCpan 4.1 software. The NetMHCpan
software is a computational method capable of predicting the binding affinity of given
peptides for MHC­I and MHC­II molecules. The calculations of the binding affinity are
based on various origins of experimental data and machine learning modelling framework.
??.
The peptide library is generated by the T­cell and cancer group at DTU Health Tech and
consist of 41 viral peptide sequences. These sequences were inserted into the NetMHC­
pan software to determine each peptide binding affinity specific for each selected HLA­
type.
The data output from the NETMHCpan software determines if the peptide is a strong or
weak binding for the chosen HLA type. The threshold of MHC­I molecules rank was <0.5%
and <2%, respectively strong binding peptides and weak binding peptides.
The scores given for each peptide was sorted according to binding affinity, wherefrom the
strongest and weakest binding peptide was chosen for the high­affinity binding peptide
(HLA ligand) and low­affinity binding peptide (HLA non­binder) in the MHC ELISA and
T­cells staining experiments.
3.3
UV­exchange of peptides
As explained in section 1.3 there are different procedures to perform the monomer peptide
exchange. In this project, UV­mediated peptide exchange was examined to generate
pMHCcomplexes specific to each tested HLA type with a selected set of viral peptides.
This method was used for both the MHC ELISA and T­cell staining procedures.

3.3.1
Peptide exchange for ELISA
Production of the pMHCcomplex for the usage in the MHC ELISA protocol first mixes
purified p*MHC or DS­MHC complexes at 50μg/ml was mixed with 100 μM viral peptide
in blocking buffer (PBS+2% BSA). Hereafter, the mix containing p*MHC was exposed
to UV­light (366 nm) for 1 hour at 20◦C. Exposure to UV­light results in cleavage of the
conditional UV­ligands, which allows for peptide exchange. The solution containing DS­
MHC and peptides were not exposed to UV but was instead incubated for 30 minutes on
ice. The presents of the chosen viral peptides work as a rescue peptide for the MHC­
I complexes by binding to the peptide­binding domain of the MHC­I complex. After UV
exposure or incubation, the solutions were centrifuged at 3300 g for 5 minutes at 4 ◦C.
The pMHCmonomers were then ready for analysis using MHC ELISA.
3.3.2
Peptide exchange for T­cell staining
The production of pMHCcomplexes to be used for T­cells staining experiments test dif­
ferent concentrations of p*MHC monomers being tested. To produce pMHCcomplexes
for T­cell staining, purified p*MHC complexes at 150 µg/ml were mixed with specific viral
peptides at 200 µM. After mixing of the different components, they were exposed to UV­
light (366 nm) for 1 hour at 20 ◦C . Centrifugation was performed as seen in section 3.3.1.
Hereafter, the supernatant was extracted, and the pMHCcomplex had a concentration of
150 µg/ml.
To investigate different ratios of pMHCcomplexes, various concentrations were tested in
two setups. In the first setup, the pMHCcomplexes were diluted into three ratios in three
different wells of 75 µg/ml (24x), 50 µg/ml (16x), and 25 µg/ml (8x). Whereas in the
second setup the pMHCcomplexes were diluted into four concentrations ratios: 50 µg/ml
(16x), 25 µg/ml (8x), 12.5 µg/ml (4x) and 6.25 µg/ml (2x). For the first setup, the dextran
backbone was fluorescently­labelled with PE while the second setup, a dextran backbone
fluorescently­labelled with PE and APC. Both were tested for the detection of CD8­T­cells.
Both dextran backbones had a concentration of 16x108 M. In order to bind For the dextran
backbones to bind to the pMHCmonomer, they were incubated for 30 min of incubation at
4 ◦C was conducted. Hereafter, the samples were frozen using Barcode­Freezing buffer
(0.5x dilution), and the samples were now ready for T­cell staining.
3.4
Standard curve and sample controls design for the ELISA
To examine the functional concentrations of each examined HLA­batch, a set of four con­
trols were included in the experimental design for the MHC ELISA. The four controls were
given the names: No peptide, No UV, HLA ligand and HLA non­binder. The No peptide
and No UV control were not exposed to any additional peptides but were both diluted in
blocking buffer. The No peptide control was exposed to UV light (366 nm), whereas the
No UV control was not exposed to UV light. To test how each HLA­type performed when
undergoing UV­mediated peptide­exchange, peptides of a high binding affinity (HLA lig­
and) and low binding affinity (HLA non­binder) were chosen. The peptide exchange was
performed as explained in section 3.3.1. The expected concentrations of each control
after dilutions and exposure to peptides are shown in fig. 3.1 for different tested MHC
ELISA controls. To determine the concentration of each conducted control generated by
the MHC ELISA, a standard curve consisting of a range of monomer concentrations was
made. By developing a standard curve with a specific set of HLA specific concentrations,
it was possible to interpolate a concentration for each tested control.

4.1
Optimization of HRP­anti­β2m­Ab concentration for the
ELISA setup
To develop a MHC ELISA that detects correctly folded functional MHC molecules, op­
timal concentrations of the used reagent needed to be examined. The first reagent to
be optimized was the HRP­anti­β2m­Ab. To investigate the functional MHC molecules,
a standard titration curve was generated using different concentrations of MHC exposed
to HRP­anti­β2m­Ab. By having this standard curve, the relationship between HRP­anti­
β2m­Ab and monomers concentration could be investigated.
To optimize the antibody, HRP­anti­β2m­Ab, for the detection of the correctly folded monomers,
different concentrations of HRP­anti­β2m­Ab was explored. The optimizations were ini­
tially conducted on a DS­HLA­A*02:01, batch A02­Y­050820. In fig. 4.1, three different
concentrations (4.3 μg/ml, 1.43 μg/ml, 0.73μg/ml) of HRP­anti­β2m­Ab were used and
visualized in three standard titration curves using the batch A02­Y­050820 with a specific
range of monomer (20 nM to 0,16 nM). Common for the three plots was that no binding
saturation of monomers was detected. Since the three different concentrations of HRP­
anti­β2m did not result in monomer binding saturation for the tested range of monomer
concentrations, further optimizations needed to be performed. For future optimization in
the project, 1 µg/ml of HRP­anti­β2m­Ab was chosen as the standard concentration for
further testing.
For the standard curve to reach binding saturation of the monomer, 80 and 40 nM was
added to the tested monomer range on batch A02­Y­050820 using 1 µg/ml of HRP­anti­
β2m­Ab. By using these conditions, a S­shaped curve should appear, as seen in fig. 4.2.
Thus, achieving binding saturation of the monomer batch A02Y­050820.
To test whether the 1 µg/ml of HRP­anti­β2m­Ab and the chosen monomer concentra­
tions range could be utilized universal for other batches of different HLA types, two wild
type molecules, HLA­A*02:01 batch A02­140421 and HLA­B*08:01 batch B0801­150420,
were investigated. By using these batches, it was shown in fig. 4.3 that when using the
chosen parameters, binding saturation of monomers was achieved. experiments. The
standard titration curve with the latter tested monomer concentration range and the anti­
body HRP­anti­β2m­Ab concentration at 1 µg/ml were kept constant in the following sec­
tions.

5
Discussion
In this project, the aim was to optimize a MHC ELISA by testing functional concentrations
on different HLA types. These optimal concentrations were then tested using T­cells stain­
ing on pMHC­dextramers with BCs to investigate if the adjusted concentrations reached
the exact optimal stoichiometry of pMHC for every staining and experiment.
Using an ELISA as a quantitative measurement of pMHC­I complexes is a commonly
used tool and are often used to investigate biotinylation levels of MHC complexes and
the affinity of the interaction of pMHC­I complexes. [22], [23]. To validate the analyzed
samples used in an ELISA experiment often a standard curve is included to correlate
the values observed for the selected control values to obtain valid and accurate results.
[13], [24] The first aim of this study was the development of a MHC ELISA protocol for
the detection of correctly folded HLA class I molecules monomers on different batches.
To develop an MHC ELISA protocol to detect correctly folded MHC class I molecules
tested on different types of HLA, optimizations of the different used reagents in the ELISA
protocol was the first to be optimized. By using different concentrations of the detection
antibody HRP­b2m in relation to different MHC concentrations for the standard titration
curve, the optimal concentration of HRP­b2m was found to be 1 µg/ml and the monomer
range for the tested standard titration curve to be 80 nM down to 0,16 nM tested on three
types of HLAs DS­HLA­A*02:01 (batch A02­Y­050820), wt­HLA­A*02:01 (batch A0201­
 wt ­140121) and HLA­B*08:01 (batch B0801­150420). Related studies show that anti­
b2m­antibodies are often used as a detection tool for UV mediated peptide exchange and

6
Conclusion
In this project, we hypothesized that an optimized MHC ELISA testing functional concen­
trations of different HLA types could be used to determine optimal stoichiometry ratios of
pMHCused for T­cell staining of pmhc­Dextramer fluorescently labelled with either PE or
APC.
The development of MHC ELISA for the detection of correctly folded MHC class I molecules
was successfully produced. By optimization of the concentration of HRP­anti­β2m­Ab and
the monomer detection range, the optimal standard curve was detected. The MHC ELISA
was conducted on MHC class I alleles HLA­A*02:01, HLA­A*03:01, HLA­A*11:01, HLA­
B*08:01, and HLA­B*15:01, where different batches were tested. For all investigated
HLA types, different batches showed that the presence of high­affinity binding peptide
exposed to UV (HLA­ligand) resulted in a substantial rescue of the MHC­I molecules.
p*MHC molecules exposed to UV in the presence of a low­affinity peptide (HLA non­
binder) and no peptide still resulted in some rescue of monomer complex. Variations of
the stability and peptide exchange efficiency were speculated to be HLA specific. How­
ever, more experiments testing different peptides with different affinities for the selected
HLA types should be considered. Validation of the back­calculated stock concentrations
based on the values generated from the MHC ELISA was used for the T­cell staining of
pmhc­dex multimers on different BCs. For most of the tested batches, the results were
inconclusive. However, the conducted T­cells staining of each batch indicated the optimal
stoichiometry ratios of used pMHCare HLA dependent. MHC ELISA optimization for the
detection of correctly folded MHC class I specific for HLA­A*03:01 batch A0301­190820
using the HRP­W6/32­Ab was successful. For the detection of stability and the peptide­
exchange ability, batch A0301­190820 illustrated higher detection sensitivity due to lower
pMHCback­calculated stock concentrations compared to the MHC ELISA relying on the
HRP­anti­β2m­Ab. The higher sensitivity level could be useful in future studies of correctly
folded functional p*MHC regarding stabilization and peptide binding abilities of different
HLA types.
