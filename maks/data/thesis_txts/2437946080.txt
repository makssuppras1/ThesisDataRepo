Abstract 
  
 
This thesis focuses on the relative permeability measurements by core flooding experiments. It 
seeks to determine the parameters which can be used for using it in real life. This thesis aims at 
changing the parameters and finding the values of relative permeability by using the simulation 
with the help of the software SENDRA. A simulation will be tested in the thesis where water 
and oil are both injected in the core at varying rates in order to calculate relative permeability 
at different fractions. Steady state conditions are used with steady state imbibition. 
We aim to find out the capillary end effects and at what flow rates and viscosity do they occur. 
We aim to reduce those end effects by changing the parameters. Normally, it is assumed that at 
steady state the water saturation is constant and does not change. However, in real situation this 
does not occur as the end effects are present and that happens due to the capillary pressure 
between the oil and the water phase. Corey and Skjæveland parameters will be found from the 
literature and the relative permeability curves will be constructed. Then the parameters will be 
used in the simulation in order to obtain the saturation, oil production and differential pressure 
profiles. Relative permeability values will be calculated from the pressure drop and that will be 
compared with the literature data curves. Based on that the range of parameters to be used in 
real life will be determined.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background 
Recovery of Oil is one of the challenges that the oil industry is constantly trying to optimize. 
This is due to the rising energy demands of the world and also its consumption. Oil is stored in 
the reservoir rocks where it is available in the small pores. It is desired to remove this oil from 
the pores and use it to meet the current demands. However, many properties dictate the amount 
of oil which can be recovered. These properties according to Boukadi et al.  include capillary 
pressure, interfacial surface tension, temperature, porosity, pore radius and relative 
permeability (Boukadi, Watson, Owolabi, 1994). Steady state experiments are carried out by 
imbibition method to understand the changes physical parameters have on the relative 
permeability. These changes are important because relative permeability also describes the flow 
of fluids in a core. One understands the effect relative permeability has on the saturation. 
Relative permeability is calculated by Darcy’s law equation, which is carried out under steady 
state conditions by the use of core scale experiments. Relative permeability describes the fluid 
flow inside the rock and the changes in relative permeability can change the volume of oil 
recovered. So, it is important to recover maximum amount of oil possible with not only the 
profit margin kept in mind but also to overcome difficulties in oil extraction and to prevent the 
underutilization. Research suggests that the capillary end effects, affects the homogenous cores 
and it helps to trap the non-wetting phase from leaving the core (Andersen, Standnes, 
Skjæveland, 2017). This in turn reduces the relative permeability. 
Part of this thesis will focus on the approach taken in the thesis to understand the measurement 
of relative permeability under steady state conditions. According to Kantzas, Bryan and Taheri, 
J function is used to describe capillary pressure curves for all porous materials (Kantzas, Bryan 
& Taheri, 2012) . It also takes into account capillary pressure, interfacial tension, permeability 
and porosity along with contact angle. J function is used to plot primary imbibition, primary 
drainage & secondary imbibition curves. 
Wettability also plays an important role in capillary pressure and relative permeability. A 
significant amount of research has been done in the field of homogenous wettability and on the 
other hand, comparatively less work has been done in the field of heterogeneous 
wettability(Masalmeh 2002). Understanding the basis of Wettability is important in order to 
increase the oil recovery (Abdullah et al. 2007). Most reservoirs are water wet before the oil 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
 
 
migration takes place where the alteration between water and oil occurs. During this alteration, 
the water occupies the smallest pores while the oil occupies the largest pores (Kantzas, Bryan 
& Taheri, 2012). After further investigations, it was revealed that water penetrates the water-
wet layers more easily than the oil wet layers. This is due to the capillary effects and simulation 
has showed that little oil would be recovered from the oil-wet layers. Wettability also affects 
the effectiveness of water flood. Imbibition forces determine how easily can the water be 
injected in the core and how does it travel through the water wet core. Each capillary gets 
invaded by non-wetting phase (oil) at different capillary entry pressure. The larger the capillary 
radius, lower the capillary entry pressure.  
There is another method known as Virnovsky’s method which measures relative permeability 
by using steady state core flooding experiments. However, this method eliminates the 
interpretation errors which are caused due to the capillary end effect (Virnovsky et al. 1995). 
In order to calculate the relative permeability of the two phases, it is very vital to calculate the 
individual phase pressure drops across the core. The capillary pressure is positive when the 
water saturation is low and negative when the water saturation is high. Semi permeable 
membrane is used in order to measure the phase pressure. 
While interpreting the steady state relative permeability experiments one can interpret it with 
two cores, homogenous and composite cores. Homogenous cores consist of the same material 
and have shorter length around 5 cms. Extensive research (Langaas, Ekrann,Ebeltoft, 1996)has 
shown that it is always desirable to first place the most permeable core at the inlet and then 
proceed with least permeable core at the outlet.  
In the experimental procedure, two phases are injected at the same time into test core with 
constant rates and also constant pressures (Kantzas, Bryan & Taheri, 2012). A System is 
deemed to be at steady state if there has been a constant pressure drop, constant oil production 
and constant saturation. Then, pressure drop and outlet flow at each rate is measured and these 
values are used in the Darcy’s law to calculate the relative permeability of that particular phase. 
Effective permeability of each fluid is measured by directly measuring the outlet water and oil 
flow rates and pressure drop across the core. Water fraction is increased and the whole above 
mentioned process starts again. If it is desired to obtain last end point value then the water 
fraction injected must be 1.0, though the core.  
When capillary end effects occur, they also change the relative permeability values. This issue 
is common in unsteady state methods, but steady state relative permeability values are also 
influenced by end effects. There is a method in which the correction for end effects are 
==PAGE:11==

implemented in the data (Maloney, Gupta, 2016). This method is called as the intercept method. 
The intercept method is not different from the normal method used during the steady state 
approach. Oil and Water both are injected at different fractions and at each fractional flow, 
steady state is achieved. Changes in pressure drop vs steady state and saturation vs. rate are 
used to correct the data, which is set at the current fraction.  
Core scale experiment involves injecting water and oil at constant total flow rates while the 
ratio of the phases is changed at the inlet of the core. Changing conditions like these causes a 
change in pressure drop, oil production and water saturation. It is imperative to wait until an 
equilibrium in the core is established again (Surdal, 1995). Then one has to measure the 
individual flow rates and pressure drop in order to calculate the relative permeability by Darcy’s 
law.  
Relative permeability is an important component in optimizing the recovery of oil. Higher 
Relative permeability of oil will help in improving the recovery of oil. A focus will be given to 
obtain relative permeability values by changing different parameters like viscosity and flow 
rate and then a comparison will be made with the reference curve.  
1.2. Objectives  
The objective of this thesis is to understand under what conditions the relative permeability 
values are closest to the reference values. The overall goal is to reduce the end effects and 
calculate relative permeability by changing the input parameters of Darcy’s law like viscosity 
and flow rates. Therefore, the parameters will be changed sometimes to a large degree and the 
relative permeability values obtained from it will help us in estimating the extent to which the 
values will be accurate. In the future, it is desired that while one is carrying out steady state 
experiments by applying Darcy’s law he or she is aware of the extent to which input parameters 
like viscosity and flow rates can be changed in order to have a fairly reasonable and accurate 
value of relative permeability.  The challenge of steady state measurements is that it takes a 
very long time to attain but it is the most accurate methods to understand the characteristics of 
reservoir. It takes a long time to achieve steady state as the system needs to attain steady state 
before one can start the measurements. This occurs due to the fact that it takes time for system 
to stabilize. For example, it takes a considerate amount of time to achieve s steady state pressure 
drop.   It is also difficult to conduct experiments for core flooding so we are using the simulation 
software SENDRA to interpret experimental results and understand the mechanisms.

data (Burdine, 1953) to the displacement experiments (Johnson,Bossler,Naumann, 1957).  
1.3 Scope  
Darcy’s law can also be applied for unsteady state but as the topic of this thesis suggests, steady 
state is to be used while measuring the parameters to calculate relative permeability. Relative 
permeability is used when there is multi-phase flow and relative permeability is substituted in 
Darcy’s law. Corey’s model  (Lake, 1989) is used to calculate relative permeability and 
Skjæveland equation is used to calculate Capillary pressure. By obtaining the Corey and 
Skjæveland parameters from literature, they are used in SENDRA where one can change the 
parameters, viscosity and length in order to obtain the relative permeability values from the 
SENDRA simulation. The scope of this thesis relates to obtaining the relative permeability 
values and understanding the extent to which the previous theories are relevant. The results 
obtained by the changed parameters in this thesis will be compared to the previous results so 
that we can control if the theories are relevant. One can increase the scope of this investigation 
and include composite cores. This added element can be a topic for another investigation which 
will not be covered in this thesis but it is merely to give an idea for the future work.

2 Theory  
2.1 Experimental procedure 
It is important to know the experimental procedure briefly as it helps to understand how the 
parameters are measured. Experimental procedure is very essential in understanding the concept 
before applying steady state concept and changing the parameters to understand the effect it has 
on relative permeability & capillary pressure.  
 
In the experimental procedure, two phases are injected at the same time into test core with 
constant rates and also constant pressures (Kantzas, Bryan & Taheri, 2012). A System is 
deemed to be at steady state if there has been a constant pressure drop. Then, pressure drop and 
outlet flow at each rate is measured and these values are used in the Darcy’s Law to calculate 
the relative permeability of that particular phase. In order to obtain additional relative 
permeability curves it is important to change the inlet ratios of the two phases. It is important 
to remember that the saturation change must be in one direction to avoid hysteresis effects.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
This experiment is for imbibition displacement for Oil –Water. Water is the wetting phase and 
oil is the non-wetting phase. Before the start of the experiment, the core is completely saturated 
with water and the test is started by injecting oil until a steady state is reached. After multiple

calculate relative permeability.

Drainage is defined by Schlumberger Oil Field glossary(2018) as ‘The process of forcing a non-

So, the initial charging of the reservoir is the drainage process.’ It can be seen in the figure

core and therefore a wetting phase water is used to inject in a reservoir. That process is known 
as primary imbibition.  
In water wet core, wetting phase (water) is attracted to the rock. Non-Wetting phase (Oil) cannot 
enter easily and a pressure is needed in order for it to force the Oil to enter the rock. Capillary 
pressure is needed to force the first droplet of Oil into the rock. This Pressure is referred to as 
‘Entry Pressure’. If ‘Entry pressure’ is increased, more volume of oil can enter the rock, hence 
reducing the water saturation.  
When there is no capillary pressure, it signifies that the oil trapped in the core can be recovered 
with ease. The point where the capillary pressure between oil and water is Zero is known as

2.2.4 Wettability  
Wettability also plays an important role in capillary pressure and relative permeability. A 
significant amount of research has been done in the field of homogenous wettability and on the 
other hand, comparatively less work has been done in the field of heterogeneous wettability 
(Masalmeh S. K., 2002). Understanding the basis of Wettability is important in order to increase 
the oil recovery (Abdullah, Buckley, Carnegie, Edwards, Herold, Fordham, Graue, Hussain, 
Montaron, Ziauddin, Habashy, Seleznev, Signer, 2007). According to Schlumberger Oil Field 
Review, wettability signifies the preference of a solid to be in contact with one fluid than the 
other. It also describes the balance of forces between the surface and the interfacial forces. If a 
wetting liquid is in contact with another surface it will spread out by displacing the other fluid. 
On the other hand, if a non-wetting fluid comes in contact with another fluid it will form a 
spherical shape over the surface. A very similar procedure occurs in the core. Contact angle 
defines if the surface is water- wet or oil-wet. If the contact angle is nearly Zero then the fluid 
is non-wetting and will not spread out on a surface. If the contact angle is around 180 degrees, 
then the fluid is wetting and will easily spread out on a surface. Intermediate wet surface forms 
a bead however the contact is more than zero degrees.  
 
 
 
 
Figure 9: On the left a drop of oil (green) is surrounded by water (blue) on a water wet surface which forms a bead where the 
contact angle is zero. On the right, the drop spreads on the oil wet surface and the contact angle is approximately 180 
degrees. The figure in the middle refers to the mixed wet surface where a bead is formed but the contact angle is more 
balanced with respect to the other two figures. Taken from Reference number (22). 
 
 
A reservoir can be water wetting, which signifies that the surface of the reservoir prefers the 
contact with water. If a reservoir is oil wetting, then the surface of the reservoir prefers the 
contact with oil. Reservoirs rocks are known to have complex structures consisting of different

mineral types. Each mineral type may have different properties such as wettability, which 
makes the wetting property of the composite rock difficult to explain. Wettability influences 
primary recovery which also includes productivity and oil recovery. Initial Water Saturation 
profile Swi is influenced by original and altered wettability during and after hydrocarbon 
migration. Most reservoirs are water wet before the oil migration takes place where the 
transition zone occurs. In this zone, the saturation changes from water at the top of the zone to 
water at the bottom of the zone. This difference is determined by capillary pressure. After 
further investigations, it was revealed that water penetrates the water-wet layers more easily 
than the oil wet layers. This is due to the capillary effects and simulation which has showed that 
little oil would be recovered from the oil-wet layers.  
Wettability also affects the effectiveness of water flood. Imbibition forces determine how easily 
can the water be injected in the core and how does it travel through the water wet core. Reservoir 
core is modelled like the ‘bundle of capillaries’. Bundle of capillaries consist of different pore 
radius. Each capillary gets invaded by non-wetting phase (oil) at different capillary entry 
pressure. The larger the capillary radius, lower the capillary entry pressure.

2.2.5 Virnovsky’s Method 
There is another method which measures relative permeability by using steady state core 
flooding experiments. However, this method eliminates the interpretation errors which are 
caused due to the capillary end effect (Virnovsky et al. 1995). In order to calculate the relative 
permeability of the two phases, it is very vital to calculate the individual phase pressure drops 
across the core. This particular method aims to construct a new inlet end piece which allows for 
measuring the individual phase pressures and also investigates the continuity of phase pressures 
across the boundaries and 2D effects close to the inlet end of the core. It is needed to calculate 
the individual phase pressure drops in order to calculate capillary pressure. Two different 
fractional flows are used to investigate the capillary pressure and phase pressure drops. One of 
the fractional flow is very high in number and the other very low. Now, these two different 
fractional flows are used to compare the capillary pressure and water saturation behaviour. Two 
images below, illustrate the situation.  It is evident that for the last image, the capillary pressure 
is positive when the water saturation is low and negative when the water saturation is high. 
Semi permeable membrane is used in order to measure the phase pressure.

2.4 Intercept Method   
When capillary end effects occur, they also change the relative permeability values. This issue 
is common in unsteady state methods, but steady state relative permeability values are also 
influenced by end effects. There is a method in which the corrections for end effects are 
implemented in the data. This method is called as the intercept method (Maloney, Gupta, 2016). 
It involves steady state relative permeability tests with different flow rates for each fractional 
flow. The corrected saturation and pressure is used to calculate capillary end effect (CEE) - 
corrected relative permeability curves due to the Darcy’s Law. The intercept method is not 
different from the normal method used during the steady state approach. Oil and Water both 
are injected at different fractions and at each fractional flow, steady state is achieved. Changes 
in pressure drop vs steady state and saturation vs. rate are used to correct the data, which is set 
at the current fraction.

Saturation profiles of water are recorded along the length of the core. An upward trend of water 
saturation towards the end of the core length is observed. Water is retained near the end of the 
core by the capillary forces, which try to balance the viscous or centrifugal forces which act on 
the oil. This increases the average saturation of water as the oil injected fraction at the end of 
the core has trapped the water and therefore an upward trend of water saturation towards the 
end of the core is seen. As can be seen in the above saturation profile, the least water fraction 
profile other than zero has shown the maximum end effects compared to the other saturation 
profiles. As the water fractions increase, the end effects are seen to reduce. This is due to the 
reason that when there is maximum injected oil fraction it interacts with the oil in the core and 
causes entrapment of the water which prevents the water from escaping. It is a delicate play of 
the viscous forces and the interfacial forces along with capillary pressure and the saturations of 
oil and water which increase or decrease these end effects. It is interesting to note that when the 
injected oil fraction is 1, no end effects are seen and the initial water saturation, 0.2 stays the 
same throughout the length of the core. When the water fraction is 1, a downward profile of 
saturation is observed as without the additional injected oil there is no entrapment. The end 
effects are strongly visible here as the viscosity is very high therefore water finds it challenging 
to displace oil and that also increases the capillary pressure and thus a stronger entrapment. All 
the curves converge at approximately water saturation of 0.5. This signifies that the capillary 
pressure is zero at the water saturation of 0.5, which can be looked at the capillary pressure 
profile. It is observed that the capillary pressure is zero when the water saturation is 
approximately 0.5. This represents the fact that fluids can easily flow without any entrapment 
when the water saturation is approximately 0.5.

equation. It is observed that all the fractions of oil and water reach steady state within the first 
two days of core flooding experiments.

Figure 43, displays the saturation profile for different flow rates of the same fraction. The 
fraction chosen was 1 ml/day water injected with 9 ml/day of oil injected. The fraction of water 
injected is 1/9. Figure 43, clearly displays that the saturation profile for the flow rate of 1 ml/day 
shows more end effects than the one for 5 ml/day. The saturation profile for 1 ml/day begins to 
increase at the fraction of 0.5 of the length of the core. While on the other hand, the saturation 
profile for 5 ml/day is constant until the very end of the core and only begins to increase at 
around 0.95 fraction of the length of the core.  Both the saturation profiles meet at the common 
point at 0.5 water saturation fraction signifying that the capillary pressure is zero at this fraction 
of the water saturation. The water saturation profiles at figures 38 and 39 support my statement 
that the lines reach a common point when the capillary pressure is zero towards the end of the 
core.  
It was reported that the relative permeability increase with increasing flow rates (Kantzas, 
Bryan & Taheri, 2012). Some authors like Labastie et al. believe that relative permeability is 
independent of flow rate except for residual oil saturation (Kantzas, Bryan & Taheri, 2012). 
While others like Heavyside et al. believe that for intermediate wet medium with low flow rates 
oil would exist in the middle of pores thus blocking the flow of water (Kantzas, Bryan & Taheri, 
2012). As a result of this, water at high flow rates would be force the oil to remain at pore 
surface. This would change the relative permeability. By analysing the results, it is evident that 
as the flow rates increase the relative permeability increases if one observes figure 35. It is true 
that the difference to observe is small but as I had explained before it was very difficult to obtain 
very low saturations for extremely small flow rates, However, I have managed to establish a 
trend which points to the fact that decreasing the flow rates would decrease the relative 
permeability values.

5. Conclusions 
 
To conclude, when the viscosity is increased end effects are reduced and greater the viscosity 
lesser the end effects. In order to eliminate the end effects, viscosity is needed to be increased. 
End effects are important because they manage to prevent the oil from escaping and the relative 
permeability of oil is decreased as a result. This signifies that lesser amount of oil leaves the 
core due to the end effects. After changing the viscosity of oil it was observed as the viscosity 
is increased the end effects reduce. However, the down side of this is that the pressure drop 
increases and the oil production decreases. The pressure drop increases at higher viscosity due 
to more force needed to displace oil out of core. An oil with high viscosity has to produce the 
same rate as the other oils with different viscosity. It was concluded that oil with high viscosity 
has to produce more force along with water when injected in displacing the oil in the core. This 
signifies that it is able to displace more water with lower water saturation than oil with lower 
viscosity could displace water. One also concludes that for the specific parameters mentioned 
in the thesis end effects are strong between 5-10 cp.  However, it can be said for general system 
lesser the viscosity greater the end effects. These conclusions are valid for steady state and 
applies to homogeneous cores.  
When the flow rate increases end effects are reduced. End effects were not very visible with the 
cases used. It is understood that one has to decrease the flow rate by a huge degree in order to 
see any end effects in the core. End effects can be seen if the flow rate goes below 1 ml/day. 
Pressure drop increases with increased flow rate and the oil production also increases. It is 
concluded that the flow rate should be much lesser than 1 ml/day in order to see any strong end 
effects. In general system, when the flow rate is decreased end effects are more evident and as 
the flow rates are increased end effects decrease. In this thesis, for the specific parameters given 
end effects were stronger when the flow rate was 1 ml/day than when the flow rate was 5 ml/day.  
The recovery factor was not very different for the flow rates used and I finally conclude that in 
real working conditions the flow rates do not play a big role in changing the relative 
permeability values unless one decreases the flow rates to extremely low values. It was proved 
that by increasing the flow rates the relative permeability increases thus proving that end effects 
can be decreased.
