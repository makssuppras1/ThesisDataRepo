Abstract
Routine monitoring of NO2 and O3 is costly, resulting in sparse monitoring net-
works that are unable to capture the spatial and temporal dynamics of urban
air pollution problems. The development of low-cost gas sensors with sensitiv-
ities in the parts per billion (ppb) range is a promising advancement that may
be applicable as a supplement to routine monitoring stations. This can enable
government and consultants to obtain higher resolution in monitoring schemes.
Furthermore, the small inexpensive sensors also have potentials within personal
exposure assessment and the involvement of citizens through crowdsensing and
citizen science projects.
Four sensor nodes were constructed at a total cost of 495-520 EUR/pc, with
two low-cost electrochemical gas sensors from Alphasense Ltd (UK), targeting
NO2 and O3 respectively, and a DHT-22 combined temperature and humidity
sensor from Aosong Electronics Co. Ltd. (CN). The sensor nodes were built
with a wire connecting to 230V AC, and designed to log data internally on a
memory card. The gas sensors output the current of two electrodes: a working
electrode (WE) intended to account for the concentration of the target gas and
an auxiliary electrode (AE) intended to account for noise and drift and not be
inﬂuenced by the target gas. A factory recommended model (denoted Model
1) subtracts the AE signal from the WE signal and divides with the sensitivity
of WE to give the concentration of the target gas.
Laboratory experiments were carried out to determine the zero oﬀsets and
sensitivities of the sensors at constant temperature (25◦C) and constant low
relative humidity (<1 %).
The sensitivity was highly linear in all sensors.
Zero oﬀsets and sensitivity of the gas sensors were compared to a previous
factory calibration by the manufacturer, and while the sensitivity was within
± 2-5 %, the oﬀsets were deviating with ± 10-20 %. The laboratory calibration
showed a fast drift in the zero oﬀset of between 2-8 ppb/day, with non-linearities
observed in several of the sensors. Additional laboratory experiments identiﬁed
response times (T90) of 1-3 minutes, limits of detection of 4 ppb and limits
of quantiﬁcation of 12-16 ppb. The combined standard uncertainties of the
laboratory experiments were 2-4 ppb.
The sensors were deployed at the roof of a routine measurement station for
four weeks in order to validate the laboratory calibrated model (Model 1),
and calibrate two slightly more complex models (Model 2 and Model 3). The
zero oﬀsets obtained in the laboratory experiments were not consistent with
ﬁeld measurements, as most sensors gave measurements during the deployment
period that were similar to the reference, only displaced by between 10 and
20 ppb, when using Model 1.
Moreover, the drift rate was a factor of 2-5
lower during the ﬁeld deployment period than what was observed during the
laboratory experiments.
When calibrating Model 2 and Model 3 with the ﬁeld deployment data, com-

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Human health is closely linked to air pollution, consequently, clean air is by the
World Health Organization (2006) considered to be a basic requirement for hu-
man well-being. The World Health Organization (2015) estimate exposure to
ambient outdoor air pollution and indoor air pollution to cause 3.7 million and
4.3 million premature deaths each year, respectively1, and the International
Agency for Research on Cancer have declared outdoor air pollution as carcino-
genic to humans2 (Loomis et al., 2013) and the ”leading environmental cause of
cancer” in all regions of the world (Straif et al., 2013, International Agency for
Research on Cancer, 2013). The Global Burden of Disease Study characterizes
ambient particulate matter as a ”key risk” (Lim et al., 2012) and the European
Environment Agency (2014) state ”air pollution is the top environmental risk
factor of premature death in Europe”.
The majority of the European population lives in areas where the World Health
Organizations’s ambient air quality guidelines are frequently exceeded (Euro-
pean Environment Agency, 2013), and the United States Environmental Pro-
tection Agency (2014a) state that in 2010 over 120 million Americans were
exposed to air pollutants exceeding the National Ambient Air Quality Stan-
dards. In mega-cities - especially in less developed regions - the problem is
even more severe (Mage et al., 1996, Gurjar et al., 2010, Chan and Yao, 2008).
Since the late 20th century pollution levels have declined in western Europe
and North America due to emission regulation, while pollution levels are still in-
creasing in many industrializing countries (Loomis et al., 2013, Akimoto, 2003).
In spite of decreasing concentrations and emission levels, substantial negative
health eﬀects are observed in Denmark with estimates of more that 3000 prema-
ture deaths annually associated with exposure to air pollution, in addition to
a variety of other negative health eﬀects (Hertel et al., 2013). Furthermore, air
pollution - in particular ozone (O3) - is toxic to plants, entailing large agricul-
tural losses (Booker et al., 2009, Kuik et al., 2000, Fagnano et al., 2009, Debaje,
2014) and damage to ecosystems (Bingham and Porter, 2015, Mikkelsen and
Ro-Poulsen, 2002, Grantz, 2014).
1.1
Sources of outdoor air pollution
Anthropogenic sources of air pollution are mainly centered around combus-
tion processes in combustion engines, wood stoves, and power plants, ﬂanked
by non-combustion agricultural and industrial sources (Fenger, 2009, Nazaroﬀ
and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). In an urban setting, the combustion engines are

gen oxides (NOx), particulate matter (PMx), polycyclic aromatic compounds

Natural sources include volatile organic compounds (VOCs) from plants, and

formation of primary pollutants (e.g. NOx) to secondary pollutants (e.g. O3)

are from mobile point sources which, combined with the relatively complex pho-
tochemical dynamics of NOx and O3 gasses, entail a highly variable spatial and
temporal distribution of pollutants, and complicates monitoring programs .
1.2
Monitoring of air pollution
Adequate monitoring of air pollution is required in order to check compliance
with current air quality guidelines, and evaluate pollution abatement strate-
gies. Air pollution is currently monitored by a network of routine measuring
stations often supplemented by modeling (Castell et al., 2013, Reis et al., 2015,
Hertel et al., 2007, Kumar et al., 2015, United States Environmental Protection
Agency, 2014a). In Europe, the routine measuring networks need to have low
uncertainty and high reliability in order to comply with Data Quality Objec-
tives for ﬁxed measurements speciﬁed by the European Air Quality Directive
(European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2008)3. This is
reﬂected in the price of routine measuring instruments4 resulting in a sparse
distribution of stations and consequently a relatively coarse spatial resolution
in air pollution monitoring (Mead et al., 2013, Castell et al., 2013).

1.3
The perspectives of low-cost sensing
Although low-cost sensors cannot provide the same sensitivity, stability, accu-
racy, and precision as traditional routine monitoring stations (Castell et al.,
2013, Hertel et al., 2016), they can potentially be a valuable supplement in the
monitoring programs (United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2014a,
Tsujita et al., 2005, Aleixandre and Gerboles, 2012), and perhaps partly re-
place traditional reference stations (Gerboles and Buzica, 2009). Employing
low-cost sensors can give better spatial and temporal resolution of pollution
maps (Hasenfratz et al., 2015b, Lerner et al., 2015, Gerboles and Buzica, 2009),
improve the knowledge about pollution dynamics, identify hot-spots, and vali-
date or reconsider location of routine monitoring stations (Kumar et al., 2015,
Tsujita et al., 2005). Furthermore, the sensors can be used as a monitoring
tool by municipalities, consultants or researchers (Gerboles and Buzica, 2009,
United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2014a), as personal exposure
monitors (Mead et al., 2013, Castell et al., 2013), for emergency response and
hazard-warning systems (Kumar et al., 2015), and for monitoring and control-
ling emissions at the source (Fujita and Campbell, 2013, van Wijnen et al.,
1995).
The prospect of getting huge amount of reliable air quality data from a dense
network of sensors, either operated by professional operators, citizens or a com-
bination, entails a vast array of opportunities. More data allow for better cal-
ibration and validation of air pollution dispersion models, chemical transport
models and exposure models (Gerboles and Buzica, 2009, Kakosimos et al.,
2010, Reis et al., 2015), improved environmental impact assessments (Hertel
et al., 2016), better identiﬁcation and characterization of sources (United States
Environmental Protection Agency, 2014a), as well as interactive maps with
minimal exposure routes (Hasenfratz et al., 2015a). Adapting ”opportunistic
sensing” through openly available databases7 would further detail the informa-
tion available (Kumar et al., 2015, Campbell et al., 2008), e.g. a crowdsensing
project could collect data about urban morphology along with sensor read-
ings, enabling better estimates of spatial variation in physical parameters as
aerodynamic roughness and albedo (See et al., 2015, Métral et al., 2007).
Finally, demonstration campaigns with school classes or volunteering citizens
can lead to better education and increased awareness of air quality issues, as
well as empower students and citizens to act as ambassadors for the environ-
ment (United States Environmental Protection Agency, 2014a, Kumar et al.,
2015, Paulos et al., 2008).

The performance and applicability of low-cost air pollution sensors - and sensor

electrochemical sensors8. Furthermore, both gasses are among the ’priority pol-

diﬀerent technologies, among which the electrochemical and metal oxide sensors
are most common, as described in Appendix D. The Alphasense Ltd. (UK)
amperometric electrochemical NO2 sensors are tested in this study, as these
have been reported to be among the most sensitive electrochemical sensors
(Aleixandre and Gerboles, 2012).

2.1
General development of low-cost gas sensors
Low-cost gas sensing is not a new phenomenon, and already in the 1980s re-
search was focusing on the optimization of sensitivity, reproducibility, response
time, aging and speciﬁcity of such sensors (Salardenne, 1987, Korotcenkov,
2013, Gerboles and Buzica, 2009). In the late twentieth century, the sensitiv-
ities achievable were generally in the ppm range15, enabling use, primarily in
automative control devices for industrial processes, automobile pollution con-
trol and coarse environmental monitoring (Azad, 1992, Weppner, 1987, Weetall
and Mccurley, 1996, Instrument Science and Technology Group, 1986, Lund-
ström, 1996). Besides sensitivity, limitations of use were imposed by problems
with selectivity and stability (Pijolat et al., 1999, Carotta et al., 2000, 2001,
Nakagawa et al., 2000).
The on-going research in the ﬁeld has advanced the sensitivity considerably
and in the 2010s low-cost gas sensors have many applications (Korotcenkov,
2013, Snyder et al., 2013). A wide range of technologies are available includ-
ing electrochemical, resistive, piezoelectric, optical, magnetic and thermometric
(Arshak et al., 2004, Korotcenkov, 2013). The most common types for ambient
environmental monitoring of NO2 and O3 are amperometric electrochemical
sensors and resistive metal oxide sensors (Akbar et al., 2006, Aleixandre and
Gerboles, 2012, Kumar et al., 2015). Appendix D outlines the working mecha-
nisms and inherent advantages and limitations of various gas sensor types.
In the twenty-ﬁrst century, micro-fabrication techniques using microﬂuidic, op-
tical, and nanostructured elements have improved sensitivities while lowering

2.4
Challenges
This section lines up the challenges reported in the abovementioned studies.
Temperature inﬂuence is severe and must be corrected for (Aleixandre and
Gerboles, 2012, Mead et al., 2013, Spinelle et al., 2013a). Metal oxide sensors
have been reported to react to temperature variation in a ”complicated” way,
which is normally corrected for with empirical equations (Tsujita et al., 2005).
Correction for humidity is also of importance (Jiao et al., 2015, Spinelle et al.,
2013a, 2015b, Mead et al., 2013). In general, the relation between signal and
concentration remains a challenge (Castell et al., 2013, Spinelle et al., 2015b,
Kumar et al., 2015), i.e. the measurement error depends highly on the choice
of calibration, post processing and artifact removal methodology (Mead et al.,
2013, De Vito et al., 2009, Tsujita et al., 2005).
Speciﬁcity, also denoted selectivity, cross-sensitivity and interference, is a gen-
eral problem (De Vito et al., 2008, Hasenfratz, 2015, Austin et al., 2006, Piedrahita
et al., 2014). The Alphasense NO2 sensors used by Mead et al. (2013) had ap-
proximately 100 % interference with O3, i.e. all O3 was captured by the sensor
and accounted for as NO2. This O3 interference is removed in the NO2-B42F
version of the Alphasense NO2 sensor used in this study28. There is a tradeoﬀ
between selectivity and sensitivity, meaning that high sensitivity allows more
interfering gasses to penetrate to the working electrode. The matter is further
discussed in Appendix D.

tion, September 18th, 2015)

Long term stability is a problematic issue (Aleixandre and Gerboles, 2012, De
Vito et al., 2008, Hasenfratz, 2015, Tsujita et al., 2005). It was reported by
Aleixandre and Gerboles (2012) to range from 2 % drift per year up to 15 %
drift per year, often more severe for metal oxide sensors (Spinelle et al., 2015b).
Periods with absence of oxygen and/or humidity can aﬀect the stability of some
sensors (Aleixandre and Gerboles, 2012). The deterioration of accuracy over
time creates a need for frequent recalibration (Tsujita et al., 2005, Hasenfratz,
2015). Reliability, robustness, durability of the sensing element are also con-
cerns (Kumar et al., 2015, Castell et al., 2013), as the working time of a sensor
is typically only half a year to a few years (Kumar et al., 2015). The minimum
ambient operating temperature can be quite high for some sensors (e.g. -5 ◦C
for the Aeroqual 500) (Lin et al., 2015).
Wind velocity have been showed to aﬀect sensor readings (Gerboles and Buzica,
2009, Lerner et al., 2015), and hysteresis have been observed and linked to
humidity (Spinelle et al., 2014a, 2015a). Piedrahita et al. (2014) report non-
linearity for metal oxide sensors and Spinelle et al. (2015b) report a relatively
slow response time of metal oxide sensors.
Comparability and consistency is reported as problematic (Castell et al., 2013,
Kumar et al., 2015), and the resolution is sometimes too low (Hasenfratz, 2015).
Furthermore, Mead et al. (2013) conclude that ’careful design of conditioning
circuitry is important’ when measuring in the ppb range.
The costs associated with low-cost monitoring networks, both professionally
driven and crowdsensing projects, are relatively high.
These include data
management, post-processing, analysis, visualization, and maintenance (e.g.
calibration, battery replacement, physical maintenance) (Kumar et al., 2015).
According to Rionda et al. (2013), ”the motivation of citizens to keep using
sensors in the medium and long range decreases over time” in the absence of
motivation techniques such as competitions with prizes.
The low-cost sensors are continually being improved, and many of the above-
mentioned challenges will be addressed in future development (US EPA, 2013).
Many properties of the sensors (e.g. sensitivity, stability, response time, inter-
ference), can be improved by changing the composition and structure of the
sensing material at nanoscale (Izu et al., 2003, Kumar et al., 2015, Aleixandre
and Gerboles, 2012, Gaury et al., 2013, Franke et al., 2006).

2.5.1
Literature recommendations
This subsection is based on the recommendations of the Protocol for evalua-
tion and calibration of low-cost gas sensors by the JRC (Spinelle et al., 2013a),
which is also summarized in greater detail in Appendix A. The JRC protocol
prescribes a number of experiments: Response time, pre-calibration, repeatabil-
==PAGE:29==
2.5 Recommended test methods
13
ity, short term drift, long term drift, interference, main calibration/validation
experiment, and ﬁeld calibration/validation experiment (see Appendix A).
The main point of the JRC protocol is that the sensor should be calibrated in
the laboratory and tested in the ﬁeld. If the uncertainty estimated from the
main laboratory experiment is complying with the criteria in the Data Quality
Objective in the Air Quality Directive (European Parliament and Council of
the European Union, 2008), the sensor is accepted for indicative measurements.
Otherwise it is rejected and additional lab experiments needs to be carried
to hopefully improve the model description and decrease the uncertainty (see
Figure A.2 in Appendix A). If it is not possible to decrease the uncertainty
through a more extensive calibration and model formulation, the split-sample
approach is employed, i.e. half of the data is used for ﬁeld calibration and the
other half for validation, to calibrate and validate a new model with the ﬁeld
data. If the uncertainty of the new model is complying with the Data Quality
Objective, the sensor may be valid for use at the site of the ﬁeld experiment
under conditions represented during the ﬁeld experiment.
Repeatability and short and long term drift
Repeatability, short and long term drift are measures of the variability of the
measurements within tests of three averaging times (ATs), three days and three
months, respectively.
The repeatability estimate is again used to estimate
the limit of detection and limit of quantiﬁcation. The estimate of the short
term drift is used to determine the maximum time between similar tests, while
the estimate of the long term drift is used to estimate a maximum sampling
period without recalibration, and can be included in the model equation or
contribute to measurement uncertainty. A more detailed description is found
in Sections 3.2.2, A.2.3, and A.2.4 in Appendix A. It is common to let the sensor
operate continuously for at least a week before calibrating (Williams et al., 2013,
Aleixandre et al., 2013, Piedrahita et al., 2014). Spinelle et al. (2014a, 2015a)
and Gerboles et al. (2015a) recommend performing the laboratory calibration
with concentrations in random order, or by a ”ram up - ram down - ram up”
approach, to identify possible hysteresis eﬀects.
Interference
Tests should be made to test the interference of temperature and humidity,
gaseous compounds other than the target gas, the air matrix, pressure, and
hysteresis.
In the case of gaseous compounds, interference is also denoted
cross-sensitivity.
Temperature and relative humidity can be expected to aﬀect the sensitivity to
the target gas (sensor gain) and sensor baseline (zero oﬀset) (Hitchman et al.,
1997, Mead et al., 2013). At the ppb level, the eﬀect on sensor baseline (zero
oﬀset) is dominating to the eﬀect on sensitivity (gain) (Mead et al., 2013), hence,
a linear calibration can be simpliﬁed to include temperature and humidity
eﬀects on the baseline (i.e.
not on gain).
However, Spinelle et al. (2015a)
identiﬁed a sensitivity dependence on temperature and humidity even at the

Laboratory calibration
The JRC protocol recommends doing a calibration experiment with a set of
varying levels of temperature, humidity and all other parameters found inﬂu-
encing the sensors. Subsequently, either a physical or parametric model should
be ﬁtted to the resulting data. The calibration is usually carried out either as a
linear regression, multivariate linear regression or using more complex machine
learning approaches. All methods share the same procedure of minimizing the
error between measurement and a reference by ﬁtting a model to the data.
Piedrahita et al. (2014) state that the simpler linear regression models are
preferable for ease of implementation. Spinelle et al. (2015b), De Vito et al.
(2009) do multivariate linear calibrations of the Alphasense NO2-B4 electro-
chemical sensor, with ﬁve constants including O3, relative humidity and tem-
perature as variables, as described by Eq. A.4 in Appendix A, minimizing the
squared residuals between sensor and reference. Spinelle et al. (2014a) use mul-
tiple linear regression to obtain a model of the form of Eq. A.6 in Appendix A,
using the Akaike Information Criterion to optimize the model with subsequent
’validation’ by plotting residuals versus both the ﬁtted value and each of the
variables to check for homogeneity.
Field validation experiment
The JRC protocol recommends a ﬁeld deployment of minimum three months
to identify additional errors not covered by the laboratory tests. The uncer-
tainty of the model developed in the laboratory tests shall be evaluated and if it
complies with the Air Quality Directive, the sensor is approved for ”indicative
measurements”. If the uncertainty of the previously developed model is too high,
an alternative approach is to ﬁt a new physical or statistical model to the ﬁeld
experiments. In this case - even if the uncertainty is low - the sensor node can-
not be veriﬁed for use outside of the speciﬁc location, micro-environment and
conditions represented by the ﬁeld deployment period (Spinelle et al., 2013a).
Several studies conclude that laboratory experiments are helpful for studying
the mechanisms and responses of the sensor in various chemical and meteoro-
logical conditions, but the response characteristics observed in the laboratory
are rarely reproduced in ﬁeld experiments when the air matrix is more com-
plex and physical conditions are more variable (Gerboles and Buzica, 2009,
Piedrahita et al., 2014, Kamionka et al., 2006). Furthermore, several authors
note that factory calibration is generally suﬃcient for ppm level tests in lab,
but for outdoor conditions (e.g. diurnal and seasonal change in temperature)
and target gas concentrations at ppb levels more complex methods are needed
(Mead et al., 2013, Shallcross et al., 2009, Kumar et al., 2015).

2.5 Recommended test methods

Such complex methods include post processing of data, as the following scheme
proposed by Mead et al. (2013) which assumes relatively stable levels of NO2:
• A temperature-corrected baseline curve of NO2 is deﬁned by changing the
value at time t to the minimum measurements from the interval t ± ∆t
for all t.
• A linear function or exponential function is ﬁtted relating the temperature
to the NO2 data. A new value of ∆t (in an allowed interval between 50
s and 1750 s) is chosen for every day of measurements, such that the
ﬁt between temperature and NO2 is optimized. Assuming the minimum
value is stable, the ﬁtted model relates the minimum value (which can be
thought of as the baseline value) to a sensor reading at all temperatures.
• The ﬁtted model of baseline values is subtracted from the NO2 data.
Tsujita et al. (2003) propose an automatic on-ﬁeld recalibration scheme to
face sensor stability issues, based on identiﬁcation of conditions in which ”pol-
lution becomes low and uniform in a certain local area”. In such conditions
automatic recalibration of sensor oﬀsets is activated29.
Saukh et al. (2015)
and Hasenfratz et al. (2012) describe ”multi-hop-”, ”rendezvous-” or ”on-the-ﬂy-
”calibration, where a network of sensors is calibrated more eﬃciently by letting
freshly calibrated sensors serve as references in the calibration of other sensors
whenever these are in close vicinity. The same approach can be used to identify
faulty sensors in a network (Saukh et al., 2014).
Using multisensors is a way of circumventing the cross-sensitivities and physi-
cal dependencies of sensors (Kamionka et al., 2006, Spinelle et al., 2015b), as
an array of diﬀerent sensors will usually react diﬀerently to the same change
in stimuli. Such systems are normally calibrated with a machine learning tech-
nique as artiﬁcial neural networks (Spinelle et al., 2015b, Reza Nadaﬁet al.,
2010, De Vito et al., 2008, De Vito et al., 2008) or fuzzy logic. De Vito et al.
(2009) report diﬃculties in accounting for seasonal changes, and site-speciﬁc
concentration distributions with this approach, which is also why Spinelle et al.
(2013a) recommends to not use ﬁeld-calibrated sensors outside of the speciﬁc
site and parameter range of the calibration experiment.

Response time
The ﬁrst experiment was designed for studying the response time of the sensors.
This is important since the response time is required to be lower than a quarter
of the desired averaging time (AT), i.e. the duration for which a limit value (LV)
is associated in the Air Quality Directive (European Parliament and Council of
the European Union, 2008, European Commision, 2010, Spinelle et al., 2013a).
As the results will eventually be compared to LV with a required averaging
period of AT, all following tests should have a minimum duration of AT (see
Section A.2.1 for further details). Rising response time is determined by a sharp
increase in concentration of the target gas according to the illustration in Figure
3.3. The lag time is the duration from changing the concentration to the sensor
reaches 10 % of the steady state value, and the rise time is the duration from
measuring 10 % to 90 % of the steady state value. The rising response time is
the combination of lag time and rise time. Similarly, the lag time for fall is the
time from a sharp drop in concentration to the measurements start to drop,
the fall time is the duration from the ﬁrst drop in measurements to measuring
10 % of the start value, and the response time (fall) is the combination of lag
time and fall time.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
5.1
Comments on results
There was a bit of stagnant water in sensor node B2, between the aluminium
box and the stainless steel plate after the second and third week of deploy-
ment, which is around the same time when the signal starts to exhibit strange
behaviour (see Figures G.18-G.19). It is likely that a disturbance in the elec-
tronics due to this humidity is entirely or partly responsible for the strange
measurements in this particular sensor node.
The limit of quantiﬁcation (12.7-15.6 ppb) will often conﬂict with the levels
that are interesting to measure, especially in rural settings, but given that the
limit of detection is adequately low (3.8-4.2 ppb), it is not a serious limitation
to the applicability of the sensors. Besides, the tests for repeatability, limit and
detection and limit of quantiﬁcation are somewhat biased, as the temperature
conditions in the laboratory could not be kept stable within a relative standard
deviation of 2 %. This means the actual values might be lower. The response
time of 1-3 minutes, is in line with what is stated in the datasheet (see Figure
E.3), and it is clearly lower than the required quarter of the desired AT of 1-8
hours, so the sensor is adequately quick for indicative measurements of urban
NO2 and O3. However, when aiming at personal exposure assessment, it is
important to notice the limits imposed by the response time.
The large burn-in eﬀect is interesting and important to remember in future mea-
suring campaign (especially if the sensors are to be calibrated by a colocation
with a reference station). The sensors are turned oﬀfor about an hour for data
collection once a week during the deployment period without a renewed burn-in
period, but one week (almost) without power between the pre-deployment and
main deployment, gives an equally long burn-in period (roughly) as before the
pre-deployment.
It is promising that WE is, to some extend, positively correlated to NO2 mixing
ratio, while AE is practically uncorrelated to both NO2 and O3 mixing ratios.
An interesting observation, is the weak negative correlation between WE and
O3 mixing ratio.
However, as no sensitivity of WE to O3 was observed in
the laboratory experiments (see Figure G.1), it is most likely caused by the
underlying negative correlation of NO2 and O3 mixing ratios, as seen in Figure
G.20, rather than by an actual reaction between WE and O3.
5.2
Keeping track of the zero
The laboratory calibration resulted in zero oﬀsets that deviated ± 10-20 %
from the factory calibration, while the sensitivity only deviated with ± 2-5 %.
The oﬀset and sensitvity are expected to have changed since factory calibration,
as we have embedded them in new electronics with some amount of electronic
==PAGE:58==
42
5 Discussion
gain and noise. Furthermore, the oﬀsets are not expected to be constant in
time, but have a signiﬁcant drift (R. Baron, personal communication, October
26th to December 18th, 2015).
Using the laboratory calibration of zero oﬀsets and sensitivity in Model 1 were
generally able to reproduce the dynamics of reference mixing ratio during the
ﬁeld deployment, albeit with an oﬀset resulting in high errors and uncertainty
(MAE around 10-20 ppb and uCR of 10-30 ppb). As the dynamis are only a
function of the diﬀerence in electrode potential and sensitivity (in Model 1),
the sensitivity obtained in the laboratory calibration is assumed to usable in
ﬁeld conditions. As the laboratory calibrated sensitivities were not far from the
factory calibrated sensitivities the latter can be seen as an excellent starting
point.
The problem, however, is the zero oﬀset. The change in the zero oﬀsets from
laboratory settings to ﬁeld settings are most likely caused by entering a new
microclimate with completely diﬀerent temperature, humidity and wind con-
ditions. The two-day calibration of Model 2 - which only introduces a linear
temperature and humidity correction of the oﬀset - changes the ﬁt of the en-
tire deployment period by 20-50 %, by reducing the gap in oﬀsets between the
laboratory calibration and the ﬁeld deployment. The problem with the ﬁeld
calibration is that there is no guarantee that the calibration based on a few days
can be extrapolated in time to an entire deployment period, and in space to a
diﬀerent microenvironment. Using a calibration period both before and after
the measuring campaign, improves the ﬁt further, but leaves the same problem.
The oﬀset could in theory be accounted for with laboratory calibrations alone,
if a wide range of diﬀerent physical conditions (temperature, humidity, pres-
sure, interfering gasses, etc.) were included in the laboratory experiments, and
accounted for in the laboratory calibrated model description, as recommended
by Spinelle et al. (2013a).
The drift in the zero oﬀsets cannot be directly observed from the ﬁeld exper-
iments, as the concentration is never zero, but the AE signal provides a very
good indication of the drift rate as it is (mostly) independent of the target gas
concentrations and (in most cases) show a steady linear trend. The tricky part
is that if the drift is equal in WE and AE, there is no need for drift correction
as the signals cancel out in the model formulations. This is seen for e.g. A2-O3
in Figure G.4.
Comparing the drift rates from the laboratory experiments (up to 9 mV in
5 days) with the apparent drift in AE during the ﬁeld deployment (6 mV in
20 days), hints that the disturbances in the signal observed in the laboratory,
may be something else than an actual drift. The laboratory experiments are
relatively short periods of measurements with postprocessed averaging times
of one minute, where the ﬁeld deployment is several weeks with averaging
times of one hour. It is likely that the zero drift is behaving highly non-linear
and irrational on short timescales, but shows a linear smooth trend on longer
timescales.

test relative concentrations.

5.3
Comments on the models
Assuming that part of the drift observed in the sensor is caused by burn-in
eﬀects, it would be more suitable with a log or inverted relationship to elapsed
time since startup in the drift correction. Such an expression would converge
with time, whereas the implemented linear relation to elapsed time since labo-
ratory calibration, is diverging. However, the laboratory experiments show no
sign of the drift being only related to startup events, hence it seems unsuit-
able with a correction term only related to such events. Another option would
be to have a log or inverse correction based on elapsed time since calibration,
but again it seems impossible to justify why the steep correction should be at
the arbitrarily chosen calibration period. Hence a diverging drift correction
has been implemented with the important limitation that frequent calibrations
must be undertaken to ”reset” the zero oﬀset and rate of the drift correction.
As the objective function is weighing small errors relatively high compared to
MAE and especially RMSE, it makes sense why the calibration of B4, which
shows extreme deviations from the reference, is very poor in terms of MAE and
RMSE. Table 4.8 and 4.9 illustrate the risk of overﬁtting, as Model 3 neatly
ﬁts the data when calibrated with the full deployment period, but when Model
2 and 3 are only calibrated with the ﬁrst two days, Model 2 performs better
than Model 3. This is because Model 3 adapts to the calibration period in
such a degree that it limits extrapolation. Model 3 has the ability to adjust to
a linear drift and, when applied to e.g. A2-NO2 which shows a clearly linear
drift trend (see Figure G.14), the performance is drastically improved when
provided the ﬁrst and last day instead of the two ﬁrst days. This is clearly
because the calibration is able to account for the drift.
Since the temperature eﬀect on the oﬀset may be dependent on recent condi-
tions, i.e. a steep change from X ◦C to X+10 ◦C, will change the oﬀset, but
if the temperature stays at X+10 the oﬀset may go back eventually, it could
be interesting to include a variable describing recent temperature changes in
future improvements. However, given the relatively low performance improve-
ment of Model 3 compared to Model 2, the simplicity of Model 2 is preferably,
and it is recommended that future projects work with Model 2 or a similar
simple model formulation.
5.4
Recommendation for future measuring campaigns
The above-mentioned observations lead to two possible directions for future
projects targeting scientiﬁc research groups or consultants: a) Field calibra-
tions at a reference monitoring station, either before, after, or both before and
after every measuring campaign, or; b) Investment in laboratory equipment
which allows for independent control of environmental factors as temperature,
humidity, pressure, wind and concentrations of various interfering gasses. In

case of a), the challenge will be to make sure the conditions covered by the cal-
ibration period are representable for the condtions experienced by the sensor
during the measuring campaign. In case of b), detailed studies of the sensor
response to a wide range of stimuli should be carried out, and an improved
model based on these measurements should be tested at a ﬁeld reference sta-
tion as described by (Spinelle et al., 2013a). I consider a) to be the most feasible
option at the present stage of development, and I think measuring campaigns
should both start and end with a period of at least one week colocation with
a routine measuring station, given that the sensor has not been turned oﬀfor
longer periods during the previous week and thus requires burn-in time.
Even in case of a), it would be very helpful to initiate all sensors with at least
one month start-up period in zero air under steady laboratory condtitons. This
would enable better estimation of the drift trend in each sensor39. After the
startup experiment, measurement campaigns could be undertaken, beginning
and ending with a period of 1-2 weeks colocated with a reference instrument.
In periods between measurements, the sensors should preferably be left on at
zero air, to avoid a long burn-in time at the next measuring campaign.
To minimize working hours, a device should be constructed that allows for
easy connection of sensors to zero air. As a minimum, gas hoods should be
constructed, so that all sensors can be calibrated simultaneously. Furthermore,
a wireless system sending data to a database, would grossly reduce working
hours in the long run. It would be a notable investment, but deﬁnitely rec-
ommendable if the sensors are to be used for monitoring campaings. Software
should be developed to extract and process data. The software should be semi-
automatic, meaning that a user should visually detect outliers and trends, and
assess data quality.
More experience is required with a moving reference and short averaging times,
before the sensors can be trusted for applications targeting personal exposure
assessment. The ideal setup would be a van with a reference monitor mounted
on the roof, and a rack for mounting low-cost sensors for tests.
The price of a sensor is low, in spite of being tripled by the necessary electronic
accesories (as shown in Appendix H), and the lifetime is relatively short as the
signal is expected to be halved in two years. In this light, thought should be
given to the fate of the components after use, and a life cycle analysis of the
sensor nodes should preferably be carried out, with focus on the elctronic waste
burden and possible release of engineered nanomaterials from the sensors.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Four sensor nodes measuring both NO2 and O3 were constructed at a total price
of 495 EUR for an A-type sensor node and 520 EUR for the B-type sensor node.
The sensors behaved highly linear in laboratory calibration experiments, have
a lower detection limit of 4 ppb, a lower limit of quantiﬁcation of 12-16 ppb,
and response times (T90) of 1-3 minutes.
When a simple model (Model 1)
for translating the signal to mixing ratio was calibrated in the laboratory, the
subsequent comparison with an urban monitoring station resulted in an mean
absolute error of 6-25 ppb for the various sensors (excluding one sensor), and a
combined standard uncertainty of 25-50 % of the reference mixing ratio, which
is higher than the hypothesis proposed in Section 1.4.1.
Calibration of a slightly more complex model (Model 2) with the ﬁrst and the
last day of the deployment period resulted in combined standard uncertainties
(uCM) of 7-15 ppb at a reference concentration of 30 ppb, and MAEs of 6-
13 ppb when validating with the remaining days of the ﬁeld deployment. A
third model (Model 3) of increased complexity was calibrated with ﬁeld data
as well, but the increased complexity did not give adequate improvement in
performance.
It can be concluded that the burn-in period is 4-6 days when the sensor have
been only sporadically powered during one week, while one hour without power
gives a burn-in of 0.5-1 hours. The main issues with these sensors is keeping
track of the zero. A drift of was observed in the zero oﬀset during laboratory
calibrations of up to 40 ppb in 5 days, while the drift in ﬁeld deployments was
a factor of 2-5 lower.
In future projects, a decision should be made whether to invest laboratory
facilities for more elaborate laboratory testing, or if focus should be on ﬁeld
calibration even if this poses some limitations on how for the campaigns can be
extrapolated in time and space from the calibration. This report recommends
the latter, given that all measuring campaigns start and end with at least
one week of colocation with a routine measuring station. Moreover, it is worth
considering to invest in developing a wireless system sending data to a database
with semi-automatic data analysis. Such an investment would be costly up
front, but drastically reduce working hours if the sensors are to be used more
extensively in the future.
• Inert calibration hoods should preferably be constructed, or bought, to
enable simultaneous zero calibration of all sensors.
• The immediate recommendations of the report are to start a laboratory
zero air measuring campagin of at least one month to further study the
drift in the zero oﬀset.

The Institute for Environment and Sustainability at the European Commision’s
Joint Research Centre (JRC), has through the The Metrology for Pollutants in

sensors for monitoring of air pollution (Spinelle et al., 2013a). This appendix
gives an overview of the tests and methodologies recommended in the protocol.
Everything in this appendix is from Spinelle et al. (2013a) unless otherwise ref-
erenced, and most content is directly cited or rephrased from the protocol, but
citation marks are omitted. The JRC protocol is itself build on Joint Commit-
tee for Guides in Metrology (JCGM) (2008) and European Commision (2010),
so most of the following can ultimately be traced back to these two sources.
Before beginning the tests, it is recommended to clearly specify the sensor de-

relevant limit values and the desired deployment environment should be spec-
iﬁed beforehand. All available information about the sensor, e.g. sensitivity,
selectivity, stability, calibration/validation experiments and model equations
for calibration, should be collected and taken into consideration. Caution is
given that after validation the sensor should be only applied in the same con-
ditions as it was tested under, and that periodical recalibration is ”strongly
recommended”.

Tropospheric NO2 and O3
This appendix brieﬂy desribes the formation, removal and toxicity of NO2 and
O3. The three main reactions governing the concentrations of NO2 and O3 in
the troposphere are (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001):
NO2 + hv →NO + O•
(B.1)
O • +O2 →O3
(B.2)
NO + O3 →NO2 + O2
(B.3)
where NO2, NO and O3 denote gaseous nitrogen dioxide, nitric oxide and ozone,
respectively, hv denote the energy of a photon and O• denote an oxygen radical.
The reactions have corresponding rate constants of 0.5 min−1, 21.8 ppm−1
min−1 and 26.6 ppm−1 min−1 respectively, giving characteristic lifetimes for
NO2 photolysis and the O• radical of 2 min and 13 µs, respectively45.
Furthermore, volatile organic compunds (VOCs), as formaldehyde, are involved
in the oxidization of NO to NO2 (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001):
HCHO + hv + 2O2 →2HO2 • +CO
(B.4)
NO + HO2• →NO2 + OH•
(B.5)
where HCHO is gaseous formaldehyde, HO2• is an hydroperoxy radical, and
OH• is a hydroxyl radical.
NOx is a primary pollutant, albeit most NOx emissions are on the form of
NO (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). Conversion from NO til NO2 take
place by Eq. B.3 if O3 is present, and by Eq. B.5 if hydroperoxy radicals are
present46. During sunlight in an urban setting , conversion from NO to NO2
will normally occur readily, since NO2 is photolyzed by Eq. B.1, producing O•
radicals that reacts with oxygen to produce ozone by Eq. B.2. Furthermore, in
sunlight VOCs photolyze by Eq. B.4, leading to hydroperoxy radicals. During
nighttime, NO may not be converted to NO2 as the concentrations of O3 and
HO2• may be limiting the reaction, and NO2 will not be broken down by
photolysis.
Unike NOx, O3 is a secondary pollutant, thus not emitted directly, but cre-
ated from emitted precursors. Eq. B.2 is the main formation reaction of O3
in the atmosphere (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001), and Eq. B.1 is the

Dynamically, it is expected that when darkness falls, O3 concentrations de-

concentration of NO2 is expected to increase slightly until either O3 or NO is

decrease by photolysis, and the O3 concentration will start to build up again,
giving a inverse relationship between the two species. As NO2 is a primary
pollutant, peaks overruling the inverse relationsship can be expected at rush
hour in the morning and late afternoon.
B.1
Nitrogen dioxide
Nitrogen dioxide gas (NO2) is identiﬁed as a key air pollutant and a key species
in the formation of smog, degrading the quality of life and aﬀecting mortality
rates (World Health Organization, 2006, Korotcenkov, 2013, Manahan, 2009).
It is often used as a proxy for general air pollution levels (Levy et al., 2014), as
the presence of NO2 is ”often strongly correlated with other toxic pollutants”
(World Health Organization, 2006). NO2 is a reddish-brown toxic gas, with a
”characteristic sharp biting odor” (Korotcenkov, 2013). It is soluble in water
droplets to produce acid rain (Fenger, 2009).
When NOx reacts with vari-
ous VOCs, the resulting mixtures sometimes gives a white haze (Korotcenkov,
2013). In the presence of hydrocarbons and UV light, NO2 is the main source of
tropospheric ozone and nitrate aerosols, constituting an important fraction of
the ambient air particulate matter (PM2.5) mass (World Health Organization,
2006).
The toxic eﬀects of NO2 include decreased lung function, aggraviation of respi-
ratory diseases (bacterial pneumonia, pulmonary embolism, lung cancer), car-
diovascular diseases (heart attack, stroke, heart diseases, artery diseases, ve-
nous thrombus) and increased asthma incidence and severity (United States
Environmental Protection Agency, 2014a). Is is often diﬃcult to rule out the
eﬀect of a number of other, highly correlated co-pollutants, such as UF parti-
cles, NO, PM or benzene, when assessing the toxicology of NO2 World Health
Organization (2006).
Furthermore, nitrogen compounds contributes to the acidiﬁcation and nutri-
ent enrichment (eutrophication, nitrogen saturation) of soil and surface water,
which leads to oxygen depletion in waters, losses of plants and animals, and
changes in biodiversity losses (United States Environmental Protection Agency,
2014a).

Ozone
Ozone (O3) is a colorless gas with slight odor in large concentrations (Korot-

throat irritation, and congestion” as well as worsening ”bronchitis, emphysema,

The ecotoxic eﬀects of tropospheric O3 include ”damaging vegetation by in-

plants, and decreasing crop yields” (Manahan, 2009). Damage to plants may
alter ecosystem structure, reduce biodiversity, and plant growth.

1. Conductivity/resistive sensors.
Physical change: Conductivity.
Upon
sensing the gas, the material changes its properties leading to a diﬀerent
resistance. Examples are metal oxide (MeO) sensors, conducting polymer
composites and intrinsically conducting polymers.
2. Piezoelectric sensors. Physical change: Mass. Upon sensing the gas, the
mass of the piezolecetric sensor coating changes, resulting in a change
in the resonance frequency which is measurable. Examples are surface
acoustic wave (SAW) devices, quartz crystal microbalance (QCM).
3. Optical sensors. Physical change: Optical properties. Upon sensing of
the gas, ﬁbres coated with a ﬂourescent dye encapsulated in a polymer
matrix, change polarity, leading to a measurable change in optical prop-
erties such as intensity, spectrum or wavelength of the light. Examples
are ellipsometry, spectroscopy (luminescence, phosphorescence, ﬂuores-
cence), interferometry (white light interferometry, modal interferometry
in optical waveguide structures), spectroscopy of guided modes in opti-
cal waveguide structures (grating coupler, resonant mirror), and surface
plasmon resonance (Homola et al., 1999).
4. Metal oxide semiconductor ﬁeld eﬀect transistor (MOSFET) sensors. Phys-
ical change: Working function. Upon sensing of the gas, the threshold
voltage of the sensor changes, due to a polarization of the surface and
interface between catalytic metal on the surface of the sensor and oxide
layer.
5. Electrochemical sensors. Physical change: Working function. Upon sens-
ing there is an electrochemical reaction between the gas and some elec-
trolyte, producing a current that is then measured.
Piedrahita et al. (2014) compare MeO and electrochemical sensors and state
that electrochemical sensors are used in multiple studies, have high sensitivity,
low detection limit, fast response, low cross-sensitivity, low power consumption
and are cheap compared to some alternatives, but they have ”more complicated
and expensive measurement circuitry” than MeO-sensors, are susceptible to
poisoning, have a shorter life span (1-3 years) and are more expensive and
larger in size than MeO sensors. Spinelle et al. (2015b) report that stability

In the following, working mechanisms of the two sensor technologies applied in
this study are revised: Electrochemical sensors and chemiluminescence sensors.
D.2.1
Electrochemical sensors
Conventional solid-state electrochemical sensors operate with direct current
(dc) methods that are either current-based (amperometric) or voltage-based
(potentiometric). Amperometry is the detection of a substance based on changes
in an electric current (Bard et al., 2001). The sensor consists of a cell with three
or four electrodes of a similar chemical composition, separated by hydrophilic
wetting ﬁlters, ensuring all electrodes are in contact with an electrolyte liquid
(e.g. sulphuric acid in the case of Alphasense) by capillary transport through
the ﬁlters (Alphasense Ltd., 2015f). The Alphasense cell contains four elec-
trodes termed working electrode (WE), reference electrode (RE), counter elec-
trode (CE) and auxiliary electrode (AE), the three ﬁrst of which are shown in
Figure D.3. A redox reaction at the WE generate an electronic charge, which
is balanced by a complimentary redox reaction at CE. The redox pair (i.e. an
oxidation reaction and a reduction reaction) drives a transfer of electrons (i.e.
a current, typically 10-1000 nA per ppm gas) between the working and the
counter electrodes, which is the output of the sensor (Alphasense Ltd., 2015f,
Mead et al., 2013). In the case of NO2 and O3 sensors, WE is oxidized and CE
reduced giving a negative current from WE to CE. This signal is then reversed
in the circuit board to give a corresponding positive voltage.
The working electrode is in contact with the atmosphere through a gas per-
meable polytetraﬂuoroethylene (PTFE) membrane, which protects the sensor

When measuring in clean air, the potentials of WE and CE are (theoretically)

The WE potential, however, is maintained ﬁxed, using the reference electrode,

D.2.2
Chemiluminescence NO2 sensors
Gas-phase chemiluminescence instruments are usually referred to as ’tradi-
tional’ and ’favored’ instruments of NOx analysis(US EPA, 2012, Moretto and
Kalcher, 2014), and are characterized by being reliable, precise and expensive,
typically priced at 10.000-20.000 USD (Piedrahita et al., 2014)
The instrument produces chemically exited NO2, by mixing NO with O3, ac-
cording to the following reaction
NO + O3 →NO2 • +O2
(D.2)
where the • in NO2• denotes the exited state. When the exited species returns
to its ground state, light is emitted which is measured by a photomultiplier. The
intensity of the light is then proportional to the NO concentration (Manahan,
2009). NO2 is converted to NO by passing the air sample through a thermal
converter. Simultaneously measuring the light emission from an airstream that
passed the thermal converter and an airstream thas bypassed the thermal con-
verter gives both the NO and NOx concentration. Subtracting the two gives
the NO2 concentration (Manahan, 2009).
Particulate matter should be ﬁltered at the air inlet as it can interfere with
the signal. Furthermore, the presence of other compounds, especially NH3 and
SO2, in high concentrations may add to the signal as they are also exited by
O3, i.e. there is a small cross sensitivity (Manahan, 2009).

• Ideally the electronic oﬀset and gain are independent of external condi-

• The Alphasense correction document, Alphasense Ltd. (2015h), is not
giving ideal recommendations for ﬁne measurements: ”It should be read
a bit like a essay explaining one way of dealing with these matters”. It is
important to realize that Alphasense Ltd. (2015h) is based on laboratory
measurements with super dry gasses.
• When correcting for the scaling factor (i.e. Eq. E.6): ”I would include
the sensor zero - subtract that ﬁrst”. It would be possible that n is more
optimal with other values than the ones provided (in Table E.4), either
dependent on temperature or constant.
• Temperature correction is tricky, e.g. ”if the temperature increase from
20 ◦C to 30 ◦C, the output goes up, but when the temperature stays at
30 ◦C for some time, the output will start to go down again”.
• ”The sensor oﬀset is not constant in time”
• There are two very diﬀerent things happening when the temperature
changes: 1) Most importantly, the sensor oﬀset changes with tempera-
ture. When the temperature goes to 40 ◦C, the output might go from 0
ppb to 500 ppb even though the actual concentration is still 0 ppb. This
should be the main correction focus. 2) The sensor sensitivity changes
with temperature as the diﬀusion rate of the target gas is temperature
dependent. This temperature dependence is numerically quite small com-
pared to the change of the oﬀset, and contrary to the oﬀset, it is constant
in time.
• Alphasense provide a reasonable measure of the temperature dependency
of the sensor sensitivity in the datasheets. In general, the sensitivity is
well represented by the factory calibration, which is done on expensive,
well-tested equipment, leaving an error margin of roughly 5 %.
It is
possible that the sensitivity is 5-10 % lower outside than what is tested
in the lab, but it’s not a ig deal. The big job is on the temperature oﬀset.
• When doing laboratory experiments, it is very easy to loose a few ppbs
somewhere in the setup. Everything needs to be purged with the gas for
some time, and new equipment always take up some of the target gas -
especially O3.

• The temperature dependency of the sensor oﬀset can be checked in the lab.
Alphasense usually do this regularly by allowing the sensor to equilibrate
to a given temperature for two hours and testing both zero concentration
and a non-zero concentration at each temperature step. However, since
this varies with sensor batch and even between individual sensors, it is
not possible to give a generalized value.
• The sensor zero needs to be reviewed from time to time. The biggest
problem with these sensors is knowing where the zero is at a given time
under a given set of conditions.
• The sensitivity is certainly linear with time, as it just represents the gas
diﬀusing intp the sensor (pure physical chemistry). Thus, temperature
aﬀects it through the diﬀusion rate.
• Humidity changes can be assumed to only aﬀect the sensor oﬀset, ”giving
bumps on the way as the zero is distorted”.
• Electrodes are made with huge mass of nanocatalyst, giving a huge surface
area (around 1-2 m2) per electrode. The NO, NO2 and O3 sensors have
carbon nanocatalysts, which eliminates cross sensitivity to CO. NO2 and
NO are diﬀerentiated as well because ”NO needs biased electronics”. O3
is excluded from the NO2 sensor by a patented ﬁlter with ”a powder that
absorbs all O3 but lets NO2 pass”. It is a regenerative reaction on the
surface, so the powder should not wear out too quickly. The O3 sensor
measures both NO2 and O3. In 2016, there will be a release of improved
NO2 and O3 sensors, with much less temperature dependency.
• The initial stabilization phase after the sensor has been turned oﬀ, is due
to a double layer of electrolyte around the electrode when power is oﬀ,
which acts as a capacitor once power is back on. Once the capacitor has
given oﬀits charge, the current stabilizes.
• Remember, matching sensor data to a reference is very diﬀerent from
showing that it works all the time in all conditions and locations. Going
from a successful lab/ﬁeld experiment with post processing of data, to
a real-time and real-world sensor applicable to all environments takes
possible 100-1000 times more time and eﬀort.

2015), the AirSensEUR shield (Gerboles et al., 2015a), the self-developed GaS-
TON board (Lösch et al., 2008, Kammerer et al., 2003), or Pirello lab board
(De Vito et al., 2009), or other commercial low-power micro controller board
(Li et al., 2014).
The two capacitances of 10 nF and 100 nF are used to decouple the sensors.
The capacitances acts as a high-pass ﬁlter, allowing high frequency disturbances
(noise) on the power signal to pass directly to ground.

F.3
Practical lessons learned
- In HCAB, the chemiluminescence monitors have PTFE ﬁlters similar to the
ones on the sensors, and they get so dirty that they are changed every week!
(but air is also pumped through obviously). Furthermore, care is taken not to
touch the ﬁlters with the ﬁngers as organic material will be oxidised by O3,
disturbing the measurements.
- Beware that the gas hoods are screwed very tight to the sensors, and prefer-
ably do a pressure test - they can easily leak and suck lab air in....
- Working with permeation tubes require planning several weeks, preferably
more, ahead, to ensure stability and proper wieghing..
- Start ordering all components and parts well before assembling deadline. De-
livery can take quite long...
- When working with air ﬂow controllers and pumps, be aware that at low di-
lution of the permeation tube air ﬂow, the ﬂow (from the Brooks Tr5850 mass
ﬂow controller) will only be suﬃcient for one outlet of around 0.5 L/min. Thus,
only one sensor can be calibratred at the time
- Sometimes the datalogger writes a meaningless symbol and starts writting in
a new ﬁle giving sometimes several hundred short textﬁles instead of one long I
don’t know the cause of this, but is started on the same day for both A2 and B2.
- MnO2 has been used earlier as a catalyst to break down O3 to O2, and silicagel
is an eﬀective absorbant for O3 (A. Egeløv, personal communication, January
22th, 2015).

APPENDIX I
Housing
I.1
Final choice
Based on the following considerations about material properties, costs and ease
of implementation, the choice was to glue PTFE ﬁlm onto pre-made aluminium
boxes with a strong epoxy glue. This proved to be impossible. In the process of
glueing the boxes they became coated with epoxy, so the end product is boxes
with an aluminium alloy, coated with hardened epoxy. The assumption is that
epoxy is acceptable as it is relatively inert in its hardened glass-like state.The
chosen design is option C in Figure I.1 with folded stainless steel plates.
I.2
Design criteria and suggestions
A number of considerations are underlying the selection of casing for the sensors,
summarized in the following optimal design criterias:
• Make a cheap solution, that can be easily implemented, also in potential
citizen science projects.
• Keep the sensor dry, out of heavy direct wind and sunlight
• Avoid materials that interact with the target gasses in any way that will
hamper the results.
• Shield electronics from electromagnetic noise
• Have the sensor in contact with ambient air, not hampered by air pockets
or behind massive enclosures
• Have a sort of grit to keep animals away
Alphasense Ltd. (2015j) give usefull recommendations on how to best screen
and ground the sensor node, and Alphasense Ltd. (2015e) provide a number
of design considerations regarding pressure and ﬂow, e.g. avoid constructions
where the ﬂow is directly towards the sensor, and go for a ﬂow across the sensor.
I.3
Material properties
Materials of consideration are polymeric thermoplastics (PTFE/PFA, PEEK,
FEP, ABS, PP and PS), metals and alloys (stainless steel, aluminium), and
ceramics:
• Polyﬂuorotetraethylen (PTFE) also known by the trademark Teﬂon, is
”the most commonly used, the best known, and, perhaps, has the highest

Design A-C entails a folded metal plate, while design D is likely a plastic bucket.
performance” of all the ﬂuorinated thermoplastics (Woishnis and Ebnesaj-
jad, 2012). Perﬂuoroalkoxy (PFA) is ”very like PTFE with very similar
ﬂuorine content and properties”, but unlike PTFE it can by processed
by conventional molten-state methods, and more expensive (Biron, 2007).
Fluorinated ethylene propylene (FEP) is also very similar to PTFE, but it
is a copolymer of tetraﬂuoroethylene and hexaﬂuoropolypropylene (Biron,
2007). FEP is also capable of melt-processing and has a lower service
temperature than PTFE (Larson, 2015). The chemical inertness of all
the ﬂuorinated thermoplasts is ”excellent”, and ”PTFE, PFA and FEP
are only attacked by very few substances, excluding O3 and NO2 (Biron,
2007, Woishnis and Ebnesajjad, 2012).
• Polyetheretherketones (PEEK) Drawbacks: Light and UV sensitive, which
means eﬃcient protection is required for outdoor use. Generally have
good chemical resistance, but can be attacked by chlorine water, oxidiz-
ing agents, concentrated sulfuric and nitric acids, halogens and phenols,
but not O3 and NO2 (Biron, 2007, Woishnis and Ebnesajjad, 2012).
• Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) is a plastic allloy made from the
three polymers represented in the name.
ABS is an amorphous ther-
moplastic, meaning it has ”relatively low mold shrinkage” and is ”less
reistant to chemical attack than a semicrystalline thermoplastic” (Larson,
2015). Resistance to both O3 and SO2 is ”not satisfactory” (Biron, 2007).

Drawbacks: Elesctrostatic build-up, natural sensitivity to heat, UV and
lightning (however, stabilized grades are marketed) (Biron, 2007).
• Polypropylene (PP) is a member of the polyoleﬁn family, which are poly-
mers of alkenes (Larson, 2015).
It is a semi-crystalline thermoplastic
which gives good chemical resistance (Larson, 2015). There is limited
resistance to O3 and satisfactory resistance to SO2 and O2 at room tem-
perature (Biron, 2007).
• Polystyrene (PS) is a transparent rigid solid at room temperature. Draw-
backs: heat sensitivity (melts at 100 ◦C), sensitive to UV light and weath-
ering. Satisfactory resistance to O3 and SO2 at room temperature (Biron,
2007).
• Polyethylenes (PE) are also members of the polyoleﬁn family (Larson,
2015). They are semicrystalline, and therefore oﬀer good chemical resis-
tance. However, it has limited resistance to O3 for the high-density PE
polymers, and not satisfactory resistance for the low-denisty PE polymers
(Biron, 2007).
• Stainless steel comes in many forms and types and is generally quite
chemically reistant (Kelly, 2006).
• Aluminium. In oxidizing environments aluminium form oxides, which are
very hard (Aldridge and Downs, 2011). Pure aluminium has a reduction
potential of -1.7 volts, meaning that it readily reacts with oxygen in the
atmosphere. However, the formation of the oxide layer on the surface
reduces the reduction potential to -0.6 volts, which cause it too behave
much like a noble metal (e.g. cobber or silver) (Zumdahl, 2009). The
reason for the stabilization is that the Al2O3 formed on the surface has
an ionic radius close to the radius of aluminium atoms, enabling a closely
packed oxide layer of 10−6 mm to 10−4 mm (Rayner-Canham and Over-
ton, 2006). Furthermore an electrochemical reaction on the surface fur-
ther increases the oxide layer to a thickness of 0.01 mm (Rayner-Canham
and Overton, 2006). According to (Zumdahl, 2009), the aluminium oxide
layer is most correctly described as Al2(OH)6. Al2O3 is often used as
catalysts support, by providing a high surface area for ”impregnation of
the catalyst” (e.g. Ag or Pt) into a porous material (Aldridge and Downs,
2011). There are also examples in litterature of aluminium oxides being
used as the catalyst in itself (Aldridge and Downs, 2011). The most com-
mon aluminium oxide, Al2O3, also known as alumina, is a well-known
industrial ceramics. I was unable to ﬁnd sources describing the chemical
resistance of alumina to O3 or NO2.
• Ceramics are generally resistant to chemical degradation (Doi et al., 2014,
Pelleg, 2014), and Al2O3 is among the most chemical resistant ceramics
due to its low enthalpy of formation (Doi et al., 2014), but I was unable
to ﬁnd sources for the speciﬁc reaction with O3 or NO2.

The DREAM algorithm
DREAM is an acronym of ”diﬀerential evolution adaptive Metropolis” algo-
rithm. It is a ”multi-chain Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulation
algorithm” (Vrugt, 2016), that ”automatically tunes the scale and orientation
of the proposal distribution en route to the target distribution, and exhibits
excellent sampling efﬁciencies on complex, highdimensional, and multi-modal
target distributions” (Vrugt, 2016). In other words, a large parameter space
can be search eﬀectively by iteratively trying out various combinations and
continously re-evaluating the optimal distribution of the parameter space. For
every parameter combination, the model is evaluated against an evaluation
function, and the algorithm samples the parameters more densely around val-
ues that give better evaluations. In DREAM several chains are active at the
same time, and a randomized jump function seeks to ensure that the chains
don’t get stuck at local optima. (Vrugt, 2016)
Monte Carlo methods are a broad class of computational algorithms that use
repeated random sampling to approximate some multivariate probability dis-
tribution. The simplest Monte Carlo method involves random sampling of the
prior distribution (Vrugt, 2016)
The basis of MCMC simulation is a Markov chain that generates a random
walk through the search space to explore the target distribution. A MCMC
algorithm generates trial moves from the current state of the Markov chain.
Convergence is diagnosed visually be seeing that the chains are mingling among
each other, around a ﬁxed mean, for at good part of the simulations. Numer-
ically, convergence can be assessed through the ”acceptance rate” and multi
chain ˆR- statistic (Gelman and Rubin, 1992).
