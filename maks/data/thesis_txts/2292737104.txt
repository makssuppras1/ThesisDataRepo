Abstract 
 
Faced with the necessity to reduce CO2 emissions and optimize resource management, cities 
have started to develop new integrated solutions which will turn them into “smart cities”. In this 
context, smart energy systems are being developed, in which intermittent and fluctuating electricity 
from renewable energy is integrated by using intelligent electrical and thermal networks and smart 
energy production. This study focuses on smart grids and smart heat pumps as an innovative solution 
to create more sustainability and more efficient energy systems in the urban environment. 
The purpose of the study is to investigate the ability of individual heat pumps to act as an 
intelligent interface between electrical and thermal networks, through smart operation and heat 
storage. This capacity to improve the energy systems’ smartness in cities is analyzed on Nordhavn, a 
new urban area being developed in Copenhagen, and characterized by low energy buildings. A model 
of a smart individual heat pump is developed in energyPRO software and a linear mathematical 
model is created in GAMS, in order to optimize both operation and investment costs. The smart 
individual heat pump system is then compared to more conventional heating solution (district 
heating, conventional heat pump) through a scenario analysis. In order to assess the competitiveness 
of the smart solution, the study performs a private economic analysis from a customer’s perspective 
and a socio-economic analysis. The impact of smart operation on peak shedding is analyzed with a 
comparison of a conventional and smart heat pump. 
The results show that the smart heat pump system is economically attractive for the 
customer, compared to traditional district heating. The smart heat pump system also presents many 
socio-economic benefits since it avoids GHG emissions and contributes to the integration of 
renewable energies by smoothing its electricity consumption. However, the study found out that the 
economic attractiveness largely depends on the heat pump efficiency. Besides, customer’s savings 
generated by smart operation of individual heat pumps compared to conventional individual heat 
pumps are not substantial, and can only be achieved with high demand fluctuations and high 
electricity prices fluctuations. The study concludes that smart individual heat pumps installed in low 
energy buildings contribute to improve the smartness of a city, but that current support mechanisms 
and price incentives must be revised to support the development of this smart heating solution.

Introduction 
 
While cities now occupy only 2% of the earth's surface, they concentrate more than half of the 
world population, consume 75% of energy and generate nearly 80% of CO2 emissions. This trend is likely to 
grow in both developed and developing countries: within 2050, 70% of the earth population is expected to live 
in urban areas [1]. 
Faced with the necessity to reduce CO2 emissions, save the raw materials, optimize resource management, 
fight against climate change but also adapt to changing needs, cities have started to develop new integrated 
solutions which will turn them into “smart cities”. Although the concept of smart city is somehow fuzzy, and 
will be subject to discussion in the report, it mostly relies in thinking, designing and managing the city 
holistically, as a system of systems, in which all the different networks (transportation, electricity, heat, 
communication…) are integrated into one another, through the use of information and communication 
technologies. 
There is currently no smart city in itself, but a multitude of experiments and innovations in some urban areas 
that plan to become smart in a near future. In all these cities, new information and communication 
technologies are at the heart of future smart cities: they collect, transmit and process data in order to provide 
value-added services to municipality officials, service managers and citizens. 
One of the many challenges of the developing smart cities is to ensure a good quality of energy supply, 
while integrating always more renewable energy, through intelligent production, transportation and 
distribution of energy for electricity demand, heat and transportation needs. In this context, the holistic 
approach is a key point for the future development of the smart energy systems. Cities must not anymore think 
exclusively in terms of individual technologies, buildings or components but examine the overall urban energy 
system and the complex interdependencies involved to be able to achieve the optimal solution for 
sustainability. The main hopes in this context rest on a substantial increase in energy efficiency and the 
consistent use of renewable energy sources. However, the integration of intermittent and fluctuating electricity 
generated by wind or solar power presents entirely new challenges. The key to solving these problems is the 
use of smart grids: a new generation of intelligent networks based on real-time bi-directional communication 
between generators, consumers and storage facilities. Urban smart grids will include both electrical and 
thermal networks and incorporate heat pumps as interfaces between these networks. 
Heat pumps are a sustainable solution for all the challenges faced by the city (energy savings, CO2 emissions 
reduction, integration of RE) because they acquire heat from renewable, locally available sources (air, water, 
ground) and produce no local emissions. They are also ideal systems in demand side management as they can 
be easily controlled by a building management system. Their ability to couple electrical and thermal energy 
allows them to store peak electricity in the form of heat, using the building as a thermal mass, and their 
relatively large electrical consumption offers attractive opportunities to help match supply and demand, thus 
improving the smart use of energy.

Introduction 
 
2 
 
All the Nordic metropolitan cities have launched low carbon targets to be reached by 2050. Among them, 
Copenhagen set the very ambitious target of becoming carbon neutral within 2025 [2], while still developing 
new urban areas in the districts of Sydhavn, Ellebjerg and Nordhavn. These cities and new developed urban 
areas may offer demonstration platforms for smart energy systems which pave the way towards a carbon 
neutral Nordic region. Recent studies [3] come to the conclusion that heat pumps associated with low-emission 
buildings, such as the ones that will be built in Nordhavn, can play an important role in the future energy 
systems. These new urban areas thus are hotspots for studying the integration of smart heat pumps in heating 
and electric grids. 
 
The aim of this study is to determine and assess the potential impact of smart stand-alone heat pumps 
on the city’s energy system. 
In order to achieve it, the report will first address the smartness issue by providing, in Part I - Smart 
Cities, a definition of smart cites, with a particular focus on smart energy cities. Copenhagen as a smart city will 
be analyzed and smart solutions associated to energy issues will be described. 
In a second part, the study will focus on smart grids and smart heat pumps as an innovative solution to 
create more sustainable and efficient energy systems. Those two concepts will be defined technically, and 
different heat pump technologies will be investigated. The interaction between smart grids and smart heat 
pumps as well as their theoretical ability to integrate more renewable energy by demand response methods 
will be described. This will lay the theoretical framework for the model further developed. This part will be 
described in Part II - Smart Grid and Smart Heat Pump Technologies on page 17. 
The Part III – Individual Smart Heat Pumpsin an urban environment: Nordhavn’s Case study, will 
present an analysis of different heating strategies, based on smart heat pump technologies, for a new urban 
development area characterized by low energy buildings that will arise from ground in 2020: Nordhavn Phase 
2. A model of a heating system involving heat pumps and smart grid interaction will be developed using 
energyPRO software and the mathematical optimization tool GAMS, in order to consider both operation and 
investment optimizations. The modeling part will involve technical assumptions (heat demand profiiles, 
operational parameters of the heat pump...) and economic assumptions and will aim to answer the following 
questions: 
1. Is integration of heat pumps in Nordhavn’s district heating system competitive? 
2. Which type of heat pump can best perform? 
3. What are the impacts of smart operation of heat pumps on electricity consumption and potential 
economic savings from a resident perspective? 
Impact of the heat pump’s efficiency (COP), heat demand and electricity prices fluctuations will be adressed, 
and smart and conventional heat pump oerations will be compared. It will be determined how competitive 
smart heat pumps are when subject to high fluctuations in heating demand and electricity prices, by 
measurement of the full load hours of the smart heat pumps. This part will be described in Part III Modelling of 
the District Heating Network of Copenhagen on page 31.

Chapter 1: Smart Cities 
 
 
Urban development and its related issues has been for several years the subject of 
many international conferences: Shanghai World Expo in 2010, EcoCité World Summit in Montreal in 
August 2011, Smart City Expo in Barcelona since 2011 among others. The 41st World Expo, held in 
Shanghai in 2010, had the theme "Better City, Better Life" showing that the city becomes a place of 
new opportunities but also new challenges [4]. 
Indeed, while the United Nations Organization announced that two out of three people will live in 
cities by 2030 and that the economic and environmental crises will mostly affect urban areas, the 
efficient treatment of urbanization problems becomes today a global priority. In recent years, the 
question of how we can live differently and in a better environment in a city has become the focus of 
policymakers and private industry alike. “Smart cities” is the latest concept when it comes to building 
the cities of the future. But why is there a need for developing smarter cities? What is a smart city? 
What smart solutions contribute to smarter cities? Is Copenhagen a smart city? Here are the main 
issues that will be presented in this chapter. 
 
1.1 Why is there a need for developing smarter cities? – The 
urban challenges 
 
The global environment in which people live, and people’s lifestyle are undergoing significant 
changes. If people and the Earth are both changing, then surely the cities that link them together 
must also change. Here, we discuss the smart city in terms of global environmental change and 
changing lifestyles. 
 Urbanization and environmental concerns 
One factor behind the need for smart cities is the international call for action in response to changes 
in the global and urban environments. The world in which people live is being changed by resource 
depletion, urban issues, and other problems brought about by climate change and increases in the 
size and concentration of population. 
First of all, a rapid urbanization process is taking place on a global scale. The 21st century is becoming 
known as the century of cities. Already in July 2007, the urban population surpassed the rural 
population in the world. According to the World Urbanization Prospects from the United Nations [1], 
every week, one million people move from rural areas into urban areas, driven by the economic 
opportunities that the cities of the world offer. If today, 4 billion people live in urban areas, this

expected world population, most of whom will live in developing and less-developed countries. Not

The global trend of urbanization and population growth, which puts ever increasing pressure on the

Secondly, cities have a major impact on the economic and social development of nations.  They act as

1.2. What is a smart city? – Definition, characteristics and means 
 
As Caragliu and Nijkamp point out in their study on European Smart Cities [5], problems 
associated with urban agglomerations have usually always been solved by means of creativity, 
human capital, cooperation among relevant stakeholders and scientific ideas: in other words, 
“smart” solutions. Then, what differentiates the Smart City concept from other urban developments? 
The following aims to give a precise definition of the smart city - that will then be used for the study - 
and to point out its essential characteristics. 
1.2.1. Definition 
The “smart city” concept has been very fashionable in the political sphere for a decade. However, it is 
still a quite fuzzy and elusive concept. Many different definitions, more or less technical and abstract 
depending on the field of study (urban economics, urban policies, or urban energies…), can be found 
in the literature, but there is little general consensus. 
Caragliu and Nijkamp [5] identified some characteristics proper to a smart city that tend to be 
common to many of the published research papers. Among them: the utilization of networked 
infrastructure (business and lifestyle services, and ICTs); the important role of social and relational 
capital in urban development; and social and environmental sustainability as a major strategic 
component of smart cities. 
The Copenhagen Cleantech Cluster [6] considers the smart city concept as an extension of the 
sustainable city (or eco-city) concept of becoming more sustainable and livable but by implementing 
high-tech solutions, and by rethinking the relation between the city governance and citizens. 
A.Murray [7] defines the smart city as a combination of three types of cities “integrated in a holistic 
and systemic way”: the knowledge city, which focus on education, personal growth, innovation, and 
intellectual capital development; the digital city, which aims at enabling everyone and everything to 
be interconnected through high-speed data networks, and finally the eco-city, whose focus is on 
environmental sustainability through the use of renewable resources. 
Based on this literature review, the following gives the “Smart City” definition which was chosen to 
be used as the baseline for this study on smart heat pumps: 
“A city is smart when investments in human and social capital and traditional (transport) and 
modern (ICT) communication infrastructure fuel sustainable economic growth and a high quality of 
life, with a wise management of natural resources, through participatory governance.” (Caragliu 
and Nijkamp).

1.2.2. Key characteristics 
Based on this definition and on the research papers and articles review ( [8], [9], [10], [11], [12]), the

organized upon three main characteristics/objectives:

-

The following presents and explain the three main characteristics and objectives of a smart city: 
 Provide a systemic approach of the city: think, design and manage the city as a system of 
systems: 
The city can truly become smart if it can understand and take benefit from the interactions 
between the different functional areas of the city (transport, energy, buildings). The goal is to get 
rid of the silos approach and to promote the cross-sectorial approach through the 
implementation of networks and data infrastructures that will solve the issues at a broad level. 
The network infrastructure for a smart city should be a network of network that uses best-of-
breed technologies [12]. 
 Position the citizen/the user at the heart of the solutions/services and rethink the city 
governance 
In the Smart city, the citizen-user will have access to a more diverse range of services and will 
himself become producer of information: the ICT systems will allow him to report to his 
community technical damage, malfunction, creating a feedback loop from users to the service 
providers. 
The Smart City will need new forms of governance, facilitating access to information "open data" 
(thus meeting the need for transparency of public activities), through ICTs. An increasing 
interaction between the citizen and the political also go in the direction of greater stakeholder 
participation. The smart city is one that will create a digital public space where the round trip 
between the city governance and the citizen will be accelerated [10]. 
 Provide solutions to the challenge of sustainable development and climate change 
Reducing peak loads and energy consumption, minimizing energy losses and optimizing 
production and consumption, saving water: here are some of the issues in line with an 
optimization of the management of energy resources, which is one of the main objectives of the 
smart city. They will be achieved through the use of more renewable energies, better energy 
efficiency and the implementation of “smart grids”. Smart grid technologies provide utilities with 
the tools to interconnect networks (electricity, heating, water, transport) and optimize their 
performance [13]. They are considered to play an essential role in the smart cities to come and 
are presented in more details in Chapter 2.

1.4. Smart energy cities 
 
As the purpose of the study is to model and analyze the impact of smart heat pumps in urban 
environment, the report will from now on focus on the energy aspect of smart cities. 
Since one of the main objective of the smart city is to provide solutions to the challenge of 
sustainable development and climate change, energy systems management are at the heart of the 
smart city concerns. Cities are developing innovative and smart initiatives in order to reduce energy 
consumption, lower energy-related emissions, and enhance quality of service. This introduces the 
concept of the smart energy city. The EU project TRANSFORM1 gives the following definition of a 
smart energy city: 
“The Smart Energy City is highly energy and resource efficient, and is increasingly powered by 
renewable energy sources; it relies on integrated and resilient resource systems, as well as insight-
driven and innovative approaches to strategic planning. The application of information, 
communication and technology are commonly a mean to meet these objectives. The Smart Energy 
City, as a core to the concept of the Smart City, provides its users with a livable, affordable, 
climate-friendly and engaging environment that supports the needs and interests of its users and is 
based on a sustainable economy.” [14] 
This definition takes over the characteristics (system integration, holistic approach, user 
engagement) and means (use of ICTs) developed in the general definition but applies them to the 
specific objective of energy resources management. 
It is important to make the difference between the smart energy city and the green city (or eco-city) 
concept. Although they both have in common the objective of fighting against climate change and 
improving energy efficiency, the way to achieve it is slightly different. The green city concept brings 
innovative solutions to the environmental challenges but without breaking the silo-approach of the 
different energy systems: electricity, heating, transport. On the contrary, in the smart energy city, the 
energy system is redesigned to encourage interactions between transport, electricity and heating, 
through the use of smart energy grids. In other words, the smart energy system extends the green 
city concept by integrating ICTs technologies and holistic thinking in the energy system.

2.1. Smart energy systems 
 
The smart city definition exposed in Chapter 1 insisted on the importance of designing the basic 
city networks (electricity, heat, transport) in a holistic way. The boundaries between the supply 
networks need to be broken in order to take advantage of the synergies that may exist between 
them. This paves the way to new smart energy systems. 
Lund and Thellufsen (2014) define the smart energy system as “an energy system in which smart 
electricity, thermal and gas grids are combined and coordinated to identify synergies between them 
and to achieve an optimal solution for each individual sector as well as for the overall system”. [17] 
The increasing integration of renewable energy in the production mix, driven by environmental 
reasons, also implies the rethinking and the redesign of the energy networks, as more fluctuating 
energy from wind and solar power are produced. The smart energy systems focus on integrating the 
fluctuating renewable energy through interaction between electricity, heating, and transport sectors, 
using electric vehicles and heat pumps as a source of short term storage and flexibility. In order to 
use efficiently the short term storage options across the different sectors, and make the fluctuating 
supply and demand match, the smart energy system must adopt the smart grid infrastructure, whose 
principle is explained in the next paragraph.

2.2. Smart grids 
 
Smart grids are networks that incorporate features derived from information and communication 
technology which enable them to be communicating. The communication between the different 
points of networks allows taking into account the actions of different players in the electricity 
system, including consumers. The objective is to ensure the balance between supply and demand at 
any time with responsiveness and reliability, and to optimize network operations. In a smart grid 
infrastructure, the supply system changes from a linear chain to a system where all players interact 
[19]. 
The energy systems of the future will be characterized by a larger share of renewable energies in the 
electricity mix. This includes large wind farms, but also smaller and decentralized production units, 
such as roof solar panels or small wind turbines installed in individual households. The electricity 
produced by these facilities will be by nature intermittent, but will primarily supply the domestic 
consumption. However, when production will overpass the domestic consumption, it will be possible 
to sell the electricity produced on the global market. On the opposite, if production is not enough to 
supply local demand, the consumer will have the possibility to buy electricity from the market. This 
creates a new generation of users, called ‘prosumers’, who are both producers and consumers of 
energy. As the contribution of ‘prosumers’ is likely to increase in the future, the electric grid needs to 
undertake developments to take into account these new energy trades. 
Local 'prosumers' and large-scale electricity-generating facilities are likely to constitute the backbone 
of the European energy system of the future [20]. In that context, integrated intelligence in 
appliances will enable them to utilize energy when it is abundant and the price is low.

3.1. Heat pump – Operating principle 
 
A heat pump is a thermodynamic system that transfers heat from one source to another, using an 
external work from a compressor. While heat naturally tends to move from a location with a high 
temperature to a location with a lower temperature, a heat pump uses a small amount of energy to 
switch that process into reverse, pulling heat out of a relatively low-temperature area, and pumping 
it into a higher temperature area. So heat is transferred from a "heat source," like the ground or air, 
into a "heat sink," like a home. 
 
Figure 8 - Principle sketch of a heat pump 
 
In the Directive 2010/31/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of 19 May 2010 on the 
energy performance of buildings (EPBD), “heat pump means a machine, a device or installation that 
transfers heat from natural surroundings such as air, water or ground to buildings or industrial 
applications by reversing the natural flow of heat such that it flows from a lower to a higher 
temperature”. 
To implement a heat pump cycle the following items are required: a high temperature heat 
exchanger, a low temperature heat exchanger and a heat transfer medium (called refrigerant) that 
transports heat from the low temperature source to the high temperature sink. Currently, the most 
common design involves four main components: evaporator, condenser, compressor and expansion 
valve, as shown in Figure 9. 
==PAGE:42==

Figure 9 - Basic heat pump cycle 
1. Evaporator: The evaporator is a low-temperature heat exchanger where the refrigerant 
enters as a low-temperature liquid, absorbs heat from the heat source by evaporation at a 
low pressure and leaves as a low-temperature vapor. 
2.  Compressor: In the compressor the low pressure of the low-temperature refrigerant from the 
evaporator is raised to a pressure that is sufficiently high to match the desired condensing 
temperature in the condenser. During compression not only the pressure but also the 
temperature of the refrigerant will increase.  
3. Condenser: The condenser is a high-temperature heat exchanger where the refrigerant enters 
as a high-temperature vapor, rejects heat to the heat sink by condensation at a high pressure 
and leaves as a high-temperature liquid. 
4. Expansion valve: On its return to the evaporator from the condenser the high-temperature, 
high-pressure liquid refrigerant must be changed to the low-temperature, low-pressure 
liquid that enters the evaporator. This is usually achieved by a throttling device known as the 
expansion valve. When the hot liquid passes through this valve, not only will its pressure be 
reduced but at the same time its temperature will drop. As the pressure drops, refrigerant 
starts to evaporate in the valve and the heat of evaporation is taken from the refrigerant 
itself which causes its temperature to drop and the result is a low-temperature, low-pressure 
mix of liquid and vapor. 
 
Because the heat pump uses electricity for heat production the price of a unit heat produced by a 
heat pump depends on the electricity price. A higher electricity price yields a higher heat price. This is 
relevant when studying the district heating market and investigating what other units a heat pump 
have to compete with.

The more efficient a heat pump is the more cost-effective and less energy consuming it will be. In 
the discussion and comparison of heat pumps and their energy efficiency the key parameter is the 
COP. The COP, or Coefficient Of Performance, describes the efficiency of the heat pump and is 
defined as the ratio between the heat produced by the heat pump,      , and the compressor 
power,     . [23] 
 
This coefficient is usually between 2 and 5, meaning that in a heat pump around 1/2 to 4/5 of the 
energy comes from the cold reservoir (source), and 1/2 to 1/5 is supplied by the compressor [23].

There are a number of factors that affect the COP of a heat pump. Major improvements in the 
efficiency of heat pump systems have been achieved through developments in the fields of heat 
exchangers, compressors, motors, motor drives and controls. Currently, the best opportunities for 
improving efficiency are in optimizing the sources and sinks, minimizing auxiliary drive powers and 
optimizing the temperature and capacity controls. Therefore, when choosing a heat pump, the 
following factors have to be considered: 
- Climate – The indoor and outdoor climates and the indoor heat loads will decide the annual 
heating and cooling demand and maximum peak loads; 
- Heat source – the type of heat source and its temperature variations affect the efficiency of 
the heat pump; 
- Temperature – the temperatures of the heat source and distribution systems for heating 
and cooling affect the COP and the capacity of the heat pump; 
- Technology – system design and selection of components such as motors, compressors and 
heat exchangers have a decisive influence on the operating characteristics of the heat pump; 
- Size – sizing of the heat pump and its components in relation to the heat demand will affect 
energy coverage and part-load operation; 
- Control system – the heat pump control system will affect how closely supply and demand 
for heating and cooling will match and how closely the actual operating temperatures will be to the 
theoretically optimal temperatures.

3.3.1. Air to air heat pump 
The air to air heat pump is a heating system that draws energy from the outside air to deliver it inside 
the house. Thus, to operate the heat pump requires two units. One, located outside, recovers heat 
from the air and the other on the inside, diffuse within the housing through a fan coil [24]. This 
configuration means that the heat pump can only supply heat from where the indoor unit is placed. 
A single air-to-air heat pump normally covers between 60 % and 80 % of the space heating demand. 
The remaining space heating demand must be covered by other sources, which would normally be 
electrical heaters or additional air-to-air heat pumps.

3.3.2. Air to water heat pump 
Unlike the air-to-air heat pump, air-to-water heat pump draws the calories in outdoor air but uses 
water to transport heat. The water will power the existing heating system, either through radiators 
or floor heating. 
Air-to-water heat pumps are normally designed to cover between 95 and 98 % of the heating 
demand. The remaining heat demand is covered by complementary electrical heating. 
Air-to-water heat pump is normally chosen where there is not enough available outdoor area for 
ground heat collectors, but where there is a water-based heat distribution system in the building. Air-
to-water heat pumps cannot deliver water temperatures higher than 55˚C, which means that it has 
to be installed in association with low temperature radiators.

3.3.3. Ground source heat pump 
The ground source heat pump (also called brine-to-water heat pump) recovers the heat in the soil 
through underground collector pipes. The heat recovered is then used to operate the geothermal 
heat pump on the same general principles than the air-to-air and air-to-water heat pumps. 
Two types of capture exist: 
- horizontal capture: pipes are buried below the surface of the garden, between 0,60m to 
1,20m on a land area of 1.5 to 3 times the surface area to be heated (depending on the needs); 
- vertical capture: pipes are placed in one or more wells to a depth between 50m and 120m. 
 
The heat pumps are normally designed to cover between 95 % and 98 % of the heating demand. The 
remaining heat demand is covered by direct complementary electric system.  
Ground source heat pumps have a high average efficiency over the year due to the more stable 
temperatures in the ground [24]. They are efficient when associated to high insulated and low 
consumption buildings, where radiators can cover the heat demand of the house with temperatures 
below 55˚C. 
Because the ground source heat pump draws its energy from the ground, the surface of the garden 
should be greater than that of housing. Thus, the installation of ground source heat pumps in urban 
areas is compromised. However, vertical drilling can solve the issue, but building owners have to 
make sure that the ground source can provide enough heat to supply the demand.

3.3.4. Large scale heat pumps: geothermal and seawater heat pumps 
Heat pumps in larger scale can be used for heat production for district heating networks, eg. by 
utilizing geothermal heat or seawater heat [26]. 
Geothermal energy is energy located in underground water reservoirs of the earth. On average the 
temperature of the reservoir increases with around 3 ºC per 100 m depth [23]. Heat from deep 
reservoirs can be utilized directly through a heat exchanger. However, both the temperature and the 
pumping costs increase with the depth. Currently three geothermal district heating plants are 
operating in Denmark – in Thisted (7 MW) and Copenhagen (14 MW) and in Sønderborg (12.5 MW). 
Seawater heat pumps use the sea as the cold heat source. The idea is not new and several projects 
were developed in the eighties (in Monaco) [27]. They take advantage of the stable temperature of 
the sea water, averaging 12°C at a few meters of depth [28]. In the last decade, several new projects 
have emerged, especially for district heating purposes in new developed areas. This is the case of two 
plants in Cherbourg (2,2 MW), [29] and La Seyne-sur-Mer (4,8 MW) in France [30]. Their 
characteristics and costs are presented in Appendix E, along with other similar projects whose 
characteristics were used in the model. 
 
 
3.4. The role of heat pumps in smart grids and cities 
 
In the smart city, the urban energy systems must be viewed as a system of systems with 
complex interdependencies between buildings, electricity and heat networks. In this scenario, heat 
pumps are set to play an important role since they are able to link thermal and electricity grids. The 
following aims at describing the ability for smart heat pumps to integrate fluctuating electricity from 
renewable sources while providing energy storage, supply and demand side management, grid 
balancing and load shifting. 
3.4.1. Integration of fluctuating electricity from RE 
As described in Chapter 1, environmental concerns, low carbon emissions and resource 
management are some of the main challenges of the smart cities of the future. These targets can be 
achieved through substantial increases in energy efficiency and in the use of renewable energy 
sources, such as wind and solar power. 
However, the electricity generated by wind and solar power is by nature intermittent and fluctuating; 
and these fluctuations in the production of electricity are not predictable. Its integration into future 
energy systems that are free of fossil fuels thus represents entirely new challenges. Indeed power 
generation systems must always ensure a balance between production and consumption of 
electricity. In the Danish power system, for example, where wind power generation accounts for 
almost 40% of the total power generation and is expected to increase to 50% in 2020, it is a 
particular challenge to ensure this balance between production and consumption. 
==PAGE:47==
 
Chapter 3: Smart Heat Pumps 
 
28 
 
Heat pumps are a key technology to take advantage of this large amount of wind power and 
integrate it to the energy grid: they can act as an interface between electrical and thermal networks 
and can create centrally controlled thermal grids that respond to supply and demand. Associated 
with thermal storages and control units, heat pumps can store superfluous surplus electricity in the 
form of thermal energy. This has been demonstrated in the Ecogrid project on the Danish island of 
Bornholm, where smart heat pumps were connected to a smart grid to integrate successfully wind 
power energy [20], [31]. 
Figure 10 shows the normalized spot market prices and wind production for week 2 in 2007. It can be 
noticed that the spot price is low when wind production is high, and vice-versa. A price incentive 
system that would stop the heat pump operation during spot peaks, but ‘force’ the operation during 
the pits would allow for more integration of wind power.

3.4.2. Energy storage, moving consumption and load shifting 
Heat pumps and electric vehicles are one of the most promising technologies for allowing 
flexible energy consumption. They have in common a large power consumption which is not 
temporally linked to the energy service. Heat pumps associated with a water-based storage system, 
for example in the form of water pipes in the floor, radiators, or water tanks, represent a special 
opportunity to integrate large amount of wind power because they can act as “energy storages”. 
Because of this storage, heat pumps may be switched on and off in relation to when there is much 
wind power available, without affecting the user’s comfort (if installed in a well insulated building). 
Their charge can be staggered, so that the electricity consumption can be forwarded or delayed from 
several hours [33].

Associated to intelligent control devices which are able to take into consideration day-ahead spot 
prices, heat pumps can also be set to turn off when the price of electricity is high. This capacity 
flexibility can make advanced heat pumps an attractive tool for electric demand control, especially 
peak load shedding or valley filling.  
Smart heat pump systems are distinguished from their standard variants by their ability to 
incorporate user behavior, climate data and pricing signals into their control systems. The following 
gives the definition of a smart heat pump that will be used as a base for the modeling and analysis 
performed in the current study. 
3.4.3. Smart heat pumps – Definition 
In this study and the model later developed, it is considered that the smartness of a heat pump 
does not depend on its heat source (thus its efficiency), but in its ability to store energy and 
communicate with the smart grid. However, this tends to exclude air-to-air heat pumps, since no 
efficient storage solution of air-heat currently exists. The smart heat pump includes a communication 
device (smart meter) which can react, on an hourly basis, to variable tariffs, and affect the heat pump 
operation through a control device. 
A smart heat pump can then be defined as the association of a conventional heat pump, a thermal 
storage and an ICT-based appliance (Figure 11). The ICT appliance itself includes a communication 
device, a metering device, and a control device (Figure 12). It interacts with the electric grid and 
market and enables the consumers to use the power when it is least expensive. The smart heat pump 
concept thus rather refers to a system than to a single appliance. 
This system must: 
- 
interconnect electric and thermal grids (through heat pump and heat storage) 
- 
be able to communicate in real-time with other consumers, generators and storage facilities 
connected to a smart grid (through the communication device/smart meter) 
- 
be able to accumulates knowledge of energy user patterns and accommodate differences in 
temperature levels (through sensors and control box)

Chapter 4. Goal and Scope of the 
Study 
 
The purpose of this study is to model individual smart heat pumps and to analyze their ability 
to integrate renewable energy and reduce peak loads.  
 
Nordhavn, a peninsula adjacent to the Copenhagen district of Østerbro, which was best 
known as a port of call for cruise ships and container traffic, is undergoing a major urban 
development that will lead to the creation of an entirely new district that will accommodate 40,000 
residents and the same number of jobes over the next 40-50 years. The urban and energy planners 
have the ambition to make Nordhavn a prime example of “The Sustainable City of The Future” by 
developing smarter and greener energy solutions [35]. 
Nordhavn thus constitutes an ideal place to develop and experiment with new heating systems 
involving flexibly-operating heat pumps. 
 
District heating has been promoted as an effective solution for the heating of buildings while meeting 
the goals of renewable energy. An analysis conducted by the Danish Energy Agency even concluded 
that district heating will be an integral component of the solution needed for Denmark to meet its 
2050 goals of 100% RE in the energy and transport system [36]. 
However, in some areas under development such as Nordhavn, future buildings will comply with the 
Building Regulation 2020 low energy requirements [37], implying very low space heating and hot 
water needs. Consequently the current heating system (i.e. district heating) may no longer be an 
economically optimal solution since the investments may be too high compared to the relatively low 
energy use. 
 
Buildings located in Nordhavn and complying with the building regulation 2020 will not be obliged to 
be connected to the public DH grid, but will be free to choose their heating system. From both a 
socio and private economic point of view, investing in individual stand alone smart heat pumps for 
every building might be an interesting choice. The present study will be modeling different heating 
strategies for Nordhavn and will investigate the following questions: 
1. Is integration of heat pumps in Nordhavn’s district heating system competitive? 
2. Which type of heat pump can best perform? 
3. What impact does smart operation of heat pumps have on shaving the peak electricity 
consumption?

5.3. Choice of modeling tool 
 
The appropriate modeling tool for the analysis was selected among four modeling tools, which are 
largely used in studies about integration of renewable energy and which were available in DTU: 
Balmorel, EnergyPRO, EnergyPLAN and STREAM. 
The model used in the analyses has been chosen based on the following criteria. The model should: 
- 
Have a sufficiently detailed representation of energy conversion and storage technologies 
(bottom-up model). 
- 
Be able to represent the district heating system, electric boilers, heat pumps and thermal 
storages. 
- 
Be able to simulate and optimize the operation of heat pumps associated to thermal storages 
over a life time of at least 20 years (simulation and scenario model). 
- 
Include hour-by-hour variations in heat production and heat demand, in order to fully 
capture the influence of heat storage and heat production during cheap Spot prices hours. 
- 
Be able to cover individual heating (relevant for scenarios focusing on individual heat 
pumps). 
- 
Be accessible and user-friendly. 
- 
Provide handy and workable simulation’s results as outputs (graphs, datasheets,…) 
 
The following gives a short presentation of the four modeling tools considered for the simulations. 
STREAM is a scenario building tool which provides a good overview of energy-systems on both the 
demand and supply side [38]. It is maintained by DTU and Ea Energy Analyses (Danish company) and 
is available for free. STREAM does not perform any economic optimization of the energy-system. 
Instead, the purpose of the STREAM is to create an overview of energy consumption, conversion and 
GHG emissions. It is also designed to project the demand for energy services in the given year. All 
thermal and renewable technologies can be simulated by STREAM. 
EnergyPLAN is a simulation tool developed by Aalborg University, and can also be freely 
downloaded. It is formulated in Delphi Pascal but is designed in tabsheets, which makes it more user-
friendly than EnergyPLAN. The tool can model energy systems at a national or regional scale. 
EnergyPLAN can perform hour by hour analyses to optimize analytically the operation of a system 
over a year. The model outputs comprise energy balances, energy productions, fuel consumptions, 
electricity imports/exports, CO2 emissions, and costs. The model covers the whole energy system, 
including individual heating. However, the EnergyPLAN does not perform investment optimization. 
BALMOREL is a partial-equilibrium tool with an emphasis on the electricity sector and CHP. It is 
developed and distributed under open source and can be downloaded for free. The tool is 
formulated in the GAMS modeling language and can be modified by the user. This means that an 
economic and operation optimization model can be added or modified to suit a specific study. Time 
aspects (subdivisions of time) and geographical aspects can also be flexibly managed and modified. 
BALMOREL can simulate the electricity sector and some of the heat sector (district heating). The 
different types of units include electricity, district heating, CHP, short-term heat storages, hydro

power, wind, and solar. BALMOREL considers all costs within the energy-system as well as SO2 and 
NOX penalties. 
EnergyPRO is an integrated modeling software package for combined techno-economic design, 
analysis, and optimization. It can model all types of electric and thermal generation and storage. It is 
also possible to create a user-defined unit, which makes it quite flexible for modeling crosscutting

6.1. Smart heat pump model 
 
The smart heat pump modeled in the study is based on the definition of a smart heat pump that 
was given in the Chapter 3.4. It consists in the association of a heat pump, a water tank and a 
communication and controlling device. 
 
The hot water tank is modeled as a thermal storage that can supply both space heating and hot 
water circuits. Heat losses through the tank are taken into consideration and are described in ???. 
The tank losses estimation leads to consider an efficiency of 99% for the tank storage. 
 
The communication device is not physically modeled but is implicitly present in the model by 
connecting the system to the spot prices market. This enables the heat pump to accommodate its 
hourly production to the hourly electricity prices. Therefore, the heat pump can operate flexibly, by 
prioritizing operation for hours with low marginal electricity production costs, which can be 
interpreted as electricity prices. When combined with heat storages, the electricity consumption for 
the heat pumps becomes flexible. The term intelligent heat storage in this context refers to the fact 
that the storage heat tank is used for flexible heat pump operation, based on what is most 
economically for the user and optimal for the energy system. As shown in Figure 18, the heat 
accumulation tank interacts with the heat pump and the central heating system.

A complementary electric boiler is added to the heating system. The role of the boiler is to cover the 
extreme peaks in the heat demand and avoid over-dimensioning the heat pump. The competition 
between the heat pump and boiler operations is captured in the model since the model will generally 
seek to minimize the use of the supplemental electric boiler, due to its low efficiency. 
 
The model implementation ensures that heat stored in the water tank comes only from the heat 
pump heat production. This is implemented by restricting the boiler not to be able to load the tank. 
 
The possible loading/unloading to/from the heat storage in a given hour is assumed instantaneous, 
given the relatively small scale of the system. As a result, no loading/unloading restrictions have been 
implemented for the water tank. 
 
Due to the linearity of the model, the modeled heat pumps will be able to operate continuously 
between zero and full load. This might be contradictory to the fact that today’s heat pumps are 
typically on/off regulated. However, with the hourly time resolution applied, the continuously 
variable operation corresponds to on/off regulation, where it merely varies how large a share of the 
given hour the heat pump is operating.

7.1. Nordhavn building stock 
 
The urban area considered in this analysis, Nordhavn Phase 2, will be an entirely new district of 
Copenhagen that will accommodate 40 000 inhabitants and 40 000 jobs in 540 000 m² of buildings. 
The total building stock was then divided in 50% dwellings and 50% offices. The average size of a 
building was considered to be 6 000 m² for both offices and dwellings, which makes the total building 
stock be divided into 45 dwelling buildings and 45 office buildings. 
 
7.2. Building consumption 
 
The building heat consumption is a key parameter in the model, if not the most impacting one. 
Indeed, the buildings’ heat demand directly determines the capacities and dimensions of the 
different heat production equipments. The higher the heat demand is, the higher the power 
capacities of heat pumps will be, and thus the investments and operating costs. The buildings’ heat 
demand must therefore be assessed carefully. 
The heat consumption can considerably vary from one building to another. This variation is mostly 
due to the insulation level of the building. Over the years, the material and building methods used in 
construction have been largely improved in order to avoid thermal bridges and increase energy 
savings. That is why over the last decades, the buildings’ heat demand has been considerably 
decreased: whereas a 1960-building has an annual heat consumption of 370 kWh/m², a 2000-
building only consumes 100 kWh/m² (Figure 24). This trend in the reduction of heat demand is likely 
to continue in the next decades, due to technology improvements but also governmental 
regulations. Indeed, in order to meet energy savings goals, the Danish government imposes strict 
regulations for buildings built after 2020: The Building Class 2020. This regulation requires new-built 
dwellings not to exceed a total heat demand (including space heating and hot water) of 20

7.3. Heat demand profiles 
 
As the overall objective of the study is to analyze the impact of smart heat pumps in enabling 
demand response, it was necessary to reflect the peak loads and daily variations in the buildings’ 
heat demand.  Consequently, hourly heat demand profiles were created based on existing studies 
and normalized water consumption profiles. 
Offices and dwellings do not only have different yearly heat demands, but also different heat 
demand variations, mostly due to the difference in the use of hot water. In order to take these 
differences into consideration in the model, it was decided to build the total heat demand profiles as 
a sum of two distinct heat demand profiles: 
- 
One space heating demand profile, similar for offices and dwellings, and based on 
hourly external temperatures 
- 
One hot water demand profile based on hot water consumption profiles. 
Two different total heat demand profiles have then been created depending on the hot water share 
for the given building category (offices and dwellings). 
According to the Danish Energy Agency, the hot tap water stands for about 20% of the total heat 
demand in the existing dwellings. But this share is expected to reach at least 50% in future high 
insulated buildings [24], because most of the energy savings will affect space heating demand. 
Because the expected share of hot water demand is quite uncertain, this parameter will be subject to 
a sensitivity analysis that will be presented later in the report. 
As far as office buildings are concerned, the use of hot water does not follow the same patterns as 
for dwellings (no baths and showers in the mornings and evenings, no cooking in the middle of the 
day), and can be considered as negligible in existing buildings. However, in future buildings, and for 
the same reason as for dwellings, the share of hot water demand is expected to increase slightly. 
That is why a share of 10% of the total heat demand was assumed in the model.

7.4. Economical models 
 
The five scenarios were each examined from both private economic and socio economic 
perspectives. These different perspectives might impact the feasibility of the options based on 
different needs and costs to bear. The different perspectives are listed below with a brief description. 
 Socio-economic analysis: In the socio-economic analysis the project is evaluated from a social 
perspective, with the final goal being to maximize the social welfare. Given these 
characteristics, the project supported from this perspective will be the one that will have the 
best allocation of resources. In this case, the political strategy will be the most relevant. The 
characteristics of this perspective are reported in Table 13. For the project under study, the 
total capital expenditures (CAPEX) of the technologies used in all the three options were 
considered, as well as the operation and maintenance costs (O & M). 
 
 Private economic analysis - Consumer perspective: In the private economic perspective, the 
primary goal is the feasibility of the project from a business perspective. However, the 
current study focuses on smart heat pump flexibility, and one of the objectives is to estimate 
potential savings in electricity consumption. That is why the economic analysis was 
performed from a customer perspective (and not from the heat supplier HOFOR). When 
analyzing the feasibility of the project from the consumer perspective, the investments for 
the heating grid in DH scenario and the marginal cost of heat (i.e. the costs of production one 
additional unit of heat) were not considered. The results of the private investment analysis 
will determine if the project is worthwhile to undertake for a building owner. For this 
analysis, the inflation (of the currency and on the heat/electricity price), as well as applicable 
taxes and discounts were considered.

Grid Tariffs 
The grid tariff covers the costs relating to the transmission grid. They are paid to the distribution 
company and are proportional to the electricity consumption. 
Nordhavn is placed within an electric distribution area serviced by DONG Energy Eldistribution A/S. In 
the simulations, the “Normal Tariff” provided by DONG is used as the reference [41]. However, 
additional economic gains might be achievable by choosing a variable rate, which is sometimes 
offered by distribution companies. This will be further analyzed in part 5.3.2. 
 
The grid tariffs itself consists of two different contributions: the transport’s contribution, and the 
public commitment contribution (PSO). For the transmission contribution, different prices apply to 
dwelling and offices, depending on the type of connection to the grid. The standard one, which 
corresponds to a connection to the general 0.4 kV grid will be used in scenarios DGHP, SGHP and 
SAHP: 37.8 øre/kWh [42]. In scenario SSHP, it is assumed that the large seawater heat pump requires 
a specific connection to a transformer station, and thus benefits from a reduced rate: 21.5 øre/kWh 
[42].  
Electricity consumers in Denmark pay the so called PSO rate on top of the transmission tariffs. This 
rate covers the subsidies given to renewables and decentralized CHP. The rate is 21.40 øre/kWh in 
2015. The grid tariffs and PSO contribution vary a lot from one year to another (cf.Tables with grid 
tariffs from years 2015 and 2014 in Appendix H). In the model, prices from 2015 will be used and

8.1.2. Total costs 
 
The following presents the conclusions that can be drawn from the economic results of the 
simulation, which are displayed in Table 23 and Figure 28. 
First, the comparison of the total costs associated to each project shows that the individual heat 
pump solutions are the most competitive (DGHP, SGHP, SAHP), whatever it is smart or not. 
The large seawater project is the least competitive. Indeed, despite relatively low investment costs in 
the heat pump equipment, this scenario requires to build an entirely new district heating grid, which 
almost doubles the total investment costs. Those ones are not paid back enough by the operation 
costs, due to a relatively low COP, which is assumed to be equal to 3. 
The DH scenario has the particularity to require very small investment and maintenance costs 
compared to the other scenarios, since those ones are under HOFOR’s responsibility. However, its 
operation costs are the most expensive ones and are more than 70% higher than operation costs of 
individual ground source heat pumps (DGHP, SGHP). 
The individual heat pump scenarios do not differ a lot. SAHP benefits from lower investment costs 
due to cheaper and easier installation of air source heat pumps, but suffers from high operation costs 
due to a temperature-dependant COP, which equals to 3,3 in average, whereas COP equals 4 in 
scenarios DGHP and SGHP. 
To conclude the economic comparison, it is important to analyze and understand the few differences 
between smart ground source heat pump and dumb ground source heat pump scenarios. SGHP’s 
investment costs are slightly higher than DGHP’s one because of the higher storage capacity. The 
tank storage capacity offers some flexibility in the production (lower operation costs), but the savings 
associated are not big enough to pay back the investment, leading to higher total costs. The 
difference in the operation costs is relatively low, and can be explained by the relatively small 
fluctuations of the 2013 spot prices.

8.1.6. First conclusions 
 
The first results from the simulations presented above demonstrate the competitiveness of the 
individual heat pump scenarios. However, in the actual conditions considered for the analysis 
(investment costs of heat pump, water tank, spot prices…), the smart heat pump technology seems 
not being worth the investment, since the total costs on SGHP are slightly higher than those of DGHP. 
On the other hand, the economic comparison run with more fluctuating spot prices (2008 spot 
prices) presented in Table 24 showed that smart heat pump system could lead to significant savings 
in operation expenses. Consequently, the competitiveness of smart heat pump solutions seems to be 
largely dependent on electricity prices and fluctuations. The comparisons between offices and 
dwellings also underlined a large dependency of smart heat pump operation with the heat demand 
fluctuations. Despite their heat storage ability and low investment costs, ASHP and SSHP scenarios 
were proven not competitive enough with District Heating, due to relatively low efficiency (COP). 
Price fluctuations, heat demand profiles, as well as investment costs and heat pump efficiencies have 
a big impact on the scenarios’ total costs. To which extend those parameters can affect the operation 
of smart heat pump systems, and in which conditions can SGHP compete with DGHP? The part 5.3. 
will answer these interrogations by analyzing in more details these dependencies and the influence 
of these parameters on the competitiveness of smart heat pump systems.

8.3. Peak load shedding 
 
In order to analyze the potential load shedding generated by smart heat pump operations, it is 
important to remind some characteristics of the model. Indeed, the model is based on a price 
incentive. The model optimizes the production by minimizing total costs. This means that the heat 
pump will tend to stop operating when prices are high. Besides, the model is built in a way that 
prevents the tank from being loaded and unloaded at the same time (water circulation in the tank 
avoided). The tank then starts operating when heat pump is turned off, or to supplement heat pump 
operation. The heat quantity delivered by the tank thus theoretically amounts heat quantity that 
should have been produced during peak loads. It then represents electricity consumption (times the 
COP factor) that is avoided. Through the use of the tanks, the smart heat pump system shifts 
electricity consumption from peak hours (high electricity price) to other hours. 
This electricity consumption shift is illustrated in the following figures, which represent the heat 
quantity delivered by the heat pump, the boiler and the tank for a dwelling building on the 8th of 
January 2020. In DGHP scenario, the electricity consumption follows the same curve as the heat 
consumption, whereas in SGHP scenario, electricity consumption is shifted to hollow hours. The 
smart heat pump smoothes the electricity consumption profile. The same trends and results are 
observed for office buildings. 
 
 
Figure 38 - Dumb vs. Smart heat pump operation, 8th January 2020 
 
As a result of the smoothing of electricity consumption profiles, the peaks in electricity consumption 
are lower in SGHP than in DGHP. The Figure 39, representing heat pump operation and electricity 
consumption over the month of January show lower peaks in the electricity consumption for DGHP. 
Results are displayed in Table 29. The decrease is however less important for office buildings. The 
total electricity consumption peak over Nordhavn’s area is about 11% lower for SGHP than for DGHP. 
This result is obtained by scaling the results of one building, which mean that the total peak would 
equal the sum of the buildings’ peaks. In reality, peak loads depend on customer behavior do not 
necessary occur at the same hour for every building.

In summer period, the demand profiles are characterized by more daily fluctuations and a lower

This simulation and its associated results demonstrate that a smart heat pump behavior does not 
necessary lead to economic savings. The potential savings depend on the spot prices level and also 
on their correlation with heat loads. Indeed, especially in the winter, spot prices tends to be very 
high during peak loads, when there is no other choice than making the heat pump operate at full 
capacity. During those time-periods, the associated expenses are much higher in SGHP than in DGHP 
(where the price is always 295 kr/MWh). The spot prices are lower in the summer time, but the 
demand in that season is also much lower, so that, on average and on one year simulation, the 
winter-expenses are not compensated by the tank load during cheap spot prices time periods. The 
same conclusion can be made for office buildings.

Chapter 9: Discussion 
 
The results presented in the previous chapter brought out many uncertainties linked to 
model. They will be discussed in this chapter. 
 
The difference in the results obtained for buildings and dwellings underscore the influence of 
the heat demand profile. These ones were built on several assumptions that can be discussed. 
The first one is the dependency of the space heating profile with the outside temperatures on a 
hourly timescale (HDD method). The heating demand will certainly remain dependant on seasonal 
temperature variations in the future, but the high insulation of new buildings can make it possible to 
turn off heating during several hours, at night for example, without affecting comfort. It would be 
interesting to study the impact of these interruptions on the fluctuations of the demand. 
The heat demand profiles depend largely on the share of hot water demand in the total heat 
demand. As seen in the previous chapter, this has a significant impact on smart heat pump 
operation. The model was built assuming a share of 50% of hot water demand in the total heat 
demand for dwellings, and only 10% for offices, but high uncertainties remain in those assumptions. 
Moreover, the model did not take into account the differences in heat consumption between 
weekends and the rest of the week. This might lead to significant changes in the results. 
 
As far as heat pump’s characteristics are concerned, a constant COP was assumed for ground source 
heat pumps. However, one of the possible impacts of on/off regulation modes on such heat pumps is 
a decrease in the efficiency. This was not included in the model but could potentially impact a lot the 
results, by minimizing the attractiveness of scenario SGHP. 
 
The model simulations were run with inputs covering one year (spot prices and heat demand 
profiles). This might be problematic for the study since the analysis was lead on a 20-year timeline. In 
the model, yearly demand profiles do not change from one year to another. As a consequence, the 
capacities given by the GAMS optimization tool might be ‘too optimal’. In other words, it is not 
possible to assess the system’s ability to handle unexpected variations in the demand/spot prices. 
Indeed, the model is based on an economic optimization, and is built as if the system’s design was 
based on accurate forecasts, for both prices and demand. However this is obviously not the case in 
reality. Smart heat pump might perform better in those unexpected situations. A further analysis 
with the same model could consist in studying the impact of prices/demand increases with fixed 
capacities. 
Similarly, electricity prices do not vary from one year to another. The choice of the 2013 spot prices 
was initially justified by the fact that they represented an ‘average’ profile over the last ten years, but 
the fact is that in reality spot prices can vary a lot from one year to another. With the model 
developed in this study, the system is not sensitive to these variations. 
==PAGE:107==
 
Chapter 9: Discussion 
 
88 
 
An element, which is not reflected in the model, is the economic and financial context. Both district 
heating and heat pump systems competitiveness depends on the fuel and electricity prices 
respectively. Smart heat pumps might perform better in the future since more integration of 
fluctuation energy will induce more prices fluctuations. However, this will be problematic if the costs 
of electricity increase too much. District heating offers better price stability and long term 
investments whereas smart heat pumps suffer from high investments and uncertainties on spot 
market prices. Individual smart heat pump solutions also suffer from a low security of supply, and in 
the model developed the system is not designed to undertake long shutdowns due to maintenance 
for example. 
 
The results presented in the previous chapter concluded in the high influence of cost profiles 
on the savings generated by intelligent heat pumps. Those savings were even not positive with the 
2013 spot prices. However, with the 2008 spot prices, SGHP generates 55kr savings for a 100 m² 
apartment. With the specific grid tariffs, savings could reach 80 kr./year for a 100 m² apartment. This 
has to be put in perspective with results from similar demonstration projects lead in Denmark. 
From 2010 to 2012, Energinet.dk carried out an experimentation project involving smart heat pumps 
connected to a smart grid: “From wind power to heat pumps” [46] [47]. Based on market date from 
2008-2009, Energinet.dk concluded on potential savings of 120 kr./year for a single house. The heat 
pump installed had a COP of 3 and the annual heat demand was 18.1 MWh. With regards to the 
results of this project, and given the very low heat demand of the buildings considered in Nordhavn, 
the savings obtained do not seem that low. 
 
The social benefits of smart heat pump operations, which are the reduction in peak capacity, the 
integration of more renewable energy, and the reduced (or non-existent) GHG emissions are 
however not visible on the electricity bill paid by the consumers. That is why development of 
intelligent heat pumps should be encouraged by specific subsidies or other incentives to help owners 
to invest in such systems. 
 
In parallel to incentives from the government or energy distributors, flexible use of electricity with 
smart heat pumps can be increased by developing new services that come along with the smart heat 
pump and which involve the customer. This can be achieved through IT platforms where the 
consumer is able to visualize its consumption or energy performance. By involving customers in such 
a project, the city of Esbjerg in Denmark was able to reduce net losses in its district grid by 11% [48]. 
 
To conclude this chapter, it is important to point out that the current study only focuses on heat 
demand. However, the buildings in Nordhavn will be equipped with an air conditioning system for 
cooling in the summer period. This could be beneficial for the SAHP developed in this study, whose 
investment costs would be paid back in a shorter time-period. This emphasizes the many synergies 
between heat pumps and electric and heating networks and can be the subject of a future study.

Smart Cities and Integration of Renewable Energies: Smart Heat Pumps

Conclusion 
 
The study aimed at assessing the potential impacts of stand-alone smart heat pumps on the energy 
system of a city. In order to achieve it, intelligent heat pump system was modeled in energyPRO and an 
economic optimization tool was created in GAMS. The integration of such modeled heat pumps was then 
analyzed, with Nordhavn’s future urban development as a study case. 
From the private economic analysis, based on a consumer perspective, it can be concluded that individual 
smart heat pump system are a cost effective solution when associated with low-energy buildings. Despite 
relatively high investment costs, smart heat pumps are more competitive than district heating systems, and 
generate savings that can be evaluated to 2,5 kr/m²/year, although this can be achieved with heat pumps with 
efficiencies higher than 3. In the current electricity market conditions, where the spot prices account for a small 
share of the total electricity price, smart operation does not generate savings over conventional heat pump 
operation. However, savings become significant (0,8 kr/m²/year) with highly fluctuating prices. Smart heat 
pumps are therefore likely to become more attractive for customers in the future, since more integration of 
renewable energy might increase price fluctuations. 
The other important benefits of their intelligent operation rely in their capacity to shift electricity consumption 
away from peak hours. Indeed, the model concluded that in the current electricity prices’ conditions, smart 
heat pumps can shift around 10% of the electricity consumption to cheaper periods, and therefore reduce peak 
loads. Smart heat pumps thus constitute an interesting solution for energy suppliers, since they reduce the 
needs for peak capacity. Besides, by using the tank as heat storage when electricity is available, they help 
integrating more renewable energy. On an environmental point of view, if supplied by electricity from 
renewable energy, which will be the case in Copenhagen from 2025, heat pumps produce zero emissions of 
GHG gases. 
Smart heat pump systems are therefore a promising solution for improving the smartness of the urban 
energy system, by enabling high integration of renewable energy and electricity consumption savings. The 
study recommends politics and energy suppliers to encourage their development by creating more efficient 
customer-oriented incentives for smarter consumption.

Appendix B – Heat Pump 
Thermodynamic Cycle 
 
This Appendix aims at describing in more details the pysics behing the heat pump. Source: 
http://www.industrialheatpumps.nl/en/how_it_works/thermodynamic_cycle/ [49]. 
Thermodynamic cycle of a heat pump 
The operating principle of a heat pump is based on the physical property that "the boiling 
point of a fluid increases with pressure". By lowering pressure, a medium can be evaporated 
while an increase of pressure will lead to condensation. 
For large scale industrial applications, Ammonia is the most suitable refrigerant. It is mostly 
used in a pump system. 
 
Log P-h diagram 
To understand the principle of operation of a mechanical heat pump, a log P-h dagram can 
be used. A log P-h diagram shows all state variables of the refrigerant. On the horizontal axis 
enthalpy (h) is shown. The vertical axis has a logaritmic scale and shows pressure. The other 
lines show: 
- 
Red: Temperature [°C] 
- 
Blue: Entropy [kJ/kg] 
- 
Green: Specific volume [m3/kg] 
The black line divides the graph according to the different states of Ammonia (NH3). On the 
left its fluid phase is shown. On the right side Ammonia is in its gaseous state. Below the 
black curve a combination of both gaseous and liquid Ammonia can be found. The area 
above the highest point is called the transkritical area. 
==PAGE:117==
 
Appendix B – Heat Pump Thermodynamic Cycle 
 
98 
 
 
The log p-h diagram shows a cycle for a mechanical heat pump. The points represent the different 
components of the heat pump. In this diagram a heat pump with a condensation temperature of 80 
°C and an evaporation temperature of 40 °C is taken as an example [49]. 
1-2 The compressor: With a compressor the pressure of the gaseous refrigerant is increased from 15 
to 40 bar. Ideally the entropy (s) stays constant during this process. In reality the entropy will 
increase somewhat, because the electric energy needed to power the compressor is partly absorbed 
by the refrigerant. Due to that the temperature of NH3 gas will rise to 120 °C. 
2-3 The condensor: The condensor delivers useful energy. In the condenser the superheated gas is 
cooled from 120 °C to 80 °C. Then condensation takes place at constant temperature of 80 °C, until 
all vapour has become liquid. The liquid flows to the expansion device. 
3-4 Expansion device: Inside the expansion device the pressure is reduced from 40 to 15 bar. Due to 
the expansion a mixture of gaseous and fluid Ammonia is formed. This mixture flows to the liquid 
separator. 
4-5/1 Liquid seperator: Inside the liquid seperator both fluid (5) and gaseous (1) Ammonia can be 
found. Its most important function is to seperate the liquid and vapour. The vapour flows to the 
compressor; the liquid is pumped over the evaporators. 
5-6 Evaporator: The liquid ammonia at the bottom of the seperator is pumped over the 
evaporator(s). Inside the evaporator a part of the ammonia is evaporated at a constant temperature 
of 40 °C. The energy needed for evaporation is delivered by a source of waste heat. The mixture of 
liquid and vapour ammonia flows back to the seperator (6) and is separated again in liquid and 
vapour.

Space heating demand profiles 
 
This section aims at giving a greater understanding of how the space heating consumption is 
distributed over the year, and how the space heating demand profiles were built. 
 
In order to distribute the space heating demand over the year it was decided to create an hourly 
distribution. Indeed, a monthly, weekly or even daily distribution would not have been sufficient for 
assessing the impact of peak loads in the winter days. The other main reason is that the SPOT prices 
data is given for every hour of the year. With an hourly distributed heat demand, it is then possible, 
through energyPRO whose calculations as based on hourly time series, to optimize the energy system 
operations with more accuracy. 
 
The hourly space heating demand distribution was built using the heating degree hour method 
(HDH), which is used for estimating (roughly) the energy needed to heat a building. The heating 
requirements for a building at a specific location are considered to be directly proportional to the 
number of heating degree hours at that location. 
Heating and cooling degree-hours are defined as the sum of the differences between hourly average 
temperatures and a base temperature [50]. The number of heating degree-hours (HDH) in a year is

Hot water demand profiles 
 
In studies involving tankless hot water systems, the performance or control of the system can 
significantly vary with the hot water draw volume and duration, and the elapsed time between the 
draws. For those systems, high resolution draw profiles are required to predict the system 
performance and energy consumption [52]. However, for hot water systems involving storage tanks, 
hourly averaged hot water profiles can be used to model energy consumption, since such systems 
respond to the thermostat temperature of the tank. In other words, the storage tank does avoid the 
domestic hot water system from firing instantaneously with a hot water draw. 
 
Many papers and studies use or model hourly averaged hot water profiles [53], [54] , [55]. They all 
have in common two peaks, in the morning and the evening. 
A study lead in 2008 by the Energy Monitoring Company in conjunction with and on behalf of the 
Energy Saving Trust (UK) [56] analyzed the hot water consumption in approximately 120 dwellings. 
The project aimed to measure volumetric consumption of DHW and the associated energy 
requirement and to identify domestic hot water heating patterns in terms of both times and 
temperatures (among other goals). The data collection enabled to extract daily average hot water 
consumption profiles for one dwelling and for the whole sample, as well as daily energy delivery 
profiles for one dwelling and for the whole sample (Figure 64). 
 
For the purpose of the current analysis, it was decided to use the daily energy delivery profile for the 
whole sample (120 dwellings).This will represent hot water demand better than the single house 
profile. The normalized profile used for generating the time series is presented in Figure 64 below:

Coefficient of performance 
Assessing a realistic COP for the heat pump in every scenario was an important task since the COP 
directly determines the electric consumption of a heat pump, and thus the economic 
competitiveness of a scenario. But, as described in chapter 3, the COP is affected by many factors 
(type and temperature of heat source, delivery temperature, size, operation…), which make it a 
difficult parameter to assess. The COP given by the manufacturers often reflect an ideal and optimal 
situation which is not likely to happen. Moreover, the efficiency of heat pumps is likely to be 
improved within 2020, due to technology improvements. 
 
For ground source heat pumps, the COP and lifetime values assumed for the analysis were extracted 
from the Technology Data for Energy Plants published in 2012 and revised in January 2014 by the 
Danish Energy Agency and Energinet.dk [24]. As the ground temperature (at a few dozens of meters 
depth) is constant over the year, the ground source heat pump COP is assumed constant with a value 
equal to 4.

Heat losses 
 
The optimization tool in GAMS initially did not take into consideration the heat losses in the water 
tank. However, as EnergyPRO software offers the possibility to model those losses, it was decided to 
take them into account. 
The thermal losses in the tank are modeled by a conduction loss through the tank wall, which 
depends on the difference of temperatures between the hot water stored in the tank and the 
outside temperature:

In this model, the hot water stored in the tank is assumed to be constant, as well as the outside 
temperature. 
 
In this kind of model, the bigger the volume is, the bigger is the tank surface, and then the heat 
losses. Assuming that the outside temperature is constant, the losses are also bigger when the inside 
temperature is high, which means that losses will be maximal when the tank is stored at full capacity.  
 
Heat storages are usually insulated by mineral wool, which offers very good insulation for a cheap 
price and a convenient installation3. The thermal conductivity of mineral wool is 0,04 W/m/K. 
Thickness and thermal conductivity of the tank can be neglected compared to those of mineral wool 
and then are not taken into consideration for the approximation of heat losses. It is assumed that the 
tank is installed in a room large enough to install 40 cm of mineral wool around the tank, which 
provides a very efficient insulation.
