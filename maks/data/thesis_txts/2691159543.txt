Summary (English)
With the ubiquity of cheap mobile computing software has become a commodity
and like many other commodities there are safety concerns.
Food, clothing
and other mature and established commodity niches are all richly covered by
legislature, regulations and standards for the safety and security of consumers.
Just as it is possible to ensure that food is safe for consumption, it is possible
to ensure that software is safe to use.
In this thesis an Information Flow Control (IFC) framework for static security
analysis of a subset of C#, a real-world imperative programming language, is
presented.
This framework is a mechanism which can be used to determine
whether a program is safe when viewed in a given security setting.
The framework is based on an attribute grammar describing the information
ﬂow of a program. It is shown to be such that the bulk of the analyses can
be expressed as logic programs, whereby a state-of-the-art logic programming
implementation can be used instead of custom implementations of search algo-
rithms. The logic program representing an analysis also provides a certiﬁcation
mechanism for the analysed program.
In order to show that the framework works in practice, a proof-of-concept im-
plementation has been produced and applied to a series of test programs. The
implementation produces Datalog programs in the Souﬄé dialect and relies on
a Linuxserver to host the Souﬄé part of the process.

Summary (Danish)
I takt med udbredelsen af billig mobil regnekraft er software blevet en vare, og
som med mange andre varegrupper er der problemstillinger i forhold til sikker-
hed. Fødevarer, beklædning og andre veletablerede og modne varegrupper er
godt dækket ind af lovgivning, regulativer og standarder, som sørger for for-
brugerens tryghed og sikkerhed. På samme måde som det kan lade sige gøre at
sørge for, at fødevarer er sikre at indtage, kan det lade sig gøre at sørge for, at
software er sikkert at bruge.
I denne afhandling presenteres et IFC værktøj for statisk sikkerhedsanalyse af
en delmængde af C#, et programmeringssprog, der bruges i praksis. Værktøjet
er en mekanisme, som kan bruges til at afgøre hvorvidt et program er sikkert i
lyset af en given indstilling til sikkerhed.
En attributgramatik, der beskriver informationstrømmene igennem program-
mer, udgør grundpillen af værktøjet. Det vises at denne attributgramatik og
repræsentationen af informationsstrømmene er sådan, at hovedparten af ana-
lyserne kan beskrives som logikprogrammer. Dette betyder at eksisterende im-
plementeringer af logikprogrammeringssprog kan benyttes fremfor håndskrevne
implementeringer af søgealgoritmer. Logikprogrammet, som representerer udled-
ningen af informationsstrømmene i et program, er desuden en måde at certiﬁcere
programmet som værende sikkert.
For at vise at dette er af mere end blot akademisk interesse, præsenteres desuden
en prototypeimplementering, som med success har været testet på en række
testprogrammer. Prototypen producerer logikprogrammer i Souﬄé-dialekten af
Datalog og benytter en Linux-server til at udføre Souﬄé-delen.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The ubiquity of information gathering devices, accessories and other parapher-
nalia means that everyone has a vested interest in standards for the ﬂow of
information through services and organisations.
One may consider diﬀerent classes of information, such as the ones listed below.
Proprietary information is the sort of information that is likely to be the
target in cases of corporate espionage. The main reasons for protecting
this kind information lies in seeing it as a ﬁnancial asset in its own right.
• Manufacturing technology, recipes
• Things not covered by patents
Personal information is information about individual human beings, and this
is more fundamental in a legal sense.
• Objective: Medical; Legal; Financial
• Subjective: Religious; Philosophical; Political
An important distinction is made in the above between the "owner" of the
information being a company or an individual person. This is not important in

practice or indeed in an academic setting, but it aﬀects how one relates to it. It
is much more appalling to consider having your medical journal, or indeed your
personal journal, disclosed without your consent, than it is to have the secret
inner workings of a company disclosed – even if you are a stakeholder in that
company.
There are diﬀerent concerns regarding the security of the diﬀerent classes of
information – for example your personal objective information should be accu-
rate and free from malign editing, and it should only be accessible by entities
(people, organizations, programs, etc.) with a valid purpose. An example of
malignant edits is identity theft, whereby the security of personal objective in-
formation is broken – someone tricks another entity into treating him or her
as if they where someone else, and can thereby perform malignant edit to the
victims personal information. This is purposefully vague , since it can occurs
in many settings and not all of them are equally severe, nor do they carry the
same legal repercussions, but they are all undesirable and can be covered by the
same mechanisms of information security.
As more an more aspects bureaucratic and legal processes become automated
there is an increasing need for eﬀectively ensuring the security of that automa-
tion. Sporadic errors – human errors – and malfeasance by swindlers when the
workload is handled by people, and there are organizational tools with which
to deal with such. The systematic errors of security in programs that automate
data management and such cannot be dealt with in the exact same way. Instead
they must be handled by the tools available in software development.
1.1
What does "Secure" even mean?
A very simple measure of security is how many people or agents have permis-
sion to view a piece of information, or how many have permission to edit it.
When the security concern is editing of information one is concerned with the
integrity of the information. This intuitively means that the fewer people can
alter something the more guaranteed is its integrity. Similarly when the concern
is viewing or reading then one is concerned with the conﬁdentiality of the infor-
mation. Intuitively information available to fewer people is more conﬁdential.
Another property of information is its availability which is about storage re-
dundancy and is as much a logistic concern as a security one. This aspect of
information security is not within the scope of this project.
The notion of "fewer people" is very speciﬁc and we shall use a more general

notion. In general however both integrity and conﬁdentiality can be captured
by a notion of security that permits gradation – something can be more secure
than something else – i.e. there is an ordering, possibly partial, on security
categories.
The concept of such an ordering of security categories is formalized as frame-
works in subsection 2.2.2, and further reﬁned to Mandatory Access Control
(MAC) security frameworks which have additional properties that make them
desirable.
This is way of tackling one aspect of the conﬁnement problem [Lam73, BK96]
– which in general amounts to limiting processes in order to prevent malicious
behaviour – since a program which is found to be malicious can be prevented
from even being compiled. Another security view that can be addressed is that
of noninterference [McC88], which involves seperating parts of a computation
in order for information which is restricted in some sense not to aﬀect results
which are not restricted.
Cryptography as a way to address these concerns is powerful in some cases, but
also limited. The security resulting from the use of cryptography relies on a
shared secret, and if this secret is released then the security disappears. And
in very real sense cryptography is not capable of addressing the concerns this
project deals with.
1.1.1
Static and Dynamic Security
There are ways to monitor and police program behaviour in order to enforce
security principles dynamically. There are also way in which to verify that a
program conforms to certain security principles in a static fashion. The dynamic
approach permits content awareness in the security principles, but introduces a
run time overhead since the monitoring and enforcement has to be performed
at run time. This is a similar trade-oﬀto the one that occurs between having
run-time errors and having compile-time errors. In a sense security breaches
can be seen as run-time errors, unless of course you are a nefarious agent.
Monitoring and policing programs as they run introduces overhead as it re-
quires enforcement mechanisms. Veriﬁcation and security certiﬁcation on the
other hand shifts the overhead to compile time, where resources are generally
considered cheaper.

1.2
Static Analysis and Software Development
Software has become a commodity and as such the development of it is increas-
ingly outsourced to places with lower wages than are common, for instance, in
Denmark where I am writing this. For a company to have software designed
and developed to certain speciﬁcations for as little money as possible makes
good economic sense, but this only work if the software actually adheres to the
speciﬁcations. For many types of behaviour the speciﬁcations can be tested or
the code can be reviewed by the client in-house, but these procedures can be
expensive and thus reduce the savings by outsourcing in the ﬁrst place.
1.2.1
Static Analysis versus Unit Testing
Unit testing is a technique in software development that is adapted from classic
manufacturing of physical good, where program components are tested as if
they were black-boxes. As a way of dealing with the state-space explosion that
occurs in connected systems, the unit testing approach is not a bad choice, but
it has its limitation in what characteristics can be tested for. This is mainly due
to the black-box nature of the approach.
Static program analysis deals with the problem of verifying behaviour in a fun-
damentally diﬀerent way than the dynamic approach of unit tests by viewing
object programs as mathematical objects and for diﬀerent analyses abstracting
diﬀerent aspects away. This permits a program to be transformed into another
object that has certain properties if the object program had certain properties,
or the other way around, which is of course a very vague description but it
connects program analysis to the concept of approximation.
1.2.2
Static Analysis and Approximation
Venn diagrams, or more precisely diagrams of nested sets are a useful tool to
show what approximation is about. Figure 1.2.1 shows an illustration of how
approximation can fail to capture a precise answer. Only the cases with one
sided errors are shown, since these are the cases that are generally useful.
Approximation in the security branch of static program analysis can be viewed
on diﬀerent levels. At the most basic level an analysis must approximate the
program - some parts must be abstracted away - so as to reduce the complexity.
This translate all the way to the level where an object program is deemed secure

If the judgment of the tool is to be of any practical use we prefer under approxi-

approximation of the capabilities of a program leads to an under approximation
of its security.
There is of course a balancing act here, since the results must be useful, and the
trivial solution of rejecting every program as insecure is certainly not practically
useful, nor academically interesting.

Target Language
Java and C# each expose a compiler Application Programmers Interface (API),

One can ﬁnd many comparisons of the Java and C#languages [Wik20a, Oba07,

C# was chosen in favor of Java as the target language for the analyses of the
project. The choice is in large part based on personal familiarity with C# and
the .Net family of languages, which has advantages in intuiting and also makes
it the preferable choice for the implementation.
While C# is itself a multiparadigmatic language, the .Net framework and the
Common Language Runtime (CLR) permits the seamless integration of libraries
written in other .Net languages. This means that diﬀerent parts of the project
can be implemented in languages based on paradigms that are more suited to
those parts.

Since the project is to a proof-of-concept intended to showcase
an approach it makes sense to choose a subset with enough expressive power,

Chapter 2
Security Analyses
Security analysis has its foundation in the military systems for restricting access
to intelligence information to people or entities with certain privileges.
For
military computer systems these are outlined in [DoD85]. With the ubiquity of
computing, information access, and data gathering the need for such security
has long been found outside the military.
This chapter consists of ﬁrst an overview of the approaches to information se-
curity that are common, then a formalization of the approach adopted here,
followed by an application of it on a subset of the C# language.
2.1
Security Paradigms
Consider a very abstract view of a program: A program is a mechanism for
transforming information. It is read from somewhere – possibly multiple loca-
tions – and combined in some way to form new, if not novel, information. The
resulting information is then stored somewhere. In a more general setting in-
stead of locations one can think of resources, whereby things like microphones
and cameras are also included.
==PAGE:26==

Security Analyses
Of course a program rarely operates in isolation and a very interesting aspect
of its behaviour is its interactions with other programs. A program may for in-
stance use the output of another as its own input, it may itself provide the input
to another program, or it may even be that its input or output are programs or
parts thereof.
2.1.1
Access Control
The most common security feature end users of computers and programs are
familiar with is based on identifying users by log-in, users ownership of data, and
the ability to give programs certain privileges. It is a view based on users having
rights to certain resources – reading and writing are the common ones – and
then giving these rights to programs they run. Microsoft introduced a version of
this known as User Account Control (UAC) with its Windows Vista operating
system that has been included in their subsequent operating systems, and that
almost everyone is familiar with, where a prompt asks you as the end user
a question along the lines of "Do you trust this program?" along with some
information from the signature of the program executable if available.
One of the problems with that kind of access control based security is that
it is based on the users relationship with an authority, be it the signatory of
the executable or the administrator of a system.
This is the problem that
security analysis addresses – not asking the question "Do you trust it?" but
instead by providing a way to answer the question "Can it be trusted?" in an
impartial manner. [SM03] on limitation of enforcement based approaches, i.e.
Discretionary Access Control (DAC) and other enforcement based access control
approaches.
It is also common across operating systems to have some resource require ad-
ministrator privileges, which a user must explicitly bestow. These are of course
a good security feature, because they limit the risk of inadvertently giving ma-
lignant programs access to the system.
2.1.1.1
Discretionary Access Control
Privileges are given in a discretionary fashion, so that an agent with certain
privileges can pass some of them along to another agent.

2.1.2
When is a program secure?
A layperson, as the vast majority of end-users are, may think a program trust-
worthy if it does what it promises with regard to functionality, as in "I trust my
music player to play my music"
Imagine a program which must read some of the users personal data as a way to
deliver the functionality the user expects – such as loading music ﬁles in order
to play them. This may require some privileges that the user grants, but then
having access to this data the program covertly (or accidentally) copies it to a
place without privileged access and thereby compromises security.
Another possibility is that the program writes something the user expects to be
her own personal data and saves it with security ﬂags indicating this. But the
program with which she created the ﬁle covertly incorporates some malignant
data, which then compromises security.
There are of course gray zones in this regard, as is for instance the case with
cookies for tracking users visiting activity across websites, since these are in
large part used to deliver services that the user might prefer to be without.
Using the abstract view of a program as a mechanism for moving information be-
tween locations (resources) and combining information from diﬀerent locations
(resources) we have that the behaviour of a program describes an information
ﬂow. As such a program is secure in a very abstract sense when the ﬂows that it
produces, or may produce, conform to certain security criteria. IFC formalizes
this notion. It is a way of approaching the conﬁnement problem [Lam73, BK96]
which deals with the dissemination of information.
2.2

[DD77] combines the two in an algorithm which performs a simple walk through
the Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) of the object program. This works for the
model language considered and is quite similar to other parts of the compilation
process.

Kinds of Flow
The diﬀerent kinds of ﬂow are K = {ex, im, sa, ⊥}, equipped with an ordering
so that (K, ≺K) is the ordered set of ﬂow kinds. The meaning of the diﬀerent
kinds is explained below.
The discreteness of the set of ﬂows reﬂects that the analysis is qualitative – the

a more involved analysis.
Explicit ﬂow (symbol is ex), occurs when information is explicitly used in an
computation. This is least secure kind of ﬂow, intuitively because a large
portion of the information ﬂow from one entity to another.
Implicit ﬂow (symbol is im), occurs when control ﬂow, or similar, aﬀects in-
formation without being involved explicitly in the computation. This is
the second least secure kind of ﬂow, intuitively because the information
that ﬂows from one entity to another is only an eﬀect on the computation
of the latter.

2.2.1.3
Cooking Metaphor for Justifying Sanitation
Consider how information moves through a program - in a very abstract way
- if the primitive analogy between algorithms/programs and cooking recipes is
used: in cooking ingredients can be moved between containers, they can be
mixed with other ingredients to form new food-stuﬀ, or mixed food-stuﬀcan be
used as an ingredient in a recipe. These containers then mirror the concept of
entities which used in IFC.
The ﬂow occurs between entities, but the entities that align most closely with
the cooking metaphor are those that are receptacles or containers.
An entity can be assigned to – information can ﬂow into it – or the information
can be combined with some other information, as for instance in adding two
integers or combining water and ﬂour – whereby both pieces of information can
be seen as ﬂowing into an intermediate entity – or the entity can be used as an
argument in an invocation.
Thus the ﬂow of ingredients through the steps of a recipe are a good analogy
for the ﬂow of information through a program.
When it comes to the cooking metaphor the ﬂow that is tracked could be one of
potential contaminants - and the security certiﬁcation then used to show that
the recipe cannot lead to contamination of the ﬁnal product. This argues for
the inclusion of sanitized ﬂows, since contaminants must be sterilized away or
chemically neutralized in some way.

2.2.2
Framework
At its most basic a security framework is a set of security categories – sometimes
called labels, but in order to avoid too much confusion we will use the term
categories – equipped with a preorder relation.
The ordering describes the permissible direction(s) of information ﬂow. The
natural intuition when it comes to security is that it is acceptable for information
to ﬂow from one category into another one that is at least as secure while it is
not acceptable for information to ﬂow into a less secure one.
(C, ⊑)
In a general setting the reading of a ⊑b is that of "a is less than or as secure as
b" or "a is not more secure than b", given that the complete ordering a < b would
be read as "a is less secure than b". Here an abstract notion of "secure" is used
since it depends on the context and the purpose but in general the ordering of
the security categories is from least restrictive to most restrictive. This touches
upon the question of What does "Secure" even mean?
The ordering from least to most restrictive is counter-intuitive for integrity where
the category – or categories – of highest integrity information is the least secure.
Information of low integrity can be based on high integrity information but the
reverse is not the case, hence low integrity is restrictive and secure while high
integrity is not restrictive and therefore not secure.
With a few further requirements of the framework we obtain lattices, which in
addition to being partially ordered sets provide a means of combining elements.
This is desirable since we ideally want a set-up which allows us to be lazy and

(S, ⊑, ⊔, ⊓) where
S is a ﬁnite, nonempty set of security categories or simply categories
⊑: S × S is a preorder, that is a binary relation that is
– reﬂexive: ∀s ∈S : (s ⊑s)
– transitive: ∀s1, s2, s3 ∈S : (s1 ⊑s2 ∧s2 ⊑s3 ⇒s1 ⊑s3)
⊔: S × S →S and ⊓: S × S →S are operations for combining/merging

l ∈S is a lower bound on Q ⊆S if ∀q ∈Q : q ⊑l and an upper bound if
∀q ∈Q : l ⊑q.

where l ⊑l0 whenever l0 is an(other) upper bound of Q. The least upper
bound of Q is denoted d Q, while q1 ⊓q2 = d {q1, q2}.
A greatest lower bound of a subset Q of S is a lower bound l ∈S of Q
where l0 ⊑l whenever l0 is a(nother) lower bound of Q. The greatest
lower bound of Q is denoted F Q, while q1 ⊔q2 = F {q1, q2}.

ordering on S and least upper bounds and greatest lower bounds of subsets of
S are unique when they exist.
Furthermore if every subset of S has greatest lower bound and a least upper

⊤= d ∅= F S and minimal or least element ⊥= d S = F ∅.

2.3
Extracting Flow Information
As mentioned earlier there are several ways of extracting the ﬂow information
from the AST of an object program.
It can be done by describing an explicit algorithm as is the case of [DD77] or by
a type system as is the case of [VIS96]. Both of these combine the two facets into
a single process, and while this has some advantages one of the main points of
this project is the expression of the analysis in an intermediate representation.
Speciﬁcations in the style of ﬂow logic1 as presented in [NN98, NN02, NNP12]
is one that lends itself readily to using Datalog to solve constraints. While this
is not the main concern at this stage, the ﬂow logic speciﬁcation also have the
advantage of being relatively easy to translate into an implementation. It is
important to note that the ﬂow in ﬂow logic is not the ﬂow of IFC.
Flow logic is way of expressing the relationship between syntactic elements and
constraints for static program analysis, not unlike type systems. It is presented
in [NN02] as an approach to static program analysis that separates the speciﬁ-
cation of the analysis from its computation.
Since the goal is to have the computation expressed as a Datalog program this
separation is a necessity in the implementation and hence desirable in the spec-
iﬁcation.
Another alternative is to use an attribute grammar as formalized in [Knu68],
which is a method for describing semantics of context free languages and which
permits the treatment of ﬂow as an attribute of the object program.

2.3.2
Information Flow in C#
In C# information ﬂow occurs in the bodies of methods and as a result of
the invocation of methods.
Some methods may be implicitly declared, and
some implicitly invoked, but the components, the T set, can in large part be
considered a set of method identiﬁers.
The set of entities, the L set, includes all named elements of the program. For
convenience we shall assume that the program has been alpha-renamed so that
every named element can be unambiguously identiﬁed by its name. A program
which has unresolvable ambiguities is semantically incorrect, cannot be compiled
and can therefore be arbitrarily rejected as insecure.
2.3.2.1
C# language subset
There is a trade oﬀbetween the expressiveness of the language subset chosen,
and the complexity of the task of specifying, verifying and implementing security
analyses for it.
As much of the syntax of C# is syntactic sugar – meaning it represents a short-
hand or a circumstantially preferable rewriting – it simpliﬁes things to focus
only on the novel features. An excellent example of this is the looping control
structures where all can be rewritten to use the while( ) construct, which can
therefore be seen as the elementary looping structure. There are however some
structure which are more aptly described as distinct elements, as is for instance
the case with arithmetic operations on the basic numeric data types. Operator
expressions in general are syntactic sugar for methods on the involved types,
but since the methods are not declared in source a treatment of them on par
with other methods would result in overly paranoid over-approximation, which
does not reﬂect the language speciﬁcation.
The C# language speciﬁcation [HPIM] describes it as being a multiparadig-
matic language, with support for event driven, functional, and OOP techniques.
However at its core it is an OOP language. Focusing on the OOP aspect is an
excellent way of limiting the scope of the ﬂow extraction speciﬁcation.

2.3.3.3
Correctness of the Judgment Clauses
The speciﬁcation of C# in [HPIM] is as an industrial standard and not as an
academic research object, meaning that the format is not as formal or with as
clear a logical foundation as for a model language. This means that correctness
proofs would require ﬁrst the formalization of the semantics. While this is a
necessary requirement for security certiﬁcation of software such a formalization
of the semantics of the C# – even just the considered subset – is not the goal
of this project, and we shall instead use a less formal approach.
The approach here will be to argue for the correctness rather than prove it.
This is in line with the most common approach of programmers to programming
languages – that an intuitive understanding of the program behaviour is more
important than the underlying logical structures. This is aided in great part by
the fact that the subset of the language under consideration is very standard
for an OOP language.

2.3.9
Method Declaration
All ﬂow of information in a C# program occurs in the bodies of methods – or of
course more precisely as a consequence of the applications of methods – which
may be implicit as is the case with ﬁeld initialisation of classes.
This taps into a broader problem of the direct and the indirect ﬂows induced by
a method. Because we want the same method to be able to handle diﬀerent sets
of security categories without cross-contaminating them we need to only relabel
the endpoints of ﬂows that are not local to the method.
Cross-contaminating ﬂow is information ﬂow between arguments resulting from
distinct invocations of the same method.
Consider for instance a static class which has a method for summing integers and
which keeps track of the maximum sum produced so far. This necessarily results
in explicit ﬂows from all the arguments to the ﬁeld in which the maximum is
stored, but there will also be ﬂows from the arguments to a temporary variable
which could result in cross-contamination if there is no allowance made for
keeping distinct applications distinct.
The test object program "SourceMethods" shown in A.12 contains a maxAdd
function which is exactly the method described above. It should not produce
ﬂow between the arguments of diﬀerent invocations – or of the same invocation.
In order to correctly capture cross-contamination, when it reﬂects the actual
semantics, there is a need to be able to identify local entities, i.e. distinguish
between local and non-local. The analysis of ﬂow resulting from the invocation

2.3.10.2
Sanitizing Methods
Presently there is no regard for sanitizing ﬂows – there is no way to sanitize –
The way to sanitize in practice is to have a the policy speciﬁcation assign a
security category to the entity representing the return value of a function. We
need to introduce a new entity for the sanitized version, and use it to introduce a
"discontinuity" in the explicit and implicit ﬂows to be replaced by the sanitized
one.
Doing it this way means that the ﬂow relation more accurately represents the
choice of the programmer. We could choose not to replace the discontinuity with
a sanitized ﬂow and simply have the ﬂow relation not reﬂect the fact that there
even is a ﬂow, but this risks making the object program seem more secure than
it is. Since sanitizing methods are a way of circumventing the security policy
and framework they need to be implemented and used carefully. To ensure that
they serve a purpose apart from circumventing the policy and framework their
implementation and use should be audited. It therefore seems quite sensible to
explicitly record when such methods have been involved in information ﬂow.
Much like the predicate identifying local entities there is a need for a predicate
identifying sanitizing methods, san(·) shall be used for this predicate.

Language elements missing
The presented clauses only cover a subset of the C# language, even when syn-

This section does however outline some of the challenges adapting the framework

2.3.13.1
Polymorphism
The OOP paradigm conceptually treats program components as objects, thereby
the name, and many of the relationships between physical objects have coun-
terparts in the program objects: "A is a kind of B"; "A can behave like B";
"A contains a B". The two ﬁrst are not obviously distinct but there is a subtle
diﬀerence, either way the latter is covered by the programming concept of ﬁelds
– an object has a reference to another object (or to a value of some kind). But
the other(s) cover a kind of polymorphism that is quite powerful and useful. "A
is kind of B" entails a sub-typing or inheritance relation, while "A can behave
like B" entails an interface relation.
Multiple interfaces can be implemented, but only a single inheritance is possible.
Interfaces describe abstract behaviour – a set of methods with signatures and
names that an implementing type must provide. An interface is a kind
of type and all instances of implementing types are instances of the inter-
face. Thus an invocation of a method on an interface must account for all
implementing methods, which poses a modelling challenge.
Inheritance and sub-typings means that a type can "reuse" another one and
instances of the former be considered instances of the latter, while the
reverse is not the case. Inheriting classes may override methods in the
parent class to introduce new or to tweak existing functionality, but there
is modelling challenge in ensuring that overridden methods and their ﬂows
are accounted for when invoked on the parent class.
Generics are types with type arguments and represent a way to have for in-
stance a class for lists that is agnostic on its members but requires them all
to be of the same type. "Behind the scenes" a generic type with concrete
type arguments is a class in and of itself – i.e. it is syntactic sugar for
having a distinct class for each and every set of type arguments.

2.3.13.2
Delegates (function pointers)
Delegates provide function types and are similar to method signatures, except
they are a type in an of themselves and instances are function pointers that can
be invoked.
This can be viewed as a departure from the OOP paradigm and entering into the
territory of the Functional Programming paradigm. Though Java for instance
circumvents this by having functional interfaces, which are way of viewing dele-
gates as syntactic sugar for ad hoc types implementing an interface. In this way
delegates can be analysed using the same principles that apply to the OOP parts.
However this still requires the handling of interfaces, as in polymorhpism, and
ad hoc type deﬁnitions.
2.3.13.3
Low level control ﬂow
The C# language allows for low level control ﬂow using labelled and goto state-
ments as well as the breaking of loops, and while these can certainly be analysed
for information ﬂow, there are ways of achieving the same functionality within
the presently analysable subset of the language.
Labeled statements and goto
While labeled statements and goto state-
ments permit complicated control ﬂow paradigms, their analysis would also
require control ﬂow analysis and hence not be possible by a simple traversal of
the AST. Further there is also no behaviour achievable with goto-statements
that cannot be achieved with the presently analysable control-ﬂow constructs.
Breaking from loops
There are no practical problems with extending the
analysis to be able to handle break statements, which permit the "breaking" of
a loop. The reason for not including it is that the behaviour can be achieved in

does not measure up to the usefulness of the augmentation.
2.3.13.4
Exceptions and Exception Handling
Exceptions are special classes which are used for exception handling. The analy-
sis need not cover the exception classes themselves diﬀerently than other classes,
but the handling may be tricky.
The challenge lies in the fact that exceptions thrown are not explicitly speciﬁed
and it essentially requires an entire analysis step to determine what exceptions
can possibly arise within a try block and which are caught by the associated
catch blocks – the rest would then induce further ﬂows – but without this
information the only way is to have all exception induced ﬂow continue (except
possibly for the base exception, which is rarely a desirable thing to catch).
Java for instance has explicit exceptions – a method must disclose the exceptions
that may be thrown as a result of its invocation, with some exceptions2 of course
for methods declared it is possible to determine the explicitly thrown exceptions,
that is the ones who arise from a throw statement in the source code.
Exception handling constitutes a control ﬂow. While not intended for it, the

Datalog
Datalog is a family of programming languages in the logic programming paradigm.
It can serve as a relational database query language and as a tool for automatic
theorem proving.
Programs in Datalog are speciﬁed using statements of a fragment of ﬁrst order
logic. The statements take the form of Horn clauses and variables are implicitly
quantiﬁed. Though it has a strong mathematical foundation but there is no
common speciﬁcation for the syntax.
Both [AHV95] and [BA01] have excellent introductions to the theory behind
logic programming as well as to concrete Datalog implementations.
3.1
Logic Programming Overview
For a details on the theoretical basis of logic programming there are excellent
textbooks with diﬀering approaches. While [BA01] takes the approach of logic
programming as a tool for automatic theorem proving, [AHV95] approaches it
as a way of extracting information from databases, that is as a query language.
==PAGE:70==
56
Datalog
The overview here is very rudimentary and assumes a familiarity with predicate
or ﬁrst order logic.
3.1.1
Predicate Logic
Predicate Logic or ﬁrst order logic forms the basis of logic programming.
While propositional logic simply deals with atomic statements such as "it is
raining in Denmark" or "Alice is wet" it is possible to describe more nuanced
situations in predicate logic.
raining(Cph) ∧in(Cph, Alice) ∧outside(Alice) ⇒wet(Alice)
3.1.2
Horn Clauses
Definition 3.1 A Horn clause is a clause of the form:
A ⇐B1 ∧. . . ∧Bn ≡A ∨¬B1 ∨. . . ∨¬Bn
with at most one positive literal. The positive literal A can be referred to as
the head while the negative literals Bi collectively form the body.
• If it consists of a single positive literal A ⇐it is a fact
• If it has only negative literals ⇐B1 ∧. . . ∧Bn, is a goal clause
• Otherwise it is a program clause with one positive and at least one negative
literal
The use of the ⇐operator for reverse-implication, "if" or "implied by", is prefer-
able in the context of logic programming because the clause A ⇐B1 ∧. . . ∧Bn
then has the natural reading:
To prove A ﬁrst prove B1 and . . . and Bn
Which is quite similar to the programmatic concept of procedures.
A more
useful analogy however is that of production, of a recipe.
==PAGE:71==
3.1 Logic Programming Overview
57
3.1.3
Resolution
A convenient way of thinking about resolution as it applies to logic programming
is as a production system. In that way a Horn clause has a product, the head,
given some ingredients, the body, whereby they become production rules – or
more precisely production rule schema, since the "product" depends on the
"ingredients".
Diﬀerent applications of logic programming have diﬀerent requirements for the
behaviour. In the case of proof assistance and automatic theorem proving it
makes sense to have a goal clause which is the negation of a lemma or statement
one wishes to prove – since the failure then constitutes a proof by negation –
and this view can of course be applied to IFC and security analysis, but more
useful information can be obtained by taking the view that the program is to
compute every statement that is true, instead of determining whether a single
statement is true. This latter view is that of a database query language.
3.1.3.1
Resolution as Computation
Thinking of the resolution occurring in the resolution process as production has
the advantage of linking it closely with computation, i.e. the production of a
result.
3.1.4
Stratiﬁcation
Negation is problematic [BA01], since it can lead to non-termination thereby
compounding the problem of non-determinism, since some executions may result
in non-termination while other do not.
In a stratiﬁed Datalog program the predicates – speciﬁcally those involved in
program clauses – are prioritised, thereby allowing the program to be treated
as what amounts to a sequence of sub-programs where each only depend on the
result of the preceding ones and not on any of the following. This means that
whenever a negated predicate occurs it is possible to determine it veracity. It
does however place some restrictions on the use of negation in recursive clauses,
so that a predicate cannot be the head of a clause that includes its own negation
in the body. More details on stratiﬁcation can be found in [AHV95].

3.2
The Souﬄé Language
The Datalog language consists of predicate deﬁnitions, variable deﬁnitions and
clauses. The predicates are deﬁned by their arity and rank, the variables are
deﬁned to distinguish between existential and universal quantiﬁcation (deﬁned
variables are universally quantiﬁed while non-deﬁned ones are existentially quan-
tiﬁed), clauses then serve to describe the relations in the universe - i.e. the
predicates. clauses are of the form p(⃗a) ⇐r1, . . . , rn
A Datalog program speciﬁes a set of Horn clauses, which are conjunctions of
literals with at most one positive, the can be written using conjunction an
implication which yields an intuitive reading. The syntax of course involves more
than the simple logic, since there is a need for specifying some behaviours for
the generation of solution in a predictable, consistent and timely manner. There
is of course also the syntactic sugar which is almost unavoidable in languages
above a basic abstraction level.

recall the equivalence ¬∃A : p(A) ≡∀A : ¬p(A) in predicate logic

for all possible values A can take. This means that all program clauses with p
at its head must be exhausted before any clause with the negation of p in its
body can be investigated.
3.2.2.3

Binding and bound identiﬁers are just as is usual for ﬁrst order predicate logic
taking into account the implicit quantiﬁers. The only caveat is if the ﬁrst oc-
currence of an identiﬁer is in a negated predicate, since this would amount to
universal quantiﬁcation on the right-hand side, it does not bind any identiﬁer.
This is for practical reasons to force a limitation on the search space, which
would otherwise be the entire universe of the interpretation.

3.2.2.4
Predicates or Relations
A common feature of Datalog implementations which is also present in Souﬄé is
that predicates must be explicitly declared. This has several advantages one
being that it makes it easier to enforce the requirement that a predicate has
a predeﬁned arity and that it semantically incorrect to use the predicate with
another number of arguments.
In contrast to other implementation of stratiﬁed Datalog Souﬄé does not have
a way of explicitly assigning ranks to predicates, but the ordering is inferred
from the clauses deﬁning the interdependency of the predicates.
3.2.2.5
Types
In the declaration of predicates the syntax requires that types are given for
parameters, which both serves to separate the universe of objects into disjoint
sets - one for each type - which could be done by custom predicates, and as part
of the built-in support for basic data-types in Souﬄé.
3.2.2.6
Input and output
Predicates can optionally be declared as input and/or output, which is how
Souﬄé can be used as a relational database query language. A program forms
a query with the relations marked as inputs as the database being queried and
the relations marked as outputs form a database themselves which represents
the answer(s) to the query.
3.2.2.7
Facts
Those clauses which have no right-hand side, and whose arguments are all con-
stant elements (i.e. strings or numbers in the sub-set under consideration) are
facts servings as the basis of computation. They can either be "hard-coded" as
part of the program proper, or they can be given as input in a Comma Sepa-
rated Values (CSV)-like ﬁle-format. Where the latter is of course preferable in
most use-cases since it permits the separation between the speciﬁcation of the

program and the data. This is compounded by the fact that Souﬄé programs
are compiled through C to native executables, meaning that the compilation
overhead is eﬀectively part of the cost of a pre-computation step.
3.2.2.8
The Universe
There is no explicit declaration of the universe in Souﬄé, but the constants used
in the input facts and the program proper form the basis of the universe. The full
language also has functionality for arithmetic operations and other operations
on the basic data-types, and indeed the introduction of new custom data-types.
This allows the computational "expansion" of the universe during the execution
of the program.

The attribute grammar expressing the extraction of ﬂow information from an
AST can be seen as describing a set of mutually recursive functions for traversing
the tree, where the judgement clauses appearing in the formula for a given
clause describe the recursion and the rest of the clause describes the extracted
ﬂow. Translating the totality of these descriptions of the extracted ﬂow for an
entire object program into a Datalog program then permits the usage of logic
programming tools and methodology in the certiﬁcation process.
While one could conceivably device an intermediate representation between the
logic clauses used in describing the attribute grammar and Datalog, there is
really no need for this. Many of the clauses describing the attribute grammar
are trivial to translate to predicate logic, and hence to Datalog. While a direct
translation is possible, it is not necessarily ideal, since it would entail generating
a lot of program clauses – for instance in the case of the analysis of invocation
expressions.
Another approach which avoids generating program clauses is to build relations
describing the ﬂow, in essence to collect facts about the object program, and
then have a static set of program clauses which depend on these facts to produce
the complete ﬂow.
This is in line with the fact that some of the problems abstracted away in

Security Framework and Policy Speciﬁcation
Arbitrary security frameworks can be represented in Datalog, since the only
requirement is that the set of security categories have an associated preorder
relation.
The ordering of kinds and the least upper bound as presented in 4.1 are used
in the expressing the ﬂow relations.
They are an example of how arbitrary
orderings and associated bounds could be represented in Datalog. It also shows
some techniques for using fewer clauses.
In the worst case however the framework (S, ⊑, ⊔, ⊓) can be represented using
O(|S|2) fact clauses, since ⊑is a binary relation on S and can therefore be seen
as set of pairs of elements from S, while ⊔and ⊓can be determined from ⊑.
For arbitrary frameworks ⊔and ⊓would take O(|S|3) facts to store explicitly in
their entirety, which falls to O(|S|2) if ⊑is anti-symmetric, because then each
pair of elements from S can have only one greatest lower bound (and only one
least upper bound).
This interesting because the fact clauses form part of the program used to cer-
tify an object program, however the complexity of the ﬂow relation is likely
to dominate this, having orders of magnitude more elements than the security
framework. So while it may be an entirely academic concern there are other
advantages to restricting the discussion to powerset-based frameworks.
A big advantage of powerset-base frameworks is that the internal structure while
simple and easily expressible in Datalog allows the expression of interesting
security concepts – DLM for instance can be considered powerset-based as is
explained in 2.2.2, and models a view of security with decentralized authority.
Another advantage is that the internal structure allows more ﬁne grained mod-
elling in Datalog than would be the case if the framework were modelled simply
by collections of facts.

Implementation
In order to show the viability of the approach presented in this report a proof of
concept implementation has been produced which supports securtity analysis of
a subset of the C# programming language. The implementation is restricted to
powerset based security frameworks with a superset ordering, where the security
categories are implicitly deﬁned by those employed in the policy.
The restriction to superset ordering is mainly because there is no way of selecting
between superset and subset ordering, though this would be relatively simple to
introduce.
An implicitly deﬁned set of security categories is simpler than an explicit one,
but introduces some risks. The changes needed to allow and enforce an explicitly
deﬁned set of categories are relatively small, but simplicity has been prioritised.
While an overview of the design is given here, more detailed documentation on

5.1.1
Custom Parser or Compiler Inter-operation
One of the questions to be answered in the eﬀort to develop a proof of concept
implementation of the analysis approach presented in this report was whether
to base it on the compiler API or to implement a custom parser.
The compiler is already part of the Trusted Code Base (TCB) and a custom
parser would not displace but rather be a further addition to the TCB, which
of course increases the risk of security problems.
The compiler API exposes a representation of the language model, including
representation of the AST
The speciﬁcation for the C# language, [HPIM], deﬁnes the syntax in Extended
Backus-Naur Form (EBNF) using 464 non-terminal symbols and 1186 produc-
tions1. This gives rise to some challenges simply by the scope of the model, but
is further complicated by its complexity. Further the .Net frameworks C# com-
piler and parser API has 268 classes for representing the structure of a C# AST.
While a great deal of the semantics are disregarded in the security analyses
some are important in guiding the analysis - this includes references to library
functions and types/classes deﬁned outside the analysed source. There is also
the matter of resolving ambiguous references where the compiler API makes the
matter trivial.
Ultimately basing the implementation on the compiler API exposed by the
.Net framework allows for more time to be spent on other parts of the imple-
mentation whereby more interesting and consequential aspects of the problem
can be examined.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The presented framework for IFC-based static security analysis of source code
in a subset of the C# language has a multitude of applications. The framework
provides a way of determining whether a given program always behaves in a
way that is benign – much like testing physical product for user safety.
It is a tool that can be applied alongside a suite of tests on the functionality
of code not developed in house, in order to ensure that it meets the speciﬁed
requirements. In this way the security of unfamiliar code can be determined
without the need for manual investigation of the details of the source. The only
eﬀort required would be the selective insertion of appriate attributes, in order
to be able to determine the security status. Of course the use of the framework
could also be part of the requirements in the contract.
These points apply
equally well to open-source develpment, where the open access to the source
means that everyone can verify the security status. Mobile apps can be certiﬁed
in a way that can guarantee to the user that when they grant the app access to
the camera or microphone, for instance, then the app cannot use it in nefarious
ways.
The static security analysis of IFC involves three distinct elements, namely
information ﬂow, security framework and security policy. These are explained
in section 2.2, where it is also explained that the IFC analysis of an object
program is a certiﬁcation process that succeeds if the program does not have a
==PAGE:100==

ﬂow from one entity to another where the policy and the framework prohibits
it.
The process of extracting ﬂow information from an object program uses the
AST of the program as a starting point. The ﬂow relation can be built up, in
a sense, by considering syntactic elements one at a time, in a recursive fash-
ion, from root to leaf. Since there are no syntactic constraints on the ordering
of for instance methods in a type declaration, there is no guarantee that any
ordering will mean that all required information is available at the right time.
What is extracted is not as much the actual ﬂow, except for trivial programs,
but a description or a set requirements of the ﬂow relation if it is to correctly
capture the ﬂow of the object program. This is where Datalog and logic pro-
gramming comes into play, as it provides a way of expressing these descriptions
and expanding them into a complete ﬂow relation. If the Datalog program also
contains descriptions of the security framework and the security policy, then it
can serve as a way to certify the object program as secure. Chapter 4 shows how
the descriptions can be expressed in Datalogand in Chapter 5 a proof-of-concept
implementation of the framework is described. The proof-of-concept implemen-
tation is limited to a single security framework which is implicitly deﬁned by the
security policy of the object program, however it is designed to be extensible in
as many ways as possible.
Future work could move along one of the lines outlined in Chapter 6. One posi-
bility is testing the eﬃciency of diﬀerent approaches to the Datalog– from the
implementation used to the formulation of the programs themselves. Another
is to allow diﬀerent security frameworks to be used, which could be done using
a DSL. The tool could also be adapted to ensure that compiled code carries
metadata on the ﬂow relation, whereby such annotated libraries could be refer-
enced and their use analysed as if the tool had access to their source. The most
obvious future work that could be done on the project is of course expanding
the subset of the object language that can be analysed. Beyond this a more in
depth documentation would be desirable.

B.2
Choices
Considerations regarding the choices for parts of the implementation
B.2.1
Parse
The goal is to develop a compiler extension and it therefore makes sense to make
use of the parsing performed during compilation. There then is the choice of
whether to use the model employed by the compiler API as the interface to the
analysis or to convert it to a custom representation which is more suitable for
analysis.
B.2.1.1
Representation
The AST representation used by the compiler API may or may not be ideal for
extraction of constraints.
B.2.2
Analyse
Ideally this constitutes translation of the mathematical framework deﬁned in the
Security Analyses and Datalog chapters, and depending on the representation
used this may require some massaging to accomplish. This speaks in favor of
choosing a representation of the AST which simpliﬁes the process.
B.2.2.1
Emission of Datalog
Internally Datalogis represented as collection of algebraic data-types, which
amounts to an AST representation of a Souﬄé program suﬃcient for the needs
of the application. In principle the same could have been achieved by using for-
matted strings, but the chosen approach achieves a separation of concerns and a
modularity which would make it simpler to use another Datalog implementation.
