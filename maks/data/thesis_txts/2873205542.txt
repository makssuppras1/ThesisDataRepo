Abstract
The ongoing European energy transition, characterized by ambitious decarbonization targets
and an exponential increase in intermittent renewable energy sources, necessitates unprece-
dented levels of grid flexibility. While large-scale renewable deployment is driven by increasing
cost-competitiveness, the integration of distributed energy resources (DERs) at the grid edge,
such as fleets of small battery systems and electrolyzers, is crucial for balancing supply and
demand fluctuations and enhancing overall system eﬀiciency. This thesis focuses on the oper-
ational and economic optimization of such DERs.
This project leverages the unique assets at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU)’s
PowerLabDK, specifically a battery energy storage system and an electrolyzer. The research
accurately assesses the physical capabilities of these assets and identifies relevant electricity
markets, including day-ahead and various ancillary services, by mapping their specific entry
requirements. Subsequently, experimentally parameterized multi-market bidding models are
developed for both the battery and the electrolyzer, designed to maximize their respective
revenues across all available market opportunities.
Through perfect-hindsight back-testing on real historical data, this work provides an esti-
mate of the theoretical maximum financial performance of these assets and finds that profitabil-
ity of the battery is insensitive to the cycling limits when working in a multi-market setting but
increased cycling adds unjustifiable degradation costs. It finds further that the electrolyzer’s
profitability is very sensitive to the hydrogen market price and marginal increases in it lead to
very large increases in profitability due to multi-market dynamics.

Test to determine the round-trip eﬀiciency under normal operating conditions.

1
Introduction
1.1
Context
The global energy landscape is undergoing an unprecedented transformation driven by the ur-
gent need to address climate change and achieve energy sustainability. The European Union
stands at the forefront of this shift, committed to ambitious decarbonization targets, including
a 55% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2030 and climate neutrality by 2050 [10]. Pro-
jections indicate a relentless increase in renewable energy penetration, with solar photovoltaic
(PV) and wind power expected to constitute the vast majority of new electricity generation
capacity [14]. This rapid expansion is not merely a policy-driven mandate but increasingly a
consequence of the overwhelming and growing competitiveness of renewable energy technolo-
gies.
In parallel with large-scale renewable projects, a profound shift is occurring at the grid’s
edge, characterized by the proliferation of distributed energy resources (DERs). Central to this
development are fleets of small battery systems, ranging from residential battery storage units
in homes [4] to commercial and industrial (C&I) battery installations [29]. Examples of this
rapid deployment are evident across Europe, where homeowners and businesses are increasingly
installing batteries to store excess solar power, optimize electricity bills, and enhance energy
resilience.
While individually small, the aggregated capacity of these distributed batteries
represents a significant and flexible resource for the grid.
For such individual, small-scale devices, the primary conceptual business model revolves
around maximizing self-consumption from rooftop photovoltaic (PV) generation [32]. By stor-
ing surplus PV electricity that is not immediately consumed, these battery systems reduce the
household’s or business’s reliance on grid power, thereby minimizing purchases at potentially
high retail electricity prices. This localized optimization significantly enhances the economic
attractiveness of PV installations. However, once the immediate self-consumption needs are
met, or when the system has excess capacity, these devices can evolve beyond passive load-
shifters. They can then react to prevailing market signals, charging when grid electricity prices
are exceptionally low (e.g., during periods of high wind generation) and discharging when
prices are high, or providing specific grid services. While individual devices typically operate
as price-takers without the market power to influence prices, their collective capacity becomes
substantial when aggregated. This aggregated capacity, for instance by a Distribution Sys-
tem Operator (DSO) or a third-party aggregator, can then be strategically bid into wholesale
electricity markets, such as the day-ahead market, thereby influencing market outcomes and
unlocking new revenue streams that are inaccessible to isolated assets [1]. It is therefore think-
able to project further developments in the future where developments in battery technology
will increase system readability and will allow direct individual access to the electricity markets
for small prosumers. The question is therefore how such small prosumers or controllable loads
(such as an electrolyzer) can be operated in multiple electricity markets to provide system
balancing services.
This thesis aims to demonstrate the practical operations and optimization of such small scale
distributed energy assets. This work leverages the unique facilities at the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark (DTU), which possesses both a research-grade battery energy storage system
and an electrolyzer that can be directly utilized to participate in real-world electricity mar-
ket simulations and experiments. These assets provide a platform for validating the proposed
operational strategies.

1.2
Objectives
This Master’s thesis is guided by a set of objectives designed to investigate the operational
and economic optimization of distributed energy resources, specifically focusing on the battery
energy storage system and electrolyzer located at PowerLabDK (PLDK) at DTU. The objectives
are as follows:
• To accurately assess the physical capabilities of the PLDK battery and electrolyzer with
respect to possibilities for operation in various energy markets.
• To identify the electricity markets that could be sources of revenue for the PLDK assets
by mapping the entry requirements and assessing the extent to which the PLDK assets
qualify for them.
• To develop a realistically parameterized multi-market bidding model for the battery in
PLDK that maximizes its revenues across all available markets.
• To develop a realistically parameterized multi-market bidding model for the electrolyzer
in PLDK that maximizes its revenues across all available markets.
• To give an estimate of theoretical maximum financial performance of the assets based on
perfect-hindsight back-testing on real historical data.

Thesis structure
The remainder of this thesis is structured to systematically address the stated objectives, moving
from a foundational understanding of the physical assets to the development and demonstration
of optimal dispatch algorithms, and finally to their performance evaluation.

BOSS system state
The Xolta battery was installed in PLDK in July 2021. During this time the system has de-

It has accumulated a number of cycles and has degraded as a result. This section estimates

2.2.1
Available capacity at nominal power
The nominal power for this project is set at 30 kW, although the system can deliver up to
50kW. For the sake of safety, however, the technical recommendation of the PLDK staff is
to run the battery at no more than 30 kW, hence the artificially imposed limit. The models
developed in subsequent sections always assume that the battery will be able to charge or
discharge at full capacity for the entire duration of the scheduling period. For example, if the
day-ahead market was promised 30 kW for 1 hour, this is what the battery should do. The
Xolta battery, however, cannot deliver its full nameplate kWh capacity at nominal power. The
typical charging behavior can be seen in Fig. 3, showing how as the voltage approaches its
maximum, the power decays exponentially due to the inability of the battery to accept charge
quickly enough. This phase of charging is characterized by uncontrollable power and is therefore
unsuitable for market bidding. The available kWh capacity is therefore limited to the point
before the constant charging phase begins: approximately 95% SOC.
During the discharge phase as the DC voltage approaches the minimum, the power starts to
decay rapidly and the BMS stops the power draw to prevent a deep discharge, as seen in Fig.
4. The SOC level at which this behavior begins is approximately 12%.
Figure 3: As the DC voltage approaches its maximum, the battery cannot accept the full charging current
and so the charging power diminishes, even though the setpoint doesn’t change. This illustrates why the
available capacity at nominal power is limited to a value lower than the maximum possible battery capacity.
The power cannot follow the setpoint after a certain voltage level is reached. Therefore, a
market-aware operation requires not the full theoretical capacity but the capacity range within
which the battery’s power is fully and reliably controllable. This available capacity is estimated
with the following procedure:
• The battery is started and discharged at nominal power until the discharge power can no
longer follow the setpoint. The SOC at this level is recorded as the minimum SOC.
• The battery is then charged at nominal power (30kW) until the power start to decay.
The SOC at this level s recorded as the maximum SOC for operations.
• The battery is rested for 1 hour to stabilize temperature and balance its chemistry.

• The battery is discharged again to the min SOC to deliver a full cycle (for round-trip-

• The market-usable capacity is then estimated by integrating the power charge and dis-

setpoint during the discharge phase begin, the discharge is stopped manually. The available

2.2.2
Historical rainflow cycling
In order to assess the system’s overall state, the total amount of accumulated cycles since
installation has to be estimated.
This number is important for getting a sense of cycling-
induced degradation. The total number of cycles calculation is based on the historical SOC
profile of the system. A major challenge to accurately calculating the cycles from the SOC
signal is that the cycles are irregular: they vary in depth of discharge, duration, range, and
mean value. The quasi-standard tool used in the literature (see [5], [31]) for cycling calculations
of a battery is the rainflow algorithm. It is applied to a series of data points, in our case SOC,
to identify inflection points in the data. The distances between each two inflection points are
designated with a partial cycle of a corresponding depth, time range and average value. These
partial cycles are then paired together to form full cycles. A very basic demonstration of the
algorithm can be seen in Fig. 5. The cycles are binned by SOC range and showed in Table 2.

lower than the per-cycle RTE. The results of this analysis are shown in Fig. 9.

Model implementation
The available historical system data from Energiedata.dk was used to determine the relevant
degradation statistics as described in previous sections. For the calendar aging, the model
implementation was a straightforward evaluation of Eq. 9 function. The cycling aging was
evaluated in the following steps:
• The raw battery power data was integrated to calculate the amount of equivalent full
cycles (EFC) according to Eq. 3.
• The approximate time of each EFC was identified by taking the midpoint between the
beginning of the cycle and its end, i.e. when the integrated power accumulates to an
EFC. This was necessary as there are long stretches of time where the battery stayed
idle, meaning that some EFCs are also stretched in time.
• The mean C-rate and temperature were extracted from the newly formed cycle-indexed
dataset.
The output of this process is a matrix containing each cycle, its approximate time, mean
C-rate and temperature as shown in Table 6. The data was used to evaluate Eq. 11. The
result is shown in Fig. 18. The combined degradation from calendar and cycling was then
evaluated and plotted together with the measurements in Fig. 19. Fig. 21 extend the model
to the nameplate projected lifetime of the system (15 years) and assumes an increased cycling
rate of 1 EFC per day. In that mode of operation, the system should breach the 80% mark
around 2032-2033. Of course these estimates may vary significantly and the model does show
only the general trend of degradation, as shown by the imperfect fit to the data. It should be
noted, however, that the conditions of the capacity tests themselves are subject to significant
variation. For example, the test in mid 2022 was held at an average temperature of 32 °C, and
the one in 2025 at 34 °C. The fact that the system is so sensitive to ambient conditions makes
it diﬀicult to estimate the SOH in the first place. Therefore, attempting to fit a model precisely
would be very diﬀicult and the additional work adds only marginal value to this thesis. The
results of this model are therefore considered an acceptable approximation.

2.3.1
Overview
Power Lab DK is equipped with an Anion Exchange Membrane (AEM) electrolyzer system
built by the German company Enapter. Enapter’s strategy is to sell modular electrolyzers that
can be easily integrated in large stacks to flexibly meet customer needs. The stack in PLDK
consists of 3 interconnected modules as the one shown in Fig. 22. The system provides a unique
opportunity for DTU students to understand the practicalities of electrolysis.
A goal of this thesis is to establish a method for optimal operation of the Enapter system
in electricity markets, with the objective of maximizing profit from hydrogen production and
provision of flexibility by reducing the power consumption rate. To develop a realistic dispatch
model, the physical characteristics of the system must be mapped as accurately as possible,
which is what this section attempts to do. The following subsections explain the aspects of the
machine that are relevant for its market-aware operation.

2.3.2
Anion Exchange Membrane chemistry
The chemical characteristics of the PLDK electrolyzer are given in Table 8. They were experi-

An AEM electrolyzer splits water into hydrogen and oxygen by utilizing a solid polymer mem-
brane that selectively conducts negatively charged hydroxide ions (OH−) from the cathode to
the anode. At the cathode, water molecules are reduced, producing hydrogen gas (H2) and

are oxidized to form oxygen gas (O2) and water.

The chemical characteristics, influencing the market-aware operation are as follows:
• Ph-level. The Enapter modules’ electrolyte is a mildly alkaline solution of diluted potas-
sium hydroxide and highly purified water. The mild PH-Level is less demanding on the
electrodes which allows for the use of cheaper, non-noble metals, making the system
cheaper.
• High water purity. In order to avoid unwanted reactions and preserve system com-
ponents, the process has to be run with extremely purified water. The DTU system is
run with distilled water. This increases the operational costs of the machine because 1)
it requires human supervision to ensure suﬀicient water reserves are available and 2) it
requires a source of distilled water, which is relatively expensive.
• Venting. Instead of being collected and pressurized, the hydrogen is vented out in the
atmosphere, to guarantee the safety of students and staff. The venting process works by
simply routing the H2 and the O2-water mix out of the building through stainless steel
pipes. While the H2 pipe carries highly pressurized ultra-dry gas, the O2 is not 100%
dry, leading to condensate formations that need to be cleaned after operation. This adds
to the startup and stop costs of operating the machine, as it takes around 15-30 minutes
to clean the machine after use. Such actions are supposedly required in the operation of
any electrolyzer and accurate O&M costs have to account for them.
• Temperature. The operating temperature of the electrolyte is 55°C. Operation below
this temperature leads to low conversion eﬀiciency. The implication for a market-aware
operation is that the machine needs some time to heat up in a preheat-phase before it
can be dispatched fully. During the preheat phase it does not produce hydrogen but
nevertheless consumes a small amount of power.

2.3.4
Preheat requirements
The operating temperature of the electrolyte in each module has to reach 55°C before nominal
operation can start. Temperatures below this value lead to increase in ineﬀiciency. Before
operation, therefore, a preheat period is necessary where the machine produces no hydrogen
but draws power to supply its heaters. In order to model this behavior correctly, experiments
were conducted to determine the required pre-heat time and the energy consumption during
this phase. Fig. 25 illustrates how experimental data was used to find the parameters of the
Newtonian heating equation:
T(t) = T0 + (Tinitial −T0) · e−k·t
(12)
Where T is the electrolyte temperature, T0 is the ambient temperature and k is the heating
exponent. A model-data fit resulted in k = 0.019. It is important to note that the temperature
does not actually reach the required 55 °C during the preheating phase; rather its increase
slows down significantly due to larger losses due at higher electrolyte-ambient temperature
differences. Even though 55 °C would be achieved eventually, this will take several additional
hours, thereby severely limiting the time span for operations. Therefore 45°C was used as a
quasi-optimal target for preheating, as the electrolyzer heats fast enough from 45 to 55°C when
a production start command is given.
Ultimately, the reason why the temperature model is needed in is to calculate the time intervals
the dispatch model has to reserve for preparing the electrolyte. These can be easily calculated

2.3.5
Hydrogen production rate
The hydrogen production rate of 1 module is 509 NL/h at maximum power. The optimal
dispatch model, described in Sec. 3.4, relies on accurate mapping of input power to hydrogen
production in order to estimate the H2 quantities produced and with them the projected profits.
The problem is that the production rate of the system is a non-linear function of the input power,
especially during ramp up and ramp-down. Therefore, to model the flow rates accurately, a
first degree polynomial was used, as suggested by [26]:
F(p) = ap2 + bp + c [NL/h]
(14)
where the parameters were obtained with a model-to-data fit function as shown in Fig. 26.
They are: a = -12.7295, b = 248.2614, c = -24.7059.
The advantage of this model is that it is accurate but more importantly, it is simple enough
to be implemented in a mathematical optimization framework as in Sec. 3.4. With it the
solver optimizes over about 4800 decision variables and even though non-linear, it performs
fast enough to run long back-tests. Any increase in flow model complexity leads to marginal
gains in accuracy but substantial increases in computational time.

3
Optimal Dispatch
This chapter explains the rationale and strategy implemented in the control logic of the battery
and electrolyzer. It presents the sources of revenue, the structure of the optimization algorithms
deployed, a detailed explanation of the objectives those pursue and the constraints they are
forced to respect.
3.1
Revenue sources
To maximize its financial outcomes, the electrolyzer and battery systems should aim to gain
every available source of revenue the markets can offer. In DK2 the possible markets are as
follows:
• Day-Ahead (Spot)
The day-ahead market, facilitated by Nordpool is where the battery can perform energy
arbitrage. Bids for the next 24 hours have to be submitted before 12:00 CET on the day
before delivery. A battery can submit both generation and demand bids.
• Fast Frequency Reserve (FFR)
This service provides grid support within less than a second in the event of a disturbance
such as generator outage or transmission line failure. FFR is designed to cover situations
where the system inertia is low, a scenario which will occur more often with decreasing
number of synchronous machines in the system. FFR is an upward service, implying
that the battery must discharge its power into the grid, in order to participate. FFR is
activated automatically by special control equipment, that must monitor the frequency
with a measurement resolution better than 10 mHz. FFR is activated when the frequency
drops to either 49.7, 49.6 or 49.5 Hz. The asset operator can chose which threshold to
operate with, depending on asset capability: if an operator chooses 49.7 Hz, the asset
must activate within 1.3 seconds of the frequency crossing the threshold. For 49.5 Hz,
the same is 0.7 seconds. FFR has to be provided for at least 5 seconds until most of
the FCR-D reserves have been activated. FFR is procured at a daily auction where per-
hour bids are submitted before 15:00 on the day before operation. The accepted capacity
reservation bids are remunerated at the marginal price. The energy used to provide FFR
is not remunerated explicitly. The operators must instead ensure they have enough energy
to deliver full power when FFR is activated [8].
• Frequency-controlled disturbance reserve, upward regulation (FCR-D-U)
FCRD is used to restore support the frequency after the outage of a major generation unit
or transmission line. FCRD is activated automatically if the frequency enters the 49.5
- 49.9 Hz range. The response has to be delivered within 2.5 seconds. The requirement
for batteries is that they can provide full power for 20 minutes after activation. FCRD
capacity is procured at a daily auction, and all successful bids are paid at the highest
marginal price. FCR-D energy is not explicitly compensated but is instead procured at
the ordinary imbalance price [8].
• Frequency-controlled disturbance reserve, downward regulation (FCR-D-D)
This service is meant to decrease the frequency back to 50 Hz in the event of sudden
frequency increase to the range 50.10 - 50.50 Hz, for example due to loss of major demand
as a result of a transmission line outage. A battery providing this service has to begin
consuming energy from the grid at power specified in the accepted capacity bid for at
least 20 minutes. FCR-D energy is not explicitly compensated but is instead procured at
the ordinary imbalance price[8].
38
==PAGE:39==
• Frequency-controlled normal operation reserve, (FCR-N)
FCRN aims to keep the frequency as closely to 50 Hz as possible. It is meant to balance
production and consumption wrt.
to generation units plan deviations and deviating
demand. Therefore FCRN offers both upward and downward linear regulation within the
49.9 - 50.1 Hz frequency range. For batteries the regulator specifies an energy storage
requirement of at least 1 hour for long-lasting frequency deviations, i.e.
a full-power
discharge must be possible for 1 hour in order to participate. The FCRN power availability
bids are remunerated at the marginal price. The energy injected or consumed for the
service is remunerated at the balancing price [8].
• Automatic frequency restoration reserve, or secondary reserve, (aFRR)
After the primary reserve (FFR, FCRD, N) has stabilized the frequency, the secondary
automatic frequency restoration reserve has to step in to push the frequency back to
nominal. The second objective of aFRR is to balance any schedule deviations on the
inter-connectors. A battery providing aFRR must be able to respond to a signal sent
from Energinet within 5 minutes. An accepted bid is remunerated at the price of the
highest accepted bid for the bidding area. The energy provided is remunerated at the
imbalance price.[8]
• Manual frequency restoration reserve, (mFRR)
Manual reserve is an upward and downward regulation reserve managed by Energinet’s
control center, activated by direct orders to mFRR suppliers to help balance the grid. This
reserve serves to alleviate pressure on aFRR and frequency-controlled normal operation
reserves during minor imbalances and to maintain balance in the event of outages or
operational constraints impacting generation and interconnections. The minimum bid
size for this market is 1 WM. Full activation of reserves must occur no later than 12.5
minutes after signal reception. [8]

3.3
BESS multi-market optimization algorithm
The following presents a mathematical framework for optimal bid placement across the markets
outlined in Sec. 3.1. The model is built to serve 2 purposes in the context of this work: 1)
to provide optimal setpoints to the battery in order to assess its physical performance and
2) to be used as a tool for back-testing, assuming perfect hindsight of market prices, and
determine overall strategy merit and theoretical maximum financial performance for such an
asset. The model is a combination of several publications papers on multi-market optimization
algorithms, tailored specifically to the PLDK battery asset. It is worth mentioning that the
model is ultimately not suited for real-time market trading, because it relies on perfect-hindsight
prices, instead of forecasts, does not account for balancing cost due to plan deviation errors.
The algorithm can, however, accept forecasts instead of perfect-hindsight prices and in that
case would be able to account for forecast-error related losses. Forecasting, however, is out of
the scope of this work. The following is a mathematical formalization of a model deployed in
Python and solved with Gurobi:

46

• 100% bid acceptance. It is assumed that all submitted capacity and energy bids are
accepted. In reality, this is not necessarily true, especially considering that the reserve
markets in Denmark are relatively shallow, as shown by the Energinet-estimated volumes
in Table 10. This implies that in reality the bids are accepted with some probability
that depends on prevailing market condition, competitive landscape and the portion of
renewables in the electricity mix. This effectively means that the revenues projected by
this model represent a theoretical maximum, rather than a realistic estimate.
• Price-taker. It is assumed that the battery is always a price taker and its bidding has
no bearing on the market prices. This is plausible, given the small size of this asset, but it
will not necessarily be true for a fleet of such batteries, such as in an energy community.
• Perfect forecasting. The model is fed historical prices, instead of forecasts, thereby
assuming perfect foresight. Forecasts were not modeled in the scope of this thesis, due to
the complexity of the topic.
• Fulfillment of market entry requirements. As seen in Table 10, the minimum bid
sizes for all markets are higher than what the PLDK battery can provide. It is therefore
assumed, that the battery can participate in markets with minimal bid requirement up
to 0.5 MW.

3.4
Electrolyzer multi-market optimization algorithm
This chapter explains the mathematical modeling behind the multi-market dispatch program
built for the Enapter electrolyzer in PLDK. It takes into account the physically determined and
tested machine parameters and operational capabilities and is generally suited for proposing
market-dispatch schedules or test revenue potential in market simulators and back testing. For
operational constraint reasons explained in Sec. 3.1, the model works in the Spot, FFR, FCR-
D-U and FCRN markets with the objective to maximize profits from hydrogen production and
capacity reserve bids. It uses ideas from optimization models developed by [17], [26], [16], [15],
adds the reserve markets and adapts multiple constraints and parameters that are unique to
the PLDK system.
3.4.1
Parameters and inputs
• ∆t: Duration of each time step. The model can do optimizations for 1 hour and 15 min
granularities. The 15 min is chosen for default as finer time steps allow for more precise
preheat-duration calculations as will be explained later.
• T: Total number of time steps in the optimization horizon. This is determined after
resampling prices based on the chosen granularity. Normally T = 96 periods, for a 24
hour horizon.
• λspot: Spot electricity prices.
• λFFR: FFR prices.
• λFCRDU: FCR-D, up-regulation prices.
• λIMB: Imbalance prices.
• µFFR: Activation factor for FFR. The factor represents a % of the committed reserve
capacity that is converted to energy due to the activation of the reserves. If the reserve
is activated for FFR, the machine will reduce its power and will not produce hydrogen at
the initial rate. If these reductions are summed over the optimization period, this would
represent the energy not used to produce hydrogen and can be statistically determined
as a fraction of the expected energy consumption.
• µFCRDU: Activation factor for FCR-D Up, analogous to FFR.
• µFCRN_UP: Activation factor for FCR-N Up.
• µFCRN_DN: Activation factor for FCR-N Down
• λH2: Hydrogen price, at which the produced quantities can be sold.
• κstart: Cost of starting an electrolyzer module. Estimate of costs of dispatching a tech-
nician to prepare the system for operation: opening the vent lines, turning on the power
supplies, ensuring adequate electrolyte and water levels, ensuring safety by checking for
alarms and problems, etc. Starting the machine requires following a specific checklist and
this brings implicit costs with it, which are summed up in this parameter.
• κstop: Cost of shutting down an electrolyzer module, analogous to the startup costs.
• κrun: Cost of running the device other than electricity: supervision, maintenance, elec-
trolyte, distilled water etc.
52
==PAGE:53==
• Θinit: Initial electrolyte temperature. This parameter is used to estimate how much time
it would take the system to heat up to nominal temperature from its starting temperature.
• τ warmup: Number of optimization periods required for the electrolyzer to warm up.
τ warmup is calculated according to Eq. 63
• P mod,max: Maximum power consumption per electrolyzer module.
• P mod,min: Minimum power consumption per electrolyzer module for active operation.
The Enapter modules are not allowed to reduce their power below 60% nominal without
shutting down completely.
• P preheat: Preheat power consumption per electrolyzer module. The electrolyzer modules
consume electricity in preheat mode, without producing hydrogen. Therefore a separate
parameter is used to account for this.
• N: Total number of electrolyzer modules. This parameter can be used to adjust the
number of available / needed electrolyzer modules. Default is 3.
• The hydrogen flow rate F (in NL/h) as a function of power P (in kW) is modeled as a
quadratic function F = α2 · P 2 + α1 · P + α0.
– α2: Coeﬀicient for the P 2 term.
– α1: Coeﬀicient for the P term.
– α0: Constant offset for the flow rate.

4.6
Back-test-results
Using the inputs described so far in the section, the results of the back-tests are presented both
for the electrolyzer and the battery, aiming to give an idea of what their financial performance
will be given multi market-operation and find which operational strategy is best for the assets.
The following two general cases are considered:
• Battery cycling limits tests. A common strategy for running a battery in electricity
markets is to limit the daily cycles to a specific value in order to prevent premature
aging. Limiting the cycles is the lever available to the operator for controlling the balance
between aging and profitability. If the parameter is set too low, this can lead to many
opportunities being lost. If it is too high, the battery may reach EOL too quickly, missing
opportunities in the future. The simulations shown hereafter attempt to find an optimal
cycling limit that balances degradation and profitability.
• Electrolyzer profitability tests. It is interesting to see how electrolyzers do financially
in a multi-market setting with various hydrogen prices.

5
Conclusion
This chapter summarizes the key findings of this thesis, discusses its contributions to the
field, outlines practical implications, acknowledges limitations, and suggests avenues for future
research.
5.1
Summary of Key Findings
This thesis explored the extent to which a small battery and electrolyzer could participate in
multiple electricity markets to maximize revenues for small investors such as homeowners. It
aimed not to be a mere datasheet-based theoretical investigation but to use models specifically
designed for real physical systems situated in PLDK.
• Assessment of Physical Capabilities (Objective 1): The detailed analysis in Chap-
ter 2 revealed that the PLDK battery and electrolyzer possess specific technical charac-
teristics that qualify them for participation in a limited number of electricity markets.
Specifically for the battery, the physical experiments revealed that the capabilities the
system can offer to the market are much less than what the nameplate capabilities stated
by the manufacturer are and that a market model that uses data-sheets only could vastly
overestimate results. For the electrolyzer it was shown that it has a different ramp up and
down rates for power setpoint change, non-linear hydrogen flow rate dynamics and signif-
icant preheat requirements. Overall, this assessment provided the necessary foundational
data for realistic model parameterization.
• Identification of Revenue Streams and Market Qualification (Objective 2):
Chapter 3 identified the spot, FFR, FCRD, FCRN markets as viable revenue sources
for the battery. For the electrolyzer these are spot, FFR, FCRD-Up (with FCRD-Dn,
being unavailable), and FCRN. The experimental performance of the PLDK assets shows
that they could plausibly meet the technical criteria for participation in these markets,
particularly thanks to their their fast response capabilities.
• Development of Multi-Market Bidding Model for Battery and Electrolyzer
(Objective 3 and 4): The developed optimization models for the PLDK assets, detailed
in Chapter 3, demonstrated their ability to maximize profits by finding complex dispatch
patterns and exploiting the best opportunities across markets.
• Estimation of Theoretical Maximum Financial Performance (Objective 5):
The back-testing on historical data in Chapter 5 provided an estimate of the assets’
theoretical maximum financial performance. The results for the battery indicate that
when participating in many reserve markets, increasing cycling limits does not improve
profitability enough to justify a the loss of State of Health related to more frequent
cycling. The results for the electrolyzer demonstrated a non-linear relationship between
profitability and hydrogen price, as even slightly higher prices allow the machine to run
more and bid its capacity to the reserve markets.
5.2
Limitations and Future Work
While this thesis provides valuable insights, it is important to acknowledge certain limitations
that could serve as avenues for future research:
• Perfect-Hindsight Back-testing: The back-testing assumes perfect foresight of prices
and activation factors.
Future work could incorporate forecasting uncertainties and
74
==PAGE:75==
stochastic optimization techniques to provide a more realistic estimate of achievable rev-
enues.
• Market Impact and Cannibalization: While acknowledged conceptually, the models
did not explicitly quantify the impact of large-scale BESS/electrolyzer deployment on
market prices (i.e., the cannibalization effect). Future research could integrate game-
theoretic approaches or agent-based modeling to simulate these market interactions.
• Degradation Model Refinement: The degradation models, while realistic, could be
further refined with more extensive long-term operational data and advanced machine
learning techniques to capture more complex aging phenomena.
• Integration with Renewable Generation: This thesis focused on market participa-
tion of the assets. Future work could integrate the optimization with co-located renewable
generation (e.g., rooftop PV) to optimize self-consumption alongside market participa-
tion.
5.3
Concluding Remarks
The transition to a sustainable energy system is an economic as much as an environmental
imperative.
This thesis has demonstrated that distributed battery energy storage systems
and electrolyzers, when optimally dispatched and integrated into electricity markets, could be
compelling investment opportunities. By transforming passive consumers into active, flexible
participants, these technologies could pave the way for a more resilient, eﬀicient, and ultimately,
a more sustainable energy future.

Hydrogen to energy conversion guide
This guide outlines the calculation of energy content for hydrogen, primarily focusing on gaseous
hydrogen under specified conditions.
Key hydrogen energy values
• Lower heating value (LHV): 120 MJ/kg = 33.3 kWh/kg. The LHV represents the
amount of heat released when a fuel is completely burned, but the water produced during
combustion remains in its vapor (gaseous) state and is not condensed. This means the
latent heat of vaporization of this water is not recovered and is considered lost with the
exhaust gases [21].
• Higher heating value (HHV): 142 MJ/kg = 39.4 kWh/kg. The HHV represents the
total amount of heat released when a fuel is completely burned, and all the water produced
during combustion (from hydrogen in the fuel) is condensed back into its liquid state. This
means that the latent heat of vaporization of the water vapor is recovered as useful energy
[21].
Conversion steps for gaseous hydrogen
To convert a given volume of gaseous hydrogen to its energy content:
1. Determine hydrogen density (ρ): The density of hydrogen gas varies significantly
with temperature (T) and pressure (P). Common reference conditions include:
• Standard temperature and pressure (STP): T = 0◦C (273.15 K), P = 1 atm
(101.325 kPa). At STP, ρH2 ≈0.08988 kg/m3 (or 0.08988 g/L).
• Normal temperature and pressure (NTP): T = 20◦C (293.15 K), P = 1 atm
(101.325 kPa). At NTP, ρH2 ≈0.08375 kg/m3 (or 0.08375 g/L).
2. Calculate mass (m): Multiply the given volume (V ) by the determined density:
m = V × ρ
3. Calculate energy content (E): Multiply the calculated mass by the desired heating
value (LHV or HHV):
E = m × Heating Value
Example calculation (1 Liter hydrogen at STP, using LHV)
• Density (ρ): 0.08988 g/L
• Mass (m): 1 L × 0.08988 g/L = 0.08988 g = 0.00008988 kg
• Energy (E): 0.00008988 kg × 120 MJ/kg ≈0.0108 MJ (or 0.003 kWh)
