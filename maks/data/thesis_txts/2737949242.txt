2 Abstract 
In line with the objective of decarbonizing the heating and cooling sectors supported by the European 
Union, district heating and cooling networks are recognized as one of the key strategies for a “collective 
green conversion” [1]. However, the current systems (3GDH) are adapted to an intensive and 
centralized fossil fuel heat production and to the supply of older buildings with a high heat demand.  
With the current decrease in space heating demand from buildings and the necessary energy 
production shift to decentralized, intermittent, and renewable heat sources, the district heating and 
cooling systems must evolve to accompany these improvements. The double-loop concept is an 
innovative district heating architecture that can answer most of these challenges and be more adapted 
to future operations. The proposed design is applied to a case study in Gentofte and modelled in two 
software programs, LeanHeat and Dymola. Even if the two programs have different modelling 
methods, the simulations give similar and comparable results. The double loop system can integrate 
multiple heat sources in the network without any capacity limitations and can handle the intermittence 
of renewables. It also secures a temperature of 15-20°C in the return lines during no-load periods by 
removing the use of by-pass valves for hot flow circulation. As no warm flow contaminates the return 
line, the double loop infrastructure could also supply a cooling demand in summer, reducing the costs 
and resource use compared to using two different solutions for heating and cooling.

4 Introduction 
Heating and cooling demands constitute about half of the European energy consumption, and 78% of 
which come from fossil fuels [2]. The EU strategy on heating and cooling [2] states that heating and 
cooling systems should be more efficient and sustainable, both for households and industries. It 
encourages an increased use of district heating and cooling networks, that present many advantages 
in the realization of this goal. Indeed, according to Hansen et al. [1], as the DH networks’ heat 
production can be centralized, it increases the system’s efficiency in the use of fuels and reduces the 
total installed capacity. It also allows a more efficient storage of heat (with large-scale heat storage) 
and the use of cheaper fuels and available waste heat. Therefore, a “collective green conversion”[1] is 
facilitated in cities where these networks are implemented. However, as stated in [2], the lack of 
district heating and cooling networks hinders waste heat and waste cold use in the EU even though 
they could be further deployed. The DH market penetration in the EU is today only around 10% 
whereas Persson and Werner [3] estimate that it would be economically feasible to increase it to about 
60% in most European cities.  
The current district heating technological development is transitioning from the third generation to 
the fourth generation (4GDH). As detailed in [4] and [5], the first generation used high-temperature 
sources and steam as distribution media but is now rarely used: the high temperatures led to 
considerable heat losses, and there were issues with steam outbursts injuring or killing pedestrians. 
The second generation used pressurized water as a heat carrier, with temperatures above 100°C. The 
third generation still uses pressurized water as a heat carrier but with supply temperatures below 
100°C and it is the type of district heating systems mostly used nowadays. It is adapted to an extensive 
use of fossil fuels and buildings with high energy demands [6]. However, as set forth in [4], future 
district heating networks should be designed for a future energy system where the use of renewable 
energies and waste heat is prioritized and where the energy consumption of the buildings is reduced 
by efficiency measures as demanded by [2] or the European Parliament in [7]. Therefore, a fourth 
generation of district heating systems should take over and answer the current challenges faced by 
the third-generation networks [4]. 
4.1 Arising challenges for district heating networks 
Many challenges are posed to the current generation of district heating networks and already question 
its competitivity and capability to adapt to future energy systems [8] [1] [6]. Among them, the general 
switch from centralized to dispatched heat sources, a necessary integration to smart energy systems, 
a decrease of the heat demand densities and the need for a substantial heat losses reduction. 
4.1.1 Heat recovery and decentralized heat sources 
As pointed out in [6], the type of fuel or heat production technologies used for district heating greatly 
influence the resulting DH energy price. The final cost of district heating energy can be split into two 
parts: the heat generation costs (about 80%) and the heat distribution costs (about 20%) [3]. Compared 
to individual solutions, the distribution costs are additional costs embedded in DH systems resulting 
from the high infrastructure capital costs, the coverage of heat and pressure losses, and the service 
and maintenance costs. For district heating to be economically competitive compared to individual 
solutions, a strong reduction in the heat generation cost must balance these extra expenses. Therefore, 
the cheapest sources of energy, as waste, renewable, or low-temperature heat sources, should be 
used and optimized, as underlined in [9] and [4]. Following the current European aim of 
==PAGE:7==
decarbonization [2], an important challenge will come from the general switch from the heat being 
produced by centralized fossil fuels and nuclear plants to the use of spread renewables and waste heat 
sources [4]. The district heating networks must adapt to the geographical scattering of the heat sources 
and increase their flexibility to ensure a suitable heat generation. 
Current district heating networks are generally tree-shaped (or branched), with one supply line and 
one return line stemming from a centralized heat plant and ramifying towards the multiple end-users 
[10][11]. However, as pointed out by Hassine et al. in [12] and Brand et al. in [13], the hydraulics of 
branched networks can be problematic for the integration of decentralized energy sources. As the hot 
water flow only goes in one direction (from the core plant to the furthest end-users), connecting 
decentralized sources situated at the end of branches is challenging. Without changing the flow 
direction, these sources only have a little pressure available and can only provide their energy to the 
few end-users situated “after them” in the branch. This greatly limits the integration of waste or 
renewable heat sources that are usually scattered in the area and starts raising the need for a new 
network geometry. 
4.1.2 Joining a smart energy system 
To optimize the general efficiency of the energy system, the European Commission in [1] recommends 
coupling thermal networks with other energy grids like electric, gas, or transportation systems. Lund 
et al. in [4] also point out the necessity and challenge of identifying synergies between thermal, 
electricity, and gas grids to achieve optimal operation. District heating systems should help reduce the 
dependence on biomass and help integrate renewable sources that will provide large amounts of 
energy at fluctuating power and time. Indeed, they can intervene in the electricity regulating power 
market with CHPs, heat pumps, and efficient large-scale thermal storage to help balance the system 
when high levels of renewables are integrated, as suggested by the Heat Roadmap Europe 4 [14]. 
4.1.3 Adapting to lower heat demand densities 
The European Parliament and Commission both indicate in [2] and [7] that the building stock should 
be renewed and renovated with more efficient and sustainable solutions to provide heating and 
cooling to their occupants. Therefore, due to new regulations such as the BR18 in Denmark [15], 
buildings’ space heating consumption is expected to drastically decrease in the future years [16]. This 
will result in a generally lower heat demand density in cities that will challenge the district heating 
competitiveness as studied in [6] and [1]. Lund et al. in [4] suggest coordinating the improvements in 
the building sector with the ones in the distribution networks to reach a maximal general efficiency. 
As new and renovated buildings can accept lower operating temperatures for space heating and 
cooling systems, the network temperatures should follow up and be reduced accordingly. However, 
thermal comfort in old, non-renovated buildings should still be ensured with these new operating 
temperatures.  
4.1.4 Reducing heat losses 
High heat losses occur in the distribution system of most district heating networks, challenging its 
competitivity in low heat density areas [6] where they can amount to 20-30% of the heat 
production [9]. To tackle this issue, Lund et al. state in [4] that the average operating temperatures of 
future networks should be around 55/25°C for supply and return lines to shrink the temperature 
difference between the water flow and the ground.  
==PAGE:8==
Lund et al. also underline in [4] the importance of eliminating the use of a by-pass flow between the 
supply and return line during no-load periods as it generates considerable heat losses. This 
phenomenon, widely described in [17] and [18], comes from the absence of space heating demand in 
summer: as only the domestic hot water demand needs to be covered, only a low hot water volume 
flow rate would be needed in the pipes. However, the heat losses occurring in the distribution network 
would degrade extensively the temperature of the low supply flow that would therefore not be 
suitable for DHW use. The common solution is to always circulate a sufficient flow of warm enough 
water in the pipes to be able to supply DHW at the substation whenever needed. This flow is then 
either used for maintaining the circulation system in larger buildings or sent directly in the return line 
via a by-pass valve. In both cases, the return temperature is contaminated with the supply flow warm 
temperatures. This issue is likely to get worse: as buildings become more insulated, the outdoor 
temperature level at which space heating would be needed in the houses will increase and the so-
called “summer season”, with mainly recirculation, will lengthen [17]. 
5 Concept definition – Double loop networks 
The current district heating technologies face challenges that shed light on the need for a new DH 
generation, the fourth one, but also for innovative network configuration and hydraulics. The proposed 
Double-loop network may be more suitable for future energy systems and can provide both heating 
and cooling energy. 
5.1.1 General description 
As described in [19] and presented Figure 1, double-loop networks consist of two main water loops, 
for warm and cool water, connecting clusters of end-users and heat sources. The main loop is the 
distribution network, which is responsible for collecting and distributing the energy, whether it is for 
heating or cooling. These two loops are hydraulically separated: conversely to tree-shaped networks, 
the supply and return lines have their own pump, the water rotates in the same direction and the lines 
do not exchange water flows other than at the plant or at the end-user substations. The clusters of 
buildings are also hydraulically separated from the main loops as they have their own supply and return 
pump responsible for the water distribution in the area. This type of DH network eliminates the 
contamination of the return temperature during no-load periods: instead of being bypassed in the 
return line, the circulation water in the cluster supply line can be reinjected in the main supply loop 
(this operation is further described in 5.1.2). To align with the objectives of the fourth-generation 
district heating, this kind of network can be operated with temperatures of around 55/25°C or lower, 
that can be increased up to 70°C during exceptionally cold periods.  
==PAGE:9==
5.1.2 No-load periods operation 
In traditional tree networks, when there is no hot water consumption (in summer for example), if the 
water stays still in the network pipes, it will cool down towards the ambient temperature. Then, if an 
end-user needs some hot water after a long period without consumption, they will have to wait until 
all the network pipes are emptied of their cooled-down water and heated back up before getting actual 
warm flow. This is why, in a traditional operation, all the supply pipes branching to a user are kept 
warm by circulating a small hot water flow, to ensure that the waiting time at the end-user is no more 
than 10 to 15 seconds. However, this flow is not consumed and ends up being by-passed into the return 
line, drastically increasing its temperature. 
In a double loop network, instead of using a by-pass to the return lines, the warm circulation flow is 
simply rotating in the main supply loop and in the cluster supply lines. However, in the absence of 
consumption, the service pipes connecting the end-users are not kept warm by the circulating flow, to 
avoid the by-pass. Therefore, to provide the required hot water comfort to a user whose service pipes 
were let to cool down due to his absence of consumption, these pipes must be small enough to be 
emptied quickly. Some houses are close enough to the main loops and can be directly connected to 
them with a simple service pipe. However, some consumers may be too far from the main loops for a 
direct pipe connection to be emptied quickly. They can be gathered into clusters, that are secondary 
Figure 1: Overview of a double-loop district heating network, from [19]. 
==PAGE:10==
loop where warm water also circulates and is sent back to the main loop if it has not been consumed 
by the users. Cluster pipes being smaller than the main loop ones, they can run into neighbourhoods, 
getting closer to the far-away end-users and reducing the necessary length for their service pipes.  
5.1.3 Substation design 
A new end-user’s substation was investigated as presented Figure 2. It can extract both heating and 
cooling energy from the network and send the DHW circulation flow back into the supply line instead 
of the return one. 
There are four different heat exchangers in the substation, from top to bottom: for space heating, for 
DHW circulation, for DHW tapping and for space cooling. On the building side, the water is circulated 
through the heat exchangers thanks to pumps ensuring that the right mass flow rate is sent through 
the systems. On the district heating side, there are two different cases: Supply/Return connections  
and Supply/Supply or Return/Return connections. In the case of a S/R connection (for space heating 
or DHW), the circulation of the water happens thanks to the differential pressure between the supply 
and return lines of the network (at least 1 bar at the last end-user). For each path, the mass flow rate 
is controlled by a valve to ensure the right supply temperature in the building side. In the case of an 
S/S or R/R connection (DHW circulation or space cooling), the water is circulated by a pump controlled 
by the supply temperature on the building side. It also ensures that the flow has enough pressure to 
be re-injected into the same network line it was taken from. 
The space cooling demand occurs in summer when the space heating systems are turned off and there 
is a low flow demand for heat in the network. The DHW demand being present  all over the year (both 
for tapping and DHW circulation), the network supply lines should stay warm enough to ensure correct 
temperatures at the end-users, i.e. above 55°C. The supply flow for cooling should come from the 
coldest line (the return one in a heating configuration), that must be kept at suitable temperatures 
such as 7-12°C. This can easily be ensured when only the tapping return flow is sent to the cold line. 
Figure 2: End-user's substation schematics. 
==PAGE:11==
To avoid cooling down the main supply line, the return flow from the space cooling is directed back to 
the return line, slightly increasing its global temperature. 
However, maintaining the network return line cold enough for cooling supply during no heat loads 
periods is challenging. For example, in large commercial buildings, where hot water draw-offs rarely 
occur, a small warm water flow is usually circulated all the time in the building’s DHW system to keep 
the building pipes warm, and therefore provide hot water with a reasonable delay and comply with 
hygiene regulations. According to DS 439 regulations [20], drinkable hot water cannot be colder than 
50°C at any point in the system, to prevent the growth of legionella. Therefore, the DHW circulation 
flow must be heated at 55°C at the substation and with the heat losses to the surroundings, the flow 
temperature drops by a few degrees in the system. However, in the substation heat exchanger, the 
return temperature to the district heating system cannot be lower than the return temperature from 
the circulation system, i.e. 50°C. As in [21], the district heating flow is usually cooled down by about 
10°C in a DHW circulation setting, leading to high return temperatures, especially when there is no 
space heating demand.  
In an area mainly composed of commercial buildings, where the circulation is usually responsible for 
85% of the DHW consumption [22], the return flow from the circulation supply should be handled 
carefully to avoid contaminating the return temperature of the whole network. In this study and with 
the new substation design, the circulation energy supply would be provided through a smaller, 
separated heat exchanger. As visible Figure 2, its return flow, instead of being sent to the return line 
of the network, would be redirected back to the supply line, slightly decreasing its global temperature. 
5.1.4 Possible answers to challenges 
The proposed double loop heating and cooling networks (DLHC) can help tackle some key challenges 
in the district heating industry (section 4.1) as discussed in [19].  
Firstly, combining heating and cooling networks in the same infrastructure would greatly reduce the 
installation cost and increase the competitivity of DHC systems compared to individual solutions. 
Having a single network already functioning in synergy would also ease the integration of the thermal 
grid to the other ones, as mentioned in [4].  
Secondly, any hot water flow can be sent from one end of the network to another in both ways, due 
to the loop geometry. Therefore, it is possible and easier to integrate decentralized heat sources 
(waste heat or renewable) at any point of the main loop without limitation to the share of the demand 
they can cover, while it is quickly restricted in a tree-shaped network. Large scale heat storages can 
also be connected at any point of the loops to handle the intermittence of renewable energy and the 
seasonal mismatch between their heat/cold production and the end-users’ consumption. Also, the 
increased number of pumps in the network (both in the main loop and in the clusters) rise the possible 
pressure drop in the pipes and allows for the use of smaller diameters, reducing the investment cost 
and decreasing the linear heat losses [4].  
Thirdly, as there is no by-pass flow during no-load periods and thus no contamination of the return 
line, the average district heating return temperature can be potentially reduced. This fact, in addition 
to shrinking the heat losses, can increase the integration of renewable energy sources and enhance 
waste heat recovery. According to 4GDH requirements [4], lower operating temperature increase the 
possibilities for viable waste heat recovery from industrial processes and geothermal sources, as lower 
grade heat can be used.  Also, low operating temperatures greatly improve the efficiency of heat 
==PAGE:12==
pumps, solar collectors, flue gas condensation processes in boilers, geothermal sources, waste heat 
recovery and thermal storages (higher capacity) [4] [8] [23]. Finally, heat losses represent a waste of 
produced heat that should be covered, and therefore an increase in the operation costs, as underlined 
in [18]. The elimination of the summer by-pass flow and the reduction of the operating temperatures, 
greatly reducing the heat losses, will also result in a reduction of the operating costs and improve the 
competitivity of this district heating network, especially in low-density areas [19] [8]. 
5.2 Purpose of the work 
Current district heating networks are predominantly tree-shaped while new distribution network 
designs are still confined to research or small-scale application [19][23].  
Therefore, the research objective of this Master thesis is to investigate an alternative network design 
and operation: double loop district heating and cooling networks. This analysis will be performed 
through a case study and by modelling the hydraulics of the network with two different software 
programs: LeanHeat Network [24] and Dymola [25]. The first one is a commercially available user-
oriented software program used for analysing the hydraulics of common DH network shapes 
(branched), while the latter is of more general use, for describing the dynamic behaviour of different 
physical systems in the engineering field and uses the open-source coding language Modelica. The 
capabilities and differences between the two software programs will be investigated and assessed in 
this work. 
Therefore, the following learning/research objectives will be considered:  
• 
Study the hydraulics and operations of double loop district heating network, 
• 
Assess the capabilities of the new network design in answering the current challenges 
addressed to the 3GDH, 
• 
Evaluate the capacities, limitations and differences of LeanHeat and Dymola in modelling the 
new hydraulics of the proposed network concept. 
5.3 Novelty of the work 
The novelty of this study is to investigate an innovative network design and operation that can ease 
the transition towards 4GDH. The investigation will address the main technical challenges associated 
to the hydraulics of double loop district heating networks and their suitability for distributing heating 
and cooling to their customers.

As developed in part 5.1.2, the service pipes connecting the end-users to the network must be small 
enough to be emptied and warmed up quickly after a long period of no-consumption. If a direct

can be gathered into secondary loops, the clusters, connected to the main loops.

Three different pipe diameters were tested in the network, while considering the resulting necessary

the pressure requirements, because they are less expensive and they lead to less heat losses. For 
correct operation of the end-user’s substations, the pressure difference between the supply and return

than the use of steel pipes, reducing the pipes-related costs by up to 40% [26], [27], [28]. However, 
they can withstand less pressure than steel pipes and the network must be designed accordingly. The 
pressure limit for plastic pipes is around 10 bars but according to regulation, there should be a 20-30% 
margin between the nominal pressure in operation and the pipe limit [27]. Therefore, the nominal 
pressure in the network should stay below 8 to 8.5 bars. Finally, for a correct operation of the pumps, 
their minimal inlet pressure should be kept above 2 bar. For each diameter trial, the pump head was 
adjusted to fit the aforementioned conditions and so that a similar mass flow rate was circulated in 
the main loops.

calculated as follows:

However, as explained in the double loop concept in part 5.1.2, some water is always circling in the 
main loops to ensures a high enough supply temperature everywhere in the network without 
contaminating the return line. Also, due to the new substation behaviour, the DH supply water used 
for the DHW circulation is redirected to the supply line because of its low temperature drop. This 
operation, in addition to the heat losses from the pipes, can greatly decrease the temperature in the 
main loop, especially in summer when there is little consumption and therefore a small flow running 
in the pipes. The minimum mass flow rate in the loops should be sufficient to alleviate this effect and 
ensure that the supply temperature is at least 55°C at the entrance of each service pipe.  
Different minimum mass flow rates were tested in the network, regarding the temperature drop at 
the critical user and the resulting pressure drop in the pipes (increasing with the mass flow rate). As 
the optimal circulation mass flow rate changes depending on the part-load, a control of the supply 
pump should be implemented during a dynamic operation. 
6.1.4.4 Cluster’s pipe and pump design 
The main loop pumps are not responsible for the hydraulic operation of the cluster: it must be 
independent and fully covered by the local pumps. They should be able to circulate the water in the 
cluster without relying on the main loops pressure difference, and to redirect a small flow of water 
back to the main supply loop (like in summer operation). As stated before, the pumps also need an 
inlet pressure higher than 2 bar for their correct operation, and they must ensure a 1 bar pressure 
difference between the supply and return lines at the critical end-user of the cluster. To be able to use 
plastic pipes in the cluster, the nominal pressure should also not exceed 8 to 8.5 bars. While respecting 
these requirements and keeping the pressure gradient and fluid velocity in the pipes reasonable, the 
pipe diameters were minimized for each cluster.

6.2 Modelling in LeanHeat  
LeanHeat Network is a commercial software owned by Danfoss and used for traditional district heating 
networks simulations [24]. Built with thermo-hydraulic models, it can both be used in the design phase, 
for a new network or the expansion of an existing one, or for control and optimization of a network’s 
operation. The optimization can be about physical parameters, like pressure- or temperature-wise, but 
also regarding capital or operational costs.  
The network modelling in LeanHeat mainly consisted of assembling the different components 
available. Each element (plants, consumers, nodes, pipes or heaters) has its specific behaviour and 
input parameters and cannot be modified by the user (except for the planned input data). For 
reference, a traditional branched network was built in the software, before trying to implement the 
double-loop model. Described in part 6.2.2, 6.2.3 and 6.2.4, some approximations and adjustments 
had to be made compared to the traditional software operation, in order to mimic the expected 
behaviour of this new model with the available components in LeanHeat.  
6.2.1 Tree network  
A traditional tree network was firstly built for a design situation: if all the consumers need space 
heating and domestic hot water at the same time. It was designed with the same pressure conditions 
as describe before: a pressure level in the pipes below 8 to 8.5 bars for a safe use of plastic pipes, not 
less than 2 bar at each pump inlet for correct operation of the pumps and at least 1 bar of pressure 
drop between the supply and return loops at the entrance of the critical user/cluster. In LeanHeat, one 
plant should be acting as a controlling pump for the whole network and is therefore responsible for 
complying with the pressure requirements.  It would generally be the main producer, if its flow reaches 
all the network branches. Street pipes, smaller branches of a tree-network, were added when the 
conditions for building a cluster were met (as detailed in 6.1.4.1). Finally, the auto-dimensioning option 
of the software was used to size the pipes in the network, among a limited set of commercial pipes. 
6.2.2 Main loops operation 
In LeanHeat, adding a pipe between the two main ends of the tree network to create the main loops 
is not enough for triggering a looping operation: the water still goes into two different directions after 
the main producer. One pipe in the main loop is left empty in the simulation, between the critical end-
user and one of its neighbours, as if there was a cut in the network there. Therefore, the rotation of 
the water in the loops had to be forced in some way. One option would be to add one-way valves in 
one of the pipes where the flow is separating in two, after the main producer, to prevent the branching 
operation. However, this only changes the location of the empty pipe, moving it where the one-way-
valve is placed. It indicates that there is still no flow circling around and that the network is just 
behaving like one long curved branch.  
To force the flow rotation, pumps were placed on the main loops with a high enough pressure head to 
circulate the water all the way along the loop, until the final connection. Once the pump capacity is 
just high enough to make the water circle in the main loop, any additional pressure head of the pump 
will increase the minimum flow in the pipes. As explained in 6.1.4.3, this flow should be designed 
properly to ensure warm enough supply temperatures everywhere in the network. 
However, in LeanHeat, the pressure in the network must be managed by the one of the plants that 
acts as a controlling pump. With an additional pump in the main loop, the two controls disturb each 
==PAGE:17==
other, and the simulation cannot run properly. As the real control of the network must be handled by 
the main heat source in the software, the plant was set to ensure a minimum pressure level in the 
supply and return pipes at the critical point of the network, 𝑃𝑟_𝑚𝑖𝑛 and 𝑃𝑠_𝑚𝑖𝑛, for the correct 
operation of the pumps and the minimal pressure difference between the supply and return loops. 
The pumps were then set to increase the pressure level on the supply and return lines by a fixed value 
(that must be set manually in the simulation). Therefore, the pumps are not in control of the pressure 
in the network, but the pressure profiles still resemble what is expected if the main pump was 
managing itself the pressure level in the main loops. 
6.2.3 Use of the heater element for S/S connection modelling 
The consumers elements in LeanHeat are only defined to draw warm water from the supply line and 
send it back colder to the return line. They only model an S/R connection and cannot be used for S/S 
or R/R connection. In other words, they can’t send back a colder water to the supply line, i.e. 
withdrawing some energy from a line without removing water flow. However, this is the purpose of 
the “heater” element: it designed to heat up or cool down a line without exchanging water with 
another one.  
To mimic the new substation behaviour, that sends the warm water from the DHW circulation back 
into the supply line, a heater element was added to the supply lines after each consumer connection. 
It withdraws the circulation power, 𝑄𝑐𝑖𝑟𝑐,  from the pipe, cooling down the loop flow. The greater the 
minimum flow in the main loop, the smaller the total temperature decrease due to theses power 
withdrawals. 
6.2.4 Cluster operation 
To direct the flow into the cluster, one pump is placed on the supply line at the entrance of the cluster 
and one on the return line at the end. To prevent the flow from going in unwanted directions, one-
way valves are added on the supply lines, at the end of the cluster and of its streets, and on the return 
lines, at the beginning of the streets and of the cluster.  
The hydraulic circulation of a cluster should not rely on the main loop pressure level but only on the 
local pumps. Theoretically, the supply pump should ensure that a small amount of flow is able to 
circulate in the cluster and go back into the main supply loop if no consumption happens. The return 
pump should ensure that the return flow has the right pressure level to enter the main return loop. 
Finally, the pressure difference between the supply and return lines at the critical end-user should be 
at least 1 bar for the substation to operate correctly. 
In the model, the return pump is responsible for ensuring a 1 bar pressure difference at the critical 
end-user of the cluster. The supply pump has a fixed pressure head that must be adjusted manually to 
ensure that a small flow is able to circulate back into the main supply loop and that the pressure of the 
flow at the inlet of the return pump is larger than 2 bar. 
The pipe dimensions were then minimized while staying within the allowed pressure level in plastic 
pipes and reasonable fluid velocity. 
6.3 Modelling in Dymola  
Dymola is a commercial software developed and owned by Dassault Systemes based on the open code 
language Modelica [25]. It is used to model and simulate complex multi-physics systems in many 
==PAGE:18==
engineering fields such as aeronautics, robotics or hydraulics. It contains many libraries of

7 Case Study 
An area of 0.2 km² in the Gladsaxe municipality was used as case study to investigate the double loop

cooling network. The area is presented Figure 6.

8 Results 
Hydraulic simulations were conducted both in LeanHeat and Dymola and the resulting pressure, mass 
flow rates and temperature profiles will be presented in this part.  
As the two software programs simulations were conducted at two different moments of the project, 
the so-called design conditions slightly vary. In the LeanHeat simulations, the first one to be conducted, 
the “design conditions” were considered to be with a heat demand equal to the sum of SH, DHW and 
DHW circulation power designs (see 6.1.1) and with a 55°C necessary supply temperature at the 
substation. The temperature drop at the end-users’ substation was set to 30°C. During the Dymola 
simulations that were conducted later in the thesis, it was found that 55°C on the DH side is not 
sufficient to supply the DHW needs due to the imperfection of the heat exchangers. In addition, the 
simulation “design conditions" were defined similarly to the SH systems design conditions: the 
operation of the network when the outdoor temperature is -12°C outside. In this case, the SH systems 
of the buildings require around 70°C as a supply temperature. Finally, as Dymola runs dynamic 
simulations, the varying tapping profiles of the buildings in the area make the “design” DHW load hard 
to find. Therefore, for the Dymola results, what is called “design situation” is when the outdoor 
temperature is -12°C, and only the SH and DHW circulation are provided for all the buildings of the 
area. 
This difference in design conditions should be erased and clarified in the future but for this analysis, its 
impact on the simulation results is minimal. Indeed, the main important parameter for the hydraulic 
calculations is the temperature decrease as the substations (to calculate the necessary mass flow rate 
from the power needs) and in both models, a 30°C was chosen as an input. In LeanHeat, this was 
directly set in the consumer model, and in Dymola, the modelled SH systems operate with supply and 
return temperatures of 70/40 °C in design conditions. Therefore, the design mass flow rates and 
pressure profiles found in the simulations are similar and comparable even with the difference in the 
“design conditions” consideration.

8.2 The Dymola model 
The Dymola simulations were conducted in design conditions, for the comparison with the LeanHeat 
results, but also dynamically and at different part-loads (including the summer operation). The results 
are presented from the substation model to the global main loop one. 
8.2.1 The supply temperature 
During most of the year, the district heating network is supposed to be operated with a supply 
temperature of about 55°C. However, from the substation simulation results, it was determined that 
using a temperature of 57°C would fit better the DHW supply which should be at 55°C inside the 
building. Also, this supply temperature is not high enough the operate the space heating system during 
the colder days of the year. A common practice in district heating networks is to gradually increase the 
supply temperature when the outside temperature is below a setpoint. Here, the necessary supply 
temperature to operate the buildings at each outside temperature was determined as visible Figure 
18. Therefore, when the outside temperature is -1°C or above, 57°C is used as a supply temperature 
(limited by the DHW supply) but if the weather becomes colder, the supply temperature from the 
district heating will be increased following the equation 𝑇𝑠𝑢𝑝= −1.44 ∗𝑇𝑜𝑢𝑡+ 52.58, with 𝑇𝑠𝑢𝑝 and 
𝑇𝑜𝑢𝑡 in degree Celsius. If the outside temperature is above 16°C, the space heating systems are 
stopped, and the district heating will only provide the DHW need. 
Due to the heat losses in the pipes and the reduction of the supply temperature by the S/S connection 
in the substation, this temperature profile was increased by a few degrees at the network heat sources 
so that the necessary temperatures of Figure 18 are provided at the last end-user.

9 Discussion 
9.1 Results analysis and modelling comparison 
The two software programs are very different in the way they are built and in their interaction with 
the users. LeanHeat Network is a user-oriented software that allows few modifications of the available 
components, but its use is quick to understand as the possibilities are limited. Therefore, the 
simulations are fast, and the program is easy to manipulate and robust to large changes. On the other 
hand, Dymola allows for creating and modifying complex and detailed multi-physics models of 
components. Therefore, even if pre-designed libraries are available, a coding language (Modelica) 
should be learned before being able to correctly use the software, reducing its accessibility. But then, 
more advanced and innovative behaviours can be tested and simulated to reflect better the new 
architecture operation. 
9.1.1 Building and substation behaviour 
9.1.1.1 Difference and similarities in the LeanHeat and Dymola modelling 
The largest difference between the two software programs is the way they can model the consumers. 
In LeanHeat, the consumers are characterized by two of the three following input data: the power 
consumption, the district heating supply mass flow rate to the building and the temperature level of 
the return flow (either with a return temperature or with a temperature difference between the supply 
and return flow). With two of these data, the software can compute the third one (with the formula 
𝑄= 𝑚̇ ∗𝐶𝑝∗Δ𝑇) and the consumer interaction with the network is defined. Additionally, the length 
of the service pipes and their heat loss coefficient can be added to model the heat losses there.  
On the contrary, in Dymola, it is possible to model a more accurate building behaviour with SH systems 
characteristics and DHW dynamic consumption. Therefore, with just the power needed as an input, 
the SH supply and return temperatures, as well as the DH mass flow rate and return temperature, are 
computed. This kind of model results in a more accurate behaviour outside of the design conditions. 
For example, the building’s SH systems are assumed to operate with 70/40°C as supply and return 
temperature in design conditions. In LeanHeat, this can be implemented with the building specific 
power consumption (calculated prior to the simulation with the HDD method as explain in part 6.1.1) 
and a 30 K temperature drop at the consumer. However, if this was verified with the Dymola 
simulations at design situation (with -12°C outside), it becomes less accurate when simulating the 
system at different part loads. A reduction in a building’s SH power demand during a day will be 
accompanied by a change in the system’s supply and return temperatures and the 30 K temperature 
drop will therefore not be relevant anymore. As visible in section 8.2.2, Dymola accounts for that 
change in behaviour, and the temperature drop in the substation varies between 30 K and 20 K 
depending on the outdoor conditions and DH supply temperature. To model this accurately in 
LeanHeat, the temperature drop should be calculated and changed manually for each part-load prior 
to the simulation. This could be feasible for the space heating temperatures at different part loads but 
as shown in part 8.2.2, the DHW consumption also has an impact on the substation return 
temperature, and the extent of this impact varies depending on the balance between the SH and DHW 
==PAGE:46==
respective consumption (or mass flow rate) and their return temperatures: from a decrease of only 2-
3°C when the SH is dominant to 5-7°C in warmer weather as visible Figure 22.  
Another important variable for the correct functioning of the substations is the pressure level and 
pressure difference between the supply and return lines. In LeanHeat, the consumers take water from 
the supply line and put it into the return line at a different temperature, whatever the pressure of 
those lines are. Therefore, it can simulate networks with pressure profiles that would cause 
misfunctioning of substations, such as the pressure level of the return line being higher than the 
pressure level of the supply line at some places in the network, without any warnings or change in 
consumer behaviour. In Dymola however, the mass flow rate in the substation depends on the 
pressure levels of the entrance and exit of the flow and on the pressure drops in the substation, in 
pipes, heat exchangers and valves. Therefore, if the pressure difference is too low between the supply 
and return lines, only a small flow can circulate in the substation, and the demand will not be met on 
the building side. If the pressure level of the return line is higher than the pressure level of the supply 
line, a backward flow will run in the substation and raise an error in the simulation. Therefore, when 
simulating with LeanHeat, the possible pressure profiles must be known beforehand, and a special care 
must be provided to check that none of the buildings are running in unrealistic conditions. 
Finally, the approximation made in LeanHeat to model the S/S connection with the heaters (see part 
6.2.3) showed similar results to the complete modelling of the substation in Dymola. At design 
conditions in the cluster, the temperature drop due to the DHW circulation (and heat losses) was 2.4°C 
and 2.6°C in Dymola and LeanHeat respectively. The cluster circulation flow is almost the same in the 
two software programs and the heat losses are too low to have a real impact on the temperature in 
the cluster (as the length of the pipes is short). Therefore, it can indicate that the heaters elements in 
LeanHeat is a good substitute for a S/S connection at the substation. This is also verified at the main 
loop level with 2.7 °C and 3°C of temperature drop in Dymola and LeanHeat in design condition. 
However, there are more differences between the two models in the main loop operation than in the 
cluster and these results should be treated more cautiously. On one hand, this temperature drop could 
actually be a little higher in LeanHeat due to the heat losses because the model was simulated with a 
supply temperature of 59°C and not 72 °C as in Dymola and the main loop length is quite large (more 
than 1 km). On the other hand, as the loop circulation flow is higher in Dymola, the temperature drop 
due to a same amount of heat losses should be lower (the fluid spends less time in the pipes) and this 
could balance the effect of the previous argument. 
Therefore, the consumer behaviour options are very limited in LeanHeat, and the software is more 
focused on the modelling of the network operation. As the consumers greatly influence the return 
temperatures and necessary pressure and temperature levels in the network, it seems important to 
model them correctly to provide accurate results. Therefore, in LeanHeat, a lot of information or input 
data about consumer behaviour and needs should be known (or calculated) beforehand and manually 
written and checked in the software to provide accurate results. As LeanHeat allows for an easy input 
of real measurement data in the models, it seems more designed to be used by networks operators 
that have access to a lot of real data and measurements from the buildings of their area rather than 
by researchers whose inputs are more limited and based on assumptions on the behaviour of 
innovative designs. 
==PAGE:47==
9.1.1.2 The new substation design  
The main difference between a traditional substation and the new design is the implementation of a 
separate heat exchanger for the DHW circulation supply and its junction to the network with a S/S 
connection. This new design prevents the DHW circulation flow from contaminating the return line 
temperature both in winter and summer. Indeed, as soon as the supply temperature is around 57°C, 
the DHW circulation only decreases the DH supply flow by a few degrees in the heat exchanger as seen 
in part 8.2.2 and it quickly represents a high part of the total supply flow to the building. This operation 
is, therefore, essential to keep the return temperature lines at colder temperature levels, increasing 
the efficiency of many heat sources, even in winter. In summer, it is even more beneficial as it keeps 
the return line around 15°C, as shown Figure 28 in the cluster operation. Therefore, the return loop 
could potentially be used to provide cooling during summer.  
However, sending back the DHW circulation flow to the supply line reduces its temperature and 
impacts the operation of the network. As described in part 8.2.4.2, even when the loop’s circulation 
mass flow rate is increased to more than 50% of the total supply flow, the DHW circulation from the 
buildings reduces so much that the supply temperature than 57°C cannot be ensured at the last end-
user in the main loop. The solution was to increase the supply temperature at the heat source (10°C 
above the normal supply temperature) and to reduce the loop circulation flow. The summer operation 
was not simulated in the main loops model but will be discussed in part 9.1.3.2. 
Finally, to have an S/S connection in the substation, a third pipe, a third heat exchanger, and a small 
pump (0.1 to 0.5 bar of pressure head) are needed to send the flow back into the supply line. This will 
increase both the CAPEX and OPEX of the substation, but this cost could be offset by the savings due 
to the lower return temperature in the network and the implementation of district cooling in the same 
infrastructure.

9.1.3 Main loops behaviour 
9.1.3.1 Design situation 
The tree network design simulation made in LeanHeat showed a total pressure drop in the supply and 
return lines of 1.5 bars each. For the double loop network, Dymola and LeanHeat showed very similar 
results, resulting in higher pressure drop levels of about 5-5.4 bars in the supply and 2-2.5 bars in the 
return loop. This increase in pressure drop is due to the flow path in the network: here, the tree 
network is composed of two main branches, top and bottom, corresponding to the loops cut in half. 
Therefore, in each branch, only half of the flow is circulated and on a length that is half of the total 
loop length. In the critical path for example, only 10 kg/s is circulated in design conditions. In the double 
loop case, the flow has only one direction and one path.  Therefore, all the flow for the network (40 
kg/s) has to be sent in one unique branch and circulate through the whole loop. A double loop system 
would therefore have a higher pump consumption than a traditional branched network. However, in 
traditional DH networks, the pumping energy represents 0.5 to 1% of the total heat delivered[31]. This 
project does not aim at an economic evaluation of the double concept but this increase in the pumping 
energy is likely to have a small influence on the total energy consumption. 
One of the main advantages of a double-loop network is that heat sources of any power capacity can 
be connected to the network and can provide any share of the consumption. In Dymola, only 
simulations with one heat source were conducted, but they can be moved to any part of the network. 
This means that decentralized heat sources can be connected anywhere and still provide for a large 
share of the consumption: as the flow is looping, the supply energy from the heat sources will reach 
all the end-users. As the pipe diameter is constant along the loops and it was designed so that 100% 
of the demand can be provided from one connection, the integration of intermittent decentralized 
energy sources will not require any network modification. With the example of a large solar field close 
==PAGE:49==
to the loops, during sunny days, it can supply all the network consumption and a back-up heat source 
connected somewhere else on the loop can take over when there is less or no more sun.  
Also, as shown in LeanHeat, connecting many small heat sources at different locations in the loops 
would be beneficial for the operation of the network. Even if they represented a small share of the 
heat production (about 15%), spreading the mass flow rate input in the loops avoids having to circulate 
a very large mass flow rate in one part of the pipes. As visible Figure 12, 5 kg/s are injected through 
the waste heat sources at 300 m from the main plant in Node 10. Therefore these 5 kg/s are not 
circulated in the first 300 m of the loops and result in a decrease of the pressure drop in the supply 
line of 0.4 bars compared to the Dymola simulation with only one heat source. Therefore, with multiple 
heat sources in the network, the pressure gradient in the main loops can be reduced and uniformized 
and the pressure difference between the supply and return line will stay more constant and therefore 
smaller, easing the integration of substation and sources standing less pressure difference. 
9.1.3.2 Part-load behaviour and summer operation 
As mentioned in part 8.2.4.2 and 9.1.1.2, the main challenge in the main loop operation is when there 
is a small or no SH demand. The mass flow rate in the pipes is decreased and its temperature is 
therefore more impacted by the heat losses and the DHW circulation connection. Also, as the actual 
heat demand is small (just DHW or with a bit of space heating), the return flow is greatly decreased 
and the water flow going back to and from the heat source is limited. Therefore, as the SH demand 
decreases, smaller amount of energy can be brought to the supply loop through the exchange of flow 
with the return line. Increasing the supply temperature at the source can partially balance this effect 
but the necessary supply temperature was already at 69°C for an outdoor temperature of 12°C. 
Another solution should be found, especially for the summer period when the only return flow will be 
from the DHW consumption. One possibility could be to have small heat pumps that warm up the 
circulation flow at different places of the network. If the double loop is also used as a district cooling 
network (see part 9.3.19.3), the cold heat source of these heat pumps could be linked to the return 
line and cool it down to accommodate the cooling needs of the area. This kind of operation could also 
be implemented sooner than when the SH systems stop, at around 11-12°C for example, as it is already 
a challenge to maintain the right temperature level in the main loops with just the use of the main 
heat source and return flows. 
Finally, the total heat losses in the network seem quite small compared to yearly average heat losses 
of traditional district heating networks. In general, they amount to 10-20% of the heat production of 
the network [9]  but here, they were found to be from 1.5 to 5.7% in outdoor conditions of -12°C to 
12°C (see part 8.2.4.2). The area of this case study having a high heat density, 35 GJ/m, corresponding 
to the level of concentrated commercial areas, its heat losses should be compared with the typical 
level of this area type: 5-10% [9]. In addition, these results were obtained in a winter operation, when 
there is a significant SH demand. Most of the heat losses will happen during the non-heating season 
(usually two third of the year), when the heat demand will be very low. Therefore, the yearly average 
share of heat losses of this network should be greatly increased by the summer operation and is yet to 
be calculated.  
9.2 Potential biases 
Several approximations were part of this study. However, this was considered acceptable as the main 
goal of the thesis was to create models that could predict and forecast the behaviour of this innovative 
==PAGE:50==
network architecture in different conditions. The model is robust to many changes such as 
consumption profiles or amount, temperature levels or pipe sizes.  
For example, one of the strongest approximations was on the DHW tapping profiles. For instance, it 
was assumed that the daily DHW energy consumption was the same every day of the year, the profiles 
were made of shares, 𝑝𝑖, of the total daily energy consumed during a time Δ𝑡𝑖 at different moments 
of the day. The resulting power and mass flow rate are calculated as explained part 6.3.1.2. However, 
this often results in very small DHW mass flow rates during tapping that may differ from real 
consumptions. For example, a shower uses approximately 9 L/min of hot water (15 L/min in total and 
with 60/40% mix of hot/cold water), a sink tap needs roughly 5 L/min [32], but for buildings with a very 
small yearly heat consumption such as 15 MWh/year, the DHW mass flow rates in the simulation were 
about 0.005 kg/s or 0.3 L/min. However, real or possible tapping profiles of the buildings in the area 
could not be obtained from the commercial partners and, in order to keep an accurate yearly DHW 
energy, the assumption described before was still chosen. Also, the total DHW consumption profile of 
an area usually follows a general profile, with peaks in the morning, lunch and/or evening and a very 
small or null consumption in the night. Here, for simplicity, the tapping profiles were made of random 
shares of 20 to 50% of the total daily energy consumption at random moments of the days, with 
random durations of 200 to 1800 seconds (usually, the bigger the energy share, the longer the tap). 
However, the model was designed so that the tapping profiles can be easily adjusted if correct ones 
are obtained.  
Another strong simplification made in the Dymola model is the decrease of the number of buildings in 
the simulation and the handling of the service pipes. Firstly, as mentioned at the end of part 8.2.4.2, 
having a smaller amount of bigger service pipes will generate less heat losses than multiple small ones 
so the heat losses from this type of pipes were doubled in the results presented in this report. However, 
the heat loss coefficient of a bigger pipe is still higher than for a smaller one and two big pipes certainly 
have a higher heat loss than what two small pipes would have. The difference in results between the 
two considerations is shown Figure 37.  
Figure 37: Differences in the heat loss amount and repartition in the network depending on the 
chosen approximation: in bright colours, the heat losses from the service pipes are directly from the

==PAGE:51==
The real heat loss amount in the service pipes lies between these two extrema but a simulation with 
all the buildings should be conducted to determine it precisely. However, the assumptions made for 
the service pipe size selection can also be criticized. As described in 6.3.2.2, to avoid a building-by-
building dimensioning, a simple criteria of fluid velocity in the service pipes were chosen to size them 
for the different end-user consumption levels. The threshold of 1 m/s was considered regarding the 
maximal SH load, i.e. a conservative velocity level (it could be increase to 2 m/s in the pipes without 
any operational issues) with a conservative design load. Consultants designing district heating 
networks would probably have more accurate criteria regarding the sizing of the service pipes, and 
they would probably be smaller in reality than what they were set to in the simulation. Again, the size 
of the service pipes in each building, as well as all of the other pipes in the network, can be easily 
adjusted in the model if better information is available in the future. 
9.3 Future work 
9.3.1 District cooling network 
As mentioned in the concept definition and in the methods, the double loop network could also be 
used as a district cooling network in summer. The simulations showed that the temperature of the 
return line could be maintained around 15-20°C in summer thanks to the removal of the by-pass valves. 
Using the return line in an R/R connection at the substation (as presented Figure 2), and regularly 
cooling down the network return temperature with cold sources such as heat pumps or adsorption 
chillers, some cooling could be provided to the buildings in the area, using the same infrastructure as 
the district heating network. The main cost element in district heating and cooling networks is due to 
the expensive infrastructure (see part 4.1.1), combined heating and cooling within the same network 
could be economically attractive. 
According to the preliminary study of these cooling needs, as described in 6.1.3 and 7.3, the cooling 
energy demand was estimated to be around 1695 MWh, with power load of 1.2 MW. As space cooling 
systems operate with smaller temperature differences than space heating ones, the cooling mass flow 
rate should be calculated with an appropriate temperature drop of about 7°C. As showed in 7.3, this 
resulted in a demand cooling mass flow rate of 39.6 kg/s. As the heating design mass flow rate was 
calculated to be about 40 kg/s and that, in practice, 45 kg/s are circulated in the pipes when the 
outdoor temperature is around -12°C, circulating the cooling mass flow rate in the return lines seems 
feasible. However, the DHW demand of the area should still be met in summer, and the network should 
be able to circulate its return flow through the pipes. Therefore, some part of the return loop pipes 
capacity should be reserved for the DHW provision: at least the design DHW mass flow rate, 4.05 kg/s 
(the design DHW capacity with a temperature drop of 30°C). As 45 kg/s can be circulated in the supply 
loop in winter, 41 kg/s could be available for the cooling supply in summer.  
However, the cooling supply would work with R/R connections, like the S/S connections of the DHW 
circulation provision. In this project, it was demonstrated that a large additional mass flow rate should 
be circulated in the pipes to keep a high enough supply temperature without many water exchanges 
with the heat source. The same phenomenon would apply to the cooling line, as each cooling station 
would increase its temperature a bit. An additional flow may have to be circulated in the return pipes 
during summer to maintain a cold enough water temperature in the return lines. This would reduce 
the network's capacity to provide enough cooling to the area. As mentioned in 9.1.3.2, small heat 
==PAGE:52==
pumps could be installed to connect the return and supply loops, warming and cooling the other to 
maintain the required temperature level.  
A more precise analysis of the cooling demand and capacity of the network should be conducted in a 
future study. The cooling side can be integrated into the Dymola substation model (and with the 
heaters in LeanHeat), and an adaptation of the main loop network heat and cold source should be 
carried out to study the hydraulics of this new operation and assess the capabilities of the new network 
architecture. 
9.3.2 Supply temperature contamination 
As developed in 9.1.3, the contamination of the supply temperature due to the DHW circulation S/S 
connections represents a significant challenge for the double-loop network operation. When operating 
in parallel to cooling demand, small heat pumps could be used to regularly warm up the supply line 
temperature and cool down the return one (as developed in 9.1.3.2 and 9.3.1). This solution seems 
very beneficial in summer, providing both the cooling and hot water circulation demand, but less in 
winter. However, the contamination of the supply temperature is already challenging with outdoor 
temperatures of 10-12°C, when there is still space heating demand and no cooling needs. Another 
option could be to cascade the return flow from the DHW circulation heat exchanger into the SH supply 
flow, as developed in [33] and [21]. It was shown in the substation results (8.2.2) that the SH systems 
could be supplied with colder temperatures than the supply temperature of the network (limited by 
the DHW temperature need) as soon as the outdoor temperature is above -1°C. Therefore, mixing part 
(or the whole) flow from the DHW circulation into the SH supply could be a solution to reduce the 
effect of the supply contamination issue. It would reduce the amount of flow sent back into the supply 
line (smaller temperature impact) while increasing the return line flow, that will later be exchanged 
for hot flow at the heat sources. The hydraulics and the actual capacities of this operation could be 
implemented in the Dymola model and studied in the future. 
10 Conclusion 
The increase of the district heating and cooling deployment in the European Union is strongly 
encouraged by the scientific community ([2] [1] [3]) to answer the environmental challenges associated 
to heating and cooling in our modern societies. However, the third generation of district heating 
networks, mostly used nowadays, is designed to provide a large heat demand using centralized fossil 
fuel plants. The future district heating generation should, on the contrary, be able to integrate 
decentralized and renewable energy sources to its operation. A major challenge will arise in minimizing 
the operating temperatures while maintaining the same comfort for the end-users. 
According to the European decarbonizations goals [2], there should be a general switch in the heat 
production of district heating networks, from centralized fossil fuels-intensive heat plants to 
decentralized intermittent renewable energies sources. The innovative architecture of double-loop 
networks, whose hydraulic operation was studied in this project, allows a more flexible operation and 
can integrate this new type of energy production, without any capacity restrictions. Indeed, as the 
water flow is circling in the main pipe loops, the energy provided to the network at any location in 
these loops will be able to reach any part of the network. Also, as the pipe diameter of the main pipes 
were chosen to be constant all along the loops and designed to support a supply of the 100% part-load 
in one place anywhere in the network, intermittent energy sources of high capacities are easily 
==PAGE:53==
supported. The whole design load can be provided by a renewable source at one end of the loops and 
then switched to another source or to a backup plant connected anywhere else on the network when 
necessary. 
The European Commission [2] also recommends the interconnection of the different energy grids 
(electricity, gas, heating, transportation…) into a large smart energy system to optimally utilize the 
intermittent renewable and fossil resources. The double loop concept can secure very low 
temperatures in the return lines in summer and could, therefore, potentially embed the heating and 
cooling grids in the same infrastructure. In addition, a coupling with the electricity grid seems 
conceivable, by using heat pump as connection points.  For example, the heat pumps mentioned in 
parts 9.1.3.2, 9.3.1 and 9.3.2 could be connected to the electricity balancing market and heat up/cool 
down the main loops a little more or a little less when necessary.  
Finally, the removal of by-pass valves and the reduced temperature in the return line in summer could 
greatly decrease the heat losses from the network and increase its profitability in low heat-densities 
areas. As the building’s space heating consumption is predicted to be drastically reduced in the future, 
the new district heating generation could accompany the improvements in the buildings sector while 
still providing heat at a low cost to reach high system efficiency. 
Both LeanHeat and Dymola were able to model the network operation in design conditions and gave 
very similar results. As the model building in LeanHeat is simpler and quicker, it can be used to get the 
global dimensioning of the network elements, such as pump head, pipe diameters or expected mass 
flow rates. These parameters facilitate the construction of the Dymola models that are more complex 
and sensitive to boundary conditions. In particular, models in Dymola can be used to study the 
hydraulics of the network in detail, at different part-loads, and from a dynamic point of view. Future 
works could focus on integrating the cooling part into the models and the DHW circulation cascading 
into space heating. The effect of these measures in answering the current issues that arose from the 
operation of the network in the summer should be assessed before conducting any developed 
potential profitability analysis of the new design.
