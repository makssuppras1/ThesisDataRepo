Abstract
An estimated 40% of the female population have high density breasts, which complicates the detection
and diagnosis of breast cancer tumors using x-ray mammograms. New diagnostic tools, like 3D Molec-
ular Breast Imaging (MBI), utilizes higher energy radiation to overcome this obstacle. At DTU Space
a 3D CZT drift strip detector has been developed, which is suitable for 3D MBI applications, due to
its three dimensional imaging capabilities and high energy and position resolutions.
The aim of this thesis is the design of a neural network capable of predicting interaction positions of
photons in the detector. The position resolution should be within 1mm in all three dimensions. A fea-
ture selection process is conducted to determine the necessary features to be used as inputs for machine
learning models. Hyperparameters are subsequently optimized using manual or cross validation meth-
ods. Lab experiments with slit beam illuminations are performed and used to evaluate the performance
of the trained models.
A total of 20 features were found in the feature selecting process, being the number of the triggered
anode, the pulseheights of the triggered anode and all cathodes, as well as both the positive and neg-
ative pulseheight of the drift signals. The sigmoid function was used as the activation function, and
the learning rate was determined to be 1.5 · 10−5 and the batchsize was 1000. The neural network
structure consisted of two hidden layers, with 10 neurons in each, and 3 neurons in the output layer.
All interaction positions predicted from the final neural network model fulfilled the position resolution
requirement. 2D histograms of the predicted interaction positions are shown to be in agreement with
the expected slit beam widths of the experimental measurements.
In this master thesis, a neural network model capable of predicting the interaction position for the 3D
CZT detector prototype located at DTU Space was designed. Experimental data was used to verify
that the predicted positions were in compliance within 1mm of the positions determined using offline
positioning algorithms.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Breast cancer is the most common type of cancer amongst women i Denmark, with more than 4500
new cases each year [1]. On average, more than every tenth woman is diagnosed with the disease. Early
diagnosis increases the probability of being cured, as well as the chance of survival [2].
One of the main challenges for x-ray mammography is diagnosing the 40% of the female population
which is in possession of high density breasts [3]. Breasts are composed of a combination of high density
tissue (glands and other fibrous tissue) and low density tissue (fat). The specific ratio between the types
of tissue differs between women.
To increase the probability of early detection, extensive screening programs are in place. The main
screening diagnostic tool of choice is the mammography [4], which utilizes low dose radiation to pro-
duce images of the breast. These images are called mammograms. Mammogram x-rays are typically
in the low end of the x-ray spectrum with energies of around 20keV. At these energies, the radiation
will penetrate low density tissue, making it appear transparent or dark on a mammogram. Due to their
higher density, tumors block the incoming radiation, and thereby appear white on the images. At low
x-ray energies, not only tumors, but also high density breast tissue appears white on the mammograms.
High density tissue can thereby mask the tumors, resulting in a decreased sensitivity of the mammo-
graph 1.
3D Molecular Breast Imaging (MBI) circumvents this problem by utilizing higher energy gamma radi-
ation, which is able to penetrate through high density breast tissue. Tumors still appear white on the
mammogram and are therefore not masked by any breast tissue. The increase in photon energy results
in a corresponding increase in absorbed dose for all else equal. As a higher dose generally incur more
severe radiation effects, it is crucial for a primary diagnostic tool to be low dose. A way of decreasing
the dose is by reducing the number of photons needed to create an x-ray image. This raises the need
for detecting systems with low rejection rate of events as well as high position and energy resolution.
At DTU Space, a novel 3D CZT detector prototype have been developed. The detector is capable of
submm position resolution and an energy resolution of 7.2keV at 662keV [5], making it ideal for 3D
MBI use.
Position algorithms have been developed for offline position determination, but they are not compatible
with current ASIC (application-specific integrated circuit) designs and can not be used for efficient
real-time analysis. Therefore, new tools for determining the interaction position of the photons in the
detector should be developed for real time applications.
The purpose of this study is to use machine learning methods to create models capable of determining
the interaction position of a photon that interacts with the detector in a single photoelectric absorption
event. To align with the position resolution previously recorded, it is required that the resolution of
the predicted position is within 1mm of the real position in all three dimensions. This requirement will
in the report be called the resolution requirement.
The development and evaluation of the 3D CZT detector models are at this stage with regard to the
3D MBI utilization. But when the detector prototype is fully developed and characterized, it can be
applied to a wide variety of uses across different fields. Most relevant for the DTU Space institute, the
detectors will at a later stage be suitable for high energy space applications.

1.1
Project formulation
The main objective of this master thesis is the design of a machine learning model capable of photon
interaction determination for the prototype 3D CZT detector. The model will be developed using elec-
tronic pulse shape signals from a theoretical 3D CZT detector model, and trained using supervised
learning methods. Required features for the machine learning models will be identified and evaluated,
and datasets of these features will be generated. Finally the performance of the developed model will
be evaluated on experimental data from the 3D CZT detector lab setup located at DTU Space.
Project milestones:
• The theoretical 3D CZT detector model will be modified to generate features required for pulse-
shape data analysis as output for the machine-learning algorithms.
• Create comprehensive training and validation data sets from the modified theoretical 3D CZT
detector model as input for machine learning algorithm.
• Design and evaluate models using a machine-learning algorithm and the modelled pulse-shapes
features as the input.
• Implement and evaluate the developed algorithm performance on a test data set consisting of
experimental data obtained from the 3D CZT detector prototype.

CHAPTER 2
Semiconductor Detector
Theory
When observing any type of electromagnetic radiation, the first stage is to get the radiation to interact
with the detector in a manner in which information on the radiation can be extracted. For instance,
this could be through a voltage or a current signal. For energy determination purposes, the change in
measurable quantity should be dependent on photon energy.
In semiconductor applications, the objective is to get the photons to interact with the detecting ma-
terial through one or several processes and thereby get them to deposit their energy as charges in the
material.
Semiconductor detectors have several desirable features for application such as their good energy reso-
lution, their compact size and their relatively fast timing characteristics [6]. They are also flexible in
the way that their geometry and design can be varied for their exact purpose and application.
In this chapter, the basic theory concerning semiconductor physics and radiation interaction with matter
will be introduced.
2.1
Photon interactions with matter
High energy radiation can interact with matter in several ways. This could be by elastic scattering or
via processes where energy is deposited into the material.
Interaction processes will result in the decrease of photon flux to I(x) at a depth x from the incident
photon flux of I0. The absorption in the medium follows Beer’s law [7]
I(x) = I0 · exp [−µx]
(2.1)
µ is known as either the linear attenuation coefficient or the linear absorption coefficient. The three
main interactions that lead to a partial or complete loss of photon energy to the detecting medium are
photoelectric absorption, Compton scattering and pair production. The linear attenuation coefficient
µ is thereby the sum of the attenuation coefficients for each of these interactions [7]
µ = µE + µC + µP
(2.2)
where µE is for photoelectric absorption, µC is for Compton scattering and µP is for pair production.
The probability that each of these processes occur is strongly dependent on both the energy of the
incoming radiation and the atomic number Z of the absorbing matter [6]. At low energies, photoelectric
absorption dominates, and at high energies, pair production is the dominant interaction. Compton
scattering dominates in the region between these two extremes. This relationship is illustrated in Fig.
2.1.
All three types of interactions result in a change in measurable signal that is proportional to the
energy deposited in the detector by the photon during the process [7]. Out of the three mentioned
processes, photoelectric absorption is considered the ideal process, as the photon completely disappears
and transfers all its energy to the detector. In the case of both Compton scattering and pair production,
some of the photon energy may escape the detecting volume. This results in a wrong recording of photon

2.1.1
Photoelectric absorption
The most desirable process for detecting purposes is photoelectric absorption. In photoelectric absorp-
tion, a photon interacts with an atom of the detector material, removing one of the electrons from
its bound shell and creating a vacancy [6]. The photon is completely annihilated in the process. The
created electron is known as a photoelectron, and is injected with the kinetic energy Ee, given as the
excess energy of the interacting photon energy Eγ overcoming the specific binding energy Eb [6]
Ee = Eγ −Eb
(2.3)
The incidence photon energy is given as Eγ = hv. Here h = 6.63 · 10−34J/s is Plancks constant and v
is the frequency of the photon.
Photoelectric absorption is the dominant interaction process for low energies. The interaction probabil-
ity scales with photon energy and atomic number Z of the absorbing material.

If a monochromatic radiation source resulted in only photoelectric absorption in the detector, the
resulting energy spectrum would depict only a single peak. As all of the photon energy is transferred to

2.1.2
Compton scattering
The second interaction process is Compton scattering, where the photon interacts with an electron in
the detecting material. The photon is deflected from its original path through interacting with the
electron. In this scattering event, a part of the photon energy Eγ is transferred to kinetic energy of the
recoil electron Ee:
Eγ′ =
Eγ
1 +
Eγ
mec2 (1 −cos θ)
(2.5)
here Eγ′ is the energy of the photon after the scattering occurs, and mec2 = 0.511MeV is the rest mass
energy of the electron. θ is the angle between the original and scattered direction of the photon. θ can
assume all values, giving a multitude of electron energies. Two extreme cases are of special interest,
namely θ = 0 and θ = π.
• 1. θ ∼= 0: In this case Eq. 2.5 predicts that the scattered photon will maintain approximately
the same energy Eγ′ ∼= Eγ and as a consequence, the recoil electron will obtain very little energy
Ee ∼= 0. No matter what, the photon will lose some of its energy to the electron even in a head
on collision.
• 2. θ = π: In the head on collision, the maximum possible energy is transferred to the recoil
electron. In the interaction, the photon is scattered back in the direction which it came from, and
the electron is catapulted along the original photon direction.
The Klein-Nishina formula is used to infer the angular distribution of the scattered photons [6]. All
scattering angles will occur for the photons, up till θ = π where the maximum energy deposition occurs.
For a monochromatic source, the resulting energy spectrum from single Compton scattering events will
consists of a continuum from around 0 (for θ = 0) up to the maximum deposited energy. This continuum

2.1.3
Pair production
The third interaction process is pair production. The process has to take place in the strong coulomb
field near a nucleus of the detecting material [6]. A minimum photon energy corresponding to twice
the electron rest mass 2mec2 = 1.02MeV is required for the process to occur, as the incident photon
completely vanishes in the creation of an electron-positron pair. While the probability of pair production
to occur at energies just above 1.02MeV is very low, it increases dramatically with photon energy, and
it is the dominant effect at high photon energies[6]. The interaction probability scales with Z2 [7]. Any
excess energy from the photon is distributed between the electron Ee−and positron Ee+.
Ee−+ Ee+ = Eγ −2mec2
(2.7)
In the energy spectrum this process will show up similarly as the photoelectric absorption, as a single
peak. But the peak is shifted to an energy 2mec2 below the photon energy Eγ, as shown in Fig. 2.2.
The generated positron is an inherently unstable particle and at sufficiently low energies, it will either
be annihilated or recombine with an electron of the detecting medium. This results in the generation
of two annihilation photons, each carrying an energy of mec2.
2.2
Semiconductor detectors
Semiconductor detectors are solid state detectors that uses semiconducting materials as the detecting
medium. The materials are crystalline by nature and defined by periodic repetition of atoms in a lattice
structure [8]. A consequence of this structure is the discrete bands of allowed energies for electrons in
the crystal. Of special interest in semiconductor physics are the valence and conduction band. The
valence band corresponds to the outer shell of electrons that are still bound to a specific site or atom in
the lattice. The conduction band in contrast represents the free electrons that can migrate throughout
the crystal volume. It is the electron in the conduction band that contribute to the electric conductivity
of a material [8].
The band gap is defined as the energy difference between the valence and conduction band, and the
magnitude of this determines whether the material is an insulator or a semiconductor [8]. Semicon-
ductors have small bandgaps with energies less than 5eV [6]. Small inputs of energy may therefore
be enough to excite the electrons from the valence band up to the conduction band, leaving behind a
vacancy in the valence band known as a hole. The average energy consumed in the making of one of
these electron-hole pairs is known as the ionization energy and is generally independent of the incident
photon energy. One of the main advantages of semiconductor devices is that the ionization energy is
usually very small, leading to a great number of charge carriers generated for a certain input energy.
[6].
The holes constitute a net positive charge, and are, similar to the conduction band electrons, free to
migrate throughout the detector volume.
In the absence of an electric field, the electron and hole
usually recombine, leaving a steady state number of electron-hole pairs in the medium. But when an
applied electric field is present, the electrons and holes move with a net drift velocity parallel to the
direction of the electric field, but in opposite directions [6]. The higher the magnitude of the electric
field, the higher the drift velocity of the charge carriers. This process occurs until a saturation point is
reached. At the saturation electric field, a further increase in electric field intensity leads to no increase
in drift velocity. Superimposed on this drift movement, is the random thermal velocity or diffusion of
==PAGE:14==
2.2 Semiconductor detectors
7
the charge carriers [6].
It is this separation of charge carriers that, due to the applied electric field, is used to enable the detec-
tion of the radiation incident on the semiconductor detector.
Dependent on their abilities, detectors are said to have energy sensitivity, and imaging and timing
capability [7]. Detectors with energy sensitivity can detect the energy that a photon deposits in an
interaction process. Imaging capability is the ability of a detector to measure the interaction of the
photon in the detector volume. At last, detectors with timing capability can determine the time of each
interaction.
2.2.1
Signal Formation
When incident radiation interacts with a semiconductor detector, it generates a number of electron-hole
pairs creating a cloud of charge carriers. The size of the generated charge carrier cloud is proportional
to the energy deposited by the photon.
Potential differences between electrodes can be used to generate an electric field E in the detector. The
presence of this field snsures that the generated charges start moving through the detector volume. A
charge in a detector will, in the presence of the electric field, move with a drift velocity of v due to the
field. The electrons will move in the direction of the highest potential (anode side), while the holes act
as positive charges, and move towards the lowest potential (cathode side). At low and moderate values
of E, this is found from
vc = µcE
(2.8)
where c = e, h for electrons and holes respectively, and µ is known as the mobility [6]. As previously
mentioned, at high values of E, a saturation point is reached, that ensures no further increase in v even
if E is increased.
Superimposed on the drift movement of charges along the electric field lines, random thermal motion
causes a movement as well. Diffusion results in a spread of the charge cloud distribution when it arrives
for collection. The spread in arrival position for the charge cloud in an electric field E after travelling

charge signal is then fed to a charge sensing amplifier, before being converted to a voltage signal. As
the shape of the output voltage pulse is given by the induced charge, the amplitude of the output pulse
can be used to determine the energy that the photon deposited in the detector. [9]

The 3D CZT high resolution detector, which constitutes the foundation of this study, was developed
at DTU Space[15]. The detector has achieved a position resolution of 0.4mm FWHM and an energy

3.1
Detector geometry
The detecting medium is a REDLEN CZT crystal of dimensions 20mm × 4.7mm × 20mm. There are
a total of 12 drift cells, each of dimensions 1.6mm × 20mm × 4.7mm containing 1 anode and 4 drift
strips, 2 on each side of the anode. The anodes of width 0.15mm are pitched 1.6mm apart, with 3
drift strips electrodes in between. Between the edge of the detector and the first anode are 2 drift strip
electrodes. The drift strip electrodes have a 4mm pitch with the width of the strip electrodes being
0.15mm as well, and the gap between them 0.25mm. On the opposite side of the detector, 10 cathodes
are attached. The cathode pitch is 2mm, with a gap of 0.1mm between the 1.9mm wide strips [15].
A clear advantage of using CZT as the material for a semiconductor is obvious from the material’s
bandgap of around 1.6eV [6]. The width of the bandgap ensures that the detector is able to generate
at room temperature conditions, without excessive thermal excitation, foregoing the need for cryogenic
cooling.
The electrical layout of the detector is seen in Fig. 3.1. The anodes are held at ground potential, and
the drift strips are supplied with a bias of Vi = Vd · i/3, where Vd = 150V and i = 1, 2 being counted
away from the anode.
The drift strip bias ensures that the radiation-induced electrons are guided
towards and collected at the anodes. The cathode strips are held at a potential of −350V, attracting
the positively charged holes.
The resulting weighing potential of the above electrical configuration has a magnitude of unity at the
anodes before quickly falling off towards zero when moving into the detector volume away from the
anode strips. Induced signals from these potentials are analyzed to generate the output signal. Due to
the movement of the holes towards the cathodes, and the falloff of the weighing potential, the influence
of the hole trapping effect on the anode signal is reduced [17]. This has a positive effect on the resulting
energy resolution as mentioned in section 2.2.2. The induced anode signal is proportional to the energy
deposited into the material, and the energy of the photon can then be determined using the induced
charge signals.

3.4
Types of events
As previously mentioned, the electric field in the detector causes the generated charge carriers to move.
The electrons drift towards the anode side while the holes drift towards the cathode side. In this way
they are simultaneously inducing charges on the electrodes. When the charge induced on a specific
anode exceeds a given threshold level, the anode is triggered, and an event in the corresponding drift
cell is registered. Subsequently, the event is categorized depending on several characteristics of the
event. If multiple anodes have been registered or if there are several bumps on the electrode signals,
this signals multiple interaction events.
3.4.1
Single cell single event
The first type of interaction considered is the single cell single event. It is the only type of interaction
considered in this report.
A single event is the term assigned to an event where a single photon interacts with the detector material,
and deposits its energy one time. This is either a photoelectric absorption event, or a single Compton
scattering in which the photon afterwards escapes the detector. For it to be a single cell event, the
deposition has to occur well within the drift cell boundary so that the entire charge cloud is collected
at the same anode. An example of this type of event is seen as Interaction D in Fig. 3.6.
Another option is that only a small fraction of the charge cloud is guided to a neighbouring drift cell
anode, inducing too little charge to trigger an event on it. If the induced charge on the main anode is
still above the threshold level, it leads to a decrease in the resulting energy recorded for the event.

3.4.2
Multiple and shared events
In this study, the focus is on single interaction events within a single drift cell. Multiple events, like
two separate single events or multiple interactions (Compton scattering) due to the same photon, are
not considered. Neither are events that happen close to the border between drift cells where the charge
cloud is shared between different readout anodes.
A short explanation of multiple and shared events and their characteristics follow here to allow for a
better understanding of the way the events are sorted.
Multiple cell single event
If an event occurs, around the boundary between two drift cells, as in the case of Interaction A in Fig.
3.6, the generated charge cloud is found on both sides of the drift cell boundary. The potential ’well’
created by the drift strips then ensures that the electron is guided to be collected by the two anodes.
The induced signal is then shared among the two anodes. The number of triggered anodes depends on
the specific trigger threshold set. A low trigger threshold records both events, while a very high trigger
threshold may record none of the events if the charge cloud is equally shared. To remove multiple cell
single events, events in which more than a single anode is triggered are removed.
Single cell multiple events
If multiple events all occur within the same cell, as it is the case with multiple Compton scattering
events within the same cell, all charge clouds are guided towards and collected at the same node. This is
illustrated by Interaction B and C in Fig. 3.6. These types of events are recognized by having multiple
risetime sections on their anode signals. They can thus be removed by only allowing a single risetime

Multiple cells multiple events
If, for instance, a sequence of Compton events occur across a drift cell boundary, these are categorized

D followed a short time later. They are removed the same way as multiple cell single events.
Cosmic rays
The previous events considered are all assumed to originate from source radiation. In contrast to this,
cosmic rays are background events of extraterrestrial origin [20]. They are high energy radiation that
when impacting on the Earths atmosphere creates an array of secondary high energy particles. As their
energy is much higher than the usual source radiation, the induced pulse heights are that much greater
and often trigger a multitude of anodes in a cataclysmic event. They are removed the same way as
multiple cell single events.

are known. Fig. 3.11 shows how the detector and the three coordinate axes are related to each other.
The origin of the coordinate system is chosen to be in the corner closest to Anode 1 and Cathode 1 and
located on the anode side of the detector. The x axis runs perpendicular to the anode strips pointing
in the direction from Anode 1 towards Anode 12. The z axis intersects the x axis in the same corner
and runs perpendicular to the cathode strips, pointing from Cathode 1 towards Cathode 10. The y
axis is chosen as the direction needed to complete a right-handed coordinate system and points from
the anode plane towards the cathode plane.

Another limiting condition is when the interaction takes place close to the cathode strip side of the
detector. The entire signal is then induced on the closest cathode while only vanishing signals are found
on the other cathodes. This is due to negative signals being induced on the neighbouring cathodes
in the beginning of the drift movement before they ultimately increase, resulting in a very small total
pulse height. The overall effect is then that the fine positioning, which was achieved by applying the
gaussian fit, is not achieved. The position resolution in the z direction then becomes the width of the
cathode strip at around 2mm.

3.6.1
Boundary and edge considerations
In the edge regions as well as the boundary regions between drift cells, problematic effects arise in both
the real detector and in the theoretical model.
Flat field images of xy and xz event positions under full detector illumination have revealed severe edge
effects on the detector [16]. These are seen especially for events taking place close to the xy edge closest
to the readout electronics (x = 0). It has also been shown, [16] that due to the weighting potential
effect, difficulties arise when trying to determine the position near the anode plane (y < 1mm).
In the boundary regions between drift cells, the generated charge cloud is shared between neighbouring
anodes. If the trigger threshold on the event detection system is set so high it basically only considers
full energy photoelectric absorption, splitting the charge cloud will result in the rejection of a substan-
tial amount of events.
All these effects must be accounted for when using the theoretical model to simulate the real detector
behaviour. At the present time, the model can not imitate the behaviour at the edge of the detec-
tor. The uncertainties in the position determination algorithm for events close to the anode side are
problematic for supervised learning because the predicted values are trained on the calculated positions.
On top of undesirable behaviour originating in the real detector, the theoretical model contains
problematic regions and behaviour in itself. When a photon interacts with the real CZT detector near
the boundary between drift cells, the generated charge cloud will be guided towards the adjacent an-
odes. This divides the charge cloud such that a fraction of the total signal is induced on the respective
anodes. As mentioned in section 3.6, the model used for this study is a single charge carrier model. The
current model is therefore not able to correctly imitate the behaviour in the boundary regions. This is
further illustrated in Fig. 3.9. Here, a flat field image shows the pulse height of the triggered anode as a
function of x. The pulseheights are taken from Dataset A, and the specifics of the dataset are discussed
in chapter 5. In the figure the red dashed lines are used to indicate where the drift cell boundaries are
located. There are recurrent patterns seen throughout the drift cells. These are expected, as charges
generated at x values out in front of an anode have a straight path to collection. Events displaced in
x coordinate with respect to an anode instead have a longer path to take and are therefore subject to
charge losses to a larger extent. Near drift cell interfaces, the model completely breaks down, extending
to (unphysical) negative pulseheights being recorded. The breakdown is due to COMSOL and is hence
inherently a part of the model.

3.7
Comparison between theoretical model and real data
The theoretical model provides the observations used for training the machine learning models, so that
they can predict the interaction positions for experimental measurements.
The agreement between
theoretical model and real detector response are thus essential for the performance of the models. If
the theoretical model does not simulate the real data in a satisfactory manner, it does not matter how
well the machine learning models manage to predict the theoretical data, as they are still trained on an
erroneous basis. Any discrepancies between the theoretical model and the 3D CZT detector prototype
should hence be identified and examined carefully.
An immediate difference between COMSOL and the real detector is the physical size of the crystal.
COMSOL simulates a perfectly uniform 20mm × 5mm × 20mm rectangle. In the lab, the detector has
been found to have a diminished width of only 4.7mm [5]. The crystal will also not be perfectly uniform
but will have impurities and dislocations. All these effects have an influence on the weighting potential
and therefore also on the charges induced on the electrodes.
Another difference that has to do with physical differences between the real detector and the theoretical
model are the coordinate system used to describe the photon interaction position. In Fig. 3.11, the two
different coordinate systems used to describe the detector volume are found. In the theoretical model,
the y axis points from the cathode plane towards the anode plane, while the y axis for the real detector
points from the anode plane towards the cathode plane. The coordinate system used to describe the
COMSOL model is as a result not a right handed coordinate system. The electrostatic calculations
used in the model have made no assumptions on the coordinate geometry, and it should therefore not
influence the simulated pulseshapes. This is then just a simple mirroring of the y axis.
On top of the geometric variations, several differences between electrode signals for the simulated and

the COMSOL based theoretical model and the real data obtained from measurements on the CZT

real detector are found.
The most noticeable one is that the current model simulates electrode responses based on a single
charge. In reality, when a photon interacts with the CZT material, a myriad of electron-hole pairs are
generated, which each represent a single charge. The charge carriers all feel the electric potential and
in response move through the detector volume. The induced charge on the electrodes can then be seen
as the combination of contributions from several single point charges.

charge moving through the detector volume inducing a changing signal to the charge being collected by
the anode. As there is only a single charge in the theoretical model, this transition is immediate and
therefore shows up as a sharp corner. In comparison, real events have a finite number of charges. Due
to the physical size of the charge cloud, the individual charges are collected by the anode at slightly

Another noticeable difference due to the simulated single charge versus the real finite charge cloud is the

dependent on energy and are instead measured in channel units. Each channel on the SIS3316 digitizer
have a different gain, resulting in slight offsets in channel value for the same interaction energy.
Another effect not fully explored in this study is charge sharing between anodes. The drift bias guides
the generated charges towards the anodes. If the photon interaction position is located close to the
interface between two drift cells, the generated charge cloud may be divided and lead to the two dif-
ferent anodes. Depending on the threshold level, this could lead to only a single anode being triggered
but with reduced induced charge, or it could lead to no triggering of any anodes at all. In the case of
only full energy depositions (high threshold level investigations), this would lead to a reduced number
of interactions on the border between drift cells. Charge sharing becomes increasingly important if it
is desirable to include all interactions in the analysis.

CHAPTER 4
Machine Learning Theory
Machine learning is a subgenre of artificial intelligence. It is concerned with the ability to use past
experiences to learn patterns and structures in order to try to predict future observations with as little
human intervention as possible. The method imitates human beings’ ways of learning and ways of
carrying abstractions from concrete examples onto future observations.
One of the most used and famous examples is that of handwritten digits. Intuitively, we understand
that a round circle with a curved line going up on the left side resembles a 6. However, algorithms
that can perceive this and take both lopsidedness, differences in handwriting and other particulars into
account are near impossible to design. In the machine learning framework, huge datasets consisting
of a multitude of observations are used to create abstractions that can be used as learning rules and
applied to future observations. Continuing the example with the handwritten digits, instead of telling
the algorithm how the number 6 looks, the algorithm is fed several thousands of pictures of digits so
that it learns to recognize the shape.
This is where the strength of machine learning algorithm lies. It can utilize both an immense amount
of available data, but it is also not constricted by human perception the world. Therefore, it is able to
recognize new or hidden patterns or structures in datasets.
4.1
Supervised learning and the standard data format
Every machine learning algorithm starts with a dataset, which is a collection of measurements of a
given object or an area of interest. There is a total of N measurements in the dataset. Each is denoted
as an observation [21].
The datasets used for machine learning can be simple or complex. The simple dataset type is used
most widely in this study. In a simple dataset, each observation consists of a fixed number of measured
values. These are called the attributes or the features of the observation. A complex dataset will in
addition have some kind of extra structure or complexity to the dataset. This could be a temporal
component or the number of features varying with observations.
The standard format used for representing data in machine learning is the N × M dataset matrix X.
Here, N is the number of observations while M is the number of features.
The machine learning tasks used in this study all fall under supervised learning, in which there is access
to both the input and output for a given problem. In other terms, both the observations x and their
observed target values y are accessible. In machine learning, it is common to distinguish between classi-
fication and regression. Classification is the term used if the target values are discrete while regression
deals with continuous target values [22].
When applying a classification or regression method to a dataset, observations are split into a training
and a test set. The chosen method is trained on the training data, and afterwards, the performance of
the trained model is then evaluated by using the test dataset. In general, data used to train the model
should never be part of the test data, and how well the training data is predicted should never be the
base for model performance. As the model has been trained to fit the observations in these cases, the
model may be prone to overfitting, and the performance measure gives no real indication of the ability
to predict future observations [21].

4.4
Supervised learning methods
In supervised learning, an array of approaches can be found which predict targets y from observations
x.
The basic idea behind supervised learning is producing a prediction rule f(x, y) which, based on a set of
observations x, is able to predict the targets y. The prediction rule is trained for the model parameters
w using both the observations and the targets.
Several different approaches to training the the prediction rule f exist. In the following, the two meth-
ods used in this study are introduced: k-nearest neighbour and neural networks.
4.4.1

Firstly, the k-nearest neighbour search method is considered.
When trying to determine properties of an observation xk, it makes intuitive sense to look at the prop-

This provides the basis of the k-nearest neighbour algorithm. The K-neighbourhood of a point x in a
dataset X, NX(x, K) is defined as the K observations in the dataset which are the closests to x [21].

4.5
Neural networks
Neural networks are inspired by the human brain and how information is processed by neurons [22].
While there are significant discrepancies between the two, the basic idea behind the processes are
remarkably similar.
In the neural network, framework neurons are information processing units that can describe a single
attribute or a combination of several. The neurons are organized in layers, and the depth of the neural
network describes the number of layers [22]. Always present layers are the input and output layer.
The number of neurons in the input layer usually corresponds to the number of attributes M in each
observation, and the number of neurons in the output layer D is the number of predictions made from
f(x), ˆy. In this way, the neural network is the mapping from an M dimensional to a D dimensional
space
RM −→RD
(4.17)
which is equivalent to going from the input x to the output y through applying a function f to the
observation: y = f(x).
Additionally present in a neural network is a number of hidden layers. In each of these layers, a number
of neurons are found. There are no specific rules governing the number of neurons for a hidden layer.
Usually, the value is determined by cross validation methods, although a general rule of thumb states
that the number should be between M and D. Another guideline is using apriori knowledge of the
relation between attributes and targets to approximate the output value. If the relationship is complex,
it may need a large amount of neurons. If the relationship is simple, a small number of neurons may
be appropriate.
The general training process for a neural network consists of three subsequent steps [21]
1. Forward propagation: Dtrain is propagated forward through the network, computing the predicted
target ˆy for a given set of parameters or weights
2. Loss calculation: A loss function is used to calculate the error between the true targets y and ˆy.
3. Backward propagation: The error is propagated backwards through the network to update the
parameters in a beneficial way.

4.5.2
Gradient descent
An important step in the neural network framework is updating the parameters in the training stage of
the model. There are several ways of doing this, but the one most commonly used is to optimize neural
networks using the gradient descent method [22]. It is consequently applied as the method of choice in
this study.
The main objective of gradient descent is to minimize an arbitrary function F (θ) described by the
model parameters θ = (θ1, ..., θM) by updating the parameters in the gradient direction of F with
respect to the parameters. Mathematically, this translates as updating the parameters in the direction
of the partial derivative of F, ∂
∂θF(θ) = ∇θF, in which the size of the step taken in this direction is
known as the learning rate α [23]:
θ = θ −α∇θF
(4.18)
Different types of gradient descents can be found, which depend on the number of observations used
to generate the gradient of F. Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) is when a single observation is used.
Batch gradient descent is when all N observations in the training sets are used for the gradient [24].
When dealing with very large datasets, SGD holds the advantage that only a single training object is
being processed by the neural network at a time, which requires less memory. Therefore, it can also
converge faster for large datasets, as the parameters are updated more often then when doing batch
descent. As seen in Fig. 4.5, stochastic gradient decent takes a very noisy path. This has the added
advantage that the noisy steps in some cases make the model avoid local minima. On the other hand,
this noisiness can result in the model not being able to converge to a final solution. Frequently updating
the model parameters can quickly become a very computationally expensive process, and the training
thereby becomes a longer process.
On the other end of the spectrum is batch descent. In batch descent, every observation in the dataset
is used to perform one update of the model parameters.
The less frequent updating of the model
makes it more computationally efficient than the SGD alternative. As seen in Fig. 4.5, batch gradient
descent has a much smoother path towards convergence. On the downside, this smooth convergence
may be towards a local minima, resulting in a model that is less than suitable for the problem. Another
disadvantage which becomes increasingly important with larger datasets is that batch descent usually
requires the entire dataset loaded to memory.

4.6
Hyperparameters for neural networks
Hyperparameters are parameters describing a neural network that can not be learned through training
[22]. This may refer to the properties of the learning process as well as the structure of the network itself.
The hyperparameters help define the neural network, and, as such, their values have to be determined
before the training process can start.
4.6.1
Optimization methods for hyperparameters
Proper optimization of the hyperparameter are essential for efficient training of the neural network
[24]. Several methods for hyperparameter optimization exist. The most commonly known methods
introduced here are grid search, random search, and manual search.
Grid search has been used since the 1990’s and is the simplest way of optimizing hyperparameters.
The subsets of the hyperparameters to be optimized are discretized, and the algorithm searches exhaus-
tively through all possible combinations before choosing the best configuration. While this approach
is guaranteed to find the optimal choice of hyperparameters [24], it quickly becomes computationally
exhaustive as the number of configurations scales dramatically with increasing number of hyperparam-
eters.
Random search still uses the discretized subset of hyperparameters values.
Instead of exhaustively
searching through all possible combinations, it randomly chooses hyperparameters combinations and
tests them. A major advantage of random search is the decreased processing time compared to grid
search. A big drawback is that it is uncertain whether the random search finds the optimal configuration.
The last method mentioned is manual optimization. In this method, the programmer basically chooses
different values of hyperparameters and tests them manually.
4.6.2
Learning rate
The learning rate α is, as discussed in section 4.5.2 the size of the steps taken in the direction of the
gradient of the loss function [21]. It is a measure of how much the model learns for each update of the
model parameters. In Fig. 4.6, three illustrative cases of the learning rate size on an arbitrary loss
function are found. If the learning rate is too small, it results in the left figure. While the minimum of
the loss function is ultimately found, it requires an extensive number of steps. Increasing the learning
rate too much, on the other hand, results in the situation depicted on the right. The steps are too great
and the solution does not converge towards a minimum. Instead an appropriate learning rate has to be
chosen as depicted in the middle graph of Fig.4.6 to compromise between computational time and the
need for finding the minimum of the loss function.
4.6.3
Network architecture
Other hyperparameters of interest for this study concern the architectural structure of the network itself.
This is the number of hidden layers as well as the number of neurons H in each of the layers. These
hyperparameters control the complexity of the neural network and the complexity of the relationship
between input and output layers. The number of hidden layers defines the depth of the network. Usually,
neural networks with more than a single hidden layer are called deep networks [22]. The strength of
neural networks are decided by their hidden layers. The universal approximation theorem states that
a feedforward network, as the one used in this study, is able to represent any function [25]. Thus, in
theory, as long as a problem can be reduced to functions, it can be solved by neural networks. Using
just a L = 2 network to imitate a function may result in an enormous number of neurons in the hidden

The other quantity relating to the network architecture is the number of neurons in a given layer. For

Activation functions
The main purpose of the activation function is to convert the input that goes to a neuron into its
output. This output is translated to the next layer in the network. Essentially, there are three types of
activation functions: step functions, linear functions and nonlinear functions.
Step functions are, as the name implies, functions that changes their output value stepwise.

Another class of activation functions is the linear functions, as the one showcased in the middle graph

4.6.5
Loss function and batch size
In order to use backpropagation to update the model parameters for a neural network, a loss function
is required. The model parameters are updated with respect to minimizing the loss function [22]. As
such, the output of the loss function has to represent the model, so that reducing the output from the
loss function results in an improvement of the model. The choice of function depends on the type of
targets.
The batch size is given as the number of observations that are fed to the network used to compute
the partial derivative in Eq. 4.18 [24]. Stated differently, it denotes how many observations are fed to
or run through the network before the model parameters are updated.
If the batchsize is 1, it simply reduces to stochastic gradient descent. If it is N, the number of obser-
vations instead becomes batch search. The batch size dictates how much information will be loaded to
the memory, which is an important factor to take into account when working with very large datasets.
As seen in Fig. 4.5, a small batchsize is very noisy. On the other hand, a large batchsize may fail to
converge.

Covariance plots of the 20 features from Feature Model 5 plotted against the coordinate axes show
strong nonlinear behaviour (see Appendix B). Any chosen activation function should therefore be able
to imitate this non-linearity.
The chosen activation function for all layers in this study was the sigmoid function

CHAPTER 7
Experimental 3D CZT data
To evaluate the performance of the computed neural network model, experimental data from the 3DCZT
detector setup located in the DTU Space detector lab was measured.
In this chapter, both the experimental setup used to obtain the data and the evaluation of the model
on the data is presented.
7.1
Experimental setup
To quantify the performance of the model, slits measurements were performed to evaluate the model
performance in one direction at a time. The radioactive source used for data acquisition was Caesium-
137 or Cs-137 (halflife of 30.17 years). Cs-137 decays primarily by beta emission to a metastable isomer
of Barium-137 (halflife of 157 seconds). In its transition to a stable isomer of Barium-137, gamma
radiation of energy of 661.7keV is emitted [7].
For collimators, two thin strips with a thickness of 200µm placed between two blocks of tungsten
were used. This left only a thin strip with a width of approximately 200µm for the gamma radiation to
pass through. The length of the slit was long enough to cover the entire length of the detector. With
the combination of the opening angle and the distance between the detector and the radiation source,
the gamma ray illumination on the surface of the detector is expected to be around 700 −800µm wide.
A 1D scan program was used to obtain the experimental data. All measurements recorded pulses for

7.2
Normalization
The theoretical model is not capable of taking the energy of the photon into account, so all pulseshape
features derived from the model will be independent of energy. As seen in section 3.7.2, the pulseheights
of the electrodes are all within the unit interval.
This applies with the exception of negative drift
pulseheights, which lies in the negative unit interval. Another exception from these unit intervals is
feature number 1, the number of the triggered anode. Instead, this feature takes values in the interval
from 1 to 12 for the full detector or between 3 or 9 for the subset of the detector used in this study.
Therefore, step 3 below is not applied to this feature.
The experimental data are, on the other hand, defined in terms of ADC channels, and cover a much
larger interval that goes from 0 into the hundred of ADC channels. This interval is dependent on both
the energy of the incoming radiation as well as the gain of the specific channel tied to an electrode. To
use the model trained on the theoretical data, the features of the real data have to be transformed to
the same interval as the ones derived from the theoretical model.
This normalization procedure required several steps:
1. Observations outside of the subset of the detector were removed from the dataset
2. Observations lying outside of the photopeak were removed
3. Channel values were converted to unitless quantities
4. Change of geometry
5. Charge trapping effects were taken into account on the anode signals
6. Features were rescaled to the [-1,1] interval
7. Bias in the y direction was added
In the following, each step is described in greater detail.
1. Subset removal
The models computed in this study are only trained on a subset of the full detector volume. As defined
in section 5.3, the detector is the box defined by the corner [x, y, z] = [5mm, 10mm, 50mm] and the three
sidelengths of 10.0mm, 3.0mm and 10.0mm in the x, y and z direction respectively. Due to the rescaled
sigmoid function used between the second hidden layer and the output layer, only target values inside
the physical dimensions of the detector subset can be predicted. All observations that the position
algorithms determine as outside this dataspace are therefore removed from the dataset.

a wide array of energies. At the energies considered in this study, pair production is not possible, but

In contrast to the feature rescaling for the features derived from the theoretical dataset, the preprocess-

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
In the following, a thorough analysis and discussion of the results is conducted. The discussion is based
on the four milestones presented as part of the project formulation in section 1.1.
The purpose of this study was not to find the best possible model, but to generate a model good enough
to fulfill the position requirements set in chapter 1 and is kept simple at the data analysis stage while
at the same time as much as possible in line with possible ASIC designs. These considerations are
reflected in the decisions regarding the models.
8.0.1
Pulse shape data analysis features
The first task was to identify the necessary features to be derived for the machine learning purposes.
To ensure that the different features would be on the same scale, different preprocessing methods were
investigated first. The investigation was performed on the 1D Timeseries datasets. The chosen datasets
were not necessarily the optimal choice for testing rescaling, as the temporal data for the 26 electrodes
were essentially all on the same scale. This is reflected on the credibility interval found in Tab. 6.2
between the model with and without normalize rescaling, as the credibility interval is only slightly
offset in respect to zero. Still, it was important to choose a rescaling method before feature selection.
The performance of the model in the feature selection process and therefore the number of and the
specific type of features included depends crucially on the preprocessing of raw data. After the features
in Feature Model 4 were chosen, a test was conducted which confirmed the conclusions from these initial
investigations.
Following the above, the next task was to identify and select the features to be used for machine
learning purposes. The features were chosen in an iterative process, which was terminated when the
error difference between the predicted positions and positions determined by the positioning algorithms
were below 0.1mm for all observations.
The model of choice for feature selection was a nearest neighbourhood method. The motivation behind
this method was its simplicity and that beyond the number of neighbours used for constructing the
neighbourhood, no other inputs were required for training. This limited the extent to which model
performance depended on the specific hyperparameters chosen.
As the predictions were made on theoretical and thereby ideal data, no noise or outliers were expected.
Therefore, a k = 1 neighbourhood was chosen. The datasets used for feature selection were three oned-
imensional datasets stacked together to create a composite 3D dataset. Overlapping observations were
removed from the dataset. Ideally, the analysis had been performed on a true 3D dataset, as a lot of the
complexity was removed by reducing it to three 1D lines. There were several reasons behind this choice,
but the main motivator was time concerns. At the time of analysis, only the IDL written theoretical
model was available. It took around 30s to generate a single observation, and parallel executions were
not possible. A smaller 3D dataset of 51 × 21 × 51 steps, equivalent to 5mm × 2mm × 5mm would take
more than 1.6 million seconds, or almost 19 days to generate. The three 1D dataset in comparison, took
only a total of around 8 hours to generate using the IDL model. When experimenting with different
features, it was therefore deemed more sensible to test on the composite dataset in order to eliminate
the need to repeat long computations. A full 3D dataset was only generated after all features were
selected.
Offline data analysis positioning algorithms that had the ability to accurately determine the interaction
==PAGE:70==

63
position with the required accuracy were already established. Therefore, the initial features selected
and tested were heavily inspired by the quantities included in the offline analysis. As a result of the
feature investigation, 22 initial features were chosen as the features necessary for machine learning pur-
poses. All features were pulseheights with the exception of the electron drift time and the number of the
triggered anode. Only the pulseheight of the triggered anode was used as single site single interaction
events were considered.
In the final set of parameters, Feature Model 5, the number of features was cut down to 20. The electron
drift time and the pulseheight of the summed cathode signal were both removed as features from the
collection.
Investigations of the temporal pulseshape data for the 26 electrodes showed a discrepancy between
drift times measured from the CZT detector and those generated by the theoretical model. Fits of
the data showed that these discrepancies could be accounted for by using another value for µ. These
investigations were solely based on experimental fits for µ, which were not not in accordance with ma-
terial properties provided by the manufacturer. It was decided that the best solution was to remove
the drift time as a feature. Simultaneously, it was decided to remove the summed cathode signal. It
was problematic for an ASIC design and did not add any information that was not already available
from the individual cathode pulseheights. Removing the two features did not impose any statistical
significant change in model performance. Indeed the performance for both Feature Model 4 and Feature
Model 5 could not be distinguished from the time data series consisting of the full temporal signal using
statistical tools. Feature Model 5 managed to predict the position of the interaction within the position
requirement, and the 20 features were kept as the final features.
The k = 1 nearest neighbour method together with the 20 selected features was proven sufficient
to localize the 3D position of the interacting photon with the required position resolution according to
the theoretical model. While this appear as a suggestion that the study requirement has been fulfilled,
there are several problems with implementing this method as part of the 3D MBI setup.
The precision of a k = 1 nearest neighbour model depends crucially on the amount of available obser-
vations. Fundamentally, the physical gridsize of the observations in the target space determines the
achievable resolution. For a high target resolution, an extensive amount of observations is required.
For the theoretical model using COMSOL, the gridsize can be defined down to 0.1mm, which was the
gridsize used for the predictions in this section. With the physical size of the DTU Space developed
CZT detector being 20mm × 4.7mm × 20mm, this would result in a total of 201 × 48 × 201 = 1, 939, 248
observations each consisting of 20 features. A k = 1 nearest neighbour search would then have to com-
pare almost 40 million individual features. And if a solution more robust to noise or individual outliers
is chosen, say by using the k = 5 nearest neighbourhood, just about 200 million feature values are to
be compared and their distance to the test features computed. Searching exhaustively through this
dataspace is a computationally cumbersome process. It becomes especially challenging if fast timing
characteristics are important as well as situations where a large number of photons are incident on the
detector simultaneously. To reduce the complexity of the target prediction step, a neural network is
implemented for position computation.
8.0.2
Training and validation datasets
The second milestone was concerned with the creation of datasets to be used in this study.
In contrast to the usual case, the observations used for training of the machine learning models are
not experimental obtained data. Instead observations are derived from a theoretical model used to
simulate the electronic pulse shapes generated by charges moving throughout the detector volume. The
resulting target predictions of any model will then rely crucially on the extent to which the model and
real data fit each other. This results in some unique challenges partaken to ensuring agreement between
the theoretical model an experimental data. Regrettably, investigations revealed several inconsistencies
==PAGE:71==

64
between measure and simulated pulseshapes.
Special problems were found in regard to edge and boundary data.
This was a key motivator for
not using full detector volume in the training process.
Instead, a subset of the detector of size
10mm × 3mm × 10mm was chosen. The subset was centered around the center of the detector, so
that none of the edge effects should have any effects. Based on a visual inspection of the triggered an-
ode pulseheight as a function of the x coordinate, observations in a cell around the drift cell boundaries
were removed from the datasets.
The main dataset, Dataset A, was split in the classic 80/20 split, with 80% or 182, 850 observations
placed in the training set and 20% or 45, 713 observations in the test set. To avoid any kind of patterns,
the test observations were randomly chosen and then visually inspected to be spread out evenly over
the entire target space.
The test dataset was further subdivided into three separate test sets to create unbiased estimates of the
neural network architecture and final model performance. Observations used for preprocessing deter-
mination and feature selection were removed from the test dataset, before observations were randomly
assigned to one of the three subsets. Approximately 15, 000 observations existed in each test subset.
All three were visually inspected to ensure that they were spread out over the detector volume.
A general problem throughout the study was the slow learning of the network. One of the main reasons
for this was the small size of change in error when taking a step with the gradient descent. The moti-
vation for the hyperparameters concerned with the learning part of the neural network is found in the
following section. A way of circumventing this problem was found by only training a changing subset
of 80% of the training data at a time. Not only was the training time for any epoch cut down, as there
was a 20% decrease in the number of observations that was included in the training set at any given
time, but the number of epochs needed to reach a particular training error was reduced as well. This
method was especially efficient at the beginning of the training process in which the training error was
large. As the training error was reduced, it seemed that the benefit of the epoch clusters was curbed,
but no thorough investigation was performed.
8.0.3
Model design and evaluation
The main objective of this thesis was the design and evaluation of the neural network used to predict the
interaction positions of photons in the detector based on the features derived from a theoretical model.
As previously mentioned, the aim was not to produce the model with the overall lowest generalization
error, but instead to produce a model good enough to satisfy the resolution requirements.
Based on the nearest neighbour search investigation as well as the low noise experimental data, it was
assumed that the problem would not be too complex. The overall approach was thus starting out with
the most simple case and hereafter add on layers of complexity only when required.
The first task of this process was to make a decision regarding the hyperparameters. The loss function
was chosen to be the summed squared error (SSE) found in equation 6.8. The SSE is not very robust
against outliers, as the squared loss puts emphasis on bad predictions. As the data used is not noisy,
but ideal data from a theoretical model, this was not assumed to be a problem.
The learning rate was chosen manually through trial and error and was decided to be 1.5 · 10−4. The
learning rate was very sensitive to even small changes in the value, and at even 1.7 · 10−4, it would
quickly result in NAN values for the computed training error. Decreasing the learning rate resulted
in an even slower learning curve and even 50, 000 epochs did not manage to reduce the training error
significantly. At the later stages of the training process, it was briefly tested to see if changing the
learning rate would result in a quicker learning process. However, as no evident difference in test error
was proved, the learning rate was kept the same all the way through. As the above was not investigated
systematically, results could potentially be due to the random initialization of the training set. In the
future, this could be an area worthy of examination to cut down on training time.
The batch size was chosen to be 1000. No real investigation were conducted for the batch size, as
samples did not reveal any significant changes. A better choice would probably have been a much lower
==PAGE:72==
8 Discussion
65
value and an exponential of 2, as research seems to suggest that this speeds up the computation time
on GPU’s [24].
The activation function was chosen as the sigmoid function, and alternatives were not explored in this
study. The choice of activation function also resulted in a change in preprocessing method, as normalize
was exchanged for minmax. The minmax task used as the input interval of [−1, 1] was optimal for the
sigmoid function, and it was easier to transform the experimental data to this interval.
The next hyperparameters were found using K cross validation. Due to the time involved with the
training of a model and restrictions on the maximum allotted time for each job when run on the HPC
Clusters1, it was decided to train each of the configurations separately. For this reason, identical splits
were not used for the different configurations, which caused a certain degree of randomness between the
initializations. This should be kept in mind when comparing the generalization error and discussing
which hyperparameter is the best. The number of hidden layers was decided based on a training period
consisting of 50, 000 epochs. At this number, and especially for the more complex models, the learning
was not stagnant, but there was still an overall small training loss incurred for each new epoch. The
number of epochs was decided for practical purposes.
Training the models for the hidden layer determination was performed before the learning cluster method
and the cluster computing on the HPC nodes took place, so the training process was very slow. Four
different values for the number of hidden layers were included. An exhaustive search was too computa-
tionally expensive, and the advantage of the manual search was that the complexity of the problem was
more easily spotted than if using random search. Values of H above 20 were not systematically tested.
Sampling values for these H values did not achieve better errors. Additionally, both the training and
generalization error started to rise again at H = 20 in all modelled examples.
Looking at the generalization errors, the optimal number of neurons seems to be somewhere in the
vicinity of 5 or 10 neurons. Following the generalization error, the H = 10 option was chosen.
After all hyperparameters were decided upon, the network was trained for a total of 219 learning clus-
ters. The training and test error were tested repeatedly throughout the training process in order to
ensure that there was still a decrease in errors and that the generalization and training error did not
start to diverge from each other. If the training error continued to decrease while the test error started
to increase, this would be a sure sign of overfitting. This did not occur at any point during the training
of the final model. Hence, it is possible that continuing with training the model could manage to reduce
the generalization error even more.
8.0.4
Evaluation of models on 3D CZT detector data
The fourth and final milestone concerned evaluation of the developed model on experimental datasets
for the 3D CZT detector prototype. A 7-step normalization procedure was implemented to translate
the experimental data to the given input range of the training data from the theoretical model. This
process could be simplified in several ways.
Due to using the input values to the theoretical model as the targets for supervised learning, and not
the positions calculated on the charge loss compensated signals, a bias was inflicted on the y coordinate

real detector could also have been accounted for earlier in the process.

interval for the theoretical features. Rescaling each feature with respect to its minimum and maximum
training value allowed for the maximum usage of the activation function’s output. However, it proved
problematic when applied to the theoretical data. This was especially true for the cathodes located clos-
est to the edge of the detector. As the subset detector was centered around the middle of the detector,
none of the training or test observations occurred in a location that resulted in a great induced signal
on the edge cathodes. When the features were individually rescaled, the [-1,1] interval represented a
very narrow range in pulseheights on these cathodes. Small electrical noise signals imposed on the

positions predicted by using the positioning algorithms do not manage to fit the experimental data at
all. This basically renders the calculated error difference worthless, as the true position of the event
has not been established. The multiple peaks in the cathode signal for three of the observations are a
curiosity with no current explanation. Samples of observations complying with the resolution require-
ment did not reveal anything like it, so it might be the reason for the poor z coordinate determination.
Based on these findings, it seems safe to say that the four high error observations can not be used to
give any indication of the model performance as there are problems with both the ts determination and
the cathode signals. They are there not considered a part of the model evaluation.
During the evaluation process, it was discovered that between the x and z scans the channels for Cath-
ode 1 and Cathode 5 had been switched in the detector lab setup.
Comparing the performance on the experimental data to the theoretical test set, the simulation showed
superior performance.
This is probably due to the fact that at the present time, there are several
unmodelled differences between the pulse shapes from the theoretical model and the 3D CZT detector
prototype. An increase in position determination accuracy should follow as the model is improved.
==PAGE:74==
CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This thesis focused on creating a neural network capable of determining the 3D interaction positions
based on pulse shape features. The pulse shape features were derived from a theoretical model.
The created model was capable of predicting the position from experimental data from the 3D CZT
detector prototype located at DTU Space within 1mm obtained from slitbeam experiments. A total of
four observations in the three slitbeam measurements did not comply with the resolution requirement.
An investigation concluded that these observations were not good indicators of the models’ performance,
as both their time of interaction and cathode signals showed problems. The RMS of each of the exper-
imental datasets were 0.36mm for X slit, 0.11mm for Y slit and 0.31mm for Z slit.
Overall, the performance of the model on a theoretical test set was significantly better with an RMS for
the observations of 0.094. The increased error which occurred when applying the model to experimental
data was prescribed to deficiencies in the theoretical model when simulating the detector response and
to noise in the detector.
In the real data evaluation phase it was discovered that there had been a switch of the channels for
Cathode 1 and Cathode 5 in the timeframe between the slitbeam scans in the x and the z direction.
This was discovered by comparing the extracted number of events and plots of the cathode signals.
The hyperparameters concerned with learning in the neural network were chosen manually and gave a
batchsize of 1000, a learning rate of 1.5 · 10−4 and the sigmoid function used as the activation function.
The loss function used was the summed square error.
Hyperparameters concerning the structure of the neural network were chosen using cross validation
schemes.
The structure of the neural network had 2 hidden layers. These layers were determined by choosing the
lowest generalization error from a K = 5 cross validation. There were 20 neurons in the input layer, 10
neurons in each of the hidden layers and 3 neurons in the output layer. The number of neurons in the
hidden layers were determined from a K = 10 cross validation estimate of the generalization error.
The input to the neural network consisted of a collection of 20 features describing different properties
of the pulseshapes. With exception of the triggered anode number, the features were all pulseheights
of the induced electrode signals. To allow for the inclusion of properties concerning the drift electrodes
as features, a novel gain analysis was performed on the drift signals. The results were four conversion
factors which were used for transforming the real CZT detector drift signals to the model range for
evaluation.
The preprocessing task performed on the raw data was chosen as the normalize rescaling tool for the
feature selection, but for further analysis rescaling was done by the minmax tool.
A comparative analysis found severe discrepancies in the drift times between the simulated and real
data for a given interaction location. There was almost a factor of 2 difference, but regardless, the
pulseheights were still very much in agreement. Hence, the discrepancies were not suspected of having
any influence on the results. Experimental fits of one of the material properties, the µτ, managed to
almost perfectly fit the experimental and simulated pulseshapes for all 26 electrodes.

CHAPTER 10
Future work
This thesis sets the stage for the use of neural networks to provide real time position determination
for the prototype 3D CZT detector developed at DTU Space. This chapter presents a short outline of
future areas of interest for the neural network work with the detector.
10.1
Updates to the theoretical model
Currently, the greatest shackle for the advancement of the neural network applications to the 3D CZT
detector is the limitations imposed by the current theoretical model. Some of the most powerful updates
for improving the simulation are presented here.
Charge cloud size
Implementing a charge cloud would allow for both the finite drift time collection and the division of
charge carriers on adjacent anodes for near drift cell boundary events.
Energy
The current model is not sensitive to the energy of the photon and only simulates pulseshapes in a
unit interval. This results in a rescaling process requires a bothersome post processing of events, which
could be avoided if the model was rescaled to keV units. Even more unfortunate, there is no practical
way of simulating events of different energies currently, so the models can only be trained to predict for
one specific energy.
Multiple events
In the current Python model, it is only possible to simulate the response from a single interaction.
Implementing multiple events makes it possible to predict a sequence of interactions or simultaneous
events.
Inclusion of boundary regions
As a low rejection rate is desired, being able to include the events close the boundaries between drift
cells would be considered beneficial.
10.2
Neural network
Along with the updates to theoretical model, there are several ways the neural network and its training
process can be improved.
All anode pulseheights as features
In the collection of features used in this study, the features regarding the anode signal were the pulse-
height of the triggered anode and the triggered anode number. In the future, it would be preferable to
instead include the pulseheight of all anodes as 12 individual features. This would not change much for
the single cell single event pulses, as the signal is basically only induced on a single anode. However,
when implementing multiple events or even boundary regions, this would be a better option, as it would
keep the number of features constant regardless of interaction type.
Reducing training time
When the theoretical model has been updated to include energy and multiple events, the amount of
observations needed to achieve a given position resolution increases dramatically. Optimizing the neural

network with respect to the training time is then essential. Implementing the option of parallel compu-
tation and optimizing the hyperparameters even further should be given plenty of interest. Especially
the using a low-value, exponential of 2 [24] as the batchsize and implementing an automatic updating
algorithm for the learning rate could be promising.
