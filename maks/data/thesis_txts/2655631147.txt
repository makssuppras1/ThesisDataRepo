Abstract
To develop eﬀective and advanced wind farm control and management algorithm, identify and characterize
the wake aﬀecting a downstream wind turbine is a ﬁrst and necessary step.
Therefore, this thesis
investigated the possibilities of characterizing and estimate the wake deﬁcit proﬁle and the wake position
in the far wake from the dynamic rotor response, load, and acceleration signals of the wake-aﬀected wind
turbine by the implementation of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neurons.
As a ﬁrst step, the sensitivity of the dynamic rotor response, wake deﬁcit, width, and position were
studied for wind speeds of 8 and 16 m/s. The studies show a distinct relation between the lateral wake
center position and the tower top and bottom bending moments at both 8 and 16 m/s. The used wake
characteristics and high-ﬁdelity aeroelastic simulations are obtained from the Dynamic Wake Meandering
model implemented in HAWC2 for the NREL 5 MW reference wind turbine.
Based on the preliminary sensitivity study, diﬀerent LSTM neural networks were developed to predict the
wake deﬁcit, width, and lateral and vertical wake center position for wind speeds of 8 m/s. The results
showed an MAE of approximately 0.131 m/s and 0.996 and an MPE of 0.931 and 1.527% for predicting
the wake deﬁcit and width, respectively. Similarly, the ﬁnal LSTMs for prediction of the lateral and
vertical wake center position lead to an MAE of 45.16 m and 25.47 m and a bias (µε) of 3.46 m and −1.42
m, respectively.
Overall, the developed LSTM neural network showed promising results in estimating the wake characteris-
tics of interest based on the wake-aﬀected turbine’s dynamic response.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Over the last decades, our demand and interest in renewable energy have increased rapidly due to rising
environmental concerns and a growing need for aﬀordable, reliable, and clean energy worldwide. As a
result, the global investments in renewable power have increased from less than 50 to 280 billion USD
per year from 2004 to 2019 [1]. However, to meet the Paris Agreement’s climate goals1 adopted in 2015,
further acceleration of the energy transformation from fossil-based to zero-carbon is necessary [3].
Within the wind industry, the transition has led to a signiﬁcant increase in the number and sizes
of installed turbines in wind power plants, reducing the cost of installation, maintenance, land use,
etc. Therefore, modern onshore and oﬀshore wind farms consist of tens or hundreds of turbines [4,
5]. However, the small turbine spacing within large wind farms has a signiﬁcant downside. As wind
ﬂows through the upstream turbines, the energy extraction from the atmospheric ﬂow leads to lower
wind speeds and higher turbulence intensities downwind. This phenomenon is called "wind turbine
wake". Research has shown that the momentum deﬁcit and the increased turbulence may cause sig-
niﬁcant power reductions and considerably higher dynamic loads on the downstream turbines [6–10].
Therefore, Wind Farm Control (WFC) has become a growing research area in which the control of
the individual turbines is coordinated to account for inter-turbine aerodynamic interaction, to reduce
costs, and to improve overall power production. In general, the various WFC strategies can be cate-
gorized into three distinct methods: wake steering, axial induction control, and wake mixing [11–13].
Figure 1.1 | Aerial view of wake clouds at Horns Rev
2 oﬀshore wind farm. Photo: Henrik Krogh/Ørsted [14]
To develop eﬀective solutions, an accurate under-
standing of the wind inﬂow at the rotor plane is
vital. The necessary knowledge includes informa-
tion on the wind speed, wind direction, shears,
veer, ambient and wake induced turbulence, pres-
ence of impinging wakes, etc. For instance, to
redirect wakes away from downstream turbines
or reduce asymmetric loading from partial wakes
using wake steering, it is essential to know which
side of the downstream rotor is aﬀected and how
much the wake needs to be redirected.
Unfortunately, the required information on the
ﬂow within wind farms is limited. The standard
equipment is often reduced to one or two anemometers, and wind wanes located on either the nacelle or the
spinner. Thus, the wind inﬂow, given by the wind speed and direction, is only estimated at one point in
space, and it is then challenging to describe the wind characteristic across the whole rotor plane accurately.
More advanced wind inﬂow measurements can be obtained by lidars and other sensing technologies [15].
However, these are still relatively expensive and are mostly used for assessment, validation, and research.
To develop advanced wind farm control and management algorithms, we are therefore strongly in need of
practical, reliable, and aﬀordable methods to accurately describe the wind inﬂow across the rotor plane
and characterize the wake situation aﬀecting the downstream turbines.
1.2
State of the Art within Wind Sensing using the Rotor Response
As an alternative to existing wind speed and wind direction sensors, the concept of using the turbine rotor
response for estimating wind characteristics has been proposed by Van der Hooft and Engelen (2004) in

[16, 17] and later on by Bottasso et al. (2010) in [18] and Bottasso and Riboldi (2014) in [19]. One of
the probably most widely known techniques is the torque balancing estimator formulated by Van der
Hooft and Engelen (2004). In this case, the idea is to estimate the rotor averaged wind speed based
on a reconstruction of the aerodynamic torque from the measurement of rotor speed, pitch setting, and
electrical torque [16]. However, as the torque balancing estimator is limited to a global estimate of the
rotor eﬀective wind speed, the concept was extended to estimate both wind speeds, shears, and wind
misalignment in [18] and [19]. This is carried out by implementing additional equations for the dynamic
equilibrium of the turbine and considering the steady-state response. Overall, the concept has shown very
promising validation results in both simulations and in-ﬁeld measurements [20–23].
Recently, the concept was further extended to infer the presence of impinging wakes by Bottasso et al.
(2015, 2018) in [24] and [23] and to track the lateral center position by Cacciola et al. (2016) in [25]. In
Bottasso et al. (2015, 2018), impinging wakes are detected based on noticeable diﬀerences between the
estimated local wind speeds and turbulence intensities on the rotor disk’s left and right sections. However,
as shown by Schreiber et al. (2020) [21], the model has diﬃculties detecting periods in which the turbine
operates in the full wake as the wake deﬁcit on the left and right rotor plane are similar. Therefore, the
proposed technique can only detect partial wakes and is not able to estimate the precise position of the
wake center [21, 23, 24]. The linear model formulated in Cacciola et al. (2016) is based on the simpliﬁed
Larsen wake model, [26], the torque balance equation, [16], and a minimization function. The method has
shown promising results in simulations and wind tunnel test [25, 27]. However, the method is limited to
tracking the lateral center position and is yet to be tested on real ﬁeld data and measurements in which
wake meandering is present.
Therefore, this project aims to further investigate possible methods to best characterize and describe the
wake situation of a downstream turbine from the turbine loads and rotor response. A ﬁrst and necessary
step for advanced wind farm control and management algorithms. However, in contrast to, the found
literature, the focus are on the implementation on Artiﬁcial Intelligence and Artiﬁcial Neural Networks
(ANNs). The implementation of ANN has already shown promising results within data mining and power
forecasting. However, no current research on modelling and characterization of an impinging wake from
the dynamic rotor response of the wake-aﬀected turbine was found doing my literature review.
1.3
Research Objectives and Thesis Outline
The following research questions and objectives were deﬁned to investigate feasible models for the
characterization of an impinging wake based on the downstream turbine’s rotor response.
1. How does an impinging wake aﬀect the loading and accelerations of the downstream turbine?
• Which wake parameters work best to describe the turbine wake situation?
• What are the eﬀects of an impinging wake on the turbine loads and acceleration signals?
• Does the correlation between the chosen wake parameters and the rotor response and turbine
loads change signiﬁcantly for increasing wind speed and diﬀerent turbine control regions?
2. Can one accurately estimate the downstream wake meandering, the wake deﬁcit, and the wake width
from the operation, loading, and acceleration signal of a wind turbine operating in the far wake?
• What metrics should be consider regarding the accuracy and evaluation of the models?
• What are the eﬀects of the surrogate hyperparameters on the model accuracy?

2
Aerodynamics and Characteristics of Wind Turbine Wakes
This chapter presents the central parts of the theory behind wind turbine wakes and the ﬂow around a
single turbine. The description contains a short introduction to the general understanding and physics
behind the near wake, far wake, and wake meandering. The theory is then followed by a section and
introduction to the Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) model and its three main components to model
the wake expanding, attenuating, and meandering downwind. At last, the main wake characteristics are
discussed, and the wake characteristics of interest are presented.
2.1
Wake Physics
As the turbulent boundary layer interacts with a wind turbine, the ﬂow ﬁeld changes signiﬁcantly, both
upwind and downwind. In the upwind region, the produced thrust from the rotor causes a signiﬁcant
reduction in the wind speed as shown by Medici et al. (2011) and Troldborg and Forsting (2017) in
[29], and [30], respectively. The studies show that the region might extend more than 3 rotor diameters
upstream. The literature also referer to this region as the induction zone. The area downwind of the rotor
plane is described as the wake and is generally divided into two regions as illustrated in Figure 2.1. That
is the near wake and the far wake.
Figure 2.1 | Illustration showing the most characteristic instantaneous and time-averaged ﬂow features from the
interaction of a turbine and the incoming airﬂow. Source: Porté-Agel, Bastankhah, and Shamsoddin (2019) [31].
The Near Wake
The near wake is the region immediately downwind of the turbine and has a length of 2-4D typically.
The turbine presence strongly inﬂuences this region, and the region is characterized by a highly complex,
Page 4 of 104
==PAGE:15==
2
Aerodynamics and Characteristics of Wind Turbine Wakes
three-dimensional, and heterogeneous ﬂow distribution. Thus, the turbine characteristics, such as the
blade proﬁle, hub, and nacelle geometry, signiﬁcantly aﬀect the ﬂow ﬁeld in the near wake region [31].
Due to the complexity of the wake, various studies model the complex ﬂow ﬁeld with a uniform velocity
deﬁcit distribution in the central part and a varying deﬁcit in the side shear layers ([31] and ref. within).
This simpliﬁcation is illustrated in Figure 2.1 (dashed lines). Nevertheless, in [32], Krogstad and Adaramola
(2012) have shown that a speed-up region can be observed in the central part of the near-wake. Particularly
at higher tip-speed ratios in which only the midsection of the blades operates in an eﬃcient turbine mode
and the blades’ inner parts operate as a propeller and supply the additional energy to the wake center.
One of the most exciting features of the near wake is the generating and shedding of vortex structures at
the blade tip and roots. The vortex structures are caused by the pressure diﬀerence between the rotor
blades’ suction and pressure side. The distinct vortices break down as a vortex pairing occurs due to the
mutual inductance instability. As the vortices break down, the wake transitions become dominated by the
thrust and power coeﬃcient and the incoming ﬂow conditions. This transition indicates the shift from the
near wake region to the far wake [31].
Far Wake
Studies carried out by Meyers and Meneveau (2012) in [33] and Stevens et al. (2016, 2017) in [34, 35] has
shown that the optimal turbine spacing is approximately 10 turbine diameters or higher when including
the eﬀect of wake-related turbulence on maintenance costs. Nonetheless, the typical turbine spacing within
modern wind farms is in the range of 6 −10D [35]. The optimal turbine spacing is thus signiﬁcantly more
extensive than what can be observed in modern wind farms. As a result, wind turbines commonly operate
in the far-wake of upwind turbines.
Contrary to the near wake region, the far wake is less inﬂuenced by the rotor features and can be described
by more universal characteristics such as the thrust and power coeﬃcient and incoming ﬂow conditions
[31, 36]. Earlier studies have shown that the streamwise velocity proﬁles have an axisymmetric Gaussian
distribution and become self-similar in the far wake when assuming uniform inﬂow. However, the velocity
proﬁle loses its Gaussian shape in boundary-layer ﬂows due to the shear components and the ground’s
presence as shown later by Chamorro and Porté-Agel (2009) in [37]. Although, the wake deﬁcit proﬁles
(i.e., the diﬀerence between the incoming ﬂow ﬁeld and the wake) still retain the Gaussian distribution
in these cases. However, there is an abnormality at the wake edges, where studies have shown a slight
disagreement between the velocity-deﬁcit proﬁles and the assumed Gaussian distribution [31]. See Figure
2.1 and 4.2 in Sec. 4.1. As the wake moves downstream, the wake grows in both the lateral and vertical
directions due to the outer ﬂow’s entrainment. Simultaneously, the streamwise velocity increases until the
wake completely recover far downstream [31].
The far wake region is also characterized by high turbulence intensities and turbulent momentum ﬂuxes.
A double Gaussian distribution can best describe the high turbulence intensity under the assumption of
uniform inﬂow. However, when considering boundary layer ﬂow, the added turbulence intensity below the
hub level is suppressed due to the surface roughness. Similarly, one can observe an enhancement of the
turbulence intensity in the upper half of the wake due to the vertical shear and momentum ﬂux [38].
Wake Meandering
Another essential wake characteristic is the wake meandering. Wake meandering relates to the unsteady
oscillations and large-scale movement of the wake with reference to the time-averaged wake center. These
random oscillations were observed and incorporated into wake predictions as early as the 1980s by Ainslie
in [39]. The phenomenon leads to additional turbulence generation and can cause severe unsteady loads
and higher fatigue and extreme loads. As the wake moves downstream, the amplitude of the meandering
Page 5 of 104
==PAGE:16==
2.2
The Dynamic Wake Meandering Model
is sensitive to the incoming ﬂow conditions and increases. Studies show that the wake meandering is more
pronounced in the lateral direction than the vertical one [40, 41]. However, it does not seem like there yet
is a common understanding of what causes the diﬀerences between the lateral and vertical displacement
[31]. In [42], España et al. argue that this diﬀerence is due to the higher value of the standard deviations
of the lateral velocity σv than of the vertical σw in turbulent boundary-layer ﬂows. Whereas, Bastankhah
and Porté-Agel (2017) in [43] hypothesize that the diﬀerence is caused by the lateral meandering tendency
of very-large-scale motions present in the incoming boundary.
Within the research community, there is almost a uniﬁed agreement that wake meandering is induced by
the large turbulent eddies (» one rotor diameter) in the ﬂow ﬁeld [31]. The hypothesis has later been
conﬁrmed experimentally by España, Loyer, and Devinant (2011) in [44]. This study showed that wake
meandering does not occur without the existence of turbulent eddies signiﬁcantly larger than the rotor
diameter. Later, Larsen et al. (2008) [45] postulated that wake meandering is primarily the result of
large-scale air movements in which "the downstream evolution of the wake deﬁcit position can be modelled,
by considering the wake deﬁcit as being a passive tracer transported in a large-scale turbulence ﬁeld."
Thus, if knowing the incoming ﬂow’s low-frequency variation, one can model the random oscillations as a
passive scalar [31]. This research founded the basis of the Dynamic Wake Meandering model.
2.2
The Dynamic Wake Meandering Model
The Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) model is based on the assumption that the whole wake behaves
as a passive tracer and is advocated by large turbulent structures in the atmospheric boundary layer [46].
The model was ﬁrst formulated by Larsen et al. (2007, 2008) [45, 47]. Although, further improvements,
calibrations, and validations of the model for unsteady loads and power productions have later been made
and is presented in [48–50]. The DWM model comprises of three essential cornerstones: the velocity
deﬁcit, the added wake turbulence, and the wake meandering. Together, the three elements describe the
essential ﬂow characteristics downstream of a turbine. Figure 2.2 illustrate the main components and the
input and output variables of the DWM model.

Quasi-Steady Wake Velocity Deﬁcit
In the present formulation, the initial velocity deﬁcit is computed based on the induced velocities and
inductions in the rotor plane. The computations are based on Element Momentum (BEM) theory under
the assumption of a uniform inﬂow at the rotor. Therefore, the eﬀects from inﬂow wind shear proﬁles
or turbulence on the wake generation are excluded, and the velocity deﬁcit proﬁle is assumed to be
rotationally symmetric. Similarly, the initial wake expansion in the near wake, due to the pressure rise, is
calculated based on conservation of mass and momentum. The momentum and mass balance equations
are assumed to hold for stream tubes and not only streamlines to match the BEM approach [48].

The attenuation and downstream development of the wake deﬁcit in the far wake is mainly aﬀected by
turbulent mixing. In the present DWM model, the wake turbulent mixing is described based on the
boundary layer approximation of the Navier–Stokes equations, in which the pressure terms are disregarded.
As for the previous models, the Quasi-Steady Velocity Deﬁcit is formulated in the moving frame of
reference, and the deﬁcit proﬁle is assumed to be rotationally symmetric and steady [47, 48].
Added Wake Turbulence
The primary sources of the added wake turbulence are the wake shear and the generated tip and root
trailing vortices. These vortices initially take the form of organized, coherent ﬂow structures. However,
as the vortices gradually break down due to instability, they approach the conventional turbulence
characteristics and form high-frequency turbulence structures. Within the DWM model and HAWC2,
the added wake turbulence is modeled based on a scaled homogeneous Mann turbulence ﬁeld. Thus, the
additional turbulence box represents the micro-scale turbulence from the upstream turbine itself. As for
the wake deﬁcit, the wake added turbulence refers to the meandering frame of reference. Therefore, the
additional turbulence box’s center position follows the wake center position [48].
Wake Meandering
In the DWM model, wake meandering is primary considered to be the result of large-scale air movements
and "is based on a fundamental presumption stating that the transport of wakes in the atmospheric
boundary layer can be modeled by considering the wakes to act as passive tracers driven by the large-scale
turbulence structures" (Larsen et al. (2007))[47]. Taylor’s hypothesis is adopted to model the downstream
propagation of the wake deﬁcit. Thus, the downstream advection of the wake is assumed to be controlled
by the ambient wind ﬁeld’s mean ﬂow velocity in the longitudinal direction. The formulation is an essential
simpliﬁcation allowing for a decoupling of the wake deﬁcit and wake meandering.
The transportation of the wake in the lateral and vertical direction is based on a suitable stochastic
turbulence ﬁeld and a predeﬁned cut-oﬀfrequency. Similar to common practice for load quantiﬁcation,
the Mann turbulence box is a proper choice for modeling the stochastic turbulence ﬁeld. To obtain the
lateral- and vertical wake meandering, a course resolution in the plane perpendicular to the mean wind
direction of 1 rotor diameter can be implemented to ﬁlter away small-scale turbulence eddies. [47, 48]

Concluding Remarks on the Wake Characteristics of Interest
The literature review and description of the wake physics and the DWM model were carried out to evaluate
and determine which parameters best describe the wake situation. Diﬀerent characteristics might need to
be considered depending on whether the downstream turbine operates in the near or far wake and the
application. Within wind farm control, the relevant wake characteristics include wake center trajectory,
eﬀective wind speed, velocity deﬁcit, wake induced turbulence, upstream turbine characteristics, and
others. These have a negative inﬂuence on the power production and dynamic loading on the downstream
turbines [51].
However, as the turbines in large wind farms often operate the far wake of an upstream turbine with
a spacing of 6 −10D, only the wake situations with at a downstream distance of 7 rotor diameters are
considered in this project. Thus, the velocity deﬁcit proﬁle can be represented and described by the
self-similar Gaussian distribution given in Eq. 2.1 as the downstream turbine operates in the far wake.
Knowledge on the wake deﬁcit proﬁle downstream might be crucial when determining if further control
actions need to be taken to reduce the eﬀects on the power production and loads from impinging wakes.

3.1
HAWC2 Simulation Environment
HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd generation) is an aeroelastic code able to
simulate the wind turbine response in the time domain. J.T. Petersen initiated the ﬁrst generation of
HAWC in 1986. However, the central part of the current code in HAWC2 was later developed by the
Aeroelastic Design Research Program at DTU Wind Energy in Denmark between 2003 and 2007. Since
then, the aeroelastic code has been used in numerous research projects and industrial applications for
both design and veriﬁcation purposes [28].
For this project, HAWC2 is used to produce high ﬁdelity simulation data of the rotor response and the
correlated quasi-static wake properties.2 In particular, time series of the shaft rotation angle and speed,
the blade pitch angle, and the displacements, acceleration, and bending moments of various components
are collected. Similarly, based on the implemented DWM model, the time series of the wake meandering
and the axisymmetric wind speed deﬁcit proﬁle are obtained [53].
Implementation of the Dynamic Wake Meandering Model in HAWC2
The Dynamic Wake Meandering Model (DWM) presented in Section 2.2, is implemented in the HAWC2
aeroelastic code, and various parameters can be adjusted to analyze diﬀerent wake situations. First, the
implemented DWM model requires three Mann turbulence ﬁelds to represent the wake: the meandering
turbulence box, the ambient turbulence box, and the micro-scale turbulence box.
The micro-scale
turbulence box represents the turbulence from the wake of the upstream turbine itself. The necessary
structures and length scales of the Mann turbulence boxes are customized to match site-speciﬁc conditions.
Based on the description and example found in Larsen and Hansen (2019) [53], the length scale of the
added wake turbulence is set to one-tenth of the rotor diameter of 126 m. For the ambient and meandering
turbulence length scale, a value of 29.4 is recommended and therefore used. The deﬁned parameters and
dimensions of the three turbulence boxes for a wind speed of 4 m/s can be found in Table 3.1 and 3.2. For
each simulation, the turbulence seed is changed to generate diﬀerent ambient turbulent ﬁelds and wake
conditions.
Additionally, the separation distance, lateral position, rotational rate, and pitch angle of the wake-
generating turbine are speciﬁed in the aeroelastic code and can be adjusted. The upstream wind turbine’s
operation properties and position provide the required information to generate the initial velocity deﬁcit,
the expansion, and attenuation of the wake deﬁcit proﬁle based on the BEM theory, pressure gradient,
and Navier-Stokes equations. This is described shortly in Section 2.2 and more thoroughly in [47] and [49].
Another signiﬁcant key parameter is the turbulence intensity of the meandering ﬂow. The turbulence
intensity is also a site-speciﬁc parameter and should normally be matched to measurements. However,
as the turbulence highly inﬂuences the wake meandering amplitude, various turbulence intensities are
considered to obtain a larger variety of wake conditions for the simulations and models. Therefore, the

Assumptions in the Dynamic Wake Meandering model
When generating the wake deﬁcit and meandering, the assumptions made in the DWM model are vital to
identify, as they might inﬂuence the simulated loads and the wake parameters. This will be especially
important if or when the developed models within this project are evaluated on LES simulations and/or
ﬁeld test data. Therefore, some of the major assumptions made in the DWM model are outlined below.
• Uniform wind inﬂow with no shear component at the wake-generating turbine upstream
• A rotational and axisymmetric wake deﬁcit
• Flow velocities are equal to the ambient ﬂow velocity outside the wake’s outer radius
• The ambient turbulence does not change with height
• The wake deﬁcit translates downwind at a rate corresponding to the mean ambient ﬂow speed
Let us discuss these assumptions in more details. First, the actual vertical and horizontal shear in a
wind ﬁeld will cause additional wind shear components in the wake deﬁcit and lead to a non-uniform
and asymmetric velocity deﬁcit downstream. The additional shear components might also assist in the
recovering of the wake as it expands and attenuate downstream. Thus, the wind shear component caused
by the surface roughness might signiﬁcantly aﬀect the wake recovering. Similarly, based on Taylor’s
hypotheses, the wake deﬁcit is assumed to translate downwind at a rate corresponding to the mean
Page 10 of 104
==PAGE:21==

ambient ﬂow speed. This is a considerable simpliﬁcation allowing for a decoupling of the wake expansion
and transportation process. Velocity proﬁles from Lidar measurements and LES simulations show that
higher wind speeds can be observed near the wake center compared to the wind speed along the wake
radius in the near wake. In the far wake, the wake deﬁcit is almost Gaussian-shaped with the highest
wind speeds along the wake’s edges, and signiﬁcant smaller wind speeds at the center [54, 55]. Thus, the
velocity diﬀerence between the wake center and edge will further aid wake recovery as it increases the
turbulent mixing. Therefore, the simulated quasi-static wake deﬁcit in the DWM model might take longer
to recover as the described assumptions reduce the turbulent mixing components. The simulated wake
eﬀects on the rotor response might be more extensive compared to ﬁeld measurements, and a higher wake
deﬁcit and smaller wake width might be observed.
For the research carried out in this project, the assumptions should not have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence.
However, for future implementation in ﬁeld tests, the slower wake recovery in the DWM, might leads to
worse estimations of the wake center position as the wake eﬀect on the dynamic rotor response are smaller
in the ﬁeld test than the one simulated in HAWC2.

4
The Wake Characteristics and Turbine Rotor Response
As presented in the introduction, the overall objective of this project was to explore and investigate
possible methods and surrogate models for estimation of the characteristics of an impinging wake based
on the rotor response and operation of a downstream turbine. Therefore, a ﬁrst and necessary step is to
obtain a deeper understanding of the wake characteristics of interest, the sensitivity of the rotor response,
and how the presence of an impinging wake might inﬂuence the downstream turbine.
The analysis is divided into three main sections. In the ﬁrst section (Section 4.1) the focus is on the
sensitivity of the wake deﬁcit proﬁle and meandering to the inﬂow velocity and conditions. Therefore,
the section mainly concerns the wake characteristics based on a few individual simulations in which the
upstream turbine operates according to the steady-state operational references shown previously in Figure
3.1. Next, the of section-wise blade accelerations, loads and moments are considered and the intention
behind the computation of the loads is also summarized. Last, the eﬀects on the rotor response and
turbine operation from the impinging wake are considered in Sec. 4.3. In this section, the analysis of
the rotor response is based on the thousands of simulations carried out according to the simulation key
parameters summarized in Table 3.4 and 3.5. These simulations are also later used for the development
and training of the neural networks in Sec. 5.5 and 5.4.

The Wake Deﬁcit Proﬁle
Research has shown that the velocity deﬁcit caused by an upstream turbine has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on
both the power performance and the dynamic loading on a downstream turbine as discussed previously in
the introduction. Therefore, the maximum wake deﬁcit and the deﬁcit proﬁle, are very important wake
characteristics to consider when evaluating the turbine loading and rotor response. In Figure 4.1, the
normalized quasi-steady wake deﬁcit proﬁle, ∆U(r)/U∞, from the DWM model is shown for diﬀerent
downstream positions and diﬀerent time-averaged wind speeds of 8 (left) and 16 m/s (right). From the

ﬁgure, it is observed that the velocity deﬁcit and the gradients are largest at short downstream distances.
However, as the wake translates downstream, the wake deﬁcit decreases and expands due to the turbulence
mixing and breakdown of the blade tip and root vortices. For the applied downstream distance of 7D, a
maximum deﬁcit of 35% and 25% of the free stream velocity can be observed for the wind speeds of 8 and
16 m/s and turbulence intensities of 20.3% and 15.4%, respectively.
The extensive change in the velocity of 25% to 35% across the wake can cause uneven loads and leads
to higher fatigue loads of the turbine components. This is especially relevant for partial wake situations
in which the uneven loading across the rotor cause results in an increased cyclic loading. This was also
observed by Sagredo (2019) in her master thesis [59]. In the thesis, it was shown that the short-term
damage equivalent loads of the blade, shaft and tower diﬀer signiﬁcant based on the lateral wake position.
Furthermore, a smaller normalized wake deﬁcit is observed for time-averaged wind speeds of 16 m/s than
at 8 m/s in Figure 4.1. The trend is caused by the turbines operating at wind speed above the rated
velocity, and thus the upstream turbine is decreasing its generated thrust by pitching of the turbine
blades, as seen previously in Figure 3.1. By reducing the generated thrust, the wake generating turbine is
eﬀectively extracting a smaller percentage of kinetic energy from the atmospheric boundary later at 16
m/s than at 8 m/s which then leads to a lower normalized wake deﬁcit.

deviation between the deﬁcit proﬁles is assumed to be insigniﬁcant, and the small variation is neglected
within this project. Thus, this project will characterize the wake deﬁcit proﬁle with the maximum wake
deﬁcit Cx and the width σx.

Based on the time-series, we can observe that the amplitude of the lateral wake meandering is signiﬁcantly
more pronounced than the vertical one. For instance, the standard deviations of the wake centre in

the lateral and vertical wake meandering. One hypothesis is that the diﬀerence is caused by the higher
variation in the lateral velocity σv than in the vertical σw, as also shown by Figure A.1 in Appendix A.
Further more, the simulations show that the vertical wake meandering is dominated by smaller scale
ﬂuctuations than the lateral meandering.

are characterized by frequencies smaller than 0.033 Hz. Thus, the maximum frequency of the vertical wake

The Wake Meandering and Velocity Deﬁcit
To further visualize the velocity deﬁcit caused by the wake-generating turbine, time series of the rotor
average wind speed, the wind speed at the turbine hub, and the normalized velocity deﬁcit at the
downstream turbine is shown in Figure 4.5 for an averaged wind speed of 8 m/s and a turbulence intensity
of 20.3 %. In the ﬁgures, the lateral and vertical position are measured by the meteorological coordinate
system deﬁned in Figure 3.2 and normalized by the rotor radius R. The deﬁcit is extracted as the
diﬀerence between the turbulence ﬁeld with and without an upstream turbine. The ﬁrst two ﬁgures show
the deﬁcit in the lateral and vertical cross-planes centered at the hub, respectively, while the four lower
ﬁgures show the wake deﬁcit in the rotor plane after 10, 23, 267, and 553 s. In addition to the velocity
deﬁcit, the corresponding wake center position is illustrated by the solid line. In the last four ﬁgures, the
wake center position obtained by the DWM model is indicated by the crosses. The deﬁned wind inﬂow
conditions corresponds to a quasi-steady maximum wake deﬁcit of −0.35 U∞, and wake width of 0.85 R.
First, one has to be aware that while the lateral and vertical wake center location might be near the center
of the turbine rotor, the wake might relatively far away when considering the other coordinate. Therefore,
one can observe periods in which the velocity deﬁcit in the lateral or vertical cross-planes are very close to
0. This is for instance the case after around 580 s in which the wake is centered beneath the swept area of
the downstream turbine.
As the wake meanders substantially in both the lateral and vertical direction due to the large turbulent
eddies in the atmospheric boundary layer, it can be observed from the time series that the downstream
turbine might be exposed to both partial wake, full impingement, and almost no wake within just 10
minutes or less. For instance, after 267 s, the impinging wake covers the whole rotor plane and causes
velocity deﬁcits of minimum 6 % across the whole rotor plane when compared to the free-stream velocities.
This will undoubtedly lead to a noticeable reduction in the power production of the turbine. Similar after
10 and 23 s, the turbine experience periods with partial wakes. The partial wakes can cause uneven loads
and an increased cyclic loading as the velocity diﬀer signiﬁcant across the rotor disk. After 553 s, the
lateral wake center are located around 2 R to the right of the downstream turbine (seen from behind) as
seen previously in Figure 4.3. Therefore, the wake itself have almost no inﬂuence on the inﬂow velocity
at the rotor plane as seen by Figure 4.5 and the rotor response will only be minimal inﬂuenced by the
generated wake.

section-wise signals in the preliminary study is partly based on previous ﬁndings by other research groups
[23, 59]. Their results indicate that the wake position inﬂuences the section-wise blade accelerations, loads,
and moments signiﬁcantly. For instance, in [59] Sagredo (2019) shows that the blade root ﬂapwise force

Secondly, the idea of including the section-wise signals was further motivated by the signiﬁcant diﬀerences

below rated, while for wind speeds above rated it will lead to an increase of the bending moment. The
advantages of section-wise signals compared to the already measured tower top and bottom accelerations
and moments are that these signals are not dampened by the mass moment of inertia of the nacelle and
rotor. Similarly, estimation of the section-wise blade signals enables the possibility to estimate the eﬀect
on the loads at either the left or right section from the impinging and not only the overall impact on the
whole rotor plane.

bottom bending moments, and the shaft torsion generally increase as the impinging wake meanders
horizontally from the rotor center and outwards. The observed changes correspond to an increase in the
overall inﬂow velocity, loading, and aerodynamic thrust as part of the rotor disk becomes less inﬂuenced
by the impinging wake. In contrast to the tower bottom bending moments, the tower top tilt and yaw
moment are also inﬂuenced by the lateral direction of the wake meandering, as seen by Figure 4.12a. This
correlates with the distinct diﬀerences in the wind speed and aerodynamic loads between the left and
right sides of the turbine, dependent on the lateral wake location. For instance, as the wake meanders
from the center towards the right side (−), the tower top yaw moment increases due to increasing higher
wind speed on the left side of the rotor plane than on the right. However, as the wake is centered outside
the rotor swept area, the bending moments decrease as the relative velocity diﬀerence between the left
and right side is reduced.
The same distinct changes in the tower bending moments and shaft torsion for diﬀerent vertical wake
center positions were not be observed (see Figure 4.12b). Overall, the vertical wake position seems to
inﬂuence the tower top tilt moment the most, while almost no clear patterns can be observed for the
remaining bending moments. The small eﬀect on the tilting moment correlates with the relative higher
wind speed on the lower part of the turbine rotor than the upper section of the rotor. The diﬀerences in
wind speed then cause higher loads on the lower half and negative tilting moments.

5
Implementation of Deep Learning to Estimate
Wake Location and Deﬁcit Proﬁle
In this section, deep learning and artiﬁcial neural networks (ANNs) are implemented to explore the
possibilities of estimating the position and the deﬁcit proﬁle of an impinging wake based on the downstream
turbines’ dynamic response. In the considered neural networks, the unsteady patterns in the wake center
meandering, the wake deﬁcit proﬁle, and the rotor response are represented by Long Short-Term Memory
(LSTM) neurons. LSTM is a particular unit of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) capable of learning
long-term dependencies of time-series or sequences.
First, the core idea and architecture behind ANNs and LSTM are presented in Section 5.1 and 5.2,
respectively.
These sections are followed by a summary of the error metrics used to evaluate the
implemented neural networks’ architecture. At last, the established knowledge on the sensitivity of the
wake characteristics of interest and the turbine rotor response in the previous sections is used to develop
and evaluated various LSTM networks for estimation of the deﬁcit proﬁle and wake center location in
Section 5.5 and 5.4, respectively.

more reliable by increasing its generalization. Therefore, an algorithm is implemented in the training
process of neural network to adjust the weight and biases iteratively and boost the estimation performance.
The iterative process allows for ﬁne-tuning of the connection strengths based on the previous error. The
algorithm repeats the process until either the pre-deﬁned threshold or the deﬁned maximum number of
epochs is reached [65].
Throughout the last decade, the literature has proposed various training algorithms. However, the most
widely used and known method is probably the Back-Propagation (BP) training algorithm developed in
1986 by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams in [66]. The BP algorithm is used in LSTMs and can easily be
described by the following few steps.
1. First, the output value of each neuron in the artiﬁcial neural network is computed based on Eqs. 5.1
and 5.2 for each training instances. The forward pass is done one layer at a time until a prediction
of the last layer’s output is made. This process is also called forward propagation or forward pass.
2. Next, based on a pre-deﬁned loss function, the algorithm measures the network’s output error by
comparison of the desired output (yj) and the predicted output (ˆyj) of the model. The most popular
and used loss functions for regression problems are the mean squared error (MSE) and mean absolute
error (MAE) as expressed by Eqs. 5.3 and 5.4. n denotes the total number of outputs.

3. Then, propagating backward from the output layer to the input layer, the algorithm measures the

4. Finally, the BP algorithm applies an optimization scheme to perform a Gradient Descent step and
tweak the connection weight to reduce the error and ﬁnd the lowest feasible loss.
Two of the most well-known optimization algorithms are the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) and
the Adaptive Moment Estimation (Adam). SGD diﬀers from the regular gradient descent by updating
the parameters after each training instance instead of after each epoch. The frequent parameter update
enables the algorithm to jump to a new and potentially better local minimal. However, this also reduced
the chance of converging to the exact minimum, as the algorithm probably will keep overshooting. Adam is
an extension of the SGD algorithm. The most notable diﬀerence between the algorithms is the individual
adaptive learning rates for each parameter within Adam. As Adam generally has shown to converge to
lower error than SGD with some exceptions, only Adam was implemented and used in this project.
It is worth noticing that there is no guarantee that the obtained solution is the global minimum when
using a numerical scheme. Instead, it is generally believed that the stochastic gradient descent used in a
neural network might only converge to a local minimum or saturation points [67].
Trouble Shooting of a Trained Neural Network in Case of Under- and Over-ﬁtting
Similarly, under-ﬁtting or over-ﬁtting can cause poor performance of most machine learning models.
Therefore, it is worth considering whether the network is trained to its full potential, or whether there is a
signiﬁcant risk of the model being under or over-ﬁtted. Figure 5.3 illustrates an example of an under-ﬁtted,
optimal, and over-ﬁtted model. Under-ﬁtting relates to when a neural network can neither model the
training data nor generalize to new data. Thus, under-ﬁtting leads to relatively high errors on both the
training and test data. In these cases, it might be necessary to train the network for a longer period, try
diﬀerent neural networks, or increase the model complexity.

Over-ﬁtting can also arise if the number of network parameters - understood by the weights and bias - are

models in which a polynomial of N’th order can be ﬁtted to N +1 number of data points. However, ﬁtting

the ﬁeld of deep learning and big data, a mere approximation is that the required amount of training data
needed for a well-performing network is 10x the number of parameters in the model [68–70]. However, the
required amount of data might diﬀer signiﬁcantly from problem to problem depending on the variability
in the data, model complexity, and data noises. The limitation on the network size has a high eﬀect on
the possible design and architecture of LSTMs as they have signiﬁcant much more parameters to train
than the more simple (feed-forward) neural networks.
5.2
Recurrent Neural Network for Estimation of Wake Characteristics
Building on top of the simple ANNs, more advanced methods have been developed, such as Multilayer
Perceptrons (MLPs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), and
Long-Short Term Memory networks (LSTMs). Each model with diﬀerent advantages and disadvantages.
However, in this project, the main focus has been on the LSTM as the unsteady trends in the turbulent
inﬂow leads to highly ﬂuctuating time series of the wake meandering and the rotor response (see Figure
4.13 and 4.18 in Section 4.3). The LSTM algorithm is implemented in Python with the open-source
machine learning repository TensorFlow [71, 72].
LSTM was ﬁrst introduced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber in 1997 in [73] to address the vanishing
gradient problem existing in the traditional RNNs, in which the vanishing gradient might completely
stop the neural network from further learning. Thereby reducing the RNNs ability to learn long-term
dependencies. As for other RNNs, the hidden neurons in the LSTM mechanism share information on the
cell state Ct and hidden state ht based on a time index. The sharing process provides the processing units
with memory, in which the past states of the neuron can be utilized as input parameters when updating
the memory. Thus, sharing information helps the LSTM network recognize sequences by building memory
blocks of long time series. The model has shown to perform both faster and better for highly ﬂuctuating
time series than many other RNNs [65]. However, the LSTM model is a very challenging neural network
to train. Training LSTM especially becomes problematic on limited data as the network have a signiﬁcant
larger amount of networks parameters than the more simple RNNs and ANNs.

5.4
Estimation of the Wake Deﬁcit Proﬁle using Deep Learning
The ﬁrst developed LSTM neural networks is for the estimation of the wake deﬁcit proﬁle described by a
Gaussian distribution, see Equation 2.1, for average wind speeds of 8 m/s. Therefore, this section concerns
the development and evaluation of single-output and multi-output LSTMs to predict the maximum wake
deﬁcit C(x) and the wake width σx. Multi-output LSTMs are studied as C(x) and σx are strongly
correlated with each other and with almost the same operation parameters and rotor responses of the wake-
aﬀected turbine (see Figures 4.1, 4.10 and 4.11). Therefore, using multi-output networks can potentially
reduce the computational eﬀort as only a single LSTM has to be trained and used when computing the
wake deﬁcit proﬁle characteristics. However, using multi-output LSTMs over single-output networks might
be a disadvantage because the neural networks are trained based on the desired outputs’ joined evaluation
scores. Using multi-output ANNs can especially be problematic when modeling unrelated data, which
are best described by diﬀerent inputs and patterns in the LSTMs. Therefore, implementing multi-output
LSTMs can lead to worse estimates than the performance of two single-output neural networks in which
the LSTMs are trained to minimize loss of the two output independently.

5.5
Prediction of the Lateral and Vertical Wake Center Position Using LSTM
As previously mentioned, awareness of the lateral and vertical wake position is a necessity in order to
implement eﬃcient wind farm control strategies for re-direction of impinging wakes away from downstream
turbines. Therefore, this section concerns the development of neural networks for tracking of the wake
meandering, tuning of relevant hyper-parameters, and a ﬁnal evaluation of the developed and trained
LSTMs for wind speeds of 8 m/s. Similar to the developed LSTMs for estimation of the wake deﬁcit
and width, the performance of both single-output and multi-output neural networks are investigated in
the process. The use of multi-output LSTMs is considered as both the lateral and vertical meandering
are induced by the large turbulent eddies in the ﬂow ﬁeld [31, 44]. However, as there are signiﬁcant
variations and diﬀerences in the dynamic response of the wake-aﬀected turbine for lateral and vertical wake
meandering, (see Figure 4.11 and 4.13 in Section 4.3), it might be that the best performing LSTMs and
hyperparameters for prediction of the lateral and vertical wake center, respectively, diﬀers signiﬁcantly.
Data Pre-Processing and Training of the LSTMs
The ﬁrst step in the development of a neural network for estimation of wake center position was the
sensitivity analysis carried out in Section 4.3. The analysis was used to explore the relationship between
the wake meandering and the dynamic rotor response of an wake-aﬀected wind turbine. The study showed
that, especially the lateral wake center position can be described by the tower bending moments. Thus,
based on the study, the following turbine signals were chosen as input features to the LSTMs.
• Down-sampled tower bottom fore-aft moment MXT B [0.33 Hz]
• Down-sampled tower bottom side-side moment MYT B [0.33 Hz]
• Down-sampled tower top tilting moment MXT T [0.33 Hz]
• Down-sampled tower top rolling moment MYT T [0.33 Hz]
• Down-sampled tower top yawing moment MZT T [0.33 Hz]
In addition to the mentioned dynamic signals, the down-sampled and standard deviation of the electrical
power signal was also considered as a possible hyper-parameter of the LSTMs. The eﬀect on the LSTM
performance when including the power signal as input feature was explored, as the presence of an impinging
wake will lead to a lower power production and higher ﬂuctuations in the electrical power for operating
when compared to the undisturbed ﬂow.
Generally, the development, pre-processing, and training of the LSTMs for prediction of the lateral and
vertical wake center are very identical to the described process in Section 5.4 for the development of the
single-output and multi-output LSTMs for estimation of the wake deﬁcit and width. Thus, only the main
diﬀerences between the processes and the computations are summarized below:
• First, due to the higher complexity and dynamics of the wake meandering, deeper neural networks
were considered for the prediction of the wake center position than for estimating the wake deﬁcit and
width. Thus, the ﬁrst two architectures of the LSTMs consist of two hidden layers with respectively
60 and 20 neurons and 100 and 60 neurons in the two layers. The third architecture consist of
3 hidden layers with 60 neurons in the ﬁrst layer and 20 neurons in the second and third layer.
The overview of the performed grid search for the best architecture of the multi-output LSTMs for
prediction of the lateral and vertical wake center position are summarized in Table 5.6.
• Secondly, instead of normalising the input and outputs of the LSTM between (0,1), the turbine
signals were normalised between (-1,1) by implementation of the StandardScaler. The diﬀerent
normalization range was chosen as the lateral and vertical wake center position both takes positive
and negative values.

• Finally, preliminary studies of developed and trained LSTMs showed that the neural networks have
diﬃculties estimating the wake position, when the wake is located relatively far away from the
turbine rotor and does not signiﬁcantly aﬀect the rotor response. To minimize the eﬀect of these
wake situations on the performance of the trained LSTMs, the lateral and vertical wake center
position were restricted to within a distance of R + 2 Q95(σx) from the center of the wake-aﬀected
turbine rotor by clipping the values outside the interval to the interval edges. Here, R and Q95(σx)
express the rotor radius and the 95-percentile of the observed wake width. As the wake width σx
is given by the standard deviation of the Gaussian velocity deﬁcit proﬁle, (see Equation 2.1), the
factor of 2 are used to obtain the 95-quantile of the width of the wake deﬁcit proﬁle. Based on the
generated simulations at wind speeds of 8 m/s, the interval edges correspond to a distance of 2.7 R
from the turbine hub. The clipping of the lateral and vertical wake center can also be observed by
the distributions of the training, validation, and test dataset shown later in Figure 5.11 on page 56.

6
Conclusion and Recommendations
In this project, possible methods for characterisation and prediction of the wake deﬁcit proﬁle and the
wake location from the dynamic response of a wake-aﬀected turbine was investigated. The conclusion
drawn from the results of the study as well as suggestions for futures work is presented in this chapter.
6.1
Conclusion
To develop eﬀective and advanced wind farm control and management algorithm, an accurate understanding
of the wake aﬀecting a downstream wind turbine is a ﬁrst and necessary step. Therefore, with this study,
I try to shed light on the possibilities of using the dynamic response, loads and acceleration signals of a
wake-aﬀected turbine to characterize the impinging wake. For this study, the simulations of the wake
characteristics and the dynamic response of the downstream turbine (NREL 5MW) are based on the
DWM model implemented within HAWC and obtained for both wind speeds of 8 and 16 m/s.
Following the outlined research objectives presented in the introduction (see Section 1), the study was
carried out by three main steps. First, the wake characteristics of interest was determined based on the
aerodynamics of wind turbine wake and the data available from the implemented DWM model in HAWC2.
As the turbines within large wind farms often operates in the far wake, the chosen wake characteristics
were; the wake deﬁcit proﬁle characterised by its maximum wake deﬁcit C(x); and wake width σx along
with the lateral and vertical wake center position.
Sensitivity of the Dynamic Rotor Response
Secondly, the sensitivity of the dynamic rotor response of a wake-aﬀected turbine to the wake characteristics
was investigated for wind speeds of 8 and 16 m/s. For both 8 m/s and 16 m/s, the study showed distinct
correlation between the lateral wake center position and the tower bending moments. Less distinct
relations were observed for the wake width, wake deﬁcit and vertical wake center position based on 2D
density. However, the computes Pearson correlation coeﬃcients indicated relative high correlation (> 0.4
and > 0.2) between the wake deﬁcit proﬁle characteristics and the standard deviation of the tower top
moments, fore-aft displacement, and fore-aft accelerations.
Some distinct diﬀerences between the sensitivity of the dynamic rotor response at wind speeds of 8 m/s
and 16 m/s were also observed. The diﬀerences are mainly related to diﬀerent operation strategies of the
NREL 5 MW RWT for wind speed below and above 11.4 m/s. Thus, at 8 m/s, it was observed that the
presence of an impinging wake led to a lower electrical power, whilst an impinging wake at 16 m/s led to
smaller pitch angles.
Development of LSTMs for Prediction of Wake Deﬁcit Proﬁle and Wake Position
Finally, the developed knowledge on the sensitivity of the rotor response due to the presence of an
impinging wake was used to develop Long Short Term Memory (LSTM) neural networks for prediction of
the wake deﬁcit proﬁle and wake position for wind speeds of 8 m/s.
For the prediction of the wake deﬁcit and width, the considered inputs to the LSTMs were: the electrical
power, the shaft torsion and the 3 s standard deviation of the tower top tilt and yaw moments along
with the standard deviation of the tower top fore-aft displacement and acceleration. Based on initial grid
search of the architecture of the LSTM for prediction of the wake deﬁcit and width, it was observed that,
both multi-out and single-output LSTM performed equally well. The ﬁnal multi-output LSTM has 3
hidden layers with 20, 20 and 20 neurons respectively, and led to a MAE of approximately 0.131 m/s and
0.996 and a MPE of 0.931 and 1.527% for prediction of the wake deﬁcit and width, respectively. Thus,
the developed model has a slight tendency to overpredicts the actual wake deﬁcit and width.
To estimate the wake position, the tower top and bottom moments were used as inputs to the developed
LSTMs. Additionally, the eﬀect of including the power signals on the LSTM performance were also

Conclusion and Recommendations
investigated. Based on initial grid search of the architecture of the LSTMs, is was observed that the
lateral and vertical wake center position are best described by two individual LSTMs and diﬀerent

position lead to a MAE of 45.16 m and 25.47 m and a bias (µε) of 3.46 m and −1.42 m, respectively.
To conclude the thesis, the results of the study, clearly indicates that there is very likely would be possible
to estimate the wake deﬁcit, the wake width, and the wake center position of a single wake based on the
dynamic response of a wake-aﬀected turbine. However, as the studies only consider the impingement of a
single wake, are carried out for only a downstream distance of 7 D, only tested on a average wind speed
of 8 m/s and are based on the quasi-steady wake deﬁcit computed by the DWM model, there are still a
long way to a ﬁnal implementation in advanced wind farm control algorithm.
6.2
Further Research
This section contains ideas and reﬂection on how this research subject can be investigated further. The
ideas are partly based on the limitation within this project due to time constraints along with some of the
problems observed when evaluating the performance of LSTMs neural networks.
• During the time period of this research, there were unfortunate not enough time to also develop and
evaluate LSTM neural networks for wind speed at 16 m/s. Therefore, it could in future study be
interesting to see if the same potential can be observed for operation at rated power production.
• Seen in hindsight: For further training of similar neural networks based on high-ﬁdelity simulations
obtained from HAWC2 and wake deﬁcit proﬁles from the DWM model, if might be relevant to train
the LSTM on simulations for diﬀerent wind speeds than just 8 m/s. This will leads to a larger
number of diﬀerent wake deﬁcit proﬁles and the model might improve its performances and increase
is generality to predict the actual dynamic wake deﬁcit caused by the dynamic behaviour of the
inﬂow conditions.
• For the prediction of the lateral and vertical wake center, it was observed that they were best
described by two individual LSTMs and diﬀerent hyper-parameters. Therefore, for further research,
it might be relevant to investigate if the performance of the LSTMs will increase with completely
diﬀerent inputs signals for the prediction of the vertical and lateral wake center position, respectively.
• For the estimation of the wake center position, it might be relevant to consider other options than
clipping the wakes located far from the turbine rotor to a predeﬁned distance. Instead one might
consider estimating the wake characteristics in two steps. First, a neural network is design and train
to determine if the downstream turbine is aﬀected by a impinging wake. If the models predicts that
the turbine rotor response are signiﬁcant eﬀected by the wake, the wake characteristics can then be
estimated using similar LSTMs to the ones developed in this project.
• Last, if looking further into section wise loads, it might be relevant to look into a weighted function
instead of the implementation used within this study. An possibility might be an uniform distribution,
such that the highest weight is given for the loads directly up, down, left, or right and decreases as
the blades get further away from the lateral and horizontal axes.
