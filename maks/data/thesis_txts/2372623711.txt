PREFACE 
This Thesis was written to obtain a Master degree in the field of Architectural Engineering. 
It is the completion of a two years Master programme proposed by the “Brussels Faculty 
of Engineering (Bruface)” which is the result of the collaboration between the “Université 
Libre de Bruxelles (ULB)” and the “Vrije Universitiet Brussel (VUB)”. The study was 
performed at the “Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU)” in the scope of a one year 
Erasmus+ exchange programme.  
 
The work is dealing with Greenlandic building blocks, dating from the 50’s and 
constituting part of the outdated housing stock of the country. It consists in a preliminary 
study of the current state of the buildings and in guidelines for their transformation. The 
main focus is given to designs proposals for a new envelope aiming to improve the 
thermal performances and indoor environment of the blocks.  
In parallel, the thesis is also discussing the issues of an efficient heating and ventilation 
system installed in Greenland but without going into deep considerations.  
 
The study started on the 23rd January 2017 and was submitted on the 14th June 2017. 
Part of the work consisted in a fieldwork of one month led in two Greenlandic cities. The 
first one was Sisimiut from 31st January 2017 to 21st February 2017 while the second was 
Nuuk from 21st February until 28th February 2017.  
 
The supervision of the project was assured by Søren Peter Bjarløv and Egil Borchersen.  
 
 
 
Kongens Lyngby, 13th June 2017

ABSTRACT 
The aim of the thesis was to propose renovation solutions for part of the outdated 
Greenlandic housing stock, composed of multi-storey building blocks dating from the late 
50’s to 70’s.  The transformation process focused on the design of a new building 
envelope which intended to improve the airtightness and thermal performances of the 
buildings in order to enhance their energy efficiency and indoor climate. This project 
intervenes as an alternative, sustainable, proposition to the future plan of the 
government of Greenland which is to demolish these old dwellings to build new 
accommodations. Based on literature data, fieldwork and calculations, the study tried to 
deal with the different aspects of the transformation procedure. For this reason, the 
investigations on the buildings blocks were discussed as well as the reorganisation of the 
structure and preparation phases. Most importantly, design proposals for the different 
parts of the buildings envelopes were developed and compared in order to provide 
efficient solutions applicable all over the country. As a result, many deficiencies were 
detected in the current skins of the buildings. The investigations also revealed the 
important impact of the block’s inhabitants on their inner environment. To solve the 
encountered issues, the potential interest of the use of sandwich panels was highlighted. 
Further investigations were then needed to check the applicability and resistance of the 
design to the Greenlandic conditions imposing challenges in the setup of the elements. It 
also appeared that when designing more efficient skins for the blocks, both performant 
ventilation and heating systems were needed to assure a healthy inner environment. In 
a word, this thesis opened the possibility to sustainably transform a quite significant 
amount of buildings by providing general guidelines, results on investigations and design 
proposals giving good resistance and thermal performances. 
 
Key words: Greenland, transformation, building envelope, design proposals, sustainable.

DANSK RESUMÉ 
Formålet med dette kandidatspeciale har været at opstille løsningsforslag til renovering 
af den forældede grønlandske boligmasse, som er sammensat af bygningsblokke i flere i 
etager, der er opført fra sidst i 50’erne frem til 70’erne. Udviklingsprocessen har været 
fokuseret på at designe en ny klimaskærm, som har til formål at forbedre bygningernes 
lufttæthed og termiske ydeevne. På denne måde opnås energibesparelse og bedre 
indeklima. Dette projekt fremstiller et bæredygtigt alternativ til den grønlandske 
regerings plan om at rive disse gamle bygninger ned for at skabe nye boliger. Baseret på 
videnskabelige artikler, feltarbejder og beregninger behandler denne afhandling 
forskellige aspekter i transformationsproceduren. I opgaven diskuteres undersøgelser af 
den grønlandske boligmasse såvel som reorganiseringen og forberedelsen af 
bygningsstrukturen således. Den vigtigste del af opgaven har været at udvikle og 
sammenligne forslag til design af de forskellige dele af bygningernes klimaskærme, for 
således at kunne opstille effektive løsninger, som vil kunne anvendes i hele Grønland.  
Som resultat af disse undersøgelser opdagedes flere mangler ved bygningernes 
nuværende ydre flader. Undersøgelserne viste ligeledes, at bygningernes beboeres 
opførsel har stor indflydelse på indeklimaet i bygningerne.  For at løse disse problemer 
blev den potentielle interesse for brugen af sandwichpaneler fremhævet. Videre analyser 
var herefter nødvendige for at undersøge, om designet ville være anvendeligt og 
modstandsdygtigt under grønlandske betingelser, hvor der især er udfordringer 
forbundet med at opsætte bygningernes elementer. Det fremgik også, at det er 
nødvendigt at indføre effektiv ventilation samt varmeanlæg for at sikre et sundt 
indeklima, når der designes nye klimaskærme til bygningerne. Kort sagt har dette speciale 
åbnet muligheden for at transformere et betydeligt antal bygninger på bæredygtig vis 
ved at opstille generelle retningslinjer, resultater og designforslag, der giver god 
modstandsdygtighed og termisk ydeevne. 
 
Nøgleord: Grønland, transformation, klimaskærm, designforslag, bæredygtighed.

RÉSUMÉ FRANÇAIS  
L’objectif de ce mémoire était de proposer des solutions de rénovation pour une partie, 
aujourd’hui obsolète, du parc immobilier groenlandais composée de blocs de logements 
à plusieurs niveaux, construits de la fin des années 50 aux années 70. Le processus de 
transformation s’est focalisé sur la définition d’une nouvelle peau pour les bâtiments 
d’habitation, ayant pour objectifs d’améliorer leurs étanchéité et performances 
thermiques et cela afin de pouvoir réaliser des économies d’énergie tout en fournissant 
un environnement intérieur sain. Ce projet intervient comme une alternative durable au 
plan actuel du gouvernement groenlandais de démolir ces habitations pour en construire 
de nouvelles. Basée sur des articles scientifiques, un travail sur le terrain et des calculs, 
l’étude essaye de considérer les différents aspects du processus de transformation des 
logements. Pour cette raison, les recherches effectuées sur les bâtiments ainsi que la 
réorganisation et préparation de leur structure ont été discutées. Plus important encore, 
les nouvelles propositions de conception des différentes parties des enveloppes des 
constructions ont été développées et comparées dans le but de fournir des solutions 
efficaces et applicables à travers tout le pays. Dans les résultats obtenus, de nombreux 
défauts ont pu être détectés au niveau de la peau actuelle des bâtiments et les 
recherches ont révélé l’impact important que les résidents des blocs pouvaient avoir sur 
leur espace de vie. Pour résoudre les problèmes rencontrés, l’utilisation de panneaux 
sandwich a semblé être une solution prometteuse. Des recherches plus détaillées ont 
ensuite été nécessaires pour vérifier l’applicabilité et la résistance des assemblages 
proposés aux conditions groenlandaises qui imposent de nombreux défis dans 
l’installation des éléments. Il est aussi apparu que la définition d’une enveloppe de 
bâtiment plus efficace impliquait l’implémentation d’un système de chauffage et de 
ventilation adaptés, assurant un environnement intérieur sain. En un mot, ce mémoire a 
ouvert la porte aux possibilités de transformer durablement un nombre assez important 
de bâtiment d’habitation en donnant des directives générales, des résultats de recherche 
et des propositions de conception offrant une bonne résistance et de bonnes 
performances thermiques. 
 
Mots clés : Groenland, transformation, enveloppe de bâtiment, proposition de 
conception, durable.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1. CONTEXT 
Today’s world is facing a major challenge. Indeed, the population and the energy 
consumption are growing while fossil energy stocks, which highly contributed to the 
development of our present society, are decreasing quickly.  
Therefore sustainable buildings are becoming more and more important and are a key 
factor in the fight against climate change. Their implementation is supported by national 
and international regulations, because they allow to save natural resources and tend to 
use renewable energies [1]. 
 
The concept of sustainability can be applied to both new construction and renovation 
projects. A building is considered sustainable when it aims to reduce its impact on the 
environment and to reduce or cancel its consumption of fossil energy thanks to informed 
design proposal, renewable energies, natural and local resources. At the same time, 
better comfort and quality spaces should be provided to the occupants of the 
construction. These users, will also have a huge impact on how the building is working 
and performing. Therefore, their behaviour and culture has to be considered when taking 
design decisions and integrate them as part of the project. This creates a better design 
adapted to their needs. Integrating the occupants in the design process also allows the 
designers to propose alternatives ways of living to the users and to inform them on how 
the project can be used in an optimal way. In addition, aesthetic aspect, durability and 
resistance remain important factors that must be taken into account and improved. 
Other essential elements are time and life cycle of the building. From the very first step 
of the project, until the end of the life of the building, all decisions should be taken in 
order to improve the environmental aspects with, for example, the use recyclable 
materials. Indeed, the reuse or transformation of the materials and their future after the 
lifespan of the building will have an important impact on the environment [2]. 
 
In cold and dry climate region (negative annual mean temperature [3]) such as Greenland 
and Artic regions in general, the awareness for sustainable architecture is quite new. The 
building traditions in those regions gave birth to poorly insulated buildings with a low 
airtightness. For example, traditional residential buildings in Greenland often have 
problems with draughts, uncomfortably low indoor temperatures and inadequate 
ventilation [3]. As a result, the buildings reduce dangerously the available natural 
resources, need a large amount of energy (mainly produced with oil) for heating and 
operation and provide poor thermal comfort and indoor air quality (IAQ) [1] [3] [4]. 
 
In this context, Greenlandic authorities have to deal with their old housing stock. In the 
late forties, new types of buildings were built in Greenland, on the initiative of the Danish 
Government. They created housing blocks to accommodate the growing population and 
bring cities to “the modern world”. Today, one can realise that these blocks are outdated 
and need a radical change. This is why it was proposed to demolish the old buildings in 
order to create bright new accommodations.  
However, another proposition could be to keep the structure of those buildings that 
belong to Greenlandic landscape and history. This would result in decreasing the need 
for importation of construction materials which are rare and expensive in the country. 
==PAGE:28==
2 
 
1.3. OBJECTIVES 
The objective of the project is to propose a methodology bringing solutions for 
renovation and transformation of a part of the outdated Greenland’s housing block stock, 
in the hope to preserve this part of the patrimony of the country. The transformation is 
achieved by the development of a new façade system that should improve the 
airtightness and energy-performances of the buildings. In addition, the layout of the 
blocks has to be adapted to correspond to the current needs of the population and to 
offer a better quality of space.   
 
1.2. MOTIVATIONS 
There were different reasons to decide to work on this project. First, Architecture and 
Engineering in Greenland offer new challenges and the opportunity in a region which is 
developing more and more. The extreme and unusual climatic conditions can help to 
have a better understanding on how a building works when inner and outer conditions 
are diametrically opposed. Indeed, the outdoor climate in Greenland is most of the time 
dry and cold while the indoor climate should provide and healthy environment for the 
inhabitants with a relatively constant comfortable heat and percentage of relative 
humidity. Secondly, the project gives the chance to deal with a question that is often 
asked in our society, “Should we preserve and transform an old building or should we 
demolish it for something new?” Today we are consciences that our resources are not 
infinite and reuse materials and preserve existing structure is a sustainable way of 
thinking that should be promoted.  
 
1.4. APPROACH 
Once the project was proved to be relevant, this thesis was developed following a trip of 
one month in Greenland. This journey gave the opportunity to visit two cities of the 
country, to collect data, to discuss with engineers working from Greenlandic based 
companies and to discuss with the native and immigrant population. A lot of experience 
and knowledge was gained from this trip and were completed by a literature review and 
research.  
The transformation of the buildings mainly focused on the design of new envelopes. 
Indeed, when designing sustainable buildings, the façade is a key factor of the project. 
These new façades had to improve the energy performance of the blocks and integrate a 
system to operate the solar potential of Greenland while respecting the architectural 
identity of the country. In addition, the ventilation system was discussed. To evaluate the 
performances of the proposed solutions, assessment methods and current standards 
were used. Throughout the process of developing a new envelop, the materials were 
assessed to ensure their performances and their capacity to be reused or transformed 
for further modifications in the buildings. Important to mention, the connections are 
fundamental when designing buildings. They keep the different parts of the project 
together and improve the airtightness of the external envelop. This last parameter is 
essential, especially in extreme climates, to control the energy efficiency and indoor 
climate of a project.  
To conclude and illustrate the project, it was decided to apply the results from the 
research to a concrete case. This case study (described in Chapter 8) was to support the 
project and prove its feasibility.

2. FROM DETACHED HOUSES TO HOUSING BLOCKS 
The objectives of this chapter are to understand in which context the buildings blocks 
were built and to verify the relevance of the project that is develop around them. To do 
so, an introduction to the blocks is given through a brief history explaining the 
architectural and engineering evolution in Greenland. Then, by attempting to list the 
buildings blocks spread over Greenland, the relevance of the project should be proved 
because the concepts and methods develop can be applied to a significant buildings 
stock. Additionally, dressing a list should also highlight the different existing typologies 
with the aim to classify them and determine design prescription adapted to each 
category. Finally, to evaluate the interest of the project, the pros and cons of the buildings 
blocks should be discussed.  
 
2.1. ARCHITECTURAL EVOLUTION 
For a long time, Artic regions were considered as hostile regions only occupied by small 
indigenous communities, with their own vernacular architecture (turf huts, tents and 
sometimes igloos [5]), spread over many settlements. However colonisation of those 
spaces (for resources exploitation) gave new opportunities to develop housing units and 
architecture. This resulting in the most typical and well-known housing type of Greenland, 
imported from Scandinavia, namely the bright coloured wooden house, which finds its 
origin in the colonisation of the region [6].   
 
In the 50th, the Danish government decided to invest in the development of a new 
housing stock for Greenland, starting with single family houses. The aim was to improve 
the health and daily life of the population by providing modern comfort [7]. At the same 
time, the authorities have estimated that it became too expensive to maintain and supply 
all the small settlements present in Greenland. Therefore they decided to bring together 
the population from the small settlements in larger towns. As a consequence, the need 
for accommodation increased really fast in these towns and the “Housing block” model 
(Figure 1) was imported to Greenland from Denmark to satisfy the growing demand for 
housing [8]. This was an enormous change in the way of living of the native populations 
who had no choice but to adapt to this European model. At first hated and perceived as 
Danish colonialism [8], these building blocks slowly fitted in Greenlandic culture and 
nowadays the population is generally attached to this part of its history.  
 
Today the interest in developing new architecture for the north is growing. The region is 
urbanising fast and the population is moving from the settlements to the cities [9]. Sadly, 
this urbanisation process lacks sometime sensitivity for the culture when designing and 
planning new projects. This interest is guided by the potential and the complexity of the 
region which enjoys a large cultural and social diversity coming from its history 
(colonialism, post-colonialism, resources extraction…) [6]. This development is 
particularly visible in Nuuk, the capital of Greenland. There, an entire neighbourhood, 
Qinngorput, planned in 2000, is still under construction and has the purpose to develop 
the city and to accommodate the growing population of Nuuk [10]. By this development, 
city is decided to affirm its status of capital. The area holds services, housing and cultural 
facilities. The accommodation buildings present at the moment were all conceived as 
modern building blocks up to 7 storeys tall.  
==PAGE:32==

Figure 1: Housing block under construction in Nuuk, 1966-68, [11] 
 
When talking about architecture in arctic, it is difficult to ignore the importance of 
engineering. Indeed, architecture in this region was highly linked to engineering through 
history. The extreme climate conditions (low temperature, wind, snow, permafrost) and 
the isolation of many population pushed designers and engineers to develop convenient 
solution to adapt buildings. Therefore architecture was seen as an optimised system, 
mainly constituted of prefabricated and imported elements, that had to prove its 
efficiency and was develop in parallel with the cold climate design field in engineering [6]. 
Nevertheless, in the process to adapt buildings in Greenlandic conditions, the 
considerations for energy savings were poor or inexistent due to the low oil prices in the 
country.  
 
Until now, it can be said that Greenlandic architecture was mainly imported from 
Denmark and slightly adapted to the conditions of the arctic. Nevertheless, a lot of 
progress was made in the understanding of the region. Researchers across the world 
conducted studies about new materials, geology, climate and indoor climate in 
Greenland, solar potential of the country ... The results should help to develop the local 
architecture, adapted to the needs of the population and well integrated in its context 
which is a challenge for architects and engineers. On a larger scale, Greenland could 
beneficiate from the architectural and engineering innovations developed in other 
extremely cold regions such as Alaska [12], Canada, Norway [13], Sweden, Finland, Russia 
and Antarctic.

2.2. ENGINEERING EVOLUTION 
At the present time, new energy efficient technologies that aim at providing better 
energy performances and healthier indoor environment are not commonly used in new 
Greenlandic buildings [4]. However, pushed by the rise of energy prices and the rise of 
the demand for healthier and more comfortable spaces, the idea of implementing such 
technologies is slowly growing and interests more and more researchers.  
 
Although the interest is growing, the challenge remains important for three principal 
reasons. Firstly, the climate which highly differs from traditional European climate. The 
temperature are extremely low, the winds are strong and cold and the sun is absent or a 
low angle during a large part of the year. This results, for example, in very low solar gains 
through windows in winter while they are traditionally used in passive buildings. 
Secondly, most of the time, people involved in the design process have a lack of 
knowledge and experience in recent technologies. Consequently the systems are not 
always properly installed and used [1] [3]. Finally, the lack of updated Greenlandic 
building regulations delays the implementation of the new technologies. 
 
When reviewing relevant literature and sources it appears that there is only a small range 
of existing buildings in arctic conditions which are energy-efficient. The list below (Table 
1) present some examples that can be considered as exemplary buildings when it comes 
to design in Greenland and more broadly, in polar conditions [1]. These models are 
regularly studied and evaluated by researchers. As an illustration, the performances of 
the Low-energy house of Sisimiut are reported every year since its construction [14]. This 
allows scientists, researchers, designers and students to understand how the buildings 
works and how to improve the implemented systems that ought to ensure thermal 
comfort and good IAQ while saving energy.

new models are still under construction. This resulting in a large variety of block spread

and the rhythm of its façade. The most famous block of this type was the building block

If the 9 categories of blocks are considered to be from the same typology, this is because 
they were built on the same principles. In fact, each block is made of a loadbearing 
concrete structure composed of parallel walls and slabs casted on site [11]. They form a 
repetitive pattern by either using the same dimensions for the spans or by alternating 
large and small spans along the length of the building. This pattern can easily be seen on 
the façade which is covered with prefabricated elements. The length of the buildings is 
not fixed and varies depending on the locations. Actually, the blocks could virtually be 
infinite in their longitudinal direction since this dimension is determined by the number 
of repetition of the structural pattern. About the height, it can vary from 2 to 7 floors.  
As such, the structural system enables to have a non-loadbearing façade along the long 
edges of the buildings. Therefore, it is possible to remove it and replace it with a new 
envelope without arming the main structure.   
It is important to note that the loadbearing structure should not be subject to large 
modifications. Indeed, the aim of the project is to preserve the structure as much as 
possible in order to save material, time and energy in the transformation process. Most 
of the bearing walls will thus be kept and only some openings should be performed to 
enable the installation of new technical systems, to make possible the modification of the 
accesses to the different flats and to provide qualitative leaving spaces.  
 
The definition of the 9 categories was based on freely selected criteria that were common 
to different groups of buildings. Those criteria were defined based on the overview of the 
Greenlandic cities through google maps and google street view but also based an 
additional literature research [11] [7]. This research provided documentation including 
plans, sections, material lists and pictures which permitted to refine the selection of the 
criteria and their definition in the different categories. The list below gives the criteria 
that were selected: 
 
- 
The number of storeys 
- 
The materials used  
- 
The position and support of the balconies  
- 
The roof shape  
- 
The access to the different flats  
- 
The repetitiveness of the structural pattern 
 
These parameters do not have a real impact on the behaviour of the structure. However, 
since each category might require different interventions regarding their specificities, 
they will have an influence on the transformation process of the façade. The definition of 
the categories as well as schematic drawings of plans and sections can be find on the 
following pages (9-17) where the red line symbolises the actual insulation barrier. The 
measurements are given in meters. For supplementary illustrations, Annexe 2 contains

2.5. SUMMARY 
Housing facilities in Greenland have known a large evolution since the end of the 40’s and 
the housing block model is now part of the Greenlandic culture. The interest in 
developing new architecture and energy efficient solutions for buildings is getting bigger 
and should give opportunities for developing the architectural identity for the country as 
well as buildings fully integrated in their environment, able to face the challenges brought 
by the harsh Greenlandic climatic conditions. In addition, the conscience for better IAQ 
and enhanced comfort is growing and will favours the implementation of indoor climate 
regulation systems.  
 
The project concerns about 117 building blocks spread over 10 of the most populated 
cities of Greenland. Those building blocks present similarities in their structure on which 
a common method for transformation can be applied. However they also contain 
differences in their materials, organisation … It was thus necessary to define 9 categories 
taking into account all the specificities of the buildings to be able to propose adapted 
transformation prescriptions.  
 
Finally the renovation and transformation of the building is a sustainable way of thinking 
that will allow to save energy (in transport and in the construction process) and save 
materials. Not to mention the conservation of part of the architectural history of 
Greenland.

The aim of this chapter is to give prescriptions and advice in the way to investigate the 
buildings blocks. Depending on the cases, additional investigations could be required, 
particularly on the loadbearing capacity of the structure that has not been investigated 
in this thesis to focus on the new envelope. However, civil engineers who had the 
opportunity to work on the structure of some of the buildings seem to agree on its

Investigation propositions are completed and supported by illustrations and results from 
the fieldwork led in Sisimiut from 31st January to 21st February 2017 and in Nuuk from 
21st February till 28th February.   
 
3.1. PRIMARY INVESTIGATIONS 
Primary investigations comprise inquiry that do not require any sophisticated material or 
invasive sampling. They are based on direct observation, documentation and records of 
initial and current state the observed buildings.   
 
3.1.1. VISUAL INVESTIGATIONS 
Visual investigation is the most common way to start on a renovation project. This step is 
essential because it gives precious information on the current state of the buildings. In 
addition, this inspection on site allows to visualise the constructions in their context 
which has a large influence on the lifespan of buildings and on the behaviour of their 
structure and constitutive materials along time. The context englobes: climatic 
conditions, topography, population customs and patterns of life, environment, direct 
surroundings… Elements that will give a better understanding of the site and inspiration.  
The investigations also permit to draw down a first list of the materials that were used in 
the buildings. It is then possible to develop primary solution for the transformation 
project. Moreover, the primary visit informs on the access conditions of the site and helps 
to define basic requirements for the design. As an example, for the transformation of the 
building blocks, it seems that most of the materials will come from abroad (Denmark). 
Because most of the Greenlandic importations requires shipping, it is then important to 
consider design element which can fit in ship containers.  
 
The study trip in Greenland gave the opportunity to observe directly building blocks from 
C1 to C3, C6 and C7. As a general result, site visits highlighted that most of the blocks 
suffer from a lack of maintenance and present deteriorations not only on the envelope 
but also on the balconies/galleries and on structural parts that are in direct contact with 
the exterior environment. As an illustration, the following pictures show some of the 
damages that could be observed during the visits. From these observations, it can already 
be determined that the connections and angles are quite fragile locations and should 
benefit of a particular attention in the future design. Materials requiring less maintenance 
might also be beneficial.

Collecting documents when investigating buildings provides valuable details. They can be 
of different form: plans, sections, details, sketches, notes, planning permissions… 
Elements that give a better understanding of the buildings, their structures and in which

significantly to documentation provision.  
 
During the fieldwork, five companies were contacted in the hope to obtain an interview

3.1.3. DISCUSSIONS WITH THE INHABITANTS 
As already mentioned, buildings’ users have a large influence on their living spaces 
performances an energy consumption. It is therefore important to exchange with the 
inhabitants and to understand their way of living and habits. This can be done through 
interviews or questionnaires.  
 
The work in Greenland enable to discuss directly with the population and more 
specifically to submit a survey to 20 people living in 8 different blocks from C2 in Sisimiut. 
Intentionally, the respondents had different profiles and were from different age 
brackets. Moreover, a gender balance was respected. The questions were articulated 
around 4 main themes selected for the potential interest they could have in the 
transformation process of the blocks. They were: thermal comfort, daylight, acoustic 
insulation of the flats and users’ wishes for the future of the building blocks. For each 
topic, the people surveyed had the opportunity to express their perceptions by either 
marking or ticking the different propositions. First written in English, the questionnaire 
was then translated into Danish and Greenlandic to make it accessible to a large range of 
people. The result of the survey are presented in the next paragraphs and the original 
answers from the interviewed can be found in Annexe 6.

Thermal comfort 
Thermal comfort describes the level of satisfaction of the users of a building regarding 
their thermal environment. It is determined, according to the ISO 7730 norm, on 2 
parameters which are: the percentage of people dissatisfied (PPD) and the predicted 
mean vote (PMV). The PMV is the result of a calculation dependent of 2 personal comfort 
parameters (physical activity and clothing) and 4 building related comfort parameters (air 
temperature, mean radiant temperature, air velocity and air humidity). Because these 
notations have variable influences depending on the people, PPD has to be taken into 
account as the proportion of people who would feel thermally unsatisfied in their 
environment [19].  
 
In the survey, the approach was to evaluate the level of satisfaction and wellbeing of the 
residents and to understand how they would regulate their environment in order to 
improve it and make it more comfortable.  
First, people were asked if they had any heating/cooling system at their disposal and, in 
case of positive answer, if they had control on it. Additionally they could tell which 
systems they were possessing. Accordingly to the answers, all the respondents had 
radiators as heating system which seemed obvious and was confirmed by documents. 
Nonetheless, only 55% of them considers to have a good control on the installed heating 
devices. For cooling, a confusion about the systems available to bring fresh air remains. 
70% of the respondents consider that they have no cooling system even though all the 
apartments are equipped with a ventilation system based on natural air infiltration 
openings situated on the façade and mechanical air extraction from the kitchen and 
bathroom (more details in chapter 7). Actually, the inhabitants usually consider the fresh 
air inlet openings to be source of discomfort and thus block them with clothes. 
Consequently the relative humidity (RH) inside increases and also creates discomfort.  
Second, the inhabitants were offered to mark the way they were feeling inside in both 
summer and winter. The grading scale was from 1 to 10, 1 being very cold and 10 being 
very warm. The results can be visualise on Figure 15 and Figure 16. It appeared (from 
Figure 15) that 50% of the interrogated people considered the inner temperature to be 
good in summer (by grading it with 5 or 6). 35% would consider the temperatures to be 
warm or too warm while 15% still consider to feel cold inside. Additional explanations 
were given by some of the respondents. In summer, discomfort is mainly caused by the 
long sunshine duration periods that bring heat gains inside. The sun is hitting the large 
windows and overheats the spaces in which people have activities and sometimes 
maintain their radiators working at low regime (for the night and cooler periods). In 
winter (Figure 16), the number of satisfied people decreases to 40%, then 50% of the 
questioned people were feeling cold or even really cold. The remaining 10% of people on 
the contrary would judge their environment to be quite warm. The results can easily be 
explained by the large temperature differences between the outside and inside.  
Finally, different possibilities to regulate indoor climate were proposed to the residents 
to understand better what their reaction was when it comes to influence their 
environment. The most recurrent answers were the use of the curtains, windows and 
radiators. A common behaviour that could be observe on sunny days was to close the 
curtains to avoid direct sun light, open the windows to get fresh air but by keeping the 
radiators running. It is also not rare to have really high temperature inside because the 
users feel the need to compensate the winter cold by overheating their flats.

Apisseq is a student dormitory, achieved in 2010, in Sisimiut. The building was conceived 
to be energy efficient and equipped with monitoring systems allowing to assess its 
general energy performances, IAQ and the performances of the devices installed such as 
the air handling unit or solar collectors [4]. It was therefore interesting to compare the 
values from the outdated building block with an energy efficient building.  
The analysed parameters, namely RH and temperature, were selected because they 
directly influence the thermal comfort of the inhabitants of the building blocks. According 
to EN15251 recommendation standard [22], considering buildings in category II, RH 
should be between 25-60% while the range of temperature should be 20-26°C (these 
values are detailed further in Table 48, part 7.1.2. Ventilation design). The RH is the ratio 
between the partial vapour pressure and the saturated vapour pressure. In other words, 
for an air volume, at given temperature and pressure conditions, RH is the water vapour 
content it contains compared to the maximum water vapour content it could contain 
[23]. RH goes from 0 to 100% and is influenced by temperature variations (it increases if 
the temperature decreases and the other way around). RH can be divided in 3 levels [23]:  
 
- 
0% < RH < 35%, the air is considered to be dry 
- 
35% < RH < 65%, the air is considered to be relatively humid 
- 
65% < RH < 100%, the air is considered to be humid

3.2.3. DOOR BLOWER TEST 
The objective of the door blower test is to evaluate the airtightness of a building envelope 
and determine its air infiltration rate. The device is composed of a fan, with variable 
speed, mounted into an adaptable structure that has to fit in a door frame directly in 
contact with the outside. In addition, the system is equipped with pressure meters inside 
and outside as well as devices to measure the airflow (manometer and hoses). Once the 
system installed, the function of the fan is then to create a pressure difference of 50Pa 
between the inside of the tested room and the outside by depressurising the inner space. 
Thanks to this method, the leaks and openings through which the outside air, at higher 
pressure, flows will be highlighted [24].  
 
In Greenland, the current regulations do not formulate any requirements regarding the 
airtightness of the buildings. The reference could nevertheless be based on the Danish 
requirements (Table 16) which are usually a source of inspiration for Greenlandic 
regulations.

3.2.4. INVESTIGATIONS ON THE MATERIALS  
In order to plan the deconstruction process and sort the materials to give them the 
appropriate treatment, it is important to identify all the materials present in the buildings 
and more especially the ones that represent a danger for the health or the environment 
before the start of the “undressing” of the structure. Indeed, when the building blocks 
were built, the construction industry used to use construction materials containing 
dangerous components. As a matter of fact, asbestos, metals, polychlorinated biphenyls 
(PCB), chlorinated paraffin [26] were commonly used for their properties. This use was 
then progressively forbidden starting in the late 70th when the materials revealed 
themselves to have a negative impact on human health [26]. Therefore analysis should 
be performed to determine the presence or absence of these components and if they 
were used, in what proportions and quantities. Then based on the results, precautions 
could be taken to protect the workers and the environment by implementing 
deconstruction and disposal procedures in the project planning.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:61==
35 
 
Different companies in Greenland but also in Europe already had to deal with that kind 
of issues and propositions for investigation have been developed [17]. For example, 
prescription have been given for the investigation and demolition of some of the building 
blocks from C2 in Sisimiut [26] and the procedure can be described as follow:  
 
The first step, as previously mentioned, is to collect as much data as possible about the 
buildings. It is then possible to dress a portrait of the blocks from the initial state 
containing: dates, documentation of modification along time, a list of the materials used, 
the maintenance procedures…  
Then samples of materials should be collected in different location of the buildings. Those 
samples should be documented with: a mark indicating for which substance they are 
tested, a number, pictures and their exact position on plans. Different sampling method 
could be applied but the use of a simple blade to remove some part of the material should 
be sufficient. However, it is essential to change the blade after each sample to avoid the 
cross-contamination of the samples.   
Third, analysis in accredited laboratories can be performed in order to determine if 
samples contain any traces of pollutants and in which proportions. From the results of 
the analysis, it could be required to collect more sample for further investigation or to 
ask for more in depth sampling. The reason for such a request is that pollutants are not 
fixed and can migrate from their initial materials to adjacent elements in direct contact 
with them or in the structure.  
Following the analysis, a report including the conclusions of the results as well as 
recommendations for the disposal and treatment of the contaminated waste, based on 
current standards and regulations, should be prepared.  
Finally, the new project for the buildings should integrate the report and consider the 
demolition as a full part of the process.  
 
As a drawback, one could say that the study bases its results only on samples and not on 
the entirety of building. It is true that it is impossible to analyse the entire building and 
extrapolation of the results is not automatic. The Engineers and Architects have then to 
take decisions that are on the safe side for the environment and the health of the workers 
but also of the future users.   
 
The next paragraphs discuss the materials listed before, namely asbestos, chlorinated 
paraffin, metals, and PCB. First a description of the components and of the dangers that 
they represent for human health and the environment is given. Then the interest of using 
such materials and their application are detailed. Afterwards, primary indication about 
the location of these materials in the building block will be given based on the result of 
the analysis performed by Rambøll on one of the building of C2 [26] that was demolished 
in Sisimiut. Finally a comment is made about the other basic construction materials. The 
disposal of the contaminated materials will be detailed in the chapter 4 and is influenced 
by the proportion of pollutants contained in the elements.

does not represent a danger for humans. However, when it turns into dust, the fibres can 
easily be inhaled and enter the lungs. This resulting in irritations and the development of 
cancers and that sometimes still 20 to 40 years after exposition [27].

Chlorinated paraffin 
Chlorinated paraffin are chemical materials that can be present under several forms: 
short, mean, long chains of chemical components (carbon and chlorine) [29]. They 
represent risks of cancer and for the aquatic life when their concentration overpass 1µg/l 
of water [30]. 
This material is mainly used as plasticizers, flame retardants in the seals, paintings, 
plastics, flooring. The use of chlorinated paraffin started in 1930 and is still in application 
today but limited by regulations and the concentrations should not be higher than 1% 
[29]. No traces of chlorinated paraffin were found in the investigated building [26].

Metals 
When discussing the danger of metals in construction specific metals are concerned: 
cadmium, chromium, copper, lead, mercury, nickel, zinc [26]. They are naturally present 
in nature and only become a danger for the environment in high concentration. Then, 
they can cause a reduction of the biodiversity and health trouble for animals. Metals 
become dangerous for humans when they are inhaled or ingested in large quantities. 
Once they entered the organism, this last one has difficulties to evacuate them and 
metals accumulate in the liver and kidneys which are the organs responsible for the 
filtration of the blood [31].   
In construction, metals can be used under different forms such as solid form or as 
particles in painting. Lead was a widely spread material in paintings until 2000. They were 
appropriate for both inside and outside application and used for treatment of wood and 
iron but could also be used to paint walls, ceilings and wooden floors. Enamel of tiles 
could also contain lead which was used to give a white colour to the material. Other 
metals could then be added to produce different colours, for example additions of 
chromium would give a yellow hue to the painting. Nowadays, painting containing metals 
are still used for protection against corrosion and in renovation projects [26].  
 
The investigated building [26] revealed different concentrations of metals in the paintings 
that were used and it was then possible to place the materials in 3 categories depending 
on these concentrations: clean, non-dangerous, dangerous. Samples revealed high 
concentrations of lead and/or zinc in the paintings used on the walls of the staircases, on 
the skirting boards, on wooden panels and on the doors. The same remark was made on 
the outside concrete and on the parapets of the balconies. The concentration were so 
high that the materials were considered as hazardous wastes. The treated metal from the 
ventilation ducts, the pipes of the worming system and the protection grids were also 
concerned. On other materials, the concentration were more reasonable and would 
place the waste in polluted category. Most likely, part of the others building category 
should be painted with paintings containing metals.  
 
PCB 
PCB are chemical substance which have similar application to the ones of the chlorinated 
paraffin. They are composed of 3 types of atoms: carbon, chlorine, hydrogen. Their 
consistency changes in function of the composition and can be either liquid or waxy. The 
components possess interesting properties for the construction field. Indeed, PCB’s are 
non-flammable, stable, boil at high temperature, electrically non-conductive [32].  PCB’s 
are not fixed in their materials and are easily transferred to the elements in contact with 
them. If the pollutants are released in nature they can have a negative impact on fauna 
and human health. They are the cause of cancers but also of other disease related to 
immune, nervous and reproductive system for example [32].  
The different properties of PCB made it a useful material used in oil-based paintings, 
floorings, seals, adhesives, plastics… These applications started in 1950 and were 
prohibited in 1977. In parallel, the components could also be used in electrical systems, 
like capacitors, transformers…  Then they would be employ from 1950 until 1986, the 
reason is that, in that case, they were enclosed inside of closed elements [26].  
 
==PAGE:64==

In the study of the building from C2 in Sisimiut, tests were conducted in order to detect 
PCB traces [26]. In the same way than for the metals, the results would allow to classify 
the polluted construction materials into 3 categories: clean, non-dangerous, dangerous. 
As a result, the concentrations of PCB appeared to be low and most of the polluted 
materials were considered as not dangerous. The chemical components were present in 
paintings, floorings and seals. They were also located in the joints of the façade and of 
the window frames. However, high concentrations were found in electric materials. The 
condensers used for the neon light were considered to be hazardous waste with high PCB 
levels. It seems that PBC will be something to pay attention to in the transformation of 
all the buildings. However, if all the categories follow the pattern of the investigated 
building, the concentration will be most of the time low which will ease the procedure 
for the disposal of the materials.  
 
Other materials  
The other materials are all the basic construction materials that actually constitute the 
biggest part of the project compared to the dangerous materials. In the building 
categories, it was possible to identify: concrete, mortar, wood, plaster, rock wool, glass, 
metal, plastic and ceramics. Nevertheless, most of these materials were previously cited 
to be in contact or mixed with hazardous materials. Depending on the pollutants they 
were adjacent or mixed to and their concentrations, the basic construction materials will 
have follow a specific treatment.  
 
3.3. SUMMARY 
Investigations are the essential first step in the realisation of projects and even more 
when it comes to the renovation of a building. First primary investigations give the 
opportunity to visualise the buildings in their context and to get an overall idea of the 
situation. It is also the moment to gather documentation to understand how the 
constructions were built and if they were subject to modifications. In addition to that, the 
importance of the buildings’ users should never be neglected. Residents, by their 
behaviour, have a large influence on the lifespan of the buildings’ component as well as 
on the energy performances. Their advice can also help to highlight problems and 
drawbacks in the conception of certain systems.  
Then, in order to design energy efficient buildings providing comfortable and healthy 
environment, more detailed investigations are required. They aim to provide quantitative 
results while the primary investigation would give more qualitative documentation. 
Under those circumstances, the detailed assessment of the current state of the 
constructions can inform on the actual issues and defaults that should not be repeated 
in the new design project. These investigations can consist in thermographic picture, 
collect of outdoor and indoor climate, door blower test…  
 
Finally, the construction of the building blocks started at a time where dangerous 
components were commonly used in construction materials. It is essential for the 
environment and for the safety of the workers and future building users, to identify the 
dangerous substances that were used in the blocks. Once they have been quantified and 
localised, it becomes possible to plan the disposal of hazardous wastes as a full part of 
the transformation project.

4. PREPARATION OF THE STRUCTURE 
The purpose of this chapter is to give prescriptions in order to prepare the concrete 
structure of the building blocks for the future transformations, namely the adaptation of 
the buildings layout, the setting up of the new envelope and the implementation of new 
ventilation, heating and energy collecting systems. To be able to install all those new 
elements, each building structure needs to be cleared of all the old wrapping materials. 
However, the previous investigations revealed the presence of dangerous pollutants in 
the blocks. In this case, it is thus important to refer to the current norms and standards 
to plan a safe material removal and disposal. Furthermore, it was possible to observe 
damages in the concrete layer covering the reinforcement at some part of the structure. 
They ought to be repaired before they are cover by the new building envelope.  
 
4.1. MATERIALS REMOVAL AND DISPOSAL 
The previous material investigations highlighted the presence of different pollutants in 
the construction materials and this in variable concentrations levels. Depending on those 
levels, material removal (from the buildings) will not require the same level of safety for 
the workers and the environment. In the same token, the future disposal of the materials 
will be dependent on those concentrations.  
In general, for most of the hazardous materials (except for asbestos), 3 contamination 
levels with 3 corresponding disposal can be identified [26]: 
 
- 
Clean, the materials can stay on site, be reused or be removed and treated in 
Greenlandic installations. 
- 
Polluted, non-dangerous, the materials need to be removed but can be treated in 
Greenlandic installations (accreditation could sometimes be required).  
- 
Dangerous, the materials need to be removed following precautions and send to 
specialised treatment installations in Denmark. The transport of such waste is ruled 
by European agreements and requires a strict respect of the labelling and warping 
standards [26]. Asbestos is directly considered to be a dangerous waste.    
 
The following paragraphs suggest how to proceed in the removal and disposal of the 
construction materials containing asbestos, chlorinated paraffin, metals and PCB 
according to defined concentration levels setting the limits between clean, non-
dangerous and dangerous.

Other materials 
Different possibilities are offered in the disposal of the materials that were not in contact 
with pollutants or that were contaminated with very low amounts. The first one is the 
recycling or reuse of the removed materials. This option is applicable, for example, to 
glass and metallic elements that can be melted to produce new materials. Similarly, 
concrete waste could also be reused as refill for settlements and foundations in the city. 
Another option would be to recycle the concrete wastes to transform them in raw 
material that could be reused as aggregates in new concrete. However, this requires a 
special thermos-mechanical treatment supposed to separate the gravel from the 
hardened cement paste. Indeed, crushed concrete still contains cement that has different 
properties from natural gravel [35]. This treatment could therefore be difficult to 
implement in Greenland.   
If the first option is not conceivable, incineration of the waste, in the city were the 
building blocks are located, should be considered. Incineration of the materials will then 
provide energy to the parts of the city equipped with district heating.  One could say that 
incinerating the wastes is not the best option and might cost high carbon emissions. 
Nevertheless, the possibility to recycle and transform materials in Greenland are very 
limited or inexistent depending on the towns. The recycling could be centralised to one 
city (e.g. Nuuk) but the amount of wastes produced across the country might not be 
sufficient to supply the installations at full time. In addition, the materials would be 
shipped which costs a lot of energy and carbon emissions. The problem would even be 
more important if the wastes should be recycled in Denmark [18].   
 
4.2. TREATMENT OF THE STRUCTURE 
The treatment of the structure should focus on the concrete structure of the blocks and 
the wooden structure forming the roof. Both should be wholesome in order to be able to 
receive the new building envelope and installations.  
 
Primary visual investigations highlighted deterioration of the concrete layer around the 
reinforcement when it was directly in contact with the outside. This deterioration could 
be due to several factors: lack of maintenance, initial porosity of the concrete, cracks in 
concrete, the corrosion of reinforcement, frost/thaw cycles.  
The discussion with the inhabitants of the blocks in Sisimiut and Nuuk but also the 
interviews revealed that indeed the buildings were suffering of a lack of maintenance but 
it is probably not the only explanation to the degradation of the concrete. The corrosion 
of reinforcement played an important role. This corrosion can be caused by carbonation 
==PAGE:70==
44 
 
phenomena or by chlorides due to coastal environment. Then, oxidization induces an 
increase of the steel volume which pushes on the concrete layer a makes it crack. 
Carbonation is occurring in presence of humidity and CO2, the CO2 is dissolved in the 
water present in the pores of the concrete. This results in the formation of a carbonation 
front which decreases of the pH of concrete (initially at 13, it is reduced to 8-9). With 
time, the front is going deeper and deeper in the concrete as long as there is humidity. If 
it reaches the reinforcement, the acid corrodes the steel that swells and can provoke 
crack in which oxygen and water can penetrate and accelerate the oxidation.  Before the 
installation of the new building envelope, it is essential to repair the deteriorations. This 
can easily be done by following these steps [36]:  
 
- 
Determination of the zone to treat. 
- 
Removing of the crumbling concrete around the reinforcement. 
- 
Cleaning of the zone to treat. 
- 
Cleaning of the reinforcement by removing the rust with a wire brush or sandblasting 
(in extreme cases, the reinforcement might need to be totally replaced).   
- 
Treatment of the reinforcement with coating to ensure passivation the passivation of 
the steel (= protection from corrosion).  
- 
Humidification of the concrete around the reinforcement to ensure a better adhesion 
of the new covering layer to the existing concrete.  
- 
Covering of the reinforcement with mortar 
 
In the future transformation of the blocks, the structural concrete should not be exposed 
humidity and aggressive conditions anymore. Therefore, no further deterioration should 
occur. The fieldwork did not give access to the wooden structure of the roofs. This part 
should also be checked and damaged timber should be replaced to enhance the stability 
of the whole.   
 
4.3. SUMMARY 
The preparation of the structure is the first step to provide a healthy inner environment 
to the future inhabitants of the renovated blocks. It is important to remove all the 
dangerous waste with precautions to assure the security of the workers. The analysis 
reveal the concentrations of pollutants in each material. Depending on the results, 
materials can be classified as: clean, polluted and dangerous. Thanks to this classification, 
norms and standards give procedure and advices for the treatment of the materials with 
respect for the environment and the health of the labourers. After the decontamination 
of the structure is performed, other modifications, such as repair works, can be realised 
with the aim to improve the stability and durability of the structure. Finally, it is also 
important to take into account the future of the non-polluted wastes. When possible, the 
materials should be reused or recycled but the actual Greenlandic installations do not 
allow an efficient material transformation or recycling and thus incineration seems to be 
one of the most relevant option in order to avoid large carbon emission due to 
transportation.

5. REORGANISATION OF THE BLOCKS 
The objective of this chapter is to give basic propositions for a new layout and 
organisation of the building blocks. The reason is that the buildings are outdated and the 
present needs of the population as well as the spaces required for the technical 
installations have changed. In addition, a reorganisation of the space, taking into account 
the habits of the residents and the current defaults in the constructions, could lead to 
energy savings and improvement of the buildings’ thermal performances. For each 
category, modification will be made:  
 
- 
On the inside, the way to access the flats will be rethought as well as the inner 
organisation of the blocks to provide technical cores able to host the new building 
installations  
- 
On the outside, the balconies will be removed to become independent structures, 
taking away large thermal bridges in the blocks façades. They will also be private 
spaces not used to access the flats anymore.  
 
Note that only general principles and propositions will be given. If the blocks were 
transformed, each single project would have its sensitivity and integration in its context.  
 
5.1. INNER SPACES   
First, if the balconies are removed the way to access the different flats should be changed. 
Indeed, except for the blocks of C1, where an inner staircase gives access to several flats 
from the inside, all the other categories possess flats with their entrance door opening 
giving either on an outer gallery (C2-4 and C6-9) or directly on the outside (C5). As such, 
this distribution of the accesses implies a loss of energy and especially for the buildings 
with the outer galleries. Indeed, it could be observed, during the fieldwork, that the 
resident were leaving the doors of the staircases open. As a result, the indoor climate of 
those zones was really close to the outside climate (as it could be seen in Figure 29 and 
Figure 30). However, the insulation of the stairwells’ walls is really thin and the 
neighbouring flats are subject to higher heat losses and need more energy for heating. 
 
To improve the situation, 2 design solutions are proposed. The first one concerns the 
buildings in C1-C4, C6, C8 and C9 which are at least two storeys high and sometime placed 
over a crawl space. While the second one focuses its attention on C5 and C7 that are two-
storeys buildings placed directly on the ground.  
 
The first proposition consists in placing vertical cores in the centre of the building where 
the daylight is weaker. They will contain the stairs giving access to the upper floors but 
also two technical shafts accessible from the outside of the flats (Figure 31). The shafts 
should be used to route the ventilation ducts, the different pipes needed for water supply 
and evacuation and the electricity. All the technics will go down to the first floor which 
will provide insulated spaces hosting the different installations that will therefore be 
more accessible for maintenance. About the apartments, they will be articulated around 
the cores with a dual-aspect living room/kitchen and one sided bedrooms and bathroom. 
With these transformations, the blocks will be accessible from the ground floor with 
doors closing automatically. Then the different storeys will be served by the inner central

were asked which kind of facilities they would like to enjoy in the buildings. For example,

5.2. OUTER SPACES  
From the field work, it could be observed that the inhabitants of the buildings blocks were 
attached to their outside environment and not only to dry their laundry. However, in the 
current situation, the balconies of the buildings (C1-C5 and C7-9) create large thermal 
bridges in the envelope because they are supported on cantilever beams directly 
connected to the concrete structure of the constructions. To solve the problem, the 
balconies should be mounted on independent structures, appended to the renovated 
buildings. In the case of the second proposal for inner transformation, however, there 
would be no need for balcony anymore and the outside space of the flats could simply be 
the surroundings or a terrace.   
 
5.3. SUMMARY  
The building blocks are outdated and modifications of the layout and of their organisation 
are necessary to propose energy efficient buildings as a final result. First, the creation of 
technical shafts was required to operate the new installations aiming to improve the 
buildings’ indoor climate and their IAQ. Depending on the categories of blocks, their 
situation might vary but should always be at the centre of the buildings where the natural 
light is less strong. More details, such as pipes and ducts routing will be illustrated in the 
case study.  Then, a rationalisation of the access was needed to avoid supplementary heat 
losses by having independent external doors. The bigger blocks would concentrate the 
entrances to the apartments on the inside while the lower buildings would provide buffer 
zones before accessing the living spaces. It should also be mentioned that to limit thermal 
bridges, the old balconies should be removed and replaced by new constructions 
mounted on independent structures, only appended to the building blocks.  
 
Finally, it is important to note that only really basic propositions have been given and 
mainly to take into account the new installations aiming to improve the efficacy of the 
blocks in term of comfort, health, safety and energy. If the blocks should be subject to 
future transformations, each of them would require specific investigations and design 
propositions related to the context in which they stand. However this would be part of a 
full and architectural project that cannot be addressed in this thesis.

6. DESIGN OF THE NEW BUILDING ENVELOPE 
The aim of this chapter is to develop a new building envelope for the building blocks by 
discussing and assessing different proposals. The new design will provide an efficient 
insulation which seems to be an obvious first step for energy savings. In addition, to 
reduce the energy expenditures, a well-designed building envelope will help to regulate 
indoor temperature year-round, improve the airtightness, reduces unwanted inside and 
outside noise and add fire protection and value to the building. When discussing the 
design of a new envelope, it is not only the building façade that is concerned but the 
entire system that is isolating the building from the rest of the outside environment. After 
the review of the different norms and standards regulating the subject and of calculation 
methods, the design will be divided into 3 parts, each focusing on precise building zones 
that have different needs and requirements:  
 
- 
The ground floor. This zone is the foundation of the building, the slab can either be in 
direct contact with the ground or suspended over a crawl space. Those two 
possibilities offer different challenges that should be addressed. 
- 
The external walls. They compose the façade itself which is where most of the 
interactions between the inside and outside environment occur. Indeed, this is 
through the façade that the light enters the flats, that the users access the building, 
that ventilation is performed… Moreover, the façade will give an expression to the 
blocks which is significant for the integration of the buildings in the city. The focus will 
be given to the wall assemblies and not on the windows that are assumed to be 
airtight and performant triple glazed components. Nevertheless, their connections 
with the wall system will be detailed in the case study.   
- 
The roof. The zone is usually the location where large heat losses occur, it is thus 
important to design it carefully and even more in Greenland where ice could build-up 
on the roof and cause damages to the buildings.  
 
In the 3 parts, the insulation materials will play an essential role in the achievement of an 
efficient envelope with high thermal resistance. This is why they will be discussed prior 
to the design proposals in the view to propose the most appropriate insulation solutions 
to each case. Finally, of course, the connections between every part should not be 
neglected because they will ensure the airtightness and efficacy of the design. The 
possibility for the design proposals to connect must therefore be considered.

6.3. INSULATION MATERIALS  
Throughout the conception of a new building envelope, insulation materials will be used 
and will represent the core of the design because they offer the best thermal 
performances and allow to reach low U-values. Two main types of insulation materials 
can be identified. The first one is the fibres based insulation which consists in fibres 
obtained from natural resources and/or recycled materials. They offer good thermal 
performances and a lot of flexibility in the installation thanks to their light weight and the 
suppleness. As an illustration, glass wool, mineral wool, natural wool (cotton, sheep…), 
cellulose fibre… belong to the type of insulation [39]. Then they are the oil based 
insulation materials coming from the petrochemical industry. They usually offer better 
thermal resistance than insulation built with fibres for the same thicknesses and can be 
found under different forms such as foams (that can be projected on the surfaces to 
treat) or panels obtained with variable processes (e.g. expansion, extrusion). The most 
commonly encountered form are polyurethane foams, polyurethane panels, expanded 
polystyrene, extruded polystyrene… [39]  
 
Based on their performances and advantages, two materials were selected in each 
category of insulation materials: Glass wool, mineral wool extruded polystyrene and 
polyurethane panels. Glass and mineral wool are widely spread in the construction 
industry they offer good performances in term of insulation, fire resistance and durability. 
Nowadays they also tend to use more recycled material in their fabrication process. These 
wools are quite easy to install and do not require an extreme precision or high level of 
formation. There transportation is also eased thanks to the possibility to compact the 
elements [40]. Other material commonly used, extruded polystyrene has performances 
similar to expanded polystyrene but enjoys of a better thermal conductivity, durability 
and resistance to deformation. It is found under the panel form of different thicknesses 
and interesting to use for slabs and flat roof insulation. Finally, polyurethane panels are 
used for their really good thermal performances provided by the high thermal resistance 
of the material which allows to reduce the thickness of the insulation. Not that the two 
last materials have a bad resistance to fire and should be combined with other materials. 
In order to compare the different insulation materials, their characteristics and 
performances were summarised in Table 27. 
 
From Table 27, it will be possible to select the most adapted insulation material 
depending on the situations for the different design proposals. Note that the insulation

procure to the envelope an optimal U-value. For the sake of the thermal performances, 
the designs will try to provide insulation aiming to reach the required U-values for two-
story buildings which are the most conservative.

6.4.1. SLAB DIRECTLY ON THE GROUND  
The improvement of the insulation of the base floor directly in contact with the soil will 
have two main advantages. The first one is the enhancement of the thermal 
performances of the buildings by reducing the heat losses through the floor. The second 
is the reduction of the impact of the buildings on the permafrost that could be present 
around the foundations of the blocks. The main challenge in this situation is that the slab 
has to be insulated from the inside because it is not possible intervene under it. This is 
why the new design proposals should be as thin as possible and allow to keep a sufficient 
free high beneath the ceiling. Two design proposals are introduced and are applicable to 
five blocks categories, namely C2, C3 and C5-7.  
 
Ground floor design proposal 1 
The first ground floor design proposal (GFDP1, Figure 33) consists in the addition of a light 
and insulated wooden structure over the base slab. First, pine wood beams are place on 
the concrete floor, perpendicularly to the longitudinal direction of the room and spaced 
of 400mm. In some cases, if the floor is not fully horizontal, plastic wedges are used 
bellow the beams to correct the imperfections. In between the beams, struts might be 
added in order to reinforce the assembly. The insulation layer, made of polyurethane 
panel, is placed in the hollow structure. The polyurethane was chosen because of its very 
low thermal conductivity allowing to achieve good performances with a reduced 
thickness (remember that the insulation is on the inside and that the height under the 
ceiling should be kept as high as possible). To cover the insulated structure, 25mm thick 
plywood plates should be used. This thickness will offer more resistance to the floor 
which has to support common purposes and the heating and ventilation installations. 
Note that an air gap is intentionally kept to improve the acoustic performances and avoid 
moisture issues. On the top of that, a PVC layer is glued. It has the advantage to protect 
the floor from humidity and to provide a resistant finishing surface, easy to maintain.

option of improving the performances by increasing the insulation layer is also dismissed 
because the ceiling height has to remain acceptable. Hopefully, Danish regulations allow 
to compensate lower performances of one part of the envelope by increasing them on 
another part [25]. This option seems to be the most realistic.

6.4.2. SLAB OVER A CRAWLSPACE 
Comparatively with the slab directly in contact with the soil, the presence of a crawl space 
allows to insulate the base slab from the bottom and gives more room for the installation 
of a thicker insulation layer that will not have any influence on the free height under the 
ceiling of the ground floor. The resistance of the floor against heat losses can thus be 
highly increased. This situation was met in C1-4, C8 and C9. Important to mention that 
the crawl space should be properly ventilated to avoid moisture accumulation and 
migration on the new installations [46].  
 
Ground floor design proposal 3 
The ground floor design proposal 3 (GFDP3, Figure 35 and Figure 36) is very similar to 
GFDP1. On the inside of the building, a structure made of wood elements spaced of 
400mm is set on the floor (with additional wedges if the slab should not be perfectly flat). 
Then glass wool is placed in the empty spaces, mainly for the improvement of the 
acoustic. This material was chosen because it can be transported under very compact 
rolls. Once again, the system is covered with plywood plates with sufficient thickness to 
ensure stability and solidity. Attention was given to keep a sufficient air layer between 
the insulation and the plywood to avoid moisture problems. The floor is then finished 
with PVC. The new element in this system is the introduction of the insulation in the crawl 
space, below the concrete slab. A lot of space can thus be saved and the thickness of the 
insulation layer can vary depending on the requirement from the zones. The insulation is 
fixed thanks to long screws directly driven in the concrete. To avoid punctual undesirable 
compression on the insulation, a circular metallic piece transfers the effort from the head 
of the screw to a larger area of the insulation layer.  
If for some reason the crawl space would require a false ceiling, a supplementary 
structure should be installed but would reduce the performances of the insulation.

6.5. FAÇADE DESIGN  
After the design of the base floor, the next step is the elaboration of a new façade system 
with different objectives. First, it will have to improve the energy efficiency of the 
buildings by reducing the heat losses through the façade. It should also improve the 
airtightness of the blocks by reducing the leaks which cause discomfort and higher energy 
consumption. A bad airtightness might also result in snow and moisture infiltrations that 
will deteriorate the materials and reduce the performances. It is important to mention 
that the improvement that are brought should not be to the detriment of the acoustic 
performances, the fire resistance and the aesthetic of the envelope.  
 
As previously mentioned, people involved in the design/construction process might have 
a lack of knowledge and experience. In addition, the Greenlandic weather conditions are 
difficult and have a large impact on building sites. This is why it would be interesting to 
develop design proposal involving prefabricated elements that would both limit human 
interventions and reduce the time needed for the setting up of the façade.  
 
Like for the design of the base floor, two different wall situations could be identified. The 
first one is the non-loadbearing façade, situated on the long edges of the buildings, which 
should be hanged to the concrete structure. The second one is the gable wall, one at each 
end of the buildings, which is a concrete loadbearing elements that has to be insulated 
and covered. Again, the discussion for both cases will be discussed as follow:

6.5.1. NON LOADBEARING FAÇADE 
The concrete structure, composed of parallel walls, allows to have a non-loadbearing 
façade on the 2 long edges of the buildings. The repetitiveness of the structural pattern 
can be seen as an advantage in the design of prefabricated elements to fix on the 
structure. Originally, buildings from category C1-C5, C8 and C9 were closed with 
prefabricated façade elements imported from Denmark [11]. They were composed, from 
the inside to the outside, of a particle board, vapour barrier, insulation layer filling a 
wooden structure made of vertical struts, a wind barrier and the cladding made of 
fibrocement plates containing asbestos ( [11] + onsite observations). The system was 
quite innovative at the time but from today’s point of view, it seems to be obsolete and 
inefficient. Along time, draughts and infiltrations at the connections of the prefabricated 
element and around their windows caused condensation issues. As a consequence, 
mould started to develop in some of the flats.  
For the remaining categories, C6 and C7, the façade is composed of an insulated wooden 
frame cladded with painted wooden planks.

Façade design proposal 1 
The façade design proposal 1 (FDP1, Figure 39) is based on how wooden frame wall 
constructions are traditionally built with the addition of an insulation layer. From the 
inside to the outside, the wall is composed first of 2 plaster boards. The choice of putting 
2 boards instead of only one was made to improve the resistance of the wall to impacts 
from furniture for example. The plaster is fixed on a hollow wooden structure filled with 
mineral wool. The next layer is composed of vapour barrier. Then the main structure of 
the wall is made of vertical timber elements spaced of 600mm where additional mineral 
wool, forming the main insulation can be added. To cover the structure, plywood boards 
are used and procure more resistance to the assembly by bracing the structure. The 
composition could be stopped here with the addition of a wind barrier but to improve 
the thermal resistance of the wall, it was decided to add an insulation layer in between 
horizontally placed wooden elements. They do not only support additional insulation but 
will also be used to fix the structure of the cladding system, avoiding at the same time to 
reduce the spacing of the main structure which would reduce the thermal performances 
of the wall. Finally, a wind barrier is placed before the addition the structure that will 
support the selected cladding. This assembly present quite good thermal performances 
with a U-value equal to 0.12 W/m²K. However, it is difficult to provide additional 
insulation to the system and no proposition was made for zone 2.

Façade design proposal 3 
The Façade Design Proposal 3 (FDP3, Figure 41) was inspired by the polar station 
“Princess Elisabeth Antarctica” [15] and the research from the “CCHRC - Cold Climate 
Housing Research Centre” [12]. The first one used self-sustaining wall elements that were 
assembled together on the structure of the polar station. They were constituted of a thick 
insulation layer (polystyrene) placed between two layers of lamellate wood, linked 
together by wooden circular spacer ensuring the resistance of the assembly and blocking 
the insulation. On the outside, the air and water tightness was ensure by foam mats and 
EPDM silicone sealant and covered by stainless steel to ensure resistance and durability. 
On the inside, a double vapour barrier made of craft paper and aluminium was installed. 
The finishing material for the inner layer that was chosen was woollen felt. Those building 
elements had the advantage to be quite light and easy to transport and assemble. Since 
everything was prepared in advance, the workers would only have to focus on the 
connections. The duration of the work was also highly reduced. Then CCHRC has 
developed structural prefabricated panels containing already the insulation and 
sheathings. The panels just need to be assembled together by qualified workers and the 
joints to be sealed carefully in order to ensure the airtightness. The benefits from this 
method are a gain of time during the construction and improvement of the airtightness 
and thermal performances.  
Based on that, FDP3 proposes the installation of prefabricated panels that could be fixed 
directly to the structure. They are composed of 2 plywood plates enclosing a mineral wool 
insulation layer. The fibres of the wool should be perpendicular to the surface of the 
plywood to improve the resistance of the assembly. The 2 panels are connected together 
with spacers for the resistance. Depending on the required thickness of the insulation, 
the density of spacers can be increased to assure the stability of the whole. Inside, a 
vapour barrier is installed and covered with a plaster board while outside, a wind barrier 
takes place and is covered with a freely selected cladding.

Façade design proposal 4 
For the façade design proposal 4 (FDP4, Figure 42), the potential application of sandwich 
panels was explored. They are composed of a thick insulation layer (up to 300mm) 
protected by thin steel plates on both large surfaces and offer good thermal 
performances, fire resistance and acoustic while limiting the amount of material involved 
in the construction process [48] [49]. The success of the system installation lies then in 
the proper sealing of the different joints to procure an optimal airtightness to the building 
blocks. The assembly is composed of a sandwich panel that is typically 1200mm width 
over up to 12000mm length. The insulation core can be made of different materials but 
mineral wool was chosen for its thermal performances and fire resistance. Important to 
notice that the fibres of the wool should be perpendicular to the steel plates surfaces to 
improve the robustness of the panels. About the fixation of the elements on the 
structure, they are directly screwed on the concrete from the outside of the building and 
only 4 to 5 long screws are necessary along each short edge of the panels. The thermal 
bridges are thus highly limited. On the inside, a metal stud structure is installed to fix the 
inner finishing layer made of plaster boards. No vapour barrier is needed thanks to the 
steel plate. For zone 1, the panel itself was sufficient to comply with the provisions for 
2020 proposed in BR15 in term of thermal performances. Therefore, if insulation should 
be placed between the metallic elements, it would only be for acoustic reasons. In zone 
2, the panels are not sufficient and additional thermal insulation is required. With a metal 
stud structure of 100mm thick filled with glass wool, the assembly yields the provision for 
2020 proposed in BR15. In that case, attention should be given to check if no 
condensation occurs on the inner steel layer. On the outside, there is no need for the 
installation of a wind barrier because the steel plate is playing this role. Variable types of 
claddings can then be fixed to the panels to offer a better aesthetic. Important to notice 
that the steel is not really resistant and special fixations need to be used for the cladding.

6.8. SOLAR POTENTIAL  
In the artic, the potential energy that could be collected from the sun is an element too 
often neglected although it could be used to heat water [4] and produce electricity [57]. 
One of the reason is the cheap price of oil which does not encourage the population and 
investors to use solar panels and photovoltaic cells. In addition, like for many subjects, 
solar energy use in Greenland is still under experimentations and has a room for 
improvement.  
The conditions to operate solar energy in Greenland are different from the ones in 
Europe. The potential solar radiations are the same than for any place in the world but 
the difference is that they are mostly available in summer. This is especially true for the 
part of the country that is situated above the arctic circle, in zone 2, where, in summer, 
the solar radiations are available all day long (for 24 hours) and from every direction. The 
high latitudes of the regions imply a low position of the sun in the sky and therefore, the 
inclination of the energy collectors have to be adapted to assure good performances [57]. 
In the case of the buildings, the low solar altitude is a chance. Indeed, the blocks offer 
large vertical windowless areas where photovoltaic panels could be installed. To give 
element of comparison, such panels placed vertically in Greenland will receive more solar 
radiation than the same panels with the same position in Denmark due to the high 
latitude of the region and would therefore be more efficient [57]. This is important when 
you know that the roof surfaces would be difficult to exploit for these installations due to 
the snow accumulation. On the other hand, the large presence of snow on the ground 
can also be an advantage because it reflects the solar radiations and increases the 
amount that is received on the collecting surfaces [57].    
With that in mind, photovoltaic panels should be installed on the façades of the buildings. 
Additionally, the top of the roof could be equipped with vertical solar panel for water 
heating, enjoying sun power at 360° [57].  
==PAGE:106==
80 
 
The implementation of technology is not the only way to take advantage of the solar 
energy. Indeed, direct solar gain through the windows can bring heat to the inner spaces. 
This was highlighted in Figure 29: Temperature data collected in Sisimiut (08-14/02/2017) 
in which a pic of temperature could be observed in a flat facing south on a sunny day. If 
well manages, direct solar gains can be positive and participate in the reduction of the 
energy consumption of the buildings. However, they could also create over heating of the 
apartments. This is why solar shadings, with vertical slats (due to low position of the sun 
in the sky), should be implement.  
Two possible location for the shadings could be chosen: on the outer side of the façade 
or on the inner side of the façade. Nevertheless, the extreme climatic conditions of 
Greenland, namely strong wind, low temperature, frost formation and snow infiltrations 
would make outside protection difficult to operate and maintain. This is why inner solar 
shading should be preferred.  
 
6.9. SUMMARY 
The future envelope of the blocks will open the door to energy savings and improve the 
thermal performances of the buildings. The new skin will have to reduce the heat losses 
and help to regulate the indoor climate. This will be done by enhancing of the airtightness 
and insulation of the envelope which should then offer a better control on the indoor 
climate. Furthermore, additional value concerning the acoustic performances, fire safety, 
human health and aesthetic should be gained.  
To achieve these objectives, different design proposals for the ground, the roof and the 
façade were made. They were guided and assessed on the basis of regulations and 
standards coming from Greenland, Denmark and Europe. The new proposals for the roofs 
and floors were quite common propositions requiring additional insulation layer and 
slight adaptation of the basic systems. On the other hand, the façades, which offer the 
largest surface area in contact with the extreme climatic conditions of Greenland, 
addressed more difficulties and were more challenging to design.  
 
In general, the old façades of the building blocks are suffering from leaks that reduce the 
performances, increase their energy consumption and can create mould growth if the 
conditions of humidity and temperature are favourable. The leaks can be the cause of an 
obvious lack of maintenance of the buildings and mistakes in the setting up the façade 
elements. Nowadays, the new constructed element still have problems with draughts, 
usually because of the lack of experience of the construction workers. To encounter the 
problem, the design proposal that was finally preferred is proposing to use prefabricated 
sandwich panels.  
 
The use of sandwich panels can offer many advantages to the buildings and bring 
ameliorations to the construction process. First, the elements are available in variable 
dimensions and offer the possibility to cover large areas in a reduced amount of time. For 
Greenland were the “good season” to perform construction work is reduced, being able 
to isolate the buildings quickly is essential.  Then, the panels need to be fixed to the 
structure by only a few points which is highly reducing the thermal bridges and the heat 
losses that they create. Third, the panels reduce the use of construction materials and 
does not require addition vapour/wind barrier thanks to the steel plates on each side that 
can play this role. The mistakes coming from the installations of those barrier are thus 
==PAGE:107==
81 
 
cancelled. Not to neglect, the materials that are used for their construction are recyclable 
and can be based on recycled materials and therefore have a positive impact on the 
environment. Moreover, the materials could easily be dismounted for recycling in the 
future if further transformation should be performed. Fourth, the panels, when they are 
well assembled, provide a good airtightness to the buildings. To do so, the elements need 
to be connected carefully with the appropriate sealant placed at the right locations. Fifth, 
the safety and health of the inhabitants is ensured. Indeed, the panels are classified as 
A2-s1,d0, which in the European standards means that they are resistant to fire and do 
not contribute to fire growth. About the health, they do not emit any pollutants. Finally, 
the thermal performances of the system can be improved by the addition of insulation 
on the inside. It should then be checked that no condensation can form on the inner steel 
layer. On the outside, the panels can be cladded with many materials. It should just be 
mentioned that the steel plate is so thin that its resistance is reduced. Therefore, specific 
fixation for the structure of the cladding must be applied. Another possibility for the cover 
some part of the building would be to use photovoltaic cells/panels in order take 
advantage of the solar potential of Greenland and harvest energy.  
 
As a drawback, it could be said that the importation of those sandwich panels would 
require more preparation and a formation of the building crews because the elements 
need to be precisely mounted with the help of a crane. The concrete structure should 
also be carefully prepared to be able to receive the new installations. However, the 
potential benefits from the system are many and its implementation should be tested.

7. INDOOR CLIMATE 
In this chapter, the purpose is not to precisely design a ventilation and heating system for 
the transformed building blocks but to give guidelines and draw the attention on the 
problems generally met in Greenland. The design of a new envelope will improve the 
airtightness of the blocks. As a consequence, they will need the installation of a 
performant air handling unit (AHU) able to assure a sufficient air exchange rate and IAQ. 
Today, ventilation systems are widely spread in Europe and the performances are well 
documented. However, in the case of Greenland, the installation of adapted ventilation 
systems is rare and the knowledge in the field is still subject to study and 
experimentations. Following an introduction to the ventilation basics and to the current 
requirements applied in Greenland and Denmark, an attempt to propose design 
principles for the ventilation of the building blocks will be made. The propositions will 
take into account recurrent problems encountered in Greenlandic AHU and try to suggest 
design solutions that could solve these issues and protect the installations.  
 
At the end, in parallel to the development of a new ventilation system, propositions for 
the design of new heating installations have to be formulated. As a final result, the 
building blocks should be more energy efficient and thus reduce their energy 
consumption but should also provide a better IAQ and thermal comfort to their users.  
 
7.1. VENTILATION SYSTEM 
While the positive effects of the new envelope on energy performances and airtightness 
were highlighted, the potential impacts on IAQ and thermal comfort caused by a tighter 
insulation of the buildings should not be neglected. A higher airtightness of the façade 
reduces the interaction between outdoor and indoor air that were provided via air 
leakages and any other opening in the building envelope (cracks, junction between 
surfaces, access doors to ventilated cavity). Air exchange rate between the outside and 
the inside is reduced and this can lead to thermal discomfort, overheating of the inner 
space and to a significant increase of the indoor concentration of pollutants due to the 
materials, moulds and human activities [58]. As a result, the IAQ is highly reduced and 
since Greenlandic population spends a lot of time inside dwellings during the cold season, 
the effect on health can be quite significant [59]. 
 
Nevertheless, when an effective insulation is coupled with an efficient ventilation, 
ensuring a sufficient airflow, the indoor air pollution is decreased and the temperature 
can be regulated. Ventilation can be defined as “the process of supplying air to or 
removing air from a space with the purpose of controlling air contaminant levels, 
humidity, or temperature within the space” [60]. The design of a ventilation system allows 
thus to remove and dilute pollutants and humidity produced indoors because of the 
occupancy, to regulate the temperature and RH and finally to provide a comfortable and 
healthier indoor environment for users.

7.1.1. VENTILATION PRINCIPLES  
Before designing a ventilation system for buildings in Greenland, it is important to 
understand how to ventilate and what are the different existing options that are offered. 
First, the way to process ventilation should follow the scheme illustrated in Figure 46.  
 
 
Figure 46: Ventilation scheme 
 
Fresh air has to arrive in “dry rooms” (where humidity production is the lowest = living 
room, bedroom). Then openings between “dry rooms” and “wet rooms” have to be 
installed to let the air flow. Finally, confined air is exhausted from “wet rooms” (= kitchen, 
bathroom) to outside. In addition to this system, it is possible to add punctual ventilation 
to cancel odours or overheating. From this model, it is possible to identify two main ways 
of ventilating: natural ventilation and mechanical ventilation [61]. 
 
Natural ventilation  
Natural ventilation (NV) can be achieved in many ways and basically consists in air flowing 
through installations on windows, doors, walls and roofs. Those installations are 
adjustable openings which do not use fans. The airflow rate then depends on the size of 
the openings and their disposition around the building’s envelope. It also varies according 
to the temperature, the humidity and wind pressure that is function of the speed and the 
direction of the wind. The climate determines mostly the size of openings to maintain a 
desired rate [61] [62]. This system that is very suitable for many types of buildings in 
Europe may face difficulties in Greenland and Artic climate in general. The pros and cons 
of these installations can be found in Table 44 [61]:

Mechanical ventilation (MV) offers, most of the time, the possibility to control the airflow 
rate in any conditions and without any human intervention. It can be decided to have a 
constant air volume system (CAV) which guarantees a pre-set constant airflow rate 
without taking into account human activities. On the other hand, one might opt for 
variable air volume system (VAV) or demand controlled ventilation (DCV) where the air 
change rate varies according to the occupancy of the ventilated spaces [63]. MV corrects 
also some of the disadvantages of NV. First, because of the mechanical air transfer, the 
airtightness of the building envelope can be assured, there are less loss of energy, it can 
provide a better sound insulation and more security. The loss of energy decreases but 
because each airflow is under control, it is possible to recover the heat of exhausted air 
and warm incoming air besides it is possible to clean the air and take off a lot of its 
pollutants with filters. The pros and cons for MV can be found in Table 45.

MV is really interesting for architects and engineers. If conduits are planned, the system 
fits with any type of structure and architecture and limit the intervention of users. Like 
for NV, it exists more than one system: mechanical exhaust ventilation, mechanical supply 
and exhaust ventilation and hybrid ventilation [62]. 
 
In mechanical exhaust ventilation system, extraction of confined air is realised in rooms 
with lower air quality. Fans extract the air which is replaced with outdoor air that enters 
through the building envelope. Even if it provides a constant ventilation, the system has 
difficulties to take heat from exhausted air and cannot warm outdoor air (because it 
comes from leaks and openings in the construction). Moreover, it usually only ventilates 
some rooms like kitchens or bathroom that have a high RH. The air in other rooms is thus 
not always renewed [62]. 
 
In mechanical supply and exhaust ventilation, air is still exhausted from wet spaces but 
supply is controlled and realised in occupied spaces (like office, living room, bedrooms). 
The flow is a closed loop, then it is possible to recover heat, thanks to a heat exchanger 
(HE) and to warm up filtered supplied air or water. It is quite obvious that this system is 
more economic and sustainable than the first one [62]. 
 
Finally, hybrid ventilation consists in placing a fan in the natural evacuation. It will help to 
improve an efficient natural ventilation. It also can be an association between natural and 
mechanical ventilation. When the first one cannot be used effectively, the second takes 
its place [62].

Important to notice that to have an optimal system, it is essential to treat the incoming 
air [62]. Indeed, sometimes, ventilation can be a source of problems. If the air is not well 
filtered, particles and pollutants can enter the airflow and deteriorate IAQ. Pipes can also 
be a place where virus, germs and moistures develop. According to those considerations, 
any effective ventilation needs to be adaptable and well controlled and used by the 
occupants of the building. Therefore it is important to inform people and teach them how 
they have to use those different systems. Even with the most efficient and adapted 
ventilation, a wrong utilisation can make the system inefficient and sometimes dangerous 
for health of occupants. The maintenance will be really important to assure the system 
security, efficacy and sustainability [61]. 
 
7.1.2. VENTILATION DESIGN  
An efficient ventilation is directly linked with outdoor temperature, humidity and climate. 
This is why Greenlandic conditions are an enormous challenge when it comes to design 
ventilation systems and some problems need to be addressed (Table 46). The ability of 
cold air to contain a moisture is small and in winter when the temperature are extremely 
low, the dry air from the outside might affect the RH of the inner environment [59]. 
Extremely low temperatures might also lead to frost formation in the HE caused by water 
condensation [64].  This problem occurred, for example, in the low energy house in 
Sisimiut where the AHU was installed in an uninsulated attic for budgetary reasons. The 
performances of the HE were then lowered [1]. In addition, the strong wind combined 
with the snowfalls can lead to the migration of snow, through the supply air openings, 
into the system and result in a lower performances and material degradations [63].

Device located in insulated space 
To ensure good energy performances and durability of the ventilation system, the 
installations should be located in an insulated space. The building categories previously 
identified offer different possible locations: in the basement/crawl space, on the ground 
floor, in the roof. The devices will be installed on the ground floor (or in the basement if 
present) and not in the roof. The reason is that, as previously mentioned, the space below 
the roof should be unheated to create a buffer zone between the inside of the building 
and the outside. Thanks to this space, ice formation caused by snow melting and freezing 
should be avoided.   
 
Variable Air Volume system  
Usually, the most commonly used ventilation systems in dwellings are based on CAV 
which means that they operate a pre-set constant air change respecting the regulations 
and ignoring the variation in the occupancy of the ventilated spaces. Consequently the 
IAQ may vary along the day depending on the users’ activities. Other options such as VAV 
and CDV systems exist. The first is working at variable air change rate corresponding to 
the load of the indoor activities (cooking, smoking, showering…) and enables to save 
energy since the system is not working at full power all day long. The variation of the 
airflow rate is performed by the alternation of 2 modes based on codes and 
recommendations [63]. The second system is also working under variable air change rate 
which depends on a fixed IAQ of the occupied space defined by CO2 and moisture levels 
[65]. In the renovation of the building blocks, VAV should be preferred to CAV. Indeed, 
CAV systems, even if they provide a good IAQ (if the space is sufficiently ventilated), use 
more energy than necessary and the constant high air exchange leads to low RH caused 
by the dry supply air. VAV ventilation, on the other hand, should be able to handle 
variations in the occupancy and will be more adapted to the current situation [63].  
In combination with the MV, the opportunity for inhabitants to open window should be 
preserved. This will offer additional comfort and help overpassing overheating in the case 
where solar shading and ventilation would not be sufficient.  
 
Heat Exchanger  
In cold climates, it seems impossible to conceive a ventilation system without 
implementing a HE. This is why they will be part of the ventilation strategy. HE allow to 
reduce the energy consumption by recovering heat from the extract air to preheat 
incoming air from the outside environment (the opposite is true for cooling by hot 
weather). They are mechanical devices which transfer heat from one fluid to another and 
are classified according to the direction of those fluid-flow (parallel-flow, cross-flow, and 
concurrent-flow) [66]. Many types of HE exist but 2 most common may be identified [66]: 
  
- 
Recuperative HE (Plate): heat is directly transferred from the hot to the cold fluid 
- 
Regenerative HE (Rotary): heat is stored in a thermal storage mass and transferred to 
the cold fluid afterwards 
 
 
 
==PAGE:116==
90 
 
Moisture recovery unit 
To ensure a good IAQ, the RH of inner spaces should be around 30-50% (ideally around 
45%) [3]. As previously mentioned cold air as a very low capacity to contain moisture and 
the air supplied to the buildings room might reduce the RH inside. To solve the problem, 
one option is to use the HE for both heat and moisture recovery. Another solution can be 
to equip the rooms with air humidifier but in order to avoid improper use of this system 
by the inhabitants, the transformed buildings should only be equipped with HE able to 
recover heat and moisture from the exhaust air.  
 
Defrosting system 
In Greenland, extremely cold air might cause ice formation into the HE. This phenomena 
occurs when the temperature of the outdoor air reaches the dew point of the extracted 
air resulting in condensation of the moisture in the HE. If the temperature of the outdoor 
air drops below 0°C, formation of frost might commence [63].  
To prevent the problem different strategies exist: preheating inlet air, by-passing the HE, 
recirculation of the extract air… However, when it comes to cold climate like in the artic, 
those solution might not be sufficient or energy efficient and need to operate on long 
periods of time [63].  
A solution proposed in the low energy house of Sisimiut was to combine 2 counter-flow 
heat exchangers in series so one would be able to operate while the other is under 
defrosting. The drawback of this system is that it is working all along the year even when 
no frost formation occurs. To improve the efficiency of the HE, the possibility to switch 
off the damper should be given [1].  
The combination of different strategies seems to be a good solution for defrosting. In the 
new design, preheating of the air should be combined with a HE system by keeping in 
mind that it must be possible to activate the defrosting function only when necessary.  
 
Snow infiltration protection  
The protection of the ventilation air intakes from snow (but also rain) should not be 
neglected in the design of an efficient MV system. Accumulation of snow on the filters 
and in the installations might lead to a blockage of the system, large pressure drop inside 
of the building and bad IAQ [67]. Usually, snow infiltrations are the consequences of an 
absent or inefficient shielding system [68] or of inappropriate inlet vents location [67]. 
This is why they should be carefully planned at early design stage. Different solutions 
aiming to blocks snow infiltrations exist: pre-filters, louvres systems, electric heating 
devices, appropriate shields [67]. In order to avoid additional energy consumption with 
electrical systems, appropriate shielding will be used in the buildings transformation 
process.  
The purpose of shields is to decrease the air velocity around the air intakes so it reaches 
at least 2m/s (max 1m/s is recommended). The reason is that snow falls slowly (0.2-
1.0m/s) and is easily blown into the ventilation system if the air velocity is too high in 
front of the vents [67]. The protection consist in a chamber (Figure 47), opened at the 
bottom, which should be positioned high enough to avoid snow accumulation. The 
reduction of the air velocity is achieved by having a large enclosed space following the 
opening. Since the top of the chamber is closed at the top, the air vents are protected 
from snow drifts [68].

7.2. HEATING SYSTEM 
Besides the ventilation, a heating system will be needed to ensure thermal comfort. 
Originally, in the early 60th, the blocks were heated with individual coal stove connected 
to a small radiator. However, this system cause rapidly hygienic and practical problems 
so that the individual heaters were replaced by district heating to aliment the radiators 
[7]. Today heating in the building blocks is still powered by district heating. The radiators 
are place below the windows and function with hot water. However, the system is far to 
be optimal because even if the users have the opportunity to check their energy 
consumption, they do not dispose of precise tools to regulate indoor temperature. On 
sunny winter days, for example, it is not rare to meet people who close their inner blinds 
and open the window to avoid overheating but who still keep the radiators running. 
The new system that will be installed will also be based on district heating and water-
filled radiators because they provide a comfortable heat and allow to evenly warm up 
spaces quite fast.  In addition, a thermostatic installation should set limits to inner 
temperature and help inhabitants to regulate their indoor climate. Such a system will also 
allow to take into account the heat coming from internal and solar gains.  
The operation of the thermostat is quite simple and can easily be explained to the users. 
First, a predefined temperature range is programmed in the system (Table 48). When the 
temperature reaches the low boundary temperature, the radiators starts running. During 
the day, different activities or solar gains through window might influence the room 
temperature and the thermostat can then reduce the power of the radiators or switch 
them off. If at one point the inside temperature is too high even if heating system is not 
running, the thermostat can inform the users who would need to open the window for 
additional ventilation and to close inner solar blinds until the situation is regulated and 
the inner temperature is back to the pre-set range.

7.3. SUMMARY 
In the design of the new envelope, a particular attention was given to the improvement 
of the insulation and of the airtightness. As a consequence, the natural airflow coming 
from the leaks and NV opening will be highly reduced. In addition, to improve the energy 
performances of the buildings the envelope by itself is not sufficient, the mechanical 
installations have a role to play. This is why it was important to design a new ventilation 
system that has for objective to ensure a good IAQ and a comfortable and healthy 
environment.   
 
There are many ways to operate buildings ventilations, based on natural or mechanical 
systems. In the case of Greenland, MV seems to be the most appropriate way to face the 
extreme climatic conditions of the region. However, traditional MV system are not 
sufficient and can encounter issues to work and perform with efficacy. Indeed, 
Greenlandic air is most of the time dry and cold and reduced the RH of inner spaces. The 
cold can also have a negative impact on the performances of the AHU and can even 
damage mechanical elements due to frost formation in the device. Another cause of 
problem and of potential material break is the migration of snow pushed by the strong 
winds into the air intake openings.  
 
Ventilation systems in Greenland are still under experimentation and testing and have a 
margin of improvement. However, some design solutions can be given to improve the 
performances of the installations. First the devices should be located in insulated spaces 
ensuring a protection from the outside cold. Then, to avoid to dry out the inner air, 
moisture recovery units could be installed and the ventilation should operate at VAV so 
it can fit more to the occupancy of the different rooms. To save energy, HE should be 
installed to recover the heat of the inside air and preheat the incoming cold air. The 
coupling of HE could also be a solution in the fight against cold inlet air and frost building 
in the system. To finish, the snow infiltrations could easily be avoided by designing the 
opening in a most clever way. The design should reduce the speed of the wind around 
the intake vents to avoid snow to be drifted into the AHU.  
 
Finally, the regulation of the indoor climate would not be complete without the 
integration of a performant heating system. The heat should be provide through district 
heating and aliment a radiator system regulated by a thermostatic installation which can 
take into account the heat gains coming from human activities and the sun. Thanks to 
those installations, the building users should enjoy a healthy and thermally comfortable 
environment with good IAQ. However, they should be informed on the systems that were 
installed in order to ensure optimal performances.

8.3. DEFINITION OF THE NEW LAYOUT 
Block 13 was built 50 years ago and the spaces needed for the installations have changed. 
As it is now, it would be difficult to install a performant mechanical ventilation system 
without impacting the living spaces. This is why the building organisation needs to be 
rethought. Moreover, the way to access the dwellings is not efficient.  
At the moment, the building possesses 23 dwellings including 14 studios of 32m². Most 
likely, this number will be reduced after transformation to provide larger apartments 
designated to families.  
 
First, the galleries will be removed from the building. The reason is that they created large 
thermal bridges by being directly connected to the concrete walls. It can be seen on 
Figure 49 that after the galleries removal, the walls and slabs will be prolonged to be 
aligned with the edge of the gable walls. The aim is to gain inner space in order to be able 
to organise the accesses to the flats from the inside of the building.   
Secondly, thanks to the increased width of the block, it becomes possible to integrate 2 
vertical cores in the middle of the building, where the amount of natural light is reduced 
(Figure 50). In each of them, the stairs giving access to all the floors will be installed. In 
addition, they will contain 2 technical shafts directly accessible and in which the 
ventilation ducts, heating tubes, water supply pipes and electricity will be guided to serve 
every apartment. These installations will find their source on the ground floor where the 
AHU and the connection to district heating will be installed. The technical room will be 
easily accessible for maintenance and isolated from the extreme cold of Greenland. Note 
that the shaft will also be used to transfer the inlet and exhaust air respectively from and 
to the roof the block.  
Thirdly, the housing units will be accessible from the new cores that will act as a buffer 
zone. With this disposition, the hope is to reduce energy losses. The residents of the 
blocks will enter the building from the ground floors equipped with door closing 
automatically. This will locate the large cold infiltrations to one location and solve the 
large heat losses encountered until now with the permanently open staircases with low 
insulation. Then, when opening their entrance door, the inner temperature of the 
apartments will be preserved.  
Fourthly, the layout of the flats will also be changed. They will be arranged around the 
cores with a dual-aspect living room/kitchen and one sided bedrooms and bathroom. The 
height below the ceiling will be quite limited after the installation of a new floor (2,6m). 
To avoid the installation of a false ceiling in the living spaces, it was decided to rout the 
ventilation ducts above the corridors giving access to the rooms. That space will be thus 
be the only one to have a lower ceiling (Figure 51).  
Finally, additional spaces will be provided. Private external spaces with a balcony for each 
flat. However, they will be supported by an independent structure to avoid thermal 
bridges. Common functions will also be installed for the inhabitant: a laundry room, 
storage rooms, sport room and a polyvalent space. Additional storage room will be 
provided outside for the wastes disposal.  
 
As a result, the new proposition offers 12 flats of 85m² each (this could be of course 
modified) as well as common spaces. The different modifications layout should have 
positive impacts on the performances of the buildings and will offer new spaces more 
adapted to today’s technical installations and lifestyle.

8.4. DESIGN OF THE ENVELOPE  
As it was mentioned in 6.5.1 and 6.5.2, the design proposals that were preferred for the 
transformation of the buildings were FDP4 for the non-loadbearing façade and GWDP2 
for the gable walls. Both solutions were using sandwich panels and in the case of zone 2, 
requiring an additional layer of insulation. However, a potential risk for condensation of 
water on the inner steel plate was present. If this should occur, it would reduce the 
performances of the additional insulation and eventually favourite mould growth. To 
check if the design is safe, the evolution of the temperature in the wall assemblies has to 
be investigated to be see if the temperature on the steel plate reaches the dew point 
temperature that would cause condensation. To perform this verification, two method 
will be used: 
 
- 
Hand calculation based on the Glaser method (1D method) from “ISO 13788: 
Hygrothermal performance of building components and building elements - Internal 
surface temperature to avoid critical surface humidity and interstitial condensation - 
Calculation methods” [72]. First, the temperature is calculated at the interface 
between each layer of the wall assembly (thermal resistances are calculated like in 
6.2). Then the partial and saturated vapour pressure are determined at the interfaces 
and to avoid condensation, the partial vapour pressure should be lower than the 
saturated vapour pressure [72].  
- 
Numerical simulation using U-wert online programme based on finite elements 2D 
method [70]. To determine its results, the software follows “ISO 6946: Building 
components and building elements - Thermal resistance and thermal transmittance - 
Calculation method” [38] and “DIN 4108-3: Thermal protection and energy economy 
in buildings - Part 3: Protection against moisture subject to climate conditions - 
Requirements and directions for design and construction”.  
 
The procedure for the verification will start by an introduction to the input parameters 
for both methods. Then, the hand calculations will be realised. Thirdly, U-wert 
programme will be used. Finally, the results will be discussed and compared.

8.4.2. HAND CALCULATIONS  
This method is based on the Glaser method from “ISO 13788: Hygrothermal performance 
of building components and building elements - Internal surface temperature to avoid 
critical surface humidity and interstitial condensation - Calculation methods” [71]. It is 
important to mention that the method gives only first linear approximation to check if 
the conditions are favourable or not to vapour condensation. Nonetheless, this method 
alone is insufficient to ensure 100% safety and additional verifications are needed. This is 
why the numerical method will be used. The hand calculation can be divided into 5 steps:  
 
- 
Determination of the inside and outside parameters.  
- 
Calculation of the thermal/vapour resistance for each layer of the wall assembly. 
- 
Calculation of the total thermal/vapour resistance for the entire wall assembly.  
- 
Calculation of the temperature and partial/saturated vapour pressure at the different 
interfaces between the layers of the wall assembly.  
- 
Calculation of the RH.  
 
The Glaser method will be explained. Then the main results will be summarised in tables. 
The most critical case will be discussed further and illustrated with graphs representing 
the sections with temperature and vapour pressure profiles. The detailed calculations 
and results can be found in Annexe 9 and Annexe 10.

8.4.3. SIMULATION PROGRAMME 
Unlike the Glaser method, the selected programme to analyse the wall assembly is using 
a finite element method to perform two-dimensional calculations. In such a way, it 
corrects the limitations of DIN 4108-3 (similar to the Glaser method) on which it is based. 
The software that was used is a German programme, U-wert, available online [70]. Its 
objective is to evaluate the sensibility of a wall section to condensation and moisture. 
Thanks to the finite element method, it procures a more accurate estimation of the 
vapour pressure distribution and also ensure that the partial vapour pressure can equal 
the saturated vapour pressure but not overpass it.  The ameliorations brought by the 
programme are: 
 
- 
Consideration of a minimum and maximum µd value. This is more accurate because 
a single erroneous value might give irrelevant data.  
- 
Possibility to use different surface resistance Rsi and Rse. To calculate the U-value of 
the wall section, the surfaces resistances are retrieved from ISO 6946. They are the 
ones presented in Table 25. On the other hand, they can be selected either from ISO 
6946 or DIN 4108-3 for the moisture protection. For DIN 4108-3, Rsi = 0.25 m²K/W 
and Rse = 0.04 m²K/W. The differences in the values can be easily explained by the 
fact that each method was meant to consider the worst case. For the calculation of 
the U-value, based on ISO 6946, a low surface thermal resistance is unfavourable. 
Contrariwise, the DIN4108-3 is to determine the moisture transfer in a wall and in 
that case, a high surface thermal resistance is unfavourable. This latter statement 
could be observed in the previous calculations when a reduction of the insulation was 
improving the resistance of the wall section to water condensation.  
 
Despite these improvements, the calculation method used, likewise the traditional 
method from DIN 4108-3, does not take into consideration the transfer of moisture by 
capillarity conductivity in the materials but only considers the transfer by uniform vapour 
diffusion. The simplification is introduced to describe the transportation of humidity in 
the construction materials by only µd and thus reduce de time needed for calculations. 
However, by doing this, the programme places the results on the safe side. Actually, the 
transfer by capillarity conductivity implies a better repartition of moisture in the materials 
reducing the RH on a surface. Therefore, the values obtained from the programme are 
conservative.  
 
Calculations  
Before the start of the simulation, the wall sections were modelled in the programme as 
illustrated in Figure 57. It was possible to either select the materials form a proposed

selected earlier were implemented in the programme (see Table 62). The only difference 
was for the µd which is determined by a lower and upper limit for more accuracy (except 
for the steel that had a µd virtually infinite). Finally, to cover the wall assembly on the 
outside, the software gives the possibility to choose between different cladding systems. 
The one that was selected consists in an air layer, 30mm thick (default value).

8.5. CONSTRUCTION DETAILS 
As it has been mentioned several time, it is essential to consider the joints and 
connections of the different elements in the design of a new building envelope. This case 
study was the occasion to highlight key locations where the joints should receive a 
particular attention. Indeed, if they are well designed, they will have a positive impact on 
the airtightness of the blocks and avoid the damages that could be caused by snow or 
water migration into the assemblies. Moreover, the connection of the skin to the 
buildings can result in many thermal bridges that have a large impact on the energy losses 
from the blocks. It is thus important to show how the elements should be connected 
together by illustrating how the joints have to be built up to ensure a good protection 
and resistance. It is also essential to demonstrate how the elements are connected to the 
concrete structure of the blocks to prove the relevance of the design proposals and show 
how they can reduce or cancel thermal bridges. The previous thermographic analysis 
evidenced recurrent issues in the connections. Indeed, many construction defaults could 
be identified around the edges of the windows and doors but also of the wall elements. 
This helped to select which joints should be investigated. The 7 following construction 
details were developed based on information provided by PAROC [48] and illustrate key 
locations of the building envelope.

Structure/panels connection detail - Figure 63 
The fastening of the sandwich panels to the building structure, for the non-loadbearing 
façade is performed as in Figure 63. The anchorage are placed only on the short edges of 
the panels and spaced of 300mm. This participates to a large reduction of the thermal 
bridges. Note that a lateral tolerance of 20mm is allowed in the positioning of the 
elements. The anchorages are then covered with tape to protect the connections.  
 
About the cladding, it consists in ceramic plates that are fixed on vertical steel profiles 
attached to the sandwich panels with rivets spaced of 300mm. The use of simple screws 
is not possible here because of the very low thickness of the panels steel layer which does 
not offer enough resistance. Then, the fixation devices, namely clips, are directly screwed 
on the steel profiles.  
 
Finally, in order to ensure a good airtightness and avoid direct contact between the 
concrete structure and the panels, sealant joints are placed on the edge of the concrete 
walls to avoid any air transfer through the assemblies. The joints need to be poured prior 
the installation of the panels so they can be held tightly between the panels and the 
concrete. The same type of joints are placed outside to avoid the infiltrations through the 
connection between 2 panels.  
 
Corner connection detail - Figure 64 
For the corner connection on Figure 64, the same principles than for “structure/panels 
connection detail” apply with some adaptations. First, because additional insulation was 
required to provide high thermal performances, the concrete panels are spaced from the 
concrete structure with vertical wooden studding.

The investigations highlighted recurrent problems of draughts and thermal bridges 
around the windows edges, this is why it seemed important to give it a particular

To fix the windows, the thin steel layer of the panels is not strong enough. Therefore, an 
additional steel profile needs to be added all around the openings, to give more stiffness 
and resistance. This profile is inserted in the sandwich panel and fixed with screws that 
are then cover with a thin steel plate with sealant along its edge to protect the panel from 
any air or water infiltrations. The windows can then be directly fixed to the thick steel 
profile. This is done thanks to flat angle brackets that are screwed on the steel plate and 
attached to the windows.  
 
To ensure the airtightness and improve the thermal performances of the assembly, the 
joint between the windows frame and the sandwich panels is composed of 2 different 
materials. At the extremities, inside and outside, a sealant joint is placed, ensuring the 
airtightness. In the middle, polyurethane foam is injected during the installation of the 
windows to enhance the thermal resistance of the connection.  
 
About the solar sahdings, they consist in vertical slats, sliding in a runner fixed to the 
ceiling. The choice of vertical slats was made to block more efficiently the sun rays hitting 
the windows almost perpendicularly due to the low position of the sun in the sky.  
 
Gable walls connection detail - Figure 67

their long edge and also what kind of system could be implemented on the façade to 
operate the solar potential of Greenland.

the sandwich panels should be equipped with sealand joint all along their long edges. The 
sealant is installed in 2 steps. First a joint is poured in the bottom steel connection of the 
panels. Then, when supperposing the elements, the joint is pressed and assure a first 
good protection. Finally, additional sealand is added in the remaining space in order to 
secure the whole assemlby.

profiles fixed to the sandwich panels with rivets for the same reasons than already

steel decks on the wooden truss, present from the original construction, and on the newly 
installed steel element. In order to avoid snow infiltrations in the cold roof, the openings 
resulting from the shape of the decks should be blocked with polyurethane foam and a 
sealant should be place along the edges of the steel plate. Important to mention that the 
screws fixing the steel decks will be covered to protect them and that a joint is present 
to avoid water infiltrations.

reduced thermal bridges and connections points between the façade elements and the 
inner structure. However, as a drawback, it was shown that the installation of the

process in order to provide good performance and efficient and resistant final result. If 
such a system should be implemented in Greenland, it would thus be essential to form 
the workers to the installation of the elements.

8.6. SUMMARY 
This case study gives the opportunity to focus on one building block and to go further into 
details. Thanks to the reviewed documentation, it is possible to analyse the current state 
of the block and to understand where the major problems are located. From this analysis, 
the current performances of the building envelope can be determined and it appears that 
the thermal resistance of the different elements (ground floor, external walls and roof) is 
very low when compared to the Danish standards.  
 
Then, the new proposition for the layout of the building was presented including the 
routing of the heating and ventilation system. The decision of integrating two central 
vertical cores in the building has many advantages. First by containing the stairs and 
accesses to the flats they avoid extensive heat losses through the vertical circulation and 
temperature drops when opening the doors. Then, the idea to equip them with technical 
shafts easily accessible seems to be an efficient solution for maintenance and to guide 
the different technics. Indeed, by concentrating the technics, the impact of the 
installations on the living spaces is highly reduced. In the design, it is limited to a reduction 
of the ceiling height in the corridors which is considered not to be problematic.  
 
After this, a particular focus was given to the building envelope and more specifically to 
the wall assemblies. The selected design proposals FDP4 and GWDP2 in zone 2 needed 
to be checked for condensation of water because of the additional inside insulation layer. 
According to the model used, Glaser method and 2D finite elements method, the design 
could be considered to be safe under the given conditions. However, the risk for 
condensation was still present because the high RH (around 95%) in some cases at the 
interface between the inside insulation and the sandwich panel. It was therefore decided 
to adapt the design proposals to reduce the risks for condensing but by still keeping good 
thermal performances. In the final propositions the thickness of the inside isolation is, for 
this reason, reduced and selected with a higher linear thermal transmittance. By doing 
this, the objective is to increase the temperature at the surface of the sandwich panel 
and allow the air to contain higher rates of moisture.  
 
To conclude, the last proposals for the wall assemblies comply with the regulation and 
seem to be safe regarding condensation resistance. In addition, they enhance the thermal 
performances of the building envelope. The connections between the elements need to 
be detailed to prove the performances of the system and illustrate how the element 
should be assembled in order to limit or avoid thermal bridges, leaks and draughts.

9. CONCLUSIONS 
This project was discussing problematics and proposing guidelines and design solutions 
for the transformation of outdated building blocks that form a quite significant part of 
the Greenlandic housing stock. Indeed, in the present situation, the developed 
propositions and discussions could be applied to more than 100 buildings which is not 
negligible for the country. However, to be applicable to this amount, the suggestions 
sometimes needed to remain basic and general. Then as illustrated in the case study, 
each project would define more precise requirements in accordance with the context and 
specific needs of the studied block. By suggesting to transform existing dwellings, the 
objectives were to keep their concrete structure to avoid an extensive material 
importation and to reduce the time needed for the construction process, not to mention 
the preservation of a part of the Greenlandic patrimony.  
 
The first step of the study was to propose an overview of the existing buildings that were 
following the same design and construction principals and also of their location. As a 
result, they were divided into 9 categories. This division was based on variable criteria 
related to the construction materials, structure and organisation of the layout. A better 
understanding of the blocks was gained from this analysis and it was then possible to 
propose adapted design proposals and indications depending on the cases. The main 
drawback of this overview is that only two cities, Nuuk and Sisimiut, could be visited in 
order to investigate the buildings directly on site and to confirm the observations 
performed through picture visualisation. The direct visit of the other locations would have 
brought more information and precisions allowing to refine the categories.  
 
Secondly, the work considered different investigation methods to analyse and evaluate 
the current state and performances of the buildings. These methods could be tested 
during the fieldwork in Sisimiut and consisted in primary and detailed investigations, 
providing respectively qualitative and quantitative results. The primary investigations 
were based on visual inspections and gathering of information and documentation such 
as drawings, plans, texts and pictures. Besides collecting documents, it was also the 
occasion to meet Greenlanders and people having a link with the construction process in 
Greenland, allowing to understand its specificities. The survey with the inhabitants of the 
blocks revealed how they were acting on their environment to adapt it to their needs and 
comfort. Even though the language was a barrier, it was possible to communicate thanks 
to questionnaires translated into Danish and Greenlandic. This highlighted the need for 
the installation of efficient heating and ventilation systems, preferably automatic to avoid 
human interventions. The detailed investigations were composed of an indoor and 
outdoor climate analysis of the blocks and thermographic pictures. The first one allowed 
to understand the interactions between the outside and inside environment while the 
second illustrated all the recurrent construction defaults and issues in the building 
façades. From these investigations, it was clear that the future design would need to 
propose a new thermally efficient envelope with a detailing of the connections to avoid 
draughts and thermal bridges. Additionally, the skin should be equipped with solar 
shadings to limit the impact of the sun at certain moments of the day. The main drawback 
of these analysis is that they were performed on a time period of three weeks and only 
in the winter. It would have been interesting to collect data at different moments of the 
==PAGE:160==
134 
 
year to be able to compare all the results and analyse different combinations of RH and 
temperatures. The performance of a door blower test would also have given more 
information on the façade and on the leaks. Finally, because the buildings were 
constructed when dangerous substances were used in the construction materials, it was 
necessary to discuss the possibility of their presence and their effects. It was really 
important to be aware of this to integrate the removal and disposal the hazardous waste 
in the transformation planning. 
 
Thirdly, from the previous observations, the aim was to propose guidelines for the 
preparation of the structure before transformation. Thanks to Danish and European 
norms, the different levels of dangerousness could be set for the construction wastes and 
prescription for the disposals in each cases could be given. The concrete structure itself 
could also be subject to deteriorations and needed to be fixed before further 
transformations.  The preparation of the structure also had to pass by a rearrangement 
of the organisation of the blocks. Depending on the categories, two propositions were 
made with the purpose to improve the functionality and to be able to implement the new 
technical installations supposed to regulate the indoor climate. Integration of technical 
rooms and shafts easily accessible for maintenance as well as buffer zone for the 
entrances were the answers to that problem. Of course, these were basic principles that 
would need precisions for each case but it gives a first good approach to the situation 
and can speed up the decision process.  
 
Fourthly, following the previous considerations, a particular attention could be given to 
the buildings envelopes. Design proposals were made for the ground floors of the 
buildings, the façade and the top ceiling. The main objectives of the new assemblies were 
to increase the thermal performances of the blocks to reach the provision for 2020 
proposed in the Danish building regulations, reduce the thermal bridges and improve the 
airtightness. Additionally, the proposed solutions were subject to modifications in order 
to be adapted to the two geographical zones of Greenland. Different results were 
observed. First, the insulation of the ground floor directly on the ground was a challenge 
because the height under the ceiling of the rooms limited the range of intervention. It 
was thus decided to use polyurethane which has a very low linear transmittance and 
allow to reach could performances with thin elements. At the end, even though the 
thermal transmittance of the assembly was highly reduced, the requirements form the 
Danish standards were not reached and the other part of the buildings would then need 
to be more efficient. Then, In the case of the roof, no difficulties were encountered, the 
new roofing would just need to assure a perfect resistance to snow and water infiltrations 
to avoid damages to the newly renovated buildings. Finally, the façade which is offering 
the larger surface in contact with the outside environment was studied. A particular 
system stood out from the rest. It was the wall assembly implementing sandwich panels 
that offered many advantages.  A good general resistance to external factors with a 
reduced amount of materials, good thermal performances, limitation of workers 
intervention avoiding large construction defaults, reduction of the thermal bridges by 
reducing the number of fixation points, reduction of the construction time and flexibility 
of the system were the benefits of the installation of sandwich panels. However, their 
current limited thickness required and additional insulation layer to comply with the 
efficiency provisions from Denmark. Due to the presence of this supplementary insulation 
==PAGE:161==
135 
 
there was a risk for water condensation at the inner surface of the sandwich panel that 
needed to be investigated. This was done thanks to the Glaser method and a simulation 
programme. In the given situations, the design was protected but small variations of the 
climatic conditions could have led to condensing issues. It was thus decided to improve 
the designs by modifying the parameters of the additional insulation to increase the 
temperature at the panel surface and therefore reduce the risks. Thanks to this analysis, 
the final design proposals were ensuring a good degree of safety and still keeping the 
above listed advantages. Some critiques could be addressed to the calculation method 
that made some simplifications on the problems. Nonetheless, these simplification make 
the analysis more conservative and should prevent the issues in the wall assemblies. 
More attention could also have been given to the thermal bridges and draught risks by 
proposing to calculate quantitative data. Even if they were not calculated, they were not 
forgotten and the detailing of the connections was there to ensure the efficiency of the 
installed systems where problems were usually identified in the investigations.  
 
Finally, from the described analysis but also from the literature review, it appeared that 
the implementation of an efficient ventilation system was essential. Although it was not 
the main subject of the thesis, it seemed important to address the challenges generally 
encountered in Greenland when design AHU. MV was selected for the advantages it 
offered, namely control of air exchange, of the indoor parameters, flexibility, limited 
human interventions, energy recovery, security and healthier environment and IAQ. To 
face the harsh climatic conditions of the country some design guidelines were given 
concerning the location of the system, its flexibility, and heat and moisture recovery and 
frost and snow protection. The subject is still under experimentation and leaves room for 
improvements.  
 
To conclude, this thesis offers an overview of outdated building blocks from Greenland 
and proposes solutions for their renovation and transformation. A particular focus was 
dedicated to the buildings envelope with the objectives to improve the thermal 
performances of the blocks and to enhance the indoor comfort and climate. A specific 
wall assembly was investigated more in detail to evaluate the possibility and advantages 
of implementing sandwich panel systems to Greenlandic dwellings. This study tended to 
develop a sustainable way of thinking by preserving the structure of the blocks and 
selecting durable materials. The concepts developed in the report should probably be 
more detailed when applied to each building. However, the hope is that the thesis opens 
a good alternative option to the demolition of the housing blocks and speeds up the 
design and planning of the transformation process by considering most of the issues 
related to the discussed topics.
