ABSTRACT 
Since its commercial introduction in 1991, the Li-ion battery has undergone a steady and 
continuous optimization, and has gone on to become profoundly indispensable to the modern 
societies around the world. Using a variety of different chemical compositions to power 
everything from mobile telephones, tablets, laptop computers, power tools and now even 
vehicles, Li-ion technology is literally found everywhere.  
Until the technology of the solid-state battery is perfected, Li-ion technology is the most 
effective way to store large amounts of electric energy. The property of thermal stability has 
proven to be, one of the most important properties of any Li-ion battery, as the occurrence of 
thermal runaway has caused numerous fires and even explosions. A thermal runaway in even 
a small battery, will inevitably cause the battery to overheat, and once the battery’s protective 
casing can no longer contain the elevated pressure, a variety of both flammable and toxic gasses 
are released from it – often spectacularly so. One of these said toxic gasses is hydrogen fluoride 
(HF), which has a very well documented hazardous impact on human health.  
The initial research for this report showed that both the risk of the occurrence of thermal 
runaway, and the quantity of emitted toxic gasses, greatly depend on both the battery’s general 
chemical composition, and its state of charge (SOC). It also showed that even identical tests can 
create rather different results, and that larger batteries do not necessarily equal larger amounts 
of emitted HF. This is why extrapolation of archived results should be avoided, and is therefore 
not used on the results achieved in the simulations of this report. 
By applying actual measured quantities of HF from a full-scale experiment into an FDS-model, 
this report has investigated different scenarios, where said HF is being released from a fire in 
the Li-ion battery pack of a small EV. The EV is placed in a confined space, in the shape of a real-
life underground parking garage, placed in the greater Copenhagen area. 
Results, measured at 1,2 and 1,6 meters above the floor, show that the investigated hazardous 
concentrations of 30 PPM (IDLH), 34 PPM (AEGL 2, 30 minutes) and 62 (AEGL 3, 30 minutes) 
occur relatively quickly, when the battery’s protective casing is compromised. They also show, 
that at the time of the fire brigade’s assumed arrival, concentrations in the garage exceeds 150 
PPM in certain areas of the garage, and continue to rise to reach peak values of approximately 
800 PPM (measured at 1,6 meters above the floor) in the time firefighters would be expected 
to be near the burning vehicle. This because Li-ion battery fires are self-oxidizing, and thereby 
notoriously difficult to extinguish, using standard firefighting techniques. 
The results further illustrate the general behavior of the toxic gasses, accumulating in corners 
where ceiling jets collide with vertical obstacles, such as walls and pillars.  
As the results measured at 1,2 meters above the floor are consistently lower than those 
measured at 1,6 meters above the floor, the combined results of this report show, that 
firefighters should stay away from corners, and should be operating from lowered/kneeled 
positions whenever possible.

RESUMÉ (abstract in Danish) 
Siden dets kommercielle indførelse i 1991 har Li-ion-batteriet gennemgået en konstant og 
kontinuerlig optimering, og er sidenhen blevet fuldstændig uundværlig for de moderne 
samfund rundt om i verden. Ved hjælp af en række forskellige kemiske sammensætninger, 
leverer de strøm til alt fra mobiltelefoner, tablets, bærbare computere, el-værktøj og nu endda 
køretøjer, findes Li-ion-teknologi efterhånden bogstaveligt talt overalt. 
Indtil faststof-batteriets teknologi er blevet perfektioneret, er Li-ion-teknologi den mest 
effektive måde, at lagre store mængder elektrisk energi på. Egenskaben kaldet termisk 
stabilitet har vist sig, at være en af de vigtigste egenskaber ved ethvert Li-ion-batteri, da 
forekomsten af en selvforstærkende termisk acceleration (thermal runaway) har forårsaget 
adskillige brande og endda eksplosioner. En thermal runaway i selv et lille batteri, vil 
uundgåeligt forårsage, at batteriet overophedes, og når batteriets beskyttende kasse ikke 
længere kan indeholde det forhøjede tryk, frigøres en række brandfarlige og giftige gasser fra 
det – ofte på spektakulær vis. En af disse nævnte giftige gasser er hydrogenfluorid (HF), som 
desværre har en meget veldokumenteret skadelig virkning på menneskers sundhed. 
Den indledende undersøgelse af denne rapport viste, at både risikoen for forekomst af thermal 
runaway og mængden af udsendte giftige gasser, i høj grad afhænger af både batteriets 
generelle kemiske opbygning, og af hvor opladet batteriet er (SOC = state of charge). Det viste 
også, at selv identiske tests, kan skabe ret forskellige resultater, og at større batterier ikke 
nødvendigvis, er lig med større mængder udsendt HF.  Af samme grund bør ekstrapolering af 
opnåede resultater undgås, og er derfor ikke brugt i simuleringerne til denne rapport. 
Ved at implementere faktiske målte mængder af HF, fra et fuldskalaeksperiment, i en FDS-
model, har denne rapport undersøgt forskellige scenarier, hvor HF frigives fra en brand i Li-
ion-batteriet i en lille elbil, placeret i et lukket rum i form af en underjordisk parkeringskælder, 
som findes i virkeligheden og er placeret i hovedstadsområdet. 
Resultater målt 1,2 og 1,6 meter over gulvet viser, at de undersøgte farlige koncentrationer på 
30 PPM (IDLH), 34 PPM (AEGL 2, 30 minutter) og 62 (AEGL 3, 30 minutter) forekommer relativt 
hurtigt, når batteriets kasse først er kompromitteret. De viser også, at koncentrationer i 
garagen på tidspunktet for brandvæsenets antagede ankomst overstiger 150 PPM i visse 
områder af garagen, og fortsætter med at stige og nå værdier på op til ca. 800 PPM (målt 1,6 
meter over gulvet) i det tidsrum, hvor brandmænd forventes at være i nærheden af det 
brændende køretøj. Dette fordi Li-ion-batteribrande er selvoxiderende, og derved notorisk 
vanskelige at slukke ved hjælp af almindelige brandbekæmpelsesteknikker. 
Resultaterne illustrerer yderligere, den generelle opførsel af de giftige gasser, der akkumulerer 
i hjørner, hvor røgens vandring langs loftet kolliderer med lodrette bygningsdele såsom vægge 
og søjler. 
Da resultaterne målt 1,2 meter over gulvet konsekvent er lavere end dem, der måles 1,6 meter 
over gulvet, viser de samlede resultater af denne rapport, at brandmænd skal holde sig væk fra 
hjørner samt operere fra sænkede/knælende positioner, når det er muligt.

This report serves as the final exam of the education entitled “Master of fire safety engineering” 
(in Danish: Master i brandsikkerhed) at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) in Kongens

are Fire Chemistry (11B04) and CAD Fire (11B12).     
The topic of this report, was, for the longest time, supposed to be very different. It was supposed 
to revolve around the pressure building in a room, where a fire has started. This because this 
author does not agree, with the Danish building legislation concerning the required amount, 
and placement, of smoke detectors in residential homes. This topic however is already well 
documented, and a lot of studies and a lot of practical full-scale experiments, have been 
performed. Also… the chance of making an actual change, is less than miniscule, as statistics of 
annual fire casualties in Denmark, does not support the need of a change.

1.2 Motivation 
1.2.1 Dangers and pitfalls of innovational development  
For as long as people have been inventing different things, people have been focused on 
improving these inventions. Either for personal or for financial gain. The bow and arrow 
developed into a crossbow. The horse drawn carriage developed into a car. The canoe 
developed into a speed boat. A developed product was, almost by definition, a better product,

and a better product was equal to more success and better sales. Sometimes though the pace of 
development for certain products, reaches a point where negative side effects are not 
researched sufficiently. If a product shows sublime abilities in one or more aspects, it may 
outshine the fact, that if it is hurtful to humans or the environment in other aspects.   
 
From the 1930s until at least the mid-70s, because of its unique composition, asbestos 
showcased said sublime abilities within the areas of insulation and fire protection. For this 
reason, it was widely used in virtually every available type of building material used in that 
area. However, it has since been documented, that working with asbestos-containing materials, 
has caused numerous types of cancer, which is why it has since gradually been faced out, 
leading to its ultimate exclusion from involvement with building materials in 20051.  
 
1.2.2 Batteries and international environmental awareness 
As the market for battery driven consumer products grew, so did the market for batteries able 
to last long and recharge quickly. In this capacity the Li-ion battery excelled, and is now used 
for powering the vast majority of electrical equipment spanning from cellular telephones, 
tablets, power banks, laptop PCs…… and as of, at least relatively recently, various types of 
electrically powered automobiles.  
 
The globally growing environmental awareness, has apparently led to an increased demand for 
alternatively fueled vehicles, which in turn has led to the demand for longer and longer lasting 
batteries. More and more EVs and PHEVs on the streets worldwide, will inevitably increase the 
need for more and more charging stations. Many of these charging stations will be placed in 
underground parking facilities, as is the subject of this report.

1.2.3 Danish legislations regarding parking facilities      
According to the national Danish legislations and building codes [4], [5] and [6] no one room in 
a parking facility <1.000m2 are subject to any requirements involving neither automatic 
sprinkler- nor automatic fire ventilation systems.  
Given that the parking facility is reserved for users who are familiar with the building’s layout 
(category of use: AK1), the fire section can be as large as 2.000m2, and still not be required to 
be fitted with either of the two active firefighting systems, provided that the fire section is 
divided into rooms <1.000m2.  
As both automatic sprinklers and automatic fire ventilation systems, are relatively expensive, 
and require a certain degree of maintenance, the choice to divide fire section containing parking 
facilities into smaller rooms, are often the popular choice with Danish contractors.  
The result of this is, potentially, that a car fire in such a room, can carry on unobstructed and 
can furthermore spread to adjacent vehicles, if the fire brigade is not alarmed in time. And as 
vehicles have grown in physical size over the years, the existing parking spots have per 
definition grown smaller, which adds to the risk of fire spread, as the space between the vehicles 
have narrowed as a result.   
 
1.2.4 Recent major incidents involving parking facilities 
According to the chapters of the Eurocodes regarding fire design with steel structures [37, 
section 7.3], steel building components only need to be protected against the effect generated 
from a maximum of 5 vehicles burning simultaneously. However, at least two major incidents 
involving parking facilities, have occurred during the past five years. At both events, a 
considerably larger number of vehicles burnt at the same time.

1.2.4.1 King’s Dock, Liverpool (United Kingdom) 
On New Year’s Eve in 2017, a fire broke out in a Range Rover parked on the 3rd floor in the eight-
story parking garage on King’s Dock in downtown Liverpool. The facility was not equipped with 
automatic sprinklers, allowing the fire to tear through the bottom seven floors. During the fire 
temperatures reached in the excess of 1.200°C, and in the vicinity of 1.150 vehicles either burnt 
out completely, or were damaged beyond repair. Ultimately the facility was so badly damaged, 
that demolition was the inevitable consequence [8]. Figure 1-2 below shows the inferno as 
caught by CCTV-cameras. 
 
 
Figure 1-2 - Inferno at King's Dock, Liverpool. Source [8] 
Today the parking building has been rebuilt, and the new facility has been fitted with both 
passive- and active fire protection such as compartmentation and an automatic sprinkler 
system [9].  
 
1.2.4.2 Stavanger airport, Sola (Norway) 
Due to an electrical malfunction, a fire broke out in an Opel Zafira family sized vehicle, parked 
on the ground floor of the parking building at Stavanger Airport on January 7th, 2020 at 
approximately 15:25 in the afternoon. When the call came to the emergency dispatcher, it was 
reported that the car park was at capacity, and there was a risk of the initial fire spreading to 
3-4 adjacent vehicles. Not long after, it was reported that about ten vehicles were on fire. The 
fire brigade reached the scene approximately 20 minutes after the fire had started, and started 
to prepare fighting the fire. After another 35 minutes, it was reported, that the fire had spread 
to the floor above. Approximately two hours after the fire had started, the car park collapsed. 
Luckily, no lives were lost. Property loses however, was enormous as the building was full. 
Firefighting did not end until 18:00 the next day [10].

1.2.5.3 Hybrid electric vehicle (HEV) 
For a hybrid electric vehicle, the ICE is supported by a Li-ion battery pack, which both supplies 
the electric components in the car, and serves as a propulsion supplement. The battery is, much 
like the battery in an ordinary ICE vehicle, charged via a generator when the vehicle is driving. 
By calculating what is most profitable, the car switches between driving by electric- and 
fossilized power. During 2020 this type of vehicle though has, more or less, been overtaken by 
its plug-in counterpart (PHEV) as the percentage part of newly registered HEVs only increased 
by 4,65 – 4,35 = 0,3 percent points. The increase for PHEVs during that same period was 8,96 
– 1,67 = 7,29 percent points [12].

Working as a fire fighter, the concept of ‘safety first’ is deeply embedded in the values of both

1.2.7 Incident with HF gasses in Denmark 
My personal motivation was slightly elevated, as I learned that a Danish firefighter had been 
subjected to HF-toxication during a call, where the fire brigade responded to what turned out 
to be a fire, in a demounted Li-ion battery pack from an early edition Tesla Roaster. The battery 
sat in an auto mechanic shop, which, upon arrival, had been completely filled with thick black 
smoke, which had started to enter the adjacent premises. As common practice dictates, the 
firefighter team attacked the fire in close proximity, using a superior waterflow to extinguish 
the fire. During the job (which took an unusually long time to complete, considering the size of 
the burning item) the firefighter was subjected to large amounts of smoke, which is not at all 
uncommon, and is the very reason why firefighters wear protective gear consisting of heat 
retardant uniform, gloves, helmet, boots and breathing apparatus. However, particles of HF 
byproducts apparently got attached to the surface of the uniform (and potentially had 
accumulated inside the uniform), and upon removing the smoke diver mask and opening the 
jacket, after completing the extinguishing of the battery fire, the HF entered his airways, causing 
HF-toxication leading to a later hospitalization of the fire fighter, as the symptoms continued.  
This is a fair example of common firefighting practice, being used on a previously unknown 
subject on fire, causing unexpected problems and discomfort.

1.4.4 Time consumption of extinguishing effort by fire fighters 
In [3] Allan Skovlund reports that the average time used (called tsluk) for two firefighters to 
extinguish an average size vehicle fire is 4 minutes. This is provided that the burning vehicle is 
parked in open air, and has not yet spread to adjacent vehicles. Post extinguishment of glow-
fires is set at 3 minutes.  
As this report focuses on fires in underground parking garages, the time frame for the fire 
fighters to extinguish the fire, would ultimately be considerably longer, as additional time is 
needed to prepare the attack. Added to this, is the fact that the scenarios in this report all 
assume that thermal runaway has already occurred in the vehicle’s Li-ion battery. Though being 
relatively new in the firefighting community, this type of fire is already notoriously known, as 
being very difficult to extinguish, as the combustion of a Li-ion battery pack is self-oxidizing. 
The reason for this is that the batteries are fitted with protective cases, and are placed in 
inaccessible areas, making it difficult to pour water or other extinguishing agents directly on to 
the burning cells. Also, the risk of reignition is present virtually as soon as the cooling is 
removed, meaning that very large amounts of water is necessary. In 2015 the NFPA issued a 
revised Emergency Field Guide [17], which, among other things, include procedures for fighting 
fires in EVs. In this guide (p. 21) it is stated that testing has indicated that, depending on 
physical size and placement, Li-ion fires can require more than 2.600 gallons (= 9.841 liters) 
of water. The guide also (on p. 22) state that fire suppression can take more than an hour, and 
that reignition can occur anywhere from several hours to days, upon finished extinguishing. 
This makes it all the more important to have the wreck removed, and monitored as quickly as 
possible. 
This report will not elaborately focus on how long it takes to extinguish the fire but will rather 
focus on the amount of time it takes for the environment around the Li-ion fire to reach different 
thresholds of HF-concentrations. The concentrations will be illustrated using various slice files 
measuring HF in PPM. Thresholds are further described in chapter 2 - Theory. As the venue of 
this report revolves around a real-life carpark, taken into consideration will however be, how 
long it takes for the nearest fire station to reach the parking facility and get ready.

1.4.7 Fire spread from Li-ion fires    
Due to the flammability of the electrolyte used in most Li-ion batteries, the rupturing of a 
battery case caused by the pressure build-up from a thermal runaway, will, if ignited, result in 
the sudden appearance of spectacular jet-flames. Some EVs (e.g. Teslas) have fireproof 
protection placed between the battery and the passenger cabin, intended to stop a fire in the 
battery from spreading to the cabin. This will in many cases cause the jet flames to shoot out 
sideways from the battery. The velocity will result in the flame having a significantly higher 
than normal heat flux in the general direction of its trajectory, which in turn will increase the 
risk of fire spread compared to a normal vehicle fire.  
Figure 1-5 shows such a jet flame from an incident in Lake Forest, California in America where, 
on August 25 – 2017, a Tesla Model X had crashed into a residential garage, and caught fire due 
to a thermal runaway in the battery. After the vehicle and garage had been extinguished, and 
the vehicle was removed from the garage, the battery reignited after, for some time, having 
been spouting heavy white smoke.

imminent. Especially since the jet flame would be concentrated on the bottom of the

2.1 Hydrogen fluoride (HF) 
Hydrogen fluoride has the chemical formula HF and is colorless in both gas and liquid form. It 
contains fluorine and has a molar mass of 20,006 g/mol. It is completely soluble with water and 
when combined with liquid, it is called hydrofluoric acid. It is widely used in industrial 
processes around the world – mainly in the chemical industry, where it is used in the 
production of refrigerants. As much as 60% of all used HF serves this purpose. 
At high concentrations HF is highly toxic, as it easily and quickly goes through the skin and into 
the tissues of the body. This instantly converts the HF to hydrofluoric acid, which is both 
corrosive and toxic, and severely damages the cells, and causes them to not function properly. 
HF can be absorbed through the skin in both gas and liquid form. Damages caused by skin 
contact with HF can be severe burns, that does not occur until several hours after contact. If HF 
is inhaled, it damages the tissue of the lungs and can cause pulmonary edema (fluid 
accumulation in the lungs) [18]. While severe skin burns require a significant amount of HF 
over a period of time, damage to the lung tissue is “achieved” at much lower concentrations, so 
avoidance of HF getting into the airways of firefighters, should be a top priority of any fire 
brigade operations manager and the firefighters themselves.   
 
According to the specific procedure card regarding handling HF from BRS (see appendix 3) the 
thresholds for hazardous concentrations of HF are as listed in Table 2-1. The IDLH-level for HF 
is 30 PPM. The IDLH-level represents the maximum concentration of a given substance in 
atmospheric air, from where it is possible to escape within 30 minutes (without using raspatory

2.3 Safety features in Li-ion batteries  
Because of the potential collateral damages associated with a failure, one of the most important 
features of any energy storing component (here among batteries), is the safety feature. Of all 
the safety parameters concerning EV battery packs, thermal stability is perhaps the most 
important one. The reason for this, is that failure in one cell (low level), can rapidly escalate to 
much more severe failures, at higher levels impacting many more cells. This can ultimately lead 
to the ignition, and complete destruction, of the entire component. In the case of an electric 
vehicle, a failure, where the battery ignites, will likely result in the total loss of the vehicle. If the 
car is placed in a parking facility not equipped with active firefighting systems, the initial fire 
can spread to adjacent vehicles, and over time add to the combined list of losses, until the fire 
brigade is successful in extinguishing the fire.  
As individual safety features can fail, the combined safety of a battery pack cannot be evaluated 
by one alone. Different features are implemented to ensure that the risk of an event is reduced, 
and other features are implemented to make sure, that the severity of the outcome, in case of 
an event, is also reduced.  
This section of the report describes the different safety features implemented in the Li-ion 
battery packs. The devices are a priority in every step of the process, such as with material 
selection, design of the cells and overall pack, electric controls, etc. 
The different safety features most frequently implemented in modern Li-ion batteries are listed 
in Table 2-10. Estimates of failure rates in rechargeable Li-ion battery cells suggest that the 
implemented safety features work very well. Failure rates are estimated to ranging from 1 in 
10 million cells all the way up to 1 in 40 million cells [24].

2.4 Thermal runaway 
All the devices and features listed in Table 2-10 are, as mentioned earlier, implemented in the 
Li-ion batteries to ensure as large a degree of thermal stability as possible. The eventual 
absence of thermal stability, could result in the occurrence of a process which is accelerated by 
an increase in temperature, that in turn releases energy, that further increases the temperature. 
The process is thereby a sort of vicious cycle, known as a thermal runaway. It is a 3-step process 
which can end in an irreversible exothermic reaction, that will eventually result in the rapid 
release of volatile and explosive gasses, and the spontaneous ignition of the battery.  
I can be initiated by a variety of factors (mostly a type of abuse), which will be described in this 
section alongside an introduction of different real-life examples hereof. A diagram of the cyclic 
process of a thermal runaway is depicted in Figure 2-17 below.

The three linear functions 1, 2 and 3 represent the cooling (or heat removal) of a vessel at three 
different ambient temperatures A, B and C applying Newton’s law of cooling. The curved 
exponential function 4 (assuming Arrhenius equation) represents the generating of heat inside 
the vessel, created by chemical reactions. In the case of a Li-ion battery line 4 is the result of the 
combined chemical reactions, which occur during a thermal runaway. Line 1 has a sufficiently 
low temperature, as it has to intersection points - point E (which I stable) and point F (which is 
unstable) with line 4. Isothermal process, which is a thermodynamic process in which the 
temperature of a system (or vessel) is steady, is possible at both points. If the temperature at 
point F deviates downwards, the temperature will continue to drop until the stable point E is 
reached. This because the heat removal outweighs the heat generation. However, if the 
temperature deviates upwards, thermal runaway is inevitable.  
Line 2 does not have intersections with line 4, but has one tangent point – point D. Because the 
heat generation is exactly equal to the heat removal, this point is critical, and is rightfully named 
“Temperature of No Return” (TNR). With the battery’s intended boundary- and working 
conditions intact the equilibrium temperature is unchanged. But if either of those conditions 
change, thermal runaway will occur when the temperature reaches TNR.  
In the case of line 3 the cooling of the ambient temperature is insufficient, which will, under any 
circumstances, lead to the occurrence of a thermal runaway.  
The Semenov plots clearly and simply illustrate the thermal runaway process, and show that 
the heat generated by the chemical reaction inside the battery, dominate the thermal runaway, 
and is therefore the key issue, when understanding how to avoid such an event, and evaluate 
energy storage safety all together [22].   
As indicated in section 2.2.3 thermal runaway can occur at various temperatures, depending on 
the battery type and chemical composition. 
 
2.4.1 Causes of thermal runaway – thermal abuse 
If a battery is subjected to external abnormal conditions (for instance an abusive test), it may 
cause the failure of the battery, to be more hazardous. The external heat may cause internal 
short circuits within a cell leading to heat generation, which can spread to the adjacent cell and 
so on. This has been widely researched in the past 10 years, and have shown, that cells in a Li-
ion battery, subjected to thermal abuse, can be placed in to three intervals. The three-step

The definition of Tonset is usually defined as a self-heating rate of 0,2°C pr. min. Under normal 
circumstances the internal heat generated, can easily be dissipated within the battery pack and 
there is no danger of a thermal runaway. If this is not the case (because of the sustained 
exothermic reactions within the battery), the battery will enter stage 2, where the temperature 
starts to accelerate. The reactions during stage 2 depend both on which active chemicals are in 
the battery, and on the battery’s SOC. If the external heating is removed at this point, and the 
tear-away-tab mentioned in Table 2-10 is functional, the battery will release gasses, which will 
lower both the internal temperature, the internal pressure, the reaction rate, and ultimately 
result in the thermal runaway (stage 3) being avoided. In a real-life scenario, this could be the 
case if a fire has spread from an ICE vehicle to an EV or PHEV. The fire would act as the external 
heat abuse, causing the battery to reach stage 1 and stage 2. However, if the surrounding fire is 
successfully extinguished before the battery reaches stage 3, the thermal runaway is avoided. 
If a sustained external heating is present, the battery will enter stage 3 (thermal runaway) 
where the high-rate reactions between the anode and cathode will cause the temperature to 
rise rapidly. A thermal runaway is widely and loosely defined as a self-heating rate of 10°C (or 
above) pr. min. At this point it is unlikely, that any outside influence or cooling mechanism 
(including extinguishing water added directly on to the battery), can prevent the further 
development of the ongoing thermal runaway.  
 
To the knowledge of this author, the scenario described above regarding fire spread from and 
ICE to an EV (or even a separate EV fire), has yet to occur in a Danish carpark.

2.4.2 Causes for thermal runaway – physical abuse 
Physical abuse of a Li-ion battery such as a puncture or a crushing (or other type of sudden 
deformation) is one of the most difficult failures to prevent or defend against. The reason for 
this is that, that type of abuse contains the possibility of creating internal short circuits within 
the battery pack. This can alter the current flow in unfavorable and unintended fashions. If the 
electrolyte within the battery pack is flammable (as is often the case) a breach in the battery’s 
protective case or seal, alongside an ongoing thermal runaway, can allow for the combustible 
materials from the battery to escape and make up an increased risk of starting secondary fires 
as seen on Figure 2-12.  
 
In 2013 Elon Musk and the rest of Tesla ran into a PR nightmare, as one of their early versions 
of the Tesla Model S burst into flames. The reason was, that the unprotected Li-ion battery pack 
had been subjected to abuse while driving, as it had been struck by road debris, which had 
punctured the battery’s containment, causing thermal runaway - see Figure 2-20.

Short circuits can occur both externally and internally. The external short circuit is the most 
common type of Li-ion battery abuse, which is why safety test procedures for both shipping and 
using Li-ion batteries, contain a short circuit test.   
The internal short circuit happens when a current path within a cell develops, which can be 
brought on by a variety of reasons. One of these was mentioned in the previous section 
regarding physical abuse. Internal short circuits can also be caused by poor manufacturing 
processes, which was in fact the reason for the infamous Samsung Note 7-scandal of 2017, in 
which Samsung ended up recalling between 2,5 and 3,0 million Note 7 mobile devices, and 
cancelling the product completely. The recall was done in two tours.  
 
The problem with the first battery was, that the battery was too big for its case, which caused a 
deflection of the negative electrodes, to the point of where they touched the positive electrodes, 
resulting in internal short circuit which further lead to overheating and, in some cases, 
explosion. The problem is depicted in Figure 2-22.

2.5.1 Benefits of the solid-state battery 
The three most unpopular aspects of EVs, when asking the general public, are that they are too 
expensive, too slow to charge and have too small a range. These aspects may very well belong 
to the past, when commercial solid-state batteries are perfected and implemented in EVs. 
 
2.5.1.1 Greater energy density 
The solid-state batteries have an energy density (Wh per kg of mass) that is far superior to that 
of the Li-ion battery. This means that batteries of the same size as Li-ion batteries have a far 
greater range (up to two or three times as long), or that a vehicle can settle with a much smaller 
battery. This is possible as it is produced without the carbon-based anode, to hold the lithium 
ions when charged. In a solid-state battery the anode is simply put the electrical contact. This 
takes care of the range-part of the problems. Figure 2-25 shows the development of battery’s 
energy density from the 1970’s, to the present and into the future. Solid-state lithium metal

2.5.1.2 Faster charging 
Compared to Li-ion batteries, solid-state batteries charge much faster. Estimations from 
various experts and R&D-teams, is that a sold-state battery can charge to 80% in as little as 10-
15 minutes. This takes care of the charging time problem, although it is still slower than the gas 
pump used to “recharge” ICE vehicles. 
 
2.5.1.3 Longer life 
Solid-state batteries have a longer lifespan. The R&D-team at General Motors hope to one day 
produce a 1.000.000-mile EV battery, which would better balance the affordability equation, 
and in the process lower the concern of recycling an otherwise very large future amount of 
worn-out and discarded Li-ion batteries. 
 
2.5.1.4 Lower dangers in case of fire 
The solid-state batteries can be produced much smaller, as most of the safety features 
mentioned in section 2.3, are not necessary. The reason for this, is that a solid-state battery 
produces 70-80% less heat than its Li-ion counterpart. This is based on performance testing of 
a QuantumScape single-layer pouch cell prototype. Also, as solid-state batteries are basically 
incombustible, as they are produced without the very flammable liquid electrolyte. This means 
that the battery is much less prone to the occurrence of thermal runaways.  
This could take care of the problem, with very hazardous byproducts from battery fires, which 
are the focus of this report.

2.5.2 Hurdles of the solid-state battery 
When describing the solid-state battery, it sounds almost too good to be true – and at the time 
it is. The reason that we do not have them yet, is that they still have unresolved problems. These 
are described in this section. 
If the problems of the below mentioned hurdles are solved, it could ultimately render 
traditional Li-ion batteries obsolete. At least with regards to the batteries of EVs, as it could 
effectively deal with all the major reasons, as to why the average person is hesitant to invest in 
an EV. It would seem that Quantumscape (who is backed by Volkswagen) is well on their way 
to succeeding in this quest. 
 
2.5.2.1 Manufacturing costs 
Producing the solid-state battery is still incredibly expensive. At this point in time, the battery 
would out-cost the vehicle itself. In 2012 the price of producing a single 20AH (amp hour) 
battery cost $ 100.000 (US). To put it into perspective, an EV would require 1000 batteries, 
bringing the price tag for a single battery pack (and not including the vehicle itself) to a 
breathtaking $ 100.000.000 (US). But, as both Toyota and Volkswagen plan to release vehicles 
powered by solid-state batteries as early as 2024, it would seem that they either have done 
already, or are on the verge of, solving the cost-equation of this technology. It should though be 
mentioned that most technologies (Li-ion included) were very expensive in their initial stages. 
 
2.5.2.2 Dendrite formation 
Dendrites is a form of root or needle shaped structure that, in the early versions of solid-state 
technology, grows on the anode during charging. With every charge, the dendrite formations 
grow larger. When the structures grow across the dividing layers of the battery, internal short 
circuit occurs, and the battery ultimately fails. Quantumscape has seemingly solved this 
problem by adding a ceramic separator, that hinders the formation of dendrites. 
 
2.5.2.3 Cracking of battery casing 
During charge and discharge, and the subsequent heating and cooling, the battery casing 
expands very slightly, which can lead to micro-cracking. Any battery is heavily reliant on the 
integrity of the casing, as in contains the tight and precise arrangement of all its components. If 
micro cracking occurs in the casing, the battery is subjected to possible contamination from the 
outside, which can lead to the battery failing.

3.1.1 Source of ignition 
In all scenarios, given in table 6.1, the source of ignition is either a battery package of an EV that 
goes into thermal runaway, or a gasoline tank that starts spill gasoline and thus follows an 
ignition. This means that in all scenarios, the source of ignition is underneath a vehicle. 
3.1.2 Thermal Runaway 
When thermal runaway of a battery is initiated, it is assumed that the entire battery will burn. 
Disregarding any form of sectioning of the batteries, that may have been. 
3.1.3 Ignition of gasoline 
Gasoline spill ﬁres of the ICE vehicles can be individually ignited, when the wall temperature of 
the &OBST reaches 140 °C, following an individual &RAMP, based on that validated in section 
5.6. 
3.1.4 Ignition of plastics 
All implemented combustible plastics, is designed to be able to undergo pyrolysis. This is done 
by applying a REFERENCE_TEMPERATURE at the &MATL line. The degassed species will then 
be able to undergo a reaction with oxygen, when their auto ignition temperature is reached and 
there is sufﬁcient amount of oxygen, see also section 3.1 and 3.2. 
3.1.5 Conduction between solids 
The current version of FDS is not able to calculate conduction between two solid obstacles that 
are placed right next to each other (McGrattan, Hostikka, Randall McDermott, et al., 2020). In 
order to overcome this a space of at least one cell thickness can be wedged in between the solids 
or in cases where it is possible one obstacle can be deﬁned with varying materials on its 
surfaces. This is amongst other done by the seats, as they hover above the ﬂoor in the car. 
3.1.6 Conduction from battery 
In continuation of the challenges with conduction between solids, a signiﬁcant assumption 
regarding conduction from the battery in EVs has been made. The obstacle making up the 
battery have been modelled with its bottom and side surfaces as battery surfaces, see sec. 3.3.1. 
Whereas, the top surface has been modelled as a steel surface. Meanwhile, there has been 
created a hole in the bottom of the chassis in the extent of the battery. This is done to ensure 
that there will be conduction from the battery to the passenger compartment. Which would not 
be possible if the battery was placed just underneath the chassis without any modiﬁcations.

Assuming four passenger seats of 550kW and a dashboard of 700kW [31, Fig. 26.94] brings the 
total HRR to 4.438kW, which corresponds fairly well to the measured PHHR of 4,7MW from the

In [28] though, the authors went to a great deal of trouble to ensure that the results of the 
simulations were as realistic as possible. Validation of the grid size for all sub-models (interior- 
and exterior plastic, tire rubber, battery pack, etc.) and of the full combined model was 
investigated for cell-sizes varying from 2,5cm, 5cm and 10cm. As results from both the sub-
models and the full validation model was accurate to a precision of a maximum of 11%, a cell 
size of 10x10x10cm was proven to be the most case sufficient with regards to energy release. 
This report will not challenge the elaborate validation made in [28], and a cell size of 10cm is 
initially chosen for the meshes in immediate vicinity of the fire, where firefighters would 
expectedly be operating, when fighting the vehicle fire. 
  
3.2.3.1 Validation of flow fields 
A validation of the flow fields is however necessary, as it determines the accumulations of 
smoke and other toxic byproducts. In [30] experiments performed by Tuomo Rinne et al. 
comparisons were made between concentrations of toxic smoke acquired from experimental 
testing and FDS simulations, respectively. In the FDS simulations of this report, cell sizes of both 
10cm and 20cm were used. The results from the full-scale experimental tests almost 
consistently corresponded most accurately with the measurements from the FDS model, that 
applied meshes with 20x20x20cm cells. The geometry of the test setup in [30] was however 
much more simplistic, compared to the geometry of the Excel Workbook used in [28].  
For this report, a model of the semi enclosure from scenario 2 was modeled with cell sizes of 
5cm, 10cm and 20 cm. The object was to compare HF-concentrations in the far-field away from 
the fire, where the plume-jets along the ceiling collides with an obstacle (wall or pillar) and 
causes turbulence, and an increase in concentration follows. The value of 62ppm (AEGL 3) was 
chosen as the benchmark, to be compared between the models. 
Note: the model with the 20cm mesh also contained a 10cm mesh around the vehicle, as it 
would otherwise not run, due to the geometry of the modelled vehicle. The mesh in the far-field 
is however modeled with 20 cm cells. Figure 3-7 shows the comparison of the 10cm and the 20 
cm meshes, and Figure 3-8 shows the comparison of the 5cm and the 10cm meshes.

When deciding on the cell sizes of an FDS-model, it is important to take both the precision and 
simulation time into consideration. As obstacles, devices, vents, etc. in the FDS model adjusts 
themselves to the nearest cell boundary line (see Figure 3-10) it is simplest to alter the sizes 
of the cells by either doubling or halving all three sides of a single cell. It is though important 
to remember that halving the sides of a single cell, results in the total number of cells of that 
mesh increasing by a factor of 8. This will naturally also increase the computational 
simulation time by a factor of 8. This procedure can however be reversed, in order to reduce

as the one tested in [27] might not burn hot and/or long enough to spread to the rest of the 
vehicles combustible material (seats, dashboard, A/C, etc.). This can depend on the battery’s 
overall geometry and placement in the vehicle, and safety measurements implemented around 
the battery. In the case of scenario 1, the potential HF derived from the refrigerants in the 
vehicle’s A/C-system (the initial spike shown in Figure 3-5) might not get emitted, due to the

As the results obtained in [27] are rather outdated, it is conceivable that the considerably larger 
Li-ion batteries of modern EVs, would result in a much hotter and longer fire, leading to a total 
vehicle loss. In case of a complete vehicle fire, the pattern of dispersion would most likely be 
different, as the elevated thermal inertia in the plume from the larger fire, would create larger 
and faster ceiling jets, which might push the HF closer to the floor upon colliding with an 
obstacle.      
  
The results also indicate, that different concentrations of HF, critical to unprotected humans, 
occur rather rapidly upon the rupture of the battery’s protective casing. This would though 
most likely lead to uncomfortable rather than hazardous situations for people in the parking 
garage, in the initial stages of the fire, as irritation of the eyes occurs at only 5 PPM as stated in 
Table 2-1. Provided that necessary egress routes are available, they will have time to escape 
before being subjected to critical conditions with regards to HF. Critical conditions related to 
visibility caused by smoke will occur first. 
 
Patterns of dispersion at both 1,2 meters and 1,6 meters above the floor show, that the elevated 
concentrations of HF occur first along walls and especially in corners, where the velocity of the 
jets along the ceiling collide with the walls, and start to accumulate and the concentrations of 
HF increases. The results also show that the different concentrations are reached faster at 1,6 
than 1,2 meters above the floor, meaning that firefighters would be advised to operate from 
kneeled positions rather than standing, whenever possible. Especially since the conditions for 
the scenario has the entrance/exit being open, meaning that certain quantities of HF are 
continuously being vented out to the exterior surroundings. If the underground parking garage 
had been fitted with a gate, all emitted quantities of HF would be confined to the room, and the 
concentrations would therefore be higher. 
 
With an open entrance/exit significant quantities of HF is being emitted to the surroundings, 
where the remaining firefighting crew + operations manager is. Even at 1,2 meters above the 
floor the levels for AEGL 3 is reached, which show that all firefighters not equipped with 
respiratory protection, should steer clear of any contact with the smoke plumes, as it can 
contain relatively hazardous amount of toxic HF.  
 
Lastly the results indicate that the smoke divers from the respective fire brigades might very 
well encounter, and have to operate in very contaminated environments with very large 
concentrations of HF (more than 5 times the listed IDLH-levels) for the duration of the time it

With regards to concentrations of HF at the different measuring points 1-4, the ABV-system,

hindering them in accumulating to hazardous concentrations early on. This means that the HF-
ramp in FDS needs to almost reach its peak, where the emission rate exceeds 1 g/s, before 
concentrations begins to accumulate and reach IDLH, AEGL 2 and AEGL 3. In a direct point-to-

at the entrance/exit. This is caused by the ABV-system dragging 13 m3/s of make-up air through 
the opening. This means that the flow direction is reversed, from going out of the entrance, to 
going from the entrance to the exhaust vents in the garage. This hinders the smoke from 
reaching the entrance. 
 
The removal of toxic emissions can seem like a very positive result. However, looking at the 
bigger picture, the ABV-system will do nothing to hinder fire spread between vehicles. On the 
contrary, Figure 4-11 shows that it increases the PHHR compared to scenario 1, meaning that 
the ABV-system ends up serving an unintended purpose, comparable to a blacksmith’s bellow, 
which feeds oxygen to the fire, and keeping it going and even making it grow.  
 
In a garage full of vehicles, the ABV-system might hinder the other vehicles from igniting, due 
to heat radiating from the smoke layer, but it would more than likely still result in the fire 
growing uncontrollably, as the fire would instead spread from vehicle to vehicle because of an 
increased radiation level from the initial fire, caused by the continuous addition of fresh oxygen.

in order to either slow down the, already ongoing, thermal runaway as shown on Figure 2-17, 
or possibly prevent a thermal runaway from occurring altogether. This would most likely 
require the assistance of one more team of smoke divers.

5 Conclusion  
 
5.1 Initial theoretical research 
The initial research for this report more than anything showed, that the properties and 
behaviors of a combusting Li-ion battery is not an exact science, as testing of identical batteries 
under identical conditions provided rather different results.  
5.1.1 Toxic emissions 
The research also showed, that the quantities of emitted toxic gasses, such as HF, depend on a 
wide variety of different factors.  
As the HF primarily stems from the combustion of the Li-ion battery’s fluid electrolyte (often 
LiPF6), the general chemical composition of the battery determines the reactions in the 
battery’s thermal runaway stages, leading to the emission of, among other gasses, HF. The 
quantity of emitted HF also relies on the battery’s SOC and internal construction, as numerous 
safety measures are taken, to heighten the battery’s overall thermal stability as much as 
possible.  
Research concerning the testing of small Li-ion batteries, show that the results obtained, should 
not be extrapolated, in order to be able to indicate emissions of quantities for larger batteries. 
Extrapolation is a procedure connected with a high degree of uncertainty, and extrapolated 
results can therefore be very misleading and unrealistic.  
5.1.2 Thermal runaway 
The initial research showed that the occurrence of a thermal runaway in a Li-ion battery is a 
self-oxidizing and self-enhancing process, which can be initiated by a variety of different 
reasons including: thermal abuse, physical/mechanical abuse, charge failures and internal 
short circuit. The fact that burning Li-ion batteries are self-oxidizing, makes them very difficult 
for the fire brigade to handle, as they cannot simply apply traditional firefighting techniques 
such as oxygen deprivation derived from applying water mist. At least not as efficiently, and 
with an increased risk of numerous reignitions, until the energy of the battery has been fully 
combusted. Cooling the battery will slow down the thermal reactions in the battery, but will not 
stop it completely.     
 
5.2 FDS results 
The results of the different fire scenarios of this report, show that results from measuring HF 
emissions achieved from full-scale testing, can be implemented in FDS simulations applying 
CFD. Doing so, slice files placed in different heights, can illustrate how the quantities of HF 
behave when being emitted in confined spaces. 
 
5.2.1 Influence of model geometry 
The results obtained in all four scenarios show, that the disperse patterns and the placement of 
accumulations of critical concentrations of HF, highly rely on the geometry of the modelled 
parking garage, as said accumulations occur when the horizontal ceiling jets cause the emitted 
gasses to collide, with vertical obstacles such as walls and pillars. This means that elevated

5.2.2 Hazardous concentrations of HF 
This report has investigated at what time steps, the concentrations of IDLH (30 PPM), AEGL 2 
(34 PPM) and AEGL 3 (62 PPM) occurs at different places in the garage for every scenario. The 
points for measuring concentrations of HF was placed in close proximity of the vehicle where 
firefighters would be expected to be, when fighting the fire. Also, a point was placed by the 
entrance, where toxic gasses are vented to the exterior surroundings, where the rest of the fire 
crew would be. 
Concentrations of each point was measured in both 1,6 meters and 1,2 meter above the floor, 
to represent both a standing and a kneeling firefighter. The occurrence of concentrations far 
greater than the IDLH and AEGL-thresholds was also proven, reaching levels as high as 800 
PPM – illustrated in appendix 8. 
 
5.2.2.1 General conclusion regarding HF-emissions 
This report has shown, that a fire in a single small EV placed alone in an underground parking 
garage, can create toxic environments. And, compared to the standards of the present day, the 
EV used in the scenarios of this report (from 2012) contained a relatively small Li-ion battery 
pack (23,5kWh). In the present day, a battery of that size might almost be found in a PHEV.  
 
All the same, when placed in a confined space, the emitted quantities from even a small Li-ion 
battery pack, creates problems in the form of very toxic environments. And though larger 
batteries do not necessarily equal larger amounts of emitted HF, it is both conceivable and 
plausible, that a fire in e.g. a Tesla Model S’s 100 kWh battery pack (one of the largest available

larger amounts of HF over a prolonged period of time.  
 
It should also be mentioned, that should the firefighters be successful in applying water directly 
to the burning Li-ion battery, the emission rate of HF would immediately increase (as proven 
in [15]) and thereby further contaminate the atmosphere in the garage.   
 
With regards to the incident with HF-toxication of the Danish firefighter mentioned in appendix 
2, one can of course only speculate about the concentrations of HF present in the room upon 
arrival. But considering a smaller room, no ventilation of toxic gasses until long after the arrival

firefighters, correctly and according to the SOPs, spent the first 15 minutes handling and 
removing LPG-vessels, the HF-concentrations in the room must have been very high. Especially 
since they were able to apply water directly on to the burning battery cells.    
 
5.2.2.2 Scenario 1 and 2 
Scenario 1 and 2 contains conditions where a thermal runaway causes the protective casing of 
the Li-ion batteries to rupture, and emit toxic HF-gasses in the garage. In these scenarios 
nothing is done actively to reduce the quantities of HF.  
 
Results of both of the two simulations, showed that the investigated concentrations occur 
relatively quickly at the respective measuring points.  
As the vehicle in scenario 2 is placed in a semi-enclosure (only two parking spaces) the lowest 
of the investigated concentrations occur almost instantly. The placement in a semi-enclosure 
also results in a slightly lowered PHHR, which is caused by a decreased amount of available 
oxygen.  
 
At the time where the fire brigade would be expected to arrive (after 16 minutes), the 
concentrations in almost the entire garage reach in the excess of 100 PPM at 1,6 meters above 
the floor. Certain places in the garage near walls and in corners, it is even higher. According to 
the time lapse in appendix 8, concentrations of HF continue to increase, to ultimately reach 
values of approximately 800PPM. At 1,2 meters above the floor, the concentrations are 
significantly lower for both scenarios. This indicates that fire fighters should fight the fire from 
kneeled positions whenever possible, and reduce the amount of time spent near walls and 
corners to a minimum. 
 
High concentrations of HF are even occurring at 1,2 meters above the floor by the entrance/exit, 
where the remaining fire fighters, team leader and operation manager would be. This indicates 
that firefighters not working as smoke divers, should avoid any contact with smoke plumes, 
unless they are equipped with respiratory protection.

5.2.2.3 Scenario 3 
Scenario 3 is identical to scenario 1, but is fitted with an automatic fire ventilation system 
(ABV), which starts upon the detection of smoke, which is allowed by the Danish building codes 
as a pre-accepted solution. The capacity is 13 m3/s divided evenly over two exhaust vents.  
The presence of an ABV-system makes a drastic difference with regards to elevated 
concentrations of HF in the entire garage. Unfortunately, it also causes the HRR to increase 
significantly, as the ABV-system continuously adds fresh oxygen to the fire. In the event of a full 
garage, this could result in the fire growing uncontrollably.  
It is therefore the conclusion of this report, that ABV-systems (both natural and mechanical) 
should be avoided in carparks, and should be removed as a pre-accepted solution in the Danish 
building codes.    
 
5.2.2.4 Scenario 4 
Scenario 4 was different from the others, as the fire did not originate with a thermal runaway 
in a Li-ion battery pack. The fire originated in an ICE-vehicle, and had to spread to the adjacent 
EV in the neighboring parking spot.  
Results show that the fire spread from the ICE-vehicle to the cabin of the EV, and emissions of 
HF was relatively modest, and did not occur until almost 30 minutes after the fire had started. 
Moreover, the elevated concentrations did not occur near the burning vehicles, but only in 
corners of the garage, where firefighters would not be.  
This could indicate that the fire brigade might have enough time, to identify the adjacent 
vehicle, and help to either slow down, or completely hinder, a thermal runaway.   
     
5.3 Firefighter safety  
The results of this report have shown, that a fire in an EV Li-ion battery pack placed in an 
underground (or otherwise enclosed) carpark, can potentially create very toxic environments 
for the firefighters. If the vehicle is removed, the emitted gasses are still toxic and potentially 
dangerous, but are no longer accumulating in a confined space to hazardous concentrations. 
The primary job should therefore be, to have the vehicle removed from the garage as quickly as 
possible. If possible, the vehicle (or at least the Li-ion battery pack) should be fully immersed 
in water in e.g. a container.  
 
Even though the general risk of a fire is not increased by having EVs and charging stations 
placed in carparks, the consequence of one, is potentially far larger, than with a fire in an ICE-
vehicle. Not only because of the elevated emissions of HF, but also because of the prolonged 
duration of the fire caused by the battery-pack, which could ultimately compromise the 
structural integrity of the loadbearing building elements.  
 
It is therefore the recommendation of this report, that Danish building codes should be updated, 
with information specific to both placement of charging stations and electrically powered 
vehicles in general. One requirement could be, that charging stations are placed as close to the 
entrance as possible. This to ensure, that the fire brigade can remove the extinguished 
wreckage, as easily and quickly as possible before it reignites. This solution will however expire, 
==PAGE:77==

when the amount of charging stations increases, in accordance with the increased demand for 
them, as the number of registered EVs grow larger.  
  
A study has been conducted by MSB, on the HF’s ability to penetrate a firefighter’s protective 
attire, when operation in such toxic environments. Luckily, the amount of HF penetrating the 
suit, that can thereby potentially reach the fire fighters skin, is very limited. The study also 
showed that even for sweaty skin (which absorbs 8 times as much HF as dry skin), it would 
take a very long time in a very toxic environment, before a lethal quantity of HF have been 
absorbed through the skin. It might therefore be possible for firefighters to operate for longer 
periods of time, than recommended in [36], but this will have to be investigated further, as the 
concentrations obtained in [35] is nowhere near the concentrations found in the scenarios of 
this report. 
 
While the threat of absorbing HF through the skin, might therefore not the primary safety focus, 
getting even small quantities of HF into the airways, should be avoided at any cost. This because, 
with regards to inhalation, HF differs from e.g. CO and CO2, that, unlike HF, are very common 
byproducts from various combustions. Inhaling CO and CO2 is dangerous as it uses the lungs’ 
function, causing asphyxia when the blood cells deliver CO or CO2 to the body’s vital organs 
instead of oxygen as intended. This process can however be reversed if caught in time, and the 
lungs’ subjection to CO or CO2 is stopped, and oxygen is re-applied. Inhalation of HF can 
however severely damage the tissue in the lungs themselves, as the HF combines with the 
moisture of the lungs, creating hydrofluoric acid, which can lead to irreversible respiratory 
difficulties (requiring surgical procedures), or even death. Even at very low concentrations.    
 
As HF can get attached to the firefighter’s suit (and possibly accumulate inside the uniform), 
and from here enter the airways when the mask is removed, the standard SOPs for removing 
equipment upon completing a smoke diver task should be reversed when involving EVs. This 
means that the mask and air supply should be the last thing removed from the fire fighter, as 
mentioned in BRS’s leaflet on procedures regarding EVs [36].  
This will require the assistance of a colleague, which should be protective gear. A suggestion of 
the order of removal of protective gear, is listed and shown below.  
 
1. Firefighter gloves are removed and placed remotely downwind from the crew 
2. Disposable rubber gloves are applied  
3. Helmet is removed and placed with gloves 
4. Wearing gloves and dust mask (or maybe smoke diving equipment), a colleague helps 
remove respiratory protective gear. 
 
5. Jacket and pants are removed, and placed with helmet and gloves while colleague helps 
6. Smoke diver takes a deep breath, removes smoke diver mask and applies dust mask, 
and continues to remove the garments underneath the firefighting uniform.

6 Possibilities for fire brigades 
This section of the report shows and describes some of the new tools developed in recent years, 
which are specifically designed to help firefighters better cope with the new challenges of Li-
ion fires. Some of them have already been implemented in daily operations, and others are still 
in the preliminary test phases of development. Some are designed specifically for fighting EV-
fires and others serve multiple firefighting purposes.  
 
6.1 EV container  
After a thorough review of the literature in the field as well as a study of actual EV fires, 
BEROS, as the first fire brigade in northern Europe, co-developed the container with Jøni 
Aabybro Aps. It is designed to supply the fire brigade with a possibility to place an EV, or a 
battery pack, in an almost fully enclosed space, after the initial fire has been extinguished. The 
container sits on the rescue crane vehicle, and is driven by the regular on-call firefighters. 
According to Nikolaj Lund Marquart (operations manager, BEROS) the EV container auto-
matically gets called to alarms involving EVs, PHEVs and hydrogen powered vehicles.  
The container can work both while still on the truck, and when placed on the ground – see 
Figure 6-1.   
 
Figure 6-1 - EV container. Source: BEROS (used with permission) 
It is fitted with a 1.000 l/min pump, which can be used to completely fill the container with 
water mist, which will help ensure, that an extinguished Li-ion battery does not reignite.  
It can be opened in both the end opposite the pump arrangement, and in the top. Opening the 
container lid allows the rescue crane to lower the vehicle into the container from above. As a 
rule of thumb, the lifting capability of the crane is, that it can lift 21 tons at a 1-meter extension, 
and 1 ton at a 21-meter extension, which allows it to remove most normal sized personal 
vehicles even from semi large distances.  
When the vehicle/wreckage or battery pack is in the container, it can safely be moved to a 
desolate area, where the fire can be handled. On a trial basis the container has been fitted with 
an inert gas-system supplied by the Danish company Fire Eater A/S.    
With regards to fires situated in underground carparks, the EV container has the immediate 
disadvantage, that it is too big to enter the carpark and make use of the crane. However, 
sometime in the future, the EV container will arrive on site with a remote-controlled diesel-
powered forklift placed inside the container. Being powered by an internal combustion engine, 
the forklift could have trouble operating in smoke filled areas, and will therefore be fitted with

This forklift can then be used to remove (by lifting or dragging) burnt out wreckages from

6.2 Rosenbauer Crawler  
HBR of Copenhagen, Denmark is currently working with Rosenbauer International AG 
(Germany) in developing a small, tracked vehicle called Crawler. It is still in the very early 
stages of development but weighs in at 350kg (with possibility of adding extra weight if 
necessary), is powered by Li-ion technology and is operated remotely. When combined with 
the attachable “vehicle roller skates” as shown in Figure 6-2, it may be able to pull vehicles from 
e.g., underground parking areas. 
 
Figure 6-2 - Rosenbauer Crawler dragging a vehicle. Source: HBR (used with permission) 
This however requires for the vehicle to at least be resting on the rim of the wheels, to be able 
to mount the “roller skates”. If these are not mounted, the Crawler will most likely (due to its 
comparatively low weight) not be able to move the vehicle. Even less if the firefighters have 
made the floor wet, which would inevitably make it harder for the Crawler to achieve the 
sufficient traction.   
The crawler is however intended as becoming almost a firefighting Swiss Army knife. So instead 
of performing one type of task really well, it can serve numerous purposes decently. According 
to Allan Skovlund from HBR, it is the aim that the Crawler will have the option of being fitted 
with both camera (with both infrared and thermal properties) and a small water cannon. Being 
powered by Li-ion batteries it is also less affected by operating in confined smoke-filled spaces.

6.4 EV Firefighter 
The most severe firefighting problem with regards to fires in EVs/PHEVs and their respective 
Li-ion batteries in general, is accessing the battery itself, and thereby having a fighting chance 
of applying extinguishing water directly onto the battery, which will help slow down the 
exothermic reaction in the progressing thermal runaway.  
As the batteries of EVs and PHEVs are very well protected this problem is not specific to vehicles 
placed in underground car parks.  
Jøni Aabybro A/S (by Jan Nyrup Jensen), who built BEROS’s EV Container, has since developed 
a so-called EV Firefighter. An arrangement constructed in lightweight aluminum, that allows 
the firefighters to attach their main fire hose to the rig, creating a cloud of water mist, that can 
then be pushed underneath the vehicle. The water mist is hereby applied directly to the bottom 
of the vehicle, where the Li-ion battery is placed in the majority of EVs, that is inaccessible to 
regular fire hoses and jets. A potential added bonus is, that the small droplets of the water mist, 
might wash out/bind some of the emitted HF, leaving less of it to be dispersed in the 
surroundings, where it can get caught on the firefighter’s protective attire.

6.5 Fireproof blanket  
The Norwegian company Bridgehill have in collaboration with the American company Darley® 
invented a fire blanket, that can easily fit onto a firetruck. Full scale tests show, that the blanket 
effectively contains both flames, smoke and other toxic byproducts and quickly extinguishes 
vehicle- and even EV fires. See Figure 43.  
If applied to EVs, the blanket should be left on the vehicle for a longer period of time, compared 
to ICE vehicles, as removing it too quickly, will cause the battery pack to reignite. This also 
means, that the vehicle cannot be moved from its original position. 
The blanket is 6 x 8 meters making it large enough to cover even large SUVs. The blanket can 
be handled with as little as two persons, is reusable up to 30 times and can withhold 
temperatures up to 2500°C (>4500°F). A product such as this one would make a lot of sense, 
for firefighters to implement in their SOPs for fighting vehicle fires.  
For the blanket to work, it does however require for the fire fighters to be able to, first of all, 
see the burning vehicle, which may not be the case in an underground carpark, like the one 
implemented in the scenarios of this report. Due to its relatively small volume, it will quickly 
fill up with smoke, if not equipped with ABV. It will also require for the firefighters to have a 
certain amount of maneuverability around the vehicle. As modern vehicles are getting bigger 
and bigger, the existing parking spaces are, by definition and as earlier mentioned, getting 
smaller and smaller. Vehicles parked close to each other, as is often the case in car parks, could 
result in decreased maneuverability for the firefighters, and hereby more difficulty applying the 
blanket safely and correctly. It could however be an idea to apply the blanket after the initial

7 Outlook 
A report such as this one must be relatively concrete, with regards to extent of the chosen topic. 
This to ensure, that the author can manage the expected output, and it does not get too 
comprehensive.  
Therefor this chapter is used to list and briefly describe related ideas, tests, input, etc. that for 
one reason or another, did not make it into this report, but could be interesting for the author 
or someone else, to elaborate on in the future. 
 
7.1 Sprinkler or water mist’s effect on smoke plume from a Li-ion fire  
As mentioned in section 1.4.5, an activated sprinkler system could in theory have the added 
benefit, of washing out some of the HF emitted from an EV-fire. In this case a water mist system 
should be superior to traditional sprinkler systems, as the combined surface on the droplets, to 
where the HF would bind, is far larger. It is however possible, that the sprinkler water or water 
mist enters the plume with too high a velocity, for the HF to have a “chance” to bind to the water 
surfaces.  
An unfortunate side effect could be, that the velocity of the applied water could cause air ripples 
and turbulence, that could disperse the emitted concentrations of HF in unforeseen directions, 
and maybe even help the HF attach itself to the fire fighters protective gear. 
It could be interesting to see this tested in a controlled environment, where water and water 
mist is applied at different velocities to the plume of a Li-ion fire, and not on the battery pack 
itself.   
 
7.2 Emission of explosive gasses from an extinguished Li-ion fire  
Even an extinguished Li-ion battery pack can over time emit large quantities of flammable 
gasses, which, in confined spaces and if unignited, can cause a significant gas explosion at a later 
time, as described in section 2.2.4.  
It could be interesting to conduct a study of quantities and emission rates of a variety of Li-ion 
batteries in controlled environments, where emitted gasses are manually kept from igniting. 
For instance, by adding inert gasses as performed by Ulrikka Bergström et al. in [13]. 
  
7.3 Alternative extinguishing agents than water 
Naturally, water is the predominant extinguishing agent used by firefighters all over the world. 
One of the downsides of using water is that it can act as a conductor of electrical charges. Cold 
water can slow down the reactions of a thermal runaway, but will have to be applied 
continuously over a long period of time. If the battery pack can be fully submerged in water, 
this is the preferred method, as it can lower the amount of water needed for the job. If a battery 
pack is submerged, the water should afterwards maybe be handled with safety procedures used 
for toxic waste.   
It could be interesting to investigate if another extinguishing agent than water (powder, foam, 
gel, etc.) should be preferred for Li-ion fires.

7.4 Acidity of extinguishing water from Li-ion fires 
As mentioned above in section 7.3 the water used for full submersion of a Li-ion battery pack 
in thermal runaway conditions, could in theory be toxic, as all of the emitted HF would bind to 
the water transforming into a low concentration hydrofluoric acid.  
As many of the newly developed protocols for fighting Li-ion fires (e.g. NFPA in [13]) 
recommends the use of a very large amount of water, it is conceivable that the concentrations 
of HF in the total amount of applied water is very low, but a study on the matter could be very 
interesting.   
 
7.5 Elevated risk of fire spread due to jet-flames from Li-ion fires 
As shown in Figure 1-5 fires in large EV battery packs, can result in spectacular jet flames 
caused by the pressure build-up, and subsequent tear, in the battery’s protective case. As most 
EVs have applied safety measures to prevent a battery fire from entering the cabin, these jet 
flames often shoot out sideways, which in a car park with narrowly parked cars could result in 
a faster vehicle to vehicle fire spread. 
It could be interesting to investigate if there in fact is an elevated risk of fire spread from EVs 
compared to their ICE counterparts. This investigation will most likely come in the shape of 
experience over time rather than testing, as testing would be very expensive.    
 
7.6 EVs in fully automatic and multi-level carparks 
As establishing a sufficient number of parking spot in big cities, is becoming increasingly more 
difficult, more and more parking garages, both under- and above ground, implement the use of 
fully automatic robots, which stack the parked vehicles in several layers. This can result in a 
vastly increased risk of a fire spreading rapidly from the initial vehicle, as the neighboring 
vehicles can be parked directly above it. In this case a traditional sprinkler system would not 
stop the fire from spreading, as the water would have to run off the vehicles on top before 
reaching the car on fire. The sprinkler system would have to, somehow, have nozzles installed 
above each parking space. 
Removing a burnt-out car could also become a big challenge, as the robot’s hoisting mechanism 
could very well get damaged in the fire.  A study on how to effectively and properly fire proof 
the robots, would be very interesting. 
In the case of parking garages, where the user parks his/her vehicle at street level, from which 
a robot places the vehicle below ground, it can be very challenging for the fire brigade to reach 
the fire. A study on preventing- or fighting of fires in such an establishment, could also be very 
interesting.
