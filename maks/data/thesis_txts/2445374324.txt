Abstract
Shrinkage cracking in concrete is a well known phenomena. However, less is known
about how reinforcement inﬂuences the shrinkage cracking by its restraining eﬀect
and how this could potentially aﬀect the stiﬀness of a structure. This report investi-
gates this matter, by conducting several experiments with concrete slabs of diﬀerent
reinforcement ratios. The shrinkage crack widths are measured and the compression
stiﬀness is tested. This is done to get an understanding of whether the reinforcement
causes any cracking and if the compression stiﬀness is reduced thereof. It was found
that the reinforcement caused larger crack widths. When test prisms were loaded
in compression, it was expected that a prism with reinforcement would have a more
steep response than a prism with less or no reinforcement, when the cross section
was not transformed for the eﬀect of the reinforcement bar. Therefore, it was found
that the higher reinforcement ratios resulted in a larger reduction of the concrete
stiﬀness. This tendency however, was not as clear when the concrete stiﬀness was
isolated by removing the reinforcement and testing again.

Introduction
In the theory of plasticity, it is a well-known fact that the strength of concrete which
is observed when testing a structure can be much diﬀerent from the strength found
from standard laboratory test prisms. This diﬀerence is mainly due to cracking. The
concrete strength, which is to be plugged into the theoretical solutions, is therefore
reduced by multiplication with the eﬀectiveness factor, often called ν. It follows that
ν ≤1. The primary reasons for the strength reduction is (a) reduction due to to
microcracking before any external load is applied, (b) reduction due to load-induced
microcracking and (c) reduction due to macrocracking. Furthermore, (a) is assumed
to happen due to shrinkage in the cement paste, which is restrained by unhydrated
cement grains and by aggregate particles. [5]. In DS/EN 1992-1-1 under section 5.4,
it is stated that if an element is based to behave according to the theory of elasticity,
the determination of action eﬀects may be carried out assuming (1) uncracked cross
sections, (2) linear stress-strain relationships and (3) mean value of the modulus of
elasticity.
The eﬀectiveness factor used in plasticity theory is introduced, partly due to mi-
crocracking before applying any source of external loading, due to internal restraints.
However, when using elasticity theory, uncracked sections are often considered and
used for calculation, even though early age micro cracking is a known phenomena.
Furthermore, the restraining eﬀect of reinforcement is not mentioned either as to
how it can inﬂuence micro cracking, thereby possibly aﬀecting the stiﬀness of the
concrete.
1.1
Background
A few years ago, a large test setup was installed at the Technical University of Den-
mark. The experiment which was conducted used a ﬁligran deck, which was loaded
in bending. In short, it was observed that the deﬂection of the deck was measured to
be larger than what was expected. It was therefore reasoned, that a possible reason
for the larger deﬂection could be due to shrinkage cracks, lowering the stiﬀness of
the slab. More precisely, the prediction was, that it was the reinforcement in the
compression zone which restrained the concrete shrinkage, such that microcracks
evolved and lowered the stiﬀness in the compression zone. There has been a wish
to research this phenomenon further, why this thesis will do so. It will be done by
stating the hypothesis and asking research questions, which could help guide the
way of evaluating the hypothesis systematically.
1.2
Hypothesis, Aim and Research questions
With the basis for this thesis described in the background, the aim and research
questions can be deﬁned and formulated as follows.
1.2.1
Hypothesis
Reinforcement in the compression zone of a concrete slab restrains shrinkage, which
cause cracking and thereby reduce the compression stiﬀness of the concrete.

Aim
The aim for this project is to investigate the validity of the stated hypothesis. Due
to the complex nature of the topic, it was chosen to state some research questions,
which could aid in the evaluation of the validity of the hypothesis.
1.2.3
Research questions
Five research questions are asked in order to divide the investigation into smaller
parts, which can help link results from experiments to an evaluation of the hypoth-

Q1: Is there a correlation between surface crack widths and reinforcement ratio in

Q2: Are there cracks that extend down from the surface of the slab to the level of
reinforcement?
Q3: Do the cracks close instantly or gradually during compression of the concrete?
Q4: Is there a tendency of reduction of the stiﬀness in the reinforced test slabs?
1.3
Motivation
The reinforcement layout in structural concrete elements often include reinforce-
ment in the compression zone. This is used for achieving e.g. a higher stiﬀness and
strength. It can also be used for symmetry in the cross section, to ensure that the
element cannot be turned the wrong way at the construction site, or from practical
reasons if a continuous element is cast. If however, the reinforcement in the com-
pression zone will hinder free shrinkage, it could lead to micro cracking and thereby
possibly inﬂuence the stiﬀness of the compression zone as the cracks has to close
before the entire cross section is activated. Therefore, if the shrinkage cracks caused
by the restraining eﬀect of the reinforcement inﬂuences the stiﬀness of the concrete,
it also inﬂuences the design of the concrete structure. A reduced stiﬀness of the
concrete could result in larger deformations than expected. Larger deformations
can be very uncomfortable to the users, and most often the stiﬀness of a structural
member inﬂuences how the loads are transferred to other structural components.

Literature review
Cracking due to restraining eﬀects in early age concrete is a broad and complex topic,
as there can be many parameters to account for. Both wide and deep research exists
for explaining this ﬁeld. ”Early Age Fracture Mechanics and Cracking of Concrete”
By Lennart Østergaard describes how internal restraints such as e.g.
shrinkage
hindered by aggregates can cause cracking. The report also explains how external
restraint can cause cracking, such as e.g.
friction between the concrete and the
material it is cast against, or temperature gradients through the cross section. The
report is thorough, detailed and describes many parts to understanding the behavior
of early age concrete.
Unfortunately, the exact eﬀect of how the reinforcement
restrains the shrinkage, and what eﬀect this could have on diﬀerent properties of
the concrete is not investigated in the report.
The article ”Inﬂuences of Reinforcement on Diﬀerential Drying Shrinkage of Con-
crete” by Gao Xiaojian, Qu Guangbin and Zhang Ailian, found that a higher re-
inforcement ratio resulted in a higher restrain against shrinkage and a higher dif-
ferential shrinkage strain in the concrete prism investigated. They also stated, that
a lot of literature was available on externally restrained shrinkage, but less liter-
ature was available for diﬀerential shrinkage distribution for concrete in dry con-
ditions, as this report will investigate.
Their ﬁndings coincided with the article
”Inﬂuence of reinforcement conﬁguration on the shrinkage and craacking potential
of high-performance concrete” by Lepeng Huang, Jianmin Hua, Ming Kang and Aili
Zhang. The latter mentioned article was for high performance concrete, but a very
important mentioning was that the article states: ”in practice, reinforcements that
have been placed cannot restrain the concrete near the surface. Some clauses of the
Chinese design code are derived from engineering experience and ignore the stresses
caused by the reinforcmeent because of the lack of related research. These clauses
have been proven to fail in many cases. Thus the inﬂuence of the reinforcement
should be investigated to precisely predict the shrinkage behavior of the reinforced
concrete and reduce the risk of concrete cracking.” The important context to take
out of this is that the topic is not thoroughly investigated and understood to a full
extend.
”Eﬀects of restrained shrinkage on concrete slabs” by Tam, K. S. Stephen, evalu-
ates the tensile stress induced in concrete slabs due to restraint of shrinkage. A
parameter study was also carried out, formulated in a ﬁnite element procedure. The
report mentions that the restraint may be provided by support conditions and/or
by reinforcing bars. The method builds upon an assumption that the cracks are
going through the entire cross section, meaning that at the location of a crack, only
the steel reinforcement contributes to the concrete member stiﬀness. This is not
thought to reﬂect the true behavior of the problem investigated in this thesis, why
proposed stiﬀness reduction values were not used to compare with the ﬁndings of
this report.

3
Theory
There are numerous factors inﬂuencing cracking in early age concrete. As early
age concrete is a broad deﬁnition, it is here considered as the ﬁrst 20 days after
production. In general, early age cracking of unloaded concrete elements can involve
plastic settlement, plastic shrinkage, autogenous shrinkage and drying shrinkage.
Addressing the exact mechanism which caused a crack to form is not always a
simple task, and the diﬀerent mechanisms can also interact, where an initiated
crack from one mechanism might be expanded further by another mechanism. The
wide factor dependence in shrinkage, restraining eﬀects and early age cracking is
therefore responsible for the complex nature of the topic.
3.1
Concrete shrinkage
3.1.1
Plastic shrinkage
In fresh concrete, plastic shrinkage cracking can happen if the surface water from
bleeding evaporates at a rate faster than the rate of which new water rises to the
surface from inside the concrete. Especially dense concrete with admixtures such
as e.g. microsilica and ﬂy ash are in high risk of plastic shrinkage cracking, as the
transportation of water is more challenged in the ﬁne pore structure. When the
water in the surface capillaries evaporate, capillary suction forces will arise. Since
the concrete is in a fresh plastic state, the strength still have not developed much
why the capillary suction forces can cause plastic cracks to form in the surface. [3].
3.1.2
Chemical and autogenous shrinkage
As concrete hydrates, the resulting solid chemical product has a smaller volume than
the sum of the volumes of the reactants [1]. This chemical process causes formation
of inner air voids in the concrete. If the concrete is in a closed system, meaning
that water will not be freely accessible, autogenous shrinkage can occur and lead to
volume reduction [3].
3.1.3
Drying shrinkage
Due to the hygroscopic nature of concrete, a diﬀerence between the moisture content
in concrete and in the environment causes transportation of water. Also, after the
main part of the hydration process has happened, there will still be water left for
potential evaporation. There is e.g. free water which is the term for water in larger
capillary pores ( >50 nm), which is what requires the least bit of energy to evaporate.
There are also smaller pores in which water can evaporate, going down to 5 nm in
size. It is especially small capillaries which can cause drying shrinkage. As the water
in the small pores starts to evaporate, the surface tension causes the small pores to
close up [3].

3.2
Restraining eﬀects for concrete
3.2.1
Casting molds
Placing fresh concrete into casting molds requires the use of mold-oil. If let out, the
friction coeﬃcient between the mold material and the concrete might be too high
causing a frictional resistance of such magnitude that it prevents free shrinkage of
the concrete, thereby leading to possible cracking. Naturally, also discontinuities
in the mold-layout can also cause restraint to such a degree that both micro- and
macrocracking can form.
3.2.2
Aggregates
Internal restraints can be understood as how the rigid parts of the material causes
gradients in the dilation of the material.
Thereby, the aggregates which have a
much higher stiﬀness than the cement-matrix could cause microcracking around
them [2]. Besides the larger aggregates, unhydrated cement particles can also lead
to microcracking in the cement-matrix [5].
3.2.3
Reinforcement
Reinforcing steel bars can restrain the concrete if the bond between the steel and
concrete has suﬃcient strength, and the concrete experience a certain amount of
shrinkage. The shrinkage and creep eﬀects regarding the stress state of the con-
crete is complex, also due to the fact that the steel bars restraining eﬀect is not
uniform throughout the section of the concrete. More investigation of this restrain-
ing eﬀect is needed. If steel bars are placed near the surface, plastic settlement of
concrete can cause surface cracking as well, due to diﬀerential settlement between
the reinforcement bar and the surrounding concrete. [4]

Research methodology
This thesis commenced on a deep search into the state of the art. The search yielded
a large amount of results on early age cracking behavior of concrete, both due to in-
ternal and external restraining eﬀects. It was not possible however, to ﬁnd research
directly describing early age cracking from the isolated eﬀect of reinforcement re-
straining eﬀects, which could be used to forecast a magnitude of reduction in the
stiﬀness of the surrounding concrete. Many papers described cracking in fully re-
strained prisms, which could only be used to an extend of giving an understanding
the extreme scenario, if a reinforcement bar could fully restrain a member. As this
is not realistic with a normally reinforced section, the consequence thereof was to
investigate the concise topic by an experimental approach. The overall plan was to
design experiments, which all would try to give an answer to the research questions
formulated for the project. By evaluating how much understanding the experimen-
tal results gave to answering the research questions, the hypothesis could possibly
be evaluated. Chapter 5 will describe the experimental program designed for this
thesis in depth. This chapter will give a brief description of the procedure in a shal-
low manner, such that an easier overview of how the overall procedure is obtained.
First oﬀ, the present thesis investigates the inﬂuence of compression reinforcement
on cracking and compression stiﬀness of concrete slabs. The primary reason for
choosing slabs instead of e.g. beams is that the restraining friction eﬀect between
the mold and concrete has less restraining eﬀect for a point in the concrete as the
distance between that point and the mold increases. In other words, the center of
the slab surface has a larger distance to the mold sides than a beam with the same
length, allowing a more free shrinkage to take place.
The experiments are carried out on three diﬀerent types of concrete slabs, which are
to be cast. One slab consisting of plain concrete purely and two other slabs with
reinforcement; the third slab with a higher reinforcement ratio than the second.
The slabs are transported into a climate room just after casting, ensuring identical
hardening conditions for them all. In the climate room, a camera is permanently
installed to take pictures of the concrete surface over twenty days. After this time,
the pictures are analyzed using a Digital Image Correlation (DIC) software. This
software shall aid in showing the development and extend of surface cracking. After-
wards, this method and the results are controlled by using a hand held microscope
for relocating and measuring the cracks.
After possible cracks are found, the slabs are cut up into smaller identical prisms,
allowing compression tests of the concrete both with and without reinforcement.
Here, the elastic modulus of the prisms are tracked by use of an extensometer
(LVDT). Also, the prisms are captured by camera while being compressed, so the
DIC software can reveal if the cracks close up during the elastic range compression.
The reinforced prisms are afterwards cored out using a diamond core drill, in order
to remove the reinforcement. They are thereafter compressed again to see the true
isolated concrete stiﬀness. Coring out the prisms are also tested as a valid method,
ensuring no damage or changes will happen to the elastic moduli by doing so. Fi-
nally, some of the prisms are cut up in order to investigate the inside of the prisms
under compression, by use of the DIC tool. The idea is to investigate if surface
cracks extend down to the reinforcement and generally see if the reinforced mem-

bers are more cracked. Parallel to the experiments, cylinders are cast for each batch
of concrete used, in order to determine the stiﬀness and strength of the concrete
batches. A number of cylinders are also cast and put into curing baths for optimal
curing conditions in compliance with the standards. The ﬁndings are also compared
to these found strengths and elastic moduli.

A various amount of articles mentions that a dense concrete increases the risk of

5.3
Test methods
5.3.1
Slab surface strain measurements
After casting a concrete slab, its surface was evened out with a plasterers throwel.
This was done in order to be able to track the surface strains during a hardening
period of 19 days. The surface strains were to be captured by the DIC tool called
GOM Correlate. The program is described in more detail in appendix C. The sur-
face needs to be as ﬂat as possible, without local out-of-plane interruptions, in order
for the software to analyze strain optimally, without distortions. Furthermore, for
the software to operate, the surface was prepared with paint. After approximately
45 minutes from casting the surface of the concrete was painted with a thin layer
of diﬀusion-open white chalk paint. On top of this white layer, small black speckles
were sprayed onto the surface using the same type of diﬀusive chalk paint. The
stochastic speckled pattern is necessary for the analysis of the pictures taken during
the hardening period. The 45 minutes of hardening was necessary for the paint to
be applied without blurring out the speckles due to bleed water and paint mixed
together. Finally, a few gridlines were sprayed onto the surface in order to track the
prisms in the surface for the DIC analysis and later cut up.
Approximately 1 hour after casting, the slabs were placed into a climate room, for
which the setup is shown on ﬁgure 11. It is placed here in order to harden under
controlled conditions. A combination of low relative humidity, a warm temperature
and a constant wind ﬂow should accelerate hydration and be optimal conditions
for inducing shrinkage [9]. The temperature and relative humidity in the room was
logged and are given in appendix B.
Figure 11: Climate room layout

camera was to take a picture every hour for 19 days.

5.3.2
Cylinder test
During the casting of each slab, 12 cylinders of the same concrete batch were also
made. The purpose of this was to investigate the evolution of stiﬀness and strength
19
==PAGE:31==
as a function of age in the climate conditions given. The procedure was to test
cylinders 1 day after casting, 5 days after casting, 10 days after casting and ﬁnally 19
days after casting. Each of the days consisted of 3 cylinder tests, where the stiﬀness
test was performed 3 times on each cylinder, in order to capture a stabilized elastic
modulus. The stiﬀness test was performed using the compression machine ”Toni
Technik 300 tonne”, and an LVDT to measure the deformation during the test. The
load is logged as an output signal from the compression machine and the strain is a
found as a function from the output from the LVDT signal. The LVDT is described
in Appendix G. The rate of compression was set to 0,6 MPa/s which is suggested in
the standard DS/EN 12300-13. This standard also states that the cylinders should
be loop-tested, with a prestressing load, but there was two reasons not to do so. First
reason was that this project investigates the initial elastic modulus, which cannot
be investigated if the prism is preloaded to the suggested stress level. Secondly, the
Toni machine used could not reach as small a value of preload as what was suggested
in the standard. A stable elastic moduli was then taken from the second and third
compression test. After the stiﬀness was determined, each cylinder was compressed
to failure, to ﬁnd the ultimate strength.
5.3.3
Inspection of surface by digital handheld microscope
After the slabs had hardened for 19 days in the climate room, they were transported
out in normal indoor environment. The pictures were extracted from the cameras
and analyzed in the DIC software. The surface strain contour plot indicated pos-
sible crack patterns. In order to verify if the DIC tool actually showed cracks, a
digital hand held Celestron microscope was used to relocate the seen cracks. Be-
sides relocating the cracks, the microscope had the possibility of snapping pictures
for documentation. It was able to magnify up to 150 times. The crack widths were
measured using a reference ruler for the microscope.
5.3.4
First compression test
After relocating the cracks via the handheld microscope, the slab prisms were cut out
of the slabs, as described in section 5.2.3. The ﬁrst compression testing procedure
was carried out 24 hours after cutting. This is due to the fact, that cutting requires
a vast amount of water for the saw blade, why the prisms were of course wet after
cutting. A wet prism can experience an increase of its elastic modulus by up to 15
% [11]. The prism were tested in compression up to 40 % of their strength. This
was decided in order of keeping the prisms in the elastic zone without leading to
crack-propagation, and also DS/EN 1992-1-1 deﬁnes the secant modulus of elasticity
as 40 % of the mean compression strength.

7.9
Theoretical prism behavior compared to test
Theoretical prisms vs tested prisms with reinforcement
To surpass the problem of comparing a non-reinforced prism to a reinforced prism,
the data from the cylinder properties for each batch are used to formulate some
theoretical stress-strain relationships. This is done by using the secant elastic mod-
uli from the cured cylinders, the cylinders from the climate room and the reference
prisms. These stiﬀnesses are used to model theoretical prisms where a ﬁctive rein-
forcement bar is introduced into the center of the cross section. The stiﬀness of the
cured cylinders and the cylinders from the climate room can bee found in appendix
D.
In ﬁgure 39 the stress-strain relationships are shown for 3 theoretical prisms and
2 actual prisms. The three theoretical prisms are; a prism where the stiﬀness of
concrete is a mean of the cured cylinder stiﬀnesses, a prism where the stiﬀness of
concrete is a mean of the climate room cylinders after hardening and lastly a prism
with a concrete stiﬀness corresponding to the mean reference prism stiﬀness. For

8
Discussion
8.1
Concrete mix design
In this project, a concrete mix with a high cement content was used in order to
achieve proper shrinkage, ensuring results which were quantiﬁable in the time avail-
able. The justiﬁcation for this decision is also further grounded in the fact, that
the same recipe was used for all prisms, so even though the shrinkage might be
slightly pronounced in the results, the results are still relative to each other, thereby
quantiﬁable against each other. As mentioned in the report, a lot of ground work
was carried out in order to obtain a concrete which showed reasonable results in the
time frame.
8.2
Time from casting to ﬁrst photos
After casting the slab, the fresh concrete was leveled out. After 30 minutes the
surface was ready to be smoothened out further using a plasterers trowel. This was
done twice, so the initial surface and bleed water could be evened out. Only by
doing so, it was possible to paint the surface without causing extensive blurring of
the painted speckles. Also, analyzing the pictures taken during hardening requires a
plane surface for the camera to register small deformation without disturbance from
the lighting and out-of-plane distortion eﬀects. The painting was done 60 minutes
after casting, why the ﬁrst pictures are taken approximately an hour after casting.
The plastic shrinkage cracks can develop right from the fresh state of the concrete
within minutes after the concrete is placed in the mold. Missing out this very early
behavior is inherent in the method using a surface paint which needs to be in sharp
contrast between the black and white spectrum ends to ensure high quality pictures
for the DIC tool. As it is expected that the drying shrinkage has a large inﬂuence
for the results, this very early plastic shrinkage behavior is not investigated further.
8.3
Climate during hardening
The climate room is installed to maintain a high temperature and low relative humid-
ity. This is purely done for the main reason being an acceleration of the shrinkage.
An aggressive environment like so does imply a higher rate of evaporation of bleeding
water of the concrete, but the climate is identical for all prisms, meaning that the
eﬀect is general, and relative diﬀerences in behavior captured are still comparable.
According to DS/EN 12390-2, standard prism testing of stiﬀness and strength of
concrete is carried out by following a very stringent procedure. By section 5.5 in the
code it is stated that the test prisms are to be held at very speciﬁc temperatures,
and that the prisms are to be put in water curing till immediately before testing
which should be the reference method. In this report, it was seen that there was
a deviation between the cured cylinders and the cylinders which had been in the
climate room. In [11] it is mentioned that testing wet prisms will lead to an apparent
increases in stiﬀness by up to 15 % , why this reference testing method will lead
to deviations between cast concrete properties on construction sites and laboratory
testing results. Furthermore, in the opposite manner, the strength is at the same
time lowered up to 15 % for a wet prism. The cylinder prisms from the climate room
49
==PAGE:61==
were tested after 19 days, instead of 28 days, due to the time limit described in the
report. It is still considered completely comparable with the 28 day properties of
the water cured cylinders, as the properties of the concrete in the climate room were
developed so fast due to the aggressive climate.
8.4
Cutting and coring of prism prisms
When coring and cutting in concrete some stress has to be applied where the dia-
mond teeth of the blade/drill edge removes the concrete material. Therefore, in this
project, the cutting and drilling has been carried out with an extreme caution and
uttermost care for the prisms. Not more than the bare minimum requirement of
force has been applied to the prisms, also resulting in a long process of cutting and
coring. Each cut through the slab was done progressively, cutting roughly 2 cen-
timeters deep for each cut, slowly progressing down through the slab. This was an
advice given from an external concrete cutting ﬁrm ”Herlev Diamanten”, which gave
great results. The inﬂuence of the cutting the prisms is diﬃcult to quantify, since
the stiﬀness could not be tested before cutting the slab into prisms. But since the
stiﬀness of the prisms are compared against each other, which all has been processed
the same way, it does not conﬂict with the tendencies found in this project.
In regards to coring out the reinforcement, the eﬀect was tested thoroughly. After
testing the reference prisms without reinforcement, they were cored out and dried.
After drying, their stiﬀnesses were tested again, to see if there were any statistically
signiﬁcant changes from the ﬁrst measurements. A Welch t-test concluded that no
such change was present, and the method was concluded valid.
8.5
Measuring crack widths
The crack widths developed during the hardening period were measured both by
the DIC software and by a hand held microscope. Both the DIC analysis and micro-
scope inspection showed that slabs with a higher reinforcement ratio also had wider
surface cracks on average, but the DIC tool gave smaller crack widths which might
have been due to pixel accuracy. To obtain exact crack widths with the DIC tool
it would be necessary to be a lot closer to the surface and having a more reﬁned
speckle pattern. This however, was a compromise between seeing a larger scaled
picture of the behavior instead of zooming in. Furthermore, close evaporation could
conﬂict with the camera lense, and there would be no guarantee of zooming into a
cracked area, as the exact locations of the cracks are random to a certain extend.
The accuracy of the software relies on the resolution and speckle pattern of the
surface. The software calculates using the facets described in appendix C which
means, any crack openings smaller than the facets is not going to be shown as a
true displacement since the software is averaging over the total facet. When the
camera is set to take pictures, the surface of the concrete contains a fair amount of
water, which evaporates in the ﬁrst 1-2 hour. But the evaporation of the changes the
nuance of the colors, which makes it more challenging for the software to recognize
facets despite the speckle pattern. During the hardening of the reference slab with-
out reinforcement, the camera was mistakenly set to save the image as both .raw
and .jpeg format, which meant that the SD-card ran out of storage at day 16. The
last three days were therefore not captured for this slab. From the results it is seen
50
==PAGE:62==
much of the strain and crack development happens in the ﬁrst 5 days, which means
the last 3 days that was missed for the reference slab does not have a signiﬁcant
impact on conclusion of the results.
As the third compression test was carried out, a tendency was seen where the cracks
on the inside of the prisms closed more than the outside surface cracks. This could
be caused due to more centred pressure than edge pressure, from the compression
machine loading plates, as the pressure head have the ability to rotate a bit. As
the prism has a quarter of its cross section cut out, it could distribute the load
less uniform. The important result however, is that that the cracks are not closing
instantly, but closing gradually during the test. This indicates, that the cracks can
inﬂuence the stiﬀness over the entire elastic region, thereby aﬀecting both the initial
as well as the secant modulus of elasticity. It is possible, that the cracks close more,
as the quarter part of the concrete which has been cut oﬀobviously cannot aid in
load bearing, but the results are to be understood as indicative mainly.
8.6
Strain measurements in slabs
The strain measured both by reinforcement strain gauges and by the embedded
strain gauges shows that the strain is larger at the top surface than inside the con-
crete, which is as expected as the surface is the location of most eﬀective drying. In
the Y20 slab the data showed that the top and bottom layer reinforcement strained
approximately the same over 20 days. The results from the embedded strain gauges
also showed that the concrete in the top experienced less shrinkage than in the
bottom. It is uttermost important to note however, that here the top is not at
the level of the surface, and the bottom gauge is not at the bottom as the lowest
point in the slab. The top embedded strain gauge was located just beneath the top
reinforcement bar, and the bottom gauge was placed on a little mount at a level a
bit beneath the bottom reinforcement. The lower gauge strained more than the top
gauge. This could be due to the fact, that the top gauge was mounted just beneath
the top reinforcement bar and the bottom gauge was mounted between the lower
bars, such that the top gauge was closer to the restraining eﬀect from the bar.
The reinforcement strains curve up over time, showing a tendency of strain release.
This is thought to be due to progression of crack opening in the concrete. The reason
why the concrete has the opposite sign could be due to the concrete between two
parallel cracks will contract a bit, as an eﬀect opposite of the former. Relaxation
of steel is not considered plausible as the stress level is low. All the measurement
ﬂuctuates in cycles with a period of 1 day. This corresponds to how the temperature
in the room cycles also, so it is considered to be thermal cyclic ﬂuctuation eﬀects,
expanding and shrinking the slab.
8.7
E-moduli of prisms
In the ﬁrst compression test, the stiﬀness of the reference prisms are compared
with the reinforced prisms. Instead of transforming the reinforced cross sections,
it was decided to plot the stress as the true load over the prism cross sectional
area, thereby not adjusting for the reinforcement. The reasoning behind that, is
that the reinforced prisms should show higher stiﬀnesses than the prisms without
51
==PAGE:63==
reinforcement. The results from this ﬁrst compression test did not show increased
stiﬀnesses of the reinforced prisms. This might be because the concrete is less stiﬀ,
thereby balancing out the higher reinforcement stiﬀness. The Y25 prisms are almost
equal to the Y20 prism stiﬀnesses which again could indicate that even though there
is more reinforcement in the loaded cross section of Y25, the concrete is less stiﬀ
than the concrete of the Y20 prisms.
The eﬀect is not so clear from the second compression test, where the reinforcement
has been cored out.
It is diﬃcult to see a diﬀerence between the Y20 concrete
stiﬀness and reference stiﬀness, but the Y25 stiﬀness is clearly reduced. Especially
the initial modulus seems to have be reduced, which could indicate the cracks has
to be closed before activating the whole cross-section, which ﬁts the results of the
increment in crack widths. If the lower stiﬀness of the concrete is caused by the
cracks induced by the restraining eﬀect of the reinforcement, the cracks should also
move more during compression, which has shown to be the case. Furthermore, the
isolated stiﬀness of the Y20 prisms did not diﬀer a lot from the reference prisms,
after being cored out. The reason for this could be due to the fact, that the core
drill could have removed too much of the concrete near the rebar, which is an eﬀect
that would thereby be more pronounced on the Y20 prisms than on the Y25 prisms,
as the latter has a larger area of steel thereby meaning that the same drill removes
less concrete and more steel for that prisms. This however is an eﬀect that would
be interesting to investigate further.
The small amount of transverse reinforcement is not considered to inﬂuence the
results in a negative way, as if the reinforcement causes a small increase to the
stiﬀness, it should only work against the tendencies which are investigated, meaning
it should not falsely amplify the tendencies of investigation.

9
Conclusion
This project aimed to investigate the validity of the stated hypothesis. Due to the
complex nature of the topic, it was chosen to answer the stated research questions
which in turn could help enlighten the validity of the hypothesis.
9.1
Summary of main results
Three concrete slabs have been cast, one without any reinforcement, one with Y20
rebars and one with Y25 rebars. The slabs are set to harden in a climate room with
a warm and dry climate. The shrinkage is measured on the surface and inside by a
DIC tool and strain gauges, repectively. After hardening the stiﬀness of the prisms
were tested in compression. The main results can be seen in table 8.
Table 8: Summary of main results
The crack pattern seen in chapter 7.6 is more intensive for Y25 slab, than the ref-
erence and Y20 slab. It is diﬃcult to diﬀerentiate the reference and Y20 slab, and
therefore not a clear tendency is seen here. When looking at the crack width a
tendency is seen. From all the analyzes of the crack widths, it is found that the
reinforced slabs have larger crack widths than the reference slab. When the DIC
results are compared to the results from the microscope measurements, it shows
the same tendency just smaller openings. The microscope is believed to give more
accurate results, as the distance from the surface challenges the accuracy of the DIC
results, however the strain contours on the surface were conﬁrmed as true cracks.
9.2
Research questions
Q1: Is there a correlation between surface crack widths and reinforcement
ratio in the slabs cast for this project?
From the DIC analysis and microscope measurements it it seen that the crack width
of the surface cracks were larger on average for the reinforced members than for the
reference slab without reinforcement. Furthermore, the crack widths of the Y25 slab
were larger on average than for the Y20 slab. The results point towards a correlation
where higher reinforcement ratio increased crack widths. The results were taken in
such a manner, that average values of separate cracks were compared, ensuring that
a longer crack with more measurements would not be weighed more heavily.
53
==PAGE:65==
Q2: Are there cracks that extend down from the top surface of the slab
to the level of reinforcement?
From the DIC analysis it was found that several cracks extend down in the concrete
from the surface. This was seen both from the cut sectional views and also in the
quarter cut prisms.
The former was not as close to the reinforcement, but the
latter could capture what happened inside, close to the reinforcement. From the
cut sectional views it is observed that the crack pattern is less developed than what
was found on the surface, which was expected due to pronounced plastic shrinkage
cracking. However, it seems that the reference slab and Y20 slab are somewhat
similar both in the top surface cracks and in the sectional cut crack patterns. It is
more evident that the Y25 slab has a more pronounced crack pattern for the surface
and side cuts.
Q3: Do the cracks close instantly or gradually during compression of the
concrete?
Prism number 5 and 8 from each slab were investigated after being quarter cut. The
cracks showed to close less on average for the reference prisms, than the Y20 and
Y25 prisms. There was a larger spread in the crack behavior in the Y20 and Y25
prisms. It can be hard to quantify directly whether a crack closes completely or not.
There is a tendency however, that the cracks close gradually, meaning that some of
the cracks have an inﬂuence on the eﬀective cross section in compression during the
entire load range.
Q4: Is there a tendency of reduction of the stiﬀness in the reinforced test
slabs?
In the ﬁrst compression test, with reinforcement, the stress was calculated as the
load divided by the entire cross sectional area of the prism, thereby not transform-
ing for the reinforcement. The Y20 and Y25 prisms showed the same initial elastic
modulus as the reference slab without reinforcement, with a larger spread in the
results though. The secant modulus of elasticity was measured a bit higher for the
reinforced prisms, again with a larger spread in the results. It could indicate that
the reinforced concrete was actually less stiﬀfor the initial elastic modulus, as it
would otherwise be expected that the reinforcement would increase the stiﬀness of
the prisms, which is not seen from the results.
After the reinforcement was cored out, it was expected that the stiﬀness from the
Y20- and Y25 prisms would be lower than for the reference prisms. The stiﬀness of
the Y20 prisms did not change signiﬁcantly, but the Y25 prisms showed a radical
reduction in the stiﬀness.
9.3
Evaluation of hypothesis
From the surface and internal crack patterns, it is hard to conclude a direct tendency.
However, when the crack widths are measured, the reinforced slabs show signiﬁcantly
larger values. The same tendency is found inside the prisms, when they are tested
54
==PAGE:66==
in compression. From all the stiﬀnesses found from the ﬁrst and second compression
test it was seen that the stiﬀnesses were inﬂuenced by the reinforcement, but the
direct relationship was impossible to quantify directly. In short, the hypothesis could
very well be true, as many results point toward its validity, but the results from this
project cannot solely conclude a valid hypothesis.

Appendix C
C.0.1
DIC analysis
The DIC (Digital Image Correlation) analyses are performed using the software
GOM Correlate. The software imports a series of pictures, where the software
analyses for displacements and or deformations in the pictures. In order to do so,
the images must have what is called a ”stochastic pattern” in grey-scale.
That
means, that a surface should be prepared with white paint as a grounder, and then
have black speckled paint dots on top. When images are imported into the software,
the ﬁrst picture is used as the reference picture. The surface which is wished to
be analyzed is then marked and the user inputs data corresponding to a mesh or a
ﬁneness of calculation. GOM CORRELATE divides the marked surface into smaller
quadratic regions called facets. Each facet contains a number of pixels deﬁned by
the user. Small facets results in a ﬁne mesh and thereby a more accurate analysis.
It requires that the picture has a high resolution and the speckle pattern is ﬁne.
Each facet works like a ﬁngerprint, which the software tries to relocate between
each picture. As seen on ﬁgure 62 a facet size is an area of pixels.
The user tells the software how many pixels a facet should consist of. After a large
amount of trials and investigations, the found setting was good at a facets size of 20 x
20 pixels, and a point distance of 10 pixels. The point distance is the center distance
between each facets, so the mentioned setting gives an overlap of 2 adjacent facets
of approximately 50 %. That distance is suggested from the GOM CORRELATE
support forums. These conﬁgurations was found by performing several analyses, and
this conﬁguration seemed to result in the most accurate and consistent results. These
conﬁgurations depends on the distance between the analyzed object and camera,
the speckle pattern, camera lens and size of cracks, and cannot be seen as a general
setting. In this report, the camera used during the hardening period was a Nikon
D800 with a 24 mm Nikon lense. During the compression tests, a Canon EOS R 5D
was used together with a 100 mm Canon lense.
Figure 62: Illustration of facet size and point distance in GOM correlate
It is possible to create virtual strain gauges in GOM correlate. This function
is useful when it comes to measuring cracks widths and strain of the concrete.

Appendix G
The LVDT (Linear variable diﬀerential transformer) consists of two set of jaws,
which is used to determine the deformation of the specimens it is attached to. The
LVDT is attached onto the specimen by hand, where it is important to use a level
to ensure the alignment of the LVDT is in the same direction as strain is wished to
be measured. The setup is seen in ﬁgure 74.
Figure 74: LVDT setup
As the concrete deforms, the movable jaw will follow. The jaw is attached with
a standard strain gauge, which will change the resistance and thereby the output as
the jaw displaces. The output is logged and is interpreted into a deformation, by
using a calibration factor. The calibration is performed by attaching the LVDT to
a steel pin, which is moved manually in small steps. Thereby a correlation between
the output from LVDT and the true displacement can be found a linear relation. A
plot of a calibration test can seen in ﬁgure 75

Appendix K
Since it is the shrinkage of the concrete that causes the shrinkage cracks that is
investigated in this project, a proper concrete mix is needed. From literature stud-
ies it was found that ﬂyash has a admixture has a retarding eﬀect, which has a
protecting eﬀect on plastic shrinkage cracks. Therefore the concrete mix is without
ﬂy ash. Some articles mentioned superplasticizer could inﬂuence the hydration of
the concrete, and was therefore left out just to get any external inﬂuence out of the
picture. For the initial experiments, several tests of concrete mixes were made to
ensure the concrete mix would strain suﬃcient to create cracks of in the hardening
period, because of the time limit on this project. It was found that by increas-
ing the volume of cement-paste to 40% of the total volume, it would increase the
strain rate and thereby the cracks development. Therefor a concrete-mix without
any admixtures and a cement volume of 40% was chosen.
