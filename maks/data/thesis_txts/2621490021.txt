Abstract
In this study, the composition of the infant gut microbiota, short-chain fatty acids
(SCFAs) and markers of infant health were investigated. This study focuses on three
steps, ﬁrstly the eﬀect of inﬂuencing factors on the infant gut microbiota, secondly
the eﬀect of the gut microbiota on SCFA composition and lastly the eﬀect of gut
microbiota and SCFAs on health biomarkers. The study relies on data obtained dur-
ing the ﬁrst year of life in two independent infant cohorts, SKOT III (n=34, samples
taken at ﬁve and nine months of age) and ProbiComp (n=255, samples taken at eight
to thirteen months of age). Data comprises 16S sequencing data from the gut micro-
biota and SCFA measurements from both cohorts and a number of health biomarkers
from SKOT III. The results were investigated with an exploratory approach. Bacte-
rial composition of the infant gut microbiota as well as SCFA composition appear
strongly inﬂuenced by age in the ﬁrst year of life. Breastfeeding, formula and pres-
ence of older siblings seem to have an inﬂuence on the gut microbiota as well as
delivery mode and weight group to some degree. Gut microbiota diversity shows an
impact on SCFA levels and composition, especially butyrate, and SCFA levels are
associated with certain bacteria. Health biomarkers showing a correlation with both
gut microbiota and SCFA levels includes IGF-1. Overall, this in vivo study indicates
that external factors inﬂuence the infant gut microbiota composition, that the gut
microbiota inﬂuences SCFA levels and that both gut microbiota and SCFAs are asso-
ciated with some health biomarkers to a certain degree. Future studies are necessary
to explore these associations further.

Outline
The research of relations between the gut microbiota, its metabolites and the signif-
icance to human health is advancing. Studies are looking at variations in gut micro-
biota diversity and inﬂuence on metabolic diseases and the general understanding of
the molecular signalling between the gut microbiota and the host. Research of the link
between gut microbiota composition and short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production is
relatively well characterised but often relies on in vitro fermentation data and animal
models.
This is an exploratory study investigating the infant gut microbiota and its impact on
human health by looking at SCFAs as key signalling molecules. This is done by inves-
tigating data obtained during the ﬁrst year of life in two independent cohorts. SCFA
measurements and 16S sequencing data of faecal samples along with health biomark-
ers measured in blood samples make it possible to do bioinformatic and statistical
analyses.
The intention of this study is to improve the information of the interrelations in
vivo. This can be a guidance for future speciﬁc research of observed connections. This
exploratory study may broaden the research within impacts of gut microbiota on the
infants health and how this is inﬂuenced from the beginning of life.
The ﬁndings of this study will be discussed based on which inﬂuencing factors appear
to have an eﬀect on the infant gut microbiota, how the gut microbiota is associated
with SCFAs and possible associations with health biomarkers. Overall ﬁndings will
be presented in a conclusion together with future perspectives.

Introduction
2.1
The Gut Microbiota
The human body is home for trillions of microbes which are believed to be important
for human life [3]. The highest density of microbes is found in the intestinal com-
partments, this community of microbes are known as the gut microbiota [4]. The gut
microbiota develops during infancy and will later in life reach adult form [5]. The
human gut microbiota plays a big role in human health by helping the breakdown of
food substances that are otherwise inaccessible to the host. They promote host cell
diﬀerentiation, they protect the host from colonisation of pathogens and they stim-
ulate/modulate the immune system [6]. Studies indicate that the early human gut
microbiota composition/development may aﬀect risk factors related to adult health
conditions [4].
2.1.1
Bacteria of the Infant Gut Microbiota
Compared to an adult gut microbiota, the infant gut microbiota is less diverse and the
structure of it is less stable and very dynamic and therefore easily inﬂuenced [4]. The
infant gut microbiota is mostly dominated by biﬁdobacteria, especially in breastfed in-
fants, and it is therefore seen as a key member of the infant gut microbiota [7]. Some
of the genera dominating the infant gut microbiota include Biﬁdobacterium, Veil-
lonella, Streptococcus, Citrobacter, Bacteroides, Clostridium, Bacillus, Enterococcus,
Escherichia, Eubacterium, Lactobacillus, Prevotella and Ruminococcus [4, 8]. These
genera are also found in the adult gut microbiota [4].
2.1.2
Development and Inﬂuencing Factors
Microbial colonisation of the newborn is believed to start during and after birth. This
occurs when the amnion breaks and the fetus moves through the birth canal. The fetus
will pass through the cervix and vagina, and microorganisms of these two body sites
will colonise the newborn [4]. The colonisation of the infant gut is a process inﬂuenced
by several environmental and host factors, such as delivery mode, breastfeeding and
presence of siblings [4]. It is still unclear at what exact age a stable adult-like gut
microbiota structure is formed, but generally this happens at an age of around 2.5 to
3 years [9–11].
Infants delivered vaginally come into contact with the maternal vaginal and faecal mi-
crobiota. This results in the infant being colonised with vagina-associated microbes.
Infants delivered by Caesarean section (C-section) will not be directly exposed to
maternal microbes and will therefore have a higher risk of being colonised by envi-
ronmental microorganisms from maternal skin, hospital staﬀ, and the hospital envi-
ronment [12]. The diﬀerence between vaginally and C-section delivered babies will
decrease with time, but C-section delivered infants stay more heterogeneous than
vaginally born infants up to 12 months of age [12].
Breast milk provides essential nutrients for infant growth and bioactive components
to stimulate the gut microbiota as it contains promicrobial and antimicrobial agents
as well as Human Milk Oligosaccharides (HMOs) [13–15]. It is well known that both
breastfeeding and microbial composition have been associated with health outcomes

during infancy and later life [16]. Studies have shown that infants who are exclusively
breastfed have a lower microbial diversity with a predominance in Proteobacteria and

Family members and close relatives such as siblings have also been seen as a rele-

colonisation [4]. Bacteroides fragilis and biﬁdobacteria, especially B. biﬁdum and B.

those without siblings [17, 18]. It has furthermore been shown that Faecalibacterium
acquisition during late infancy is predominantly inﬂuenced by the presence of older
siblings [19].
2.1.3
Host-Microbiota Interactions
There is increasing evidence that metabolites of the gut microbiota have wider sys-
temic eﬀects in the host by acting as signalling molecules and being involved in gene
expression regulations [13]. Some of the most recognised microbial metabolites are
Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs), indole derivatives and ATP which can act at distal
sites by entering circulation [20]. SCFAs may indirectly regulate host-produced hor-
mones including PYY, Glucagon-Like Peptide 1 (GLP-1), leptin, ghrelin and Insulin-
like Growth Factor-1 (IGF-1) [20, 21]. All of this together could indicate that the gut
microbiota serves as a virtual endocrine organ by its metabolic capacity to produce
and regulate multiple compounds that reach the circulation and can inﬂuence the
function of distal organs and systems [21].
2.2
Short-Chain Fatty Acids
SCFAs are products of the intestinal microbial fermentation of Non-Digestible Car-
bohydrates (NDC) and have six or less carbon atoms [22]. Acetate, propionate and
butyrate make up over 95% of the SCFAs in the GastroIntestinal Tract (GIT) in an
approximate ratio of 3:1:1, respectively [23, 24].
2.2.1
SCFA-Producing Bacteria
Acetate is produced by most of the enteric bacteria, while propionate and butyrate
production pathways are more conserved within fewer bacteria and is more sub-
strate speciﬁc [25]. Butyrate is mainly produced from the butyryl-CoA:acetate CoA-
transferase pathway [26]. Most butyrate-producing bacteria are found within Ru-
minococcaceae and Lachnospiraceae from Firmicutes phylum but also within Erysipel-
otrichaceae and Clostridiceae [25, 27–30]. However, many bacteria not able to produce
butyrate are also present in these families like Blautia spp, Eubacterium eligens and
Ruminococcus spp. Two diﬀerent pathways result in propionate formation which are
succinate and propanediol. Succinate is the major route for propionate production
and is mainly found in Bacteroidetes but Veillonella spp are furthermore known to
produce propionate by this pathway [31]. Roseburia and Blautia species from Lach-
nospiraceae are known to produce propionate by the propanediol pathway [31, 32].
Bacteroidetes phylum contains most of the acetate and propionate producing bacte-
ria, while members of Firmicutes are the main producers of butyrate [33]. However, no
clear groupings of SCFA-producing bacteria can be made. Furthermore, metabolites
from one organism may function as substrate for another organism by cross-feeding.

2.2.2
Physiological Eﬀects of SCFAs
SCFAs have been proposed to be a link between dietary intake, diversity of the gut
microbiota and human health [25]. There is increasing evidence of SCFAs aﬀecting
host physiology, metabolic processes and inﬂuencing chronic inﬂammatory conditions
such as allergic disease and obesity [13]. SCFAs are also shown to have an eﬀect on
activation and diﬀerentiation of immune cells [13].
Acetate and propionate are mainly substrates for the peripheral tissue and liver while
butyrate is the main energy source for colonocytes [30]. Acetate is present in high
concentrations in the GIT and mainly function as co-substrate for some butyrate-
producing bacteria [28]. Propionate is known to have multiple positive impacts on
health like stimulation of intestinal gluconeogenesis and reduced stress when present in
higher intestinal amounts [41, 42]. Butyrate is the most studied SCFA and is the main
substrate for colonic epithelium maintenance by regulation of cell proliferation and
diﬀerentiation [25, 43, 44]. Another recognised cellular mechanism of butyrate is the
facilitation of access to DNA repair enzymes by inhibition of the histone deacetylase
[45]. These two mechanisms are believed to be cell-unspeciﬁc whereas some other
mechanisms of butyrate seems to occur in speciﬁc cell lines. Furthermore is butyrate
known to impact the modulation of immune and inﬂammatory responses along with
the function of intestinal barrier [46, 47].
SCFAs are natural ligands for Free Fatty Acid receptor 2 (FFA2) and Free Fatty
Acid receptor 3 (FFA3) which are receptors found on a wide range of cell types,
including enteroendocrine and immune cells. This links SCFAs to energy harvest and
metabolism through enteroendocrine cell (EEC) signalling, adipogenesis and IGF-1

Methods
This study relies on analyses of data obtained during the ﬁrst year of life in two
independent infant cohorts, the SKOT III cohort [1] and the ProbiComp cohort [2].
Data comprises 16S sequencing data from the microbiota, SCFA measurements, and a
number of biomarkers for health. All laboratory work (section 3.2) and bioinformatics
(section 3.3) were performed by others, but a brief overview of the methods used are
given below.
3.1
Study Cohorts
The SKOT III cohort consist of 34 full term breastfed infants. At ﬁrst visit the infants
were required to be within 5.0-6.5 months of age and have breastfeeding as the primary
energy source. Maximum two meals of solid foods per day were allowed. At second
visit the infants were required to be 9 months +/- 2 weeks. SKOT III was originally
designed to provide a unique opportunity for investigation of the potential causes of
Excessive Weight Gain (EWG) which is only rarely observed in exclusively breastfed
infants. The study included Normal Weight Gain (NWG) infants as controls.
The ProbiComp cohort includes a total of 290 Danish infants aged between 8 to 13
months. These infants were randomly allocated to receive a placebo or a combination
of Biﬁdobacterium animalis subsp lactis and Lactobacillus rhamnosus daily for a 6
month intervention period. This study focuses on faecal samples taken before the
start of intervention.
3.2
Sample Handling
All laboratory work for SKOT III and ProbiComp were performed by Laursen et al.
[52, 53]. Faecal samples were stored at −80°C until DNA extraction from 200 mg
faeces (SKOT III) and 250 mg faeces (ProbiComp). Handling of faecal samples for
gut microbiota analysis were done by the following procedure shown in ﬁgure 1. First
DNA extraction was performed. Then Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) was con-
ducted to amplify the V3-region of the 16S rRNA gene from the extracted DNA. A
post PCR clean-up was performed by MagBio puriﬁcation and ﬁnally samples were
pooled to obtain equimolar libraries. Sequencing of the 16S rRNA amplicon libraries
was performed using the Ion OneTouchTM and Ion PGM platform. For further speciﬁ-
cations and a more detailed description of the laboratory work, please see the following
articles [52, 53]. All faecal samples of SKOT III had SCFAs measures performed by
Pekmez et al. [54], who furthermore handled the processing of raw data. SCFA anal-
ysis of 50 selected faecal samples from ProbiComp were performed by MS-OMICS
[55], who conducted all sample preparations, measurements and data analysis.

3.3
Bioinformatics
CLC genomic workbench was used to prepare data by de-multiplexing reads according
to barcodes and trimming by removing primers and discarding reads below 110 bp and
above 180 bp. DADA2 Pipeline [56] ran in R was used to create an Amplicon Sequence
Variant (ASV) table. It sorts all sequences by how identical they are and resolves
diﬀerences of as little as one nucleotide. Assignment of taxonomy to ASVs based on
Ribosomal Database Project (RDP) was conducted by QIIME [57] along with alpha
(Shannon index and observed ASVs) and beta diversity (Bray Curtis) analyses. ASVs
in this study are mentioned by the assigned taxonomy and are therefore pooled if
multiple of the same taxonomy were present.
3.4
Statistics
Normal distribution of data was evaluated by Shapiro-Wilk test using the GraphPad
Prism software (v. 8.4.3, GraphPad Software Inc.., La Jolla, CA). Non-parametric
tests (Mann-Whitney U test, Wilcoxon signed-rank test, Spearman’s Rank correla-
tions) were done by GraphPad Prism software or R software. Statistical p-values
given at any analysis throughout this project is considered signiﬁcant when p < 0.05.
When appropriate, raw p-values were adjusted for multiple testing by the Benjamini-
Hochberg False Discovery Rate (FDR) approach [58].
Diversity Analysis
Alpha diversity represents the diversity within a sample and is represented by both
Shannon Index (SI) and observed ASVs. Beta diversity is the representation of diver-
sity between samples and is visualised by a dissimilarity matrix based on Bray Curtis
(BC).
The SI is used to characterise species diversity in a community and accounts for both
relative abundance and evenness of the species present. A high index represents a
more diverse and evenly distributed community. Observed ASVs count the number of
unique ASVs in each sample. Statistics for alpha diversity are calculated by Wilcoxon
signed-rank test (paired samples) and Mann Whitney U test (unpaired samples) in
R to test for signiﬁcant diﬀerences between groups.
BC is an abundance-based distance metric used to generate a Principal Coordinate
Analysis (PCoA). A PCoA plot tells how diﬀerent the microbial composition is in one
environment compared to another and is often based on the two axes with the highest
diversity explanation. Objects ordinated closer to one another are more similar than
those ordinated further away. The reason for using BC is it allows for distinguishing
similar species potentially with diﬀerent metabolites. Statistics for beta diversity are
calculated by Adonis2 function in R which is based on the principles of McArdle &
Anderson [59]. This algorithm is analogous to MANOVA (Multivariate analysis of
variance), but do not need the assumption of multivariate normal distribution.

Correlation Analysis
Correlation analysis of SCFAs with alpha diversity parameters, genus/species and
health biomarkers are made by Spearman’s rank correlations. This is a non-parametric
method measuring the strength of direction of association between two ranked vari-
ables. A Spearman’s rank correlation coeﬃcient (rs) will explain a monotonic trend
between the two variables where a perfect correlation is either +1 or −1 and oc-
curs with a positive or negative monotonic relationship, respectively. An rs value of
zero indicates no relationship between two variables. P-values are used to indicate
signiﬁcant correlations.
False Discovery Rate
When correlation tests are performed on a high number of variables, the risk of
identifying false signiﬁcant correlations by chance is greater. FDR is a method which
adjusts the statistical conﬁdence based on the number of tests performed by removing
what p-values are estimated to be false positives. This adjustment is conducted when
appropriate and is based on the Benjamini-Hochberg method with q < 0.05 as cutoﬀ
[58].

Study Strategy
This study focuses on 3 diﬀerent steps. 1: the eﬀect of inﬂuencing factors on the infant
gut microbiota; 2: the eﬀect of the gut microbiota on SCFA composition; 3: the eﬀect
of the gut microbiota and SCFA on health biomarkers. Figure 2 visualises a ﬂow sheet
of this strategy.
The ﬁrst step of this study will look into the eﬀect of delivery mode, siblings, breast-
feeding, weight group, formula and furred pets on the infant gut microbiota. This
will be analysed by ﬁrstly looking at diversity of the gut microbiota as a guidance
for which inﬂuencing factors show an actual eﬀect. Inﬂuencing factors that show a
signiﬁcant eﬀect will be analysed further by looking at the microbial composition
when separating the samples into the diﬀerent groups of each inﬂuencing factor. It
will be investigated if any genus or species show a higher or lower abundance in the
gut microbiota of infants in the speciﬁc groups. Second step of this study will look
into how the microbiota diversity inﬂuences levels of SCFAs as well as correlation of
SCFAs with genera and species. Final step will look into possible correlations between
the gut microbiota, SCFA levels and health biomarkers.

5.1
Cohort Characteristics
SKOT III cohort consists of 34 full term breastfed infants. One infant dropped
out before the nine months visit and a few samples have been excluded in the fur-
ther analysis due to either use of oral/maternal antibiotics (n=2) or no sequencing
reads obtained (n=5) in the 16s rRNA amplicon sequence analysis of faecal material.
This results in 31 and 29 infants with gut microbiota data at 5 Months (5m) and
9 Months (9m), respectively after removing excluded samples (5m, n=3; 9m, n=5).
SCFAs have been measured in all faecal samples, except one sample (5m, n=1) which
is missing SCFA data. Acetate, propionate and butyrate are focused on individually.
Isobutyric acid, 2-methylbutyric acid, isovaleric acid, valeric acid, 3-methylvaleric
acid, isocaproic acid and caproic acid are pooled in total SCFA along with acetate,
propionate and butyrate. Some samples do not have any health biomarker measure-
ments (5m, n=6; 9m, n=4). A number of samples are missing data points accordingly;
eight samples (5m, n=5; 9m, n=3) are missing delivery mode information, one sample
(5m, n=1) is missing information on siblings, seven samples (5m, n=4; 9m, n=3) are
excluded from the EWG/NWG group due to altered growth patterns. See table 10 in
appendix 9.1 for details on samples which are excluded or have missing data.
ProbiComp cohort includes a total of 290 infants age 8 to 13 months. This project
focuses on the data collected before the start of intervention, where 255 faecal samples
were successfully sequenced of the V3 region in the 16s rRNA gene. One sample
was excluded because of too few reads (n=1). SCFA levels have been measured in
50 samples where as acetate, propionate and butyrate are focused on individually.
Isobutyric acid, isovaleric acid, valeric acid and isocaproic acid are represented as
total SCFA along with acetate, propionate and butyrate.
Table 5 shows study characteristics of SKOT III and ProbiComp. Information about
infant age, gestational age and gender distribution is provided. Inﬂuencing factors
include delivery mode, older siblings, breastfeeding, weight group, formula and furred
pets. SKOT III does not have information about formula and furred pets and no sta-
tistical analyses are conducted for delivery mode and breastfeeding prevalence because
of too few infants in the C-section and no infants not being breastfed. ProbiComp
does not include the weight group factor. Due to an age span of ﬁve months in the
ProbiComp cohort, an analysis of the age distribution in each factor has been made
to be aware of co-correlation with age. Formula is the only factor which shows signif-
icant (p = 0.0292) age diﬀerence between the two groups (infants receiving formula
vs. infants not receiving formula).
The second part of table 5 gives an overview of whether the infants have been in-
troduced to diﬀerent types of foods or not. Most infants at 5m of age in the SKOT
III cohorts have not received solid foods, meanwhile most infants at 9m of age have
received solid foods. All infants of the ProbiComp cohort have received solid foods.
This will be taken into consideration when discussing the results of this study.

diversity. The eﬀect of formula may be present, but is small and potentially overlaps
with other inﬂuencing factors.

Discussion
The link between the gut microbiota, SCFAs and human health is an active era of
research. This study investigates connections between inﬂuencing factors, gut micro-
biota, SCFAs and health biomarkers of infants by looking at two independent cohorts.
When discussing results of this study, there are a few things to be aware of. Comparing
results of two independent cohorts can help validate theories, as long as diﬀerences
between the two cohorts are taken into consideration. The SKOT III cohort con-
sist of full term breastfed infants whereas the ProbiComp cohort does not have any
requirements about the infants being full term breastfed. Another thing to have in
mind is that all gut microbiota and SCFA measurements in this study are from faecal
samples. Composition of bacteria in the gut, as well as the SCFA composition, can
look diﬀerent than the composition in faeces. Only 5 −10% of SCFAs are excreted in
the faeces and it is likely that faecal, luminal, and mucosal bacterial populations can
be very diﬀerent from each other [21, 60]. A ﬁnal note before discussing the results
is that our study has used the strategy of looking at genera that show at least 25%
prevalence and 0.5% abundance and thereafter investigate species below these speciﬁc
genera. Due to this strategy there might be a risk of losing interesting species that
belong to genera not studied in this project due to the cut oﬀ.
The SKOT III cohort gives the possibility to investigate the inﬂuence of age on the
gut microbiota composition as well as the SCFA composition. Age has an inﬂuence
on both alpha and beta diversity of the gut microbiota seen by a higher SI and more
observed ASVs in older infants as well as a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the beta diversity
of the gut microbiota. A signiﬁcant increase of SCFA levels between 5m and 9m of age
are also observed, especially levels of butyrate. Changes in the gut microbiota from
5m to 9m of age as well as changes in SCFAs are most likely due to introduction of
solid foods. Transition to solid foods greatly impacts the development of gut microbial
diversity and thereby inﬂuences SCFA levels as well [17, 61, 62].
In the ProbiComp cohort there is a signiﬁcant positive correlation of SI and observed
ASVs with age as well as signiﬁcant explanation of age on the beta diversity of the
gut microbiota. We do not see any signiﬁcant correlations between SCFA levels and
age in ProbiComp, probably due to the higher age of the cohort (8-13m) where most
infants in this cohort have already transitioned to solid foods. We do see a positive
correlation between SI and levels of propionate, butyrate and total SCFA. A high
SI index represents a more diverse and evenly distributed community which explains
why a higher SI index results in higher amounts of SCFAs. For SKOT III 9m and
ProbiComp, we observe that butyrate levels are explained by beta diversity of the
gut microbiota, further indicating the change in gut microbiota to more butyrate-
producing bacteria.
The main change in bacterial composition in SKOT III from 5m to 9m is the de-
crease in relative abundance of Biﬁdobacterium, which is the most prominent genus
in breastfed infants. A signiﬁcant increase in relative abundances are observed for
Anaerostipes, Blautia, Roseburia, Ruminococcus2, Faecalibacterium, Veillonella and
Escherichia/Shigella where genera within Clostridia, like Roseburia, Anaerostipes and
Faecalibacterium are known to increase in infants gut microbiota after weaning [63].
In general, at 5m of age we see genera correlating with propionate, while at 9m of
age genera mostly correlate with butyrate. This trend ﬁts the knowledge of increase

in butyrate-producing bacteria after weaning or when infants are introduced to solid
foods.
Breastfeeding appears to have a strong inﬂuence on the infant gut microbiota diver-
sity, where breastfed infants show a less diverse gut microbiota and a higher relative
abundance of Biﬁdobacterium and Lactobacillus in accordance with the literature [63].
Formula also appears to have an inﬂuence on the gut microbiota although to a less
extent. Even though the nutritional composition of infant formula attempts to mimic
the one of breast milk, a diﬀerence is still observed in the gut microbial composition
of infants exclusively receiving formula compared to exclusively breastfed infants, as
shown in a study of 1032 infants [18]. Our data show a higher relative abundance of
Clostridium XIVa and lower Lactobacillus in formula fed infants. However, the Pro-
biComp cohort includes many infants who are both formula fed and breastfed, and
it is therefore diﬃcult to conclude the actual cause of change in the gut microbiota.
The lower relative abundance Lactobacillus in formula fed infants could be a cause of
the infant not being breastfed anymore.
Infants of the ProbiComp cohort with older siblings show a more diverse gut mi-
crobiota. Faecal samples of SKOT III 9m and ProbiComp have a higher relative
abundance of especially Faecalibacterium, including F. prausnitzii which is also seen
in literature [19]. Literature indicates the eﬀect of older siblings on the gut micro-
biota gets more prominent with age. This can explain why we do not see any eﬀect
on SKOT III 5m, only a small eﬀect on SKOT III 9m, but a higher eﬀect on the
ProbiComp cohort [17].
Delivery mode does not seem to have a big inﬂuence on the gut microbiota in our
data. The sample sizes of C-section delivered infants in SKOT III are very small
(n(5m)=4 and n(9m)=6) and therefore we have to be careful with drawing conclu-
sions regarding this data. The diﬀerence in alpha diversity between delivery modes of
infants in the ProbiComp cohort are near the threshold and there are no signiﬁcant
diﬀerences observed in relative abundances of genera. This could be because of the
relatively high age of the cohort (mean=10 months) as literature indicates the gut
microbiota inﬂuence of delivery mode is known to decrease with time and fade out at
1-2 years of age [12]. It is important to be aware of the potential correlation between
delivery mode and breastfeeding since C-section sometimes creates complications and
could prevent breastfeeding.
The weight group does not seem to have a big inﬂuence on the gut microbiota. Infants
belonging to the EWG group of SKOT III 9m are shown to have more observed ASVs.
however, a higher relative abundance of Enterococcus is seen in 5m NWG infants.
This study also investigates correlations between genera and SCFA levels in faecal
samples. In both cohorts the genus Bacteroides is positively correlating with pro-
pionate. The succinate pathway is found mainly in Bacteroidetes and is the major
route for propionate formation from dietary carbohydrates [35]. Furthermore, Alis-
tipes from Bacteroidetes phylum use the succinate pathway and positively correlates
with propionate in ProbiComp cohort. B. thetaiotaomicron is positive correlating
with all three SCFAs in SKOT III cohort. This species is an acetate producing bac-
terium, explaining the positive correlation with acetate [64]. The positive correlation
with butyrate could be because of cross-feeding of acetate consumers producing bu-
tyrate. In both cohorts it is observed that Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Roseburia
positive correlates with butyrate. Both are known butyrogenic bacteria that produces

butyrate via butyryl-CoA:acetate CoA-transferase with a consumption of acetate [35,
65]. Pseudobutyrivibria is recognised as a butyrate producing bacteria and is also seen
to positively correlate with butyrate levels in the ProbiComp cohort [66]. G. formicilis
positively correlates with butyrate levels in ProbiComp, which is a genus belonging to
the family Ruminococcaceae known to include butyrate-producing species [35]. In the
SKOT III cohort at 9m of age we see genera negatively correlating with butyrate lev-
els including Biﬁdobacterium, Lactobacillus, Enterococcus, Escherichia/Shigella and
Streptococcus. This indicates an eﬀect of change in diet from milk-based feeding to
solid food which has been shown to correlate with decreasing abundances of Biﬁ-
dobacteriaceae, Lactobacillaceae, Enterococcaceae and Enterobacteriaceae [61].
Health biomarkers are measured in blood samples of the SKOT III cohort but are not
collected from all infants and it is therefore important to be aware of the low sample
sizes for these measurements. Since the SKOT III cohort consist of infants from two
diﬀerent weight groups (NWG and EWG), correlations for health biomarkers with
bacteria and SCFAs are furthermore conducted for each of these weight groups to
indicate if the observed correlations are driven by one of those groups. The sample
sizes of the weight groups are so small, so it is decided to focus on the overall correla-
tions with further veriﬁcation by the weight group correlations. If both weight groups
(NWG and EWG) show the same correlation (positive or negative) as the overall, the
result may be more trustworthy. If alternating correlations are observed, the result is
not as strong but may be directly inﬂuenced by weight of the infants. The division
into weight groups are only used as guidance due to the low sample numbers.
Multiple signiﬁcant correlations are present between bacterial genera and health
biomarkers. albumin, ferritin, HDL-cholesterol, IGF-1 and leptin seem to be asso-
ciated with gut microbiota in both 5m and 9m old infants, whereas only IGF-1 seems
to be correlating with SCFA levels as well. Some of these observations are discussed
further.
HDL-cholesterol shows positive correlations with genera at both 5 months and 9
months. HDL-cholesterol is a transporter of fat molecules from cells back to the liver
where the fat gets re-used or excreted. Levels of HDL-cholesterol have previously
been associated with the gut microbiota and it has been suggested to go through
gut microbiota metabolites, like SCFAs [67, 68]. However, the inﬂuence of speciﬁc
bacteria on HDL-cholesterol levels have not been investigated yet.
Leptin has overall negative correlations with genera at 5m and 9m, although a weak
positive correlation is observed for EWG at 5m. Leptin is a hunger-reducing hormone
which is secreted after meal intake. Correlations have been shown between leptin and
SCFAs where addition of propionate and acetate result in increased amounts of leptin
[69–71].
This study shows observations that are interesting - but not able to conﬁrm in liter-
ature. This includes the correlations of the gut microbiota with ferritin and albumin.
Ferritin shows negative correlations with genera of the gut microbiota at both 5m
and 9m. It is a protein functioning as a carrier of iron in blood serum and is known
to be produced by almost all living organisms [72]. Albumin shows both negative and
positive correlations with the gut microbiota at both 5m and 9m as well as an overall
negative correlation with all three major SCFAs, especially seen at 9m of age with

acetate and propionate. It is also observed that infants of EWG have a signiﬁcantly
higher concentration of albumin compared to NWG at 9 months of age. Albumin is
the most abundant circulating protein, which functions as a transporter of fatty acids,
hormones and other metabolites [73, 74].
The relationship between IGF-1 and SCFAs through the gut microbiota have been
described before [20, 22]. IGF-1 is a hormone with known actions on skeletal growth
and the gut microbiota is capable of dynamically modulating circulating IGF-1 in the
host, suggesting that microbiota induce host IGF-1 synthesis to inﬂuence growth [20,
22]. Even though many diﬀerent animal studies have been conducted which points
towards a correlation between the gut microbiota, especially Lactobacillus, and IGF-1
and bone growth, no human studies have been able to conﬁrm this yet [75].

Conclusions and Future Perspectives
This study shows that the bacterial composition of the infant gut microbiota, as well as
SCFA composition, are strongly inﬂuenced by age in the ﬁrst year of life. Inﬂuencing
factors observed to have a potential eﬀect on the gut microbiota in this study are
breastfeeding, formula and presence of older siblings as well as delivery mode and
weight group to a less extent. It is shown that SCFA composition is inﬂuenced by the
diversity of the gut microbiota and that genera associated with higher SCFA levels
(8m of age and above) includes Bacteroides, Roseburia, Faecalibacterium, Alistipes
and Gemmiger including the species B. thetaiotaomicron, B. uniformis, F. prausnitzii
and G. formicilis. Multiple correlations are present between bacterial genera and
health biomarkers, whereas albumin, ferritin, HDL-cholesterol, IGF-1 and leptin seem
to be associated with gut microbiota in both 5m and 9m old infants whereas IGF-1
shows association with SCFA levels as well.
To conﬁrm our ﬁndings of bacteria correlating with SCFAs, it would be interesting
to grow the bacteria in vitro and investigate their metabolites. Furthermore may this
in vivo study conﬁrm some results already observed in vitro. This study has not
investigated cross-feeding, which could be done by correlating bacteria with other
bacteria, to see if the growth of bacteria and production of their metabolites depends
on the abundance of other bacteria.
There is need of more research to fully understand microbiota-host interactions. Fu-
ture in vivo studies are important to deﬁne the impact of gut microbiota and SCFAs
on infant health and to deﬁne which bacteria inﬂuence infant health. This could be
conducted by transferring speciﬁc bacteria into germ-free animals and investigating
correlations with health biomarkers. It could also be interesting to study the eﬀect of
microbe-derived metabolites such as SCFAs on the host by blocking pathways shown
to have an eﬀect on host health.
Development of the infant gut microbiota appears to be strongly inﬂuenced by dif-
ferent factors. One approach to optimise infant health is to be aware of which gut
bacteria are associated with human health and thereby promote the growth of these
bacteria, especially in infants lacking speciﬁc factors.
