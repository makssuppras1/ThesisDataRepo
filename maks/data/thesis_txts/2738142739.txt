Abstract 
Due to global warming and fossil fuels being a finite resource, there has been an increasing 
pressure to find alternative energy sources. One such source is hydrogen, which can be 
produced from electricity, and used to produce electricity in a solid oxide cell. However, the 
operation of such a device is limited by the thickness of its electrolyte and multi-step sintering 
fabrication process. As a result, new fabrication methods must be determined. Screen-printing 
of an electrolyte onto green fuel electrode substrates was investigated with 8 mol% yttria 
stabilised zirconia-based inks using a polyvinylpyrrolidone dispersant and ethyl cellulose 
binder. It has been found that cracking is prominent during drying of these inks, so increased 
binder content was investigated. Printing parameters were explored, and it was determined that 
a pressure of 3 bar, speed of 60 mm/s, and a snap off distance of 0.1 mm gives the least 
deposition of ink. Additionally, sintering onto LaSrFeNiTiO3 based fuel electrodes was found 
to be most successful. No delamination was observed, and the electrolyte layer was dense. The 
electrolyte layer was also found to be approximately 9 μm. Printing uniformity was also an 
issue as well as the presence of soft agglomerates. Viscosity increased with milling time, 
contrary to what is expected. This leads to the conclusion that the contribution of binder and 
dispersant used in this system is not appropriate and would benefit from further exploration 
and adjustments as well as there being an optimum amount of milling time. 
 
1 Introduction 
 
1.1 Need for sustainable energy 
There has been an increasing pressure to find alternative, sustainable energy sources due to the 
increase in global warming as a result of the increase in greenhouse gas emissions.1,2 These 
greenhouse gases trap heat from the sun, resulting in global warming.1 Additionally, current 
energy production and its impact on the environment is one of the most pressing issues 
discussed globally. Approximately 40 Gton of CO2 eq was emitted globally at the end of the 
last decade. Unfortunately, the gap between current emissions and those that would result in a 
warming of 2 ˚C is small. This goes against what was pledged during the Paris agreement.2As 
a result of this, governments, businesses and individuals have been attempting to decrease 
emissions through various techniques such as: implementing carbon taxes, creating incentives, 
and using alternative energies, such as fuel cells.1,2 
The current main source of energy production is from fossil fuels. This results in the emission 
of a large amount of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases and pollutants.1 Additionally, 
energy demand is increasing, yet fossil fuels are a finite resource, and therefore, cannot meet 
future energy demand.1,2 As a result, new and efficient energy technologies need to be 
developed in order to secure energy for the future, as well as decreasing its current impact on 
the environment.2 
Renewable energy production has grown significantly in the past decade from 110 GW in 2011 
to 280 GW in 2020.2 One of the technologies contributing to this is hydrogen production, which 
has been described as a new energy paradigm and has been considered a promising solution 
for clean energy.2,3 Production for hydrogen in this application must be clean and efficiently

produced. Unfortunately, current forms of production consist of steam reforming of methane, 
which is a fossil fuel-based method. One way to overcome this, is to produce hydrogen from 
electrolysis of water.2 
1.2 Electrolysis and solid oxide cells 
Electrolysis of water has shown to be key in the development of energy conversion.4 Namely, 
with respect to storing surplus energy from renewables such as solar and wind, allowing these 
technologies to reach their full potential in terms of energy generation. This can help to manage 
the load on the grid and ensure a constant, reliable supply of electricity. Electrolysis does this 
by converting electrical energy into chemical energy, which can then be stored.4–6 The energy 
can then be recovered by using the produced hydrogen in a fuel cell. Since renewables account 
for around 20% of the electricity grid, this is a good solution to deal with surplus generation 
when demand for electricity is low.4,5 
The general set up of a fuel cell consists of two electrodes, a fuel electrode and an oxygen 
electrode, separated by an electrolyte. Different kinds of fuel cells exist, each with their own 
conditions and parameters. Some examples include: the proton exchange membrane fuel cell 
(PEMFC), phosphoric acid, and solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC) as depicted in Figure 1. These 
fuel cells differ by the type of electrolyte that is present in each.6 However, of the previously 
mentioned fuel cells, only SOFCs do not operate at low temperatures. Additionally, SOFCs are 
reversible and can operate as a solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC). This reversibility of the 
solid oxide cell (SOC) allows for conversion from electrical energy to chemical energy and 
vice versa.4

Other advantages to using SOCs include fuel flexibility, near zero carbon emissions, and 
providing water heating and cooling to homes, due to excess heat generated.1,2,6 SOCs operate 
at high temperatures, usually at around 1000 ˚C, with some systems operating at an 
intermediate temperature of 600-800 ˚C.3,5 Although this comes with high operating costs, it 
does also give some benefits. It allows for the generation of a high-power density without the 
use of precious metal catalysts. Additionally, as previously mentioned, it allows for the 
production of waste heat.  
SOCs are characterised by having two porous electrodes with a dense electrolyte.6 The 
electrolyte of such a system is usually made of 8 mol% yttria stabilised zirconia (8YSZ). This 
has been found to give the highest ionic conductivity of O2- ions.8 Additionally, the electrolyte 
should have a density above 90% in order for efficient ionic conductivity. Electrodes, on the 
other hand, should have a porosity of 20-40% to allow for proper gas diffusion, and mixed 
ionic and electrical conductivity. They should also be homogeneous when it comes to porosity, 
defects and grain size. These aspects will allow for efficient electrocatalytic activity, which is 
a key property for a functioning electrode. The fuel electrode is the location at which the fuel 
is oxidised. The oxygen electrode is where oxygen from the air is reduced into O2- ions, which 
then travel through the electrolyte to the fuel electrode. At the fuel electrode, O2-  ions then 
reacts with H+ to form steam and electrons, which can then form a current. The movement of 
these electrons and oxide ions maintains a charge balance throughout the system.6 Due to its 
role in maintaining a charge balance. the electrolyte is one of the most important components 
to a SOC.8 
1.3 Importance of the electrolyte 
As previously discussed, the electrolyte acts to transport O2- ions from the oxygen electrode to 
the fuel electrode. This occurs due to naturally occurring oxygen vacancies in the structure of 
8 mol% yttria stabilised zirconia (8YSZ). This is required so that there is pure ionic 
conductivity through the membrane and no electrical conductivity, since this can cause a short 
circuit in the system. However, in order for 8YSZ to operate as an effective oxide ion selective 
membrane, a high temperature needs to be reached.8 This is because YSZ has a high activation 
energy.9 These high temperatures affect many aspects of this technology. Namely, the 
components that can be used since these must be stable at high temperatures. This means that 
specialist materials are required, as well as high operating costs.1,4–6,8 
The quality of the electrolyte layer is also important. The electrolyte layer must be dense and 
defect free to avoid hot spots as a result of combustion reactions.8 These types of defects can 
occur as a result of the fabrication technique used, which can greatly affect performance.1 As 
a result, the electrolyte is usually the determining factor when it comes to operating 
temperatures as a result of ohmic losses.5,8,10 Therefore, optimising the fabrication of this layer 
is key in decreasing overall operating temperature.6,8 Lowering temperatures could offer a 
higher round-trip efficiency. It has been reported that operating temperatures of 400-650 ˚C 
could give a round-trip efficiency of 70% in fuel cell mode.4 Reducing the operating 
temperature will allow for a broader range of materials to be used and potential applications. 
Overall, this will reduce cost of fabrication and use.3,5,6,11 
Resistance present in the 8YSZ electrolyte increases significantly when the thickness is greater 
than 10 μm, therefore the aim is to decrease the thickness as much as possible.8,10 As a result, 
one can reduce the operating temperature by decreasing the thickness of the electrolyte layer.1 
It has been predicted that a thickness of 1-3 μm could result in an operating temperature of 600 
˚C.8

There are three main architectures of SOCs. Fuel electrode supported, electrolyte supported, 
and oxygen electrode supported cells. They vary depending on which is the thickest part of the 
cell. In an electrolyte supported cell, the electrolyte is the thickest, which results in high ohmic 
resistance and therefore high temperatures required for operation. Lower temperatures are 
needed when one of the electrodes is supporting the cell. This is because the electrolyte is the 
thinnest part of the cell and therefore the ohmic resistance is lower, lowering operating 
temperature.12 
Figure 2 shows how changing the thickness of the electrolyte affects the performance of the 
cell. Here, Park et al.13 also compared a cell with PrOx integration in the electrolyte, however, 
this is outside of the scope of this work. As can be seen, the performance of the cell with 
thickness of 2.5 μm shows better performance than that of 8 μm. At higher current densities, 
the power density is higher. Additionally, as current density increases, the cell voltage 
decreases at a slower rate for the thinner electrolyte. This reflects a decreased ohmic 
resistance.13

1.4 Green on green technique 
The process of manufacturing a SOC should be low cost, scalable and have a high yield. 
Current fabrication methods for a fuel electrode supported cell does not meet these 
requirements. The current method includes two sintering steps. The co-sintering of the fuel 
electrode support, fuel electrode, and the electrolyte, followed by the sintering of the oxygen 
electrode. This is an energy intensive process. Therefore, it is of interest to reduce the number 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
of sintering steps during fabrication. Co-sintering can reduce costs and energy demand for 
fabrication and manufacturing by reducing the number of sintering steps with the fabrication 
of an SOC, bringing manufacturing costs closer to the desired goal.7,14 
It is possible to achieve single step fabrication with electrolyte supported cells, as reported by 
Menzler et al.14 However, this would result in the electrolyte being the thickest part of the cell. 
As previously discussed, this is not ideal due to the increased ohmic resistance observed in 
thicker electrolytes. This can be seen in the results that the group obtained. At a temperature of 
800 ˚C and cell voltage of 0.7 V, the current density was found to be 1 A cm-2, which is 30% 
lower than comparable cells. Therefore, new fabrication methods should be explored in order 
to decrease the number of sintering steps for a fuel electrode supported cell.14 
Other work has been done by Zhang et al.15 where a gadolinia doped ceria (GDC) electrolyte 
was screen printed onto a green fuel electrode support and co-sintered. In this case, at a current 
density of 2 A cm-2 and temperature of 750 C, the power density was found to be 1.68 W cm-
2, which is higher than a lot of the state-of-the-art SOFCs.15 
1.5 Fabrication methods 
There are many different ways to fabricate these SOCs, including, but not limited to: tape 
casting, screen printing, dip-coating, spray coating, electrochemical vapour deposition, 
physical vapour deposition, atomic layer deposition, chemical vapour deposition, and spin 
coating.6,8,11,16 These are all well-known ceramic processing techniques, with tape-casting, 
screen printing, and dip-coating being the most common. They are cheap methods that can be 
easily implemented in manufacturing.8 
With regards to other techniques, they suffer from various drawbacks. For example, chemical 
vapour deposition suffers from requiring high temperatures and producing corrosive gases, and 
atomic layer deposition is expensive and requires strict control of experimental conditions.11 
The common techniques used for the fabrication of electrolyte supported cells are tape casting 
the electrolyte and screen printing the two electrodes. However, a fuel electrode supported cells 
are fabricated via tape casting and lamination of the fuel electrode support, fuel electrode, and 
electrolyte, followed by screen printing of the oxygen electrode. However, tape casting is a 
thick film technique and greatly limits how thin the electrolyte can be made. Therefore, scalable 
thin film techniques should be explored to reduce the ohmic resistance exhibited in thicker 
electrolyte films.14 
1.5.1 Screen printing 
Screen printing is one of the most common fabrication techniques and has been gaining interest 
from researchers. It is a simple, cost-effective method that consists of pressing an ink or paste 
through a mesh to achieve the desired result.6,16 The thickness, in this case, can be controlled.6 
The main principal is the deposition of an ink on the edge of the mesh, followed by a squee-
gee distributing and presses the ink through the mesh and onto a substrate. This method is 
suitable for large scale production due to it being inexpensive, having minimal waste and being 
highly reproducible.6,16 Figure 3 shows an EKRA E2 screen printer that has been used for this 
work.

Figure 3. EKRA E2 screen printer 
 
In order to achieve the desired results, one must consider various parameters. These include: 
the screen counting mesh, print number, rheology of the ink, and sintering temperature.6,16 
Layers can be improved by paying close attention to the composition of the ink as well as the 
print number. To optimise the rheology of the ink, the components and their relative quantities 
must be considered.  
A ceramic ink consists of the ceramic powder, a solvent, binder, dispersant and plasticiser. 
Appropriate inks for screen printing have been reported to have a solid loading of 25-31 vol% 
and a binder content of 1-3 vol%. Despite this, higher solid loading can lead to a denser result, 
which is desired for an SOC electrolyte.6  
How easily an ink can be dispensed and printed is dependent on the solvent and the binder 
used. Common solvents include terpineol and texanol. The binder content must be carefully 
calculated since too much binder can lead to a porous final result and decrease the printability 
of the ink due to tackiness. However, too little binder also poses a problem, such as 
delamination. Therefore, a binder content of approximately 1 vol% is recommended. It has also 
been noted that ethyl cellulose is a highly effective binder, with only 0.5 vol% required to affect 
the ink's rheology. This could mean that less binder is required in the ink, leading to a denser 
electrolyte layer.6 The ideal range of viscosity for YSZ inks has been reported to be between 
4-12 Pa s at a sheer rate of 100 s-1.16 
Although it is said that screen printing is for thicker films8, thinner films have been achieved 
using this technique.17 It has also been reported that electrolyte layers of 5 μm or more give an 
open circuit voltage of more than 1.0 V. As previously described, the layer is greatly affected 
by the rheology of the ink and parameters of the screen printing. Faster printing times and few 
print numbers will result in thinner layers.6 
1.5.2 Rheology of inks 
Understanding the rheology of inks and pastes is key when it comes to ceramic processing. 
Rheology shows the deformation and flow of a material.18,19 It is used for quality control, which 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
is essential when it comes to screen printing. Of note is how the viscosity of a material changes 
with the shear rate and over time. A material is described as being shear thinning when the 
viscosity decreases with increasing shear rate. Vice versa, shear thickening occurs when the 
viscosity of the material increases with increase shear rate. In these cases, the material is 
described as non-Newtonian. However, if the viscosity does not change with the shear strain, 
the material is described as Newtonian.18 Within shear thinning, there is a phenomenon known 
as thixotropy. This occurs when shear thinning is observed at applied stress. However, when 
the stress is removed, the material returns to its original viscosity within an appropriate time 
frame. This can be observed in many commercial products, most notably paints and coatings.20 
This can be seen in Figure 4. Here, a hysteresis loop can be observed. This loop indicates the 
presence of thixotropic behaviour. As the sheer stress is decreased, the shear rate decreases to 
the starting value faster than the sheer stress was applied.21 This is an important factor when it 
comes to screen printing since it controls the size and the shape of the print. The size and shape 
are retained after printing due to the increase in viscosity. This is most critical in smaller 
printing areas.  
 
 
Figure 4. Graph showing the thixotropic behaviour of various SiC slurries prepared for 3D 
pritning.21 
 
Rheology of inks can be determined by using an instrument called a rheometer. There are 
various ways in which these instruments can be set up. One such way is in a parallel plate 
configuration as seen in Figure 5.19 The top plate here is the plate that moves during the 
measurement, while the bottom remains stationary.19,22 The liquid material is maintained 
between the two plates via capillary forces. The gap should be five to ten times greater than the 
maximum particle size.22 Torque is applied via the top plate. This allows for control of the 
stress level or the strain. In this case, the stress is the independent variable whereas strain is the 
dependent variable. This method is the best to determine the yield stress.19

1.5.3 Characterisation of printed layer 
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is one to the most common imaging techniques with 
regards to microstructure and characterisation of solid surfaces.23,24 This technique results in a 
resolution up to 10 nm.24 The instrument consists of an electron gun, which is placed at the top 
of the column.23 The electrons are then accelerated to between 0.1 and 30 keV through a high 
voltage.23,24 A hairpin tungsten gun then increases the diameter, which results in better 
resolution.23 This is then followed by electromagnetic lenses and apertures that focus the beam 
to a concentrated spot on the sample. The sample is then scanned by this electron beam.23,24 
This scanning is conducted via the scanning coils present in the instrument.24 The beam then 
interacts with the sample, which results in various types of emission. This includes x-rays, 
backscattered electrons, and secondary electrons. These can be detected by the appropriate 
detectors.23,24 All measurements are conducted under high vacuum to avoid absorption and 
scattering of electrons due to air.23 The sample is mounted onto a metal stub that has been 
coated in 40-60 nm of carbon or a metal such as gold or palladium.24 
From this technique, black and white images can be generated to show the surfaces of the 
samples. Cross sections are prepared by fracturing the samples and embedding it in epoxy, 
followed by carbon coating to give a conductive surface. From these images, distinct layers 
and their respective thicknesses can be acquired.

3 Results and Discussion  
3.1 Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) 
Figure 8 shows the debinding and sintering profile used for this work. The temperature ramp 
was set to 15 ˚C/h up to 200 ˚C. The temperature was then ramped at 15 ˚C/h up to 410 ˚C, 
followed by another 15 ˚C/h ramp to 600 ˚C. At each hold temperature, the temperature was 
held for 4 hours to allow for proper burnout of organics. Once this debinding profile was 
complete, the temperature ramp was set to 60 ˚C/h to a temperature of 1300 ˚C for sintering. 
This temperature was held for 10 h to ensure complete densification and sintering of the half-
cell. This profile was created based on the TGA data of the organic additives in the ink. 
The TGAs of the starting ingredients is shown in Figure 9. The decomposition ranges are 
summarised in Table 4. Both DPG and PEG 400 burn out at approximately 200 ˚C. This was 
set as the first debinding step. Ethyl cellulose and PVB both burn out at around 410 ˚C, 
therefore, this was set as the next sintering step. Finally, PVP K30 burns out close to 600 ˚C.

3.3 Particle size distribution (PSD) 
The three initially prepared inks have comparable particle size distribution. However, it was 
observed that there was a considerable proportion of soft agglomerates present in the inks, 
shown in Figure 13. An attempt to decrease these agglomerates was made by using a planetary 
ball mill to prepare the inks. This was conducted for DPG_PVPK30_Ink_V3 and onwards. 
However. As can be seen in Figure 13, it had no effect on the proportion of agglomerates 
present in the final ink. The agglomerates could be decreased when diluted and sonicated, but 
not during milling, indicating that these agglomerates are due to a dispersion issue rather than 
a problem with the milling. Therefore, an alternative dispersant contribution should be 
considered in future iterations.

3.4.2 Printing parameter exploration 
Table 6 summarises the average mass differences obtained during printing from the parameters 
explored. For this, the DPG_PVPK30_Ink_V1 formulation was used. Contrary to literature25 
printing pressure shows a decrease in the layer printed onto the laminated fuel electrode. 
==PAGE:27==

Usually, at higher pressures, more ink is pushed through the mesh and deposited onto the 
substrate due to the shear thinning behaviour of the ink. It is unclear why the opposite has been 
observed. However, it has also been reported that important parameters for screen printing, 
such as printing pressure, should be optimised for each ink and screen printer.26   
 
 
Table 6. Summary of the average mass gain due to printing and their respective parameters 
 
At higher printing speed, the deposited layer is also found to be less. This correlates to what is 
expected from literature.26 However, at 70 mm/s, the layer deposited is greater than that 
obtained at 60 mm/s. As before, this is a parameter that should be optimised for the ink. At 
higher velocities, it has been reported that ink slippage can occur. This is when the squee-gee 
slips on the ink and becomes detached from the screen. This can result in unwanted ink being 
deposited, resulting in a higher mass of ink being deposited.26 However, since such a small 
difference in mass is observed, this could be due to an error in the balance used to measure the 
mass.  
Snap-off distance is the distance between the mesh and the substrate. Therefore, at higher snap-
off distances, less ink is deposited since the pressure is not sufficient to push the ink through to 
the substrate. As can be seen in Table 6, the higher the snap-off distance, the lower the mass 
deposited. This fits what has been recorded in literature.27  
Using these results, it was determined that the best parameters to give the least deposited mass 
of ink on the substrate surface were a printing pressure of 3 bar, a print speed of 60 mm/s, and 
a snap off distance of 0.1 mm. However, it should be noted that these mass changes are small 
and could also be as a result of an error from the balance or even from the tape sticking to the 
screen and resulting in unwanted deposition of ink. Mass changes were also recorded for the 
other ink formulations under these parameters, as summarised in Table 7. For these, the 
identified optimum conditions were maintained. The distribution in these mass changes could 
be down to systematic errors from the balance. DPG_PVPK30_Ink_V1 has a much lower 
deposition than other formulations. This could be due to difference in the fabrication of the 
inks.

3.4.3 Drying Temperature exploration

used for these experiments was DPG_PVPK30_Ink_V2. Figure 17 shows the different results

formulations with increased binder were explored. Since drying at 40 ˚C gave less cracking, 
this drying temperature was selected for future prints.

pinholes can be observed. These are likely introduced during printing when the screen is lifted 
from the substrate surface. This increases the porosity of the electrolyte layer, which is the 
opposite of the goal of this work.

4 Conclusion 
Since fossil fuels are a finite resource, alternative energy sources must be found. SOCs are one 
such promising technologies. However, the thickness of the electrolyte and the several sintering 
steps involved in fabrication hold it back from being commercialised. An explorative study was 
performed to formulate the screen-printing inks using 8YSZ electrolyte powders, PVP 
dispersant and ethyl cellulose binder to print thin electrolyte layers. A green-on-green printing 
process was explored by printing 8YSZ layers on NiO-3YSZ/NiO-8YSZ and NiO-
3YSZ/LSFNT tape casted green substrates.   
Screen printing with a pressure of 3 bar, a speed of 60 mm/s and with a snap-off distance of 0.1 
mm was found to give the least deposition on a substrate. It was also found that the prints were 
not uniform. Therefore, alternative contributions of the PVP K30 dispersant should be 
explored. There was a presence of soft agglomerates, and it was observed that the viscosity 
increased with milling time. This indicates that the selected amounts of dispersant did not result 
in stable and well dispersed 8YSZ inks for screen printing, and that there is an optimum milling 
time.  
Cracking was observed after drying at various temperatures. As a result, binder was increased 
at different increments. However, cracking was still observed, and large agglomerates were 
also present. The investigated range of binder amounts did not result in defect free prints. As 
with the dispersant, the contribution of ethyl cellulose as a binder in this formulation should be 
further explored.
