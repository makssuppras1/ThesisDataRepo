Abstract
Efficient operating room scheduling is essential for optimizing hospital resource alloca-
tion, minimizing costs, and improving patient care. One of the primary challenges in OR
management is accurately predicting surgical procedure duration, as incorrect estimates
can lead to delays, idle time, and increased operational costs. This study explores the use
of Machine Learning models to predict procedure durations based on real-world hospital
data, evaluating both regression and classification approaches.
The regression models, including Random Forest, XGBoost, and Long Short-Term Mem-
ory Deep Neural Network, struggled to provide precise predictions, with the best model
(Random Forest) achieving an R² score of 0.45 and an average prediction error of 15
minutes. Given the limitations of regression, classification models were tested to catego-
rize procedures into predefined duration intervals. Random Forest and XGBoost classi-
fiers achieved the highest accuracy of 0.55 and 0.56, respectively, but misclassification
of longer procedures remained a critical issue. The study identified class imbalance and
overlapping feature distributions as key factors affecting model performance, leading to a
systematic underestimation of longer surgical durations.
Feature importance analysis revealed that service type, room-related attributes, and sur-
geon workload were the most influential factors in predicting procedure duration. Orthope-
dic procedures showed unique patterns, with section-related features playing a significant
role, whereas Urology and Gynecology-Obstetrics achieved better accuracy due to their
stable durations and sufficient case volume.
The findings highlight the potential and challenges of using ML models for OR scheduling.
While machine learning offers a data-driven alternative to experience-based scheduling,
further improvements are needed to enhance prediction accuracy, especially for longer
procedures.

Introduction
Operating rooms (ORs) are vital to hospital operations, being both one of the most costly
services and a major source of revenue [1], as they incorporate healthcare spending and
influences patient well-being alongside the institutional productivity at the same time. The
effective management of an operating room is the cornerstone in managing resources,
operating costs, patient waiting times, and healthcare outcomes.
The most important problem in OR management is the estimation of the time each surgery
will take since that has direct implications on the efficiency of surgical schedules, staffing,
and overall patient flow. Efficient planning and scheduling of ORs are crucial for optimiz-
ing performance and controlling the substantial costs associated with these services. In
addition, accurately estimating the duration of the surgery is vital to optimize operating
room efficiency. Overestimating or underestimating the time required for procedures can
result in various operational challenges, such as unutilized facilities, extended work hours,
or the need to reschedule operations [2].
However, this process is challenging due to the inherent variability in surgical durations,
which are often estimated based on surgeons’ experience, a method that is susceptible to
bias and inaccuracies. These inaccuracies can result in inefficiencies such as idle time,
overtime, and longer patient waiting times. Despite the importance of accurate schedul-
ing, many hospitals continue to rely on experience-based methods rather than quanti-
tative tools for the allocation of time blocks and resources [3], highlighting the need for
data-driven approaches in the management of the operating room [4].
New developments in artificial intelligence and machine learning could provide new strate-
gies for refined surgical time prediction. ML models could use the already existing exten-
sive hospital datasets to learn intricate structures and relationships that affect the length
of surgery. These models could optimize scheduling, improve resource distribution, and
possibly reduce the total cost of healthcare services.
2.1
Problem Statement
Accurately predicting surgical procedure duration is crucial for efficient OR scheduling, yet
traditional experience-based methods often lead to delays, inefficiencies, and resource
mismanagement. The complexity and variability of procedures make precise estimation
difficult. Machine learning offers a data-driven solution. This research explores ML tech-
niques to enhance surgical time prediction, aiming to improve OR efficiency, scheduling
accuracy, and hospital resource utilization.
2.2
Research Goals
This thesis is situated at the intersection of healthcare management and data science,
with a particular emphasis on reducing uncertainty in Operating Room scheduling. The
research centers on predicting different aspects of surgical times using real-world sec-
ondary data from hospitals in Region Zealand. More specifically, the following questions
will be addressed:
• Data Quality: What data quality characteristics could be used to enhance the accu-
racy and reliability of the provided data?

Literature Review
This chapter reviews key studies in OR cost analysis, scheduling challenges, and opti-
mization strategies. It also explores the role of predictive modeling, machine learning, and
computational techniques in improving OR efficiency. The following sections will discuss:
• The financial cost of ORs and rising surgical expenditures
• Uncertainty in OR scheduling and its impact on efficiency
• Optimization efforts, including stochastic models and real-time scheduling algorithms
• Machine learning-based predictive models for OR time estimation
By examining these areas, this review aims to identify existing gaps in the literature and
highlight potential research directions for improving OR efficiency through data-driven
decision-making.
3.1
Cost of Operating Rooms and Surgery Procedures
Financial data from over 3,044 hospitals between 2005 and 2014 in US indicate that the
average cost per minute of operating room (OR) time ranges between $36 and $37 [5].
Direct expenses, such as wages, surgical supplies, and benefits, account for 54. 6% to
59. 1% of total OR costs, while indirect expenses, including facility maintenance, utilities,
and administrative costs, comprise the remainder. Among direct expenses, wages and
benefits for OR personnel represent the largest share, ranging from $13 to $14 per minute,
whereas non-billable surgical supplies contribute $2.50 to $3.50 per minute. Notably, OR
costs remained consistent across different hospital ownership models (nonprofit, for-profit,
and government-owned) [5].
Rising Surgical Expenditures
Surgical expenditures within the United States have been raising at an alarming way.
Spending accounted for 29% of the healthcare budget in 2005 [6]. This reasoning can be
accounted for multiplicity, such as the improvement of surgical instruments, demographic
shifts, increased prevalence of defensive medicine, structural inefficiencies in the insur-
ance scheme.
Following historical trends and economic projections, the value of surgical care spend-
ing will increase from $572 billion in 2005 to $912 billion in 2025 (4.6% of GDP to 7.3%
of GDP). The constant increase in spending on surgeries has deep economic impacts
including the increased expenditure on health care by individuals, the increasing national
debt, and the escalating taxation needed to support Medicare, Medicaid, and even private
insurance policies.
3.2
Uncertainty in Operating Room Scheduling
A crucial obstacle in operating room scheduling is the variability that comes with surgical
procedures, which makes it hard to plan accurately. The literature suggests multiple ori-
gins of uncertainty such as surgery time, patient arrival time, resources availability, and
patient care requirements [7]. Duration uncertainty is considered the most severe in the
context of operating room productivity. Even within the same category of surgeries, indi-
vidual patient characteristics, available instruments, and even the number of surgical staff
may alter the length of a procedure.

Despite knowing the probability distribution of surgery durations, there are still uncer-
tainties with regard to pre-surgery preparations, intraoperative steps, and recovery times.
When a specific procedure is completed ahead of schedule, the next patient is not al-
ways prepared, leading to gaps with little or no OR utilization. Because ORs are highly
dynamic environments, changes must be performed on the spot. Taking this into account
this paper suggests that future research should focus on developing predictive and pre-
scriptive models that integrate real-time data, machine learning, and adaptive scheduling
techniques to better manage OR uncertainties.
Zhou et al.[8] studied the constraints associated with using historical surgical durations for
estimating future surgical case times. This study mentions that solely relying on surgery
durations from the past is probably not going to have too much impact on reducing the
frequency of cases finishing late. The authors contend that this approach under consider-
ation omits the variability in patient’s condition, the complexity of the surgery, and how the
procedure is performed, which leads to overestimations and inefficiency in OR scheduling.
The study claims that better scheduling accuracy can be achieved through the incorpora-
tion of extra predictive factors like surgeon-specific performance information, the complex-
ity of cases, and intraoperative changes in real time. By integrating data-driven methods
and predictive analytics, hospitals can develop more reliable forecasting models that help
minimize delays, reduce overtime costs, and enhance overall OR efficiency.
In the end, the findings demonstrate that while historical data serves a valuable base-
line, stronger predicting techniques like machine learning models and real time monitoring
should be used to enhance surgical scheduling and resource leverage for the hospital.
3.3
Optimization Efforts
Denton et al.[1] have explored the difficulties associated with the scheduling of operating
rooms (OR) due to unexpected durations of surgery . These authors presented a stochas-
tic optimization technique which inherently resolves the uncertainty of OR facility planning
on the level of minimizing delays, idle times, and overtime costs. Their model considers
both the sequencing of surgeries and the timing of their start, aiming to enhance overall
OR efficiency.
The researchers proposed a sequencing strategy prioritizing surgeries based on the vari-
ance in their expected durations. Specifically, they suggested scheduling procedures
with lower variability earlier in the day to reduce the cumulative impact of potential de-
lays. Using data from a real hospital, the case study showed that total waiting times for
the surgeons and OR teams, as well as, the idle times and the overtime costs were all
significantly lower.
The study underscores the importance of effective sequencing and scheduling in man-
aging the inherent uncertainties of surgical procedures. By implementing the proposed
model and sequencing rules, hospitals can achieve more efficient OR utilization, leading
to cost savings and improved patient care. The findings highlight the potential benefits of
adopting data-driven scheduling practices in surgical settings.
Vladu et al.[9] investigated the impact of computational algorithms on OR efficiency, specif-
ically with regards to surgical scheduling and team dynamics. They implemented a new

3.4
Predictive Modeling for OR Optimization
Several studies have employed ML algorithms to minimize errors in surgical time estima-
tion, with most findings indicating that ML models outperform surgeon estimates. Some
studies focused on identifying optimal ML models by comparing different algorithmic ap-
proaches.
Abbou et al.[10] explored the role of predictive modeling in improving OR scheduling effi-
ciency. Recognizing that ORs are central to hospital operations and major cost centers,
the study aimed to develop a ML-based predictive model to estimate surgery durations
more accurately.
The study covered a period of 10 years of clinical and administrative data obtained from
two large public hospitals in Israel. The dataset also captured the entire range of preop-
erative factors including patient age and gender, diagnosis, laboratory tests, risk factors,
planned procedures, types of anesthesia, and experience of the operating surgeon. With
this information, the authors trained an XGBoost ML model with the goal of predicting the
surgery duration. They evaluated the model against a naive baseline which predicted the
median of surgery times based upon pre-existing data.
The results demonstrated that the ML model significantly outperformed the naive base-
line, explaining nearly 70% of the variance in surgery durations. Key factors influencing
surgery length included the type of procedure and the main surgeon’s experience.
Shahabikargar et al.[11] utilized boosting, bagging, and random forest techniques to pre-
dict the duration of 60,000 elective surgeries. Their findings indicated higher prediction
errors in complex specialties such as gynecology and cardiothoracic surgeries.
Bartek et al.[12] improved surgical duration predictions for over 47,000 cases by employ-
ing random forest and gradient boosting models that incorporated patient-specific and
procedural characteristics. Predictive models were designed using both linear regres-
sion and supervised machine learning with the XGBoost and Random Forest algorithm.
The models were grouped into three types: all-inclusive, service specific, and surgeon
specific. As with all models, the performance was compared with institution’s average
method which calculated average historical procedure times and surgeon estimates for
each individual. Measures of evaluation include accuracy, overage (exceeding the pre-
dicted duration by 10% ), underage (failing to reach the predicted duration by over 10%),
and how many of the estimates were accurate with 10% tolerance threshold.
The machine learning models proved to have superior predictive capabilities compared
to traditional methods. Out of all models, the XGBoost models had the most predictive
capability, with the linear model having the least, the surgeon specific machine learning
model outperformed the service specific machine learning model achieving higher ac-
curacy, lower percentages of overage and underage, and a greater proportion of cases
within the 10% tolerance threshold. Specifically, the ability to predict case durations within
10% improved from 32% using the institutional standard to 39% with the surgeon-specific

such as average case-time duration by procedure and by surgeon, had the biggest feature

Tan et al.[13] introduced a two-step ML model to predict surgeon ranks and case du-

like how many procedures and surgical minutes have performed the last 7 days, surgeon’s
age and patient’s information like ICD(International Classification of Diseases) codes and
ASA(American Society of Anesthesiologists) scores.
Jiao et al.[15] proposed a model that integrates structured and unstructured data to esti-
mate the distribution of surgery durations. The data set consisted of 53783 surgical cases
performed over 4 years at a tertiary-care pediatric hospital. Features extracted included
categorical (ASA, Physical Status, inpatient status, day of week), continuous (scheduled
surgery duration, patient age), and unstructured text (procedure name, surgical diagno-
sis) variables. Their study demonstrated that a mixture density network outperformed
conventional statistical models in predictive accuracy.
Martinez et al.[3] compared four ML algorithms—Linear Regression, Support Vector Ma-
chines, Regression Trees, and Bagged Trees—to assess their predictive capabilities in
a hospital where scheduling was traditionally based on surgeon experience. Historical
data from 2004 until 2019 was used to train the algorithms.The comparison of algo-
rithms was conducted based on the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) of the predicted
surgery duration and their respective computational times. Variables that were used for
the predictive models were procedure ID, surgeon ID, surgeon experience, service and
patient information. The highest-performing algorithm was then evaluated against the
existing experience-based method currently used in practice.The study found that the
Bagged Trees algorithm significantly reduced scheduling errors, especially in specialties
with larger datasets.
Chu et al.[16] examined ML applications for predicting OR usage times to enhance schedul-
ing efficiency and minimize idle time. Their department-specific models, developed using
XGBoost, incorporated a limited set of variables to estimate surgery durations. The best-
performing model achieved a root-mean-square error (RMSE) of 31.6 minutes, a mean
absolute error (MAE) of 18.71 minutes, an R² value of 0.71, a mean absolute percentage
error (MAPE) of 28%, and a proportion of estimates within a 10% variation at 27%. These

3.5
Conclusion
The literature on OR scheduling and optimization underscores the complexity and finan-
cial burden of surgical services.
As hospitals transition to value-based care models,
optimizing OR scheduling through machine learning, predictive analytics, and real-time
scheduling algorithms will be crucial. By integrating these data-driven approaches, hos-
pitals can enhance scheduling accuracy, minimize inefficiencies, and improve overall sur-
gical service quality.

Data Analysis
4.1
Description of Data Set
This thesis project is entirely based on a dataset provided by Region Zealand, which
was delivered as an Excel (.xlsx) file containing 36,735 rows and 46 columns. Each
row represents a unique operation case, identified by a Case ID, with all relevant details
consolidated. The dataset covers operation cases conducted between January 1, 2019,
and November 1, 2023, at two hospitals in Zealand: Næstved and Slagelse. The dataset
contains four types of data:
• Integer: e.g. Case ID, Primary Surgeon ID, Previous Case ID etc.
• String: e.g. Room, Primary Procedure Name, Executing department name etc.
• Time: e.g. Patient in Facility, Room Ready, Procedure Start etc.
4.2
Data Quality
4.2.1
Methodology
Achieving high data quality is a pivotal foundation for AI driven research and analytics,
especially in areas where decisions are made through data that is accurate, relevant, and
comprehensive. Inadequate data quality can result in poor conclusions, and efficiency as
well as an impact on predictive value. Therefore, robust and proper quality measure and
data cleaning processes need to be employed in order to enhance the trustworthiness,
uniformity, and utility of the dataset [18].
This section tackles different types of data quality problems found in the data set and
the steps taken to process them. The subsequent sections will describe the following:
• Completeness: Measure of the percentage of data that is missing from the dataset.
• Relevance: Screening for irrelevant attributes and disposing of them.
• Integrity: Filtering out more or less unreasonable and incorrect information.
• Uniqueness: Suspend all duplicated case IDs to keep the proper case ID order.
• Validity: Checking meta temporal relations ordering to ensure logical order is main-
tained.
4.2.2
Completeness and Relevance
To assess the completeness of the data set, the first step is to calculate the missing values
for every column/variable. Handling missing values is a crucial step in data preprocessing,
as missing data can introduce bias and affect model performance. If a variable has a
high percentage of missing values, the importance or relevance of the variable must be

Conclusion
The analysis of the surgical dataset from Region Zealand provided key insights into data
quality, intraoperative processes, service types, and scheduling inefficiencies. The dataset,
comprising 36,735 surgical cases, was thoroughly examined to ensure completeness, rel-
evance, integrity, and validity, with missing and irrelevant data systematically handled to
improve its reliability for predictive modeling.
Several patterns emerged from the analysis, highlighting the challenges associated with
operating room (OR) scheduling. Shorter procedures (e.g., Ophthalmic, Urology, and
Gynecology-Obstetrics) exhibited lower variability and more predictable durations, while
longer surgeries (e.g., Orthopedic and General Surgery) displayed greater variability,
making prediction more challenging. The identification of outliers in surgical durations,
particularly extreme values in pre-procedure and post-procedure times, suggested po-
tential inefficiencies in hospital workflows, impacting overall scheduling accuracy.
Temporal trends revealed that first surgeries of the day tend to have longer durations,
while delays accumulate as the day progresses, particularly for non-first cases. This
suggests that hospital operations become more unpredictable later in the day, likely due
to unexpected intraoperative complications, emergency cases, or accumulated schedul-

When preparing data for machine learning models, it is essential to transform both cat-

One-Hot Coding
One-hot encoding transforms categorical variables into a binary ma-
trix where each category is represented by a unique binary vector. Categorical variables
represent categories or labels rather than numerical values (e.g., ”Male”/”Female”, ”Or-
thopedic”/”Urology”). Since machine learning models require numerical inputs, categori-
cal features must be converted into a numerical representation. One-hot coding is used
because it avoids misinterpretation by machine learning models that might assume a nu-
merical relationship between category labels if they were assigned arbitrary numbers.

with larger values can dominate the learning process, leading to biased models.Standardization
(also called Z-score normalization) transforms numerical values so that they have Mean

5.1.3
Model Evaluation
For regression the models are evaluated with:
• Mean Absolute Error (MAE): Measures the average absolute differences between
predicted and actual values.
• Mean Squared Error (MSE): Computes the average squared differences, giving
more weight to larger errors.
• Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): The square root of MSE, providing an error
measure in the same units as the target variable.
• R-squared (R2) Score: Indicates how well the model explains the variance in the
data, with values closer to 1 being better.
For classification the models are evaluated with:
• Accuracy: Measures the proportion of correctly classified instances among all in-
stances.
• Precision: Indicates how many of the predicted positive cases are actually positive.
• Recall: Measures how well the model identifies all actual positive cases.
• F1-score: The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, balancing false positives
and false negatives.
• Confusion Matrix: The confusion matrix will be displayes for the best predictive
models.Displays correct and incorrect classifications across all categories.
5- Fold Cross-Validation
5-Fold Cross-Validation is a technique used to evaluate ma-
chine learning models by splitting the dataset into five equal parts (folds) and iteratively
training and testing the model on different subsets of the data. The 5- Fold Cross-Validation
technique has been used for the classification models that outperformed the other pre-
dictive models, specifically Random Forest and XGboost classification. That way it is
measured how well the model generalizes.

5.1.5
Feature Engineering
To maximize the dataset’s potential, new features will be derived from existing variables.
Some raw variables may contain redundant information or noise. Feature engineering
helps refine and extract only the most useful aspects of the data. Moreover, By deriv-
ing new features, we can compensate for missing information, making the dataset more
robust.
Intra-Operative Stages Duration Variables
Converting complex raw data into meaningful features (e.g., transforming timestamps into
durations) makes the dataset easier to analyze and interpret. Hence, timestamps related
to intraoperative stages will be converted into durations in minutes. For this transforma-
tion:
• Pre-Procedure Duration will be calculated as the time difference between ”Proce-
dure Start” and ”Room Setup Start”.
• Procedure Duration will be determined by the difference between ”Procedure Com-
plete” and ”Procedure Start”.
• Post-Procedure Duration will be obtained by subtracting ”Procedure Complete”
from ”Patient Out of Room”
Surgeon-Specific Variables
To enrich the dataset with surgeon-related workload information, two new variables have
been created:
• Surgeon_Total_Cases: This variable represents the total number of cases each
surgeon has performed throughout the dataset. It is computed by grouping the data
by the ”Primary Surgeon ID” and counting the occurrences of each surgeon.
• Surgeon_Average_Daily_Cases: This feature captures the average number of
cases performed per day by each surgeon. First, the number of cases per day
per surgeon is determined. Then, the mean daily case count is calculated for each
surgeon using a second grouping operation.
These new variables provide valuable insights into surgeon workload and scheduling pat-
terns, which could be beneficial for predictive modeling.
Room-Specific Variables
To further enrich the dataset with insights into operating room utilization, two new variables
have been created:
• Room_Total_Cases: This variable represents the total number of cases performed
in each operating room throughout the dataset. It is computed by grouping the data
by the ”Room” column and counting the occurrences for each room.
• Room_Average_Daily_Cases: This feature captures the average number of cases
performed daily in each operating room. First, the number of cases per day per room
is determined. Then, the mean daily case count is calculated for each room using
a second grouping operation.
These newly created variables provide a deeper understanding of operating room utiliza-
tion.

5.3
Conclusion
The evaluation of both regression and classification models for predicting surgical proce-
dure duration revealed several key findings. Regression models failed to achieve satisfac-
tory results, as indicated by low R² scores across all tested models. Among them, Random
Forest regression performed best, explaining 45% of the variability in procedure duration,
with an average error of approximately 15 minutes. XGBoost regression followed closely,
capturing around 42% of the patterns in the data. Despite these models identifying rele-
vant predictors, such as Primary Procedure ID, patient age, and room-related information,
their ability to generalize was limited, particularly due to high variability in procedure du-
rations. The LSTM model, while outperforming baseline linear regression, also struggled
to align predictions with actual durations. Given the shortcomings of regression models,
classification approaches were explored as an alternative method for predicting proce-
dure duration.
The classification models also faced challenges, with all predictors achieving an accu-
racy below 0.60. The baseline Logistic Regression model performed the worst (accuracy
= 0.44), while k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) slightly improved upon it (accuracy = 0.48).
As expected, Random Forest and XGBoost classifiers outperformed the rest, achieving
accuracy scores of 0.55 and 0.56, respectively. While these models showed a modest
improvement over random guessing, their performance was still limited. The classification
results showed that short-duration procedures (Class 0: 0-29 minutes and Class 1: 30-
59 minutes) were predicted with relatively high recall, while longer-duration procedures
(Class 2: 60-89 minutes and Class 3: 90-140 minutes) were significantly misclassified.
The recall for Class 3 was alarmingly low (0.14), indicating that the model rarely identified
long-duration surgeries correctly, leading to a strong bias toward underestimating proce-
dure duration.
One major factor contributing to these misclassifications is class imbalance. Since shorter
procedures (Class 0 and Class 1) were more frequent in the dataset, the models priori-
tized them over longer surgeries. Decision-tree-based algorithms like Random Forest and
XGBoost tend to optimize for overall accuracy, which inherently favors majority classes.
As a result, Class 2 and Class 3 were frequently misclassified into shorter categories,
leading to low recall for longer procedures. Additionally, overlapping feature distributions
between medium and long-duration surgeries (Class 1 and Class 2) further contributed to
misclassification errors, as the model struggled to differentiate between these procedure
lengths. Threshold-based misclassification also played a role, where small prediction er-
rors near class boundaries (e.g., predicting 59 minutes instead of 60) resulted in incorrect
classifications.
The analysis of feature importance revealed that service-related, room-related, and section-
related features were among the most influential across models. Orthopedic procedures
stood out, as section-related variables played a significant role in their predictions, whereas
in other services, these features were less relevant. Urology and Gynecology-Obstetrics
exhibited better classification accuracy, likely due to their more stable procedure durations
and a sufficient number of cases. On the other hand, Ophthalmic and General Surgery
procedures showed lower accuracy, either due to high variability in duration (as in General
Surgery) or an insufficient number of cases for training (as in Ophthalmic surgeries).

6
Discussion and Future Work
While this study demonstrates the potential of ML for surgical procedure duration predic-
tion, several areas require further exploration to enhance model performance and real-
world applicability. Future research should focus on improving model accuracy, handling
data limitations, and integrating real-time scheduling adjustments.
One key improvement would be addressing class imbalance, as longer procedures were
frequently underrepresented, leading to misclassification. Oversampling techniques like
SMOTE [19] or cost-sensitive learning methods could be explored [20]. Another approach
would be to collect data from additional hospitals to improve generalization, incorporat-
ing a broader range of long-duration surgeries and diverse procedure types. Additionally,
standardizing procedure classifications could enhance predictive models, increasing their
accuracy and applicability. These improvements would contribute to developing a more
robust and globally applicable model.
Another avenue for improvement is feature engineering. While the study identified service
type, room-related attributes, and surgeon-specific factors as key predictors, incorporat-
ing real-time intraoperative events, patient-specific factors such as medical history (e.g.,
hypertension, diabetes, or other chronic conditions), blood pressure, and other biologi-
cal metrics, along with historical surgeon performance, could further enhance prediction
accuracy. Additionally, examining the entire surgical team, rather than just the primary
surgeon, could provide valuable insights, as social dynamics and team interactions may
also influence procedure duration.Deep learning approaches, such as transformer-based
architectures, could be explored for capturing complex dependencies in surgical proce-
dures.
Integrating real-time hospital data and adaptive scheduling algorithms would further im-
prove OR efficiency. Current ML models rely on historical data, but dynamic scheduling
models that adjust in real time based on ongoing surgeries and emergency cases could
enhance predictive power. For instance, if a first case experiences a significant delay, the
ML model could assist in redistributing procedures to other rooms, adjusting scheduled
operation times, or reallocating staff to optimize workflow, enabling more procedures to
be completed in a day while minimizing patient delays. Reinforcement learning could be
leveraged to develop models that continuously learn and optimize OR scheduling based
on real-time feedback.
Finally, future research should prioritize generalization and clinical deployment. Based on
my experience in the medical software field, it is crucial to ensure that machine learning
(ML) models, often perceived as ”black boxes,” are explainable. Healthcare profession-
als must trust and understand the tools they use, as decisions in clinical settings carry
high stakes, potentially affecting patient safety and outcomes. Additionally, ML models
should be rigorously compared against existing scheduling practices, demonstrating su-
perior performance before adoption. Validation in real clinical environments is essential to
identify and mitigate potential risks before full-scale implementation. Testing across mul-
tiple hospitals with diverse datasets would further ensure robustness, adaptability, and
reliability in real-world medical applications.

A.1.2
Interquartile Range (IQR) Method for Outlier Detection
The Interquartile Range (IQR) Method is a statistical technique used to identify and re-
move outliers in a dataset. Outliers are values that significantly deviate from the majority
of the data points and can distort statistical analysis and machine learning models.
The interquartile range (IQR) measures the spread of the middle 50% of the data and
is defined as:
IQR = Q3 −Q1
(A.1)
where:
• Q1 (First Quartile) is the value below which 25% of the data falls.
• Q3 (Third Quartile) is the value below which 75% of the data falls.
A data point is considered an outlier if it falls outside the acceptable range, defined as:
Lower Bound = Q1 −1.5 × IQR
(A.2)
Upper Bound = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR
(A.3)
Any values outside this range are considered outliers and may be removed or further
analyzed.

A.2
Calculating Pearson’s Correlation Coefficient
Pearson’s correlation coefficient (r) quantifies the strength and direction of the linear re-
lationship between two continuous variables. It ranges from −1 to 1, where:
• r = 1 indicates a perfect positive correlation.
• r = −1 indicates a perfect negative correlation.
• r = 0 suggests no correlation.
A.2.1
Formula
Pearson’s correlation coefficient is computed as:
r =
P(xi −¯x)(yi −¯y)
pP(xi −¯x)2pP(yi −¯y)2
where:
• xi, yi are individual data points.
• ¯x, ¯y are the means of x and y.
• The numerator represents the covariance between x and y.
• The denominator normalizes the covariance by the standard deviations of x and
y.
A.2.2
Steps to Calculate Pearson’s Correlation
1. Compute the mean of both variables (¯x and ¯y).
2. Subtract the mean from each data point to obtain deviations from the mean.
3. Multiply corresponding deviations and sum the results (numerator).
4. Compute the standard deviations of both variables (denominator).
5. Divide the covariance by the product of the standard deviations to get r.

Random Forest
Random Forest is an ensemble learning method that combines multiple decision trees to
improve predictive performance and control over-fitting.
A decision tree is a predictive model that splits the data into subsets based on feature

Random Forest builds on the idea of ensemble learning by constructing a multitude of
decision trees and combining their predictions. The method known as bagging (bootstrap

• Reduces the correlation between individual trees.

Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are a special kind of Recurrent Neural Net-
work (RNN) designed to learn long-term dependencies in sequential data. They are widely
used in tasks such as natural language processing, time series forecasting, and speech
recognition.

Traditional RNNs suffer from the vanishing and exploding gradient problems, which make
it difficult for them to capture long-range dependencies. LSTMs overcome these limi-

• Forget Gate ft: Determines which information should be discarded from the cell

• Output Gate ot: Controls the output based on the cell state.

A.3.6
k-Nearest Neighbors
The k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN) algorithm is a simple, non-parametric method used for
both classification and regression tasks. Its simplicity, ease of implementation, and effec-
tiveness make it a popular baseline method in many machine learning applications.
Given a query instance, the kNN algorithm follows these steps:
1. Distance Calculation: Compute the distance between the query instance and all
training examples using a suitable distance metric (commonly the Euclidean dis-
tance).
2. Neighbor Identification: Identify the k nearest neighbors (i.e., the k training in-
stances with the smallest distance to the query).
3. Prediction:
• Classification: Assign the class that is most frequent among the k nearest
neighbors (majority voting).
• Regression: Compute the prediction as the average (or weighted average) of
the target values of the k nearest neighbors.
The Euclidean distance is a common choice for measuring similarity. For two points x =
(x1, x2, . . . , xp) and y = (y1, y2, . . . , yp), it is defined as:

The value of k is crucial:
• A small k may lead to high variance and sensitivity to noise (overfitting).
• A large k can smooth out the decision boundaries too much (underfitting).
The k-Nearest Neighbors algorithm is a robust and intuitive method for both classification
and regression. Its performance is largely influenced by the choice of k, the distance
metric used, and the scale of the features. Despite its simplicity, kNN is a valuable tool in
many machine learning pipelines.
