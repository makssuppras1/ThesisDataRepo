Abstract
This project will describe the design of an 1 MHz Active Clamp ﬂyback converter. The converter is designed to
be an oﬀ-line 10 W power stage, with open-loop control. The ZVS capabilities of the active clamp topology can
negate high frequency switching losses by adding leakage inductance to the circuit. It will utilize the emerging
Gallium Nitride FET’s for both main and clamp switching, in order to reduce switching losses further. While
size is not a design constraint small magnetics are desirable for the application. The transformer will be
implemented as an integrated PCB transformer, with advanced high frequency core material.
Both CCM and DCM operation will be analysed, as design procedures for choosing clamping capacitances are
described. Experiments with the assembled power stage, show eﬃciencies consistently at about 76 % at 230
Volt. Major loss components were identiﬁed to be the output diode and transformer core. The results show
that the solution to the core loss issue, is by adding more leakage inductance which will eliminate the high
ﬂux density and retain ZVS capabilities.

Chapter 1
Introduction
For home electronics, in the size of cell phones and tablets, the power supply level will range from 5-10 Watt.
The power converters used for these applications are connected to the grid through a wall plug and will gener-
ate voltages in the range of 5-12 Volt. Predominately these power supplies are built on the Flyback topology.
The ﬂyback topology is desirable for manufacturers because it has a small part number, and hence is cheap
to produce. At the moment they run at a low frequency, because it is the most economic solution. A low
frequency directly results in large inductors, but their eﬃciency ratings are able to accommodate the eﬃciency
regulations which are required for distributing.
The large inductors are expensive, but ramping up in frequency will increase losses in the switching compo-
nents many fold. However with advances in electronic components and by using more advanced topologies the
frequency can be increased, the inductor can be reduced in size and the manufacturers will be able to produce
cheaper power supplies. This all come at the criteria that the eﬃciency stays above approximately 76 % at 10
Watts, 12 Volt output for the European market [14].
In this project a single digit megahertz-frequency power stage, will be designed and implemented. The output
is constant set at 12 Volt and 10 Watt.
For being able to uphold eﬃciency at higher frequencies a soft-
switching scheme, will be employed in the form of an active clamp ﬂyback. Soft-switching will negate the
extensive switching losses, which appear when increasing the switching frequency. Achieving soft-switching
is not just about choosing the right topology. It will be attained more easily if the electrical switch itself is
optimized for it. Gallium Nitride (GaN) FET’s are an emerging technology looking to take over the position,
which the MOSFET’s have held for decades. GaNFET’s have very good electrical characteristics for high
frequency switching, and will be used in the implementation. PCB integrated magnetic components, is one
way to decrease the cost of manufacturing of the transformer. Instead of a wire wound around a core and
assembled, the windings are etched into the PCB, and the core is clicked on. This simpliﬁes the production
greatly. However, applying high frequency magnetic ﬁelds can be problematic. Ferromagnetic materials will
yield diﬀerent results, depending on the material composition. The many advances being made in this depart-
ment will be utilized too. Having eﬃcient high frequency core material, will be integral for eﬃciency of a high
frequency converter.
The actual operation of the converter, will be analysed in this thesis. There are many variables in an active
clamp ﬂyback, and many of these aﬀect the eﬃciency. After the converter power stage have been implemented,
the Zero Voltage Switching needs to be timed correctly for highest eﬃciency. Both DCM and CCM operation
will be examined, as the clamp capacitance can decide the operation mode. Frequency dependant losses will
be pointed out. The full input voltage range will be tested to validate operation around the world. An analysis
of the loss origins is presented to pave the road for the next low power active clamp ﬂyback development.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
In order for making this converter possible, previous advancements of the topology will be exhibited and
discussed here. The active clamp ﬂyback converter goes back a long time and one of the most detailed ex-
planations is [3] originating from 1994. In this, a 500 Watt 100 kHz active clamp CCM ﬂyback has been
implemented to gain a higher eﬃciency than an RCD-clamp which is still the most common clamp type.
Ultimately they gain approximately 4 percentage-points from shifting from RCD-clamp to active-clamp.
Article [4] describes a design of a low power DCM active clamp topology from 1992. Here, a 30 Watt 500 kHz
converter is implemented. K. Yoshida gives a direct comparison between CCM and DCM operation with the
change of a clamp capacitance. The focus is on the lower noise generated in DCM both on the input and the
output. It results in between 70 and 90 % of power eﬃciency.
Article [3] and article [4] present equations, diagrams and waveforms to develop the active clamp ﬂyback
topology. This is the knowledge foundation of the topology and the two articles are still cited by high fre-
quency ﬂyback articles today. But with older components and techniques their results will not be discussed
further.
In more recent years article [5] from 2016 presents a 65 W CCM active clamp ﬂyback converter. The ap-
proach to PCB transformer development is insightful, with magnetic-physics simulations to support their
design. In their winding ratio analysis both the secondary and primary RMS currents are high. At a ﬁxed
output voltage and power the RMS current should be constant. The actual circuitry is missing which would
make it diﬃcult to reproduce. The core material, size and windings are stated so this part can be replicated
and tested if needed. It seems that they use Cascode type of GaNFET (from Transphorm) for their main
switch. X. Huang mention the issue of having to add leakage inductance, but never elaborates on the value,
type or implementation of this.
Half of the article describes how EMI is aﬀected by the topology which
is necessary in their power level. It is clear that shielding between primary and secondary windings in the
transformer yields much less EMI for their converter. This information may not be that useful for a 10 Watt
application.
Article [8] does not use the active clamp circuit but has insights about PCB transformer technology. Their
design is thoroughly tested and all parameters are listed. They generate a surprisingly low inductance which
will generate high currents. However their converter touches 5 MHz operation in some cases, which explains
the low inductance. The experimental analysis of transformer eﬃciency is described as a sinusoidal power
input and a pure resistance on the output. This results in complete ﬂux cancellation, thus a minimal amount
of ﬂux build-up in the core. In ﬂyback operation the build-up of core ﬂux density and frequency are primary
core loss sources. Therefore the transformer eﬃciency analysis seems at fault. They show an eﬃciency table
where both input voltage and output power is changed at the same time. This makes it challenging to anal-
yse the eﬃciency ﬁgure. Since one of the control modes is based on frequency changes, it would have been
informative to show how eﬃciency behaves with frequency. Eﬃciency ﬁgures for 10 Watts are just around 85
%. Their boundary mode operation allows complete ZVS but the heavy ringing may prove detrimental to EMI.
Article [9] uses a slight variation of the active clamp ﬂyback, where the active clamp is connected across

3.1.1
Flyback Converter
The topology of the ﬂyback converter is presented in ﬁgure 3.1. It consists of a main switch, a diode and a
ﬂyback transformer. The ﬂyback transformer itself, can also be referred to as a coupled inductor. Its anatomy
is the same as a conventional transformer, but the name implies how it is used. In the conventional use of a
transformer, a primary current runs in the primary winding, and at the same time a secondary current runs
in the secondary winding. This is called ﬂux cancellation, as the energy/magnetic ﬂux is transferred instantly
between the windings. In a ﬂyback transformer/coupled inductor, it works as two diﬀerent inductors with a
common core. In one period the primary winding can charge the core, and in the next the secondary winding
can discharge the core.

For CCM the ﬂyback converter works in cycles of 2 periods. First the switch is on in ﬁgure 3.2a, drawing

to supply the load during the entire ON-cycle. Lk represents the leakage inductance, which is an unavoidable
parasitic element to transformers. When the switch turns oﬀ, the current in the leakage inductor is cut oﬀ.
This results in voltage spikes or ”ringing”, which will be elaborated later.

3.1.3
Snubbers and Voltage Clamps
In ﬁgure 3.2b the leakage inductance Lk is marked as ”ringing”. The leakage inductance is a parasitic element
in the transformer. It is thought as magnetic ﬂux, which does not manifest itself in the magnetic core and
hence, does not reach the secondary winding. When Qmn shuts oﬀthe leakage current has nowhere to go, so
it resonates with the parasitic output capacitance, Coss of Qmn. This creates a resonant ringing across Qmn,
which can have an amplitude that is damaging to the switch. Therefore a RCD snubber/clamp is usually
applied to a ﬂyback converter. It stands for ”Resistor, Capacitor and Diode”. The capacitor is the important
component for actually handling the leakage current, as it catches and stores it.
This is also referred to
as ”clamping” the voltage, across the inductor. The diode stops the capacitor current from reversing, and
resonating back into the inductor. During the on-period, the capacitor will discharge into the resistor and be
ready for the next oﬀ-period.
Figure 3.6: The workings of a RCD snubber in the ﬂyback oﬀ-period.
The RCD is an entirely passive circuit, which decreases complexity and it solves the problem of ringing.
The downside is, that all the energy from the leakage inductor is dissipated, and is an overall decrement to
eﬃciency. The power dissipated is all the energy in the leakage inductor, times the frequency:

3.2
Speciﬁc Technologies
Going into MHz frequencies in power electronics require some of the newest technologies. In this section the
implications and advantages of using GaNFET will be presented. The GaNFET allows for faster switching,
but the solid magnetic cores can be a limiting factor in eﬃciency when switching at a high frequency.
3.2.1
GaNFET Technology
GaNFET is looking to be the successor of MOSFET’s in the coming years [8] [5]. The enhanced-GaNFET’s
are made to resemble the MOSFET much in how they are operated. A problem with the ﬁrst GaNFET’s was
that they were depletion-mode devices. This meant that negative gate-source voltage had to be applied for
turn-oﬀ. For small, cheap circuits it is simply impractical. Cascode devices were developed to solve this issue.
It is a GaNFET and a MOSFET which are connected in series. But the cascodes gate is on the MOSFET
gate which then controls the depletion-mode GaNFET. The switching speed depends positively on the load
current because it is self commutating. This feature is not desirable in low power solution. The e-GaN will
work with many MOSFET gate driving circuits which makes GaNFET transition into mainstream easier.
MOSFET’s have intrinsic diodes which let current ﬂow reverse if subjected to a negative drain-source voltage,
regardless of gate-source voltage. Due to high reverse recovery energies these diodes are ineﬃcient at high
switching speeds. Conducting in reverse is an important feature in the active clamp topology. An e-GaNFET
does not have an intrinsic diode but can naturally conduct in reverse bias. In ﬁgure 3.12b the reverse conduc-
tion characteristics for the GaNFET used later is shown. If used passively, with no gate-source voltage, the
eﬃciency is worse than if used actively with gate-charge. With the active clamp switch conducting in reverse
every switch cycle, this could become beneﬁcial to eﬃciency.
GaNFET’s generally have smaller input and output capacitances. The smaller input capacitance (Ciss in
ﬁgure 3.12a) directly means that the ON/OFF transition happens much faster. A smaller transition period
allows the designer to ramp up the switching frequency. The output capacitance, Coss, is important when
trying to achieve ZVS. This value is highly non-linear towards the lower voltages, which will be traversed in
a ZVS event. Therefore the eﬀective Coss for soft-switching can only be estimated.

3.2.2
Gate Driving
One of the challenges with GaN devices is the gate driving at high frequencies [1]. Their optimal working
gate-source voltage is very close to their absolute maximum rating. At high frequencies ringing is much more
common because wire/trace impedance is frequency dependant. But with little headroom above the optimal
gate-source voltage very little ringing is tolerable.
Gate ringing is very associated with PCB layout strategies. The distance from the gate driver to gate has to
be minimized and is of high priority. In ﬁgure 3.13a and 3.13b LOF F and LON are intrinsic inductances in
traces and the series components. They oﬀer nothing good as they slow the ramp of current going to and from
the FET. ROF F and RON however limits the maximum current and will damp the system. As such they are
components which can be chosen freely.
Turning ON the device requires current coming from the decoupling capacitor. Therefore LON will always be
larger than LOF F . A higher inductance requires more damping, so RON needs to be larger than ROF F . The
resistors can be be calculated if the inductances are known, but they are diﬃcult to measure so the speciﬁc
resistances are chosen from rule of thumb. RON = 10 −20Ωand ROF F = 1 −2Ω[13].

3.2.4
Planar Magnetics
Planar magnetics describes all low proﬁle magnetics but is often mentioned in connection with PCB magnet-
ics. Instead of using wound copper wire around a core, PCB traces in several layers are used. Inductors and
transformers are a key component in most power converters. It is also a big, heavy and expensive component
compared to most semiconductors and capacitors. These reasons are the most prevalent for increasing switch-
ing frequency.
For high frequency applications all parasitic components are crucial for the operation. In resonant converters it
is not necessarily about reducing them, but knowing the ratio of inductance to capacitance that is of concern.
Hand or machine wire-wound magnetics are hard to replicate, resulting in high manufacturing tolerances.
The inductance, winding ratio, and the leakage inductance will diﬀer from component to component. The
PCB magnetics are etched and cut to much higher precision. The component is not necessarily more ideal in
relation to theoretical values, but the variation between each component is greatly reduced. A designer can
tune a converter to the magnetics, and the manufactured products will operate much more like intended.
For the designer PCB magnetics oﬀer more freedom than buying shelf components. Winding ratio and in-
ductances are printed to speciﬁcations. Materials is the greatest beneﬁt since high frequency power materials
often only are sold for handmade magnetics. When all that is said, designing magnetics is no easy feat. It
is an entire ﬁeld of study in itself. It will be designed from basic magnetic theory and experiences of other
researchers.

The PCB layout in high frequency applications is very important. Any length of trace has some parasitic
inductance, and the impedance is a direct multiple of frequency. Therefore all important power loop connec-

The length optimization is one thing, but the PCB is the workbench for the future lab testing. It should be

PCB. The PCB implementation was made in Altium Designer. For the PCB spiral windings an open-source

Practicalities
The PCB is designed to use ”Banana plugs” which is the standard power cable connection used in the lab.
The sturdy screw/nut banana type is used instead of the smaller solder-on type, at the expense of board area.
To carry signal from the signal generators BNC-inputs, to the board, shielded cables are needed.
SMA
connectors are a good deal smaller than BNC, so SMA connectors are ﬁtted to the PCB.
The input voltage and switch node voltage are potentially dangerous to humans so it was decided to use BNC
connector for the important switch node waveform. BNC is compatible with normal oscilloscope probes so it
makes a robust and safe test point. The high frequency probe (HP 10430A) has a smaller head size so the
test point BNC was ultimately rendered useless.
The prevalent test point-type is the pig-tail/curl. A via for the probe head and a ground via next to each other.
In the ground via, a spiral wire can connect to the probeheads ground. This makes a minimum impedance
loop for the oscilloscope measurement, which generates less noise.

3.5.3
Modiﬁcations of Layout
After the PCB was produced, the transformer section had to be cut out with a dremel power tool. This was
done, to be able to connect the series external leakage inductor. Relatively long wires connect this cut-out
piece. On the primary winding, the added parasitic inductance is negligible, because much extra leakage
inductance is already added. On the secondary winding, the positive wire is soldered directly to the diode,
which will give the diode better thermal dissipation than if soldered to the board. This also means that
the shield, which was included between the primary and secondary winding, was unconnected throughout all
experiments.

3.8.3
Frequency
Because of the low inductance generated after installing air gaps, the AC currents are consequently quite high.
Lowering this current, will lower the ﬂux density of the core which generate losses. But lowering the current
will lower the energy in the leakage inductor. This will hamper ZVS capabilities. The output diode might be
aﬀected negatively, by the increased frequency. The frequency will be raised by 100 kHz at a time. Thermal
images can help clarify how much the losses are moved from the core to the main switch.

Discussion
During understanding, designing, implementing and testing, obstacles were encountered. Since the converter
includes GaNFET, PCB transformer, quasi-resonant operation and works at high frequency many decisions
were taken on diﬃcult premises. The most integral task was to achieve ZVS at such a low power level with a
high input voltage - while not having excessive losses in the transformer.
In this space the results will be discussed, as they present the actual performance, of the implementation
and design. Hereafter the method of designing and implementing the Active clamp ﬂyback converter will be
evaluated. In prospect, future experiments and converter circuits will be discussed. If a new Active clamp
ﬂyback converter were to be designed, the lessons from this design should be utilized.
5.1
Implication of Results
The theory used is good for approximating the system. As soon as parasitics and losses are included, the
duty cycles and ZVS times have to be revised. This is seen in the simulation, where the ZVS dead time
was calculated to 16.7 ns but had to be increased to 100 ns before the quarter resonant was visible in the
simulation. As the total leakage inductance is known, the fault lays at the output capacitance of the main
switch. It was anticipated that the eﬀective capacitance Coss,eff in a ZVS event would be higher than the
rated Coss = 17pF@400V. A more precise value in the data sheet [16] shows Coss,eff = 35pF from 0 to 400
V. If the quarter resonant is 143 ns and the leakage inductance is known, the Coss,eff can be calculated as

But this output capacitance is very unlikely. The absolute maximum that the data sheet shows is 150 nF at
0 Volt. It is more likely that another inductance or capacitance is added in the resonance. The resonance goes
through the transformer, coupling it to the secondary side. Some quick simulations show that the resistive
output load can change the ZVS resonance period. But at this time the reason for this is remains speculative.
The calculated main duty cycle in DCM was accurate. It was only adjusted from 10.5 % to 12 % to obtain 12

The simulation gives a good approximation of the real system operation. The voltage waveforms are much
the same in the lab-experiments as in the simulations. It would be interesting to examine if the in the lab-test
and simulation currents are just as similar.
ZVS Deadtime
The criterion of ZVS is that the leakage inductance should carry more energy than the output capacitor.
In the simulation ZVS was easily achieved in the 100 ns. In the lab the best case ZVS was with the high
clamp capacitances (ﬁgure 4.5) which dropped from 280 V to 40 V. Theoretically the base-case circuit should
need a total leakage inductance of about 3 µH. With a factor two margin, 6.6 µH total was used in the lab
experiments. Since this L-C resonance is coupled through the transformer, it eﬀectively becomes a damped
L-C-R resonance which will reduce the amplitude and energy of the resonance. Another cause to the lack of
ZVS is that the transformer winding itself is a resistive component. The ZVS current is coupled to the load,

and the transformer/windings can be quite resistive in themselves. The amount of ZVS dead time aﬀected the
output voltage quite much. By changing the dead time by ≈30 ns the main duty cycle had to be adjusted by

The ZVS dead time did not aﬀect eﬃciency by much, but it should be tuned to a speciﬁc system for optimizing

DCM and CCM modes
The diﬀerence between DCM and CCM, played a big role in the design and implementation of this converter.
The term ”critical capacitance” was coined in this work, as the critical value between DCM and CCM. When
it is calculated, it is very easy to implement and change any active clamp ﬂyback between DCM and CCM. In
these experiments it was clear, that DCM operation simply had better eﬃciency than CCM. The output diode
did get warmer, with the high clamp capacitance, which suggests it was aﬀected by increased reverse recovery
losses. But the change in temperature was too small, to make this claim with certainty. It is interesting, that
the higher clamp capacitance was able to achieve better ZVS capabilities - the best of the experiment set. The
reason for this can be observed in the simulations. In the DCM simulations, it is evident that the primary
current resonance, has to decrease for some time to obtain DCM current on the secondary side. In CCM the
resonant current never begins to decrease, because of the long resonance time. Thus keeping a higher current
in the leakage inductance and more ZVS potential.
Because the current could not be measured in the lab test DCM operation is not easily proved.
In the
simulations it was hypothesised that CCM or DCM operation could be observed through the secondary wind-
ing voltage. Figure 4.7a suggests DCM operation because the voltage drops before the clamp signal goes OFF.
From the simulations the small fast resonance was observed, when the voltage reaches the bottom. This is the
diode turning OFF. With a higher clamp capacitance, the voltage drops at the exact signal of the clamp going
OFF. The voltage drops, and goes into a heavy 10 MHz resonance, with an amplitude of around 25 volt. This
is because of CCM operation, since the resonance frequency does not change between the 50 nF and 230 nF
case. The inter-winding capacitance, can be a component with this resonance. The ringing will be reﬂected
to the primary winding, and thus the primary current, making 10 MHz ripples. It is unknown if this has an
eﬀect on eﬃciency. It is certain that the output diode needs a blocking voltage of more than 60 volt, when the
highest input voltage is applied. One way to analyse this resonance further, is by improving the simulation.
In this work the simulation output was grounded to the input ground. A more realistic representation is to
connect both the top and the bottom with the respective inter-winding capacitance. This allows the currents
to circulate between the primary and the secondary, and could yield insight into limiting the resonance.
There were indications of DCM operation with a clamp capacitance below the analytical Ccrit,clp.
DCM
operation showed a higher eﬃciency than CCM, presumably because of the soft-switched output diode.
Frequency
As the core was getting warm, possibly due to high ﬂux density, a decrease in current was ideally needed.
This could be done by increasing the inductance to have a higher impedance, or increasing the frequency. The
problem with decreasing the current, would be the decrease of ZVS potential, and simply more switches to
create losses. This was exactly what happened, as the eﬃciency dropped 4 percentage points linearly towards
1.2 MHz. The lower frequency of 900 kHz was also tried, but this also resulted in lower eﬃciency. Before 800
kHz is tried, it cannot be stated for sure, but about 1 MHz seems to be the optimum frequency to operate
the converter at. It is diﬃcult to see exactly how the lower ﬂux density, but higher frequency aﬀects the core.
Since the temperature scale is moved 5◦it would seem that the core was getting warmer. Most interesting
is how the diode gets about 4.5◦hotter, which shows it is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by frequency in general. The
main switch has 20 % more switching losses and with lower pZVS. The heat-outline of the main switch, is
indeed visible on the thermal image of 1.2 MHz.
Increasing the frequency did not help eﬃciency, because the switching losses increased more, than decreasing
the peak current helped core losses.

Input Voltage
The input voltage waveforms showed that with lower voltage more ZVS could be achieved. Since the input
current of the converter have to be higher to achieve the same power output, the energy in the leakage inductor
is higher. The lower voltage on the main switch also results in less energy stored in Coss. Even at that, none
of the cases achieved full ZVS. At 90 volt the ZVS curve drops fast, but it bumps out towards the end. The
reason for exact behaviour has not been concluded. 110 V input voltage yield the highest eﬃciency. For low
input voltages, the current has to be relatively higher, as the input power have to remain the same. A higher
duty cycle is employed to achieve 12 volt output. This eﬀectively means that Ipk, and thus Bpk is higher
for the low input voltages. With approximately double current of the base-case, the thermal images show
signiﬁcantly less temperature in the core. This can only mean that core loss is more associated to the slope of
the ﬂux density than the peak value.

Core loss
From the E18 core datasheet [19] the maximum core loss was cited to be 0.33 watt at 50 mT, 1 MHz, 100◦C
with no air gap. It does not mention, what kind of core excitation is used for this power loss, or if the tem-
perature comes from excitation. If such a temperature was reached with 0.33 W, then the measured result of
0.267 W at 80◦C is a good estimate. In the core loss experiment the ﬂux density was about 150 mT, but the
active power core loss only came in at 0.267 W which is low. However this experiment was made with a core
which had an air gap which changes the saturation curve. This could potentially alter the amount of core loss
from what the data sheet states.
In the thermal image the core is heated more in one side than the other. This may be because of a fault
in making the air gap. If one of the side legs, have a larger air gap than the other, it will be a larger reluc-
tance. And like current, the ﬂux chooses the path of least reluctance. Eﬀectively only one loop of the core is
then used, and the measurement should be redone. In this type of core loss measurement, the current is the
hardest to measure. With a current probe, there will be signiﬁcant phase shift in the current measurement
[10]. This shift will alter the measured active power.
This core loss experiment is considered to be faulty. The technique is well proven but some parts of the
measurement, such as phase shift and the unbalanced air gap, are not understood well enough at this point
to give the result merit.
Loss analysis
The thermal images are good to get a feeling of what is going on, but it all turns to speculation because of
the components thermal characteristics. In the base-case with 76 % eﬃciency the exact input power is 12.949
W, and the output is 9.84 W. So a total of 3.1 W is dissipated in the system. The four major bodies of loss
is the output diode, the transformer, the main switch and the clamp switch. Since the transformer core loss
measurement came out to be 0.267 W, the total analytical amount came to be
Ptotal+core = 1.862 + 0.267 + 2.129W
(5.2)
That means approximately 1 W is unaccounted for. The two consistently hottest objects are the diode and
the transformer, so it is most likely that the 1 Watt lies in one of those two components. The diode is well
documented for conduction losses, but not in terms of frequency losses. The diode and transformer core are
resting at approximately the same temperature. But the core has a much larger surface area, resulting in a
smaller thermal resistance . The transformer also bears many more unknowns than the diode. The core loss
measurement, is not entirely credible at its current state. Logic in terms of the data sheet value, points toward
the core loss being a good deal higher than 0.33 W.

It could also be conduction losses in the windings. As mentioned, the AC-resistance for 1 MHz was used for
a higher frequency resonance. However it was also assumed that the peak of the resonance was constant, but
when transferring power to the load, the resonance is damped signiﬁcantly. Therefore the current estimate is
high, but the resistance estimate is low. Thermal images show that the windings are nowhere critically hot.
It is unlikely that they could transfer power from the outer rings, and into the core without it being visible.
Therefore the winding loss estimate stands. The last watt is proposed to come from core losses which would
lay the core losses at around 1.25 W.
When having to compare to the articles mentioned, there are parameters that make the results challeng-
ing to compare. This project uses high input voltage and very low power. In relation to the work done in
[9] core loss was not mentioned as a main loss contributor. Their input voltage was 18 Volt and they have
a similar input inductance to this work. Because of the large input voltage diﬀerence makes it diﬃcult to
compare the work in the article to the converter presented in this project. AC-Conduction loss was measured
to be a huge contributor. At 10 Watts their eﬃciency was between between 75-80 %, which is comparable to
this project. But they predominately faced AC-conduction losses where this project faced core losses. They
examine three diﬀerent dead times. For 10 Watt output their lowest eﬃciency was with 50 ns dead time and
their best with 20 ns, but these numbers are very dependant on the exact ZVS resonance.
Article [8] uses CRM ﬂyback topologies instead, but their power level is similar to this work, but also uses
low input voltage. Their 10 Watts eﬃciency lays between 82-86 %. They do not show exactly how their
converter behaves with input voltage or frequency directly. Their choice of diode must account for a lower
power dissipation since they have a quite high eﬃciency. The diode is also larger and easier to keep cool. It
could be tried for the converter in this project.
Article [5] implements a much higher power (65 Watt) AcC ﬂyback. This power level is signiﬁcantly higher
which would make ZVS easier because of the higher energy levels in the leakage inductance. Their converter
works in the same input voltage range, but their eﬃciency is almost constant across the entire range.
5.2
Retrospect
There is quite a discrepancy in the analytical RMS current against the peak currents. This is because the
theoretical DCM duty cycle calculation may be applied wrong. The equation used does not account for turns-
ratio. However, simulations and experiments proved the calculated DCM duty cycle close to the needed duty
cycle for achieving 12 volt output. The most descriptive article about the AcC ﬂyback in DCM is [4].
The critical inductance value which was calculated early on is not a reason for CCM or DCM is the active
clamp converter. The period of resonant currents alone decide if the output diode will experience DCM.
The most critical part to design was the transformer, which also ended up being a major part of losses. It
was identiﬁed early on that the ﬂux density in the core would be quite high. This could only be solved by
increasing the inductance or increasing the core cross section area. At the same time the main goal of HF
converters is to make them smaller, so the inductor should be limited in size. The critical inductance was
used as a guide to how much inductance was normal in a DCM converter. When a core size is chosen as much
inductance as possible should used. This though, increases the amount of windings which increases the AC
losses. In retrospect, since the winding losses were relatively small more windings could have been applied.
But it is a balance between core losses and AC-winding losses.
The primary concern was getting enough inductance after an air gap was employed. The air gap seemed
necessary, to decrease the ﬂux density, but the air gap was critical to lower the inductance and allow enough
current in the leakage inductance for ZVS. The external leakage inductance played an important role. If it
was not there the switching losses would have approached 1.9 W alone. The external inductor used, was
unknown but evidently made for high power and high frequency. Since it did not show any heat signature,
it was deemed a low source of losses in spite of its big surface area. Finding an external inductor with low
HF losses is not trivial therefore only one size was used. In the next section the role of the external leakage
inductor will be elaborated further.
The choice of windings ratio and secondary windings was not overly thought through. The analysis showed

that the windings ratio had little impact overall. At the same time the possible AC-resistance had to be taken
into consideration, so the total amount of windings should be limited. During this analysis the choices of [5]

and decisions had to be made to move forward.
The clamp switch did not need to be the GS66502B. This was an over design in terms of blocking voltage
voltage. The decision was made on premature simulations and the continuous mantra of excessive disturbances
at high frequency. In reality the eﬀects of the high frequency was not noticeable in the drain-source waveform
at all. A lower voltage rating GaNFET as clamp switch could optimize the design.
All measurements were done on the same experimental PCB by changing the parts and inputs. It is not
impossible that some parts have degraded during the experimental sessions. Ideally several PCB’s with same
and diﬀerent circuit details should be used. These could be able to assess degradation and diﬀerences due to
tolerances.
5.3
Prospect
Since the problem with losses has been hypothesized to be the steep slope of the ﬂux density, the natural next
step is to increase the inductance. However this will limit the peak current and energy in the leakage inductor
which is needed to achieve ZVS. The solution is to only increase the leakage inductance. This way the slope of
the ﬂux density would be lower and the energy in the inductor can be enough to achieve ZVS. A problem to this
idea is that much more of the power has to be delivered through the Lk-Cclp resonance. This circulation would
increase winding AC losses and Qclp reverse conduction losses. In the clamp capacitor experiment this was
analysed to some degree, by increasing the Cclp to 230 nF. It did not increase losses dramatically, but it will be
diﬀerent if the leakage inductor is large since the energy to the resonance originally comes from this component.
It will not be easy to increase the leakage inductance elegantly. One solution could be to include an ex-
tra PCB magnetic component of similar material as the transformer. Maybe a diﬀerent core size can be used
if Bpk is not as critical as it seemed. As long as enough inductance can be created, without introducing much
increased AC-winding losses. With such an inductor all 4 layers can be used to decrease the AC-resistance
signiﬁcantly. However the main goal is still to decrease the size of the converters. An extra PCB magnetic
component will take up a lot of board area. An alternative which might work is to design a transformer with
a high leakage inductance. There are many publications and guides which teach how to minimize leakage
inductance. If these guidelines can be reversed and taken to the extreme it might be possible to at least
limit the size of the external component. The addition of leakage inductance would decrease the slope of ﬂux
while still attaining ZVS capabilities. Full ZVS capabilities is not a must as even partial ZVS can drive the
losses signiﬁcantly down. For the development of this system it is advised to have some current measurement
equipment to assess the currents directly.
The output diode is detrimental to eﬃciency.
Even with the better power characteristics obtained when
going with a lower blocking voltage, it will never be really eﬃcient. The solution which is also used in article
[5] is to have a synchronous rectiﬁer. The synchronous rectiﬁer can be implemented with a switch of choice,
so a GaNFET here also would be ideal.
The synchronous rectiﬁer can increase complexity which is why it was not included in this design. The switch
should only open when the voltage on the secondary winding is more than the output capacitor. Else the
output capacitor will feed back into the winding. The most elegant solution is to have a circuit close to the
switch which measures the voltage across the switch. When it is positive the switch goes ON and vice versa.
In this way the switch is not directly controlled by the main control circuit and no problems with feeding a
signal through the galvanic isolation will need to be solved. The synchronous rectiﬁer would greatly improve
the eﬃciency and it is a proven solution so it should be implemented in a new design.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
An operational power stage for the active clamp ﬂyback topology have been developed. It was designed for
maximum eﬃciency in a relatively small footprint. To achieve the highest possible eﬃciency some of the best
components on the marked had to be used. The new and emerging GaNFET’s were utilized for primary and
clamp switching. Even with soft-switching techniques employed, switching at high frequency generates losses.
With GaNFET’s this is minimized as much as possible. A PCB ﬂyback transformer was designed and im-
plemented. The proper design of this component showed to be essential as losses were experimentally assessed.
Diﬀerent aspects of the implemented converter were examined. The exact ZVS deadtime did not yield much
in terms of eﬃciency, but the optimization comes at no cost. There was an optimal deadtime which can only
be found experimentally because of non-linearities in the components used. The comparison between diﬀer-
ent clamp capacitances showed that the clamp capacitance can be changed to decide if the converter should
operate in CCM or DCM. These results showed the most interesting waveforms and DCM operation claimed
the highest full-speciﬁcation eﬃciency of 76 %. By changing the switching frequency the peak currents can be
decreased. This was interesting in terms of decreasing the core losses but proved to no avail. The switching
losses were increasing more in any case.
The American standard input voltage of 110 V showed signiﬁcantly less core losses than at 230 V. This means
that the core losses were more coupled to the rate of change in ﬂux density than the saturation current,
which was theoretically far surpassed. The proposed solution to this problem, is to increase the leakage in-
ductance. This will decrease the slope of the ﬂux density by adding inductance while attaining the essential
ZVS capabilities in the circuit.
