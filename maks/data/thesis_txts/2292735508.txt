Abstract 
This thesis is aimed at evaluating electric grid conditions which has high percentage share of 
wind power in the system. The structure of the report is built on the framework of testing and 
evaluation of Wind Turbine type IV model (provided by PSS/E). Different type IV WT 
models are tested on a single machine infinite bus system on RTDS and the best one is 
incorporated in the NORDIC 32 system, which is the main test system on which all the 
studies are being done. This system is chosen because it closely emulates the dynamics in the 
real NORDIC system. The simulation platform on which the studies are done is PSS/E. The 
components in the network responsible for dynamic behaviour of the system are being 
studied and presented as well. Care has been taken that when Wind farm is integrated in the 
system the time step for simulation is set to 5ms. This is necessary in order to capture all the 
dynamics in the power electronics (Converter system) of the type IV wind turbines. The 
initial aim was to compare the simulation results on PSS/E and RTDS but due to technical 
challenges and deviations of work from evaluating the system condition to understanding the 
control system of type IV Wind Turbines, the modelling part on RTDS is left for the future 
work.Various stabilities scenarios concerning Voltage, Small Signal and Transient stability 
are being developed and the results are analysed. At last conclusions are being made and 
possible future work which can be carried out on the same lines is mentioned.

Chapter 1 - Introduction 
1.1 Roadmap for RES Integration and Need for Modelling Future Power 
Systems 
In Denmark, over the next few years, the government has ambitious plans of transforming the 
power systems from being a fossil fuel based to a completely renewable energy source based 
entity. By the year 2030, 50% of power generation and consumption is to be played by the 
renewable energies. A staggering surge in the percentage share from the wind power has 
already been seen in the last decade and its share rose from a mere 12% in 2000 to 30 % in 
2012. However, with such a high penetration of renewable sources in the grid, the technical 
challenges to be faced by the grid operators and engineers are enormous. The basic nature of 
the power system is quite fluctuating due to the unpredictable nature of the load side 
behaviour. Adding to this, with their high percentage share, the complex and intermittent 
behaviour of renewable sources could prove to be highly dangerous, given the fact that the 
entire power system stability would be based on these. A very cautious approach in 
transforming the power system from fossil based to the green fuels based body is hence 
required.  
Moreover, the geographical location of Denmark and its own electricity demand profile 
makes it a typical place. In future, due to the ambitious aim of integrating the pan European 
electricity network with the Nordic network, for better interconnectivity and stronger grid, 
Denmark’s grid will have to handle a huge amount of power which will not be consumed 
here but it rather it will only be wheeled through its grid. As mentioned before a lot of wind 
power capacity is also to be incorporated in the Danish electricity grid which means a whole 
new infrastructure with new cables and associated power electronic devices is imminent.  It is 
also assumed in the studies conducted, that in near future, due to aesthetic reasons, all the 
overhead transmission lines will be dissembled and cables would be laid down for the desired 
connections. All the new connections are already done with cables. This transformation will 
hugely impact the supply and consumption of VArs in the system. The distribution grid on 
the other side is assumed to be of the same nature until a foreseeable amount of time. Hence, 
in any of the studies to be done, this factor can be assumed as constant.

1.2 
Technical Factors 
The generation pattern as mentioned before is set to be changed from a constant nature to a 
very intermittent and unpredictable nature. Apart from wind, the solar energy has made big 
progress in regards to its growth and applicability in the society. Further due to the high 
incentives provided by the government and ever dropping prices of the PVs, this resource can 
now be exploited in a much cheaper way. Nevertheless, the new generation forms are set to 
be a mixture of wind and solar energy. The electric system with such a mix calls for new and 
sophisticated methods for keeping it in a stable and secure state of operation. Due to the basic 
nature of the power generation, there will be needed a huge storage capacity in the grid to 
cater for the times when there is a sudden drop in generation due to sudden climactic changes 
and weather pattern.  
One of the major reasons why the new future grids might be even more prone to stability 
issues is the economic approach which is taken to make it a success story. While the 
incentives given to the generators and the BRPs will be able to take care of the system state 
during emergencies, the normal state of the system will be dragged to the limits because of 
the same reasons. Unlike the present practices, where most of the power present in the grid is 
taken care of by the bilateral agreements on the basis of long term mechanisms, the future 
power system will face a situation where the power quantum will mostly be dictated on the 
daily or monthly basis. This will make the system highly stressed. Distributed generation 
which will form the backbone of the future power systems, will inevitably lead us to a 
situation where the control of both active and reactive power flow to the point where it is 
needed the most will be utmost challenging. This means that the new controllers and control 
logic will be needed to each of such grids.  
1.3 Stability Issues in the Grid 
The stability issues in a power grid can lead to a system wide abnormal behaviour of 
components and cause a major disturbance or ultimately a black out. Hence, before these 
future power systems are fully implemented and being made functional it is very important to 
know and study scenarios to understand the real time operational conditions which will be 
faced in near future. On the basis of governmental plans and the upcoming wind and solar 
farms and their geographical locations, various cases with respect to different stabilities can 
be built and studied on simulation platforms. The results of such study cases, which will 
realistically represent the real time production-demand situation in the grid, the future system 
operators will be able to effectively implement the corrective measures and preventive 
protection schemes in the system to take care of the emergency situations.  
This report is prepared considering mainly wind power penetration in the power system due 
to its major contribution in terms of renewable energy integration over the last 2 decades. The 
various grid code requirements from operators in different countries regarding connection of 
wind farms in recent years have had a huge impact in shaping the turbine technologies and 
newer developments. These strict grid codes are utmost important as the level of penetration 
from wind has increased multi-fold which affects the system stability and performance 
because of intermittent nature of wind.

1.4 Grid Code Requirements for a Wind Farm 
For the grid stability factors as mentioned above, different Grid Operators have framed 
different Grid codes for connection of Wind Farms to the existing power network depending 
upon the local network design and power flow scenarios. Most of these standard codes are 
pertaining the reactive power supply capabilities of such wind farms and their performance 
during fault conditions. Basically by stricter grid codes it is meant that the wind farms are 
required to behave more like conventional generators during fault conditions and static 
stressed conditions. This means that with increased share in the power markets, wind farms 
should be obliged to regulate and control power, frequency and voltages in a power system. 
These requirements call for better Fault Ride Through (FRT) capabilities of wind farms and 
that they can support the grid during disturbances. Following are the grid codes set by 
Energinet,dk for wind farms. 
By power control capability it is meant that the Wind Turbines should be able to behave like 
conventional generators and that they can participate in primary and secondary frequency 
control. Whereas in this report, Voltage, Small Signal and Transient stability cases are 
simulated and tested, the aspect frequency stability is being left out since the considered 
generic model WT4 model produces uncharacteristic spikes on PSS/E and these spikes are 
greatly amplified at a power below the rated power and hence the results would be largely 
unreliable. The voltage control capability means that the wind farm supports the grid voltage 
by supplying demanded reactive power.  
FRT characteristics define the necessity and need of the WFs to remain connected to the grid 
during the low voltage conditions, which typically are seen during fault conditions of certain 
time duration and a certain level of voltage drop. This is a very important feature to be 
considered because if the wind farms get disconnected in the scenarios with high penetration 
of wind power, there will be a huge drop in the active and reactive power of the grid which 
can lead to a system wide instability.  
Of the four choices, nowadays increasing number of wind farms are employing type III and 
type IV Wind Turbines because of their better performance and ability to extract more power 
from wind as compared to type I and type II wind turbines. Performance here means supply 
of active and reactive power at all times, during different conditions in the grid and in the 
wind conditions. The inclusion of power electronics in type III and IV turbine technologies 
has helped the wind industry to make a strong stamp because of their superior control 
capabilities of active and reactive power. To see the effect of increased wind power in the 
tested power system, type IV turbines have been considered. The working principle and 
control system of type IV WT is discussed in detail in Chapter 5. During the course of this 
work, due to time limitations and complications with regards to working on RTDS and the 
concerned type IV Wind Turbine model, the objectives have deviated a lot from being 
modelling the system on RTDS to restraining to system studies on PSS/E and analysing the 
system by integration of wind farms.

2.1 Frequency Stability 
In a power system, the parameter of frequency is of utmost importance as it reflects the 
condition of the entire power system. Frequency in a power system determines the state of the 
generating machines and as well as the loading conditions in the system. Aim of a system 
operator must be to keep this frequency constant or very close to the nominal set frequency of 
the system. This is necessary because constancy in frequency means that the loads of the 
system receive a good quality of power. Also, a constant frequency means constant speed of 
synchronous machines, which is important for a stable system operation. 
Maintaining system frequency constant is a matter of maintaining active power balance in the 
system. This means at any given time the power produced by the generators must match the 
power requirement by the loads. The speed governors which form a part of prime mover and 
energy supply system can play an important role in stabilizing the system during fault 
conditions. These governors are found in hydro generators and hence in the test system of 
NORDIC 32, the generators in the northern part of the grid which are hydro generators will 
act as frequency stabilizers. Fine tuning of the governor parameter values (various time 
constants involved) is important and these might vary on a large scale from one system to 
another.  
2.1.1 Droop of the Machines and Participation in Frequency Stabilization 
When the frequency changes due to change in the system load, the speed governing system of 
the generators start acting and try to bring the frequency back to the initial steady state value. 
In doing so, the turbine gate/valve opens up or narrows down the supply of the steam in it, in 
order to increase or decrease the machine speed i.e. the mechanical input increases or 
decreases as per the required electrical energy. Such an action is required to maintain 
constant speed of the rotating machine. Hence, such governors are called as isochronous 
governors. An isochronous governor works well in an isolated system but when the system 
consists of more than one generator/machine, there will be a race among these machines to 
control the system frequency on their own settings. Hence, to coordinate this issue, each 
machine in the system has its own load sensing parameter. This is known as the droop “R” of 
the machine. Different values of R decide which generator in the system participates how 
much in frequency control action.

2.2 Small Signal Stability 
Small Signal Stability is a form of rotor angle instability and can be sub divided in Aperiodic 
and Oscillatory instability. It can be defined as ability of a Power system to remain stable 
under small perturbations is called the small signal stability of the system. It is important to 
study this response of system because even though the perturbations are small, the effects 
they can have on the system can be of considerable harm and the generators in the system 
might lose synchronism against each other. Essentially, there are two basic factors which lead 
to this situation viz.  
(a) Steady increase in rotor angle due to lack of synchronizing torque 
(b) Rotor oscillations due to lack of oscillating torque 
In defining any given system, there have to be defined some state variables to represent the 
state of the system completely. In case of Small Signal Stability analysis of a power system, 
the state variables of the system are essentially, the rotor speed (ω) and the rotor angle (δ) of 
the generating machines. One of the most important steps in analyzing if the given system is 
small signal stable is to linearize the system around a certain operating point. Linearizing of 
the system means that the perturbations of concern are so small, that they can be linearized. 
This is the frame of the system which we are interested in, when investigating small signal 
stability of the system. 
2.2.1 Excitation System and Small Signal Stability 
Small signal stability studies are required because in a power system there is always a 
possibility of small disturbances exciting relative angles of rotors in the system which does 
not damp out with time. The chances of such small disturbances occurrence increase with 
increase in size of the power system and the number of control systems hence augmented in 
the system. Because of this there is a small relative angular motion between the machines i.e 
the machines oscillate against each other and this occurs because of very small disturbances. 
Hence, this phenomenon can be considered as a linear phenomenon due to the small size of 
the disturbances considered. These oscillations grow with time if not properly damped.

2.3 Transient Stability (Equal Area Criterion) 
2.3.1 Background 
The concept of stable and unstable equilibrium points is to be studied in detail in this 
assignment. This calls for the transient energy function of the system which evaluates the 
system state in post fault condition. A simple rolling ball analogy is used to explain this 
concept. The total energy of the system is assumed to be constant and is equal to the kinetic 
energy injected in the system due to extra available power and the time dependent potential 
energy gained by the system. This means that the variable ω dictates the kinetic energy in the 
system and δ helps in finding the potential energy of the system.  
Understanding the above concept helps in realizing that by looking at the angle δ, which 
varies with time (Swing Curve), we can keep a trace of potential energy gained by the 
system. This gain in energy should be less than the critical energy of the system which 
defines the points beyond which the system runs into the unstable region. As shown, the 
critical energy of the system depends upon the initial state of the system and the direction in 
which the system is disturbed. By this we mean, that in a practical system, there are many 
elements including transmission lines, transformers, synchronous machines, FACTS and each 
of these elements have their own defining parameters such as line lengths,  reactances, rotor 
speeds, operating times which if disturbed can lead a system towards instability with respect 
to them (direction of disturbance) 
As seen from the above explanation, it is understood that the rotor angle δ increases with time 
post fault occurrence. The potential energy of the system hence gained can be expressed 
according to the following equation

As the angle δ is a function of time, this means that the potential energy is also a function of 
time. This energy quantum must be maintained less than the critical energy of the system. 
Hence the time required by the protection system to isolate the faulted element or part of the 
system should be minimum or correspond to a value at which the potential energy is less than 
the critical energy of the system.  
2.3.2 Equal Area Criterion for Investigating Transient Stability 
The need of a Power System to be analyzed in terms of transient stability needs a thorough 
understanding of underlying concepts of system’s variable and how they vary with time since 
the point of time the fault occurs.  For a Power System, these state variables are the rotor 
angle δ and the rotor speed ω (this speed is the relative speed with respect to the steady state 
synchronous speed. Analyzing the transient stability invariably includes analyzing the 
behavior of these state variables. A system during transient period can be necessarily 
considered as a system with constant input power which in this case is the mechanical power 
(Pm) because of the time frame that we are making these studies in. A fault in a power 
system means that the Electrical Energy required by the system reduces which means that the 
system has extra input energy as compared to the required output energy. Due to this

2.4.3 Phenomenon of Voltage Collapse 
It is seen that the load power factor has a significant influence on VR i.e. on the receiving end 
voltage for a given power transmitted on the transmission line. It can be seen that the 
maximum power which is transferrable over the transmission line has an increasing trend as 
the power factor is made to lead. This means that the power carrying capability of the line 
increases if the load is of capacitive nature and it decreases if the load is inductive. 
The very fact that a given transmission line has a maximum power transfer capacity points to 
the logic of physical limitations of the material. The reason why the PV plots look like they 
do is the behavior of the current and the voltage in/across the line. Since the studies are done 
to check the maximum power which can be transferred, the only way it can be done is by 
reducing the load impedance. This results in increase in the current and hence the power 
transferred. However, the voltage across the line drops. Up to a certain point (critical point), 
the increase in current dominates the decrease in voltage. Hence, the power goes on 
increasing. However, after this limit the drop in voltage is bigger than the increase in current. 
So the trend reverses and the power transferred through the line decreases.  
 
2.4.4 Effect of ULTC and Load Composition in Voltage Stability 
Load characteristics and distribution system voltage control devices are one of the key factors 
in controlling the voltages in the system. The active and reactive components change with the 
voltages in the system and hence they interact with the given power system. The ULTCs try 
to hold the voltages at a point of consumption at a constant level, this is alright in the normal 
controlling range but during low system voltage levels this in itself might have disastrous 
effects. During each tap changing action, effectively, the XI2 and RI2 losses would increase 
on the transmission level, which means that there will be a greater drop in transmission 
voltages. [12] 
When the ULTCs reach at the end of their tap range, i.e after the initial time delay provided 
(30s), the subsequent tapping time constant (8s) and the tapping mechanism time constant 
(2s) and further depending on the number of taps, which will determine the time in reaching 
the tapping end point, the distribution system voltages begin to drop because of the original 
fault in the system. This leads to decrease in the residential active and reactive loads. The 
industrial loads initially remain almost constant. However, due to drop in residential loads 
which leads to decrease in line loadings and hence reactive power consumption, the industrial 
capacitors supply less reactive power. But as compared to the industrial loads which still have 
significant reactive power consumption, the supply is less. Hence in totality, the total reactive 
load increases. [12, pg 969]

5.1 Need and Considerations for Modelling  
For the system studies to be carried out in this report, modelling of wind turbines forms one 
of the main aspects for the stability studies. Since, traditionally, large power systems form of 
predominantly synchronous machines, modelling of these machines is fairly detailed and a 
high level of sophistication is achieved in their modelling. However, due to large and 
aggressive penetration of renewable power in modern power systems, a detailed modelling of 
such renewables is needed. In this project, stability of the system with a considerable 
percentage of wind power is to be studied. Hence, a detailed modelling of wind turbines is to 
be done to study the effects of such magnitude of wind power in the system. Since, most of 
the upcoming wind farms employ type IV wind turbines for power generation; this model is 
incorporated in this project. PSS/E has in-built generic models for the wind turbine which 
have been used in this project. The WT4G1 (Convertor and Governor control) module and 
WT4E1 (Electrical control) module is being considered. The reasons for choosing type 4 
wind turbine lies in  
The presence of power electronic circuitry between the grid and the turbine and hence its 
superior operating characteristics. The converter allows power produced at different 
frequencies due to variable wind speed to be injected into the grid at the power frequency 
which can be achieved through control signals given to the converter. Since, at all the times 
the line side converter will follow the grid frequency the operation of this type of turbine is 
possible even during the transient conditions. Hence, better Fault Ride Through (FRT) 
features [1] 
 
For studying the dynamic phenomena like voltage and angle stability various components of 
wind turbines are to be represented. These components can be enlisted as below: 
 Generator model 
 Mechanical control model 
 Electrical control model 
 Aerodynamic model 
 Auxiliary control model 
However, through detailed studies it had been observed that too much detailed modelling of 
wind farms considering all of these aspects and a model which aggregates all such parameters 
give us the same results when investigating dynamic phenomena. Hence it is justified to only 
consider the generator and electrical modules. The mechanical control modules can be 
aggregated and represented in the wind farms. Also, since all of the wind turbines in a one 
farm are essentially of the same type, the entire plant capacity can be represented by one 
model of turbine and then this model can be scaled up to the plant capacity. One important 
aspect which has to be borne in mind while modelling of components is that the power 
system oscillations typically have the frequencies in the range of 0.1 Hz to 10 Hz and hence 
the oscillations occurring in this bandwidth should be correctly represented [1]. Hence, wind 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
28 
 
turbine dynamics which have very high frequencies can be seen as non-affecting members to 
the complete model. A correctly modelled system considering this requirement will hence 
dictate the time taken for simulation.  
Unlike the DFIG technology, the usage of full rated convertor technology means interfacing 
the power system with the wind farm with a back-to-back power electronic converter. This 
means that both the real and reactive power limits of the plant depend upon the limits of this 
converter which will be a function of heat capacity and current ratings of this convertor. 
Hence it is essential to consider the ratings of this convertor while modelling his type of wind 
turbine.  
As seen from the above considerations, and as per the theory of the system stability, it is 
important to understand that every element in the system is responsible for its stability limit 
and that modelling of these elements can affect the system’s state in a fairly strong manner.  
5.2 Type IV Wind Turbine at a Glance – Capability and Conversion 
Principle 
As had been described earlier, type IV WT is a variable speed wind turbine system where the 
speed of the generator can be adjusted according to the wind speed in order to maximize the 
overall energy conversion efficiency of the system. Apart from active power control, variable 
speed wind turbines are also able to control the flow of reactive power to the grid. In this 
project, a variable speed wind turbine with a Permanent Magnet Synchronous Generator is 
considered. 
The PMSG is a synchronous generator where instead of rotor windings, the rotating field of 
the rotor is produced by permanent magnets and therefore no power supply is needed for field 
excitation. Since there is no field current and the magnetic field of the rotor is constant, the 
stator voltage cannot be regulated by the excitation and depends only on the speed of rotation 
As the kinetic energy of the wind rotates the blades of the turbine, a mechanical torque is 
applied on the rotor shaft. The rotor shaft operates as the system's prime mover and transfers 
the generated mechanical torque to the rotor of the synchronous generator which in return 
generates the electrical power output. 
The wind generator is connected to the grid through a full capacity converter composed by an 
AC-DC and a DC-AC converter, interconnected with a DC-link which decouples the 
generator from the grid. This enables the turbine to run efficiently even in the changing 
environment of the wind speeds. Hence, the variations in the wind speeds are not reflected in 
the power grid side of the converter system.  
In variable speed applications, power converters are a vital part of the system as they convert 
output voltages of varying frequency, first to DC voltage and then to AC voltage 
synchronized to the frequency of the grid. Wind turbines with PMSG are always equipped 
with full capacity power converters. This scheme of converter is also known as frequency 
converter, since it essentially converts all the frequencies on the machine side to a single 
power frequency on the grid side.

5.3 Different Representations of Type IV Wind Turbine Models 
In this project, as the main aim is to find the system state (in this case Nordic 32) with a large 
amount of wind power injection, the modelling of wind turbines (aggregate models) is to be 
of precise manner. As mentioned earlier, since most of the newly employed WTs are of Type 
IV, only this type of wind turbine modelling would be considered here. Further, type IV WTs 
are superior to the rest of the turbine models as the mechanical rotating parts are eliminated 
from the grid side converter and only the power electronic part of the model is to be modelled 
and simulated. As given by Siemens there are 4 different types of representations of this 
turbine model. These models are based on considerable approximations done on the power 
electronics and the ultimate actions they perform. By this we mean, the action of delivering 
power into the grid. Since this can be thought of as supply of current or a machine 
maintaining a specific voltage at a node, the 4 different representations are as follows: 
1. The detailed model which includes all the mechanics as well as the power electronics 
2. Constant Current Source Representation           3 
3. Constant Voltage Source Representation 
4. An Aggregate Model without any machine 
 
As per the new requirements by different grid operators, wind turbines or WPPs should be 
able to support the grid during fault conditions as well as, be able to ride the system through 
fault conditions. This means that these above mentioned turbine representations have to be 
able to  
 
 Supply reactive power during low voltage conditions 
 Should have decoupled active and reactive power supplying capability 
 Should be able to bring the system oscillations under control as fast as possible 
 Should use minimal computational power of the simulation software so that faster results 
can be realized 
 
All these parameters are important because when there is a faulty situation in the system, 
according to different stability criteria, the system can take different amounts of time to 
stabilize. For transient stability and small signal stability, the amount of time in which the 
system is restored to normalcy is of high importance. This is important because if the system 
takes a longer time, then it is at risk of receding into a state of instability because of 
numerous other factors.

5.4.1 RTDS configuration - Hardware/Software 
 
RTDS (Real Time Digital Simulator) is a powerful computing tool used for power system 
simulations. One of the main advantages of RTDS is its capability of real time computations 
which allows it to be used in testing hardware (like a circuit breaker operation) in real time. 
Hence, an RTDS user can simulate the scenarios, which occurs actually in the power system.  
Techniques like Digital Signal Processing (DSP) and Reduced Instruction Set Computer 
(RISC) are employed in RTDS. Since, real time operation of a power system means high 
levels of details of various components in the system, it demands for high computational 
speeds. RTDS uses advanced parallel processing techniques to cope up with this requirement. 
The model library provided by RTDS can be divided into power system components, control 
system components, and VSC small time-step modeling. The main network is solved with a 
normal time-step size of about 50μs, whereas the VSC circuit is solved with a time-step of 
1.4 to 2.5μs. The PB5 card has 2 identical RISC processors (Freescale MC7448) operating at 
a clock frequency of 1.7 GHz. Up to 2 PB5 processors in each rack can be assigned the task 
of the network solution. The other processors solve the equations of the power system and 
control components. The software determines the role of each processor in a simulation [4]. 
This would be noticed in the following section, where different processors are assigned to 
different representation of type IV wind turbine to carry out specified tasks.  
Each PB5 includes 24 analogue output channels with 12-bit D/A converters and is equipped 
with eight GT optical transceivers for the connection of peripheral I/O devices and direct high 
speed card-to-card communication. PB5 is also compatible with GPC processor cards. The 
Giga Processor Card (GPC) contains two IBM PowerPC 750GX RISC processors each 
operating at 1 GHz. The RISC Processor card (RPC) consists of two PowerPC 750Cxe RISC 
processors operating at a clock speed of 600 MHz [3]. RTDS software also includes a 
powerful Graphical User Interface (GUI), called RSCAD, through which the user is able to 
construct, run and analyze the simulation cases. This feature is used to test the different WT 
representations mentioned in previous section.

acceleration of the rotor. As a result of this, the rotor speed increases. However, since the 
control system (the pitch controller) as shown in Figure 5.1 is in place, it prevents the speed 
to exceed the rated value by increasing the pitch controller angle with a time constant of 0.06

6.1 Modelling of Large Wind Farms in Power System Simulations 
A wind generator and its controls is a very complex electromechanical system. While it may 
be possible to represent all of its functions in detail, this is generally not necessary as many 
will not be relevant in the final performance output of the power system and hence the subject 
area of concern. For different stability studies, various levels of detailed modelling has to be 
practised depending upon the time frame in which the studied phenomenon occurs. Hence, 
Wind turbines should be represented with sufficient accuracy to investigate the particular 
phenomena of interest. As previously noted, for power system stability simulations, 
equipment models should be chosen with bandwidths in the range 0.1 to 10 Hz. The 
bandwidth of the dynamic model has a direct relationship with the required simulation time-
step and resulting simulation speed. The simulation step size needed for a large power system 
stability study having hundreds of models including both conventional and wind generation is 
determined by the requirements of the one model having the smallest time constant. Wind 
turbine dynamics that are instantaneous or near-instantaneous compared with the 0.1 to 10 Hz 
bandwidth should therefore be modelled by algebraic logic instead of dynamic models with 
very small time constants. [13] 
 
6.1.1 Problem of Numerical Instability Due to Improper Simulation Step Size 
Wind turbine models should have a numerical integration time-step requirement of 5ms due 
to the presence of power electronic components and their fast acting nature. Hence all the 
load flow and dynamic simulations are being carried out with a time step of 5ms.  Dynamic 
system time constants, simulation step sizes, and choice of integration algorithms will affect 
the numerical stability of power system simulations, which include wind turbine models. The 
level of modelling detail required may result in numerical problems appearing particularly 
when wind turbine models requiring very small time-steps are incorporated into existing 
large-scale power system simulations.

Partial Conclusion - Wind Power and Small Signal Stability 
The occurrence of small signal stability in a system is the result of insufficient synchronizing 
and damping torques. This can occur due to inadequate modelling of components in the 
excitation system. On the other hand it can also result from less inertia in the system. 
Incorporating type IV wind farms in a power system effectively reduces the inertia of the 
equivalent MVA base machine as compared to the conventional system because of separation 
of turbine from the main network by the converter power electronics.  This results in 
reduction of the natural damping and synchronizing components of the system. Hence the 
system becomes small signal unstable and ultimately runs out of synchronism as is seen in 
cases B and C.

Reason for Transient Stability enhancement  
The enhancement can be attributed to the fact that the power electronics of a wind turbine 
acts extremely fast as compared to the governor control system of the conventional 
synchronous machines for balancing of power injection. By this we mean that during the 
fault, when the power required in the system is less, the control systems of synchronous 
generators fails to act fast enough and hence the energy supplied in the system during this 
faulted time is higher than required. Due to this surplus energy the rotors of the machine 
accelerate causing angular separation with respect to the synchronously rotating frame. 
However, with a wind farm placed in the system, due to very short response time of the 
converter system, the turbines respond to the power demand and reduce their output very fast.  
So effectively, with wind farm in the grid, the ratio (X/R) seen by the conventional generators 
which is prone to running out of synchronism is reduced [18]. This means that the amount of 
power it can supply to the grid will be more and hence its rotor will not accelerate as fast as it 
would in case of all the conventional generators. Consequently, in total, the energy input to 
the system is effectively lowered during the transient period with a wind farm in the grid. 
Hence, the system survives for longer time during such faults. This can affect the critical 
clearing time setting given to the circuit breakers and can ease the burden of fast acting 
requirements. Further, type IV WTs provide a superior performance in regards to supply of

Partial Conclusion – Transient Stability with Wind Integration 
As seen in the cases in preceding section, it is seen that the transient stability of a power 
system for a given disturbance is enhanced by the presence of wind power in the system. The 
superior reactive power control capability of a type IV wind turbine dictates the transient 
behavior of the system with its converter system action. The converter has a very high 
response time to the grid condition and when the network suffers a fault its fast acting control 
system regulates the amount of power injected in the system by controlling the active 
component of the current. Further, the reactive component of the current takes charge of 
supplying additional reactive power in the system during this faulted period. Because of fast 
action, the transient energy in the system is less. As a result, the critical clearing time of the 
fault is increased and hence the circuit breaker operating times can be longer and hence more 
reliable. On the other hand if a fault is too severe, the network behaves almost in the same 
way. As seen in Case B, with the tripping of the transmission line, the synchronous condenser 
at bus 4041 also trips instantaneously, since the prevalent system loading is high and this 
machine field current is operating close to limits. This triggers a deficit of 200 MVAr of 
reactive power in the system. Hence even with the fast response system of WTs the system 
runs out of synchronism.

Chapter 10 - 
Conclusion and Future Work 
 
To conclude, during the course of this project, different aspects of learning objectives have 
been achieved at different stages. These are as follows: 
 Why studying wind power integration in power systems is so important 
 Since the report is focused on impact of wind power on stability, the type IV WT 
model is studied in detail and understanding of its control system is achieved 
 The simulation software PSS/E has been extensively used in this work for simulation 
of NORDIC 32 system and during this a good level of proficiency is gained in 
conducting these simulations on this software 
 The main aim of this project was to model the system on Real Time Digital Simulator 
(RTDS). However, Due to certain project target deviations such as learning the 
control system of type IV Wind Turbines and to understand the root cause of 
uncharacteristic spikes (in Voltage and current Signals), modelling on RTDS was 
further complicated and could not be carried out. Nevertheless, a basic learning of 
RTDS is being achieved through WT testing 
 In-depth studies of different types of instabilities in power system and their causes are 
being investigated. How the presence of wind power in a power system influences 
different stability margins of the network is also examined 
Summarizing, in Chapter 1, the background and need behind integration of wind power in 
modern power systems is presented. The grid codes designed by the System Operators which 
define standard performance (in terms of stability) of Wind Turbines during fault conditions 
are being presented. Chapter 2 contains the theory and the basic factors responsible for 
various instability issues in a large power system. The components in the power system 
which are responsible for triggering these stability issues in the grid are presented in Chapter 
3. The studied system in this work is NORDIC 32 test system and it is presented in Chapter 4 
in brief. The detailed network element models are documented in appendices. To conduct 
various studies on this system, the type IV Wind Turbine model has been chosen due to its 
superior performance in terms of reactive support during fault conditions and better 
efficiency in terms of extracting active power during different wind conditions. Three 
different model representations of type IV WTs are being tested on RTDS to choose the best 
performing model which is the detailed model giving responses both on electrical and 
mechanical levels. All of this is documented in Chapter 5. Chapter 6 elaborates on the 
problem of initialization and uncharacteristic spikes from wind farms which occur in PSS/E 
while performing simulations are caused due to the power electronics in the converter system 
of the WT which need a very small time step of 5 µs. An investigation of the WT model 
revealed that the spikes are governed by the limiters of the converter physical rating, hence it 
can be concluded that their occurrence is only a result of incapability of the simulation 
software to perform calculations at such small time steps. Apart from these spikes, the 
standard type 4 WT model (WT4E1 and WT4G1) as provided in PSS/E performs well. To 
correctly start the dynamic simulation of the network, it has to be initialized for some time 
(10-20 seconds) and the simulation step size is kept at 5ms.  
==PAGE:74==
 
 
66 
 
The wind farms are incorporated in the system in the central region, where thermal generators 
are located. This fulfils the requirement of minimum environmental impact from electric 
power generation.  In Chapter 7, the three stability issues viz. Voltage Stability, Small Signal 
Stability and Transient Stability with wind power integration are being analyzed with the help 
of various cases. The behavior of the network with wind integration is in accordance and as 
expected with the case studies in [16] and [17]. For sake of clarity partial conclusions are 
provided in each of the three instability scenarios.  
Concluding on stability aspects, it can be said that different kinds of stability boundaries are 
influenced in different manner with wind power integration. The Voltage stability of a 
network reduces with type IV WTs integration, as the converter current rating has a limit 
which is lower than the overload capacity of field winding of a conventional synchronous 
generator. Further, the stability also depends on which location and voltage level, the wind 
farms are connected at. Small Signal stability of a power system is also reduced with 
increased wind power as the net inertia of the system reduces due to the electrical separation 
of the rotating machinery from the main network by a converter. As compared to 
conventional generators, the Transient stability however, is enhanced with wind power. The 
converter power electronics act fast to increase or decrease the injection of active/reactive 
power in the grid as required, thereby arresting surplus amount of energy in the system during 
transient period and avoiding rotor angle departure from synchronous frame.  
Future Work 
The aim of this project was to model future NORDIC 32 system on Real Time Digital 
Simulator (RTDS) which as mentioned before could not be fully achieved due to 
complexities as mentioned in the conclusion. However, the root cause was identified but the 
time factor did not allow for further simulations. Hence, it is expected to carry out the same 
simulation cases on RTDS as performed on PSS/E. It will be a good idea to compare the WT 
simulation results with the ones obtained herein with regards to the uncharacteristic spikes. 
The cases developed here are limited and hence more such cases can be obtained. Changing 
of WT model generic parameters were out of the scope of this project. With an aim of 
obtaining better performance by WTs in the network, changing these parameters may also be 
included in future studies.

As other dynamic models in the system, the OLTCs in a power system play an important role in 
maintaining the networks security. These tap changers act in the system during low voltages to 
control the load side voltages, however, the voltages on the higher side are continually pulled down 
in this process thereby reducing the voltage stability of the system. Hence, there is a requirement for 
a specific time delay before there is any tapping action seen in the network (Td), so as to prevent 
any unnecessary tap changing in the system i.e. during short time voltage dips. Also, since this is a 
mechanical system, there is provided some time (Tc) for the tap changing action itself and also on 
between subsequent tapping signals (Tsd).
