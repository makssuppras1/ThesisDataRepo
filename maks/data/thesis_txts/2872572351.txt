Abstract
The rise of quantum computing threatens classical key encapsulation mechanisms, driving the
search for secure, eﬃcient post-quantum alternatives. While the lattice-based key encapsulation
mechanism (KEM) Kyber [Bos+17] has been standardized by the National Institute of Standards
and Technology (NIST) as the new default KEM called ML-KEM, it lacks authentication and
unforgeability - crucial properties for ensuring trustworthy key exchanges.
This thesis investigates whether the split KEM (sKEM) FrodoKEX+ [Col+24] can be instantiated
over the more eﬃcient ring learning with errors (RLWE) problem while maintaining post-quantum
security, deniability, unforgeability, and IND-CCA-style robustness simultaneously.
We improve upon FrodoKEX+ by instantiating it over the more eﬃcient RLWE problem and
adopting a more eﬃcient reconciliation method. We prove RingStrikesBack’s correctness,
OW-CPA security, deniability, and decapsulation-OW-CPA security via hybrid game reductions
to decisional RLWE, and we employ isomorphism based arguments to analyze invertibility and
uniformity in the ring setting.
We propose two instantiations with explicit parameters: an instantiation over the small (old)
Kyber ring (n = 256, q = 7681, error distribution Ψ = Bin150, and reconciliation parameter
p = 3) and an instantiation over a larger ring (n = 512, q = 12289, error distribution
Ψ = Bin150, and reconciliation parameter p = 3). The smaller ring achieves 143 bits of classical
security, while achieving a public key size of just 448 bytes, which is almost 50 times smaller
than FrodoKEX+’s public key size of 21780 bytes. The larger instantiation achieves NIST
Security Level Five, which is a classical security level and deniability level of at least 256 bits
and quantum security level of at least 128 bits. We provide benchmarks demonstrating that
RingStrikesBack over the larger ring compares favourably to FrodoKEX+, an existing sKEM
based on plain LWE, in terms of three critical parameters: network traﬃc needed to establish a
shared secret, correctness, and both classical and quantum security. RingStrikesBack over the
larger ring achieves a public key size of just 928 bytes, which is almost 25 times smaller than
K-Waay’s public key size of 21780 bytes.
Overall, this work demonstrates that RLWE based sKEMs can simultaneously achieve deniability,
unforgeability, and IND-CCA-style security within a practical parameter regime.
To our
knowledge, this is the ﬁrst construction to meet all these criteria in the RLWE framework.

Chapter 1: Introduction
The advent of quantum computing poses a signiﬁcant threat to classical cryptographic systems.
Quantum algorithms, such as Shor’s algorithm [Sho97], can solve the integer factorization and
discrete logarithm problems in polynomial time, rendering RSA and Diﬃe-Hellman insecure
in a post-quantum world. As a result, there is sustained and growing interest in developing
cryptographic schemes that remain secure against quantum adversaries.
Among the most promising and actively studied approaches are those based on lattice problems,
particularly the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem. Lattice-based cryptography has attracted
signiﬁcant ongoing attention due to its strong theoretical foundations and eﬃcient implemen-
tations. This continuing relevance is demonstrated by the National Institute of Standards
and Technology’s (NIST) Post-Quantum Cryptography competition, which recently selected
four schemes for standardization. Notably, three of these algorithms are built on lattice-based
cryptography: CRYSTALS-Kyber (standardized as ML-KEM [Bos+17]), CRYSTALS-Dilithium
(standardized as ML-DSA [Duc+17]), and Falcon (standardized as FN-DSA [Fou+19]). This
prevalence underscores both the robustness and widespread adoption of lattice-based construc-
tions in post-quantum cryptography.
K-Waay, a recent proposal by [Col+24], introduces a quantum-secure split key encapsulation
mechanism (sKEM) based on LWE called FrodoKEX+. This construction can serve as a
replacement for the extended triple Diﬃe-Hellman protocol used in the Signal messaging
protocol. In this thesis, we investigate the feasibility of adapting the FrodoKEX+ construction
to use the more eﬃcient Ring LWE (RLWE) variant [Reg10]. Our primary objective is to
identify and overcome the challenges associated with this transition while maintaining critical
security properties such as deniability and unforgeability. To the best of our knowledge, these
properties have never before been simultaneously achieved within a RLWE-based protocol.
1.1
Related Work
[Reg09] formally introduced the LWE problem to the cryptographic community, proved a
quantum reduction from LWE to worst-case lattice problems such as GapSVP, and constructed
a public key encryption scheme whose security relies on LWE’s hardness. [Pei08] presented
the ﬁrst purely classical reduction from LWE to standard lattice problems by introducing
eﬃcient Gaussian sampling techniques; however, this reduction required a modulus that grows
exponentially with the lattice dimension. [Bra+13] improved Peikert’s approach by developing
a modulus reduction technique based on gadget matrices, reducing the modulus size to a
polynomial in the lattice dimension and thereby making LWE-based constructions more practical.
Building on these results, [LPR13] and [Pei14] introduced the RLWE problem, which instantiates
LWE over polynomial rings. This ring structure produces an asymptotic speedup of roughly
a factor of the ring dimension n in both storage and computation, enabling more eﬃcient
encryption schemes.
Using the KEM framework from [Pei14], [BNP14] proposed an RLWE-based Diﬃe–Hellman-like
key exchange protocol. They demonstrated its practicality by integrating their implementation
into the OpenSSL TLS stack, showing acceptable performance in internet settings. Shortly
thereafter, [Bos+16] developed a highly optimized RLWE key exchange protocol using carefully
chosen parameters, a more eﬃcient noise distribution sampling method, and an improved
error-reconciliation procedure. Their implementation achieved a performance improvement
greater than an order of magnitude compared to [BNP14].

[Bre+21] introduced the split key encapsulation mechanism (sKEM), a primitive in which
a sender encapsulates a shared secret using both the sender’s secret key and the receiver’s
public key, and the receiver decapsulates using its secret key together with the sender’s public
key. This symmetric structure supports asynchronous pre-key bundles, as used in the Signal
protocol’s long-term and ephemeral pre-key architecture.
Building on [Bos+16], [Col+24] proposed a deniable, post-quantum Diﬃe–Hellman-like protocol
that extends LWE key exchange with batch decryption, allowing reuse of ephemeral keys,
reﬁnements to the sKEM primitive from [Bre+21], and an adaptation of the TCH transform
[HV22] to the sKEM setting.
In this thesis, we extend the deniable, post-quantum key-exchange frameworks of [BNP14],
[Bre+21], [Bos+16], and [Col+24] by combining the optimized error reconciliation algorithm
of [SLL21] and the error distribution of [Bos+16] with concrete security proofs in the style of
[Col+24].

2.2
Key Encapsulation Mechanisms
In general, when two parties, commonly referred to as Alice and Bob, wish to communicate
over a long distance, they employ a public key encryption scheme. Despite its practicality in
such scenarios, public key encryption is typically less eﬃcient than symmetric key encryption.
Consequently, once a secure channel is established, the use of symmetric key encryption is
often preferred due to its lower computational overhead.
The primary limitation of symmetric key encryption lies in its requirement for a shared secret
key between the communicating parties. This presents a fundamental challenge often described
as a ”chicken-and-egg” problem: to use symmetric encryption, a shared secret key must already
exist, but securely establishing this shared key itself appears to require a secure communication
channel.

Key encapsulation mechanisms (KEMs) oﬀer a practical solution to this dilemma. A KEM often
involves the initial use of public key encryption solely for the purpose of securely transmitting
a randomly generated symmetric key. Once both parties possess this shared key, they can
transition to a symmetric key encryption scheme for the remainder of their communication,
thereby beneﬁtting from improved performance without compromising security.

2.3
Ring Learning with Errors
In this section we will deﬁne the RLWE problem, which is at the core of our sKEM, and some
RLWE variants which will be needed in various proofs.
In its basic form, LWE presents an adversary with a collection of linear equations that have
been deliberately corrupted with small amounts of random noise. Speciﬁcally, an adversary
receives pairs of the form (a, t) where a represents a random vector and t equals the inner
product of a with a secret vector s, plus a small error term. The computational diﬃculty lies in
recovering the secret vector s from these noisy linear equations, even when provided with many
such samples. One might try to solve this using Gaussian elimination, however in doing so one
blows up the error terms e which completely dominate the secret s.
The RLWE problem introduces algebraic structure to the standard LWE framework to achieve
greater computational eﬃciency. Rather than working with arbitrary vectors and matrices as
in LWE, RLWE operates within polynomial rings. This ring structure transforms the random
vectors of LWE into structured polynomial coeﬃcients, while maintaining the essential security
properties of the underlying problem. The key advantage of this structured approach lies in its
eﬃciency gains. Where LWE operations require handling large matrices with n2 elements, RLWE
leverages the ring structure to reduce storage and computational requirements to approximately
n elements through polynomial arithmetic. This dramatic reduction in complexity makes
RLWE-based cryptographic schemes signiﬁcantly more practical for real world deployment
while preserving the post-quantum security guarantees that make lattice-based cryptography
attractive. The structured nature of RLWE further enables eﬃcient implementations using
techniques such as the number theoretic transform (NTT), enhancing performance without
compromising security assumptions.

Chapter 3: Split Key Encapsulation Mechanisms
The primitive at the core of our protocol is a split key encapsulation mechanism (sKEM),
which we will explain in this section. [Bre+21] originally deﬁned the sKEM primitive. [Col+24]
introduced many of the security notions which we will need.
A sKEM extends the conventional KEM primitive by enabling both parties to contribute
cryptographic material during the encapsulation process, ensuring that the resulting shared key
K incorporates keying material from both participants. This framework accommodates distinct
key generation functions, KeyGenA and KeyGenB, allowing each party to employ cryptographic
parameters tailored to their speciﬁc operational requirements.
This architectural ﬂexibility proves particularly valuable when the encapsulating party requires
diﬀerent cryptographic keys than the decapsulating party. The bilateral contribution of keying
material enables implicit authentication of the encapsulating party within protocols following
the X3DH framework, as the shared secret inherently depends on authentic material from both
participants.
Following the terminology established in [Bre+21], our construction presented in chapter 4
adopts an asymmetric approach to simplify the design, operating under the assumption that
Bob consistently performs the encapsulation operation. Nevertheless, the construction can
be readily generalized to support symmetric sKEM functionality. This generalization becomes
particularly straightforward when KeyGenA equals KeyGenB, which represents the conﬁguration
employed in RingStrikesBack. Consequently, this chapter focuses exclusively on the asymmetric
sKEM deﬁnition to maintain clarity and alignment with our speciﬁc construction approach.

3.1.1
Split Key Encapsulation Mechanism Deniability
We now introduce the notion of deniability that we aim to achieve.
Informally, the setting is as follows. Alice and Bob engage in a key exchange protocol using a
sKEM to derive a shared secret key. We assume that the public keys are used exclusively for
this key exchange process.
Consider a scenario where Alice, after following the protocol honestly, attempts to implicate Bob
by proving his participation to a third party. After receiving Bob’s ciphertext and deriving the
shared key, Alice presents evidence to a judge (represented as an adversary): both public keys, the
computed shared key, and her own private key. The judge faces a critical determination—whether
the presented ciphertext originated from Bob or represents a fabrication that Alice could have
generated independently.
This deniability property addresses signiﬁcant real-world security concerns.
In corporate
whistleblowing scenarios, for example, an employee may transmit sensitive information to
external parties. Organizations seeking to identify and retaliate against such whistleblowers
face substantial obstacles when communications employ deniable key exchange protocols, as
deﬁnitive proof of participation becomes mathematically impossible to establish.
A scheme is said to be deniable if there exists a simulator which, given Alice’s view of the
interaction, can produce a ciphertext and a key that are computationally indistinguishable from
the ones that would have been generated by Bob.

4.2
Reconciliation
Reconciliation serves as a critical error correction mechanism in RLWE based sKEMs, addressing
the fundamental challenge that arises from the noisy nature of lattice-based cryptography. In
RLWE-based schemes, both parties derive shared values that are mathematically related but
not identical due to the intentional noise components that provide cryptographic security. This
noise, while essential for security against quantum attacks, creates a practical problem: the
parties’ computed values diﬀer by small random amounts, preventing them from establishing
an identical shared secret key.
The reconciliation process systematically eliminates these small discrepancies to produce
matching key material.
The mechanism typically operates by having one party compute
reconciliation information that guides both parties toward the same discrete value. This process
involves mapping the noisy shared values to a smaller, discrete space where the noise diﬀerences
become negligible.
The reconciliation procedure must balance several competing requirements. It must be robust
enough to handle the expected noise levels while remaining eﬃcient in terms of communication
overhead. Additionally, the process must preserve the cryptographic properties of the underlying
scheme, ensuring that the reconciliation information does not leak suﬃcient details about the
shared secret to compromise security.
In the context of authenticated key encapsulation mechanisms, reconciliation enables the
establishment of identical session keys despite the inherent noise in lattice-based computations.
This capability is essential for practical deployment, as cryptographic protocols require exact
key agreement between communicating parties. Without eﬀective reconciliation, the noise
characteristics that provide post-quantum security would render the schemes unusable for real
world applications. The eﬃciency and accuracy of the reconciliation method directly impact the
overall performance and reliability of the cryptographic scheme, making it a crucial component
in the design of practical RLWE-based systems.

4.3.1
RingStrikesBack With Multiple Encapsulations
The RingStrikesBack protocol supports multiple executions against the same ephemeral key,
enabling eﬃcient asynchronous communication patterns. In practical deployments such as the
Signal messaging protocol, the sKEM would execute each time a sender initiates communication
with a recipient. This design addresses a fundamental challenge in asynchronous messaging:
maintaining communication capability when recipients are oﬄine for extended periods.
When a recipient like Bob remains oﬄine long enough that his supply of fresh ephemeral public
keys becomes exhausted, senders can continue to establish secure channels by reusing existing
ephemeral keys for multiple encapsulation operations. Upon Bob’s return to the network, he
can process all ciphertexts corresponding to the same ephemeral key simultaneously through
batch decryption.
This architecture provides two critical operational advantages. First, it ensures that senders
maintain the ability to initiate secure communication regardless of the recipient’s online status.
Second, it eliminates the requirement for recipients to remain continuously online to generate
fresh ephemeral keys for each incoming message, thereby supporting practical deployment
scenarios where persistent connectivity cannot be guaranteed.

Chapter 7: Instantiation
This chapter presents concrete parameter selections for our RingStrikesBack construction, with
the objective of achieving security levels comparable to existing sKEM protocols. Our analysis
proceeds through two distinct instantiation strategies, each reﬂecting diﬀerent design priorities
and security trade-oﬀs.
Initially, we explore an instantiation over the established CRYSTALS-Kyber ring structure, moti-
vated by its widespread adoption and well-understood cryptographic properties. This approach
oﬀers signiﬁcant practical advantages by leveraging established cryptographic infrastructure,
beneﬁting from extensive security analysis conducted by the cryptographic research community,
and potentially accessing optimized implementations and hardware acceleration developed for
the CRYSTALS ecosystem.
Consequently, we present a second instantiation utilizing a computationally harder ring struc-
ture that provides substantially improved security guarantees. This analysis illustrates the
fundamental tension between leveraging established cryptographic parameters used in MLWE
and meeting contemporary security requirements for post-quantum key exchange mechanisms
based on RLWE.
7.1
Kyber Ring Instantiation
7.1.1
Parameter Selection
Our initial parameter selection targets NIST Security Level 1, corresponding to 128 bits of
classical security and 64 bits of quantum security, following application of the TsKEM
CH
transform.
This security level provides a meaningful comparison baseline against FrodoKEX+, the primary
existing sKEM construction in the literature.
We establish the ring dimension as n = 256, which enables the encoding of 256 bits of entropy.
This conﬁguration provides security equivalent to a 128-bit symmetric key under quantum
attack models employing Grover’s algorithm [Gro96], achieved through the extraction of exactly
one key bit per polynomial coeﬃcient.
The modulus q must satisfy speciﬁc algebraic constraints to ensure the validity of our security

Chapter 9: Future Work
This chapter outlines potential avenues for extending the present work and addressing limita-
tions identiﬁed in our analysis. The proposed research directions encompass both theoretical
improvements to our security proofs and practical enhancements to our construction’s parameter
choices and underlying algebraic structures.
As mentioned in chapter 8, changing our scheme to utilize MLWE would come with many
beneﬁts, and this is the main direction of future work which we would like to explore. One
key beneﬁt is that Module Learning with Errors (MLWE) provides superior security scaling
compared to RLWE, with hardness approximately proportional to exp (O (d · n · log(q/σ))),
where d represents the module dimension and σ the standard deviation of the error distribution,
compared to RLWE’s exp (O (n · log(q/σ))) scaling. This improved scaling relationship allows
increasing security through modest increases in the module dimension d.
This allows us to choose a ring, for example the new Kyber ring, and tune d to achieve the
desired security. This is how Kyber, and other MLWE schemes, achieve varying security levels
over the same ring, for example achieve NIST Security Levels One, Three, and Five over the
same ring in Kyber [Bos+17].
We will detail some of the challenges in switching to the new Kyber ring of q = 3329 and
f(X) = X256 + 1 as well as adopting MLWE. This parameter choice oﬀers signiﬁcant practical
advantages, as future hardware optimizations developed for Kyber would directly beneﬁt our
construction. In the short term, we would also directly beneﬁt from all of the optimisation
done by the CRYSTALS team on their schemes, such as their eﬃcient NTT and NTT−1
implementations.
9.1
Challenges In Adopting Module Learning With Errors
9.1.1
Kyber-Compatible Parameters
Our current construction employs the CRT isomorphism to address counting arguments, such
as determining the probability that a uniformly random matrix A from Rm×(m+1)
q
possesses a
nullspace of dimension one, calculated in lemma 2.16. This calculation becomes substantially
more complex for general modules where the CRT isomorphism does not apply directly.
The modulus q = 3329 introduces an interesting algebraic property: the polynomial f(X) =
X256 + 1 factorizes into 128 quadratic factors rather than 256 linear factors. This intermediate
complexity level may prove more manageable than the general case while still requiring theoretical
development.
The transition from linear to quadratic factors necessitates calculating the number of non-
invertible elements within each quadratic factor and adapting our existing proof techniques
accordingly. While this approach would likely increase the security loss in our deniability proof,
the ability to increase the module dimension would allow us to recover the lost security.
As a last resort, one can numerically calculate the fraction of invertible elements in each
quadratic factor by brute force or by simulations. Each quadratic factor has 33292 ≈1.1 · 107
elements, which is well within the reach of brute force.
Having computed this fraction of invertible elements one can update each loss with this new
factor, which certainly will increase the loss substantially.

9.1.2
Proof of Deniability
The principal challenge in developing this thesis centered on establishing deniability in the
RLWE case. Having successfully addressed this challenge, and with the plain LWE case covered
in [Col+24], we believe extending the deniability proof to MLWE remains feasible, though it
presents signiﬁcant theoretical complexity.
The primary obstacle involves our dependence on CRT isomorphism arguments. While we
demonstrated above how to compute the invertibility probability for uniformly random elements,
the underlying challenge proves more intricate. In lemma 2.21, we established that sampling a
uniformly random matrix A and computing its nullspace produces results indistinguishable from
directly sampling the nullspace uniformly, provided the nullspace dimension remains ﬁxed. Such
arguments become substantially more challenging without the theoretical framework provided
by CRT isomorphism.
A second signiﬁcant challenge requires proving that the MLWE equivalent of VZ(e−d) exhibits
uniform or near-uniform distribution. We anticipate that a proof methodology in the RLWE
setting will provide valuable insights for addressing the MLWE case. The theoretical approach
developed for RLWE may prove directly transferable to MLWE, oﬀering a pathway to overcome
this challenge. If not, one may again simulate the probability as in section 6.1.
9.2
Practical Implications Of Module Learning With Errors
Successfully establishing deniability in the MLWE setting would yield a modiﬁed version of
Kyber enhanced with masking terms to achieve deniability properties. Such a result would
represent a signiﬁcant contribution to the cryptographic community, particularly given Kyber’s
widespread adoption and standardization by NIST.
The security loss associated with deniability proofs is expected to exceed that of other security
notions such as IND-OW-CPA. However, since Kyber currently provides no deniability guarantees,
any level of deniability would constitute a meaningful improvement to the construction’s security
proﬁle.
To prove deniability for Kyber, we anticipate requiring slight modiﬁcations through the addition
of masking terms fA and fB. We expect that these masking terms will not invalidate the
existing proofs presented in the original Kyber paper [Bos+17]. This preservation would allow
researchers to select between proof methodologies in the style of K-Waay [Col+24] or Kyber
for each security property that both constructions address, such as IND-CCA security.
An alternative research direction involves formally proving the invertibility bound of VZ(e −d),
which we evaluated through simulation in Chapter 6. Several analytical approaches merit further
investigation that we were unable to pursue within the scope of this work. These unexplored
research directions may yield analytical calculations that provide theoretical foundations for the
empirical results we observed, and might be transferable to the MLWE case.
9.3
Establishing Near-Uniformity of VZ(e −d)
Chapter 6 attempted to demonstrate that the expression VZ(e −d) exhibits uniform or near-
uniform distribution properties but did not achieve a complete theoretical characterization.
Several promising approaches remain unexplored and merit further investigation.
9.3.1
Canonical Embedding Approach
The canonical embedding framework, as developed by Lyubashevsky [LPR13], provides a
powerful tool for analyzing distributions over cyclotomic polynomial rings. This embedding
transforms algebraic numbers within cyclotomic rings into real coordinate vectors, thereby
enabling the application of geometric and analytical techniques through well-deﬁned norms.
The Ring Strikes Back
55
==PAGE:62==
The canonical embedding preserves coordinate-wise multiplication and addition operations,
suggesting potential applications to our uniformity analysis. A productive research direction
would involve characterizing the distributional properties required of Ψ to ensure near-uniformity
of VZ(e −d) under the canonical embedding. Once these properties are established, one could
systematically select error distributions Ψ that satisfy the identiﬁed criteria.
An alternative approach within this framework involves applying error distributions directly
within the canonical embedding space, potentially simplifying the analysis of the resulting
distributions and their uniformity properties. This would however complicate calculations of
correctness.
9.3.2
Advanced Statistical Analysis
The expression VZ(e −d) represents a sum of products of binomial distributions, creating a
complex but analytically tractable distributional structure. While direct characterization of
this distribution presents computational challenges, systematic statistical analysis could yield
valuable insights.
One promising approach involves calculating the probability that Z(e −d) equals zero, which
would provide a lower bound on the min-entropy of the distribution. This entropy estimate
could then support a leftover hash lemma argument, extending section 6.3.
9.4
Research Priority and Impact
The near-uniformity analysis represents the most direct extension of our current work and
would strengthen the theoretical foundations without requiring changes to the construction.
The modulus reduction and MLWE extensions oﬀer greater practical impact but require more
extensive theoretical development.
Each direction addresses speciﬁc limitations identiﬁed in our current work while opening
pathways toward more practical and widely applicable cryptographic constructions.
The
successful completion of all of these open questions would signiﬁcantly advance the state of
deniable key encapsulation mechanisms and their integration with standardized lattice-based
cryptography by making a deniable MLWE based sKEM reminiscent of Kyber.

The rise of quantum computing threatens classical key encapsulation mechanisms, driving the search for
secure, eﬃcient post-quantum alternatives. While the lattice-based key encapsulation mechanism (KEM)
Kyber [Bos+17] has been standardized by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) as
the new default KEM called ML-KEM, it lacks authentication and unforgeability - crucial properties for
ensuring trustworthy key exchanges.
This thesis investigates whether the split KEM (sKEM) FrodoKEX+ [Col+24] can be instantiated over
the more eﬃcient ring learning with errors (RLWE) problem while maintaining post-quantum security,
deniability, unforgeability, and IND-CCA-style robustness simultaneously.
We improve upon FrodoKEX+ by instantiating it over the more eﬃcient RLWE problem and adopting a
more eﬃcient reconciliation method. We prove RingStrikesBack’s correctness, OW-CPA security, deniability,
and decapsulation-OW-CPA security via hybrid game reductions to decisional RLWE, and we employ
isomorphism based arguments to analyze invertibility and uniformity in the ring setting.
We propose two instantiations with explicit parameters: an instantiation over the small (old) Kyber
ring (n = 256, q = 7681, error distribution Ψ = Bin150, and reconciliation parameter p = 3) and an
instantiation over a larger ring (n = 512, q = 12289, error distribution Ψ = Bin150, and reconciliation
parameter p = 3). The smaller ring achieves 143 bits of classical security, while achieving a public key size
of just 448 bytes, which is almost 50 times smaller than FrodoKEX+’s public key size of 21780 bytes. The
larger instantiation achieves NIST Security Level Five, which is a classical security level and deniability level
of at least 256 bits and quantum security level of at least 128 bits. We provide benchmarks demonstrating
that RingStrikesBack over the larger ring compares favourably to FrodoKEX+, an existing sKEM based
on plain LWE, in terms of three critical parameters: network traﬃc needed to establish a shared secret,
correctness, and both classical and quantum security. RingStrikesBack over the larger ring achieves a
public key size of just 928 bytes, which is almost 25 times smaller than K-Waay’s public key size of 21780
bytes.
Overall, this work demonstrates that RLWE based sKEMs can simultaneously achieve deniability, unforge-
ability, and IND-CCA-style security within a practical parameter regime. To our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst
construction to meet all these criteria in the RLWE framework.
