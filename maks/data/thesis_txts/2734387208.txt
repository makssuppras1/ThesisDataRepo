Abstract
Ilulissat, with its growing tourist industry and buildings, faces an increment in power demand. Combined
with a national strategy of implementing a variety of renewable energy sources, this leads to an investigation
of the frequency stability of an isolated hybrid power system in Ilulissat.
To perform such an investigation, a model of Ilulissat’s power system is developed in DIgSILENT’s Power-
Factory. An existing model sets the basis for a further development, whose main focus is the hydroturbine’s
dynamic control system and the PV system implementation.
The developed model is based on acknowledged literature, accessible data, and field experience. Although a
full validation process is not possible, the results do not lead to a model rejection.
Results show that frequency instability arises for severe irradiance fluctuations at a large PV penetration.
The level of stable PV penetration decreases if irradiance fluctuations are combined with large changes in
load. Furthermore, the instability concerns the frequency nadir, which means that improving the system’s
inertia will allow a higher level of PV penetration in Ilulissat without causing frequency instability.
Overall, Ilulissat’s power system’s frequency stability is highly dependent on the hydroturbines, but it also
tolerates a PV system, thereby utilizing more and a wider range of renewable energy.

Introduction
On the central-western coast of Greenland, in the Avannata municipality is Ilulissat. With a population of
4848 (2023), it is Greenland’s third-largest town. Historically, Ilulissat’s main industry has been catching
and processing seafood, mainly from the Icefjord and the Disco Bay. The Icefjord is globally known for its
icebergs breaking from the Jakobshavn Glacier and drifting out in the Disco Bay; it has been a UNESCO
World Heritage Site since 2004. Ilulissat Icefjord and other tourist attractions have resulted in a huge growth
for the past years, and today a large part of Ilulissat’s occupancy has become tourism. This growth also
requires an extension of the power system in Ilulissat, both in satisfying the incrementing power demand
and in the electric infrastructure.
Like most countries, Greenland’s ambition is to fulfill the green transition and to produce energy exclusively
from renewable energy sources (RES) [1]. Nukissiorfiit is Greenland’s government-owned energy company
and supplies 70 towns and villages as well as other areas with energy, i.e.
water, heat, and electricity.
Nukissiorfiit is responsible for the electric power production and operation of all Greenland’s power systems,
including the power system in Ilulissat.
Ilulissat’s power system is isolated without any possibility to connect to a main grid. The electric energy is
supplied from a 22.5 MW hydro power plant (HPP), consisting of 3 × 7.5 MW hydroturbines, and located in
the Paakitsoq region. A 45 km 60 kV transmission line connects the HPP and the main 60/10 kV transformer
station in the town, which furthermore supplies 9 radial lines.
The 10 kV distribution grid is made as a meshed network but is operated as a radial network. A 12 MW
Combined Heat and Power Plant (CHPP) is located in the town and is only used as a backup and emergency
power plant.
For normal household consumers, the substantial need for electrical heating during the whole year, and
especially in winter time, increases the electrical power consumption significantly. In addition, the fishing
industry and the airport are among the larger single consumers connected to the grid.
In 2013, the HPP replaced the CHPP, and Ilulissat’s power supply was no longer based on fossil fuels but on
RES. For more than 10 years, Ilulissat has been powered by RES, but with the current growth, Ilulissat will
face a crossroad and has to decide which RES should be implemented to meet the upcoming power demand.
Projects regarding RES in Greenland are made prior to this project, whose outcome i.a. has given a lot of
practical and theoretical experience [2] [3]. Establishing HPP has proven to be the far most reliable and
effective RES for larger systems, as the hydropower technology from the ice sheet has proven to work in
geographic locations beneath the permafrost. The turbulence in water pipes hinders the water to freeze, and
the source is durable.
There have been previous attempts to install wind turbines (WT) and photovoltaics (PV) in Greenland.

Previous studies from Canada and Alaska have proved WT technology to work in the Arctic locations.
Although the technology itself is confirmed, the logistics regarding transportation of WT during installation
and maintenance has become obstacles to installing WTs. PVs, on the contrary, has proven successful both
regarding technologically as well as in logistics. A PV system’s electric power production relies on how
much solar radiation will encounter the solar panels. Here, temperature, the sun’s position, reflections from
surroundings, and the panels’ tilt angles are critical in its efficiency. Greenland’s geographical location entail
both benefits, such as low temperature and high reflections, and disadvantages for PV system’s efficiency
such as the sun’s position. Since the PV production does not rely on a rotating system compared to WT,
the need of maintenance decreases. Also, the specialist knowledge of PV systems is more extensive, as it
already has been used for several years in Greenland.
1.1
Problem Definition
For this project, a PV system will be implemented as an additional power source in the current power
system in Ilulissat. On the contrary to the continuous hydropower, a PV system is intermittent and may
cause instability in a power system. Power system stability can be divided into three categories: angle,
frequency, and voltage stability.
As an isolated power system, Ilulissat is particularly exposed to potential instability.
By implementing
an intermittent PV system, the power system will most likely be challenged most against the frequency
stability. The interaction between the HPP and PV, as well as their dynamic control system, will be central
in addressing and mitigating these potential stability issues.
A simulation model has earlier been made in DIgSILENT’s PowerFactory (PF) of Ilulissat’s current power
system for other analysis purposes. This model has been made accessible for this project. The model will
be developed further to also be capable to conduct a frequency stability analysis of Ilulissat’s power system.
The objectives of this project’s problem definition are:
• How can Ilulissat’s HPP and future PV control systems be implemented and modelled in DIgSILENT’s
PowerFactory to be able to determine possible frequency stability problems in the electric power sys-
tem?
• How is the frequency stability of Ilulissat’s current electric power system combined with a future PV
system when being subjected to changes and disturbances?

2.2
Synchronous Generator
An electric generator is a machine that converts mechanical energy into electrical energy in a process of
electromagnetic induction. The most common generator type for power plants and large-scale generation
is the synchronous generator (SG). The rotor of a SG can either be round or salient, and this is usually
determined by the number of poles, where the latter is used for machines with a high pole number. The
most common configuration is alternating current flow within the armature winding, and DC rotor flux is
produced either through application of DC excitation to a field winding or by permanent magnets. A SG is
characterized by its rotor rotating in synchronism with the magnetic field set up by the armature windings.
This means for a steady-state operation that the rotor speed is proportional to the frequency at the armature
current.
A generator’s mechanical power input comes from its prime mover, which can be based on either renewable
energy sources such as wind, hydro, biogas, tidal and geothermal, or conventional energy sources with various
types of fossil fuels. Prime movers’ devices are windmills, turbines, and engines that create a mechanical
torque in the direction of rotation of the rotor.
The operating speed of hydro turbines is relatively low, which means that generators used for hydropower
are most commonly salient pole synchronous generators with a higher number of poles to be able to produce
the rated frequency. The rotor of a salient pole SG is equipped with a field winding and commonly also
short-circuited damper windings. The field winding excitation can either be provided through slip rings and
page 7 of 72
==PAGE:17==
2
Technical Review
brushes or through brushless excitation. A brushless excitation system consists of a rotary transformer, a
variable voltage inverter, and a fast diode rectifier on the SG rotor. The damper windings are formed as
bars in the pole face and may be connected to short-circuited end rings. The current in a damper winding
is zero during steady-state with balanced load but serves to dampen speed oscillations.
As the rotor coils of a SG are excited from a DC source, a magnetic field is created. When a prime mover
then causes the SG’s rotor to rotate, the revolving magnetic flux intersects the armature coils, inducing
voltage across the windings according to Faraday’s law of induction. This induced voltage is also called
counter electromotive force (emf), illustrated in Fig. 2.2.3.

The induced current in a loop will try to keep the B-flux through the loop constant.

The inductances can be grouped in these three main groups:
• Self-inductance that occurs in a coil when a change in electric current produces emf across the coil

2.3.3
PV Control System
In contrast to the synchronous generator in Section 2.2, a PV does not include a prime mover as a mechanical
input, and thus nor a governor for speed control. The PV generates through an electrophysical process of
solar radiation and semiconductors, and its interface to the power grid and hence the frequency control
system consists of power electronics through the inverter.
Frequency control for a PV system is embedded in the grid synchronization. For a three-phase PV system,
grid synchronization techniques can be either open-loop or closed-loop systems, where the latter for the
project’s PV system is more favorable, since its feedback mechanisms enhances the stability and precise
performance. For a closed-loop control system, the Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) is the most used method, and
will be described further [17].
A PLL is used to read the grid’s phase angle, and thereby controls the phase, frequency, and voltage.
Fig. 2.3.4 shows a simplified block diagram for a PLL system, where the grid voltage is read and normalized.
A Park transformation transforms the spatial distribution signals from the power grid into a rotating dq
reference frame (RRF). This transformation locks the RRF to the rotating space vector from the grid, and
makes the grid signals constant for the controller. This means that the direct voltage vector, Vd, is aligned to
the grid’s space vector, Vg, and the quadrature voltage vector, Vq, becomes zero. Now, if a change happens
in the grid signals, Vq becomes non-zero, and by using a PI controller, the error is eliminated. With the
grid’s angular speed as reference added to the error signal, the angular speed is first limited. It then provides
a frequency output and via an integrator the phase output for synchronization between the inverter and
grid.

2.4.1
Frequency Control
In an AC power system, frequency represents the number of complete cycles of alternating current that
occur in one second. Frequency is a global parameter, meaning that all units must operate at the same
frequency, and maintaining a constant and stable grid frequency is of high importance. A considerable drop
in frequency can damage equipment and components in the power system.
The active power supplied by a generator is related to the mechanical power input to the generator, and any
imbalance between mechanical power and electrical power will lead to a change in speed and, consequently,
frequency. It can therefore be stated that a power system’s frequency is dependent on the active power, and
the frequency control is thus based on the active power as well.
Fig. 2.4.1 shows the fundamentals of a frequency control. Initially, a normal operation with a rated frequency
shows a steady-state curve. At t = 0, a disturbance occurs leading to a drop in frequency. This event can
be divided into three main stages: Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Frequency Control. These three stages
will be allocated at different lapses of time, as they will serve different purposes for handling the disturbance.
Note the red color nuances for each stage on the power plot referring to the title above the frequency plot.

Rotating components in power systems such as generators and motors have a moment of inertia, which
serves as a resistance to changes in rotation and is proportional to the unit’s stored kinetic energy. As the
disturbance occurs and the frequency drops, this kinetic energy source will be tapped for a short period
before the primary frequency response reacts; this is called the Synchronous Inertial Response (SIR), and
results in a rapid spike in the power output. The rotating components with their inertia are critical for the
arresting period, as a high amount of inertia will slow down the frequency change and thus provide more
time for the Fast Frequency Response (FFR) that completes the arrest of the frequency.
Fast Frequency Response is the time period right after the initial frequency drop when the governors of the
system reacts and send an output signal on its prime movers as described in Section 2.2.2. Until now, only
non-sustained frequency response has been active; that is the power system’s inertia. This changes to the
sustained FFR, whose purpose is to inject or absorb sufficient amount of energy to/from the grid to arrest
the frequency and stabilize it. For a loss of generator or increase in load, the governors will increase the
mechanical input power. For a load reduction or fault, on the contrary, the mechanical input power will be
reduced. The nadir is defined as the frequency’s global minima, and for an ideal system, this point would be
equal to the fnew in Fig. 2.2.4. This means that the strive for FFR will always be to limit the nadir as much
as possible by turning the curve the opposite way. FFR can consist of power injection/absorption from, i.a.,
SG, PV, WT, ES, and turbine-governors. After the frequency drop has been arrested and stabilized, the
primary frequency control is completed and is followed by the secondary frequency control.
It is important to note that for a droop control to work properly as in Fig. 2.2.4, there has to be a frequency
error after the primary frequency control. It is thus the secondary frequency control, which gets activated
relatively long time after the nadir, that will restore the frequency from the stabilized fnew to the rated f0.
The secondary frequency control is normally performed using Automatic Generation Control (AGC). As it
is seen in the power plot in Fig. 2.4.1, this power provides only temporary support and will be subsequently
supplanted by the tertiary frequency control power.
The tertiary frequency control is in Section 2.2.3 called the re-dispatch. As the power curve shows, it will
gradually increase to a certain level, and thereafter stay constant. The frequency control of the disturbance
is hereby completed.

Section 2.4.1 describes the fundamental of frequency control for all sizes of power systems. A large and
stiff market-based power system does obviously require all aspects of frequency control due to its bulk and
impact, but also due to the large business and market systems that are strongly connected to it. Such a
system also has a high number of resources for frequency support, inertia, and redundancy, and a disturbance

must have a certain size to surpass these factors and thus impact the stability significantly. With all this
said, it is important to note that this project is not a part of a large and stiff market-based power system,
but a small, isolated, state-owned power system.
Ilulissat’s remote geographical location and hence its power system status with no possibility to connect to
a main grid defines it as an isolated power system due to its independence from other grids.
The solution for an isolated system has historically been to either extend the grid to an interconnection
between other isolated or main grids, or supply by conventional generation. Before the Paakitsoq hydro
power plant was completed in 2013, Ilulissat was supplied by diesel generators located in the town, which
today are used for emergency backup power supply.
Interconnections between Greenland’s isolated grids will to some degree be rational due to the large potential
of hydro power. For isolated grids, both the aforementioned frequency support, inertia, and redundancy are
aggravated, but with interconnections, all three factors would likely increase. However, interconnections
by either submarine cable or transmission lines are very expensive and difficult to implement in Greenland
because of long distances, fjord crossings, varying landscape with high mountains, environmental approval,
and harsh weather conditions [20] [1].
For this project, implementing a PV source to the HPP keeps Ilulissat’s power system isolated, but extends
its definition to an isolated hybrid power system, due to its reliance on own multiple generation resources.
For a system operator, a main principle must at any point be to provide a high standard of service to the
consumer in terms of stability and power quality, regardless of the system’s power sources.
As an isolated grid, Ilulissat’s power system is not committed to any common standardization or harmoniz-
ation of grid codes. The hydrogenerator’s under- and over-frequency protective relays in Ilulissat are set to
trip at 47.5 Hz and 52.5 Hz. Here, no under-frequency load-shedding is considered, and therefore a frequency
relay trip will disconnect the HPP and hereby cause a total system blackout.

3.2.2.3

Now, there has to be distinguished between a governor’s actual regulation settings and a modelling for
simulation purposes. While a governor’s actual regulation settings deals with the system’s response to a

physical factors: frictions, time delays and other time constants. It is therefore not enough to implement
the commissioning regulation settings only but also to parameterize the physical factors.
The library in PowerFactory has several hydro governor templates with varieties, such as linear/nonlinear,
with/without surge tanks, and large/small number of parameters for modelling the details of a HPP.

3.2.2.6
Choice of Governor Template
So after all, is the HYGOV template sufficient to make a proper analysis?
In the extraction of the Paakitsoq Work Description in Supplementary Appendix A, it is clear that the gov-
ernor in the Paaktisoq HPP is a PID electro-hydraulic type, which means that the governor is also equipped
with a PID controller to tune the turbine’s performance. The HYGOV template is not a conventional PID
governor. According to the PSS/E, the WEHGOV represents a Woodward Electronic hydro-governor with
PID control, and is thus a more correct representation of a PID controller. The rejection of WEHGOV is
mainly because this model is not so commonly used, and it has not been possible to get proper litterature,
user guide, and similar project references for a correct modelling.

Starting Time, Tw = 0.9 s. Since this value is well below the limit of 3 s, the derivative gain should not be
a necessity to implement in the control system. Moreover, the literature states that if the derivative gain is
not implemented, the now PI governor is equivalent to that of the mechanical-hydraulic governor, which the
HYGOV template represents. One can therefore conclude that for the relatively low Tw, HYGOV is a valid
template for representing the governors.

3.2.3.1
A Solar Cell’s Irradiance and Associated Modelling
A central part of the frequency analysis is the PV system’s irradiance, and its interaction with the HPP. In
Appendix C.14, the Solar Radiation slot is fed with a signal from the Measurement Solar Radiation slot and
outputs the solar radiation to the Photovoltaic Model slot that represents the array.
The Solar Radiation slot is configured to simply convey its input from the Measurement Solar Radiation,
where measured solar radiation has been uploaded, and hereby will affect the I-V curve characteristics in
Fig. 2.3.3.
A power system’s frequency stability can be challenged by a PV system’s intermittency. This intermittency
is to a high degree depending on the weather conditions, which determine both the amount and the changes
of a solar cell’s irradiance. A sunny and cloudless day will enhance both the amount of production and the
stability, while an overcasted day will result in low but stable production. The most challenging weather
conditions for stability are considered to be cumulus clouds [30]. The cumulus clouds make the solar ir-
radiation fluctuating, as the radiation will be partly hindered. Having additional high wind will result in
increased fluctuations for the PV system and thus require more regulation from the HPP.
The solar radiation data used for the PV penetration is measured in the EastGRIP station, an international
ice coring project led by the University of Copenhagen in Denmark [31]. The station is partly powered by
a PV system, which is mounted 360◦across and around the main building, a geodesic dome. There are 8
pyranometers mounted as well around the dome to make 360◦measurements of the solar irradiance. There
has been provided data for all 8 pyranometers for a time period of 15 days with a sampling rate of 2 seconds.
Fig. 3.2.5 and Fig. 3.2.6 show examples of the solar radiation for all 8 pyranometers for two days in August
2022, which together with Appendix C.16 lay the basis for the PV penetration in the frequency analysis.
The timeline’s unit is in seconds, and a full day is represented as 86000 s. However, it must be noted that
the pyranometers are set to GMT, and the plots should therefore be shifted 3 h and 27 min to the left. The
EastGRIP irradiance at 12:00 is represented on the plots as the irradiance at 15:27.

a frequency decrease, the dispatched power, Pdispatch, increases to Pnew. Examining the two other curves in
Fig. 4.2.6, the process is confirmed. The mechanical input power from the turbine now increases, resulting
in the necessary electrical output power to arrest the frequency.
The necessity of having the governor enabled for a hydroturbine has been confirmed, and no common practice
would disable a generator’s governor. A hydro governor’s design to feed back speed error for controlling a
gate’s position and hence the water flow ensures precision and enhances stability significantly. If the governor
is disabled, the system must rely on alternatives such as mechanical flow control using valve positioning or
some methods of load-following algorithms. The alternatives are untenable and should not be considered.

similar to the previous in Fig. 4.2.5, but without an AVR instead, is seen in three plots in Appendix D.1.
The three plots illustrate the frequency, mechanical input power, electrical output power, reactive power,
and voltage for two cases: with and without the AVR. Besides this, a third case is displayed as a dashed

curve on the frequency plot. Note for the power and voltage plots, the ”AVR off”-curves are gray.
Starting with the AVR enabled, a load increase results in a higher demand. This is met by the system’s
inertia and SIR, resulting in a transient spike on the electrical output power. Since the governor is enabled,
the wicket gates open and provide higher mechanical input power to the turbine. The hydrogenerator is thus
able to continuously deliver a high electrical output power. The terminal voltage decreases some, but to
restore it, additional reactive power must be injected, which is performed by the increased excitation voltage.
It is thus seen that the disturbance does not cause a system collapse, but both frequency and voltage are
preserved.
Now, with the AVR disabled, the observed incorrect behaviour is seen in the frequency plot in Appendix D.1.
A steady-state level above the nominal frequency and a remarkable overshoot are not correct dynamic re-
sponses when the load increases.
When subjected to the disturbance, the same transient spike caused by SIR occurs. The governor opens the
wicket gates to provide higher mechanical input power. However, the excitation voltage does not respond to
the change but is kept constant. This results in the hydrogenerator unable to inject more reactive power to
the system, and thus the terminal voltage decays to a critical level.
Although the voltage, active- and reactive power response are as expected, it is difficult to conclude the
reason for the frequency behaviour at the top plot since the response is not as expected. Because of an
electrical output power decrease, lower reactive power injection, and a severe voltage decay, the frequency
should not be able to end in a steady-state error above the nominal frequency.
An additional attempt is made to locate the source of the error. The 60 kV line is disconnected from the
HPP, and hereby the remaining load as well. Two equivalent loads are added to the HPP’s 0AFA10 busbar;
one equivalent to the 28 % load increase and one equivalent to the remaining load of Ilulissat. The dashed
line on the frequency plot shows the resulting frequency, which has the same characteristics as the solid line
but with a slight offset. The same tendency with similar offsets appears for the voltage, active and reactive
power characteristics, and is thus not shown.
When the AVR is disabled, no voltage error signal is delivered to the ”SYM” slot in the dynamic control
system in Appendix C.12. It is not possible to investigate the consequence this has on the hydrogenerator
because PowerFactory does not allow further insight to the ”SYM” block diagram.

The third concept investigates the frequency stability of an isolated hybrid power system, whose power

These disturbances can be divided into four subjects, where two relates to changes in the power system,

– Penetration of PV system with different power ratings from 0.00 MW (No PV) to 10.00 MW.
Three notes for these simulations:

be mixed with the PV system’s performance disturbances. For the second case, Section 4.4.2, only the PV
system’s performance will be investigated, and hence no changes in the power system.

frequency stability analysis of Section 4.4. There will only be considered changes that result in a frequency
decay. This means that only a decay in PV production or a load increase will be considered. The reason
is twofold: 1) Simulating an increment in PV production in PowerFactory causes other initial frequency
responses that spoil the planned frequency response, and 2) to make the frequency response more visually
coherent to Fig. 2.4.1.
Third and finally, the PV system will always produce its rated power, while the HPP will provide the
remaining demand. As the PV penetration increases, the HPP will produce less, but it will not at any point
become zero and not provide its inertia.

Discussion
Based on the theory in Section 2 and the outcome from Section 3 and Section 4, outlines will be looked at
from different points of view. Because of a systematic division between objective 1 and 2 throughout the
report, these are also separated for a section each.
5.1
Modelling of Ilulissat’s Power System
A central question when building a model for analysis purposes is how exact and detailed is a proper model?
How much is required for the particular purposes?
For an isolated and regulated power system like in Ilulissat, the requirements are not necessary equal to a
large market-based power system, e.g. the Continental European power system (CE). Here, every market
participant must fulfill several requirements based on grid codes to be allowed for operation of production
and consumption. For these purposes, a high-detailed power system model may be a necessity for the TSO
to be able to foresee any potential problems and avoid economic liability for compensation caused by severe
disturbances.
In Ilulissat, on the other hand, there are no market participants and the TSO is thus not required to issue
grid codes or take participants into account for their operational and planning decisions. Nevertheless, a
desire for enhancing the power system security, operational reliability and high power quality should still be
of high priority. A detailed power system model will contribute positively to this.
The level of details does, however, depend on its purpose. Primary frequency control stability analysis is
typically rated as short-term, a higher execution time than long-term but not as fast as electromagnetic
transients (EMT). This requires a certain degree of simulation samplings, but should this also reflect the
level of details in the modelling?
For this project, it is not the simulation tool that limits the level of details. Numerous templates including
tailoring of them are available. Overall, it is somewhat medium detailed. Several physical phenomena of
the HPP are excluded or assumed ideal. The PV has kept a lot of its default settings, and its accuracy
and quality is not considered. All loads are fully balanced and have the same power factor and coincidence
factor. So why not more detailed?
It turns out that a certain, not high-detailed dynamic model is most applicable. It is congruent to literature
[5] [24], and a wide field experience among engineers and technicians entails more references for the para-
metrization (Supplementary Appendix F). Because of limited load data, more general modelling is used, but
in a qualified way.
For the HPP’s attributes, results from Section 4.2.1 show that to a certain degree, it seems acceptable to
use qualified assumed generator properties in an isolated radial grid for frequency analysis. A major fault
close to the power source requires most accurate properties, while as the fault moves further away from the

source, the lines’ parameters will soak the source’s parameters; the inertia becomes decisive.
Regardless of the model’s details, validating the model is central. A standard method is holding it up against
the actual system. Very small amount of data from previous events have been available, but two methods
have been used for validation.
The first validation process is comparing the used dynamic model values to a set originating from a similar
turbine. The results show a stable and almost identical frequency response, but how should this be inter-
preted and what is the success criteria?
Such analyses do not validate that the used parameter values result in a frequency response that matches
the actual measured in Ilulissat’s power system. The congruence between the parameter values of FAE and
Used has little to do with the actual settings, and the principle of comparing to the actual is still not done.
It does, however, confirm the importance of every parameter used in the HYGOV. Any single parameter
switch between the FAE and Used results in a deterioration of frequency response. There is no indication
of, that the parametrization of the FAE-values has been any similar to this project’s parametrization, but
on the contrary through advanced algorithms and methods [25]. Without concluding if it is a coincidence or
a validation, it is thus likely that two different paths or methods have led to the same destination.
Second validation process replicates the only actual event provided and compares the frequency response.
Having only one event is a clear weakness in the validation. For resemblance, if only a change in load made
in the earlier comparison between the hydrogenerator attributes in Section 4.2.1, a global conclusion would
be that inertia is the only parameter that impacts the hydrogenerator during any disturbance in frequency
analysis. But by conducting several simulations, that conclusion became limited to a change in load only as
in Fig. 4.2.3.

process. Can the model be validated, when a part of the deviation is close to 1
3? Here, some uncertainties
are relevant to highlight.
First, the provided image (Appendix D.2) has only three labels on each axis, and its intervals are 20 min
(horizontal) and 10 Hz (vertical). The frequency interpolation is based on 10 Hz, but is used for measuring
a deviation of ∆fpeak = –0.023 Hz and ∆fsett = –0.004 Hz, correspond to –0.23 % and 0.04 % of the 10 Hz
interpolated measurings (Table 4.3.1).
Second, the image itself is of poor resolution. The areas for peak and settling points are large, and hence
the deviations can be both large and small, depending on where the measure points are placed (Fig. 4.3.5).
Third, the secondary frequency control is automatic in the actual power system, while not included in this
project. The secondary frequency control time schedule is unknown and thus a possible source of error.
Fourth, the load event is replicated using the developed power grid from Section 3.2, resulting in a 2 km
longer transmission line than the actual.

5.2
Frequency Stability of Ilulissat Power System
With guidance from Nukissiorfiit, a few locations and events are used for the frequency stability analysis.
Although Section 4.2.1 reveals the most severe disturbances on the 60 kV transmission system, it is not
considered, as a fault seldom occurs here. This, however, rises the question: how likely should a disturbance
occurrence be contra its severity? Minor disturbances happen more frequently than the major, so where
should the limit be set?
For Ilulissat, this limit is rather well defined though with the radial lines’ CB. The whole distribution system
is protected by 12 relays, where of 8 are relevant here, while the remaining are either redundant or not in
use.
Using load increase instead of a decrease as a disturbance is also choosing the least common, as the TSO
normally will be notified prior to e.g., a startup in the morning and thereby avoid a large imbalance. A
sudden load decrease happens, on the contrary, unintentionally. This is made to be consistent with literature,
whose explanations are based on large and stiff meshed grids, where losses of generating units are relevant
and cause frequency decay.
A central presumption for investigating the interaction between HPP and PV is PV’s potential fluctuating
power production; a proper representation of an actual fluctuation is important. No irradiance data from
Nukissiorfiit’s current PV systems has been accessible, nor does any of the searched radiation database have
samples in seconds. This weakens the basis for this interaction analysis as the geographic location becomes
imprecise.
On the other hand, the dataset of 15 days in August 2022 with a 2 s sample from an ice coring project in
central Greenland does improve the basis. The different weather behaviour between Western Greenland and
in the middle of the ice sheet is unknown. However, the measurements are probably still closer and more
realistic than most other places globally to represent the actual Ilulissat weather. Moreover, a sampling rate
of 2 s is a strong dataset for irradiance measures. And since the focus is exclusively on the fluctuations,
- that is, the changes between measurement points - and not on the amount of irradiance, such a dataset
allows for proper and realistic fluctuation representation. Not only the largest fluctuation, but also the most
frequently appearing over a time period. Finally, a high variation in irradiance for those 15 days enhances
the dataset’s reliability.
Using one of the settings from Table 4.1.1, the frequency stability in Ilulissat’s power system is stressed.
When a frequency instability is discussed, an important notice is that a low nadir does not necessarily cause
instability itself. For Ilulissat, though, a too low nadir will cause the hydrogenerator’s under-frequency pro-
tection to trip and consequently result in a system blackout.
With no PV and connecting the most heavily loaded lines experience a large frequency drop, while for other
lines, the frequency is maintained well. Increasing the PV penetration at a constant irradiance will not

aggravate the frequency stability significantly (Fig. 4.4.3). As the PV starts to fluctuate rather severely
(Fig. 4.4.2), an incrementing PV penetration will impact the stability. The heavily loaded lines enter quickly
a more severe area of instability, and when the PV penetration reaches about 4 – 6 MW, all lines are able to
challenge the system’s frequency stability (Fig. 4.4.4). The tendencies show a marginal improvement with
no disturbance but severe fluctuations.
Throughout the analyses, it is clear that the frequency instability is caused by fluctuations from an inter-
mittent PV system. The inverter with no kinetic energy stored is unable of opposing the changes during
fluctuations, while the HPP’s rotational masses, on the contrary, are able to oppose the changes. As stated
earlier, the more rotating masses connected, the stiffer the power grid becomes. The inertia is not dependent
on the power supply contribution from the power source, but whether it is connected and synchronized or
not. The plots representing various inertia levels for the same line connection reveal how a slightly decrease
in inertia makes the system significantly more unstable. On the other hand, it also shows that an increased
inertia will improve the frequency stability even further. Activating all three turbines at the same demand
for improving the inertia will also lower each generator’s contribution and thereby its load percentage. The
generator becomes less efficient, which may result in aging, losses, and an efficiency degradation with time.

Conclusion
6
Conclusion
Ilulissat’s actual isolated power system has been developed to a hybrid system with the PV implementation.
The hybrid system is modelled in DIgSILENT’s PowerFactory, and through comprehensive research and
data collection, simulation cases are created. Based on this, analysis on Ilulissat’s frequency stability has
been performed, providing assistance for current and future power system developments.
Since this project’s modelling has been developing an existing model to be able to analyze the frequency
stability, the main focus has been on the power sources and more specific the dynamic control system.
Instead of a high-detailed dynamic model with little guidance and literature support, a medium-detailed but
acknowledged model is chosen for replicating the actual hydroturbine’s control system and behaviour. With
this approach, each parameter value can be recognized theoretically.
Comparison between the hydrogenerator’s former estimated attributes and the verified attributes show an
overall improvement. Especially for the large faults, where correct attributes values are strongly recom-
mended, while its importance diminishes for smaller faults and disturbances. Further comparison between
enabling and disabling the GOV and AVR confirms the necessity of including them.
The model’s validation process contains a lot of uncertainties due to very limited accessible data. The valid-
ation process is, however, performed, and the deviation results are reasonable. Although a validation is not
possible, a rejection is neither advisable.
Ilulissat’s power system frequency remains stable in general. Having only constant irradiance, instability
issues may arise only when the most heavily loaded lines gets connected, regardless of the PV penetration
level. If the irradiance fluctuates, any line connection with a PV penetration above 6 MW may cause in-
stability.
For a change in load caused by a single large consumer, stability will only face challenges above 8 MW pen-
etration of fluctuating PV.
Without disturbances, but at the most severe fluctuation, stability remains for under 6 – 7 MW of PV pen-
etration.
The analyses show that Ilulissat’s power system remains stable up to a few MW of PV penetration, when
being subjected to the most plausible disturbances. Also that the system’s inertia is sufficient and the HPP’s
control system manages to maintain the frequency control.
If both hydro units’ inertia levels are increased by 50 %, the power system’s frequency would remain stable
for a few additional MW of PV penetration.
The closing remark concerning the frequency instability is that no instances of undamped oscillations have
been observed; only occurrences of a too low nadir, whose instability is caused when the hydrogenerator
under-frequency protection trips.

Conclusion
6.1
Future Work
In light of the conclusions reached in this project, some important avenues for future investigation emerge.
A main objective will be to rigorously validate the dynamic model developed in this project to ensure its
reliability. This requires a larger sets of data from the actual power system to ensure that the actual and
modelled frequency responses are identical for all types of disturbances. By doing so, the model can become
the cornerstone for Nukissiorfiit’s current operation and future planning in Ilulissat.
Collecting new datasets for the solar radiation in Ilulissat would also be beneficial to be able to better asses
a PV system’s feasibility. Not only would such a measurement be beneficial locally, but Nukissiorfiit’s other
districts would certainly also draw benefits from such data.
Another part of the modelling part is to improve Ilulissat’s load profiles by adjusting each local bus according
to its actual consumers. Such an improvement may result in a slightly different configuration in the power
system, and furthermore even result in different orders for the lightly and heavily loaded radial lines.
There are several well-known and reliable solutions for improving a system’s inertia if this becomes necessary
in Ilulissat. Synchronous condensers, energy storage systems, and virtual synchronous machines are widely
used for this purpose. In spite of this, Ilulissat does already have its CHPP, which currently only serves as
a backup or in emergency situations. A rather alternative field of research is to investigate, whether the
CHPP’s SGs can be modified to be able to run without their prime mover during normal operation and
provide inertia to the system. For emergency and backup situations, a mechanism such as a clutch will
reconnect its prime mover and hereby generate power to the power system [35].
