Preface
This project is a final project in MSc. Architectural Engineering at DTU Sustain. The re-
port is created by Freja Ejdrup Andersen from February 2024 to July 2024.
The project explored the environmental impact of different HVAC systems, in addition to
an economical comparison. The effect of occupancy on thermal comfort and energy use
was investigated.

Abstract
This case study has focused on indoor environmental quality (IEQ), realistic energy con-
sumptions, whole life carbon emissions (LCA) and whole life cost (LCC) of an office build-
ing. The methodology used evaluated IEQ as a prerequisite to ensure realistic energy
consumptions in combination with LCA and LCC. A consistent definition of the system
boundaries was used between the LCA and LCC in compliance with the Danish building
regulation. The impact of different reference study periods (RSP), operational emission
factors and occupancy behaviour, in the form of load schedules, was identified.
The office case study explored varying thermal mass, technical systems and internal loads
in a Danish context. The four explored configurations were a wooden structure (light) or
concrete structure (heavy) with either a radiator system (VAV+RAD) or an active beam
system (VAV+ACB).
With a RSP of 50 years and 2023 emission factors, the average whole life carbon emission
was 9.1 kg CO2-eq./m2/year. Using a shorter RSP of 41 years, the annual whole life
carbon emissions increased by an average of 12.8 %. With 2023 emissions factors, the
average whole life carbon emission decreased by an average of 27.7 % and 14.9 % for
a RSP of 50 and 41 years, respectively. From the baseline of a 50 year RSP and 2023
emission factors, the average operational emissions were found to contribute 41.2 % of
total whole life carbon emissions. With an increased energy consumption using English
load schedules, the average contribution increased to 48.8 %. HVAC systems were found
to have a contribution between 11.6-15.9 % on whole life carbon emissions.
The annual whole life cost was found to be 353.4 DKK/m2/year and 424.6 DKK/m2/year
for a RSP of 50 and 41 years, respectively. The respective impact on the total cost from
operational energy consumption was 8.5 % and 7.1 %. The introduction of other load
schedules showed a negligible cost increase during the entire lifespan of less than 0.1
%. Introducing a carbon tax showed little economic incentive for decision making and
only increasing the total cost by 3.1 % and 2.4 % using 2023 and 2030 emission factors,
respectively.
Overall the findings showed that the high thermal mass case had a smaller temperature
variation along with higher cost and greater carbon impact. Another discovery was that
the changes in energy consumption, due to the use of different load schedules, had more
impact on the LCA than the LCC.
Through a literature review a gap was found with regards to the impact of construction
errors on the LCA and LCC at the practical completion of the project.
The methodology used in this study was found to be effective in evaluating LCA and LCC.

1
Introduction
In 2021 around 40 % of the global emissions were caused by buildings (IEA, 2022) and
in 2023 it became a requirement in the Danish building regulation to provide an LCA for
all new buildings (Schmidt, 2022). This has pushed the development and acknowledged
the growing need for sustainability in the building sector.
However, there are often discrepancies between the simulations and actual performance
of the building. This could be attributed to the uncertainty of occupant behavior, future
emissions from energy production, actual lifetime of the building, and further cost and
emissions from replacing elements of the building due to critical errors in the function-
ality/performance of the building (Andersen & Negendahl, 2023; Andersen, 2012; Buso
et al., 2015; Peansupap & Ly, 2015; Ryberg et al., 2022). Furthermore, there is also a
lack of transparency when it comes to the assessment of the impact of different building
materials and how they count their contributions (Astle et al., 2023; Ding, 2020).
A study by Dejaco et al. (2020) argue for the importance of introducing methods combining
LCA and LCC in the early design phase to aid decision making in phases with low levels
of detail.
1.1
Objectives
With the amount of uncertainties and increasing focus on sustainability, this study aim
to identify key factors that influence the overall economical costs and carbon footprint of
buildings.
To achieve the goal, the objectives are as follows:
1. Through a literature review identify current best practices, uncertainties and gaps in
both the literature and the Danish building sector.
2. Perform energy- and indoor environment simulations on an office building case
study to identify the most energy efficient setpoints while still maintaining a satis-
factory indoor environment.
3. All case study iterations should meet a baseline indoor environment of EN16798-2
Cat. II for useful comparison.
4. Use the framework of the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) to investigate the carbon
footprint and Life Cycle Cost (LCC) of the case study building for two lifetimes.
5. Take two emission factors for energy into account when calculating the LCA; the
emission of 2023 and prediction for 2030.
6. Consider the impact of occupancy, lighting and equipment schedules by using dif-
ferent national building energy codes load schedules. Include the energy deviation
to the Danish load schedules in the LCA and LCC.
7. Briefly reflect on the impact of critical errors and shortcomings at the practical com-
pletion of the project.

Background
2.1
Indoor environmental quality requirements
Indoor environmental quality (IEQ) is a measure of the thermal environment, humidity and
pollutants in the building. To design a building EN16798-2 (2018) recommends default
criteria for indoor environment. Different categories are recommended for different use
cases: Cat. I is for occupants with special needs, Cat. II is for normal use and operation
and Cat. III is moderately acceptable environment, with some risk of reduced occupant
performance. An exceedance of the recommended category thresholds of 3 % of the
occupied time is acceptable.
2.1.1
Operative temperature
One of the most frequently used indicator for describing the thermal environment is the
operative temperature. An operative temperature is a measure that represents the non-
uniform environment, by taking a weighted average of the radiative and convective heat
transfers (Nielsen, 2018). To comply with category I or II in a landscape office, the op-
erative temperature for heating should be between 21-23°C or 20-24°C respectively and
for cooling, they should be between 23.5-25.5°C or 23-26°C respectively (EN16798-2,
2018).
2.1.2
CO2-concentration
The CO2-concentration above outdoor concentrations should not exceed 800 ppm to
comply with category II. The default average outdoor concentration is assumed to be
400 ppm (EN16798-2, 2018).
2.1.3
Relative humidity
Relative humidity (RH) is a ratio of the water vapor in the air to the amount of water the
air can contain at that given temperature. The recommended range is between 20-80 %
(EN16798-2, 2018).
2.1.4
Predicted mean vote and predicted percentage of dissatisfied
Predicted mean vote (PMV) and predicted percentage of dissatisfied (PPD) are thermal
comfort indicators that describes the thermal state of the body by taking clothing, activity,
air temperature and velocity, mean radiant temperature and humidity into consideration.
To comply with category I, the boundaries for the PMV is between -0.2 to + 0.2, while the
PPD has to be below 6 %. Complying with category II, the range for the PMV is between
-0.5 to +0.5 and PPD has to be below 10 % (EN16798-2, 2018).

2.4
Life Cycle Assessment
A Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is the assessment of the environmental impact, that de-
scribes the impact of a building through its entire life cycle. It can also be used to identify
components that have a significant environmental impact, also known as a hotspot analy-
sis. The different life cycle stages are the product stage A1-3, construction process stage
A4-5, use phase B1-7, end of life C1-4 and potential benefits of disposal D (Masson,
2023). The Danish building regulation requires that the following life cycle stages are in-
cluded in the assessment; A1-A3, B4, B6, C3, C4 and D (BR18, 2024a, §297-§298). The
life cycle stages can be seen in Figure 2.4.
Fig. 2.4: Life cycle stages illustrated. The included life cycle stages
that are required by the Danish building regulation are in colors and the
excluded stages are in grey. Inspired by: BR18 (2024a, Guide 1.2 §250-
§298).
Module D should not be included in the compliance with the threshold, but documented
separately. The regulation requires the documentation of carbon footprint and an assess-
ment period of 50 years (BR18, 2024a, §297-§298). This period is also known as the
Reference Study Period (RSP) and it represents the expected service life of the building
(Hauschild et al., 2018, Chapter 28).
The Danish building regulation have included a guide that describes whether or not the
building components are required to be included in a Danish LCA. The list includes build-
ing components in the following categories: Building base, primary building components,
complementary building components, surface construction parts, plumbing, Electrical and
mechanical installations, inventory and technical equipment and lastly Planting and paving
(BR18, 2024b, Annex 2, Table 6).
To improve the reliability of the LCA results Environmental Product Declarations (EPD’s)
are often used. EPD’s are product specific documentation of the environmental prop-
erties of construction products through their life cycle (EPD Danmark, 2021d). Though

2.5
Life Cycle Cost
Life Cycle Cost (LCC) is the sum of costs added through the entire life cycle. In build-
ing construction it includes the cost of design and planning, construction, operation and
maintenance and demolishing. It is assumed that the costs that occur upstream in the
supply chain are represented in the price of the product.
There are three types of LCC; Conventional LCC, Environmental LCC (eLCC) and Soci-
etal LCC (sLCC). The Conventional LCC is a purely economic evaluation meant to support
decision making. It only includes internal cost such as the purchase price, maintenance
and operational costs over the life span. The Environmental LCC uses similar system
boundaries, functional unit, and methodological steps and covers all costs associated
with the life cycle as a traditional LCA. It includes internal cost and can include external
Sustainability through Simulations
7
==PAGE:21==
costs such as cost of environmental impact like taxes on pollution, though careful mea-
sures have to be taken to avoid double counting the environmental impacts from the LCA.
Lastly, Societal LCC monetarises social impact of other externalities, including both envi-
ronmental impacts and social impacts. This assessment should not be combined with an
LCA as it could be subject to double counting (Hauschild et al., 2018, Chapter 15).
Life Cycle Cost (LCC) in combination with LCA make up two of the three pillars of a
Life Cycle Sustainability Assessment (LCSA), with the last pillar being Social Life Cycle
Assessment (SLCA). An important factor to consider is that cost data can be gathered
in different currencies and reflect different time periods and it is therefore important to
translate it using appropriate exchange and discount rates to a common currency based
on a common year (Swarr et al., 2011).

3
Literature review of recent studies
3.1
Life Cycle Assessment in office buildings
A lot of studies have focused on LCA in buildings and the carbon footprint of its constituent
elements (Rabani et al., 2021; Shindo et al., 2023; Simonen et al., 2017; Wolf et al., 2015;
Zimmermann et al., 2021).
A similar study by Shindo et al. (2023) combined IEQ, energy and LCA through simulation.
They found a primary energy consumption of 19 kWh/m2/year and 12.6 kWh/m2/year for
their office building, compliant with a Cat. II IEQ from EN16798-1, for a VAV system and
TABS, respectively. The energy consumption using static emission factors from 2023 only
included the energy of HVAC systems of heating, cooling, fans and pumps. Their whole
life carbon emissions was 10.1 kg CO2-eq./m2/year for VAV and 9 kg CO2-eq./m2/year
for TABS. They found that their operational energy use represented 29% and 18% of the
total carbon emissions for their office building with a VAV system and TABS respectively.
Zimmermann et al. (2021) conducted a study on existing buildings, including 22 Danish
office buildings, and found that the embodied emissions constituted around two to seven
times more than the operational energy use, with the average being between three to
four times for a lifetime of 50 years. They found that the average annual whole life carbon
emission was 9.5 kg CO2-eq./m2/year. One assumption was however, when data was
lacking, that the contribution from HVAC was based on median values (0.46 kg CO2-
eq./m2/year) from the available cases. They used gradually decreasing emission factors
from 2015 to 2050.
Simonen et al. (2017) set out to create a benchmarking database from existing data for
embodied carbon emissions from the LCA stages A1-A5 of constructions excluding HVAC
systems. The criteria for being included in the database was to report area and embodied
carbon impacts, which leaves out important information as what LCA stages in cradle-
to-construction are included. They reported that more than 50 % of the studies explicitly
excluded stage A4-A5. The database included 362 office buildings with various building
scopes. The most common was to include structure and foundation, the second was
structure only and the third included structure, foundation, enclosure and interior. The
median of the three building scopes was 375 kg CO2-eq./m2, 438 kg CO2-eq./m2 and 400
kg CO2-eq./m2 correspondingly.
Wolf et al. (2015) also looked at embodied whole life carbon emissions of 200 existing con-
structions and found that the building structure of 22 office buildings resulted in between
130-340 kg CO2-eq./m2.
Trigaux et al. (2021) analysed 23 existing benchmarking systems with different LCA frame-
works and systems boundaries. They found an average embodied carbon emission for
non-residential buildings of around 8 kg CO2-eq./m2/year with a whole life carbon emis-
sion of 25 kg CO2-eq./m2/year.
Rabani et al. (2021) looked at the resulting whole life carbon emissions including con-
struction, using a 60 year lifespan, when retrofitting a building and found that their all air
system produced 354 kg CO2-eq./m2 and the radiator system produced around 330 kg
CO2-eq./m2. Through their retrofitting calculations they found a reduction in the opera-
tional energy use by 69-73% of the initial 77% contribution, leaving the operational energy
use to be around 22% of the total emissions.

Ylmén et al. (2019) investigated the environmental impact of including HVAC systems and
found that the embodied carbon emissions (A1–A3) constituted 38 kg CO2-eq./m2 and the
use stage (B4 and B6) carbon emissions 100 kg CO2-eq./m2 using a 50 year study period.
Although the findings of Ding (2020) also show that the use phase had the most impact,
their literature revealed that improvements to technologies and renewable energy produc-
tion, had shifted the focus from operational energy use to upstream emissions of material
processes and energy flows. When making comparisons they stress the importance of
transparency of goal, scope and system boundaries in LCA studies and that for direct
comparison it is important to adhere to a standardized LCA framework.
Astle et al. (2023) also recognize the importance of comparing studies with corresponding
life cycle stages, system boundaries and building elements. Through a comparison of cal-
culating the carbon emissions of a theoretical building using Danish (EPDs) and Swedish
(generic data) LCA frameworks and data, they found the results to be significantly different
even when reducing the scope of the comparison and that caution should be taken before
making any comparisons between studies. Rasmussen et al. (2022) argue that applying
generic data to the LCA introduce uncertainties and potential biases and a factor of 1.3
should thus be used to offset these uncertainties.
3.2
Reference study period predictions
Though the building regulation have a fixed requirement for the life cycle period of the
building, the actual lifetime of the building is an important factor for accurate assess-
ments. Based on 104,927 cases of demolition in Denmark, a study by Andersen and
Negendahl (2023) found the lifespan prediction of existing building typologies. For newer
office buildings built between 1990 and 2010 the lifespan was predicted to be 41 years.
The average lifespan of office buildings demolished between 2010 and 2019 was 61 years
and the standard building lifespan used in LCA for Danish offices was 80 years.
Trigaux et al. (2021) looked at the RSP of different benchmarking systems for LCA and
found that the Danish systems used 50, 80 and 120 years. They found, based on 23
benchmarking systems, that the preferred RSP was between 50-60 years. For bench-
marking Rasmussen et al. (2022) included 60 Danish cases that all had a study period of
50 years, they do however argue that the choice of RSP is context dependent and thus
no general recommendation is made. They also state that a shorter time span of 30 years
may align with the shorter study period used in life cycle costing.
3.3
Occupant behaviours influence on energy consumption
Though a lot of uncertainties occur in an LCA, there are also often discrepancies between
the energy simulations and actual performance of the building. A study by Andersen
(2012) of 290 identical houses, found that the energy consumption varied by a factor
of 20, between 10-200 kWh/m2. They also investigated 35 apartments and found that
the standard deviation of the heating consumption was significantly greater if the tenants
moved. Leaving the conclusion that occupant behavior has a significant impact on the
energy consumption in the building.
Buso et al. (2015) implemented occupant behavior in their simulations. They tested their
models using a probabilistic approach for opening windows and drawing blinds, which
accounted for the stochastic nature of occupant behavior. They simulated each of their
15 models using 10 different probability schedules across three geographical locations.
Their study found that the probabilistic simulation resulted in higher energy consumption’s
than the standard deterministic simulations. In regards to robustness of the building, they

found that a heavy mass, small windows and fixed solar shadings were more robust to
occupant behavior. They also found that the building envelope’s response to the climate
affected the occupant behavior and the energy performance most in countries with large
seasonal differences.
3.4
Life Cycle Assessment and Life Cycle Cost
Combining LCA and LCC is a quantitative approach to help aid decision making and is
often done by allocating a compensation cost to carbon emissions, to aid the choice of
carbon neutrality. The combination has often been used in renovation projects to assess
the best strategy and reduce operational energy (Dejaco et al., 2020; Giorgi et al., 2019;
Ropo et al., 2023).
Dejaco et al. (2020) combined LCA and LCC to identify a method for combining environ-
mental impact and economy to attribute monetary value to the LCA. They analysed four
scenarios: Timber frame wall system, cross laminated timber (CLT), prefabricated con-
crete panels and masonry. Their initial results showed that the concrete scenario had a
higher cost and greater carbon impact through its life cycle. Another finding was that CLT
had a greater cost but half the carbon footprint of masonry. Generally they found that
60 % of the total cumulative cost came from the direct construction cost, with the second
most impact came from repair, replacement and cleaning.
They then introduced two approaches of attributing monetary value to the LCA, one where
they introduced a carbon tax and one that introduced a virtual pollution prevention cost
based on 135 €/tCO2 from the IPCC. The carbon tax constituted 5 % of the total life
cycle cost whereas the average prevention cost represent 20 % of initial investments.
Their findings showed little incentive on decision making when applying carbon tax, but
considerable impact when applying a prevention cost. They conclude that more accurate
life cycle inventory data such as EPDs are needed to reduce the margin of error introduced
by generic data.
The study did however have discrepancies between the system boundaries and stages
used in LCA and LCC. They included repair, replacement and cleaning in the LCC, while
only including replacement in the LCA. In all cases they excluded the operational energy
consumption and technical systems in both LCA and LCC.
Ropo et al. (2023) investigated the LCA and LCC of on-site renewable energy technolo-
gies and optimized LCC in order to reduce the operational energy and consequently car-
bon emissions. Their findings showed that it was necessary to include carbon compen-
sation to reach carbon neutrality, even though it only accounted for 0.3-1.9 % of the total
LCC. They conclude that to further develop the methodology for a cost-optimal carbon-
neutral building design, other building components such as the envelope should be in-
cluded.
3.5
Impact of critical errors and shortcomings
Multiple studies have acknowledged the importance and potential impact of errors on
the LCA and LCC (Andreasson, 2023; Bjerring, 2024; de Place Hansen et al., 2021;
Svendsen, 2024). Few studies are available when it comes to the impact on LCA and
LCC by critical errors and shortcomings at the practical completion of the project.
A report by Strobech (2024) summarized the reported errors leading to construction dam-
age in new Danish buildings, in 2023. Of the 46 reported errors, 38 % of the errors
occurred in the exterior walls. Errors in the external walls were also found to be the most
prominent cause of construction damage in 2021 and 2022.
Sustainability through Simulations
11
==PAGE:25==
One study was found to focus on LCA in combination with construction errors. Looking
at global warming, Ryberg et al. (2022) found that it was advantageous to correct the
construction errors and that uncorrected errors resulted in a 25 % increase in emissions
due to heat loss. However, the study does have limitations to the applicability of the
current study, as it was based on one case in Greenland.
Most literature focused on the impact of design errors such as Peansupap and Ly (2015).
They investigated the impact of design errors between structural- and other building com-
ponents in building construction projects in the capital of Cambodia. It was found that
the critical error with the highest impact occur between structure and mechanical works.
They conclude that though these errors were identified as having the most impact, the
frequency of which these design errors occurred in building construction projects was not
determined. In their literature review they found a cost increase due to design errors of
around 14.2 %.
Another study, by Baiburin (2017), investigated the defects and frequency of errors in the
construction stage mainly in Russia. Through their literature review, they found that the
greatest number of defects relate to the building structure. The frequency of defects in
construction, by type of structures, was 7 % for wood and 17 % for reinforced concrete.
With the major causes of the defects in reinforced concrete being; deviation from the
project, damage to elements and improper treatments of joints.

4
Methodology
In order to determine the key factors that influence the overall economical costs and car-
bon footprint, a simulation based case study of an office building was performed. A visual
overview of the workflow used in the study can be seen in Figure 4.1.
Fig. 4.1: The iterative workflow of this study.
Firstly a layout for the office building was chosen along with two construction assemblies
of varying thermal mass and two different active systems. The loads, parameters and
initial set points were then determined and inserted into IDA ICE. A dynamic energy sim-
ulations was performed on the four cases using Danish load schedules for occupancy,
light and equipment to determine their heating and cooling needs. They were evaluated
based on energy and Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ). When the models complied with
IEQ-requirements and the energy consumption was minimized the energy consumption
was then used for dimensioning the active systems. Another iterative process proceeded
where the set points were adjusted to further minimize energy consumption, while still
maintaining the IEQ for Cat. II. The models were then tested using different load sched-
ules. The energy consumption of the Danish load schedules along with the resulting
maximum and minimum energy consumption of these schedules were then used to cal-
culate the operational emissions and cost for the different cases. Here the impact of using
different emission factors and lifetimes of the building was explored. The Life Cycle As-
Sustainability through Simulations
13
==PAGE:27==
sessment (LCA) and Life Cycle Cost (LCA) were then calculated for two constructions and
combined with the operational energy to show the results for the entire building. The crit-
ical errors and shortcomings at the construction project handover, was briefly discussed
due to their impact on cost and emissions.
An overview of the case study methodology can be seen in Figure 4.2. The figure shows
the different model configurations of the office and analysis applied to the cases.
Fig. 4.2: An overview of the general methodology flow.
The software used for dynamic building performance simulation was IDA ICE 5.0. IDA
ICE is a detailed and dynamic simulation software capable of assessing multi-zone mod-
els and whole-year analysis periods when calculating thermal indoor climate and energy
consumption of the entire building (EQUA, 2023a). In the early stage design when no
room units have been specified, the software allows the user to use ideal room units with
a maximum capacity that can cover all heating or cooling needs (EQUA, 2013). The IEQ
and energy consumption was exported to Excel for further data analysis.
The data processing was performed in Excel, which is a spreadsheet program designed
to format, organize, calculate and analyse data (Gillis, 2021). Excel was also used to
calculate the emissions and cost of the operational energy consumption.
When calculating the Life Cycle Assessment, the Global Warming Potential (GWP) was
acquired from Environmental Product Declarations (EPD’s) to calculate the building’s
carbon emissions. Product specific data was mostly obtained from the EPD Danmark
database (EPD Danmark, 2021d) with supplementary data from Ökobaudat (2023). EPD
Danmark (2021d) is a Danish organisation that provides product specific EPDs of build-
ing materials in compliance with EN15804, while Ökobaudat (2023) is a German database
that provides standardized ecological evaluations of building materials, construction, trans-
port, energy and disposal processes.
Excel was also utilized when calculating the total Life Cycle Cost of the building con-
struction. The product specific cost data was obtained through various websites (see
Section 4.5.1) with supplementary generic data obtained from Molio Prisdata: New con-
struction (Professional parts (blue database)) accessed through Sigma Enterprize EG A/S
(2024).

4.1.1
Office layout
The case study is a geometry featuring a section of a larger multistory office building,
placed between two floors. It contains two open plan offices of 100 m2 separated by a 40

parameters.

The window to floor ratio (WFR) was chosen to be 15 %, which correlates to a window to
wall ratio (WWR) of 25 % including the frame. This corresponds to an average daylight
factor of 2.3 % with the calculation plane height at 0.85 m. The total window area was
divided into five windows of 2 x 1.5 m (w x h) for each exterior wall, with an even spacing.

U-value for the frame from IDA ICE of 2 W/m2K. This results in total U-value of the window
of 0.65 W/m2K.
Two construction types were chosen: Light construction and heavy construction. The light
construction consists of wooden structures and can be seen in Table 4.1, while the heavy

and the slab thickness. To eliminate uncertainties between the two constructions, the U-

The Design Reference Year 2001-2010 specified by the Danish Building Regulations was
used as the basis for the simulations. The datasets were first presented in the technical
report by Wang et al. (2013) and it contains hourly values for ten weather data param-
eters and daily values for one parameter, combined representing a typical year in Den-

in a dataset for a complete calendar year. The location for the data was chosen to be
Sjælsmark.
4.1.6
Active systems
Two active system configurations were chosen. The first configuration was heating through
radiators placed below each window, with ventilation and cooling performed by the air
handling unit (AHU). The second configuration used the AHU for ventilation requirements
and the latent load (wet bulb temperature) while the active beams (ACB) with a four-pipe
design handles the sensible load (dry bulb temperature change) of heating and cooling.
Both configurations used variable air volume (VAV) with temperature and CO2 control.
The AHU is equipped with a heat exchanger, that heats up the supply air with the return
air. The first configuration with radiators was called VAV+RAD and the second with active
beams was called VAV+ACB.
In order to dimension the active technical systems the cases were modeled in IDA ICE.
The construction, internal loads and domestic hot water (DHW) were inserted and an
ideal heater and cooler were used to determine the heating and cooling demand of the
building. Here the energy consumption and IEQ were loosely assessed to determine
the initial setpoints and controls. The peak demands of the light construction were 5.92
kW and 7.38 kW for heating and cooling respectively, while the respective heating and
cooling demands of the heavy construction were 5.76 kW and 7.18 kW. From this the
active systems were dimensioned, tested and the setpoints and controls were further
adjusted. The method for assessing the IEQ and energy consumption is described in
Section 4.2.
The setpoints and controls of the two active systems were tested in both light and heavy
construction. However, since the optimal setpoints for best IEQ and lowest energy con-
sumption were mostly the same for both constructions, the setpoints and active systems
were chosen based on the simulations of the light construction. This also made it possible
to directly assess the results between the light and heavy construction.
The AHU was dimensioned based on the ventilation rate for cooling at 859 L/s and was
the same for both configurations. The systemair configurator was used to find the specific
AHU type (Systemair, 2024b). The AHU used had a specific fan power (SFP) of 1.47
kJ/m3 and heat exchanger effectiveness of 82.1 %.

4.2
Indoor Environmental Quality and energy optimization
To evaluate and optimize the IEQ and energy consumption an iterative process occurred.
This involved adjusting different setpoints and controls, such as AHU temperature, heating
and cooling setpoints, light control, AHU fan schedule etc..
In order to evaluate the results and make the configurations comparable they should all
meet a baseline IEQ of Cat. II. This meant that the occupancy time outside the criteria
should not exceed 3 % (EN16798-2, 2018). While this was the case, the criteria was
chosen to be complied as close to 100 % as possible. The thermal comfort was evaluated
based on the operative temperature throughout the year, while the average, minimum and
maximum CO2-concentration and relative humidity was used to determine if the building
complied with the comfort range.
Though IDA ICE is not a validated tool to show the compliance with the Danish energy
frame, the value was used as a reference for comparison. Since the model was only a
section of the building, the target value for the yearly primary energy use was chosen as
41 kWh/m2.

4.5
Life Cycle Cost
One of the key aspects of combining LCA and LCC is to refer to a consistent definition of
the product system and apply consistent system boundaries and thus the Environmental
LCC described in Section 2.5 was used along with the systems boundary from Section 2.4,
also seen in Figure 4.7. And thus for the purpose of this study, only the acquisition cost
of materials along with the cost of energy was included. The cost of materials consists of
the initial cost of construction materials and technical systems and the cost of replacing
and disposing the materials at the end of their lifetime.
The total cost is separated into two categories, the total cost of construction materials and
the cost of operational energy use through the entire lifetime of the building. An thus like
with LCA a system boundary is made excluding the construction process stage and thus
wages and rent of heavy construction equipment is neglected. The wages and rent of
heavy construction equipment is also neglected for the demolishing stage.

5.4
Life cycle assessment
In this section the results of the LCA will be evaluated for a lifespan of 41 years and the
required 50 years from the Danish Building Regulation. The results will be shown as total
yearly emissions and accumulated emissions throughout the lifespan of the building.
The average whole life carbon emissions of the four scenarios using 2023 emission factors
and a RSP of 50 years resulted in a total of 9.1 kg CO2-eq./m2/year. The distribution
of emissions was 41.2 % from operational emissions, 11.6 % from embodied emissions
from HVAC and 47.2 % from embodied emissions from construction. Using 2023 emission
factors and the same RSP of 50 years, the total decreased to 6.6 kg CO2-eq./m2/year with
operational emissions comprising 19.1 %, embodied emissions from HVAC comprising
15.9 % and embodied emissions from construction comprising 65 %.
When looking at a shorter RSP of 41 years with 2023 emission factors, the total whole life
carbon emissions increased to 10.3 kg CO2-eq./m2/year with the distribution of 36.5 %
operational emissions, 12.5 % embodied emissions from HVAC and 51 % embodied emis-
sions from construction. Again, the average whole life carbon emissions decrease with
the use of 2030 emission factors to 7.8 kg CO2-eq./m2/year with operational emissions
being 16.2 %, embodied emissions from HVAC being 16.5 % and embodied emissions
from construction being 67.3 %.
Operational emissions (B6) were found to have a significant impact on the total LCA when
using 2023 emission factors, as seen in Figure 5.5. By introducing 2030 emission factors,
the operational impact was substantially reduced. Thus, with light constructions, the envi-
ronmental impact was then more substantially impacted by replacements (B4) and waste

In this section the results of the LCC was evaluated for a service life of 41 years and 50
years following the system boundaries of the LCA framework. The results are shown as
total annual cost and accumulated cost throughout the lifespan of the building.
The annual life cycle cost was divided into the initial cost and replacement cost of the
construction and HVAC systems and the operational cost and cost of disposal. The annual
life cycle cost can be seen in Figure 5.10, while the total life cycle cost can be seen in
Figure B.6.
The average annual whole life cost of the four scenarios with a RSP of 50 years resulted
in a total of 353.4 DKK/m2/year with a distribution of 8.5 % from operational energy con-
sumption, 22.6 % from initial and replacement cost of HVAC systems, 67.6 % from ini-
tial and replacement cost of constructions and 1.2 % from disposal. When looking at a
shorter RSP of 41 years the annual whole life cost increased to 424.6 DKK/m2/year with
the distribution comprising of 7.1 % from operational energy consumption, 23.0 % from
initial and replacement cost of HVAC systems, 68.7 % from initial and replacement cost
of constructions and 1.2 % from disposal.
Generally the light construction scenarios had a lower annual cost than the heavy con-
struction scenarios. This was mainly due to the high initial construction cost. When look-
ing at a RSP of 50 years, the initial construction cost of the light constructions constituted
around 30 % of the total cost, while the heavy constructions comprised around 40 %.
Combining construction and HVAC cost, the initial cost share of the light construction
scenarios was around 42 % of the total cost and 51 % of the total cost for the heavy con-
struction configurations. The construction replacements comprised of 35 % and 30 % of
the total cost for light and heavy construction scenarios, respectively. While the total cost
of replacements contributed around 47 % and 41 % for light and heavy construction con-
figurations, respectively. The operational share of the total cost constituted only around
8-9 % for both heavy and light constructions, while cost of disposal only constituted around
1 % of the total cost.
Since the light and heavy constructions were the same between their respective mechan-
ical systems, the initial and replacement cost was also the same in both constructions.
The technical systems were also identical between the light and heavy construction, but
the cost differ slightly due to different total floor areas of the light and heavy construction.
Comparing the two lifespans of 41 and 50 years, it can be seen that the annual cost was
around 20 % higher for the shorter lifespan. This was due to the allocated annual cost of
the construction and HVAC systems with a long RSL.

Discussion
Due to the impact on operational carbon emissions from simulation setpoints and con-
trols, it was important to evaluate the thermal comfort as a prerequisite to ensure that the
operational energy consumption was not artificially lowered. The IEQ requirements with
Danish load schedules were met with 100 % compliance of the thermal comfort threshold
of Cat. II. However, the relative humidity exceeded the minimum requirement of Cat. II in
more than 7.4 % of the occupied hours. This could be due to high air change. Since the
simulations were made using a crossflow heat exhanger this issue could be improved by
using a rotary heat exchanger, as it more effectively can recover air humidity.
The HVAC systems were designed using Danish load schedules and were found to suf-
ficiently handle the changes in load of different load schedules.
The implementation
showed little change in thermal comfort compliance with Cat. II but large variation when
simulating the energy consumption of the building. The setpoints and controls were de-
signed to comply with Cat. II. However, if the thermal comfort was evaluated based on Cat.
I, an effect on compliance could be seen. Though the load schedules do not represent
realistic dynamic occupancy behavior they indicate an impact that increase the primary
energy consumption of up to 38.5 %. Another substantial impact on the the primary en-
ergy consumption came from the lighting power, which was found to have a contributing
of up to 41 % and thus it is important to implement dynamic lighting control based on
daylight availability.
Although IDA ICE is not a validated tool to show the compliance with the Danish energy
frame the results were compared to the threshold of 41 kWh/m2. The Danish load sched-
ules resulted in a compliance with the threshold for all scenarios, with the highest primary
energy consumption being 38.8 kWh/m2/year with VAV+ACB. However, when implement-
ing other load schedules only 1 to 5 resulted in compliance with the threshold between all
building configurations.
Since this study had low transmission losses through the envelope a high cooling con-
sumption and low heating consumption was expected. This hypothesis was confirmed as
the cooling consumption had the second most impact on the energy consumption. The
findings were in contrast to those of Shindo et al. (2023), which showed a trend of high
heating- and low cooling consumption. Looking into the cause of the discrepancies, the
geometry of their office was also facing south and the U-value of the facade was also low
at 0.05 W/m2K. The results could however be due to the high U-value of their glazing at
2.0 W/m2K and very low solar heat gain coefficient of 36 %. A visual comparison of the
simulated primary energy consumption of the HVAC systems of pumps, fans, heating and
cooling can be seen in Figure 6.1.

The literature review revealed a lack of data regarding the impact of construction errors
on LCA and LCC at the practical completion of the project.
Different problem areas were identified, with Strobech (2024) indicating an issue in the
exterior wall, Baiburin (2017) identifying that the building structure had the greatest num-
ber of defects and Peansupap and Ly (2015) found that the critical error with the highest
impact occurred between structure and mechanical works. An impact on LCA was found
in Greenland with a 25 % increase in carbon emissions (Ryberg et al., 2022). A cost in-
crease due to design errors was identified to be around 14.2 % (Peansupap & Ly, 2015).
Though the literature lacked relevant studies it indicated important areas for future inves-
tigations.
A potential bias in this study could have been introduced for the light construction as both
the cost and emissions were based on the individual components and not prefabricated
elements like the heavy construction wall. This could skew the results as the cost and
emissions would increase do to the fabrication.
Based on the literature it was expected that the high thermal mass case had a smaller
temperature variation along with higher cost and greater carbon impact. This was con-
firmed. It was also found that the use of different load schedules had more impact on the
LCA than the LCC.

7
Conclusion
The impact on IEQ, energy, LCA and LCC was investigated for an office building case
study with varying thermal mass, technical systems and internal loads. The LCA and
LCC was investigated for two RSP of 41 and 50 years. In addition, the LCA also included
the evaluation of the operational emission factors of 2023 and 2030. The goal was to
identify key influential factors, that if mitigated, could reduce the initial and operational
costs and environmental impact.
The thermal comfort was evaluated through an iterative process, when determining set-
points and controls. The thermal comfort with Danish load schedules resulted in 100 %
compliance with Cat. II. It was determined that the IEQ should be evaluated as a prereq-
uisite to ensure realistic energy consumptions.
The energy consumption was found to be higher for light construction than heavy con-
struction with the same HVAC system. In each respective construction type, the primary
energy consumption for the VAV+ACB scenarios were higher than with VAV+RAD. A sub-
stantial contribution to the energy consumption was found to come from lighting power
making up between 37-41 % of the total energy consumption. The impact of different oc-
cupancy, lighting and equipment load schedules was investigated. It showed little change
in thermal comfort compliance with Cat. II, but an increase in primary energy consumption
of up to 38.5 % was found using English load schedules.
The impact of RSP and emission factors was explored. The total whole life carbon emis-
sions were found to be 9.1 kg CO2-eq./m2/year, 6.6 kg CO2-eq./m2/year, 10.3 kg CO2-
eq./m2/year and 7.8 kg CO2-eq./m2/year in the respective order of; 50 year RSP & 2023
emission factors, 50 year RSP & 2030 emission factors, 41 year RSP & 2023 emission
factors and 41 year RSP & 2030 emission factors. In the same order, the average oper-
ational emissions were found to contribute 41.2 %, 19.1 %, 36.5 % and 16.2 % of total
whole life carbon emissions. With increased energy consumption using the English load
schedules, the average contribution was 48.8 %, 23.0 %, 42.8 % and 19.2 %, in chrono-
logical order.
The impact of RSP and load schedules was also investigated on LCC. The annual whole
life cost was found to be 353.4 DKK/m2/year and 424.6 DKK/m2/year for a RSP of 50 and
41 years, respectively. The impact on the total cost from operational energy consumption
was 8.5 % and 7.1 % for a RSP of 50 and 41 years, respectively. The heavy construction
was found to have a higher initial cost, but a slightly lower replacement and operational
cost. The introduction of the English load schedules resulted in a negligible cost increase
during the entire lifespan of less than 0.1 %. Introducing a carbon tax to encourage carbon
neutrality showed little economic incentive for decision making and only increasing the
total cost by 3.1 % and 2.4 % using 2023 and 2030 emission factors, respectively.
Overall the findings showed that the high thermal mass case had a smaller temperature
variation along with higher cost and greater carbon impact. Another discovery was that
the changes in energy consumption, due to the use of different load schedules, had more
impact on the LCA than the LCC.
Through a literature review a gap was found with regards to the impact of construction
errors on the LCA and LCC at the practical completion of the project.

The overall key factors and strategies to reduce emissions were identified to be:
• Implementing dynamic lighting control based on daylight availability.
• The change in load schedules indicate an impact from occupancy behavior that
could increase the primary energy consumption. Since the impact was more promi-
nent on the LCA than the LCC a monertary incentive could be introduced.
• A reduction in thermal mass of the building would reduce the cost and emissions.
However, the construction with high thermal mass showed most stability in thermal
comfort and a reduction in energy consumption. A compromise between the two
could be to reduce the amount of concrete in non important constructions like the
partition walls, to create a hybrid construction.
The methodology used in this study can be used for future works to effectively evaluate
IEQ, energy, LCA and LCC.

A.3
Ventilation rates
The calculations of the ventilation rates is based on the theory from Section 2.2.1. The
floor area of each office is 100 m2.
The ventilation rate of cooling was calculated based on eq. (2.2). The heat loads were
obtained from 12 people at 91.5 W/person resulting in 1098 W from occupants, 960 W
from equipment, 500 W from lighting and a maximum solar heat gain from the IDA ICE
simulation of 5700 W.
q = (1098W + 960W + 500W + 5700W)
1.2kg/m3 · 1006J/(kg K) · 8K
= 855L/s
(A.1)
The minimum ventilation rate was based on Equation (2.1). With 12 occupants and a
ventilation rate per occupant of 7 L/(s pers) and ventilation rate for emissions from building
materials of 0.7 L/(s m2).
qtot = 12 · 7L/(s pers) + 100 · 0.7L/(s m2) = 154L/s
(A.2)
