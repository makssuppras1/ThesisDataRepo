Abstract 
One of the most frequently cause of food-borne disease in humans worldwide, is the pathogenic 
bacterium Salmonella. Among the serovars, Salmonella enterica ssp. enterica serovar Dublin (S. 
Dublin) is the most common in the dairy herds in Denmark. S. Dublin is host-adapted to cattle and 
can cause both economic losses and reduced welfare in cattle herds through abortions and the high 
mortality rate among calves. This thesis is a part of the un-going CalfCare project in Denmark that 
aims to reduce the mortality rate in calves by developing a novel feeding system that includes a sour 
milk product fermented by special lactic acid bacteria (LAB).        
     Neutralized cell free spent medium (nCFSM) were prepared from milk and reconstituted milk 
powder fermented with the strains L. helveticus Z32, L. acidophilus LA-5 and L. rhamnosus LGG 
based on four different fermentation protocols; a 1% and 10% inoculation level with a 6 h and 18 h 
fermentation time. The most effective protocol was a 10% inoculation level of the LAB inoculum in 
milk fermented for 18 h. The nCFSM obtained from fermented reconstituted milk powder resulted 
in less inhibition of the hilA gene expression compared with nCFSM from milk-based fermentation, 
which indicated that fermented reconstituted milk powder may lower the effect on calves when sub-
stituted for fermented milk. In fermented milk L. helveticus Z32 strain showed the highest inhibi-
tion (60%) of hilA gene expression of S. Typhimurium LT2 followed by L. acidophilus LA-5 
(22%), whereas L. rhamnosus LGG showed least inhibitory effect (1%). The inhibitory effect on 
hilA was reproduced in S. Dublin when measuring downregulation of virulence genes by RT-qPCR. 
Further, the RT-qPCR analysis of the inhibitory effects in S. Dublin when grown in nCFSM from L. 
helveticus Z32 showed downregulation of gene expression of ssrB (19-fold), flhD (3-fold), fliC (6-
fold), ssaG (48-fold) and prgI (5-fold).  
   In contrast to the expected, the five peptide fractions reported for L. helveticus LH-2 did not show 
the inhibitory effect of hilA virulence gene to be concentration dependent.    
 
Screening of 62 LAB cultures was performed with the purpose of identifying the LAB strains with 
the strongest inhibitory activity against the expression of hilA. The most effective strains which all 
resulted in at least a 2-fold downregulation of hilA were L. helveticus Z32 (85%), K. varians (70%), 
Lactococcus lactis O224 (61%), Bifidobacterium BB02 (61%), L. helveticus LH 521 (58%), L. 
brevis (57%), L. acidophilus LA3 (55%) and S. carnosus (53%).  
Additionally, the effect of the individual components of the growth media DeMan, Rogosa and 
Sharpe (MRS) showed especially the components diammonium hydrogen citrate and manganese 
sulfate contributed to an enhanced inhibitory effect on the LAB strain L. helveticus Z32 when add-
ing these to milk.

1.   Introduction  
Salmonella is among the most frequently diagnosed cause of food-borne disease in humans world-
wide. The major sources of infection is infected animals and can transmits to humans through con-
taminated water and food, such as meat and eggs (Giaccone et al., 2012). The infection in animals 
can be prevented by minimizing the spread through an effective surveillance and control effort 
(Graziani et al., 2017).  
 
In many of the approximately 2,500 dairy herds in Denmark, neonatal diarrhea and related mortality 
in calves is nowadays a major problem. It constitutes a serious animal welfare and economic burden 
for the farmers (Cho and Yoon, 2014). In Denmark, about 800,000 calves are born annually, of 
which about five percent dies before they are eight weeks old (Anonymous, 2020a). The primary 
causes of diarrhea in calves are the pathogens Escherichia coli, Salmonella, Clostridium 
perfringens and Cryptosporidium parvum (Cho and Yoon, 2014). The two most commonly isolated 
Salmonella serovars in cattle are Salmonella enterica ssp. enterica serovar Dublin (S. Dublin) and 
Salmonella enterica ssp. enterica serovar Typhimurium (S. Typhimurium) (Funke et al., 2017). 
Salmonella can cause diarrhea in both adult cattle, most often S. Typhimurium, and in calves, most 
often S. Dublin where the highest mortality occurs in the 10-day to 3-month old calves (Fossler et 
al., 2005). When humans are infected with S. Dublin through meat it may cause severe blood poi-
soning and the mortality rate of S. Dublin infection in humans is therefore relatively high. About 
15% of the infected people dies and deaths are most often seen among elderly and immunocom-
promised persons (Anonymous, 2020a). A surveillance and control program was introduced back in 
2002 with the purpose of eradicating S. Dublin (Nielsen et al., 2010). Despite the efforts S. Dublin 
still exists in up to 260 Danish herds (Helweigh and Petersen, 2020). The campaign has been ad-
justed a number of times in the subsequent years and is still running. Recently, a new project called 
CalfCare, with the objective to provide a method to reduce halve the incidence of diarrhea and mor-
tality in calves and contribute to a reduction in the current use of 2.5 tons of antibiotic given to 
calves annually (Anonymous, 2020a).  
 
The CalfCare project aims to develop a novel feeding system for calves that includes a sour milk 
product fermented by special lactic acid bacteria (LAB). The idea is to exploit the ability of some 
LAB strains to break the milk protein into bioactive peptides, which specifically target the virulence 
mechanism in pathogenic gut bacteria as described for S. Typhimurium by Tellez et al. (2010).  
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
This master thesis is a part of the CalfCare project and focus on downregulation of virulence genes 
in Salmonella. Part of the pathogenicity of Salmonella is genetically encoded by two major type-
three secretion systems (T3SSs) regulated by DNA binding proteins, hilA and ssrB (Curkic, 2016). 
Research has shown that specific LAB strains are able to downregulate the virulence gene expres-
sion hilA and ssrB of Salmonella (Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2010) under laboratory conditions.  
The work of thesis was to provide protocols for fermentation of milk and reconstituted milk powder 
and thereafter to investigate the ability of selected LAB strains to downregulate expression of the 
virulence gene hilA. The gene expression was investigated using two technologies; A biolumines-
cence assay using a hilA reporter construct hilA::luxCDABE and RT-qPCR for detecting of mRNA.  
2.   Study objectives 
The project works by the hypothesis that a fermented milk product also may target virulence gene 
expression in E. coli and S. Dublin and thereby may be used to increase health in calves with the 
perspective is also to be health improving in other animals and humans. The overall aim of this 
study was to investigate whether LAB starter cultures after fermentation of milk produces com-
pounds able to downregulate the virulence regulatory gene expression hilA in Salmonella, and to 
investigate if this property varies between LAB strains.    
 
The overall aim of the study was investigated through eight specific objectives: 
1. To test the effect of inoculation level and fermentation time in milk on hilA gene expression 
of S. Typhimurium LT2 and to suggest which is more optimal for the final protocol.  
 
2. To compare the capacity of three selected LAB strains to downregulate the virulence gene 
expression of hilA of S. Typhimurium LT2. 
 
3. To test whether milk and reconstituted milk powder fermented with LAB strains have the 
same downregulation effect. 
 
4. To identify individual strains of the mixed starter culture RD-1 and investigate their individ-
ual effects on S. Typhimurium LT2 hilA gene expression.

5. To use RT-qPCR to test downregulation of key virulence genes in S. Dublin among the 
nCFSM from the selected LAB cultures identified in objective 2.  
 
6. To compare the capacity of 62 commercial LAB cultures to downregulate hilA gene expres-

3.   Background  
3.1 Salmonella   
3.1.1 Salmonella characteristics 
Salmonella is a gram-negative, non-spore-forming, rod-shaped and facultative anaerobic motile 
bacterium with peritrichous flagella. Salmonella is closely related to the genus Escherichia, and 
belongs to the family Enterobacteriaceae (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). The size of Salmonella ranges 
from 0.2-1.5 μm in diameter and 2-5 μm in length (Jajere, 2019). Salmonella has the ability to grow 
at a temperature range of 5-47°C with an optimum at 35-37°C (Graziani et al., 2017). Salmonella is 
inactivated by pasteurization at 70°C. Salmonella grow at a pH range between 4-9, with an opti-
mum around 6.5 and 7.5. The bacteria require a water activity (aw) of 0.99- 0.94 to grow but can 
also survive at aw < 0.2 such as in dried foods (Pui et al., 2011). Salmonella do not require NaCl for 
growth, but they can grow in the presence of 0.4-4 % NaCl concentration (Jajere, 2019).         
Salmonella classifications  
Salmonella genus represents the most common foodborne pathogens, frequently isolated from food-
producing animals that are responsible for zoonotic infections in humans and animal species (Jajere, 
2019). Salmonella can be divided into zoonotic strains, human- specific strains and strains that are 
host specific to the individual animal species (Chlebicz and Śliżewska, 2018).   
 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
Bacteria is classified based on the phylogeny, which is a comparison of 16S rRNA or other gene 
sequences (Pui et al., 2011). The Salmonella serotypes are placed under two species: Salmonella 
bongori and Salmonella enterica, where S. enterica is further divided into six subspecies; S. enter-
ica, S. salamae, S. arizonae, S. diarizonae, S. indica, and S. houtenae (Porwollik et al., 2004). Of 
these, only S. enterica is isolated from warm-blooded animals and accounts for more than 99% of 
clinical isolates, whereas the remaining subspecies including S. Bongori are mainly isolated from 
cold-blooded animals and account 1% of clinical isolates (Pui et al., 2011). Salmonella genus is 
classified into 2,579 serovars, among these 2,557 serovars belongs to the S. enterica species 
(Grimont and Weill, 2007) on the basis of the O (lipopolysaccharide [LPS]) surface antigen and H 
(flagella) antigens (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). Salmonella is based on the host preference, where S. 
Typhi, S. Paratyphi A and C, and S. Sendai are termed “host-specific” as they are specific to hu-
mans, and cause severe systemic diseases (Giaccone et al., 2012). S. Typhimurium and S. Dublin 
are serotypes that belong to the subspecies S. enterica. 
3.1.2 Clinical aspect of Salmonella  
Salmonellosis is among the most common foodborne disease and pose serious health problems that 
affect public health and development worldwide. On a global scale it is estimated that 93.8 million 
cases of gastroenteritis caused by Salmonella occur each year, of which an estimated 80.3 million 
are foodborne cases (Finn et al., 2013). Besides, 155.000 death cases are reported annually due to 
Salmonella infections (Majowicz et al., 2010). The major source of infection is production animals 
and mainly indirect through contaminated water and food, such as eggs and meat.  
 
In human disease, the clinical pattern of salmonellosis is causing gastroenteritis usually character-
ized by fever, abdominal pain, nausea and diarrhea (Hurley et al., 2014). Most Salmonella infec-
tions are self-limiting within 4-7 days in healthy adults, but can be life threatening especially to 
children, elderly, pregnant women and immunocompromised individuals. Disease usually occurs 
after the ingestion of more than 50.000 bacteria and after an incubation period of 6-72 hours after 
exposure, depending on the host susceptibility and inoculum (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013).  
 
Typhoid and paratyphoid fever are diseases of the intestinal tract in humans caused by S. Typhi and 
S. Paratyphi. The symptoms of typhoid can include prolonged fever, severe headache, constipation 
or diarrhea whereas paratyphoid fever has milder symptoms and lower mortality rate (Bhutta, 
2006). Infection typically occurs due to ingestion of food or water contaminated with human waste 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
(Pui et al., 2011). S. Typhimurium and S. typhi are closely related about 90% of the genes are iden-
tical. The remaining 10% of the genes that differ are due to virulence factors, which determine their 
pathogenic potential (Tiwari et. al., 2017). In contrast, non-typhoidal Salmonella (NTS) serovars 
have a broad host range that cause diarrheal disease in humans and can infect a wide range of ani-
mals (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). Most nontyphoidal Salmonella infections are caused by S. enterica 
subspecies enterica serotype S. Typhimurium, S. Dublin etc. (Bush and Vazquez-Pertejo, 2020). S. 
Typhimurium causes gastroenteritis leading to diarrhea, vomiting, fever and abdominal cramps.  
3.1.3 Salmonella Dublin 
S. enterica serotype Dublin is a host-adopted bacterium to cattle and is the most common Salmonel-
la serotype in cattle production in Denmark that leads to infected herds. Salmonella infection in 
cattle results in severe diarrhea in calves or typhoid-like illness that often results in a chronic carrier 
state (Fierer and Fleming, 1983). In cattle the Salmonella bacteria are manly shed in faeces and can 
therefore be transmitted from one animal to another by direct contact (Wray and Sojka, 1981). The 
calves that develop infections and survive will become long-term carriers. This can lead to abor-
tions with S. Dublin (Peek et al., 2018). In humans, S. Dublin is the most invasive of the zoonotic 
Salmonella bacteria found in Denmark and can lead to severe disease as it typically causes blood-
stream infection, hospitalization and death (Harvey et al., 2017). The mortality rate of S. Dublin 
infection in humans is therefore relatively high. About 15% dies, and deaths are often seen in elder-
ly and immunocompromised (Anonymous, 2020a). 
The negative effects on cattle health, the financial losses in the infected herds and the severe cases 
of human S. Dublin infections has in 2002 led to the start of a national surveillance and control pro-
gram in all Danish cattle herds. The ultimate goal was to eradicate S. Dublin from Danish cattle 
herds (Nielsen et al., 2010). The mortality rate in dairy calves aged 1 to 180 days was, on average 
8.6% in Denmark in 2007. This was considered a welfare problem, and therefore the Danish Cattle 
Federation started a campaign to reduce calf mortality to 6.5% by end of 2009 (Nielsen et al., 
2010). The control program has been through major and minor changes during the years. All cattle 
herds in Denmark have been classified on the basis of milk- and blood samples into three levels; 
level 1 (assumed free for S. Dublin), level 2 (suspected infected with S. Dublin) and level 3 (infect-
ed with S. Dublin). The newest data stated that 2,453 herds are probably free for S. Dublin, despite 
the effort there are still 260 herds in level 3 (Helweigh and Petersen, 2020).

3.2 Salmonella Typhimurium virulence factors  
There is general lack of knowledge regarding specific S. Dublin virulence factors as most literature 
focus on S. Typhimurium virulence factors. To have an understanding of the virulence genes of S. 
Dublin, virulence factors of S. Typhimurium are described in this section.   
Pathogenic bacteria have specific strategies for entry into the host cell including survival and multi-
plication within the cell. Salmonella infection is initiated when contaminated foods pass through the 
stomach (Jones et al., 1992). The bacteria that survives the acidic environment in the stomach colo-
nize the Peyer’s patches in the intestine and penetrate the gut barrier through M cells (specialized 
epithelial cells) ((Marcus et al., 2000).  
S. Typhimurium possesses many virulence strategies to overcome the pathogenic process by inter-
act with the host defense mechanisms. Virulence is associated with the ability to invade and survive 
within host cells (Knodler and Steele-Mortimer, 2003). Pathogenicity islands (PAI) are large chro-
mosomal regions that are present in pathogenic bacteria and confer virulence properties. PAI’s are 
characterized by (I) their large size (several kb), (II) a base composition that is different to the core 
genome (e.g. lower percentage of bases G+C), (III) association with ‘mobile DNA elements’, (IV) 
genetic instability that leads to loss of PAI, (V) frequent association with genes encoding tRNA, 
(VI) the presence of one or more virulence genes (Gal-Mor and Finlay, 2006). S. Typhimurium 
pathogenesis relies on the virulence factors that are encoded on different genomic islands. Two im-
portant are termed Salmonella pathogenicity islands 1 and 2 (SPI1 and SPI2) (Bayoumi and 
Griffiths, 2010). Further virulence genes are located on the pathogenicity islands SPI3, SPI4 and 
SPI5 on the chromosomal region, which have different roles in gastrointestinal and systematic 
pathogenesis (Hensel, 2000). The five SPIs have identified to be clearly involved in S. Typhimuri-
um virulence together with further virulence components such as the pSLT plasmid-carried spv op-
eron, several types of adhesins, flagella and the essential components for biofilm formation 
(Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). The infection depends on the ability of S. Typhimurium to invade and 
grow within the host cells which requires the activities of numerous virulence proteins encoded by 
up to 4% of the genome (Figueira and Holden, 2012).  
3.2.1 Salmonella Pathogenicity island 1 (SPI-1)        
The systemic infection of Salmonella requires two important steps, invasion of the intestinal epithe-
lial cells and growth by multiplying inside phagocytic leukocytes. Both steps are dependent on the 
activity of numerous virulence proteins. The invasion into host cells relies on SPI-1, a 40 kb island 
located at 63 centisomes (cs) on the S. Typhimurium chromosome (Galán, 1996). This genomic 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
region has an overall GC content of 42% (Marcus et al., 2000). SPI-1 encodes various components 
of a type III secretion system (T3SS-1), which is a multi-component system that forms a protein 
transport channel through the two bacterial membranes by a needle complex (Bayoumi and 
Griffiths, 2010). T3SS-1 enable bacteria to translocate virulence proteins, also called effector pro-
teins into the host cell to specifically interfere with the host cell (Lou et al., 2019). The needle com-
plex crosses over the bacterial envelope as seen on Figure 1, where a needle-like extension (made 
up by PrgI) protrudes from the inner and outer membrane of Salmonella to the host cell membrane 
(Lou et al., 2019). The prg/org and inv/spa operons encode the needle complex, whereas the sic/sip 
operons encode the effector proteins and the translocon (SipBCD) (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). The 
needle complex is combined of a multiring base (that in S. Typhimurium is made up by the proteins 
InvG, PrgH and PrgK), a central inner rod (PrgI) and the protruding needle-like structure. One of 
the essential components of the needle complex is a group of membrane proteins (InvA, SpaP, 
SpaQ, SpaR and SpaS) that compose the so-called export apparatus, important for the stability and 
assembly of the needle complex (Wagner et al., 2010). The needle tip proteins that bind to the end 
of the T3SS needle play an important role in the process. SipD from S. Typhimurium interacts with 
the corresponding needle subunit PrgI to make the needle tip complex and regulate the secretion of 
effectors from Salmonella into the host cell (Lunelli et al., 2011). Upon host cell contact, the protein 
SipD forms a platform for the assembly of translocon proteins (SipB and SipC) and interacts with 
their N-terminal ectodomains (Kaur et al., 2016). These translocons, that are encoded by SPI-1 play 
an important role in Salmonella contact with the host cells, invasion and colonization of intestinal 
epithelial cells (Knodler and Steele-Mortimer, 2003).

3.2.2 Regulators of SPI-1  
Controlling the expression of SPI-1 is a crucial process during the progress of the infection. The 
SPI-1 genes are highly regulated by DNA- binding proteins including the AraC regulators hilD, 
hilC and RtsA. The main transcriptional regulator of SPI-1 T3SS is hilA that binds directly to viru-
lence operons and activate their expression. hilA has been shown to be able to induce the invasion 
by inducing expression of the inv/spa operon (Bailly-Bechet et al., 2011). The regulation of the ex-
pression of hilA is controlled by a complex feed-forward loop that involves three transcriptional 
activators; HilD, hilC and RtsA. Of these three, hilD has the predominant role, while hilC and RtsA 
acts as amplifiers of the inducing signals (Cott Chubiz et al., 2010). HilD, hilC and RtsA can acti-
vate their own, each other’s and hilA expression by forming a triangular network system and regu-
lating each other positively, while hilE can regulate by acting as a negative regulator of hilD 
(Bailly-Bechet et al., 2011) (Figure 2). The hilE protein will repress the expression of the SPI-1 
gene when it is binding with hilD. HilD will be inactivated and thereby prevent the activation of 
hilA (Paredes-Amaya et al., 2018). HilD also controls the expression of other virulence genes locat-
ed outside SPI-1, e.g. RtsA is encoded in an operon with RtsB on a 15 kb genomic region 
(Ellermeier and Slauch, 2003). HilD is member of the AraC/XylS transcriptional regulator family 
and induce directly the expression of hilA, which activates the expression of InvF. HilA and InvF 
activates the expression of all components of the T3SS-1, their chaperones and effector proteins 
(Paredes-Amaya et al., 2018).

3.2.3 Salmonella Pathogenicity island 2 (SPI-2) 
SPI-2 is a 40 kb island divided into two segments. The smaller 14.5 kb portion contains a group of 
five ttr genes involved in tetrathionate reduction and seven open reading frames (ORF) with un-
known function (Hensel, 2000). The larger 25.5 kb portion of SPI-2 is important for the ability of 
Salmonella to survive and replicate inside the host cell. SPI-2 contains four types of genes that are 
important for virulence: ssa, the genes that encodes the T3SS-2 apparatus, where ssaG is one of 
them; ssr, encodes the regulators; ssc, encodes the chaperones and sse encodes the effectors 
(Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). The regulation of SPI-2 is less complex than SPI-1, the essential regula-
tory system of SPI-2 is the two-component regulatory system (2CRS) ssrA-ssrB. The 2CRS are 
used by microorganisms to sense a specific environmental stimulus and respond to changes in the 
environment by a corresponding response regulator (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). SsrA is a membrane 
sensor kinase, whereas SsrB is the SPI-2 central response regulator. In general, the T3SS1 genes 
and effector proteins are needed for invasion of intestinal epithelial cells, while SPI-2 genes are 
induced after the bacterium is phagocytosed by macrophages to ensure a favorable environment for 
its survival and multiplication (Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2010) (Figure 3). Besides the pathogenicity 
islands, flagella are necessary for successful infection. The transcriptional regulator, ssrB, of SPI-2 
and the T3SS-2 encoding gene ssaG are both among the target virulence genes in our study.

3.3   Flagella  
Bacterial flagella are long helical filaments attached to rotary motors embedded within the mem-
brane, it enables the bacterium to swim and swarm over surfaces in liquids (Saini et al., 2010). The 
bacterial flagellum consists of at least three structural components, a basal body, a hook and a fila-
ment (Kutsukake et al., 1988). The filament is formed by thousands of subunits of FliC or flagellin, 
which ends in a hook that connects it with the cell’s engine. Finally, the basal body is a multiprotein 
complex embedded in the cell membrane, wherein the flagella motor is located (Cortés et al., 2019). 
Flagella are surface appendages that are required not only for motility and chemotaxis but also for 
several processes in pathogenesis (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). In S. Typhimurium, more than 50 genes 
are involved in flagella function and formation (Chilcott and Hughes, 2000), these genes are tran-
scribed in operons into three classes, termed class I (early genes), class II (middle genes) and class 
III (late genes) (Kutsukake et al., 1988) (Figure 4). The classes are expressed in a cascade-like man-
ner, where class I include the master regulatory genes (flhD and flhC) required for the expression of 
the entire flagella regulon. The two operon products (flhC and flhD) activate together the s70 pro-
motors of class II genes (Claret and Hughes, 2000) by binding within the middle-class region and 
interacting with the C-terminal region of the a subunit of RNA polymerase (Chilcott and Hughes, 
2000). Class II encode the hook-basal body protein, which make up the flagella secretory apparatus. 
Further, it encodes as well the RNA polymerase sigma factor, s28, encoded by fliA, which switches 
on the expression of class III genes (Schmitt et al., 2001). The class III promotors direct transcrip-
tion of genes needed late in the assembly process, which include the hook-associated proteins FlgK 
and FlgL that forms the hook-filament junction and the flagella cap FliD. Furthermore, the alter-
nately expressed flagella filament proteins FliC and FljB, the genes that encode the flagella motor 
torque generators MotA and MotB etc. (Karlinsey et al., 2000).           
The expression of flagella genes occurs at the early exponential growth phase since they play the 
most initial role in pathogenesis by allowing Salmonella cells to reach the intestinal epithelium for a 
proper interaction (Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). Flagella are facilitating invasion by enabling Salmonel-
la to swim to sites of invasion (Saini et al., 2010). Several systems have been implicated in Salmo-
nella pathogenesis of SPI-1 T3SS, among these are flagella and type 1 fimbriae (pili). Type 1 fim-
briae are hair-like appendages involved in pathogenesis by facilitating binding to intestinal epitheli-
al cells. As with flagella, type 1 fimbriae do not play a direct role in intestinal invasion but rather 
they contribute to intestinal colonization and persistent infections (Althouse et al., 2003). The tran-

3.4   Cross talk  
S. enterica needs to coordinate the expression of several cellular systems during the infection cycle 
(Ismeta Curkic, 2016). These genes are expressed in a hierarchy encoding systems related to motili-
ty, adhesion, invasion and intestinal persistence (Collazo and Galán, 1996; Fàbrega and Vila, 2013). 
The T3SS of SPI-1 is the key among them, but other systems as flagella and type 1 fimbriae are 
also implicated in the pathogenicity of Salmonella (Saini et al., 2009). The first genes expressed are 
flagella as the bacterium needs to swim to the sites of invasion in the distal small intestine. The bac-
terium stops synthesizing the SPI-1 T3SS in the stationary phase and instead begins to express the 
type 1 fimbrial genes that are involved in intestinal colonization and persistence (Saini et al., 2010).

3.5   Probiotics  
Probiotics are living and non-pathogenic bacteria that function as microbial food supplements that 
have beneficially affect the host by improving the intestinal microbial balance (Mokoena et al., 
2016). Most probiotics belong to the group of organisms known as lactic acid-producing bacteria 
(LAB) or yeast that improves human or animal health and are available in supplements and fer-
mented foods such as yoghurt, fermented milk or other foods (Parvez et al., 2006). The require-
ments for an organism to be claimed as probiotic are as follows: it must be generally recognized as 
safe (GRAS), tolerate low pH, high concentration of conjugated and de-conjugated bile salts, it 
should be tolerated by the immune system and not result in the formation of antibodies (Mokoena, 
2017). Probiotics have beneficial effects, such as improvement of intestinal health, enhancement of 
the immune response, reduction of serum cholesterol and cancer prevention (Kechagia et al., 2013). 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
Probiotic effects seem to be dose dependent. The usual effective dosage in humans is 107-109 
CFU/mg per day (Minelli and Benini, 2008). Probiotic products contain a single microorganism or 
a mixture of several species and are available as capsules, tablets, packets or powders usually in 
yogurt or dairy drinks (Whitman, 2010). Studies have reported probiotic bacteria to play an im-
portant role in the modulation of immunological, respiratory and gastrointestinal functions 
(Behnsen et al., 2013).  
 
3.6   Lactic acid bacteria  
LAB is a group of Gram-positive, non-spore forming, rods or cocci non-motile bacteria (Bintsis, 
2018). LAB ferments carbohydrates and form lactic acid as the major end product (Aguirre and 
Collins, 1993). LAB are the most common microbes used in food fermentation, that contributes to 
enhancing taste and texture in fermented foods (Mokoena, 2017), beverages and feed products 
(Savijoki et al., 2006). LAB has a growth optimum at pH 5.5 to 5.8 and require complex nutritional 
requirements for amino acids, peptides, nucleotide base, vitamins, minerals, fatty acids and carbo-
hydrates (Khalid, 2011). All LAB grow anaerobically but they are not sensitive to the presence of 
oxygen and can grow under aerobic condition as “aerotolerant anaerobes” (Thomas and Michael, 
1991). The classification of LAB is primarily depending on whether they are cocci or rods. Rod-
shaped LAB belongs to the family Lactobacillaceae, while cocci belongs to Streptococcaceae  
(Archibald, 1986). The microorganism is further divided into homofermentative and heteroferment-
ative depending on the end products of the fermented carbohydrates (Zúñiga et al., 1993). The 
homofermentative LAB produce lactic acid as the main end-product by oxidizing hexoses via gly-
colysis. Heterofermentative LAB use the pentose-phosphate pathway and produce lactic acid in-
cluding CO2, acetic acid and/or ethanol (Vandenberghe et al., 2018). Species of homofermentative 
LAB include Lactobacillus delbureckii, Lactobacillus helveticus, Lactococcus lactis, Lactobacillus 
amylophilus and Enterococcus faecalis, while heterofermentative species include Lactobacillus 
brevis, Lactobacillus fermentum, Leuconostoc lactis and Rhizopus spp. (Vandenberghe et al., 
2018). Some LAB has the ability to suppress virulence gene expression in pathogenic bacteria by 
utilizing and degrading special proteins in milk (Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2010), which is further 
explained below.

3.7   The composition of milk  
Milk and milk products are nutritional food products that contains valuable proteins, minerals and 
vitamins. Cow´s milk contain more than 25 different proteins (Masoodi and Shafi, 2010), which 
consists of approximately 80%(w/w) casein, 20% (w/w) whey proteins (lactoglobulin and lactalbu-
min) and is also a rich source of minerals such as calcium (McGregor and Poppitt, 2013). The milk 
protein is a source of bioactive peptides encrypted in the large protein molecules of casein- and 
whey protein (Korhonen and Pihlanto, 2007). Casein proteins are characterized by ester-bound 
phosphate, high proline contents and are precipitable from milk at pH 4.6 (Guo and Wang, 2016). 
Casein is the most abundant protein in milk grouped under the phosphoproteins casein and made up 
of four different subunits; aS1-casein, aS2-casein, b-casein and k-casein (Bhat et al., 2016). Milk 
has also a wide variety of physiological and biological functions due to its protein components. 
Milk proteins can be degraded into peptide fragments by enzymatic hydrolysis e.g. by LAB.  
 
3.8   The proteolytic system of LAB  
Lactic acid bacteria are microorganisms that require various nutrients such as vitamins and amino 
acids to grow (Dallagnol et al., 2013). Among these nutrients, amino acid is the essential building 
blocks of proteins, which are important in sustaining the growth of living organisms (Toe et al., 
2019). Milk and meat products are rich in proteins and have excellent amino acids profiles. LAB 
metabolizes the large proteins found in milk to obtain free essential amino acids by their proteolytic 
system (Liu et al., 2010). The proteolytic systems of LAB produce enzymes during fermentation 
composed of proteinases, peptidases and transport systems (Bu et al., 2013). Fermented milk has a 
higher content of peptides and free amino acid, especially valine, histidine, serine and proline, than 
non-fermented milk (Griffiths and Tellez, 2013). The proteolytic systems of LAB consist of three 
components: (i) cell-wall bound proteinase that initiates the degradation of milk protein into oligo-
peptides, (ii) peptide transporters that take up the peptides into the cell, and (iii) various intracellular 
peptidases that degrade the peptides into shorter peptides and amino acids (Liu et al., 2010).  
Enzymatic hydrolysis is one of the most commonly used methods to hydrolyze large protein mole-
cule to bioactive peptides that contains 3-20 amino acid residues per molecule (Haque et al., 2009).  
Different strains of LAB show different proteolytic activity, where the proteolytic activity is affect-
ed by change in pH. It has been reported, that the strains with a low degree of downregulation might 
lack a cell wall associated proteinase enzyme that is important for the breakdown of proteins to bio-
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
active peptides (Tessema, 2015). The most studied LAB strain is Lactococus lactis, have a well 
described proteolytic system (Savijoki et al., 2006). Several LAB strains (e.g. lactococcus lactis, 
Lactobacillus helveticus) have been reported to release bioactive peptides by fermentation 
(Mohanty et al., 2016). L. helveticus has the ability to reduce bitterness and give characteristic fla-
vor to cheese, which makes L. helveticus an important component of starter cultures and therefore is 
one of the commonly used LAB strains in the food industry, especially in the dairy industry (Fan et 
al., 2019). Bioactive peptides released during fermentation of LAB have been shown in various 
studies to downregulate virulence gene expression in pathogenic bacteria (Fan et al., 2019; 
Venegas-Ortega et al., 2019).  
 
3.9   Downregulation of virulence gene expression in pathogenic bacteria using LAB strains  
Previous studies imply that certain LAB strains can downregulate the virulence gene expression in 
specific pathogens (Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2012, 2010; Ding et al., 2005; Medellin-Peña et al., 
2007; Tellez et al., 2011; Yun et al., 2014). Mundi et al.,  (2013) have reported that numerous LAB 
strains (including Lactobacillus acidophilus LA-5) and Bifidobacterium have the ability to down-
regulate virulence gene expression in Campylobacter jejuni. Furthermore, another study document-
ed an inhibitory effect against Clostridium difficile. The in vivo experiment showed that L. acidoph-
ilus had a significant anti-virulence effect on C. difficile (Yun et al., 2014). Anti-virulence activity 
against different genes of E. coli have also been demonstrated, where L. acidophilus (LA-5) have 
prevented colonization of EHEC O157 (Medellin-Peña and Griffiths, 2009). Tellez et al., (2011) did 
isolate five peptide fractions from milk fermented with L. helveticus and showed that mice fed with 
a dose of 0.02 µg/day, of fragment had in general a higher health score, survival rate and a lower 
Salmonella burden in the spleen and liver compared with those in the control group. Overall, studies 
indicate that LAB strains affect the attachment and invasion of Salmonella both in vitro and in vivo 
through downregulation of the virulence genes hilA and ssrB (Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2012; Tellez 
et al., 2011).

4.   Methodology to measure gene expression   
Regulation of gene expression is a part of the process of the transcription from gene to a functional 
protein in a cell. Gene expression occurs in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic organisms 
(Anonymous, 2020b). Each cell contains genes that consists of DNA information database of cell, 
that code for a specific protein. Gene expression is the process by which the instructions in DNA 
are converted into a functional product, such as protein (Guo, 2014). By transcription, the infor-
mation in the gene is copied from deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) into messenger RNA (mRNA) by 
the enzyme RNA polymerase (Guo, 2014). Genes encode proteins and proteins dictate cell function. 
Different methods can be used to measure gene expression in a single cell organism by detecting 
the expression of functional gene products (mRNA) and protein molecules. Example of gene ex-
pression analysis method are the use of a hilA gene reporter construct in a bioluminescence assay 
and Reverse Transcriptase quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction (RT-qPCR) to measure the 
mRNA level, which are further described below.   
4.1   Bioluminescence assay  
Bioluminescent reporters are used in many areas of biology for quantifying and characterizing bac-
terial gene expression. Bioluminescent is a fast, sensitive, non-destructive detection method ena-
bling frequent measurements (Iqbal et al., 2017). The background of bioluminescence assay comes 
from many aquatic organisms that emit light and this emission of light is called bioluminescence. 
Lux operon encodes genes for self-regulation and for the production of luminescent proteins. The 
operon is isolated from the Vibrio fischeri bacterium. The bacterium V. fischeri is a gram-negative 
bacterium that has bioluminescent properties and encodes genes that are responsible for the produc-
tion of proteins involved in the generation of luminescence (Visick et al., 2000). An enzyme that 
catalyzes a reaction that will result in the emission of light is called a luciferase (Baldwin et al., 
1995). The bacterial luciferase is coded by the luxA and luxB genes both required for luminescence. 
Aside from the luciferase genes are the luxC, luxD and luxE also associated with lux systems in 
luminescent bacteria (Meighen, 1994). The luxC, D and E genes code for three proteins, that divert 
tetradecanoic acid away from the fatty acid biosynthesis pathway into the long chain fatty aldehyde 
substrate for the luminescent reaction (Meighen, 1994). The luciferase genes luxAB are flanked by 
the luxCDE in the order luxCDAB…E in all lux systems. The lux regulation is divided into two 
transcribed operons. The left operon contains a single gene (luxR), that encodes a transcriptional 
activator protein. Whereas the right operon consists of luxL, which along with luxR and the 218 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
base pairs differs the two operons, that is the primary regulatory circuit and the five structural genes 
(luxC, luxD, luxA, luxB and luxE) required for the bioluminescence activity (Shadel et al., 1989).  
 
A reporter system can be constructed to measure gene expression by quantifying emission of light. 
The plasmid construct hilA:luxCDABE used in this study was created by fusing the hilA promotor 
with the lux genes, luxCDABE, and insert this operon into a plasmid. The lux gene regulators luxR 
and luxL were removed, so the regulation of the lux-genes was controlled by the hilA promotor 
(Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2012). HilD is a transcriptional activator that binds to the promotor of hilA 
on the chromosome and plasmid, which activates hilA in the plasmid construct in S. Typhimurium 
LT2 (Figure 6 A). The RNA polymerase binds to the promotor and transcribes all the lux-genes in 
the operon to mRNA. The mRNA then binds to the ribosomes and is then translated to protein and 
light emission occurs. The mRNA is measured by RT-qPCR, whereas the light emission is meas-
ured by bioluminescence assay (Figure 6 B).

4.3   MALDI-TOF  
A widely used method for identification and differentiation of bacterial strains is the matrix-assisted 
laser desorption ionization time- of- flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spectrometry. It is a simple, rapid, 
low-cost and efficient method that is based on mass spectra (Croxatto et al., 2012). MALDI-TOF 
provide a unique insight into the biology and chemistry of bacterial cells. In a few minutes it can 
find the best match of a wide variety of microorganisms including bacteria, fungi and viruses from 
pure cultures of the organism (Croxatto et al., 2012; Lay, 2001). Figure 8 illustrate the process step 
for MALDI-TOF/MS, where samples are ionized into charged molecules and the ratio of their mass 
and charge (m/z) can be measured.  
 
Figure 8. The sample is mixed with a matrix on a conductive metal plate. After crystallization of the matrix, the metal plate is 
introduced in the mass spectrometer and bombarded with brief laser pulses. High voltage is then applied to accelerate the 
charged particles through an electrostatic field and spouted through a metal flight tube that is subjected to a vacuum where 
smaller ions traveling faster than larger ions until they reach a detector. Thus, bioanalytes separated according to their time- 
of flight (TOF) create a mass spectrum that is composed by mass to charge ratio (m/z) peaks with varying intensities 
(Croxatto et al., 2012).

5.4   Preparation of neutralized Cell Free Spent Medium (nCFSM) 
Neutralized Cell Free Spent Medium (nCFSM) was prepared through several steps as illustrated on 
Figure 9, each step is further described below.   
5.4.1 Preparation of inoculum and enumeration   
LAB inoculum was prepared by adding 0.1 g freeze dried powder or colony material to 50 mL heat-
treated milk or heat-treated reconstituted milk powder. The tubes were incubated with sealed lids at 
the optimum temperature (indicated in Table 1) to prepare an overnight inoculum. The cell concen-
tration of the inoculum culture was determined by preparing 10-fold dilution series and spot plating 
of 3x10 µL from each of the desired dilutions on MRS agar and incubated anaerobically for two 
days at the temperature seen in Table 1. Colonies were enumerated for each dilution, where the dilu-
tions containing more than 250 colonies were marked as overgrown (OG) and not included in the 
calculation of cell concentration.    
5.4.2 Inoculation of milk and reconstituted milk powder  
In order to investigate the importance of the size of inoculum, two inoculation levels (1% and 10%) 
were tested. A 1% inoculum of the starter culture was prepared by inoculating 50 mL heat treated 
milk or reconstituted milk powder with 500 µL overnight inoculum. A 10% inoculum was per-
formed by transferring 5 mL of the overnight inoculum to 45 mL of the heat-treated milk or recon-
stituted milk powder. The inoculated tubes where incubated at desired temperature overnight (17-22 
hours). The final cell concentration of the inoculum was determined by spot plating 3x10 µL on 
MRS agar and incubated anaerobically at the desired temperature for each strain (Table 1) for two 
days.

5.4.3 Preparation of supernatant from fermented milk and fermented reconstituted milk powder.  
After fermentation, supernatants of the fermented milk or reconstituted milk powder were obtained 
by centrifugation. For samples where acid-coagulation did not occur during fermentation, coagula-
tion was ensured by adjusting pH to 4.5 with L-(+)-Lactic acid solution in H2O ³ 85% (LA) (Sigma 
Aldrich) using pH strips (Fisher Scientific). The pH was lowered to coagulate the milk to enhance 
the effect of centrifugation and to isolate the supernatant. The acidification step was omitted if the 
fermented milk was naturally acidified with a pH below 5. The fermented milk samples were centri-
fuged (Eppendorf Centrifuge 5810 R) at 18 514 g for 3 min at 4°C. After centrifugation, a white 
coagulate layer was formed at the top of the tube and a pellet in the bottom. The coagulated layer 
was removed with a spoon and the supernatant was further centrifuged for 10 min at same condi-
tions. A little coagulated layer was seen at the top of the tube at this step, which were removed 
again with a spoon. The supernatant of » 35-40 mL was transferred to a new tube, where 6 M 
NaOH was used to adjust the pH to 7.2 in order to avoid effects caused merely by acidification. The 
pH adjusted supernatants were further centrifuged for 5 min to remove all coagulated fat layer to 
enable easy sterilization. Sterilization of the supernatant was performed by filtering through a 0.2 
µm-filter (Millipore, Fisher Scientific Biotech Line ApS) with a syringe. The sterilized supernatants 
termed as nCFSM were aliquoted in portions of 1350 µL to Eppendorf tubes and stored at -18°C 
until use. The same procedure for isolation of supernatants was repeated to produce a negative con-
trol supernatant for heat-treated milk and reconstituted milk powder which had been neither inocu-
lated nor incubated and later in the study termed as non-fermented.    
5.4.4 Preparation of plate for measurement of growth and hilA gene expression of Salmonella 
Typhimurium LT2 by bioluminescence assay       
The nCFSM from section 5.4.3 were thawed and mixed with 150 µL LB+ ampicillin and marked as 
the ‘negative’ sample as the prepared tube was without S. Typhimurium LT2. From the prepared 
negative sample, 750 µL was transferred to a new tube marked as ‘positive’ and mixed thoroughly 
with 7.5 µL S. Typhimurium LT2 resulting in 107 CFU/mL S. Typhimurium LT2 reporter strain 
hilA::luxCDABE. Besides, several control samples were prepared and measured on the same lux 
plate to compare the effect of nCFSM from fermented milk with LAB strains with the non-
fermented control sample. The milk/ reconstituted milk powder control samples were prepared by 
thawing the nCFSM from section 5.4.3, and mixed with 10xLB SF. A positive control with 107 
CFU/mL (7.5 µL) of the S. Typhimurium LT2 reporter strain added to the nCFSM was prepared.

Furthermore, a positive LB control prepared by transferring 150 µL 10xLB SF to 1350 µL sterile 
filtered water and similarly adding 107 CFU/mL S. Typhimurium.   
Each prepared sample (positive and negative) were distributed as 200 µL into triplicate wells of a 
sterile opaque CorningR 96-well plate (Sigma Aldrich) and incubated at 37°C in a Victor Multilabel 
Counter (PerkinElmer Life Sciences, Canada). Measurements were done using following opera-
tions: a linear fast shake for 1 second before lux was measured followed by a linear fast shake for 1

defined as luminescence counts min-1 expressed by OD490 (RLU/ OD490) (Henriksen et al., 2014).

5.5.2 RNA extraction.  
The frozen S. Dublin pellet was thawed on ice. Once defrosted, RNA was purified using the Qiagen 
mini kit (QIAGEN, Germany), with few modifications described in appendix C.  
5.5.3 Elimination of DNA from RNA by DNase treatment. 
All RNA samples were treated with DNase to eliminate the trace of DNA in the RNA samples. All 
the following steps were carried out on ice. The RNA concentration was measured using Qubit 3 
(Invitrogen), where each RNA sample were transferred to PCR tubes to obtain 200 ng individually 
before DNase treatment was conducted using TURBO DNase kit ((2U/µL 1000 units), Thermo 
Fisher Scientific) as follows:  
The total volume of each DNase treatment reaction was 20 µL, where the volume of RNA should 
not be more than 17 µL, but resulting in an amount of 200 ng RNA. The reagents RNA (x µL), wa-
ter (17 µL-x µL RNA), 10xbuffer (2 µL) and DNase (1 µL) were added in the amount indicated in 
the parenthesis. The sample was incubated at 37°C for 30 min on a PCR machine (Thermocycler, 
Biometra). Followed by inactivation of DNase by adding of ethylene-diamine-tetra-acetic-acid 
(EDTA) to the final concentration of 15 mM (0.6 µL was added to the 20 µL reaction from a 0.5 M 
stock) and heated for 10 min at 70°C.  
5.5.4 Conversion of RNA to cDNA by Reverse Transcription (RT).  
A high capacity cDNA reverse transcription kit (Applied Biosystems®, ThermoFisher Scientific) 
was used to convert RNA to complementary deoxyribonucleic acid (cDNA). The reverse transcrip-
tase (RT) master mix was prepared in RNases- and DNase free Eppendorf tubes (Fisher Scientific 
Biotech Line) by mixing kit components as follows (for one sample): 0.8 µL 25x deoxyribonucleo-
side triphosphate (dNTP) (100 mM), 2 µL 10x RT Random Primers, 2 µL 10x RT buffer, 1 µL 
MultiScribe Reverse Trancriptase (50 units) (RT) and 4.2 µL nuclease-free water (Applied Biosys-
tems®, ThermoFisher Scientific). The 10 µL RT- mix was transferred to PCR tubes containing 10 
µL RNA (DNase treated). The synthesis of cDNA was conducted using PCR (Thermocycler, Bi-
ometra) with the settings: 25°C for 10 min followed by 37°C for 120 min, 85°C for 5 min and a 
holding step at 4°C. The produced cDNA was stored at -20°C until use.

strain X (red) if measured accumulated, whereas reading at the specific timepoint 12 h gives a too 
narrow and potentially misleading interpretation that strain X downregulates the virulence gene 
expression significantly compared to the control.

7.   Results  
In the project CalfCare, screening of different LAB strains was carried out, with the purpose to de-
velop a milk fermented product with infection protection properties. As part of this, this study in-
vestigated the ability of selected LAB strains to downregulate the hilA virulence gene expression of 
S. Typhimurium LT2. To optimize the screening procedures for LAB strains, different elements 
were investigated, to develop optimal screening protocols.  
7.1   The effect of individual components of the MRS media on hilA gene expression. 
The effect on expression of the virulence gene construct hilA::luxCDABE of all components of the 
growth media MRS was investigated by adding it to LB individually (see appendix D). The growth 
of S. Typhimurium LT2 in the control sample (LB control) obtained an OD around 0.7 after 23 h 
(Figure 12). The growth of S. Typhimurium LT2 was improved in the presence of yeast extract, 
manganese sulfate, diammonium hydrogen citrate and L- cysteine HCl (Figure 12). The component 
glucose improved the growth of S. Typhimurium LT2 within the first 4 h but fades hereafter to a 
final OD of 0.57.

to the components of MRS media compared to the LB control was observed. The hilA gene expres-

yeast extract, L-cysteine HCl, manganese sulfate, and diammonium hydrogen citrate (Figure 13).

Five peptides, which have been identified in milk fermented by L. helveticus LH-2 (Tellez et al., 
2010) were tested at concentrations of 0.5 µg/mL, 10 µg/mL and 25 µg/mL for capacity to down-
regulate hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 using the bioluminescence assay. The growth 
of S. Typhimurium LT2 in LB in the presence of the peptides (F1, F2, F3, F4 and F5) resulted in 
final OD of 0.65 to 0.75 (appendix H), indicating that the peptides do not affect the growth of S. 
Typhimurium. The hilA expression in the presence of the individual peptide fractions showed that 
downregulation was concentration dependent (Figure 20). The highest peptide concentration (25 
µg/mL) had in general a higher inhibition followed by 10 µg/mL and 0.5 µg/mL, except F3 where 
the lowest concentration showed the largest downregulation of hilA (Figure 20C). When S. Typhi-
murium were grown in Fmix (Figure 20F), the concentration dependency of the hilA gene expression 
was not observed, as the lowest tested concentration 0.125 µg/mL had the largest downregulation of 
hilA. In general, all peptides had a substantial effect compared to the LB control.

8.   Discussion  
The different fermentation protocols will be discussed in this section with the focus of finding a 
suitable protocol that optimally will suppress the hilA virulence gene expression in S. Dublin by the 
ability of different LAB strains. The first part is a brief explanation of the reliability of the obtained 
data.  
8.1 Reliability  
It is important to be aware of the limitations and reliability of experimental results. A biological 
conclusion based on a limited dataset can be a misleading for future studies. Data is more reliable 
when repeated as biological replicates, as there are coupled more information, than data with a sin-
gle replica or technical replicates. The inhibitory effect of fermented milk and reconstituted milk 
powder on hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 have been repeated as biological triplicate 
including technical duplicates. Furthermore, the RT-qPCR results presented in this thesis has been 
conducted by a specialist at The University of Copenhagen (Liu Gang, CalfCare, 2020) based on 
our nCFSM prepared at DTU.  
Another point worth mentioning is that the RLU/OD490 data were not corrected for blank, which is 
the explanation why some of the OD490 graphs starts at OD 0.2 or above at time 0. However, when 
OD were corrected based on blanks, this introduced negative values in RLU/OD490 graphs (appen-
dix J). A possible explanation of this may be, that the cell concentration were around the detection 
limit on the spectrophotometer and by dividing with an OD close to 0 it led to false values with ei-
ther very high or negative RLU/OD490 values, which would lead to a picture of the hilA gene ex-
pression, that is hard to interpret biologically.    
8.2 Effect of individual components of MRS media on virulence gene expression hilA in S. 
Typhimurium LT2. 
Numerous studies have used MRS as growth media for culturing of the LAB strains (Bayoumi and 
Griffiths, 2010; Medellin-Peña et al., 2007; Nordeste et al., 2017). MRS contain casein, from which 
the inhibitory effect on hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 is expected. However, it was 
of interest to investigate if other components of the MRS medium could contribute to downregula-
tion of hilA. The results indicate some degree of inhibition of hilA gene expression due to MRS 
components comparing with the control sample (LB control) (Figure 12). The growth of S. Typhi-
murium was not optimally in the presence of the MRS component glucose when tested in LB (Fig-
ure 12), whereas the growth of glucose and the downregulation of hilA in the presence of glucose 
==PAGE:57==
 
57 
was good when tested in nCFSM from L. helveticus Z32 (Figure 14 and 15). Delcenserie et al., 
(2012) have also tested the effect of the presence of glucose on the virulence gene expression of E. 
coli, showing that all concentrations of glucose (0.1 to 1%) had an inhibitory effect on expression of 
genes from the Lotus of Enterocyte Effecement (LEE) operon. The effect of glucose on the 
virulence gene expression of hilA in S. Typhimurium LT2 is not reported so far, however it has 
been considered if glucose could contribute with an inhibitory effect since Salmonella are able to 
produce acid from glucose (Marth, 1969). Furthermore, has sodium acetate shown to suppress the 
expression of K99 pili of Escherichia coli and the greatest suppression was observed when the me-
dium contained both sodium acetate and glucose, while glucose alone did not affect the expression 
(Francis et al., 1983). MRS was initially considered to be used as growth medium for the compara-
tive screening of the 62 LAB cultures. However, due to these findings, it was decided to use milk as 
a growth medium because the objective of the CalfCare project is to identify LAB strains for fer-
mentation of milk. Thus, using milk for the screening would probably give a more realistic picture 
of what to be expected from the LAB strains on the hilA gene expression of S. Typhimurium LT2. 
The results also showed, that the MRS components diammonium hydrogen citrate and manganese 
sulfate can possibly led to downregulation of hilA when tested in LB and did contribute to increase 
the effect of L. helveticus when adding these to the milk before fermentation (Figure 15).    
8.3 Inhibitory effect of fermented milk supernatants on hilA virulence gene expression (in S. 
Typhimurium LT2 measured in a bioluminescence assay.  
Several studies have used a specific timepoint to express the virulence gene expression. Henriksen 
et al., (2014) based the results on the timepoint 12 h, while Tessema, (2015) focused on 4 h. This 
thesis initially focus on the hilA gene expression on the timepoint 12 h as described by Henriksen et 
al., (2014). However, when measuring the gene expression, it can be difficult to choose a specific 
timepoint e.g. 12 h as the inhibitory effect on the virulence gene can vary in time between the LAB 
strains. Important information on expression between 0-11 h can be lost. Another problem of using 
a specific time can be seen in appendix K, where the hilA expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 grown 
in nCFSM seems to decrease after 5-8 h opposed to entering the exponential phase when grown in 
milk. Choosing a specific timepoint in this case, will lead to misinterpretation of the expression. 
Therefore, to avoid this, the overall virulence gene expression was estimated as an accumulated 
virulence gene expression (sum(12h)) measured as the area under the expression curve from t=0 h 
to t=12 h.  
==PAGE:58==
 
58 
Furthermore, it might give some sense to measure downregulation by an accumulated expression 
over a time period of 12 h, as S. Dublin in the coming feeding trial, will be more continuously ex-
posed to the fermented milk with 12 h feeding intervals. It is however, very difficult to predict, how 
the complexity of the intestinal environment will affect the overall ability of the fermented milk to 
downregulate virulence genes in S. Dublin in vivo.  
 
Throughout all experiments LB was added to the supernatants to test growth of Salmonella. Thus, a 
LB control was included to test that LB did not have an inhibitory effect on the hilA gene expres-
sion. No significant difference (P>0.05) between the LB- and milk control was observed, indicating 
that LB does not have an inhibitory effect on the hilA gene expression when looking at the accumu-
lated expression (sum(12h)) (Figure 16 D). However, the expression curve for milk and LB control 
were not fully identical, when looking at the timepoint 12 h (Figure 16 C), as the LB control de-
creased the hilA gene expression with more than 50% in relation to the milk control. Contrary to 
milk, the reconstituted milk powder showed a significant difference (P<0.05) between the LB- and 
milk control of hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 (Figure 18). The reason for this is not 
clear, and it makes conclusions on data from the reconstituted milk powder more uncertain. 
 
Previous studies have used inoculation levels of 1% (Medellin-Peña et al., 2007) and 10% 
(Henriksen et al., 2014; Tellez et al., 2010), therefore these inoculation levels were considered for 
the development of the final milk fermentation protocol. The 10% inoculation level lead to nCFSMs 
with better inhibitory activity against hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 (Table 6) possi-
bly due to the higher LAB cell concentration at this inoculation level (Table 5). For the fermenta-
tion protocol it was also considered to ferment the milk for 6 h or 18 h. The lack of information 
regarding the cell concentration for L. acidophilus LA5 at 18 h fermentation (Table 5) makes it un-
clear, which fermentation time (6 h or 18 h) will lead to the highest cell concentration and thereby a 
more suitable protocol to follow for this organism. L. rhamnosus LGG indicates a higher concentra-
tion, whereas L. helveticus Z32 decreases in concentration after 18 h fermentation (Table 5). The 
decrease of concentration for L. helveticus Z32 might be due to autolysis in the stationary phase 
(Lortal et al., 1997). Autolysis is also known for other thermophilic bacteria e.g. Lactobacillus del-
brueckii subsp. lactis (Hannon et al., 2006) and it is not only seen in the stationary phase but also in 
the exponential phase. Cell lysis can also occur due to phage infection. Replication of bacteriophage 
inside the cell can lead to lysis and eventually burst the cell (Marcó et al., 2012). Considering the 
==PAGE:59==
 
59 
objective of developing an on-farm fermentation system, both a 6 h and a 18 h fermentation can be 
handled by the farmer.  
 
The 1% and 10% fermentation protocol indicated that the inhibitory effect of hilA gene expression 
might depend on the LAB cell concentration, as the highest LAB concentration lead to the highest 
downregulation of hilA. The intention was therefore to further optimize the protocol by measuring 
cell concentration of the overnight inoculum. The overnight inoculum resulted in the highest cell 
concentration and the highest downregulation of the virulence gene hilA, but the inhibitory effects 
could be misinterpreted as the LB- and milk controls showed unexpectedly high RLU/OD490 ex-
pression level than compared to the four other fermentation controls (Figure 16C and D). The re-
sults presented in this thesis, together with previous studies, led to a decision to use a 10% inocula-
tion level and 18 h fermentation as basis for screening a library of LAB strains. 
 
Technical variation in the bioluminescence measurement between the several plates being used to 
measure lux could add to the overall variation observed in the experiments. However, the data for   
milk- and LB controls on Figure 16C and D indicates that these controls behaves reasonably alike 
across the plates, which indicates that the reading of lux is reasonable reproducible across plates.  
 
Results showed that L. helveticus Z32, L. acidophilus LA-5 and L. rhamnosus LGG were able to 
downregulate the hilA gene expression of S. Typhimurium LT2, although to a varying degree (Fig-
ure 16C and D). The nCFSM from L. rhamnosus LGG showed the least downregulation in the in-
oculum and 18h fermentation, which is somewhat in line with previous work where L. rhamnosus 
GR-1 was not downregulated in neither ssrB nor hilA gene expression of S. Typhimurium LT2 
(Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2010). Furthermore, they mention that L. rhamnosus GR-1 had no effect 
when comparing with the control group (Bayoumi and Griffiths, 2010). By observing the results for 
the accumulated (sum (12h)) gene expression of hilA for 18 h fermentation this study supports this, 
as L. rhamnosus LGG had nearly no effect when comparing with the milk control, since 1% 18 h 
showed a decrease of 2 % and 10% inoculation level at 18 h fermentation showed 1% inhibition 
relative to the milk control (Table 6). Kort et al., (2015) point out, that L. rhamnosus LGG failed to 
downregulate in milk due to its inability to degrade casein and lactose and explain further that the 
food matrices should be enriched with either degradable sugars as glucose, proteolytic enzymes, or 
hydrolyzed nitrogen sources as yeast extract to enable propagation. When comparing strains, it has 
==PAGE:60==
 
60 
to be remembered that strain variation occurs and a complete reproduction of the results of 
Bayoumi and Griffiths (2010) using GR-1 and our result using LGG, cannot be guaranteed.  
 
L. helveticus Z32 showed the highest downregulation of hilA for the accumulated expression (sum 
(12 h)) (Table 6). L. helveticus contain up to four cell envelope proteases (Jensen et al., 2009), 
which may explain the high downregulation seen with L. helveticus Z32. L. acidophilus LA-5 and 
L. helveticus Z32 are both lactobacillus species with high proteolytic activity when fermenting milk 
or milk protein based media (Griffiths and Tellez, 2013; Savijoki et al., 2006). Bioactive peptides 
with inhibitory effect of virulence genes are produced by strains which have a highly effective pro-
teolytic system (Mohanty et al., 2016), which can explain the high inhibitory effect of these strains 
compared with L. rahmnosus LGG.   
 
Overall, the experiments have given guidance to develop the fermentation protocol to be used when 
screening LAB strains. Before using the results to advice, the development of the large-scale fer-
mentation protocol at the dairy company, it is important to consider having the same way of spik-
ing, which was not available in the current study. In our experiments, the spiking level for prepara-
tion of inoculum were different as for L. helveticus Z32 it was colonies from a plate, whereas L. 
acidophilus LA-5 and L. rhamnosus LGG were commercial freeze-dried powders.  
8.4 Inhibitory effect of fermented reconstituted milk powder on hilA gene expression in S. 
Typhimurium LT2 measured by the bioluminescence assay.  
In the field trials in CalfCare, shortage of milk for fermentation make it necessary to supplement the 
milk for the calves with reconstituted milk powder. It was therefore relevant to investigate if fer-
mentation of reconstituted milk powder provides comparable downregulations as tank milk normal-
ly offered to the calves.  
Overall, the testing of the reconstituted milk powder fermented with LAB strains showed less inhi-
bition of the hilA gene expression compared to the experiments conducted with milk, indicating that 
a use of reconstituted milk powder may reduce the effect from the fermented milk product. In con-
trast to fermentation of milk, where a low downregulation observed, L. rhamnosus LGG showed an 
upregulation of the hilA gene expression of S. Typhimurium LT2 when tested in reconstituted milk 
powder.

8.5 The effect of pure peptide fractions on hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2.  
The capacity to downregulate the ssrB virulence gene in Salmonella by five specific peptide frac-
tions derived from beta-casein and alfa-lactalbumin isolated from milk fermented with L. helveticus 
LH-2 has been reported (Tellez et al., 2010). In the current study, different concentrations of the 
peptides were tested individually for capacity to downregulate the hilA gene expression in S. 
Typhimurium LT2. As expected, the highest concentration for the peptides tested individually re-
sulted in the best downregulation of virulence gene hilA, except for F3 that unexpectedly showed 
the highest effect at lowest concentration. An in vivo study on the bioactive peptides isolated from 
L. helveticus LH-2 showed a concentration dependent effect against ssrB gene of Salmonella and 
Fmix showed a total inhibition of the expression of the ssrB gene for the highest concentration 
(25µg/mL) (Tellez et al., 2011). The Fmix in our study did not show that the inhibitory effect of hilA 
gene expression is as concentration dependent as expected and may cause that ssrB gives a better 
response than hilA. The deviation of F3 in our study may be due to errors in the laboratory especial-
ly when pipetting a specific, and very little amount of S. Typhimurium LT2 to the luminescence 
measurement or during preparation of the peptide solution. However, more stringent conclusion 
must await a trippel replication of this experiment.  
 
8.6 Screening of 62 LAB cultures for comparison of their ability to downregulate hilA gene 
expression in S. Typhimurium LT2. 
The fermentation protocol, which was developed formed the basis for a screening for the best can-
didates to downregulate hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 among 62 commercial LAB 
cultures. Nine cultures were strain cocktails and 53 were monocultures (Table 1), and as such des-
ignated as strains. The screening of the 62 cultures demonstrated, that several strains were able to 
downregulate hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2, but to different levels. L. Paracasei 
(BGP1 and BGP2) did show the largest downregulation of the hilA expression with 98% relative to 
the milk control, indicating that these were the most efficient strains to suppress the hilA gene ex-
pression of S. Typhimurium LT2. However, as the light emission was measured relative to the cell 
concentration and growth of S. Typhimurium LT2 in the nCFSM from L. Paracasei strains im-
paired having an OD around 0.24-0.38 after 23 h, the high downregulation of L. Paracasei has to be 
considered carefully (appendix I). The hilA downregulation observed for P. pentosaceus P6 (75%) 
and L. reuteri RC-14 (81%) were also obtained at low OD values (< OD 0.5). L. acidophilus LA3, 
which showed 55% downregulation of the hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 (Figure 
==PAGE:62==
 
62 
22), has also shown to have a downregulation effect against genes in other pathogenic bacteria e.g. 
E. coli (Nordeste et al., 2017). Nordeste et al., (2017) did test the ability of L. acidopilus LA3 to 
produce bioactive molecules against E. coli in pigs and showed, that piglets feed with the LAB 
strain had a significantly better health improvement and weight gain compared with those in the 
control group. A recent study has demonstrated by a simple experiment that L. lactis can eliminate 
S. Typhimurium LT2 in pasteurized camel milk (Bragason et al., 2020). The elimination of S. 
Typhimurium LT2 might be due to, that L. lactis acidify milk and thereby prevent the growth of S. 
Typhimurium LT2. The pH from milk fermented with L. lactis in our study was 4.0 (appendix F). 
LAB strains that both acidify and show a good inhibition effect on the virulence gene hilA may be 
potential to use in the CalfCare project for the fermented milk to be fed to calves, because the path-
ogen will be affected by two inhibitory factors. However, did the acidification factor of LAB strains 
not have a directly effect in this study as the supernatant were neutralized before Salmonella was 
added.   
 
The multi-strain culture RD-1 comprised three different bacteria species. The isolation of the indi-
vidual strains of RD-1 gave an opportunity to investigate how a multi-strain culture behaved com-
pared to its individual strains. Colonies from each of the strains of RD-1 were identified on MAL-
DI-TOF and revealed that RD-1 was a mixture of S. carnosus, L. curvatus and K. salsicia. Except 
for K. salsicia, this confirmed the content claimed by the deliverer. It was expected that K. varians 
was isolated, however, the identification of K. salsicia may be explained by the lack of detection of 
K. varians in the MALDI-TOF database. The two Kocuria strains are closely related (Yun et al., 
2011) and K. varians was therefore the best match. RD-1 showed surprisingly an up regulation, 
while the individual strains showed a downregulation of hilA gene expression. This suggest, that the 
behavior of a multi-strain culture may be different than the individual strains. This also implies, that 
the inhibitory effect of virulence genes may necessarily not increase by mixing two or more strains. 
Different LAB strains may be needed to provide inhibition of virulence genes in different patho-
gens. Thus, thorough testing of strain cocktails has to be carried out in order to ensure an acceptable 
inhibition across the target pathogens.  
  
Due to time constraints we have only been able to do a single testing. To ascertain reliable conclu-
sions on inhibition, it is important to repeat the experiment as triplicate. It could also be interesting 
==PAGE:63==
 
63 
to clarify further the relevance of a strong inhibitory effect of hilA gene expression from LAB 
strains showing an apparent limited growth capacity. 
 
The reliability of the inhibitory effect of hilA gene expression of S. Typhimurium LT2 (Figure 22) 
and the growth of S. Typhimurium LT2 (appendix I) for the nCFSM strains L. helveticus Z32, L. 
acidophilus LA5 and L. rhamnosus was tested using biological replicates in milk and reconstituted 
milk powder. The replicate experiments showed the similar effect, where L. rhamnosus LGG re-
sulted in the best growth of S. Typhimurium LT2 but the strain with lowest downregulation of hilA 
gene expression. L. helveticus Z32 thereby showed the opposite effect than L. rahmnosus LGG as 
the growth of S. Typhimurium LT2 were slightly lower, but the inhibitory was significantly higher. 
In general, was L. helveticus Z32 the best of the tested strain to downregulate the hilA gene expres-
sion in S. Typhimurium LT2 when tested in milk and reconstituted milk powder with biolumines-
cence assay.  
 
8.7 Inhibitory effect of fermented milk nCFSM on virulence gene expression in S. Dublin  
The lack of reporter gene construct in S. Dublin, made it necessary to screen with the S. Typhimuri-
um LT2 construct in bioluminescence assay and then decide for the best LAB candidates to perform 
a virulence gene expression analysis on selected virulence genes using RT-qPCR. The RT-qPCR 
measurement of gene expression is not based on a specific timepoint but rather a specific OD 
(OD=1) that can vary depending on the tested strain.       
 
The virulence genes chosen as target for RT-qPCR analysis are essential as covered in the introduc-
tion. Six genes have been selected of which three are transcriptional regulators (hilA, ssrB and flhD) 
that are controlling expression of genes for effector proteins (prgI, ssaG and fliC). When downregu-
lation was observed for the regulators the expected downregulation of the effector genes was only 
clearly observed for L. helveticus Z32 using a 10% inoculation level (Table 9) and was less clear for 
L. acidophilus LA-5 and L. rhamnosus LGG.   
 
The RT-qPCR data (Figure 19) supported the bioluminescence data on fermented milk that a larger 
downregulation was observed using the 10% inoculation level compared to the 1%. Also, the larg-
est downregulation of hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 was seen for L. helveticus Z32 
with L. acidophilus LA-5 being second-best. L. rhamnosus LGG showed an upregulation in both 
==PAGE:64==
 
64 
assays and thus again confirmed the findings obtained with the bioluminescence assay. These data 
indicated that expression of hilA gene expression may be equally regulated, despite this, it is im-
portant to be aware of, that these two Salmonella serovars differ in their clinical manifestations.  
 
The high Ct value seen in the data on appendix G indicated that there was a little amount of cDNA 
present in the samples, which were termed as underdetermined and indicate unreliable results. The 
difference between the Ct values among the replicate samples did also affect the reliability of the 
data. These data were not included, and the experiment was repeated by people with good experi-
ence that is expected to make less errors that may occur during the following steps; when growing 
S. Dublin in the nCFSM, when freezing the cells at the right OD (OD=1) and all the steps during 
extraction of RNA, elimination of DNA from RNA sample by DNase treatment and conversion of 
RNA to cDNA by revers transcription.    
 
There are advantages and disadvantages with both of the methods for gene expression analysis, RT-
qPCR and the luminescence assay. The luminescence assay is a more rapid and cheap analysis 
method as the luminescence plate can be prepared on a short time and it does not require a lot of 
chemical substances (kits) like the RT-qPCR. Another advantage of luminescence is that the ex-
pression is measured over time, whereas RT-qPCR is based on a single time point measurement, 
here defined to be at OD=1.0. The advantages of RT-qPCR may be a more precise quantification of 
the expression as it is measured directly on the mRNA, whereas the level of mRNA is indirectly 
measured by a level in the luminescence signal from the hilA::luxCDABE gene construct.

9.   Conclusion  
This thesis confirmed that supernatants isolated from several different LAB strains used for dairy 
production are able to downregulate the virulence gene expressions of S. Typhimurium LT2 and S. 
Dublin. Using different fermentation protocols, it was concluded that a 10% inoculation level with 
18 hours of fermentation was more favorable than 1%. The optimized fermentation protocol result-
ed in a higher cell concentration of LAB and better suppression of the hilA virulence gene expres-
sion of S. Typhimurium LT2 when measured with a bioluminescence assay. Expression analysis of 
the virulence genes hilA, ssrB, flhD, fliC, prgI and ssaG, using RT-qPCR showed how these were 
more downregulated in S. Dublin when LAB fermentations were done with a 10% inoculation level 
than 1%. This analysis also confirmed that the highest suppression of virulence gene expression was 
observed in nCFSM from L. helveticus Z32 followed by L. acidophilus LA-5. Other strains which 
showed effective downregulation against hilA gene expression in the bioluminescence assay were 
K. varians (70%), Lactococcus lactis O224 (61%), Bifidobacterium BB02 (61%), L. helveticus LH 
521 (58%), L. brevis (57%) and L. acidophilus LA3 (55%).   
 
The level of downregulation may be associated with the proteolytic activity of LAB and the produc-
tion of bioactive peptides with inhibition properties as the highly proteolytic strain L. helveticus 
Z32, showed the highest inhibition (60%) of the hilA virulence gene expression in S. Typhimurium 
LT2. This is in line with existing literature. In contrast to the expected, five commercially synthe-
sized peptide fractions from milk protein, reported for L. helveticus LH-2, did not show the inhibi-
tory effect of hilA virulence gene to be concentration dependent.   
 
The reconstituted milk powder fermented by LAB strains, from which nCFSM were produced, re-
vealed that the inhibitory effect of hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 were lower com-
pared to when nCFSM was produced from fermented milk. The inhibitory effects of L. helveticus 
Z32 and L. acidophilus LA-5 were 16%, 6% downregulation, respectively. L. rhamnosus LGG 
showed an upregulation effect with 75%.  
Most of the individual MRS components showed some effect on the virulence gene expression. The 
components diammonium hydrogen citrate and manganese sulfate had a contributing effect of the 
inhibitory effect nCFSM from L. helveticus Z32 when these components were added to the milk 
before fermentation start.

10.   Future perspectives  
The testing of the 62 LAB cultures for identification of the most efficient cultures to downregulate 
hilA gene expression in S. Typhimurium LT2 has the perspective to design a strain cocktail com-
posed of two or several strains, which can be used for a yoghurt to feed calves in herds with diar-
rheal problems among calves. This will be investigated in the CalfCare project. The CalfCare pro-
ject has to replicate our preliminary screening of the 62 cultures before final conclusions can be 
made on inhibitory effect against hilA gene expression in S. Dublin.   
 
We observed that the components diammonium hydrogen citrate and manganese sulfate contributed 
to an enhanced effect of the nCFSM from L. helveticus Z32 on downregulation of hilA. If this can 
be reproduced when replicating, it could be interesting to test the effect of adding diammonium 
hydrogen citrate and manganese sulfate to fermented milk and see if it further enhances the inhibi-
tory effect of the fermented milk.  
 
We showed that fermentation of reconstituted milk powder does not provide as good inhibitory ef-
fects as fermented milk. It could however still be important to investigate if the milk in the dairy 
herds can be supplemented with the milk powder before fermentation to see the inhibitory effect of 
the hilA gene expression remains at the same level as pure milk.   
 
Overall, this thesis focused on LAB ability to downregulate virulence genes in S. Dublin related to 
calves. The project also opens for a perspective for prevention of disease in humans and other ani-
mals by constructing a milk product that will have infection protection properties against other en-
teric bacteria. From laboratory work, it has been reported that LAB is able to downregulate viru-
lence genes in Campylobacter, E. coli and Clostridium difficile.

Appendix A. Preparation of LB broth  
1x LB (Sigma Life Science) was prepared by transferring 1 LB tablet to 50 mL demineralized water 
and mixed thoroughly on a magnetic stirrer. The prepared 1xLB was hereafter sterile filtered 
through a 0.2 µm -filter (Milipore, Fisher Scientific Biotech Line ApS). The same procedure was 
used to prepare a 10xLB, but instead of 1 LB tablet 10 tablets were used.   
Appendix A1. Preparation of S. Typhimurium and S. Dublin cultures.  
S. Typhimurium LT2 (from DTU strain collection) was streaked on a Tryptic Soy Agar (TSA) plate 
and incubated at 37°C overnight. From this one colony of S. Typhimurium was transferred to 10 
mL 1xLB with ampicillin and mixed by vortexing. S. Dublin culture was prepared based on the 
same procedure with the exception that S. Dublin did not contain ampicillin.

Appendix C. The procedure of Qiagen mini kit 
1. Collect ice and place it on a tray with low edges (easier to work with) 
2. Add 300mg glass beads into FastPrep (cut the tip of a 1mL pipette tip) 
3. Note the samples on the side of the tubes and place them on ice 
4. FROM NOW ON EVERYTHING IS DONE IN FUME HOOD AND AS COLD AS POS-
SIBLE, use RNA purification kit from QIAGEN. 
5.  600µL buffer RLT (6µL b-ME in 600µL buffer RLT, 1:100) was added in each sample 
tube, and then vortex and transfer to FastPrep tubes with beads. 
6. Use FastPrep apparatus, run program 1(speed=6.0, 45 sec.), twice, COOL cells on ice for 5 
mins between. 
7. Transfer supernatant 500µL to a new tube (avoid beads) and add the same volume of 70% 
Ethanol and pipette up and down (Do not centrifuge), proceed immediately to next step. 
8. Transfer up to 700µL of the sample, including any precipitate that may have formed, to an 
RNeasy mini spin column placed in 2mL collection tube. Close the lid gently, and centri-
fuge for 15s, at 14000rpm. Discard the flow through. 
9. Add 700µL buffer RW1 to the RNeasy spin column, close the lid gently, and centrifuge for 
15s, at 14000rpm to wash the spin column membrane, discard the flow through. 
10. Add 500µL buffer RPE to the RNeasy spin column, close the lid gently, and centrifuge for 
15s at 14000rpm, discard the flow through. 
11. Add 500µL buffer RPE to the RNeasy spin column, close the lid gently, and centrifuge for 
2min at 14000rpm. 
12. Place the RNeasy spin column into a new 2mL collection tube and discard the old collection 
tube with the flow through. Closed the lid gently and centrifuge at full speed (14000rpm) for 
1min. 
13. Place the RNeasy spin column into a new RNAse-free 1.5mL Eppendorf collection tube 
(supplied).   
14. Add 30 to 50µL RNase free water directly to the spin column membrane. Close the lid gen-
tly, incubate 1min on ice, and then centrifuge for 1min, at 14000rpm. 
15. If expected RNA yield is more than 30µg or using the eluate from step 15 (if high RNA 
concentration is required). 
16. Measure the concentration on the Nanodrop and store RNA samples at -80 for further stud-
ies.
