Abstract
Augmented Reality (AR) has seen growth both in the academia and the industry, with
a large range of potential beneﬁts being identiﬁed, however it is yet to be widely adopted by
organizations.
This study investigated what challenges are currently present with AR adoption, by
performing a systematic literature review. The identiﬁed challenge areas within the literature
were then combined into a framework of AR adoption challenges, which is based on technol-
ogy adoption theory, namely the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and the Technology-
Organization-Environment (TOE) Framework.
The framework was then validated through expert interviews with industry experts
from AR solution providing companies, where additional challenge areas not previously identi-
ﬁed in the literature were discovered. The framework was thus updated based on these ﬁndings,
and future areas of research were outlined.
The resulting framework of AR adoption challenges aims to support AR adopting
organizations, and AR solution providers in understanding which challenges can be experienced
throughout an AR implementation process. The framework also provides a starting point for
future researchers of AR adoption, regardless of the application area.

1
Introduction
Augmented Reality (AR) has seen tremendous growth as an upcoming technology over
the last decade, and a lot of potential beneﬁts have been identiﬁed that can be accomplished
by using said technology (Evangelista et al., 2020).
At the same time, AR technology has not yet become common place neither among
the consumers, and neither within organizations, with current implementations being limited
to speciﬁc use cases, causing a large development overhead when the same technology needs to
be applied to a new use case.
This project aims to explore the area of adoption of Augmented Reality (AR) within
organizations, speciﬁcally by investigating what challenges do AR adopting organizations and
AR solution providers need to be aware of when implementing an AR solution.
Existing
research on AR adoption exists, however it mostly focuses on challenges that are experienced
within certain industries and certain use cases.
As such, this project aims to provide an overview to organizations involved in AR
adoption regardless of the associated industry or use case, through means of performing a
systematic literature review to identify challenges with AR adoption. These challenges are then
combined within a singular framework, based on the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and
Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) framework, and evaluated through interviews
with AR industry experts. The research covers the diﬀerences found between the literature and
the industry, and provides an updated framework with insights from both of the information
sources.
The intention of the research is not only to serve as an overview for organizations
involved in AR adoption, but also to provide a starting point for researchers looking into
streamlining the process of AR adoption, as resolving the issues with the implementation of AR
can beneﬁt various sectors such as education, manufacturing, medicine, military and others.

2.1
Introduction of Augmented Reality (AR)
Augmented Reality (AR) is a concept where the users real world perception is aug-
mented by adding layers of computer-generated information.
The information can be aug-
mented through various means, commonly through augmenting visual senses, however AR can
also be used to augment hearing, touch, smell etc. (Carmigniani & Furht, 2011).
Milgram’s Reality-Virtuality continuum (Milgram & Kishino, 1994)(see ﬁgure 1),
which is a scale between reality and virtuality, provides an overview of how AR diﬀers be-
tween other similar technologies. The span between the extremes of reality and virtuality is
deﬁned as Mixed Reality (MR), which includes AR and Augmented Virtuality (AV). Based on
Milgram’s Reality-Virtuality continuum, AR is closer to the real environment, meaning that
while AR mainly utilizes the real environment, it is enhanced with virtual elements. On the
other hand, AV is closer to a virtual environment, an example of which would be a technology
like Virtual Reality (VR).
Figure 1: Milgram’s Reality Virtuality Continuum. Sourced from Milgram and Kishino (1994).
Through investigating the relevance of AR, it can be seen that AR as a technology
has grown a lot in popularity in the recent decades, both in academia and in the industry. In
terms of academia, Scopus was used to search for research publications containing the term
Augmented Reality. As can be seen in ﬁgure 2, the amount of publications seem to be on an
upward trend, indicating growing interest in the technology from the research community.

Similar pattern can be observed in the industry, as global spending on AR and VR
has increased by 50% from 2019 to 2020, with ﬁnancial analysts estimating AR and VR for the
retail industry to reach a market size of over 2 trillion USD by the year 2027 (Vardomatski,

As a part of the growing interest in AR, the beneﬁts of augmented reality have been
extensively researched within various sectors, where examples of such beneﬁts include providing
context-speciﬁc information and improving eﬃciency of manual operations in an industrial
setting (Röltgen & Dumitrescu, 2020), encouraging learning and creativity in an educational
setting (Onime et al., 2017), helping customers make more informed buying decisions in e-
commerce (Lu & Smith, 2008) to name a few.
While AR is a promising technology that has seen steady rise in interest, with valuable
beneﬁts that can potentially help organizations with their work processes, it is a relatively new
technology, and it is yet to become widely adopted by consumers and organizations alike. As
such, the focus of this research is to investigate what factors can aﬀect an organizations ability
to implement AR within their work processes.
2.2
Theories and Models on Technology Adoption
Many technologies, such as computers, internet, smartphones etc. that are now com-
monplace within the society, had at one point been considered promising new technologies
before they matured, just as AR is being considered currently. Thus, technology adoption
is a topic that has been extensively researched previously, and this section aims to provide
an overview of existing theory of models and frameworks, that have been applied to various
technologies.
3
==PAGE:10==
2.2.1
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) is a research model proposed by Davis (1989),
and has been widely used to estimate the willingness of individuals to adopt new information
technologies (Salem Al-Mamary et al., 2016). The main variables used in TAM are perceived
ease-of-use and perceived usefulness. Davis (1989) describes perceived ease-of-use as a degree to
which the user ﬁnds the technology to be free of diﬃculties in using the technology, i.e. whether
the technology is not hard to use and that the beneﬁts outweigh the diﬃculties of using the
technology, while perceived usefulness is described as a degree to which the user believes the
technology will help them to perform their job (see ﬁgure 3).
Figure 3: Technology Acceptance Model. Sourced from Miller and Khera (2010).
Additionally, researchers, including the author of the original TAM model, have ex-
panded on the model by proposing TAM2 and TAM3. The two variations of the TAM model
serve as extensions to the original TAM model, where the external variables are speciﬁed.
TAM2 was proposed by Venkatesh and Davis (2000), and it expands on TAM by
introducing speciﬁc variables that impact perceived usefulness and attitude toward using a
technology, such as subjective norm, experience, image, job relevance, output quality, result
demonstrability and voluntariness. While TAM2 focused on expanding on perceived useful-
ness, TAM3, proposed by Venkatesh and Bala (2008), expanded on the variables aﬀecting
perceived ease-of-use. These variables included computer self-eﬃcacy, perceptions of external
control, computer anxiety, computer playfulness, perceived enjoyment and objective usability.
An overview of the three TAM models can be seen in ﬁgure 4. TAM model and its variations
have since been widely used by researchers studying adoption of new and upcoming technolo-
gies.

2.2.2
Uniﬁed Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT)
Uniﬁed Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) was proposed by
Venkatesh et al. (2003).
UTAUT combines ideas from various other theories, models and
frameworks, including TAM. It proposes that performance expectancy, eﬀort expectancy and
social inﬂuence are the factors that inﬂuence the behavioral intention of an individual to use
the underlying technology, while facilitating conditions inﬂuence the use behavior (see ﬁgure
5).

2.2.3
Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) Framework
Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) framework was proposed by Tornatzky
and Fleischer (1990), and it explains 3 constructs that inﬂuence technological adoption by an
organization: Technology, Organization and Environment. Within the context of said frame-
work, the technology context includes factors such as technologies available to the organization
internally and externally, the organizational context covers factors relating to the organization
in terms of company size, organisational structure etc., and lastly the environmental context
refers to factors outside of companies control such as competition and government regulations
(see ﬁgure 6).
Figure 6: Technology-Organization-Environment (TOE) Framework. Sourced from Tornatzky
and Fleischer (1990)
6
==PAGE:13==
Compared to TAM and UTAUT, which focus on the individuals acceptance of a tech-
nology, the TOE framework focuses on technology acceptance on an organizational level (Bryan
& Zuva, 2021).
While TOE and TAM can be used in solitude, some researchers have opted to integrate
the two models when investigating technology adoption, as these two models can complement
each other (Bryan & Zuva, 2021). The TAM model focuses on the individuals acceptance,
and is ﬂexible with the external factors that inﬂuence the perceived ease of use and perceived
usefulness, while the TOE framework focuses on acceptance on an organizational level, and
provides a structure to the factors that inﬂuence said acceptance. As the goal of this study is
to provide an overview to AR adoption challenges, it makes sense to utilize a combination of
the two models, as the TOE framework provides a categorization to the factors inﬂuencing AR
adoption, while the TAM model serves deeper insight as to how each of the factors contribute
to the attitude of using AR.
2.3
Augmented Reality Adoption
Several studies have also investigated AR adoption within speciﬁc domains. One of
these studies had investigated AR adoption within the industrial sector, by utilising the TOE
framework to identify challenges and success factors within industrial AR (Masood & Egger,
2020). The study had deducted that while the technological context of TOE is the basis for
a successful industrial AR implementation, the organizational context is important as well.
A similar study was performed within the e-commerce domain, where the TOE framework
was also utilized (Chandra & Kumar, 2018). The study identiﬁed key factors inﬂuencing AR
adoption within e-commerce, and developed and evaluated a framework, which the authors
state "...can serve as a starting point for e-commerce ﬁrms to formulate their strategies around
the prominent factors, including technology competence, top management support, customer
readiness, and relative advantage." (Chandra & Kumar, 2018).
In terms of utilising TAM model for analyzing the acceptance of AR, Cabero-Almenara
et al. (2019) used TAM to analyze AR acceptance by university students in an education faculty,
and utilized the TAM instrument for evaluation purposes. The study found that the TAM
instrument possessed acceptable levels of reliability, and that the speciﬁc AR implementation
was perceived as easy to use by the students. Similar research has been done to investigate AR
acceptance in construction industry (Elshafey et al., 2020), and in training of welding engineers
(Papakostas et al., 2021), where Papakostas et al. (2021) decided to extend the TAM model by
adding two external variables, namely "Perceived Enjoyment" and "System Quality".
While a signiﬁcant amount of research has been done in AR adoption using the TAM
model, the studies using the TOE framework are rather limited and focus on speciﬁc industries,
and no studies had been identiﬁed that incorporate both of the frameworks to analyze AR
adoption. This in turn provides an additional incentive to explore how the TOE framework
and the TAM model can be integrated to analyze the adoption of AR within organizations.

3
Methodology
To investigate the factors aﬀecting AR adoption within organizations, pre-existing
technology adoption frameworks had been investigated, as described in the previous section.
Based on these ﬁndings, it was decided to follow an approach undertaken by other
researchers who have investigated technology adoption through the means of combining the
TAM and TOE framework, to better contextualize the various factors aﬀecting technology
adoption.
Using TAM as the basis of the framework, external factors inﬂuencing perceived use-
fulness and perceived ease-of-use can be categorized within the technology, organization and
environment contexts.
Thus, the ﬁrst step was to identify the factors, i.e. challenges, that aﬀect AR adoption,
and the chosen method to do so was to perform a systematic literature review.
3.1
Review Process
The main objective of the systematic literature review is to gather the challenges that
have been identiﬁed within the literature and potential identiﬁed solutions to these challenges.
As such, this literature review aims to answer the following research questions:
• What challenges have been identiﬁed when it comes to Augmented Reality implementation
in organizations?
• How can these challenges be addressed?
The literature review approach used within this study is the Preferred Reporting
Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) (Page et al., 2021), by using the
PRISMA 2020 Checklist which can be found on PRISMA’s website1.
3.1.1
Sources & Inclusion Criteria
In terms of the sources of the literature, due to accessibility it was decided that the
Scopus database was going to be utilized to identify the studies, while the universities electronic
library was used to source the studies. To meet the inclusion criteria, it was deduced that a
study had to meet the following criteria:
• Study must explicitly address challenges with the process of AR implementation
• Study must be written in English
• Study must be written in the years 2010-2020
• Exclude studies that do not focus on organizations/businesses
• The main technology that the study focuses on must be AR

3.1.3
Selection Process
Once the studies were gathered based on the search strategy, a selection process was
then undertaken to exclude studies which did not meet the inclusion criteria.
The ﬁrst round of screening was used to exclude all of the studies that are unrelated
to AR. This was done by analyzing the title, abstract and author keywords to see whether the
concept of AR was present. Where necessary, other contents of the study were investigated to
conﬁrm or reject the selection criteria.
The second round of screening was used to exclude the studies where AR was not
the main focus of the study.
For a study to be included based on this criteria, the study
must have focused on AR technology as a whole, e.g., if a study had explored the concept of
“Computer vision algorithm for object tracking”, and mentioned AR as one of the uses for this
concept, the study did not pass the criteria, as it had only researched a single component of
the AR technology, rather than AR technology as a whole. In addition to the title, abstract
and author keywords, introduction and conclusion were analyzed from this point on, as well as
other contents of the study if it was deemed necessary.
The ﬁnal round of screening was used to exclude studies which did not include an
implementation within or by an organization.
The selection was not limited to a speciﬁc
type of an organization, and thus studies could address implementations within businesses,
10
==PAGE:17==
educational institutions, start-ups etc., as the study aims to encompass challenges that can
occur in adopting organizations regardless of their size, industry, etc.

Data Items & Data Collection Process
After the selection process was done, the included studies were read in full, analyzed,
and information deemed relevant was collected.
The following data items were collected from each study:

2. Author(s)
3. Year
4. Country/region
5. Industry/ﬁeld
6. Type of organization

Items 1, 2, 3, 11 and 12 are the items that were necessary to answer the research
question of this literature review, however additional items were added to allow the research to
capture additional insights in regards to the AR implementations.
3.1.5
Data Synthesis
In terms of data synthesis, existing systematic literature reviews based on technology
adoption were researched, to gain a better understanding of the various methods that can be
utilized.
Some of the studies had chosen to separate the studies based on the research topic,
and then to describe the topics individually (Novais et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2018), however a
large amount of technology adoption studies had instead chosen to identify themes within the
research, and then to describe them individually (Batubara et al., 2018; Karakaya & Sriwan-
nawit, 2015). Additionally, some of the studies choosing this approach would describe how the
themes intertwine with each other (Brous et al., 2020; Scott Kruse et al., 2018).
The approach undertaken within this study was that once data collection was done,
the challenges collected were analyzed. Themes were identiﬁed within these challenges, and
11
==PAGE:18==
they were thus combined into groups called challenge areas, in order to quantify the ﬁndings.
The data was then combined in a table to provide an overview, and connections with a previ-
ously described challenge areas would be identiﬁed. Additionally, the year, country/region and
industry/ﬁeld were visualized to provide the context of these studies.
3.2
Framework Design
As described in section 2.2.3, when evaluating adoption of other technologies, some
researchers have used a hybrid model combining the TAM model and the TOE framework.
Due to the ﬂexibility of both frameworks, it was decided to follow the same approach, in an
attempt to provide more contextual information to AR adopting organizations and/or AR
solution providers.
As such, the framework of AR adoption challenges was made to be a combination
of the TAM and TOE framework.
Once the challenge areas had been identiﬁed from the
SLR, they would be aligned with the three TOE contexts: technological, organizational and
environmental, based on the deﬁnition of each context. The challenge areas were then analyzed
to identify whether a connection can be made between the challenge area and either perceived
ease-of-use or perceived usability, with external factors speciﬁed in TAM2 and TAM3 models
being used as the arguments for why such a connection can be established.
3.3
Interviews & Evaluation
Based on the developed framework, the interview process was used to gauge whether
the industry perceives the same challenges as had been identiﬁed within the literature. The
interviews were carried out using a semi-structured approach, where the interview questions
were used as a guideline. The interview questions can be seen in appendix A.1.
Within the context of this study, experts from AR solution providing companies had
been interviewed. The ﬁrst step in the interviews was to gather contextual information regard-
ing the company and the experts employment at said company. Then, a general question was
asked about the challenges that the companies experience with AR technology adoption. The
purpose of this question was to start an open ended discussion, giving the expert a chance to
talk about challenges without any bias, allowing the experts to express the challenges that they
have experienced which might not have been a part of the literature.
Once the discussion had been exhausted, the interviewee would then be asked several
questions regarding the TOE contexts. The aim of this part of the interview was to expand
more on the challenges that might not have come up without a speciﬁc context, however still
without disclosing the challenges identiﬁed in the literature.
Lastly, if throughout the interview some of the challenge areas had not been brought
up by the experts, then they would be asked speciﬁcally about those challenge areas. This was
used to see what the experts opinion was on the matter, and whether they agree that it’s a
challenge that they have encountered.
12
==PAGE:19==
In terms of evaluation, a small discussion would be had at the end of the interview,
where the experts were presented with the resulting framework based on the challenge areas
identiﬁed through the SLR, and provide their feedback as to whether they think the framework
is consistent compared to their experiences within the industry.
3.4
Analysis of the results and discussion
Once the interviews were completed, they were transcribed and analyzed. The method
of analysis was similar to the approach undertaken in the data synthesis of data found in the
systematic literature review.
It was decided to code the transcript based on the emerging
themes found within the interviews, and once the themes had been identiﬁed across individual
interviews, they were merged together with other interviews, where similar themes were grouped
together in cases where it was relevant. Lastly, the themes were compared against the existing
challenge areas, with connections being made between the emerging themes and challenge areas
where it was applicable.
The results section presented the interviewed experts, as well as the themes in terms
of their relation to the challenge areas. Themes that did not ﬁt in with the challenge areas
were also presented, with the expert feedback on the framework being presented at the end of
the section.
The interviews provide another angle on challenge areas within AR, and as such, the
interview ﬁndings were discussed and compared against the ﬁndings from the SLR. The diﬀer-
ences between the challenge areas identiﬁed in literature and in the interviews were described,
as well as an interpretation was provided as to why the challenge areas have diﬀered between
the two sources of information. The discussion also touches upon the interpretation of which
challenge areas were the most relevant for the experts.
Lastly, the framework of AR adoption challenges was updated based on these new
ﬁndings and on the technology acceptance theory, with study limitations and future works
being discussed afterwards.

4.2.2
Costs
In terms of costs, several studies specify that initial costs of acquiring hardware and
developing software are too high (Iatsyshyn et al., 2020; Rovithis et al., 2019; Upadhyay &
Khandelwal, 2018), while others also mention the impacting costs of maintaining and updating
the systems (Alarcon et al., 2020; Rovithis et al., 2019). Similarly to the technical challenges,
a study indicates that costs will lower with time, as AR moves more into mainstream adoption
(Upadhyay & Khandelwal, 2018).
Based on these ﬁndings, with costs being too high for acquiring hardware and de-
veloping software, it can be deduced that organizations who choose to save on these costs
might run into technical challenges, speciﬁcally with limitations of features if the software is
underdeveloped or inappropriate hardware is chosen.
4.2.3
Missing Standardization
The studies have indicated that there is a lack of standardization across multiple areas
in AR.
One such area that is not standardized is the hardware selection (Caricato et al.,
2014; Hadar, 2018; Lorenz et al., 2018), with one of the studies delving deeper into the subject
by proposing a framework that allows managers and researchers to evaluate the selection of
the devices based on their technological, process and organizational aspects (Caricato et al.,
2014). The challenge with hardware standardization was further supported by a study, where
an appropriate selection of hardware was not possible, as none of the devices that the author
had identiﬁed could match the speciﬁed requirements (Lorenz et al., 2018).
Another area that is lacking standardization is software selection (Alarcon et al., 2020;
Vogt & Shingles, 2013), it’s development (Iatsyshyn et al., 2020) and maintenance (Ullo et al.,
2019). The potential solutions for the lack of software standardization include implementing
appropriate data storage and management standards during AR development (Ullo et al., 2019)
and having an organization undertake the task of standardization either by creating a platform
that could encompass the necessary use cases for a speciﬁc domain (Ling, 2017; Vogt & Shingles,
2013), or by having a large organization such as Google lead the way in terms of overall
standardization in AR (Siltanen & Aikala, 2012).
By resolving standardization, it could also minimize the costs and resources spent
associated with AR solutions, as developers would spend less time on implementing specialized
solutions for each component required for a speciﬁc use case. An example of that would be the
previously mentioned potential solution regarding data storage and management by Ullo et al.
(2019) as it would be one area that developers would not need to implement.
4.2.4
AR Content Creation
More than a quarter of the studies have identiﬁed issues with AR content. There
seems to be a lack of AR content that can be used immediately (Onime et al., 2017; Sirohi
21
==PAGE:28==
et al., 2020; Upadhyay & Khandelwal, 2018), as well as there are challenges with creating AR
content (Godet-Bar et al., 2010; Hadar, 2018; Jee et al., 2014; Jetter et al., 2018; Jiao et al.,
2013; Lapointe et al., 2020; McCorkle, 2018; Mourtzis et al., 2017; Quint et al., 2017). Some
of the studies have indicated that the process of creating content speciﬁcally for AR is time
consuming (Bosch et al., 2020; Dini & Mura, 2015; Freudenthal et al., 2011; Ling, 2017), with
other studies supporting the notion by indicating that creating AR content has a high degree
of complexity (Masood & Egger, 2020; Petros et al., 2010; Vogt & Shingles, 2013).
There have been various solutions proposed as to how the challenge of AR content
creation can be alleviated. Some suggest that developing easy to use authoring tools that can
be used by non-experts would allow for rapid development of AR content (Hadar, 2018; Jee
et al., 2014; Petros et al., 2010), while others also suggest that automatic generation through
speciﬁc algorithms or artiﬁcial intelligence can be a potential solution (Bosch et al., 2020;
Lapointe et al., 2020; Mourtzis et al., 2017). Another approach would be by potentially using
existing data for automatic generation (e.g. construction data already accessible within the
organizations systems) (Jiao et al., 2013). Lastly, a solution could be to develop AR content
by making the various components of the content as generic as possible, so that they can be
reused for development of future AR content (Freudenthal et al., 2011). Some of the studies
have also suggested that while creation of AR content is a challenge, it will be alleviated with
new technological advances (Dini & Mura, 2015; Vogt & Shingles, 2013).
Based on these ﬁndings, it can be deduced that the diﬃculty of AR content creation
could also be alleviated by standardization, as the use of a standardized authoring tool, together
with standardized data storage and management, could allow non-expert users to get familiar
with the authoring tool and allow them to create AR content across various industries. A
standardized authoring tool would also directly lower the costs that are currently associated
with AR content creation.
4.2.5
Privacy & Security Concerns
Various studies have expressed concerns in regards to privacy and security (Berkemeier
et al., 2019; Carmigniani et al., 2011; Irshad & Rambli, 2015; Masood & Egger, 2019; Roesner
et al., 2014; White et al., 2014). AR devices, similarly to smartphones have a lot of ways of
gathering data from users including but not limited to visual and audio information (Roesner et
al., 2014). One of the studies proposed an AR design framework that puts emphasis on privacy
protection, while other studies have suggested adopting a similar strategy to smartphones,
which use permission systems to allow the user to change which data an application can use,
can be a potential solution to the privacy and security concerns (Carmigniani et al., 2011;
Roesner et al., 2014). One of these studies, however, indicates that this solution would work
for the short term, but for the long term more research would be needed to identify at which
points should data be collected and how this data should be used.
While having established privacy and security mechanisms in an AR solution does
not necessarily indicate cost savings, it can negate the ﬁnancial and organizational risk that an
22
==PAGE:29==
organization might experience if these mechanisms were not in place.
4.2.6
Health & Safety Concerns
In terms of health and safety, the concern that was identiﬁed the most was that using
AR technology can immerse the user in the virtual content, thus causing the user to distract
from his surroundings, which can cause issues if AR is used e.g. in a surgical or automotive
setting (Aslan et al., 2019; Dixon et al., 2013; Gabbard et al., 2014; Onime et al., 2017; Quint
et al., 2017). A solution to such an issue could be to use "on-demand" AR instead of full-time
AR, meaning that augmented content would only be present if it’s necessary for the user within
that moment in time (Dixon et al., 2013).
Other challenges identiﬁed were ethical concerns (Berkemeier et al., 2019; Carmigniani
et al., 2011; White et al., 2014), general concerns regarding health and well-being that can be
inﬂuenced by various factors such as static postures and limited ﬁeld of vision (Garrett et al.,
2018), and AR technology potentially not being suited for prolonged use (Jetter et al., 2018;
Qeshmy et al., 2019). A potential solution could be found by further investigating the potential
long-term impact of AR and reﬁning safety and ergonomic process guidelines (Garrett et al.,
2018).
Resolving health & safety concerns can be crucial for identiﬁcation of appropriate use
cases, as if a solution is not able to address said concerns, it might be excluded from being used
in domains where health & safety risks are high, e.g. surgical and automotive domains.
4.2.7
Ease of Use
Some of the studies have identiﬁed a challenge in terms of ease of use (An et al., 2020;
Koubek & Procházka, 2012; Lau et al., 2019; Martins et al., 2015; Neumann et al., 2020),
with one of them delving deeper into factors that should be considered when evaluating an AR
system, with factors concerning ease of use being system interaction, application interface and
representation (Martins et al., 2015).
Ease of use is also related to usability, as these factors might lead to lower motivation
by the end-users to use the system.
4.2.8
Usability
Usability is a challenge area that has also been identiﬁed (Berkemeier et al., 2019;
Cheng & Tsai, 2013; Jiao et al., 2013; Lau et al., 2019; Martins et al., 2015), with one study
identifying factors aﬀecting usability as motivation and eﬀort, spatial association and general
functionality (Martins et al., 2015). While studies have identiﬁed usability as a challenge for
AR, usability is a wide term encompassing various underlying factors, commonly described to
include the 5 "E’s": eﬀectiveness, eﬃciency, engagement, error tolerance, ease of learning 2,
which are intertwined with already identiﬁed challenge areas such as technical challenges, ease
2What is Usability? - https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/usability
23
==PAGE:30==
of use, etc. As such, while it is being included as a challenge area for the time being, it is
arguable whether usability warrants it’s own technical challenge.
4.2.9
Integration with Other Systems & Maintenance of the System
Another challenge that has been suggested is integration with other systems (Alarcon
et al., 2020; Capodieci et al., 2016; Masood & Egger, 2019; Milligan et al., 2019; Ohlig et
al., 2020) and maintenance of the system (Ullo et al., 2019). Potential proposed solutions to
integration were remodelling the underlying business process to utilize both the existing and
AR systems in unison (Capodieci et al., 2016), and having a software provider for the AR
system who is a key partner of the organization (Ohlig et al., 2020).
By properly integrating an AR solution with existing systems that an organization
uses, costs can be negated by minimizing the amount of overhead required to maintain not only
the AR system, but also it’s connectivity with pre-existing systems, as well as removing the
possibility of data being lost due to having two separate data storage systems associated with
each solution.
4.2.10
Training of End-Users
Training of end-users is a challenge area that has been suggested as well (Barroso-
Osuna et al., 2019; Carmigniani et al., 2011; Iatsyshyn et al., 2020; López-Belmonte et al.,
2020; Moser et al., 2019; Zelinska et al., 2018), with a study indicating that the end-users
might lack the necessary skills to use AR (Neumann et al., 2020), and the learning curve
might be too high for some end-users (Alarcon et al., 2020). Additionally, one study indicated
that there is a lack of eﬀective AR training solutions (Upadhyay & Khandelwal, 2018). No
speciﬁc solution has been identiﬁed within the literature apart from measuring the end-user
competencies and developing appropriate training regimens (Neumann et al., 2020).
It can be argued that challenge of training of end-users inversely correlates to how
easy to use the system is.
The easier the system is to use, the less amount of training is
required, thus also leading to potential cost savings that would otherwise be spent on training
the end-users.
4.2.11
Identiﬁcation of Appropriate Use Cases
Several studies indicate that there are challenges with identifying appropriate use cases
where AR is actually beneﬁcial (Jetter et al., 2018; Masood & Egger, 2019; Smith et al., 2019),
as in some cases adding AR to an existing use case might not provide much beneﬁt to improve
the process, but rather might have been added due to it’s novelty (Grzegorczyk et al., 2019; Liu
et al., 2010; Rovithis et al., 2019). One of the challenges with identifying appropriate use cases
is deﬁning the appropriate requirements (Freudenthal et al., 2011), which can potentially be
solved using a user centric design approach during the development of the system (Freudenthal
et al., 2011), as well as utilising interviews and hands-on sessions (Neumann et al., 2020). Lastly,
24
==PAGE:31==
each function of the use case should be properly assessed, to avoid poor implementation and
user engagement (Smith et al., 2019). As such, it can be deduced that improper identiﬁcation
of appropriate use cases can directly lead to technical challenges, issues with ease of use and
usability of the system.
4.2.12
User Acceptance
Studies have also expressed concern with the end-user acceptance (Berkemeier et al.,
2019; Koubek & Procházka, 2012; Masood & Egger, 2019, 2020; Stoltz et al., 2017), with some
of the speciﬁc challenges being social acceptance of AR devices (Aslan et al., 2019; Carmigniani
et al., 2011; Woltering et al., 2020), worries about job losses due to AR technology (Jetter et al.,
2018) and suspicion against new technologies in general (Rovithis et al., 2019), while some users
also have an overall negative perception of AR technology (McCorkle, 2018). A solution for
worries about job losses and suspicion against new technologies can be alleviated by utilizing
communication strategies that aim to boost conﬁdence and emphasize individual beneﬁts and
possibilities of using AR (Jetter et al., 2018; McCorkle, 2018).
4.2.13
Other
This section contains challenges that were identiﬁed within the literature, that did
not ﬁt within other challenge areas, and had 3 or less studies addressing them.
In certain use cases, collaboration between multiple end-users is necessary, however in
the AR solutions that were utilized, the collaboration required additional development or was
simply not possible (Jiao et al., 2013; Milligan et al., 2019; Neumann et al., 2020). Integrating
AR into an existing system, which already allows collaboration, might be a solution (Jiao et al.,
2013), while one of the studies identiﬁed that a device, that was in development at the time,
does allow collaboration and should be used for these purposes (Milligan et al., 2019).
One of the challenges was that employees have variable proﬁciency levels, and thus
when they use AR, they expect a diﬀerent set of instructions based on their level of experience
(Bosch et al., 2020). The proposed solution was to develop the system in such a way, where
the system can adapt to the user (Bosch et al., 2020).
Other issues included lack of public awareness of AR technologies (Sirohi et al., 2020),
perceived enjoyment in end-users (Lau et al., 2019), and long term stability of the AR solution
(Vogt & Shingles, 2013).

Framework Design
To provide an overview of the identiﬁed challenge areas, a framework was designed
based on the TAM and TOE framework. Starting oﬀwith the TAM, the framework for AR
adoption challenges utilizes the concepts of "Perceived Usefulness" (PU), "Perceived Ease of
Use" (PEOU) and "Attitude Towards Using AR". Within the original TAM deﬁnition, PU and
PEOU are inﬂuenced by external variables, however they are not deﬁned. This, thus provides
a degree of ﬂexibility in terms of the variables that inﬂuence an individuals intention to use a
technology.
Since this study is investigating AR adoption within organizations, the TOE frame-
work is useful as it focuses on the speciﬁc contexts that aﬀect technology acceptance on an
organizational level. Thus, combining the two frameworks is beneﬁcial to utilize the strengths
of each framework, and provide further insights in AR adoption within organizations. The TOE
contexts provide a categorization of the external factors, in this case challenges, while the TAM
concepts provide a further insight as to how each of the factors contribute to PU and PEOU,
which in turn contribute to the attitude towards using AR. As such, the external factors were
divided into the three TOE contexts.
Once the basis of the framework was done, and the challenge areas had been identiﬁed
from the SLR, the challenge areas were added to the framework based on the TOE context
they belong in, as well as initial connections between the challenge areas and PU & PEOU
have been made based on the TAM2 and TAM3 deﬁnitions of external factors (see ﬁgure 11).
Figure 11: Framework of AR adoption challenges based on the SLR.

In terms of the challenge areas, some of them had not been put within a TOE context,
namely "Ease of Use" and "User Acceptance". The decision to not add them was made because
"Ease of Use" is already represented in the framework through the "Perceived Ease-of-use"
concept from the TAM model, while "User acceptance" is perceived to be the same concept as
the "Attitude towards using AR".
The technical challenges identiﬁed in the literature directly relate to the technology
context from the TOE framework, as having technical issues limits the organizations availabil-
ity of the said technology if an issue is encountered. From the TAM perspective, technical
challenges can be related to the "Objective Usability" factor as deﬁned in TAM3, which in turn
connects to PEOU.
Missing standardization, AR content creation and usability have also been added to
the technology context in terms of the TOE aspect, as missing standardization is a barrier for
initial technology availability, while AR content creation limits an organizations possibility to
adapt an existing AR solution to a diﬀerent use case. Usability was also related to technology
availability, as low usability could make the solution unavailable to the organization if users are
not able to use it eﬃciently. Missing standardization and AR content creation areas were not
connected to a TAM concept, as no clear connection could be identiﬁed through the literature,
however usability directly relates to the "Objective Usability" factor of the TAM3 model, which
connects to PEOU.
The factors added to the organizational context within the TOE aspect include costs,
integration with other systems & maintenance of the system, identiﬁcation of appropriate use
cases, training of end-users and health & safety concerns, as they relate to the characteristics
and the resources that an organization possesses.
Costs relate directly to the organizations resources within the TOE context, however
no clear connection had been made to either PU or PEOU.
Integration with other systems & maintenance of the system relate to the size of the
organization, the available skill set of the employees, and the underlying business processes,
which is why it was added to the organizational context. Additionally, this challenge area was
connected to Perceived Ease-of-use, as it relates to the concept of "Objective Usability" from
TAM3.
Identiﬁcation of appropriate use cases was placed in the organizational context as it
directly depends on the characteristics of the underlying organization, including their industry,
organizational structure and work processes. It was also connected to the Perceived Usability,
as it relates to concepts of "Job Relevance", "Output Quality" and "Result Demonstrability"
within the TAM2 model.
Training of end-users are in the organizational context as it is a matter of the com-
petencies of employees, size of the company and the resources the company possesses, as, for
example, training a single employee requires a lot less structure and resources compared to
training thousands of employees. Training of end-users challenge area has also been connected
to PEOU, as the challenge area relates to "Computer Self-eﬃcacy" within the TAM3 model.

Lastly, privacy & security challenge area was the only challenge placed within the
environmental context, as in terms of privacy they need to comply with regulatory bodies such

is outside of organizations control. This challenge area had also been connected to PEOU as it
relates to the concepts of "Perception of External Control" and "Computer Anxiety".
At this point in time, other challenge areas were not connected to the TAM concepts of

Interview Results
In total, 5 interviews were performed, with 6 experts from 4 diﬀerent companies being
interviewed.
The interviews were performed in a semi-structured way, where the question guide was
used as a means to start a discussion regarding the topic of AR adoption challenges. The aim
of these discussions was to allow the experts to disclose the challenges that immediately come
to their mind, instead of biasing them towards a speciﬁc challenge area. Once the discussions
had been exhausted however, the experts would be asked about speciﬁc challenges that were
found in the literature, that had not yet been discussed in the interview. Lastly, the framework
of AR adoption challenges was presented, and the experts provided their feedback.
6.1
Description of the companies and the interviewed experts
Experts A, B, C, D are a part of a Company #1, which is a company that provides the
companies within the retail industry with a subset of AR technology called WebAR. WebAR
is a type of AR which can be accessed through your mobile browser, and is further described
in the next section. Expert D speciﬁcally, is the founder and the CEO of said company, and
describes their responsibilities as leading the team, and spending time with their customers
and other organizations that inquire about AR, educating them on how they can utilize this
technology.
Expert E is employed at Company #2 as a Global Business Development Lead, and
they describe their role at the company as follows: "...it’s very much a commercial driven role,
trying to ﬁnd new business opportunities. [...] seeing how can the customer buying journey [...]
be enhanced with the use of augmented reality.". The expert has an extensive background in
ﬁlm, and had decided to switch to AR with the rise of 360 degree video, as it looked like a
promising immersive medium for storytelling speciﬁcally. The company in question is an AR
agency, that helps other companies, regardless of their size, by developing their AR content and
strategies, and has worked with various Fortune 500 clients. Speciﬁcally, the company focuses
more on AR systems used for marketing and advertisement.
Expert F is a co-founder and a CEO of an AR/VR production studio (Company
#3), which creates experiences for organizations across various industries and is exploring
the possibilities of AR/VR in businesses and institutions.
While describing an example of
their work, Expert F stated that Company #3 is currently working on a municipality AR
application, which is used to show citizens about how a new ﬂooding system will assist them
once the construction is done, which aims to improve the citizens understanding and patience
in regards to construction work performed.
Lastly, Expert G is a founder and CEO of Company #4, which provides an easy-to-use
AR platform, that can be used by non-experts. The companies current focus is on education,
where students of all ages can create AR presentations on diﬀerent subjects. Another focus
area is within advertising and communication, which can be used to distribute AR experiences
29
==PAGE:36==
through a web URL, with a similar concept that Company #1 utilizes.
6.2
WebAR
As WebAR had not been researched as a concept within the context of this study, the
experts were asked to introduce how WebAR works, and how it compares against common AR
implementations.
The diﬀerence between common AR solutions and WebAR is that WebAR allows
the end-users to visualize AR content directly through their mobile browser.
Due to such
implementation, the functionality is more limited compared to what can be achieved through a
dedicated AR mobile application or an AR application on an AR headset, however the beneﬁts
of it prove useful in use cases such as marketing, product presentation and others. Expert
B indicated that WebAR is not an optimal solution at this moment for use cases where high
interactivity is necessary, for example in a production company where an AR solution would
allow you to select diﬀerent points of interest and retrieve all kinds of information. On the other
hand, they state that WebAR can save clients a large amount of money where such interactivity
is not needed, and the implementations can still fulﬁll the clients needs. Adding onto the topic,
the experts from company #1 state that they are quickly advancing this method of delivering
AR content to the consumers, as they have been able to create WebAR implementations that
use animations and sound, and see a lot more potential within the ﬁeld due to internet speeds
getting faster.
The following sections describe the themes identiﬁed from the interviews. Initially,
the themes are described on the basis of the three TOE contexts present within the framework
of AR adoption challenges, with other identiﬁed themes being discussed after that.
6.3
Technology context
6.3.1
Technical challenges
Technical challenges are a challenge area that was identiﬁed in the literature, belonging
in the technology context part of the framework of AR adoption challenges (see ﬁgure 11).
Through the interviews, there were several themes identiﬁed relating to this challenge area,
some relating to the technical issues with development, while others relating to hardware and
software limitations present at the current time.
To gain an understanding of how the interviewed experts and their companies under-
take a task of creating an AR solution for a client, the experts revealed pieces of information
regarding how their developmental process works.
Company #3 and #4 follow a similar approach, where they work closely with the
client during the development process. Company #3 utilized this approach within their solution
relating to visualizing municipalities new ﬂooding systems, by utilizing the knowledge provided
from the client. Company #4 also explicitly mentioned that they utilize the "Lean Startup"
30
==PAGE:37==
methodology, and within their project for education, they also displayed AR demos to students
and teachers, to gather their feedback and improve their product.
On the other hand, company #2 outsources their development to a major technology
provider, and instead positions themselves as a company that provides demos for the interested
clients, and develops their AR strategy. Interestingly, this approach had been suggested within
the literature as a solution to technical challenges for the adopting organisations (Dini & Mura,
2015; Grzegorczyk et al., 2019; Mourtzis et al., 2014) (see section 4.2.1). While company #2
is a solution provider, it seems that they have found a position where they are able identify
solutions for interested companies while connecting them with the technology provider.
In terms of speciﬁc challenges that they had experienced, a large focus was placed on
hardware issues and developmental problems, which is logical considering that the interviewed
companies are AR solution providers. Due to all of the experts working in AR solution providing
companies, they were not able to provide much insight as to what technical challenges can
be experienced from an adopting organizations perspective, however it is useful to present
regardless due to some adopting organizations developing their own in-house solutions, thus
these challenges can still be applicable.
The issues relating to hardware have mainly been described in regards due to the
available technology still being relatively immature. When talking about available current AR
hardware, Expert F stated "The HoloLens is made for innovation projects [...] I have a hard
time seeing how, well, how much beneﬁt they all do out in the actual world, you know, on a
big scale. But in an innovation project, they make a lot of sense.". When explaining more in
regards to the HoloLens AR device, the same expert said "[...] the price of the HoloLens, the
fragility and battery lifetime and all, you know, hardware barriers. I think those are deﬁnitely
something to take into account.". The expert later on added, that additional concern relating to
hardware is that as with a lot of other new technologies, hardware gets outdated very quickly,
to which the expert claims that "it’s a fear". By analyzing this statement, it can be interpreted
that both the solution providers, and the client organizations might have a fear of committing
to a speciﬁc hardware solution, as replacing hardware every few years can be a long, resource-
consuming process.
Expert G additionally strengthens the point of AR being an immature technology, by
using an analogy comparing AR to the medium of video:"[...] it feels like if AR was was video
right now, what we’re talking about is, ’is this video in HD, 4K, 8K? What is the frame rate?’
All these things, and we haven’t gotten to the point where we talk about what’s actually in the
video. So we need to get there. Before it starts to really make sense for companies. We need to
get past the bus and get into the bus.". This statement provides an insight into the diﬃculty of
identifying the appropriate use cases where the technology can be used, while the technology
is still so relatively new for organizations.
Adding to the technical challenges, from the software development perspective Expert
F discussed that there are various compatibility issues when developing AR solutions for mobile
devices with diﬀerent versions of Apple’s iOS and Android, and that not only the mobile

operating system version updates can impact the solutions quite heavily, additionally version
updates within the developmental environment can cause the same challenges.
Lastly in terms of technical challenges, due to two out of the four companies (#1 and
#4) developing WebAR solutions for web-browsers, internet speeds play a big role for the ﬁnal
solutions. The utilized assets had to be constrained to 15-20 megabytes as a maximum (15MB
identiﬁed as a limit by Expert B, while 20MB identiﬁed as a limit by Expert G), however

And it’s just a matter of, you know, making

6.3.2
Missing Standardization
Touching upon the area of missing standardization, one of the prevalent issues men-
tioned by the experts was that 3D models are stored in various diﬀerent formats, where at
the same time diﬀerent devices are require diﬀerent formats. As pointed out by Expert G,
the issue with that is that these 3D models are not easily converted, as during this conversion
process the 3D content might lose audio, animations and textures. This indirectly resembles
the information available in the literature, as diﬀerent 3D formats can be attributed to lack of
standardization of data storage and management. This issue is further supported by Expert B,
who states: "We’re having to deal with using two diﬀerent formats, one for Android, and one
for iOS. That’s the downside...". Experts A and B did later on explain, that they have found
a conversion solution that works for their use cases, however Expert B still instilled that "[...]
So there are ways to make these models accessible, no matter what device you’re on, it’s just a
little bit of a hurdle, and it’s something we’re still perfecting.".
Additionally, when Expert D was asked about standardization in the ﬁeld of AR, they
went on to state: "There isn’t any.". They delved deeper into the matter by mentioning that
the lack of standardization can be seen in terms of how the user interacts with the device.
They described that with current technology, users have learned the ways of interacting with
the technology whether it would be by means of keyboard or by touchscreens, however such
progress has not been seen in AR just yet. They continue: "How do you show all of these
things, there are no standards for that yet. So we need to practice and ﬁnd the standards for
that.". Lastly, Expert D mentioned that the companies that could provide this standardization
could be companies that develop Content Management Systems, which relates to the ﬁndings
within the literature, as one of the proposed solutions identiﬁed was that a large company could
undertake the task of standardization with other companies following suit.
Expert F supported the issues with standardization by expressing: "[...]
a lot of
standardizations and tools are not working that well, in my mind.", however they also described
that it is not a concern that is usually brought on by their clients: "I don’t think it’s a big
decision making thing in companies, as they often don’t know the pros and cons of diﬀerent
32
==PAGE:39==
standards and things like that.". This statement also supports a challenge described in section
6.4.6, as the lack of education among the stakeholders of an AR implementation leads to a
lack of awareness of which hardware, software and data management/storage solutions would
position their solution as sustainable over a longer period of time when inevitable upgrades and
maintenance need to be performed.
6.3.3
AR Content Creation
Due to the way that companies have operated so far, a lot of them have not had the
need to create 3D models, especially ones that can be used in an AR solution. As Expert G
points out: "So that’s a problem, that most organizations aren’t used to creating 3D content.
It’s only some game companies who are, but they have been doing it for a very long time.".
Literature had identiﬁed that a potential solution could be to utilize existing data, which
multiple experts also support.
When asked about the time it takes to create AR content, Expert A replied: "[...] it
would probably vary quite a lot depending on what we have access to, other versions of it like
a CAD model or another 3D model, or if we have access to the real thing, if we have access
to materials and so on.", with Expert E delving further into the matter by stating: "[...] they
still have a lot of their assets in 2D, or they just have photographs. And you know, is that asset
ready or not prepared? And if they have them, they still need to be optimized to some degree.
And I think that is a problem.". This statement however displays that using pre-existing data
is not as clear cut, as the data still needs to be prepared and optimized for AR, even if the
adopting company has a complete 3D model.
Adding on to the subject of AR content creation, Expert A pointed out that the
diﬀerence of having pre-existing data for their use cases creates a diﬀerence of a matter of days,
however further on the expert mentioned that for more complex projects it might make a bigger
diﬀerence. "[...] but it’s deﬁnitely not a week, so it’s a matter of days. [...] Yeah. If they are
rigged, animated characters, then it’s deﬁnitely a longer project, but for regular products - not
that big."
6.3.4
Usability
In terms of usability, Expert F commented that issues with usability can have a large
impact on the solution. In a project that they participated in, they explained that they had
issues with getting kids to understand how to properly use an AR application, which in turn
lead to the application scrapping the AR functionality altogether and focusing on 360 video
and games instead. The expert further stated: "[...] this is something that people need to get
used to. So it’s a barrier, but an overcomeable barrier."
While other experts didn’t speciﬁcally bring up usability, based on the factors identi-
ﬁed within the literature that aﬀect usability, such as motivation, eﬀort, spatial association and
general functionality, it could be deduced that these factors are intertwined with other challenge
33
==PAGE:40==
areas discussed within the interviews. For example, motivation can be related to the attitude
of using AR, eﬀort could be connected to the Perceived Ease of Use, and general functionality
can be related to technical challenges. As such, the results from the expert interviews could
not conﬁrm or reject the notion that usability should be it’s own challenge area.
6.4
Organizational Context
6.4.1
Costs
Costs are a challenge area identiﬁed by the literature, that are related to the orga-
nizational context of AR adoption challenges. When describing costs, Expert G stated that
creating complex 3D AR content is one of the drivers of costs for an adopting organisation,
as usually they do not have the skill set within the organization to create such content. They
continued stating that this leads to the company hiring a consultant, and if said consultant
does not have an in-house 3D Modelling team, leads to them hiring external contractors, thus
quickly driving up the price. The expert states that if an organization ends up needing to spend
a lot of resources on such a project, they might end up not doing it at all, or doing it only once,
unless they see an unnaturally high return on investment.
Additionally, Expert B states that the best way to save costs as an adopting company
would be to develop internally instead of outsourcing development, and has been seen with
some companies starting to create dedicated AR teams, however they ﬁnish by stating that
"[...] but yeah, that still takes time. It’s something that where you need the team to be solely
dedicated on that.". When describing WebAR however, Expert B did indicate that the method
of delivering content through a browser saves a lot on development time, thus minimizing clients
monetary expenses.
6.4.2
Integration with Other Systems & Maintenance of the System
In terms of integration with other systems and maintenance of the system, Expert B
had pointed out that at times AR adopting organizations have issues with performing simple
integrations, for example adding a piece of HTML code into the client’s own website. As the
expert states: "I think we’ve overestimated our client’s ability to just take a piece of HTML
and put it on the website.". While this type of integration is common to WebAR, ultimately
such integrations are much simpler to integrate into existing systems compared to a dedicated
AR application, which is an advantage of WebAR. In the case of company #4, their developed
solution hosts the AR content on their own website, thus the adopting organization does not
need to integrate anything, rather the content can be accessed through a URL. Expert G states:
"Because the beauty of [PLATFORM NAME REDACTED] is it’s just a web link. And you don’t
need to integrate a web link anyway.".
Expert G also mentioned that integration with other systems if the target solution is
a dedicated AR application is much harder, which is supported by Expert D who states: "[...]
nobody has a Content Management System in place, or a Product Information Management
34
==PAGE:41==
System in place, which can just host 3D models that can be used for augmented reality, they
don’t have that. None of the systems support that.". The implications of it are that integrating
with existing IT systems is currently a challenge that will require a dedicated developmental
eﬀort to implement.
Lastly, in terms of the maintenance aspect of the challenge area, Expert E pointed
out that implementing AR into an organization is not a one oﬀthing, as software updates will
eventually require maintenance, with each software update posing a risk of creating bugs within
the AR implementation: "So it’s not really a one oﬀ, you know, once you start that journey,
then you have to be able to support it adequately into the future."
6.4.3
Identiﬁcation of Appropriate Use Cases
Identiﬁcation of appropriate use cases was a big topic of discussion with all of the
experts, with several themes emerging from them.
AR seems to be in a position where organizations and consumers understand that
it has the potential to provide valuable beneﬁts, however these beneﬁts are largely unknown.
Expert G described it as such: "So augmented reality is a new medium and a new technology.
So often when I talk to candidates, they know they want AR, but they don’t know what they
want at all.". They went on to further describe that in their experience, a lot of organizations
end up using AR as a "checkbox project", meaning that the organization mainly ends up using
it to showcase potential clients that they use AR, instead of actually deriving beneﬁts from
using AR.
Other experts have also touched upon the sentiment, with Expert B stating that while
companies might want to use AR for the novelty of it at this point in time, it is only temporary,
as at the end of the day companies and organizations alike need to see a clear path to a return
on their investment. They stated: "But surely that will pass and we’ll get into the era where
people want to see numbers, they want to know for sure that it creates value.".
Experts from all four companies agreed that the key driver for companies that are
interested in adopting AR is the Return On Investment (ROI). ROI can be directly tied to the
challenge of identiﬁcation of appropriate use cases, as ﬁnding the right use case means not only
ﬁnding a use case that can be applied within an organization, but also ﬁnding a use case that
provides value to the organization.
Experts indicated that there currently is a large problem with quantifying the ROI,
due to a variety of issues being present. Due to AR being a new technology, it is hard to predict
which indicators should be measured to understand the ROI, and for already known use cases,
the eﬀects of an AR solution to bring value to the organization are largely unexplored. Expert
C stated the following relating to this: "[...] we don’t really have those big cases where, for
instance, we go out with a paper saying this company selling clothes online has made this much
more revenue after they implemented AR in their webstore". Expert D also followed on the
topic by stating that even when the value of an AR solution is proven to an individual, it is a
complex process to prove the beneﬁts of an AR solution and it’s use case to the whole client
35

organization. They mentioned: "[...] more often we inspire somebody at some level, and then

6.4.4
Training of End-Users
When asked about the training of end-users, Expert E mentioned that due to AR
being a new technology, training of end-users will be relevant for the time being, especially in
circumstances where the goal of the AR solution is to enhance the customer buying journey in
marketing.
Similarly to what was already touched upon in section 6.3.2 by Expert D, Expert F
states that the requirement of training will lessen over time, as current issues are due to AR
being a new technology. Using an analogy of the touchscreen, and how the majority of the
users have gotten used to the medium, Expert F states: "[...] so all these things I think will
happen. It’s happening already now.".

While training of end-users can be a necessity for the time being, it can be deducted
that the amount of training required is inversely correlated to the end-users perceived ease-of-
use. As such, by tackling other challenge areas that inﬂuence the perceived ease-of-use such
as integration with other systems, technical challenges and privacy & security concerns, AR
solution providers could indirectly minimize the amount of training necessary.
Expert G described a similar notion by expressing that it is impossible to control/train
end-users when the end-user is a consumer in retail use cases. Expert G went on to state
"[...] if an organization barely understands what AR is, probably the people they’re trying to

technology, each end-user will try to interact with it in a way that feels intuitive to them. For

with an AR application should proceed. As such, Expert G believes that AR solution providers
and their clients should be considerate about guiding the end-user when creating an AR solution
within such use cases.
6.4.5
Health & Safety Concerns
In terms of health & safety, Experts D, E and F stated that health & safety has not
been that big of a challenge in their experience, however all of the mentioned experts, including
Expert G believe that it can be a concern in speciﬁc use cases.
Expert G went on to explain their experience with clients from the construction indus-
try, where using an AR headset had been problematic due to the device limiting the users ﬁeld
of view. In such cases, they and the client opted to use a mobile device instead, however other
clients prefer their employees to have their hands free, thus choosing an AR headset instead.
Expert D further delves into the topic by stating that "[...] we have no experience. So
far, we should use common sense when we build these things. We should care about the people
and their safety when they use it. But then we should just get moving.", clarifying that AR
in terms of health & safety does not pose a high enough risk that it would be a critical issue:
"[...] there are many, many other things that human beings do that are way more dangerous
than this". The expert also added that AR can actually help improve the health & safety of the
end-user, where, with the right implementation, the AR solution could put the users attention
to speciﬁc details in their surroundings.

out by Experts A, B, D and G.
Expert B had delved deeper into the matter by stating that company #1 spends a
lot of time on educating their clients about AR, and working together with them to ﬁgure
out potential use cases of AR within their organization. They continue saying that while a
converted client is always preferable, even if the client does not follow through, the company

In terms of how company #1 goes about educating their potential clients, they use

have diﬀerences in their deﬁnition, they are largely used to describe the same technology.

Privacy & Security Concerns
In terms of privacy & security, Experts B and E commented that at this point in
time the privacy & security is not a large concern, however Expert E points out that the data

6.5.2
Other Environmental Factors
When discussing factors that are outside of an adopting organizations control, one
of the issues was discussed in the section 6.3.1, which is that AR solutions might depend on
external third parties, with Expert F describing that updates in other services might render a
current AR implementation inaccessible and require it to be maintained.
Another challenge area that had been described, which can be connected to the en-
vironmental context, is that some industries such as health, gambling and tobacco industries
have rigorous regulations, which introduce additional costs and precautions that an AR solu-
tion provider might need to take before being able to implement such a solution. As described
by Expert D: "We build software that is based on augmented reality and location data. And we
can’t introduce that in the space of pharmaceutical production. Because the requirements for
introducing that are really high, and just the process of that is a limitation.".
While the two challenges are factors that are outside of the adopting or providing
organizations control, thus belonging in the environmental context of the framework of AR
adoption challenges, the challenges are limited in the sense that they apply to speciﬁc use cases,
and do not cover the broader scope of AR implementations. The third party dependencies are
also connected to the broader challenge area of "Integration with other systems & maintenance

6.6
Future of AR & AR adoption
While not being a part of the framework of AR adoption challenges, a point of discus-
sion was about the future of AR and it’s adoption. When discussing the matter, the discussions
largely stemmed around AR headsets becoming commonplace for a regular consumer. Experts
from company #1 seemed to be especially optimistic about the headsets, holding a common be-
lief that dedicated AR headsets are necessary to unlock the full potential of AR as a technology,
and adding to the topic that they believe AR headsets will, with time, replace smartphones.
Expert D stated the following relating to the topic: "[...] we’re just practicing on smartphones.
We’re just practicing. This is not the goal, this is practice. When we talk 10 years from now,
we’re both going to be wearing glasses [...] because that’s where our data is. That’s where our
colleagues are, that is how we work.". Expert D followed up on the statement by saying that
the smartphones are limited in the interaction aspect, as the interactions are limited to using a
set screen size, and the interaction can only happen through the use of touch and voice. Expert
B did mention, however, that while the industries will be the ﬁrst adopters of AR headsets, it
will take longer for an everyday consumer to use AR headsets. When discussing the timeline
of such adoption, they estimated: "[...] I mean, a few years for industries, maybe 50 years for
consumers?".
Expert F delved a bit deeper into how this adoption might be undertaken, stating that
big technology companies such as Meta (previously called Facebook), Microsoft and Apple, all
hold immense power in pushing organizations and consumers into adopting new technologies,
in this case AR. They went on to say that if all of the big technology companies state that this
technology will be the future, organizations would not worry about the perceived ease-of-use
and perceived usefulness, as they would simply want to be involved with the technology purely
due to the fear of being left behind in the technology race. Expert F stated: "Because if the big
technology companies are saying that we’re doing this, then we have to be doing something in
this ﬁeld. And we have to learn this ﬁeld, and then we can start looking at, you know, perceived
usefulness and perceived ease-of-use. [...] I think it’s the right thing, but there is a fear of being
lost in this technology race, being left behind. Yeah, and that fear is rational, and people should
act on it.". Similarly, Expert G also believes that big technology companies, speciﬁcally Apple,
might be the ones pushing AR into becoming more commonplace, as they have a track record
of doing so with technologies such as smartphones, tablets, wireless headphones and others.

7
Discussion
The goal of the framework of AR adoption challenges is to provide organizations with
a structural overview of what are the currently identiﬁed challenges when it comes to adopting
an AR solution.
The results of the interviews provide an inclination that the experts agree that the
challenges compiled through the systematic literature review are present within the industry as
well, with some experts also supporting the proposed solutions identiﬁed through the review.
Additional challenge areas have also been identiﬁed through the interviews, speciﬁcally the
education of stakeholders regarding AR, dependency on third-parties during development, and
industry regulations.
The consensus across the experts throughout the interviews seemed to be that under-
standing what AR is (Education of Stakeholders), how can it be applied within the organization,
and what value does it bring (Identiﬁcation of Appropriate Use Cases) are the key aspects that
organizations need to tackle for a successful AR implementation, as experts put high emphasis
on these topics throughout the interviews.
One of the experts also suggested that a framework of AR adoption challenges that
focuses on a speciﬁc industry and a speciﬁc subset of AR technology might be more informative
to help understand the three aspects mentioned before.
At the same time, the intention behind the framework of AR adoption challenges is
to serve as a starting point to organizations. Not all of the challenges will apply to speciﬁc use
cases, as explored throughout the expert discussions with e.g. regular consumers not worrying
about privacy as much compared to an organization who wants to protect sensitive information,
and new challenges might also arise as a part of implementing a speciﬁc AR use case.
7.1
Combining the literature with the interview data
To summarize the key ﬁndings of the interviews relating to the challenge areas, there
are some diﬀerences between the opinions of experts on the speciﬁcs of the challenge areas, for
example in terms of privacy & security, where one of the experts believes that privacy might
be the biggest barrier to mass adoption of AR by consumers (Expert G). On the other hand,
another expert believes that consumers worries about privacy are declining, and that it will
only matter for organizations who need to keep their data secure (Expert D). At the same time,
both experts agreed that it is a challenge regardless, which is a pattern that followed through
all of the previously identiﬁed challenges. Based on their feedback, the challenges summarized
in the framework are all actual challenges present with AR adoption by organizations.
In addition to the pre-existing challenges within the framework of AR adoption chal-
lenges, the biggest diﬀerence between the data gathered from the interviews and the literature
was that the challenge area of education of stakeholders/creators was discovered. As per expert
descriptions, a lot of organizations that want to implement AR, don’t necessarily know what is
it that they want speciﬁcally, or what is possible through the use of augmented reality. It has
42
==PAGE:49==
also been identiﬁed as one of the biggest challenges currently present with AR adoption.
Potential reason as to why this challenge was not identiﬁed within the literature could
be that, since AR is a new, novel technology, the researchers who were motivated to pursue
an AR implementation and describe it in a research paper were already familiar with the
technology beforehand, thus not encountering such an issue.
One of the experts had mentioned during the feedback part of the interview that
consumer behavior is also a challenge area that would belong in the environmental context of
the framework, as it is a factor that is outside of the organizations control (Expert G). While it
is true that the challenge area is outside of the organizations control, consumer behavior was not
added the environmental context, but was rather appended as a part of the "Training of end-
users" challenge area. Within the context of an AR solution being utilized by an organization
internally, the end-users of the solution will be employees that need to be trained. On the
other hand, in a business-to-customer AR solution, the end-user becomes the consumer. As
such, while there is an overlap in this challenge area between the organizational context and
the environmental context, consumer behavior can still be inﬂuenced by providing indirect
training in forms of manuals, tutorials, and on-screen guides.
Thus, the challenge area of
"Training of end-users" was renamed to "Training of end-users / Guiding consumer behavior",
with the implication being that organizations that potentially use the framework of AR adoption
challenges can utilize the challenge area regardless of whether the AR solution is being used
for internal work processes, or as an application delivered to the consumers.
One area that was scarcely touched upon by the experts directly was usability. As
described in section 4.2.8, the term "usability" covers various other aspects that can be tied
to pre-existing challenge areas and TAM concepts. For example, Error Tolerance 3 can be
attributed to the technical challenges that a solution might face, while Engagement 4 could be
attributed as the "Attitude towards using AR" concept from TAM, as the engagement deﬁnition
describes the user ﬁnding a technology as easy to use and appropriate for the industry/topic.
Based on this notion, the challenge area of usability is going to be kept within the framework
of AR adoption challenges, however the speciﬁc challenge area does warrant further research
and validation, as it was not conﬁrmed within these expert interviews whether usability should
be its own challenge area, or if it is completely covered by other challenge areas already. Thus,
it was marked with a star (*) within the updated framework, and a note had been added to
describe this matter.
Another aspect discussed within the interviews were the external factors, that can
be present in certain use cases, such as dependency on external parties when developing an
AR solution and industry regulations for industries like gambling, tobacco and health.
As
was mentioned in the section 6.5.2, while these are valid challenges that an organizations can
experience while adopting an AR solution, ultimately the amount of use cases where these

7.2
Comparing the ﬁndings with other AR adoption literature
Compared to other literature, which investigated AR adoption in speciﬁc industries
and use cases (see section 2.3), the framework of AR adoption challenges does include the
factors identiﬁed within the literature.
One key diﬀerence between other adoption literature and this framework is that some
of the researchers had chosen to include another dimension called "Perceived Enjoyment" within
the User Motivation area of their framework (Cabero-Almenara et al., 2019; Papakostas et al.,
2021). Papakostas et al. (2021) concludes that while perceived enjoyment has high positive
impact on the individuals perceived usefulness and perceived ease-of-use, it does not directly
aﬀect the attitude towards using AR. Within the context of the currently proposed framework,
although the factor had been identiﬁed by one of the studies in the SLR (Lau et al., 2019),
perceived enjoyment ultimately was not investigated or brought up by the experts, however it

While some of the equivalents in the framework are self-explanatory, as is the case
with lack of compatibility with the current system compared to integration with other systems
& maintenance of the system, the challenge area where it could be argued, that it does not have
a direct equivalent in the framework of AR adoption challenges is the factor of top management
support in Chandra and Kumar (2018). In it’s study, Chandra and Kumar (2018) describes top
management support as being an important facilitator in technology adoption, and that lack
of commitment of top management can lead to the failure of the initiative to implement new
technology. Under the pretenses of the framework of AR adoption challenges, the argument is
that in an organization, top management is still consisted of individuals, and that it is their
attitude towards using AR that will enable them to provide the appropriate support to the
initiative, which in turn is based upon their perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use,
which is based upon the the identiﬁed challenge areas. As the study investigated AR adoption
through the use of the TOE framework, it would not include attitude towards using AR, as it
is a concept taken from the TAM model, however it warrants further investigation to ensure
that top management support is equal to the attitude towards using AR.
7.3
Implications of the study
In terms of implications for academia, as described in previous sections, AR as a
technology is still relatively new, and thus the ﬁeld of AR adoption research is also rather
limited. The study contributed to the domain of AR adoption by examining and synthesizing
the challenge areas that are currently present in the literature, and relating them to the industry,
46
==PAGE:53==
through means of expert interviews. The study also contributes to the ﬁeld by compiling the
challenges into a singular framework of AR adoption challenges, which can serve as a starting
point to further expand on the framework by identifying new challenge areas that can either
apply to a broad range of AR implementations, or identifying challenges that pertain to speciﬁc
industries and use cases. The study can also serve as a starting point to further AR adoption
research looking into taking these challenges into account, and exploring methods with which
the challenges can be minimized.
Additionally, the study utilized a combination of the TAM and TOE frameworks,
which contributes to the ﬁeld of AR adoption by exploring this framework arrangement, as no
other research using a combination of TAM/TOE pertaining to AR could be identiﬁed.
In terms of practical implications, the proposed framework of AR adoption challenges
serves as an overview on which challenges an adopting organization or an AR solution provider
can experience when pursuing an AR implementation project. As the AR industry is still on a
growing trajectory, more industries will ﬁnd use in the technology, with new use cases emerging
with time.
As such the framework provides an indication of challenges that organizations
undertaking such implementations might experience, without being constrained to a speciﬁc
industry or use case.
The framework has also been validated through means of expert interviews, with
experts being part of the companies that provide AR solutions to organization, thus combining
insights from the academia and the industry.
7.4
Limitations of the study
The main limitations of the study include bias of the results of the systematic literature
review and limited scope of the data gathered.
In terms of the SLR, the review was done through a single database (Scopus), and only
included results written in the English language, as such, more challenge areas could potentially
be identiﬁed by utilizing more databases and incorporating results in other languages.
In terms of the gathered data, the framework of AR adoption challenges aims to
providing insight to adopting organizations and AR providers as to what challenges can arise
during such implementations. However, the companies from which the experts were interviewed,
could all be classiﬁed as AR solution providers. As such, the framework is missing validation in
terms of what challenges are experienced by the adopting organization. Further segregation in
the area of adopting organizations would also be useful, as organizations that have successfully
implemented and use an AR solutions might have experienced diﬀerent challenges compared to
an organization that stopped the implementation process before completing it.
Additionally, the companies were all either based in Denmark, or in case of Company
#2, had one of the oﬃces in Denmark. As such, while the companies work with international
clients, the geographical scope is limited and AR providers in other areas of the world might
experience diﬀerent challenges. This can especially come true when discussing the matter of
privacy & security, as companies in Europe are all subject to GDPR regulations, which might
47
==PAGE:54==
not be a relevant issue in other areas. Similarly, the geographical bias could be present in the
results of the systematic literature review as well, as majority of the studies originated from
Europe.
Another limitation in terms of the gathered data was that the data was qualitative,
which is more prone to bias due to the limited sample size and researchers interpretations.
While precautions were taken to limit the amount of bias in this data, for example by letting the
experts express the challenges they have come across freely before delving deeper into challenge
areas identiﬁed in the literature, the results still might include unintended conﬁrmation bias.
Lastly, another limitation is that there is lack of data to conﬁrm the relevance of
certain challenge areas as had been identiﬁed with usability. The research also provides limited
insight into the connections between not only challenge areas and perceived usefulness and
perceived ease of use, but also the connections between each other.
7.5
Future works
Future research in terms of AR adoption in organizations building up from this re-
search can be performed in various ways.
One approach to perform immediate future research would be to validate the ﬁnd-
ings further by gathering additional qualitative data while addressing the limitations of this
research, speciﬁcally involving organizations with intent to adopt AR within various industries,
organizations who have adopted AR, and replicating the ﬁndings across diﬀerent geographical
areas.
Further research could also provide new insights and strengthen the validity of this
research through means of gathering quantitative data, using, e.g., an adopted version of the
measurement instrument developed by Davis (1989) as a part of the TAM model, which would
in turn allow the researchers to establish correlations between the diﬀerent challenge areas.
An alternative approach to this study could also be taken. While the study used TAM
and TOE frameworks as the basis of the framework, UTAUT was also brieﬂy introduced in the
section 2.2.2. While the UTAUT framework focuses more on the individuals user acceptance, it
could be worthwhile to investigate how the challenges with AR adoption in organizations can
be described through it’s use, as it could potentially provide further insights compared to the
use of a combined TAM & TOE framework.
As the framework of AR adoption challenges proposed in this paper focuses on the
overall AR implementations without being limited to an industry or a use case, it would also
undoubtedly be beneﬁcial to validate the study through speciﬁc use-cases. A surgical AR imple-
mentation will have diﬀerent challenges compared to using AR in an advertisement campaign,
thus the current framework can be used as a basis to explore and narrow down the relevant
challenges, or on the other hand append the framework of AR adoption challenges with a newly
identiﬁed challenge area, if it is found that a challenge spans various use cases and/or industries.
While the goal of the thesis was to gather the challenges that organizations might have,
the bigger purpose of this research was to help organizations with the AR adoption process. As
48
==PAGE:55==
such future research should look into utilizing these challenges and identiﬁed potential solutions
to create a process or a framework, which can act as a best practice that organizations can
follow throughout an AR implementation process. As one of the experts pointed out when
providing feedback to the framework: "[...] how do you actually create a process from knowing
nothing about AR to having implemented it? And that’s an interesting one, you know, that’s
the moneymaker." (Expert G). These best practices might diﬀer between industries, the speciﬁc
AR technology used and other factors, however standardizing the AR implementation process
would contribute to more organizations looking into and exploring the potential of AR, and
more speciﬁcally understanding what is necessary for them to complete their goal of introducing
AR within their organization.

8
Conclusion
The study has identiﬁed the challenges present with AR adoption within the literature,
and veriﬁed that the ﬁndings are consistent with the industry, with additional challenges being
uncovered during the expert interviews.
From the information gathered through the literature and expert interviews, a frame-
work of AR adoption challenges has been proposed, laying foundation that can be used for
other AR adoption research, to investigate challenges relevant for speciﬁc industries/use cases.
More research is needed for the framework AR adoption challenges, as validating
the existing challenge areas and establishing connections between them can help AR adopting
organizations and AR solution providers alike to evaluate what they should be putting their
focus on throughout an AR implementation process, in order to make this said implementation
successful.
The resulting framework from the performed research provides an overview of the
challenges for organizations.
The question for future research now becomes how can these
challenges be minimized through a streamlined, coherent process, that can be replicated among
various organizations and use cases. Identifying such a process, even if it is for speciﬁc use cases
and industries, should lead to more organizations being able to implement AR, which is how
AR can potentially transition from being a novel technology used for one-oﬀuse cases in its
current state, to becoming a staple technology that consistently provides value for organizations
in various industries.
