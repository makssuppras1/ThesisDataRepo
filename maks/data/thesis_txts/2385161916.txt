Abstract
This thesis presents the details for development of a measurement system, capable of measuring the bi-
directional reﬂectance distribution function, based on two 6 degree of freedom robotic arms from Universal
Robots. The system is capable of performing measurements of isotropic materials on a large range of
view/light conﬁgurations and results of both sparse and dense sampling schemes are shown.
The mechanical uncertainties of the system has been quantiﬁed and the geometric repeatability in image
space has been found to a standard deviation of 0.46 px and a calibration artifact has been developed and
used to correct positional errors in the system.
The BRDF has been measured of 5 different samples, 3 LEGO bricks, a notebook and a 3D printed object.
Four of the objects where visually inspected for evaluation. Whereas the measurements of the notebook has
been compared to another system.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The entertainment industry is heavily relying on material appearance for Computer Generated Imagery (CGI)
that is used as special effects in movies and computer games. Up until very recently almost all material
design was done manually by artists. This is ﬁne for simple materials, but making all materials seem lifelike
and realistic to the user, this is very time consuming for the artists. Due to an increase in computing power
and a greater public demand for realistic virtual representation, this topic is actively researched [28].
The section for image analysis and computer graphics at DTU Compute is actively researching both geometry
acquisition and appearance modeling. As such they are interested in expanding their research facilities to
include a system for measuring the Bi-directional Reﬂectance Distribution Function (BRDF) of physical
objects for use as reference data in their research. Such a system is often referred to as a gonioreﬂectometer,
meaning a device that measures angle and reﬂection. Having experimental equipment which is able to
measure the BRDF of materials would aid in the development of new material models and possibly also
allow research in new acquisition techniques.
In this thesis we will investigate the possibilities of performing BRDF measurements utilizing two 6-Degree
of Freedom (DoF) robotic arms from Universal Robots. Effectively using a 12DoF system to measure a 4DoF
function. The investigation includes building the system and quantifying the measurement uncertainties and
errors of the system, with the intention to correct and report them. Ultimately striving to create a system that
is suitable for generating reference data and allowing members of the section to perform BRDF research
related experiments with the equipment.
Limitation and Requirements
Measuring a full 4D BRDF at 1◦resolution would result in the need of (90 × 180)2 ≈262 million samples.
If the acquisition time pr. sample is just one second the total time to measure the BRDF would be more than
8 years. Due to this we will introduce some limitations to the requirements of the system. We will assume
all samples to be homogeneous and isotropic reducing the number of samples pr. BRDF by a factor of 180
due to symmetries. Further we will limit the the resolution of the sample acquisition to 7.5◦thus reducing
the number of samples for a "full" BRDF measurement to 3 456, which under the same assumptions of 1s pr.
sample would take around an hour.
As Universal Robots do not provide the information required to address the robots inverse and forward
kinematics, the kinematics of the robots are not addressed in this thesis and the internal kinematics of the
robots will be treated as black boxes.

1.1
Related Work
Due to the large amount of solutions made to measure BRDFs this section will mainly focus on the
approaches which are similar to ours - gonioreﬂectometers.
All recent BRDF related work is based on Nicodemus nomenclature of BRDF [17] and radiometry [16].
Many different BRDF measurement devices have been developed through the years. They can be grouped
into three main groups: camera/light arrays, mirror/kaleidoscope and gonioreﬂectometer setups. In 1990
Murray-Coleman and Smith [15] made a gonioreﬂectometer setup where the sample, detector and light
source are motorized and allows for 4DoF acquisition. In 1992 Ward [27] published a setup based on camera
and mirrors effectively requiring 2DoF less for mechanical movement.
In 1997 Foo [3] built a gonioreﬂectometer to acquire isotropic BRDFs, based on rotating light source,
stationary detector and rotation of sample. In 1998 White et al [29] built a gonioreﬂectometer with a
motorized detector and light source, where the sample is placed on a turntable allowing for a capture of
the full 4D BRDF. In 1999 Marschner et al. [21] made a setup which required samples to be cylindrical,
exploiting that the geometry was known and thus acquiring multiple measurements at a time. A similar
approach made by Matusik et al. in 2003 [14], using spherical samples, where 100 different materials has
been measured and the dataset is commonly referred to as the MERL BRDF dataset.
More recent setups focus on measuring the Spatially Varying Bi-directional Reﬂectance Distribution Function,
SVBRDF. One of the models to do this is called Bi-directional Texture Function, BTF, which is essentially
just an BRDF with an image of the surface for each BRDF measurement. In 1999 Dana et al. [5] built a
gonioreﬂectometer device to be used to measure the BTF. This device was used in the production of the
CUReT BTF and BRDF database. In 2001 Dana et al [4] built a mirror based setup to allow multiple samples
to be acquired at the same time. In 2010 Holroyd et al. [11] developed a system, based on the Stanford
Spherical Gantry, where the sample is placed on a rotation stage and two robotic arms on which both have
mounted a coaxial camera and light source by using beam splitters. This system is also capable of geometry
acquisition. In 2013 Filip et al. [9] produced a system capable similar to Holroyd et al. but with just a single
camera and light source on separate arms. This system was used to make the UTIA dataset.
While a few of the gonioreﬂectometers utilize robot arms with more than 2DoF none of mentioned systems
attempt to utilize 6DoF general purpose robotic arms to move the camera and light source. A single robotic
arm was used to control the camera in the work by Jannik B. Nielsen [18].

2.1
Radiometry
Radiometry is the concept describing and measure electromagnetic radiation and in this thesis we focus on
the spectrum of visible light, which is somewhere around 380 to 780 nm [22]. As explained by Nicodemus
in [16], radiometry takes a basis in the energy carried by a photon at a certain wavelength, radiant energy,
Q. Derivatives of this with respect to time, area and direction are expressed as radiant ﬂux, irradiance,
radiant exitance and radiance. Even though the radiometric quantities are dependent on wavelength, we
do not consider them on a per wavelength basis, but instead on a Red, Green and Blue (RGB) as separate
color bands. The theory in this chapter is based on the radiometry section in the book "Physically Based
Rendering" by Pharr et al. [20].
The radiant energy is deﬁned by the Planck-Einstein relation, which describes the energy carried by a photon
at a speciﬁc wavelength λ, where c is the speed of light in vacuum and h is the Planck constant

2.2
Camera Theory
According to FLIRs white paper [12], the main concept behind the pixels in a Charge-Coupled Device (CCD)
camera is based on converting photons into electrons which then are collected in a well, see Figure 2.4. The
buildup of electrons will create a negative charge proportional to number of electrons in the well. This is
measured with an Analog to Digital Converter, ADC, and is the pixel value in an image.
Figure 2.4: Illustration how a pixel works. It shows how photons are converted into electron that are stored
in a well of which the charge is measured by an ADC.
The photon to electron conversion rate is called the quantum efﬁciency, QE. It is expressed as a percentage
for each wavelength in the visible spectrum. For color cameras the QE is usually expressed as a single
number for the main wavelength in each RGB channel, red being 640 nm, green 525 nm and blue 470 nm.
==PAGE:24==
8
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
When the photons are converted to electrons they are collected in a well, the capacity of the well is called the
saturation capacity and is measured as the number of electrons e−. The saturation capacity can also be seen
as the potential dynamic range of the camera but other factors as quantum efﬁciency, temporal dark noise
and dark current also affect the actual dynamic range of the camera [8]. The saturation capacity is mainly
determined by the pixelsize, larger pixel means larger wells and thus higher dynamic range, given that the
other parameters stay constant that is.
Temporal dark noise is the existence of electrons in the well even when it is not exposed to light and is not
inﬂuenced by exposure time. Temporal dark noise is Poisson distributed [8]. Another electron noise source
is the dark current, which unlike temporal dark noise, is inﬂuenced by both temperature and exposure time.
The dark current is noise generated by the electronics in the camera. An issue often seen in its extreme cases
when doing long exposures of the night sky with older DSLR, Digital Single-Lens Reﬂex Cameras.

2.2.2
Bayer Filter Artifacts
In most digital cameras a ﬁlter is applied to each pixel, which only lets in a speciﬁc spectrum, usually split
into red, green and blue. This is called a Bayer ﬁlter. It enables cameras to acquire color images on a single
sensor by applying a mosaic pattern of two greens, one red and one blue ﬁlter per 2x2 pixel neighborhood.
Due to this Bayer ﬁlter it might be advantageous to perform binning and thus avoiding the interpolated
values and the reconstruction artifacts. The downside of this will be halving the resolution.

Gonioreﬂectometer Mk I
The mechanical parts of the gonioreﬂectometer is consisting of two robots mounted on an optical breadboard,
see Figure 3.6. The general use case of the system will be to place a sample on the breadboard, between
the two robots and have the robots move the light and camera to all positions on the hemisphere above the
sample. Due to the reach of the robots, it is only possible to measure isotropic BRDFs, but this could be
extended by adding a turntable and place the samples on that. The robots are mounted on the table using
adapters seen in Appendix A.
Each robot has its own local coordinate frame, called the base frame and I use the subscripts r1 or r2 to
distinguish between the robots. The base frame is located at the center of the mounting plate. To simplify
the interaction, when working with both robots, a common coordinate frame is introduced, lets call that the
world frame subscript world. This frame can be arbitrarily placed, depending on how the user ﬁnd it best
suited for the task at hand, but three solutions seem more relevant to the current task.
To be able to deﬁne these transformations we must account for positional errors in the system, e.g. machining
imprecision when the parts where produced. We know the maximum error of most parts but we do not know
the precise error. Universal Robots state that the maximum error in precision of tool position is 0.1 mm.
Where as most mechanical parts, optical breadboard, adapter plates and robot mounting plate all are produced
with 0.1-0.5 mm precision. These errors accumulate and need to be corrected in the transformation.
One solution would be to deﬁne the world frame in one of the base frames. This makes it so that there is
only one transformation to ﬁnd, between r1 and r2. This also means that just one of the robots will have an
error in relation to positions given in the world frame. A downside to this is that the relation between the
position of a sample in the real world and this world frame would be rather unintuitive.

4.1.2
Camera Driver
There exists many different ways to control a camera from a computer and in this section we will go through
the considerations related to control the Grasshopper3 camera from PtGrey Inc.
The most important requirements for the driver is to control exposure and camera trigger. Then we want to
acquire HDR images and lastly optimize for speed. The acquisition speed is particular important for dense
acquisitions as we need a HDR image for when the light is on and another of the background lighting in
order to subtract it. The camera aperture has been set so that full dynamic range can be expressed with
10 images from 2 ms to 1024 ms. The minimum possible acquisition time is then the sum of exposures,
2046 ms pr. HDR image. Setting the exposure time and transferring the images to the PC takes time as well,
roughly 80ms pr. image. Giving a total of approximately 2850 ms pr. HDR image.
PtGrey provides a Source Development Kit (SDK), FlyCapture2 SDK and FLIR provides a SDK called Spin-
naker SDK. Both SDKs are based on C/C++ and thus an extra layer must be written to allow communication
from Python.
Spinnaker SDK uses the GenICam protocol to communicate with the cameras, which allows the change of
camera to any other brand who produces GenICam compliant cameras, which is most industrial cameras.
The Spinnaker API is event based and abstracted away from register reads and writes.
FlyCapture2 is made for direct hardware access and though their SDK provides utility functions, writing
register calls are inevitable. This leaves much of the bookkeeping and handling of the asynchronous hardware
up to the end user. In general the FlyCapture2 SDK is much more low level than the Spinnaker SDK. Thus
Spinnaker SDK is used in this system.
Another thing to consider is the protocol to use for communicating between the Python control script to the
C++ camera driver. The other robotic system in the laboratory uses string communication over a socket,
this is rather inconvenient and requires a lot of extra work in parsing settings and so on. Due to this another
approach of adding Python bindings directly into the program using pybind11 1. This approach allows us to
write the driver in C++ and call the functions directly from a Python script.
4.2
Measurement Procedure
Measuring a BRDF the canonical way is a rather simply concept. For every light direction on the hemisphere
an image is taken from every position on the hemisphere. While the concept is simple the acquisition time
for a system measuring at 1 deg resolution of an homogeneous isotropic material, would result in the need
of 90 · 90 · 180 ≈1.5M samples. Which is still not feasible. By scanning at a 7.5 deg interval instead, we
only have 2888 samples, thus the acquisition time will be decreased drastically. Still when handling 2888
positions, the acquisition time pr. position is of great importance.

4.3
Sampling Schemes
Deﬁning the optimal directions of which to scan a BRDF is an active research topic. To aid in that research
the system needs to support both dense and sparse sampling schemes. Dense sampling are scans covering
half a hemisphere with certain resolution. Where as sparse sampling schemes are based on selected subset
light and camera directions.
Dense Sampling
Performing dense measurements with the setup is challenging for two reasons. The ﬁrst being that the
mounts for both the light source and camera are quite big, preventing operation in near proximity of each
other. Because of this physical constraint, dense sampling is for this system deﬁned as the light directions
θi ∈[90◦, 30◦] and the camera positions θo ∈[0◦, 165◦] and φo ∈[82.5◦, 30◦]. This is quite the limitation
in regards of covering the full BRDF, they can be expanded with clever path planning accounting for the
robot pose in each positions, but is another topic not addressed in this thesis.
The second challenge is that due to kinematic singularities, sometimes causing the robot to reconﬁgure
almost a full 360-rotation in one of the joints, each of the positions has to be manually veriﬁed and possibly
tweaked. When the list contains (165/7.5)(52.5/7.5) = 154 positions, tweaking all of them takes quite
some time. Making dense sampling a very impractical task.
Rusinkiewicz Coordinates / Half-Vector Notation
When using a constant stepsize in spherical coordinates one will have much higher density of samples in
the polar areas. Usually the polar area does not contain much information in regards to BRDF and thus
another sampling method is suggested by Rusinkiewicz [23]. This sampling method, usually refereed to as
Rusinkiewicz coordinates, suggest that instead that the sampling should be based around the half-vector.

This makes sense as this representation gives the highest density around the specular highlight rather than in
a zone of no interest.
In this setup we support the use of Rusinkiewicz coordinates as well as the conventional spherical coordinates.
The user can deﬁne the directions in either Cartesian, spherical or Rusinkiewicz coordinates.
4.4
HDR Imaging
To be able to measure the high dynamic range that the reﬂected light has we will use the concept of taking
multiple images with different exposures and combine them into a single High Dynamic Range image.
This is described by Debevec et al [6]. We will refer to a list of exposures as stops and for this application
stops = {2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1024}. The stops are deﬁned so that that exposure time doubles
for each stop. When we make the HDR the pixel value is multiplies by 2stop number.
When creating HDR images of stray light and general background light, this becomes an issue in particular.
We assume that there is no signal at all in very short exposure, but the dark noise will still produce a small
signal in the sensor. It is likely to introduce larger errors when performing difference images, where the
subtracted image is ampliﬁed noise.
4.5
Stray Lighting
The gonioreﬂectometer is not placed in a dark room but in a open work environment with ﬂuorescent tubes
for lighting, windows where the shades cannot block the sun completely and people coming and going.
These conditions with an almost random lighting environment makes radiometric precision measurements
rather tricky.
In order to account for non-zero environment lighting one can do difference images. To obtain a difference
image, one must acquire a HDR image with the light source on, HDRon, and another with the light source
off, HDRoff. Then you will get an image with the contributions of the light source and the environment and
another image with just the environment. Addressing the reﬂected irradiance as being the signal we can then
express the signal as
HDRsignal = HDRon −HDRoff
In an ideal world we will obtain an image containing just the signal. But due to noise in the sensor we can
encounter numerical issues and besides noise the most critical error is a negative signal, which cannot exist
as you cannot have a negative photon count. To account for the we clamp negative values to zero, which
works in practice, but the the problem should be addressed and lies in the way sensor noise is handled in the
generation of the HDR image.
4.6
Absolute vs. Relative Measurement
There exists two methods to convert from intensity measurements to BRDF, an absolute and a relative
method. The absolute method requires knowledge of the irradiance emitted by the light source and the area
of the sample of which we measure the reﬂected radiance. This can be measured by directly looking into the
light source with the camera. In this system, this approach is difﬁcult as the intensity of the light source
requires the aperture to be almost completely closed to measure any signal not completely overexposed. But

4.7
Position Correction in Image Space
The assumption that the center of the camera always points towards the center of the sample works in the
ideal case. But as the system in reality have many minor errors which all contribute to a relatively large
error in position and rotation of the robot, ultimately resulting in the camera center not hitting the sample at
all. See Figure 4.3. As the highlight for some materials change rather rapidly across just a few pixels, this
introduces a large error in the BRDF.
Due to the high repeatability of the robotic arms, with a subpixel standard deviation, we can assume that
calibrating the position of the center of the sample in pixel space will be reliable enough to be a feasible
solution to the alignment error.
In order to correct for this displacement, some calibration artifact must be used. It has to be able to detected by
a preferably simple algorithm and visible from grazing angles. The latter making checkerboard impractical,
as it is difﬁcult to ﬁnd corners in grazing angles.
Spheres on the other hand are easy to detect at grazing angles using either Circular Hough Transform or with
the right preprocessing and then ﬁnding the center of mass. This led to the creation of a correction artifact
seen in Figure 4.4a, the green color is for segmentation purposes. Another feature of the correction artifact is
that it will be mounted on the table and be within a millimeter of the theoretical position. This artifact can of
course be created at much higher precision, but to determine if it improves measurements this is sufﬁcient.

Analysis of Geometric Errors

5.2.1
Geometric Repeatability
There exists multiple methods to determine the accuracy and precision of a camera setup. The ones discussed
in this section is based on camera calibration with a checker board pattern. A scanning sequence will be
repeated a signiﬁcant number of times and from each scan location the position of a checkerboard is found
and the camera parameters are calibrated. Generally the standard deviation might be better suited for this as
it is deﬁned as the mean of the point wise distance to the mean.
Method 1: Across all experiments determine the variance of the estimation of the camera parameters and
based on the variance something can be said about the repeatability. Some obvious issues with this will be
that not all positions provide good coverage of the checker board and thus their estimation of parameters
will never be good, resulting in high global variance, even if the position of the robot does not change.
Method 2: For each camera position, determine the variance of the camera parameters across the positions.
Then the mean of the variance can determine the accuracy of the system, or even the maximum variance.
Angle with bad coverage of the checker board might still have high variance as the solution is found by an
optimizer and not analytical.
Method 3: Like the second method but only the standard deviation in pixel space is considered effectively
providing a repeatability measure described in pixels. This method is directly comparable with metrics given
by Schwartz et al. [24], thus giving the advantage of comparing performance to state of the art research
equipment.
Using the Gonioreﬂectometer Mk II to acquire a dense 7.5◦resolution scan of a 9x7 checkerboard 50 times,
we were able to acquire enough samples to compute the standard deviation of each checkerboard corner. To
be conservative, the largest computed standard deviation was used and we obtained that σx = 0.26 px and
σy = 0.43 px. Due to the repeatability relying solely on the actuators i.e. the UR5s, we can assume that the
repeatability of the two systems are the same.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
Addressing the results in Figure 6.2, it can clearly be seen that the measured BRDF of the blue LEGO brick
is much darker than the original brick. When normalizing against the Spectralon, the lack of blue light causes
weak signals to be ampliﬁed heavily, making materials like the notebook obtain a blue/purple tint. However,
a complete lack of signal in all three color bands, like in the blue brick, will result in a very dark color. This
is an artifact of the LED. The original thoughts were that the "ﬂatness" of the spectrum was more important
than the coverage, as long as all 3 color channels can be saturated in one of the HDR exposures. Even though
this is how it works in theory, in practice we are unable to saturate the blue channel for most materials, as the
dynamic range simply is not big enough. Thus I will suggest buying another LED, speciﬁcally the Neutral
White MNWHL4 LED from Thorlabs. The spectrum looks similar but the blue channel is well represented.
While it is nice to do visual comparisons of results, we need to measure samples with known BRDF such
that we can compare them numerically and report our ﬁndings. This is the most important thing for future
work with this system. Without that we cannot truly deﬁne the radiometric uncertainty of the measurements.
Another aspect which impacts the result signiﬁcantly is the region of interest in the image of which the
median is used as the actual measurement instead of a single pixel. We can see in the obtained images,
that the assumption of a constant surface normal across is only true for the Spectralon. The other tested
materials all showed minor surface defects causing high frequency noise in the data, e.g. a few completely
saturated pixels in an area of very low intensity. Using this region of interest also proves to be an issue
when approaching grazing angles as the surface is close to parallel with the camera. This results in the
surface being represented by just a few pixels, causing a large region of interest to include completely
arbitrary values. A possible solution to this could be weighting the height of the region with cos θ, and hence
decreasing the region for grazing angles.
The choice to use Python-URX instead of a full stack framework like ROS was to focus on the simplest
implementation, minimum dependencies makes the project more manageable. However it had one big
drawback, since we had no control of the kinematics, this was all left to the Universal Robots control box -
CB3. In addition, the use of ROS results in a loss of accuracy, as the precisely measured parameters of the
robots are not available to ROS [1]. Therefore, utilizing the URScript through python-URX was the best
choice at the time, since accuracy was more important than the ability to manage the movement of the robot.
All it had to do was move from one point to another, requiring no advanced temporal control of trajectory
planning.
Even though path planning was not considered to be a part of this project a signiﬁcant amount of time has
been spend struggling with path-planning to avoid collisions and singularities. Changing to ROS midway
was not a feasible solution. I therefore suggest an investigation of the possibility that path planning can be
37

optimized by using one of the numerous kinematics solutions in the ROS framework. By simulating the full
system in ROS, it might be possible to perform intelligent path planning and simulated collision detection.
This can be utilized to increase the regions that the robots can move and thus provide a wider coverage of
the BRDF. I would argue that the better planning outweighs the loss of accuracy, and it might even speed up
acquisition time.
For Gonioreﬂectometer Mk I we could calculate the maximum absolute angular error since all parts had
traceability. This was not possible to do for the current production of Mk II as we do not have traceability on
the production of the mechanical structure. I see two solutions to that problem. The ﬁrst and obvious one is
to produce a new version of high precision and with traceability. The other solution is to calibrate the system.
This can be done in many ways, but I would investigate the possibilities for hand-eye calibration.
While the focus of this thesis solely has been on point measurements, it might be with worth investigating
using all of the pixels in the camera. The measurements should be possible using near-ﬁeld imaging instead
of far-ﬁeld, as of such we will perform multiple measurements within a single image. An issue with that is
the size of the mounts which probably would be the limiting factor when trying that approach.
Furthermore, the system could be expanded to include measurements for anisotropic materials by adding a
rotation stage. This should be possible with minor modiﬁcations to the pipeline, although the main downside
is increased acquisition time.
Even though the system can be improved, the goal of developing a robot based BRDF measurement system
has been achieved. The fact that we have been able to reconstruct a BRDF show this.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this thesis we have described the development of a robot based BRDF measurement system. The system is
capable of measuring an isotropic homogeneous BRDF and the geometric repeatability has been quantiﬁed.
The system is capable of a wide range of light/view-conﬁgurations: in-plane, sparse and dense sampling
schemes have been used to generate the application example section. We found that due to robot placement
in Gonioreﬂectometer Mk I, singularities where often struck and thus path-planning was a tedious and time
consuming process, preventing dense measurements. This led to the development of Gonioreﬂectometer
Mk II, where the base axis of the robots were aligned with the BRDF coordinate system, yielding less
singularities and self collisions thus allowing easier path-planning and dense measurements.
Using general purpose robotics arms is indeed a possible framework for BRDF acquisition and the results
are comparable to other measurement devices.
