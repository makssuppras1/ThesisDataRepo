Abstract
Over the last decades, combination antiretroviral therapy (cART) has increased life expectancy for people
living with HIV (PLWH) substantially. However, an increase in incidence of comorbidities related to metabolic
syndrome (MetS) such as type 2 diabetes (T2D) and cardiovascular disease (CVD) has become a growing
concern as it leads to premature mortality among PLWH. Previous studies have shown that the use of cART
induces abnormalities in the metabolome of PLWH which may lead to the comorbidities; especially in people
older than 50 years. However, the underlying biological mechanisms of MetS in PLWH still remains incompletely
understood.
The aim of this study was to characterise metabolic patterns in PLWH with MetS following antiretroviral
treatment for more than 10 years. This was done by focusing on the complexity and structural composition of
the lipidome (lipidomics) combined with key identiﬁed polar metabolites (metabolomics) from PLWH included
in the Copenhagen comorbidity in HIV infection (COCOMO) cohort study.
First, we identiﬁed key lipids increased in PLWH with MetS compared to PLWH without MetS via a consensus
of four statistical methods; partial least squares discriminant analysis (PLS-DA), random forest (RF), Mann-
Whitney U and limma. Second, we used an integrated network and a community analysis to deﬁne communities
of lipids and key metabolites and identiﬁed commonalities within these communities. Third, we tested if the
communities and speciﬁc biomolecules were associated with any clinical variables via regression analyses.
We found 13 lipids to be increased in PLWH with MetS compared to PLWH without MetS, and 6 of these
lipids were enriched within the top community that also contained previously identiﬁed key metabolites. We
found the top community to be associated with the clinical variables; visceral adipose tissue (VAT), exposure
to early-generation ART and MetS. Within the top community we found two diﬀerent structural lipid patterns
to be associated with VAT and the use of ART with non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTIs).
In conclusion, structural composition patterns of several glycerolipid species and their correlation with polar
metabolites and clinical variables highlight the importance of the structural lipid composition in the context of
excess risk of MetS-related comorbidities among PLWH, such as T2D and CVD.

Introduction
Acquired immunodeﬁciency syndrome (AIDS) was ﬁrst described in the early 80s, and in 1983 human immun-
odeﬁciency virus (HIV) was isolated. According to statistics from 2019 conducted by UNAIDS [1], 38 million
people are infected with HIV worldwide and over half a million deaths and nearly 1.7 million new infections are
registered each year. These large numbers have however, decreased signiﬁcantly over the last decade [1, 2, 3].
The decrease correlates with a four-time increase in people with access to antiretroviral therapy (ART) going
from 6.4 million to 25.4 million people from 2009 to 2019 [1].
Combining several antiretroviral drugs with diﬀerent biological targets is termed combination antiretroviral
therapy (cART). cART has become a success since 1995, as it has increased life expectancy for people living
with HIV (PLWH) substantially. However, an increase in incidences of HIV-comorbidities such as obesity, type
2 diabetes (T2D) and cardiovascular disease (CVD) related to metabolic syndrome (MetS) (i.e. abdominal
obesity, hypertension, hypertriglyceridemia (elevated levels of triglycerides) and altered glucose levels), has
become a growing concern. Several factors are likely to play a role in the pathogenesis of MetS in PLWH.
In chronic HIV infection, complicated interactions between eﬀects of persistent low-grade immune activation,
metabolic toxicity from ART, and HIV unrelated risk factors may increase the risk of MetS in PLWH. However,
the pathophysiology of MetS in PLWH are still incompletely understood [4, 5, 6].
Chronic HIV is characterised by a pro-inﬂammatory state with chronic systemic inﬂammation which induces
progressive pathophysiological changes associated with aging [7, 8].
In addition to the pathophysiological
changes associated with inﬂammation, ART may act as a metabolic toxin in PLWH [7]. Metabolic toxicity from
long-term use of ART is mainly associated with disruption of the lipid metabolism [5, 9], and this is mainly
associated with use of protease inhibitors (PIs) and early-generation thymidine analogues (nucleotide reverse
transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs)), see appendix table A.1 for list of ARTs [10, 11].
Several studies have investigated the association of HIV infection and the association of ART with metabolic
abnormalities related to MetS (e.g. abdominal obesity, T2D, CVD, hypertension, hypertriglyceridemia and
dyslipidaemia) [6, 12, 13, 14]. Most studies, however, focus on conventional blood lipids, such as triglyceride level
and total cholesterol. These biomarkers do not suﬃciently reﬂect the complex alterations of the lipid metabolism
in PLWH with MetS. Thus, studies exploring the complexity of the alterations of the lipid metabolism are needed
to explore the underlying biomolecular mechanisms of this phenotype.
Untargeted/shot-gun lipidomics is an approach which assesses hundreds of lipid species across multiple biological
pathways. The method provides data of all lipids present in biological samples (i.e. the lipidome) and may help
in the discovery of new patterns and disease markers associated with MetS in PLWH [5]. Plasma lipidomics
studies of non-HIV populations have identiﬁed multiple lipid species within the lipidome to be associated with
features of MetS (i.e. obesity, hypertension and insulin resistance) [15]. In addition, obesity has been shown

1.1
Aim of the study
The overall aim of the study is to characterise the eﬀect of metabolic patterns in PLWH with MetS, which
have followed ART treatment for more than 10 years. Hereby, we aim to investigate the underlying biological
mechanisms of MetS in PLWH, focusing on the lipidome. An exploratory analysis of the lipidome comparing;
HIV infected without metabolic syndrome (HIV-NoMetS) (n = 100) with HIV infected with metabolic syndrome
(HIV-MetS) (n = 100), was conducted to identify a set of key lipids. Also, a network analysis contributed
to reveal deeper underlying patterns within the metabolome (polar metabolome and lipidome) of HIV-MetS.
Additionally, we investigated the inﬂuence of clinical variables on the metabolome. By analysing the complete
lipidome of PLWH with MetS we aimed to obtain an understanding of the structural composition patterns,
which determine the biological phenotypes. The following three speciﬁc aims were made to reach the overall
aim.
1. Find diﬀerential abundance lipids between HIV-NoMetS and HIV-MetS
2. Explore the commonalities between lipids and key metabolites in HIV-MetS through a network analysis
3. Explore how lipids and key metabolites in the most central community have an eﬀect on each clinical
variable through regression analysis

2.1.1
HIV pathogenesis
HIV is a retrovirus that infects and replicates within activated immune cells. These cells include macrophages,
dendritic cells and CD4+ T cells. CD4+ T cells are the main target cells for infection and supports the majority
of the viral replication; see illustration of the HIV replication cycle in ﬁgure 2.1 [2, 3].
Infection begins when a CD4 receptor on a target cell recognizes and binds the glycoprotein complex gp120-gp41
from the HIV-viral envelope (Step 1, ﬁgure 2.1). The CD4/gp120 binding induces conformational change that
allows binding of the co-receptor. This binding causes the glycoprotein, gp41 to unfold and insert a part of its
viral structure (i.e. the fusion peptide) into the plasma membrane of the host cell, hereby inducing a fusion of
the host cell and the viral envelope. The viral nucleocapsid, which comprises the viral genome and associated
viral proteins, can now enter the cytoplasm of the host cell (Step 2, ﬁgure 2.1) [2, 3].
Once the viral core is in the host cell and uncoated, the HIV replication cycle is initiated. The ﬁrst step is for its
single-stranded RNA to be reversed transcribed into double-stranded cDNA by the viral reverse transcriptase
enzyme (Step 3, ﬁgure 2.1). The viral DNA migrates to and enters the host’s nucleus and uses the integrase
enzyme to integrate itself into the host genome, thus becoming a provirus (Step 4, ﬁgure 2.1).
The HIV
genome is small and consists of three major genes gag, pol and env, which encodes structural proteins, viral
replication enzymes and the viral envelope glycoproteins, respectively [3]. The virus utilizes the cell apparatus
for transcription and translation of viral RNA and proteins (Step 5, ﬁgure 2.1). Virions are assembled and
released by budding out of the host cell’s cytoplasmic membrane (Step 6, ﬁgure 2.1). The viral protease cleaves
the polyprotein chains releasing a mature virion ready to infect a new host cell (Step 7, ﬁgure 2.1) [2, 21].
Infection of activated cells can lead to either productive viral replication, or viral latency induced by infected
cells that are dormant soon after infection. In long-living CD4+ T cells a reservoir of latent provirus can be
created, which can be activated several years after initial infection, as the T cells are being reactivated [3].

2.2
Metabolic syndrome (MetS) in HIV-infected
Most PLWH in high-income countries are well controlled by ART, and a large and increasing proportion of
these people are above 50 years [24]. The introduction of ART as a treatment for PLWH has increased the
concern of metabolic disruptions aﬀecting adipose tissue and lipid- and glucose metabolism [24, 25].
MetS is a cardio-metabolic cluster of heart attack risk factors such as abdominal obesity, raised triglycerides (i.e.
hypertriglyceridemia), reduced high-density lipoprotein-cholesterol, raised blood pressure (i.e. hypertension),
insulin resistance and raised fasting plasma glucose [26] (See appendix table B.1 for the speciﬁc deﬁnition of
MetS). MetS risk factors signiﬁcantly increase the risk of serious life-threatening chronic diseases such as T2D
and CVD [27, 28]. Having several MetS risk factors at once, further enhance this risk. Both insulin resistance
and abdominal obesity are regarded as signiﬁcant factors of the underlying cause of MetS [26].
Insulin resistance occurs when cells become less sensitive to insulin. Insulin is a hormone produced by beta cells
in the pancreas, which facilitates glucose absorption. Extensive amounts of glucose in the blood triggers beta
cells to produce more insulin in order to process and absorb the abnormal amounts of glucose. Eventually, beta
cells may wear out as they are weakened by the insulin over-production, which can lead to T2D. The insulin
resistance also induces other metabolic damaging eﬀects, such as hypertriglyceridemia [25, 26]. ART treatments
for PLWH have been associated with insulin resistance and T2D [24]. Abdominal obesity is associated with
and contributes to the development of insulin resistance and systemic inﬂammation. It is an accumulation of

excessive adipose tissue (i.e. fat tissue) in the abdominal area, called visceral adipose tissue (VAT), and provides
protective padding for organs in the abdomen [9, 26, 29].
Factors such as genetics, physical inactivity, environmental inﬂuences, ageing, a pro-inﬂammatory state, and
hormonal changes also have causal eﬀect of MetS. However, the role of these factors varies depending on ethnicity

The physical inactivity associated with a sedentary lifestyle, a lifestyle with minimum movement,
increases the risk of developing hypertension, a risk factor of CVD. Also, nutrition inﬂuences the metabolism.
A high intake of food containing high amounts of added sugar and salt, also associated with a sedentary lifestyle,
has been shown to inﬂuence the metabolism in a damaging way [30]. Furthermore, metabolic disruptions leading
to MetS, have been shown to be inﬂuenced by age, a pro-inﬂammatory state and hormonal changes. All of
which are prevalent in increased amounts in the population of PLWH [6, 7, 25, 26].
2.3
Investigating MetS in HIV-infected via omics techniques
The metabolism in general and the lipid metabolism, in particular, play a signiﬁcant role in HIV-comorbidities
related to MetS. More in-depth insight into these underlying biological mechanisms is needed to explore the
complex biological interactions and alterations in the entire metabolome of PLWH with MetS.
2.3.1
What are omics techniques and why use them?
Omics techniques are holistic approaches used to investigate the complex nature of biological systems. Figure
2.2 gives an overview of diﬀerent omics techniques applied within the diﬀerent biological levels. A collective
quantiﬁcation and characterisation of the interplay of biomolecules within an organism, is obtained by omics
analysis, which can be translated into the structure, function, dynamics, and phenotypes of the concerned
organism. The quantiﬁcation and characterisation of biomolecules with omics techniques depends on biological
samples, which can be either an organism, an organ, a tissue, a tissue extract, a cell, an organelle or bioﬂuids.
Each individual biological sample has a unique metabolic ﬁngerprint, which can provide speciﬁc information of
metabolic patterns depending on the used sample type [31]. This study focuses on blood plasma samples (i.e.
bioﬂuid samples). The plasma includes only the ﬂuid part of the blood (no cells), and its main role is to transport
metabolites around in the human body. All polar metabolites (i.e. amino acids, carbohydrates, nucleosides and
other small compounds) and non-polar metabolites (i.e. lipids) combined make up the entire metabolome of
a plasma sample, and they are deﬁned as the polar metabolome and the lipidome, respectively. By analysing
the metabolome of plasma samples, it is thus possible to gain insight into the underlying complexity of the
biological mechanisms in PLWH with MetS.

Metabolomics and lipidomics are the study of the entire metabolome and the lipidome, respectively (Figure
2.2). Metabolomics characterise and quantiﬁes the entire metabolome, however, does not fully capture the
complexity of the non-polar metabolites. Lipidomics focuses on the non-polar compounds of the metabolome
(i.e. the structural composition of diﬀerent lipid species) and has become an independent omics due to the
complexity, structural properties, wide abundance and important roles that lipids manage within organisms. A
combination of metabolomics and lipidomics techniques is needed in order to get a detailed characterisation of
the metabolic status within PLWH with MetS, and the two techniques supplement each other [32, 33, 34].
Omics techniques are classiﬁed into untargeted and targeted approaches. Untargeted analysis is an unbiased
approach that can discover new biomarkers, whereas the targeted approach analyses properties of speciﬁc
compounds more in depth. The approaches diﬀer in their analysis steps and data processing, and both have
their advantages and disadvantages. As an incomplete understanding of the underlying biological mechanisms
in PLWH with MetS still remains, this study is based on untargeted approaches for both the metabolomics and
lipidomics analyses [32]. In a prior study from our group, we analysed metabolomics data and identiﬁed key
metabolites [17]. Thus, this study focuses on analysing the lipidomics data and integrating the two omics data
to investigate metabolic patterns in PLWH with MetS from the same cohort.
2.3.2
Lipidomics: Studying the lipidome
Lipids are essential metabolites and maintain many cellular functions, such as being components of the mem-
brane, storage for energy, signalling molecules, and other. The lipidome can provide a snapshot of the cellular
metabolic status, thus relevant to investigate in relation to metabolic distress caused by MetS in PLWH [31, 32,
35]. Many diﬀerent classes of lipids exist, and they are grouped according to their properties and major cellular
functions (Figure 2.3). All lipids included in ﬁgure 2.3 are built of fatty acid chains and functional groups.

2.3.3
Statistical tools: Analysis of lipidomics data
A large range of statistical tools for analysing biological data exists. To maximize the extraction of relevant
information from the lipidomics data, a combination of machine learning methods (unsupervised and supervised)
and univariate methods are used. Unsupervised analyses are based on no prior grouping (no labels), whereas
supervised analyses use prior knowledge (labels) to create prediction models.
Unsupervised methods
Unsupervised analyses are used to explore and visualize the diﬀerentiation of the samples across the lipidomics
data. Principal component analysis (PCA) and k-means are unsupervised methods used to explore patterns,
trends and ﬁnd groups of samples within the data. PCA is the most common method used for dimensionality
reduction. The method identiﬁes patterns, trends, outliers and indicate groups of samples, by covering the entire
sample space. PCA explains the variation in the data by variables deﬁned as principal components (PCs). The
method captures the maximum variance of data by using a reduced space (i.e. feature selection). The PCs are
weighted combinations of the variables (in this case lipid concentrations) and several PCs are computed [39,
40]. K-means clustering is a non-hierarchical clustering method where the samples are clustered into k clusters.
K is pre-speciﬁed, and the result of the clustering depends on the speciﬁed number of clusters [41]. Each sample
9
==PAGE:21==
Theory
belongs to the cluster where its measurements are closest to the centroid of the concerned cluster [40]. K-means
in combination with PCA can give a good identiﬁcation of trends in the data.
Supervised methods
Supervised analyses use labels in a training set of the data to supervise the creation of a prediction model.
The model is then tested with a test set with unlabelled data [42, 43]. Feature selection makes is possible to
identify important lipids diﬀerentiating our two sample groups. The supervised methods partial least squares
discriminant analysis (PLS-DA) and random forest (RF) are used as classiﬁcation models. PLS-DA classiﬁes
the samples by creating a logistic regression model. The relationship between the lipid concentrations and the
two classes are given in the regression process by constructing a set of loading weights. Feature selection is then
conducted by extracting variables importance on projection (VIP) values from PLS-DA weight vectors. The
VIP values provide a score value that rank the variables according to their importance in the projection by the
PLS-DA model [39]. Performance of the model are assessed by the cumulative Q2Y metric. Q2Y can take on
values between 0 and 1, where a higher value indicates a better performance [44]. RF is a classiﬁcation-tree
approach used to predict classes of the samples based on decision trees. Variables are ranked according to
their importance in the classiﬁcation; thus, RF can be used for feature selection based on the ranked variables.
Performance of the RF classiﬁcation model is evaluated by a receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, by
using the confusion matrix to compute the true positive rate and false positive rate for the model. Also, the
area under the curve (AUC) value is computed to assess the goodness-of-ﬁt of the model. AUC indicates the
probability that the class of a value is correctly predicted, where a value equal to 1 indicates a perfect model
and a value above 0.5 is better than a random classiﬁer [39, 40].
Univariate methods
Univariate methods consider each variable one at a time, as contrast to the machine learning methods (unsuper-
vised and supervised), which consider all variables at the same time. Based on the format of the data, diﬀerent
univariate methods can be assessed. If data are non-parametric a rank sum test, Mann-Whitney U, is used, as
it does not assume any distribution. The method is based on randomly selected values from each of the two
groups, which determines the degree of similarity between the groups [45, 46]. When applied to lipidomics data,
this method tests the lipid concentration of each lipid individually against the two groups, thus the method is
prone to induction of false positives, due to the many hypotheses test. If data are log2 transformed a paramet-
ric distribution is used and data are assumed to be normally distributed. In this case the limma test can be
used to measure the diﬀerential abundances of lipid concentrations. Limma is a method which uses regression
models to handle complex experimental designs and is designed to overcome the problem of small sample sizes.
The limma method was originally designed for analysis of microarray data (i.e. genomics), however, have been
applied within other omics ﬁelds [47].
Univariate methods test multiple hypotheses, and the number of hypotheses increases together with the prob-
ability of wrongly rejecting the null hypothesis, thus inducing ’false positives’ [45]. The false discovery rate
(FDR) is used to correct for multiple hypotheses and typically the Benjamin-Hochberg (BH) method is used.
The FDR is typical chosen over the family-wise error method (Bonferroni), because the Bonferroni correction
tends to lose important biological information due to its more conservative computation [39]. Besides FDR
adjusted p-values both volcano plots and heatmaps are a way to visually assess the output. Volcano plots
illustrate the statistical signiﬁcance (y-axis) against the fold-change (x-axis). Heatmaps can give an overview of

2.3.4
Bioinformatics tools: Biological networks by integration of omics data
Analysis of lipidomics and metabolomics data separately is important to discover key diﬀerential abundance
biomolecules within the respective ’-omes’. However, by applying methods from systems biology we can obtain
a deeper insight into the interactions within the lipidome and polar metabolome by integrating lipidomics with
metabolomics [49, 50]. Network analysis is an important method within systems biology, and combined with
a community analysis, a pathway analysis and an enrichment analysis, we can get a more holistic view of the
biological network compared to individual analyses [32]. Like other high-throughput techniques omics techniques
can produce ’false negatives’ and ’false positives’. Thus, an integration of omics techniques may improve the
reliability of the results, or it might add noise and make it harder to get something more reliable compared to
the individual omics analyses [51, 52].
Biomolecules in a network are termed nodes and their interactions are termed edges. A Spearman rank corre-
lation (Spearman’s ⇢) computes the interactions of the biomolecules within the biological network. To verify
the biological network a random network is built, where nodes are similar to that of the biological network
but connected with probabilities (edges) instead of Spearman’s ⇢. Random networks act as null models for
their respective real-world networks and are an important benchmark. If a property of the real-world network
is found in the random network, it implies that it is no diﬀerent than what would be obtained by chance,
indicating a potentially poorly deﬁned approximation of the biological network to a real-world network. The
random network is based on the Erdos-Renyi model, a model based on the Euclidean distance between nodes
[52, 53, 54].
Complex networks, such as biological networks, tend to be robust.
Robustness refers to the ability of the
biological system (in this case the metabolome) to maintain homeostasis, despite external or internal changes.
Various network measurements can be used to evaluate the complexity and connectivity of a network. The
measurements assess if the network is connected enough to allow the complex network ﬂows to happen within
the lipidome and polar metabolome. By assessing a combination of several metrics, we are able to gain insight
into how nodes behave and inﬂuence each other in the network. We focused on measurements for nodes such as
degree, betweenness, closeness, eccentricity, and eigenvector centrality. Also, we focused on measurements for
the network such as the average degree, average path length, clustering coeﬃcient and the minimum "cut" [52,
55], the measurements are elaborated in table 2.2.
It is expected that any node in a network with no particular organization will have similar centrality as those
in the random network, and that the random network has a more compressed degree distribution, which may
not be true for the real-world network, as it may have biomolecules with only one or few connections [52, 53].

3.4
Diﬀerential abundance analysis of lipidomics data
Diﬀerential abundances of lipids between the two groups (HIV-NoMetS/HIV-MetS) were discovered through a
collection of diﬀerent methods including unsupervised, supervised and univariate methods.
3.4.1
Unsupervised analysis: Dimensionality reduction and cluster analysis
Applying dimensionality reduction with PCA retrieved a subset of principal components explaining most of
the variance based on a threshold value > 1%, which the variance of each principal component should exceed.
For clustering with k-means, the number of clusters were set to k = 2, which corresponded to the two groups
HIV-NoMetS and HIV-MetS. The two unsupervised methods have been used to investigate diﬀerent trends
within the lipidomics data according to the clinical variables (i.e. condition, sex, ART, etc.) and a random seed
has been set for reproducibility (Section 2.3.3).

==PAGE:27==
Methods
3.4.2
Supervised analysis: dimensionality reduction and classiﬁcation
Supervised classiﬁcation with binary classiﬁcation modelling was used to predict HIV-NoMetS and HIV-MetS
based on lipid concentrations. Key lipids were found by feature selection by using PLS-DA and RF (Section
2.3.3). In order to obtain more precise prediction models, R packages with implemented cross validation have
been used, and the performances of the models were evaluated by using the Q2Y value and the AUC value, for
PLS-DA and RF respectively. PLS-DA was used to classify the samples by creating a logistic regression model
by using the R package ropls [44]. However, there is a downside to this method, as models trained on data sets
with a larger number of variables compared to the number of samples can be prone to overﬁtting, as in the case
of our data set which have 917 variables and 200 samples. Thus, the method is used with reservation. Feature
selection is conducted and a subset of VIP values er derived based on values with a score > 1 by using the
getVIP() function. RF classiﬁcation modelling was used with the R package MUVR [62], which is developed for
creating results that are robust with a small sample set compared to using the R package randomForest only.
MUVR minimizes overﬁtting on data sets with many variables and fewer samples, just as the lipidomics data
set. The MUVR package has incorporated cross validation in the function and due to the relatively few samples
in our data, the k-fold (nOuter) was set to 8. This value is considered high and was set to increase the number of
samples in the model training. Also, the number of repetitions (nRep) was maximized at 50 and the proportion
of variables kept per iteration (varRatio) set to 0.85. Variables found from the optimal modelling performance
were selected according to their rank, where lower rank indicated stronger predictors for the response variable.
The MUVR algorithm was run with parallel processing as it performs resampling of the data in each repetition.
Thus, reproducibility was hard to obtain without the possibility of setting a seed, this was however accounted
for by maximizing the number of repetitions (nRep = 50) [62].
3.4.3
Univariate analysis
The non-parametric Mann-Whitney U test was used on the data. The p-values were FDR corrected and a subset
of lipid variables derived from the univariate test with an adjusted p-value < 0.001.
The R/Bioconductor
package limma [47] was also used to measure the diﬀerential abundances of the lipid concentrations.
This
method expects a normal distribution, thus lipid concentrations were log2 transformed. Multiple regression
models were computed, and the p-values were FDR corrected and a subset of lipid variables derived with FDR
< 0.001. The log fold changes were not considered for any of the two methods, due to the small sample size (n
= 200) (Section 2.3.3).
3.4.4
Discovering key lipids from lipidomics data
Based on a consensus of the supervised methods (Section 3.4.2) and univariate methods (Section 3.4.3), a
set of key lipids with diﬀerential lipid abundance between HIV-NoMetS and HIV-MetS was determined. The
mathematical basis of the methods used for feature selection diﬀer, thus the methods provided diﬀerent results.
However, the intersection of the diﬀerential abundance lipids of the methods were regarded as lipids of interest
and were further determined as the key lipids in this study. Additionally, to gain a deeper insight into the
lipidome and polar metabolome of PLWH with MetS, a network analysis of all lipids and key metabolites was
conducted to see how the biomolecules interact and group.

3.5
Lipid-Metabolite network- and enrichment analysis
We did a network analysis combined with a community analysis, which identiﬁed areas of interest in the
biological network. Also, the analyses identiﬁed areas where an enrichment analysis could give a deeper insight
into the underlying biological mechanisms of MetS in PLWH. Thus, it becomes possible to analyse the biology
of lipids and metabolites at a systematic level. By combining a correlation analysis with graph theory and a
community analysis we analysed the interaction between lipids (n = 917) and key metabolites (n = 11), which
led to pathway- and ontology enrichment speciﬁc analyses.
3.5.1
Lipid-Metabolite network and community analyses
Correlation analysis of lipids and metabolites
Initially a correlation analysis of all lipids (n = 917) and signiﬁcant metabolites (n = 11) was conducted. The
key metabolites have been found in a previous metabolomics study of the same cohort [17]. Spearman’s ⇢was
computed on the non-parametric lipid-metabolite data set (variables = 917 + 11, samples = 200). To limit the
number of false positives, we controlled for the multiple hypotheses tests by using FDR (Section 2.3.3).
Network construction and evaluation
Two networks were built and analysed. One weighted network with weights based on the positive correlations
found in the correlation analysis (i.e. FDR < 1e-07, Spearman’s ⇢> 0.38). The other network was a random
network with the same number of nodes and probabilities as edge number. Nodes represented biomolecules,
and edges interactions between the biomolecules. Due to lack of annotation of the biomolecules, we considered
the edges as undirected, which indicates a two-way or reversible relationship between the biomolecules. In
the Spearman correlation analysis, we compared ranks and normalization of data was therefore not required.
However, for building the network graphs we used a mix of lipid quantiﬁcations and metabolite quantiﬁcations,
and for this purpose data were normalized. Graphs for the two networks were constructed with the Python
module igraph [63].
A centrality analysis of the nodes in the networks evaluated the degree, betweenness, closeness, eccentricity
and the eigenvector centrality. Key characteristics of the network were evaluated by average degree, average
path length, clustering coeﬃcient, if the network was connected, and the minimum "cut" (Section 2.3.4). All
measurements combined revealed a reliable biological network diﬀering from randomness and this allowed for a
more in-depth analysis of network communities.
Community analysis and network visualization
A scoring function determined the partitions of communities based on sets of densely linked nodes; the used
scoring function was the modularity. High modularity indicates dense connections within the community and
sparse connections across diﬀerent communities [57]. The communities in the two networks were detected by
use of the Python module leidenalg [56]. The Leiden algorithm is faster and have a better partition quality
compared to the built-in Louvain algorithm in the igraph module, both use modularity as scoring function
(Section 2.3.4) [56].
The positive weighted network along with the community partitions were imported into Cytoscape (version
3.5.1, freely downloaded network tool) [64] in order to visualize the global lipid-metabolite network. Average

node degree for each community were computed and evaluated. An in-depth analysis of each community was
performed to investigate the involvement of the nodes within a community in common biological pathways and
processes.
3.5.2
Pathway and ontology enrichment analysis
Properties of lipids and metabolites within the diﬀerent communities were investigated in-depth to gain a
mechanistic insight into the lipidome and polar metabolome, which can help explain the underlying biolog-
ical mechanisms of MetS in PLWH. Initially, two well-acknowledged pathway enrichment tools were used to
identify enriched pathways in HIV-MetS compared to HIV-NoMetS. Lipids with diﬀerential abundance be-
tween the two groups (Limma, FDR < 0.1) were submitted to Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA) (Qiagen,
US). Afterwards, lipid-metabolite sets from the community analysis were submitted to the online web-tool
MetaboAnalyst (https://www.metaboanalyst.ca) [65].
Both tools did however, not return any signiﬁcantly
enriched pathways (Limma, FDR < 0.1), possibly due to lack of lipid annotation in the data bases. Thus,
two novel lipidomics speciﬁc web-tools were applied; LIPEA (https://lipea.biotec.tu-dresden.de/home) [66] and
LION/web (http://www.lipidontology.com) [37]. The tools were used to identify speciﬁc altered pathways (LI-
PEA) and used as a lipid ontology enrichment tool (LIONweb). Separate analyses of each lipid-metabolite
set from the community analysis were submitted to both LIPEA and LION/web (Target-list mode) together
with a background list of all lipids and metabolites (n = 917 + 11). The tools are lipid speciﬁc and based on
identiﬁcation of lipid abbreviations, thus do not account for the metabolites.
3.6
Integration of clinical data with omics data
Properties of the lipid-metabolite communities were further investigated by inclusion of the clinical data. The
aim of this analysis was to investigate the associations between the clinical variables (Section 3.3) and the
lipidome and the key metabolites, while also considering their respective communities.
3.6.1
Analysing global network trends
To identify global trends within the network the R/Bioconductor package PCAtools [67] was used to correlate
the lipidomics PCs from the PCA analysis with the clinical data and test the signiﬁcances of these correlations
using Pearsons’s r on log2-transformed data.
The PCs accounting for 90% of the explained variance were
included and considered in the analysis.
Additionally, trends of the structural composition of lipids in the
network were explored by using the R/Bioconductor package LipidomeR [48] based on the limma R package
[47].
3.6.2
Analysing network community trends
Network community trends were identiﬁed by ﬁrst exploring the structural composition of lipids in each com-
munity by the ontology enrichment tool LION/web [37].
Moreover, regression models were constructed to
predict the relationship between each clinical variable (n = 13) and each identiﬁed community (n = 3). A
total of 13 ⇥3 = 39 models was computed, and p-values were FDR corrected. An adjusted p-value < 0.12 was
interpreted to infer an association between the communities and the clinical variables. This cut-oﬀwas based
on how many false positives we were willing to allow. An overall value for each community was computed for
each sample in two steps. First, the abundances of each lipid and metabolite were normalized by computing

3.6.3
Analysing biomolecular trends of the central community
To identify biomolecular trends within the most central community regression models were constructed to

age and condition[NoMetS/MetS]. Thus, a total of 10 ⇥917 = 9170 models was computed. P-values were FDR
corrected and an adjusted p-value < 0.07 rejected the null hypothesis, that there was no association between
the concerned clinical variable and the concerned biomolecule.

Discussion
The study resulted in three main ﬁndings. First, we presented data suggesting increased abundance of the
glycerolipids TAGs and DAGs in PLWH with MetS compared to PLWH without MetS. Second, a network
integration of the lipidomics (analysed in this study) and metabolomics (previously analysed [17]) data suggested
interactions between speciﬁc glycerolipids structural composition patterns and key metabolites involved in the
glutamate metabolism. Third, we presented data indicating a relationship between these speciﬁc glycerolipids
structural composition patterns with HIV and MetS speciﬁc clinical variables.
Non-infectious complications have become an increasing problem for the population of PLWH, and especially
MetS is a concern, which leads to cardiometabolic diseases, such as CVD and T2D. An incomplete understanding
of the metabolic patterns in PLWH with MetS still exists. Thus, this study was set out to investigate the
underlying biological mechanisms of MetS in a HIV population which has followed a long-term ART treatment
for more than 10 years. To our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst structural composition analysis of the eﬀect of
metabolic patterns in PLWH with MetS. Additionally, this is also the ﬁrst study of its kind conducting an
integration analysis of the lipidome and the polar metabolome in the context of MetS in PLWH.
5.1
Increased abundance of glycerolipids in HIV-infected with MetS
Perturbation of the lipid metabolism in the plasma of people with MetS is extensively studied and include an
increased level of free fatty acids and triglycerides (i.e. hypertriglyceridemia) within the bloodstream, which
may be related to abdominal obesity and insulin resistance (Section 2.2) [32, 68].
However, there are few
lipidomics proﬁling studies investigating the role of the systemic composition of lipids (i.e. structure, function,
interactions and dynamics) in the context of factors of MetS [5, 69], and such studies have quantiﬁed 135 and
211 lipid species, respectively. In comparison our data contained > 910 quantiﬁed lipid species. The lack of
lipidomics studies in general and the lack of such studies including a high number of quantiﬁed lipid species,
are due to the recent development in lipidomics.
In our study, we found 13 key glycerolipids from the classes DAG (n = 2) and TAG (n = 11) to be signiﬁcantly
altered between the two study groups. It is worth noting the composition of the 13 lipids. The two DAGs
((DAG(16:0/18:1) and DAG(16:0/18:3)) both have 34 (16 + 18) carbons and 1 and 3 double-bonds, thus long-
chained unsaturated fatty acids. Additionally, 10 out of the 11 TAGs were long-chained polyunsaturated fatty
acids (i.e. 52-54 in carbon number and 2-5 in double-bond content). The last TAG had a lower carbon number
of 44, compared to the others and was saturated. The lower the double-bond content, the denser structure of
the lipid, and a denser structure means a more stable molecule, which is hard to break down [70].
These ﬁndings correlate with the ﬁndings made in previous studies of MetS. A review by Hinterwirth et al.
[71], evaluating lipidomics studies of CVD in non-HIV infected populations, was done. They included studies

that showed that lipids (especially TAGs) with lower carbon number (i.e. 44-54) and lower double-bond content
(i.e. 1-4) were associated with an increased risk of T2D, whereas the risk decreased with lipids of higher carbon
number and higher double-bond content (i.e. longer chains and more unsaturated lipids). Moreover, it had
been observed that an increase in DAGs was associated with hypertension, another MetS-related factor [71].
Information about fatty acid structure is a useful indication of lipid metabolism, and increased accumulation of

fatty acids might cause impairment of mitochondria functions [72].
5.2
Biomolecular patterns in clinical and omics integrated network
The global network characteristics showed that our biological network had real-network properties, compared
to randomness. This correlates with the conclusion of the review by Barabási et al. [52], evaluating studies
using network analysis on biological systems, suggesting that a high degree of internal order within biological
systems governs a molecular organisation.
The global trends of the integrated network gave an indication of how the network was built and its properties,
which showed positive correlation with VAT and increased levels of glycerolipids. The network analysis allowed
us to look at coordinated abundance shifts in lipids. Interestingly, through enrichment and community analyses
we observed that community c1 contained glycerolipids with a lower carbon number and lower double-bond
content compared to c2 which showed glycerolipids with higher structural composition numbers (Table 4.4).
The relationship between communities and clinical variables (Appendix table I.1) through regression analyses
indicated that community c1 positively associated with the clinical variables MetS, VAT and exposure to
early-generation ART (FDR < 0.12).
Community c2 was positively associated with MetS, though with a
lower estimate compared to c1 (FDR < 0.12). Diﬀerent from other studies we were able to associate whole
communities of speciﬁc lipid compositions with HIV and MetS speciﬁc clinical variables by a comprehensive
network analysis. To our knowledge, this has only been done in few previous lipidomics studies [73] and not in
the context of MetS in PLWH. However, our ﬁndings are related to previous ﬁndings that showed TAGs with
a lower carbon number and lower double-bond content play a considerable role in MetS [69, 74]. Our results
additionally suggest that exposure to early-generation ART (i.e. thymidine analogues/Indinavir/Didanosine)
and increased VAT may also lead to lower carbon number and lower double-bond content in glycerolipids (Figure
4.13), suggesting a role for this lipid category in the metabolic patterns in PLWH with MetS.
Previous studies investigated the association of lipidomics proﬁles with the progression of CVD in PLWH
receiving ART treatment, compared to non-HIV infected individuals [5] in a large cohort (737 vs 200 patients in
this study). Compared to our study we were able to quantify a much larger number of lipid species (211 [5] vs
917). The study showed elevation in long-chained polyunsaturated (i.e. fatty acid tails of 13 to 21 carbons and
double-bond content ≥2) lipid species, which is also observed in our study. Additionally, their study suggested
signiﬁcant alterations in lipid species such as CE, LPC, LPE, PC, PIs and CER. Other studies in both HIV and
non-HIV populations also found alterations in levels of other lipid species (i.e. diﬀerent from DAG and TAG),
to be associated with MetS factors. This included CE, CER, LPC, PC, PE and SM [5, 69]. Some of these lipid
species were also altered in our study population (i.e. CE, CER, PE and SM), however the glycerolipids showed
strongest predictive values. Our detailed ﬁndings of coordinate abundance shift in glycerolipids, might be due

to our considerably larger amount of quantiﬁed lipid species (n = 917) compared to other studies ([5, 69, 74]
quantiﬁed < 215 lipid species each).
Regression analyses (Appendix table J.1) highlighted the two following trends with respect to coordinate abun-
dance shift in glycerolipids within community c1. First, TAG species with carbon numbers between 48-54 and

NNRTIs, respectively. Both the use of NNRTIs drugs and exposure to early-generation ART have shown to
cause disruption of the mitochondrial functions in previous studies [11, 75].
Moreover, the composition speciﬁc glycerolipids showed to be correlated with some of the key metabolites
found in the previous metabolomics study on the same cohort [17]. These main ﬁndings from the metabolomics
study indicated alterations in the amino acid metabolism and more speciﬁcally perturbations of the gluta-

in non-HIV-infected populations observed that amino acids are one of the major metabolite groups dysreg-
ulated in obese individuals, particularly the branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs) (i.e.
leucine, isoleucine
and valine) [32, 68]. Additionally, these studies observed increased concentrations of glutamate, whereas the
ﬁrst step of the BCAAs catabolism is production of glutamate [70].
Thus, the ﬁndings of perturbation in
the glutamate metabolism in PLWH with MetS obtained in our metabolomics study supports the ﬁndings
from the HIV-uninfected population. Additionally, it should be noted that the polar metabolite acylcarnitine
(pimeloylcarnitine/3-methyladipoylcarnitine (C7-DC), abbreviated PC/3-MAPC), an important member of the
fatty acid metabolism, was found to be signiﬁcantly down-regulated in PLWH with MetS. This molecule fa-
cilitates the transportation of long-chain fatty acids into the mitochondria for catabolism through β-oxidation
[70]. These ﬁndings support the hypothesis of mitochondrial dysfunction in the context of the lipid ﬁndings.
Additionally, it should be noticed that both the amino acid metabolism and the glutamate metabolism are
related with the mitochondria.
To our knowledge, correlation between composition speciﬁc lipids and polar metabolites has not been seen in
previous studies of MetS. The polar metabolites and key lipids are clustered together in the same community and
are positively correlated with each other (FDR < 1e-07, Spearman’s ⇢> 0.38). These ﬁndings suggest a pattern
and relationship between polar metabolites involved in the glutamate metabolism and neutral glycerolipids
having a speciﬁc structural composition of their fatty acid chains (i.e.
long-chained polyunsaturated lipid
species). These ﬁndings have, to our knowledge, not been suggested in previous studies. In order to identify
this cluster of associated lipids and polar metabolites as potential biomarkers in PLWH with MetS, our ﬁndings
should be validated in other cohorts.

could play a part in the alterations of the identiﬁed biological mechanisms.
The synergistic eﬀect may be
based on the following diﬀerent factors. (1) An increased pro-inﬂammatory state induced by HIV resulting
in chronic systemic inﬂammation through immune activation, which induces progressive pathophysiological

5.3
Evaluating analysis methods used in the study pipeline
Several diﬀerent approaches can be applied when handling big data, such as omics data. The analysis pipeline
created in this thesis (ﬁgure 3.1) was based on the data formats combined with methods applied by other
studies, with respect to the time limitation of the thesis.
The importance of cleaning data
Depending on the scope of the study data cleaning are done to diﬀerent extent. However, an important step in
order to achieve unbiased conclusions [60]. The ﬁelds of lipidomics and metabolomics are constantly evolving,
and the methods used by our supplier to obtain results are considered as high standard, as they are able to
quantify a vast amount of lipids and metabolites, respectively. Imputation of data is done to avoid removing
important samples from the data and to avoid problems with missing values when doing the statistical analyses.
Our supplier did a minimum imputation, which impute each incomplete variable by the smallest observed
value of that metabolite.
No information on how much of our data the supplier imputed was given.
One
study evaluated more than 30 diﬀerent imputation approaches for metabolomics data and identiﬁed imputation
by use of machine learning methods as the best method [77]. Additionally, they suggested to use minimum
imputation only with drug metabolites. Other studies have also applied imputation by machine learning [73].
By removing variables (biomolecules) with no or near-zero variance, after receiving the omics data from our
supplier, we assured data sets with variables containing a variance. Furthermore, data were transformed when
applied with models assuming normal distribution. The most common transformation methods used for non-
linear data are the logarithmic- and the power transformation [60]. The log transformation was chosen over
the power transformation, as our data contained no zero values, due to the imputation done by the supplier
(log transformation cannot handle zero values, whereas the power transformation can). The eﬀect of possible
outliers was not further explored, due to time limitations and scope of thesis. By investigating the distribution
of each variable, possible outliers could have been detected and either corrected or removed from the data set.
However, as this was not done, possible outliers might have skewed the ﬁnal results. Moreover, the clinical
variables were selected based on the quality of their format from the questionnaire (non-hierarchical structured
variables selected, due to small sample size), and a total of 13 HIV-MetS speciﬁc variables was chosen, due to
the relatively small sample size (n = 200).

Considerations regarding the use of the statistical methods
For the purpose of learning and applying statistical methods on omics data, we used several diﬀerent methods.
Based on an extensively literature study of previously applied methods for metabolomics and lipidomics data
analysis, a mix of machine learning (unsupervised and supervised) and univariate methods were used [39,
34]. Thus, a combination of mathematically very diﬀerent methods was used to determine diﬀerences in the
abundance of lipid concentrations between HIV-NoMetS and HIV-MetS. Identifying an intersection of key lipids
based on a consensus of diﬀerent statistical methods has, to our knowledge, only been done by this study and
the metabolomics study [17].
A set of key lipids were identiﬁed based on the intersection of signiﬁcant lipids from four methods without
accounting for the performance of each individual method (Figure 4.5). The supervised methods showed a dif-
ference in performance, where the RF did a good job (AUC = 83.2%) at correctly classifying the lipids compared
to the PLS-DA method, which had a poor performance (Q2Y = 0.319). The latter performance is most likely
due to a small sample-size, however, could also be due to poor optimization of the model. Univariate methods
with FDR corrected p-values were used. Such methods assume independency of the variables, which may be
contradicting to use on biological data sets, as biomolecular networks are complex and very interconnected,
which is hard to account for even by FDR correction [42, 52, 78]. Univariate methods are however still widely
applied on omics data [5, 34, 39]. In contrast, machine learning methods, such as RF consider the whole sym-
phony of changes in biological samples and are increasingly being applied in the omics ﬁeld. Thus, one could
argue that, based on the complexity of biological networks and on the performance of the RF MUVR model
(AUC = 83.2%), the RF method should have been used solely to identify a set of key lipids with diﬀerential
abundances between HIV-NoMetS and HIV-MetS. However, it was decided to use the intersection of the four
methods as it gave a consensus of diﬀerent statistical methods. Moreover, when identifying biomarkers or a set
of optimal lipids, it could be argued that the ’Min’ MUVR model (7 strong predictors) could have been used
instead of the ’Max’ MUVR model (13 strong predictors). Both models showed same good performance (AUC
= 83.2%), thus both good candidates. However, for this study we wanted to capture the largest set of lipids
diﬀerentiating the two study groups, in order to gain more insight into the trends of data.
Evaluating the approach of the bioinformatics analysis
Pathway-, ontology- and network analysis increase the information generated by the individual omics studies.
However, we did not ﬁnd any signiﬁcantly enriched pathways, which may be because lipidomics is a relatively
new ﬁeld, thus no prior knowledge regarding lipid relationships exists, and also very few lipids sets are available
leading to few annotated lipid species. We were not able to ﬁnd any studies relating biological pathways with
speciﬁc lipid species within the plasma of people with MetS. Network analysis was used to investigate the complex
relationship between the biomolecules, to characterise metabolic patterns. The integration of metabolomics and
lipidomics was done to establish possible correlations between the lipidome and polar metabolome, and maybe
in this way connect a lipid proﬁle to a pathway, as metabolites generally are more annotated compared to lipid
species. Only the key metabolites [17] were included from the metabolomics data in order to reduce the number
of variables. Moreover, a weighted network based on positive correlations from the Spearman rank correlation
was created to enhance the strength of the network interactions and to avoid confusion between negative and
positive correlated biomolecules within the network. Weighted correlation network analysis by using either the
igraph Python module or the WGCNA R package has previously been done in lipidomics studies and also in
omics integration studies [5, 37, 73].

5.4
Strengths and limitations
The main strengths of this study include a well-characterised HIV study cohort matched on MetS, sex and age.
Furthermore, high-throughput and high-quality lipidomics (i.e. > 910 quantiﬁed lipid species) and metabolomics
(i.e. > 1100 quantiﬁed metabolite species) data. Careful considerations regarding inclusion of HIV and MetS
speciﬁc variables, well-considered and documented data preparation, and application of state-of-the-art bioin-
formatics methods are also considered as strengths of this study. The present study also has some limitations,
such as non-fasting glucose samples, which could have interfered with our ﬁnal results. Another limitation was
the cross-sectional design, as no conclusions on causality could be drawn, we were only able to assess the preva-
lence of the diseases in the plasma samples. Furthermore, the sample population was relatively small (n = 200),
thus diﬀerence between sex was not examined (this was also due to time limitations). Lastly, no non-infected
controls were included as benchmark in the study.
5.5
Perspective and future research
There are many interesting future studies, which can be done. First, it would be interesting to ﬁnd metabolic
biomarkers in PLWH without MetS, with risk of developing MetS. This could be obtained by a prospective
study-design with repeated sampling. Moreover, it would be interesting to look at what role the sex variable
might play in the structural composition patterns and mechanisms of the lipidome and polar metabolome.
Previous studies have shown that sex and age signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the metabolic signature [79]. If more time
were available, we would have made the same analyses as conducted for the whole population on the male
samples (n = 180) to compare with ﬁndings from the whole population. To do this speciﬁc study set-up, and
for future study set-ups it would be beneﬁcial to increase the sample size and also include a control group
consisting of individuals without HIV and MetS. With a control group it would be possible to compare changes
in the lipid composition pattern and the associations to the polar metabolome and clinical factors with that of
patients with HIV-MetS.
Another interesting study would be to integrate our ﬁndings from the lipidome and polar metabolome with other
omics techniques (e.g. transcriptomics, proteomics, microbiomics) and thereby make a possible connection to
known biological pathways, and then obtain a global picture of alterations within the plasma of HIV-MetS. Such
a multi-omics study including lipidomics has previously been successfully conducted in an Alzheimer’s disease
study [73]. Moreover, a subsequent study could focus on targeted lipidomics and further explore the structural
composition of the glycerolipids in PLWH with MetS, which might reveal even more about the composition
patterns in the lipidome. Finally, future studies including similar study cohorts would be needed in order to
validate the ﬁndings that we have made in this study. Such studies would be needed in order to help guide the
choice of ART treatment and the development of even better ART drugs for PLWH, and thereby contribute to
prevent the increase of MetS-related comorbidities among PLWH.

6

Conclusion
The aim of this study was to investigate metabolic patterns in a HIV-infected population with MetS, which
have followed a long-term ART treatment for more than 10 years. We focused on combining clinical data with
lipidomics and metabolomics data to investigate underlying biological mechanisms of MetS in PLWH.
In this study, we have three main ﬁndings. First, a set of 13 key glycerolipids with increased abundance in PLWH
with MetS compared to PLWH without MetS, was identiﬁed. Second, our network and community analyses of
the integration of the lipidomics and metabolomics data identiﬁed a top community (c1) enriched in long-chain
polyunsaturated glycerolipids, which contained 6 of the key glycerolipids and 6 polar key metabolites, which
are involved in the glutamate metabolism. Third, we found the top community to be positively associated with
MetS, VAT, and exposure to early-generation ART (i.e. thymidine analogues/Indinavir/Didanosine). Within
the top community c1 we found two diﬀerent structural lipid composition patterns to be associated with VAT
and NNRTIs use.
In conclusion, these ﬁndings suggest the importance of the structural composition patterns of glycerolipids in
the context of excess risk of MetS-related comorbidities among PLWH, such as T2D and CVD. A better knowl-
edge of the structural composition of various glycerolipid species and their correlation with polar metabolites
and clinical variables expands our understanding of the role of lipids in PLWH with MetS. However, an in-
complete understanding of the pathophysiology of MetS in PLWH still remains, as researchers are hampered
by the lack of prior knowledge regarding lipid species relationships. Once we have a better understanding of
structural composition patterns of lipid species and their role in biological pathways, novel lipid biomarkers and
therapeutic targets could be established to avoid metabolic abnormalities in PLWH with MetS, as an extension
to conventional blood lipid measurements.
