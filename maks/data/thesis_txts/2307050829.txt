Abstract
Data for oﬀshore wind resource assessments is usually either extrapolated from data of a
few oﬀshore met. masts or derived from mesoscale numerical weather prediction (NWP)
models or reanalysis data. In comparison to met. masts, ﬂoating LiDAR systems (FLS)
hold out the prospect of reduced costs and fewer restrictions regarding their deployment.
Moreover, FLS have a smaller uncertainty compared to simulated wind time series.
Performance veriﬁcation tests between oﬀshore met. masts and FLS have shown good
agreements for the wind speed measurement. One challenge of the deployment of FLS,
however, is the approach to dealing with data gaps that might occur due to system failures.
The extent of the error that is caused by data gaps is depending on the gap length, the
number of gaps, their distribution and location in the wind time series and also on the
respective year and location of the measurement site.
Coherent data gaps in the wind time series of FINO1 with a length of 30 days where found
to cause a mean error of 1.3 % of the annual mean wind speed in the year 2007 and of
1.4 % in the year 2006. However, the extent of the error shows seasonal variations which in
turn cannot be generalized as they change from year to year. The extend of the error of
the annual wind power density and also the error of the annual energy production (AEP)
generally exceed the one of the annual mean wind speed. However, a high error of the
annual mean wind speed does not necessarily coincide with a comparably high error of the
AEP. A reason for this is that the wind speed frequency distribution is usually ﬁtted by a
Weibull probability density function from which the AEP is derived in a second step but
also because changes of the wind speed do not result in changes of the energy production
when the wind speeds exceed a certain value.
Because missing data from LiDARs cannot be reconstructed from measurement data of
backup sensors, data which is generated by the mesoscale numerical weather prediction
(NWP) model WRF (Weather Research and Forecasting Model) is used in order to ﬁll the
data gaps which have been created before. Even though the correlation of the 10-min mean
wind speed and wind direction values of the two wind time series is only moderate, their
annual characteristics match fairly well.
Compared to the error which is caused by missing data, the error of the wind time series
which has been complemented by means of the data from WRF is considerably reduced.
However, the extent of the reduction of the error exhibits seasonal dependencies and is
smallest in summer where the atmospheric stratiﬁcation is stable.

1. Introduction
The accomplishment of wind resource assessments is inevitable for the planning process of
a wind energy project. Thorough knowledge about the site speciﬁc wind conditions provides
information about the proﬁtability of a wind farm, the most suitable wind farm layout and
requirements due to structural loads.
In order to ensure the bankability of a project it is essential to ensure that the uncertainties
that are related to the wind energy project are as low as possible. This means that the
results of the measurement should accurately represent the true conditions.
Onshore, most measurement campaigns are accomplished by means of meteorological (met.)
masts even though LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) systems have gained popularity
in the recent years. Oﬀshore, the installation of met. masts is associated with very high
costs and possible constructional restrictions. Due to the absence of other data sources
it is therefore often drawn on data from reanalyses or atmospheric models. Compared
to on-site measurements this data is conﬂicted with relatively high uncertainties, though.
One of the reasons for this is that most of the NWP models are simply not designed for
predicting the wind ﬁeld in the boundary layer. This is why the deployment of ﬂoating LiDAR
systems (FLS) in wind resource assessments has a great potential. These systems hold
out the prospect of reducing both the costs and the uncertainties of oﬀshore measurement
campaigns. Further advantages of ﬂoating LiDAR system (FLS) are their ﬂexibility and
that they face few restrictions regarding their deployment.
FLS are usually equipped with an autonomous energy supply system that ensures that the
measurement equipment can operate throughout the whole measurement period. However,
in the case of a system failure, a long data gap could exist in the measurement data. This
is especially the case in situations where the weather conditions do not allow maintaining
the system.
The objective of the thesis is to analyze the extent of uncertainties that could possibly
result from missing data records during a one-year measurement campaign of a FLS. The
uncertainties due to data gaps are then related to other uncertainty sources associated with
the deployment of this measurement system during wind resource assessments. In addition,
it shall be assessed if it is reasonable in terms of reducing uncertainties, to use data that
has been simulated by means of the mesoscale NWP model WRF (Weather Research and
Forecasting Model) for ﬁlling the data gaps. Although the uncertainties that are related to
data which is obtained from atmospheric models is assumed to be rather high, apart from
data of a few oﬀshore met. masts or from satellite observations this data often is the only
available data that reﬂects the oﬀshore wind climate.
The analyses mostly focus on the uncertainty in the wind speed measurement. This is
because these uncertainties are assumed to be more crucial than the uncertainties in the
wind direction measurement. While the wind speed directly inﬂuences the energy production
of a wind farm and thus its proﬁtability, the wind direction is primarily important to optimize
the wind farm layout, which in turn inﬂuences the wake losses of the wind farm. Analyzing
the eﬀect of measurement uncertainties in the wind direction on the wake losses is beyond

2. Theoretical Background
In this section it is ﬁrst of all elaborated on wind resource and energy yield assessments.
This encompasses the relevant standards and guidelines both for deploying met. masts
and remote sensing devices, the actual accomplishment of wind resource and energy yield
assessments and the uncertainties related to wind resource assessments. Afterwards, the
particularities of the oﬀshore wind climate are described.
Furthermore, LiDAR and ﬂoating LiDAR systems are introduced and ﬁnally, wind ﬂow
simulations are addressed. An emphasis of the wind ﬂow simulations is the application of
the Weather Research and Forecasting Model (WRF).
2.1. Wind Resource and Energy Yield Assessment
Wind resource assessments are a crucial element of each energy yield assessment carried out
in the planning phase of a wind energy project. The main reason why both wind resource
assessments and energy yield assessments are performed is to evaluate the proﬁtability of a
project. To ensure the project is bankable, the uncertainty related to the AEP of a potential
site has to be as small as possible.
Wind resource assessments are usually performed with cup anemometers and wind vanes
which are mounted on a reasonably high met. mast. In addition to the mentioned measuring
devices the met. mast is usually also equipped with additional meteorological sensors and
a data logger. However, because of the high costs of increasingly tall met. masts which
are needed in order to met the requirements to wind resource assessments, remote sensing
devices (RSD) like LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) and SoDAR (Sound Detection
and Ranging) systems have gained popularity in recent years. These systems have the
additional advantage that they are very ﬂexible.
2.1.1. Standards and Guidelines
A formal international standard for wind resource and energy yield assessments does not exist,
yet. However, a new standard by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) – the
standard IEC 61400-15 “Wind turbines - Part 15: Assessment of site speciﬁc wind conditions
for wind power stations” – is in progress and is planned to be oﬃcially released in 2018 [15].
As of now, wind resource assessments are performed according to recommendations and
best practices which again are based on other IEC standards – especially on the standard
IEC 61400-12-1 “Wind turbines - Part 12-1: Power performance measurements of electricity
producing wind turbines” [16]. This standard is especially relevant because many aspects
that are important for power performance measurements, also apply to wind resource
assessments including the deﬁnition of relevant measurement parameters, descriptions about
applicable measurement setups and requirements for the measuring equipment.
One of the most detailed and most widely recognized guidelines for wind resource assessment
is the procedure “Evaluation of site-speciﬁc wind conditions” [18] by the Measuring Network

of Wind Energy Institutes (MEASNET) which was ﬁrst published in 2009 and which has
recently been revised. The guideline focuses on measuring the local wind conditions by using

on met. masts. While the ﬁrst version only addressed procedures relevant for wind resource

supplement but often also as stand-alone alternative to mast measurements. This represents
a contrast to the standard IEC 61400-12-1 on which it is based. In the second edition of the

constricted to applications where it can constantly be monitored by a met. mast.
Also part 6 of the technical guidelines for wind turbines [6] by FGW e.V. which is referred

German. Just like the MEASNET guideline, it includes a description about the performance
of both wind resource and energy yield assessments with both the use of met. mast
measurements and RSDs. This document also says that it is possible to perform RSD
measurements without the monitoring, as this can be impracticable.
Procedures which explicitly address the use of LiDAR instruments are given by recommended
practices of the International Energy Agency (IEA). The document “Ground-Based Vertically
Proﬁling Remote Sensing for Wind Resource Assessment” [13] describes how to install and
operate ground-based SoDARs and LiDARs and also how to verify the observed data by
means of reference met. mast measurements. The veriﬁcation procedure described in [13]
is on the whole consistent with the second edition of the standard IEC 61400-12-1.
Another recommended practice by IEA Wind though still a draft issue is the “Recommended
Practice for Floating Lidar Systems” [14]. It depicts the deployment of ﬂoating LiDAR
systems (FLS) both for assessing the wind resource for oﬀshore wind projects and for system
trials. Contrary to ground-based RSDs the reference system with which a FLS is validated
does not have to be a met. mast but can also be “trusted” LiDAR. FLS will be described in
more detail in Section 2.4.
2.1.2. Accomplishment of Wind Resource and Energy Yield Assessments
The aim of performing a wind resource assessment is to assess the wind conditions at the
site of a potential wind farm and at the hub height of the respective turbines.
In all standards and guidelines it is emphasized that a site speciﬁc wind resource assessment
should at least cover the simultaneous measurement of wind speed and wind direction,
preferably at several heights. Unless stated diﬀerently it will be focused on the MEASNET
guideline [18] in the following. Here it says that the parameters should be sampled at a rate
of at least 1 Hz and averaged over a period of 10 minutes. Apart from the mean values, the
standard deviation and the maxima and minima encountered during the averaging interval
should be recorded. Additional meteorological parameters which are recommended to be
recorded are the air temperature, the air pressure, the humidity and the ﬂow inclination.
When performing the measurements the speciﬁcations of the standard IEC 61400-12-1
have to be considered. This particularly relates to the use of appropriate measurement

equipment which has to be calibrated and mounted according to the instructions. If met.
mast measurements are performed the top mounted anemometer should have a height of at
least two-thirds of the hub height of the wind turbine (WT) which is planned to be installed
at the site and the met. mast has to be equipped with at least one additional anemometer.
The second anemometer is needed as backup sensor but also to determine the wind shear
and thus the wind proﬁle at the site. Just like wind speed, the wind direction should also
be recorded by at least two sensors mounted at diﬀerent heights. Among others this is
necessary for determining the wind veer.
RSDs should only be deployed as sole measuring device in ﬂat terrain but can be used as
supplement to met. mast measurements in complex terrain. In any case a veriﬁcation test
according to the speciﬁcations of the standard IEC 61400-12-1 has to be undertaken before
its employment and in case it is the only measurement device also afterwards. Especially
for large wind farms and in complex terrain, it might be necessary to perform several wind
measurements to representatively model the local wind conditions in the end. The spatial
extrapolation of the wind data to a speciﬁc point and the hub height of the proposed WT
is performed under consideration of the local topography and by applying suitable ﬂow
modeling methods.
In order to assess seasonal variations of the wind climate the duration of the measuring
campaign should cover at least twelve complete consecutive months.
After the actual performance of the measurement campaign the quality of the data has
to be assessed. Signiﬁcant errors or biases may have to be ﬁltered out and data gaps
may be ﬁlled by reconstructing data from the data measured by the backup sensors. The
MEASNET guideline states that the availability of the ﬁltered and reconstructed data of
both the primary wind speed sensor and the primary wind direction sensor should not be
smaller than 90 %. As opposed to this an availability of the primary wind speed and wind
direction sensor of 80 % is considered as acceptable according to the technical guideline
TR 6. However, it has be ensured that the relation to long-term reference data is not biased
by the data gaps [6].
Long-term reference data has to be considered because the wind climate typically shows
signiﬁcant inter-annual and seasonal variations.
This means that the results from a
measurement campaign only represent the period of time during which the measurement
data has been recorded. To be representative for the period of time when the wind farm is
operating the wind conditions thus have to be extrapolated to a future long-term period.
To do this it is assumed that the past variations of the wind climate also provide a good
estimate of future variations. If only short-term data is available for the site, long-term
reference data that has been recorded at a diﬀerent location, preferably at a relatively short
distance to the project site, has to be used. The reference data has to overlap with the
period during which data has been measured at the project site and should comprise as
many years as possible. Usually a reference period of 10 years is considered reasonable.
A long-term extrapolation of the wind conditions can either be performed by measure-
correlate-predict (MCP) or by long-term scaling methods. For applying both methods a
relationship between the site data and the reference data has to be found for a period of
at least one year during which data has been measured simultaneously. This relationship
can then be applied on the data to assess the long-term wind conditions at the site. MCP
methods require time-series of wind speed and wind direction that have been recorded with

Oﬀshore wind resource assessments present some speciﬁc challenges, many of which are
also signiﬁcant for the installation and operation of oﬀshore wind turbines (OWTs). The
equipment has to withstand the corrosive environment with salt water and a high humidity
and also extreme winds and waves during storm events. Moreover, the accessibility of
oﬀshore sites may be restricted due to bad weather conditions limiting both the periods
during which the measurement system is set up and during which repair and maintenance
can be performed. However, due to the smaller spatial variation of the meteorological
conditions fewer meteorological stations are usually required for measuring the wind resource
with a high amount of accuracy [3].
As onshore, in relation to measurement systems most conﬁdence is placed in tall met. masts.
Constructing oﬀshore met. masts is a very expensive procedure though and it is time
consuming to get the required permissions and to construct the tower. Alternative measure-
ment systems – though often rather regarded as supplement to met. mast measurements –
are ﬁxed or ﬂoating RSD and satellite-based radar measurements [3].
Another challenge exists in the long-term estimation of the wind resource. There are only a
few existing measurement systems from which data is available. Nor is it generally possible
to apply measurements from coastal meteorological stations, though they can be useful as
reference for MCP methods. Compared to real oﬀshore conditions the characteristics of
the boundary layer in the coastal area is more changeable and there are thermally-driven
ﬂows due to the diﬀerences in temperature between land and sea [7]. This will be further
addressed in Section 2.2.

2.2. Particularities of the Oﬀshore Wind Climate
In general, it can be assumed that wind speeds encountered oﬀshore exceed the ones
measured at a similar geological position over land. This results mainly from the absence of
topographical eﬀects at sea and from the fact that the roughness of water surfaces is very
low compared to the roughness of land surfaces. However, in contrast to onshore conditions
the roughness is changing in dependence on the wave height and thus on the wind speed
present in the lower part of the atmosphere.
Just as the surface roughness also the turbulence intensity is correlated with the wind
speed which in turn shows dependencies to the atmospheric stability. At low wind speeds,
thermally driven turbulence is dominating and the turbulence intensity is comparatively
low. With increasing wind speeds the turbulence intensity decreases until it reaches a
minimum at wind speeds of 8 m/s to 12 m/s. If the wind speed increases above these values
the turbulence intensity increases again because of the additional mechanically generated
turbulence that is caused by the increased roughness of the sea surface [23].
9 | 76
==PAGE:23==
2. Theoretical Background
Due to the low surface roughness both the shear and the turbulence intensity are lower
than on land resulting in comparatively lower wind loads. However, additional loads on
the structures of met. mast and OWTs are introduced by waves and currents. Another
peculiarity of the oﬀshore wind resource is that the atmospheric stability shows a smaller
variation than onshore. This is a result of the greater heat capacity of water which results
in more constant surface temperatures and a less distinct daily cycle. Because of the lack
of topography the meteorological conditions including the wind speed are furthermore more
homogeneous [3]. The impact of the atmospheric stability on the other hand is larger for
oﬀshore wind farms than for onshore wind farms. Because of the collocation of OWT to
large wind farms with many parallel rows and columns, wake eﬀects are generally more
signiﬁcant oﬀshore. Losses due to wake eﬀects are especially high during stable stratiﬁcation.
Due to the absence of turbulent mixing between the air masses in this situation, the distance
that the air passes until the wind speed recovers to its equilibrium after having passed a
WT is higher than in neutral or unstable conditions [23].
During unstable conditions turbulence is caused by thermally generated eddies in situations
when the surface is heating the colder atmosphere and the wind speed is smaller than
approximately 10 m/s. In northern Europe such situations are mostly occurring in autumn
and winter and are less frequent oﬀshore than onshore. Stable situations on the other hand
particularly occur in spring and summer when the lower atmosphere is considerably warmer
than the water surface. In these situation there is a strong stratiﬁcation of the atmospheric
boundary layer and the ﬂow is fairly laminar. During stable conditions wind speeds up to
about 15 m/s are reached. Despite of the low turbulence intensity during stable conditions
signiﬁcant stress on WT blades can be caused by large wind shears in the atmospheric
surface layer that occur in these situations. With increasing wind speeds the atmospheric
conditions become more neutral and at wind speeds larger than 25 m/s they can be regarded
as near-neutral. In these situations turbulence is mostly generated mechanically, thermal
eﬀects on the other hand are irrelevant [2, 23].
At coastal oﬀshore sites some particular wind patterns furthermore exist. Due to the lower
roughness at sea the ﬂow accelerates from land to sea and decelerates when the ﬂow is
blowing in the reverse direction. The temperature diﬀerences between the water and the
land surface and diﬀerences in the heat ﬂux are furthermore aﬀecting phenomena like the
sea-breeze, a diurnal cycle of onshore and oﬀshore breezes, and low-level jets. The sea
breeze originates during the day time when the land surface heats more then the water
surface. This causes a stream of cold air from the sea to the land in the lower part of
the atmosphere. During the night the process is reversed because the surface temperature
at sea is now exceeding the one at land. Low-level jets can particularly be observed in
the Baltic Sea which is more or less enclosed by land. They are encouraged by a stable
near-surface stratiﬁcation which can be induced when warm air from land ﬂows over the
relatively colder sea. Unlike in the reverse condition when cold air ﬂows over the warmer
sea and an induced unstable near-surface stratiﬁcation develops over a short distance from
the coast, the stable stratiﬁcation endures over several hundred kilometers. The reason for
this is the reduction of the turbulent mixing of the atmosphere [5].

2.3. LiDAR Systems
Wind LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) systems remotely measure the three-dimensional
wind vector at various heights and are applied in wind resource assessments, wind turbine
performance testing and for determining the wind proﬁle at a location [23].
The function principle of LiDARs is based on the transmission of light of a speciﬁed wave
length in the near infrared band around 1.5 µm. A part of this light is backscattered from
many small aerosols in the air that move at the same velocity as the air and thus represent
the local wind speed. When the light hits an aerosol its motion changes the frequency of
the light. If the target moves in direction towards the LiDAR the frequency is increased
while it is reduced if the aerosol moves away from the LiDAR in direction of the light beam.
By analyzing the small frequency or Doppler shift ∆f between the emitted frequency fe
and the backscattered frequency fb it is possible to draw conclusions about the radial wind
speed vr which is the wind speed in the direction of the light beam [2, 23]:
∆f = fb −fe = 2fe
c vr
(2.3)
Where c is the speed of light.
Background light on the other hand has a diﬀerent wave spectrum so that it can easily be
distinguished from the radiation of the LiDAR. Nowadays LiDARs are equipped with an
optical telescope that combines the transmitting and receiving antennas needed to transmit
and receive the radiation [23]. In Figure 2.3 the laser and detector are illustrated separately
in order to simplify the function principle.
Figure 2.3.: Function principle of a wind LiDAR system [23]
The three velocity components of the wind vector are obtained from orienting the light beam
towards at least three diﬀerent angles and combining the radial wind speed determined at
each direction. This implies that the wind speed is actually measured at diﬀerent points
and requires that the horizontal ﬂow within the measurement volume is homogeneous [23].
Two types of LiDAR systems are commercially available: continuous wave (cw) LiDARs
and pulsed LiDARs. The two systems diﬀer with regard to the form in which they emit the
radiation. While cw LiDARs continuously transmit a light beam and analyze the backscatter,
pulsed LiDARs transmit sequences of light pulses and then determine the Doppler shift in
the backscattered light of each pulse.
cw LiDARs measure the wind vector of each height successively by adjusting the focus
of the optical telescope according to predeﬁned measurement heights or ranges. At each

range the wind vector typically is conically scanned. The radial wind speed at the respective
heights is determined by applying a weighting function to the measured frequency shifts.
Particles that are close to the focusing range where the beam has the greatest intensity are
weighted the most while those which are further away are weighted less. While the minimum
range of the telescope depends on its focus capabilities, the capability of each telescope to
focus decreases with an increasing distance from the lens. The vertical resolution of the
system thus limits the maximum measurement height of cw LiDARs. They are therefore
also considered as short-range devices. Recent systems are able to measure the wind vector
at heights from 10 m up to 150 m [2, 23].
Pulsed LiDAR systems on the other hand measure the wind speed at up to more than
ten ranges or range-gates simultaneously by determining the arrival time of a light pulse.
Because the time that passes from the emission of a pulse until the reception of the
backscatter is directly proportional to the distance that the light had traveled, it is possible
to deduce the measurement height [2]. Contrarily to cw LiDARs the maximum measurement
height of pulsed LiDARs is not limited by the focusing capabilities of the system but rather
by declining Carrier-to-Noise (C/N) ratios at high distances, especially in situations when
the number of aerosols in the air is small. In practice, measurement heights of 40 m up to
about 300 m are reached. However, the spatial resolution of pulsed LiDAR systems is lower
than the one of cw LiDARs and they are much slower regarding the data acquisition. While
cw LiDARs process up to 500 measurement samples per second, pulsed LiDARs can only
handle up to four samples in the same period of time [23].
As the function principle of both LiDAR systems is based on the assumption that the ﬂow
within the measurement volume is homogeneous their application in complex terrain has to
be considered with caution. In ﬂat terrain and during periods in which the measurement
values are neither aﬀected by rain, fog, mist or low-hanging clouds, however, the 10-min
average wind speeds obtained from LiDARs and calibrated cup anemometers correlate very
well. During performance tests, correlation coeﬃcients of about 0.99 have been obtained
for both cw and pulsed LiDARs [23].
2.4. Floating LiDAR Measurement Systems
In comparison to ﬁxed met. masts ﬂoating LiDAR systems, i.e. LiDARs which are mounted
on a ﬂoating platform or buoy have major advantages. They are far less expensive than tall
met. masts and RSDs which are installed on ﬁxed platforms and obtaining a permission
for the deployment of the measurement system is much easier. Furthermore they can be
deployed in greater water depths, they cover more measurement heights and they are more
ﬂexible, meaning that they can be moved around for site prospecting and they can easily be
positioned at a diﬀerent site to be used in another wind resource assessment campaign later
on. Challenges in the usage of FLSs are arising from the need to compensate or correct the
motion of the ﬂoating device and to ensure the power supply of the system. As compared
to other meteorological sensors LiDARs consume a rather high amount of power. In order
to provide this power the ﬂoating platform has to be equipped with an independent power
generation system. In most cases this is a combination of a generation system and a battery
bank for storage. The power generation unit can consist of solar panels, small wind turbines,
a fuel cell, a generator or a combination of these systems. Both the supply and the storage

2.5. Wind Flow Simulation
Microscale wind ﬂow modelling is important for assessing the wind resource at the potential
project site and thus part of every energy yield assessment. In this case the ﬂow models
use the measured wind time series and a topographical description of the site to provide a
spatial description of the wind resource.
Mesoscale NWP models on the other hand make it possible to simulate wind time series
which may serve as replacement of measurement data in some cases. Wind time series
which are drawn from mesoscale NWP models or directly from reanalysis data can save time
and costs. However, the use of simulated wind ﬂow data is aﬄicted with large uncertainties,
even though the simulation methods and models are constantly improving [3]. According to
the guidelines simulated wind time series are therefore at the most accepted as reference
data for performing long-term extrapolations and also for this purpose only mesoscale
analyses that are initialized by the use of reanalysis data should be used [6].
Mesoscale NWP models have been developed for weather forecasting and for research
purposes. Just like computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) models which have been developed
for turbulent ﬂow modeling in aviation, mesoscale NWP models solve Navier-Stokes equa-
tions. Because they also include schemes and models of the atmospheric conditions and the
soil they are also capable of simulating thermally driven phenomena like for example the
atmospheric stability or the sea breeze. On the downside, mesoscale NWP models require
enormous computational eﬀort and typically have model resolutions which are much too low
for wind energy purposes as they usually span grids that have distances of several kilometers
between the respective grid cells over the surface of the earth. For that reason mesoscale
models are sometimes coupled with microscale models to get a more detailed estimation of
a site speciﬁc wind resource [3].
2.5.1. Wind Flow Simulation by means of WRF
The WRF model is a mesoscale NWP model that has been developed for research and
operational forecasting by the National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCAR) in collab-
oration with other institutes. The range of meteorological applications that can be handled
by WRF comprises large eddy simulations but also global simulations spanning from some
multiples of ten meters up to several thousands of kilometers. When performing simulations

development of the velocity components, the temperature and other atmospheric conditions.
Diﬀerent schemes and models available can be chosen according to the speciﬁc needs for
the categories microphysics, cumulus parameterization, planetary boundary layer (PBL),
land-surface modeling and radiation [25].
While the microphysics schemes include resolved water vapor and processes of cloud
formation and precipitation, the cumulus schemes are reproducing vertical ﬂuxes due to
unresolved updrafts and downdrafts. Furthermore, they are used for compensating motion
outside of the clouds and provide vertical heating and moistening proﬁles and the convective
component of surface rainfall. The PBL schemes are handling vertical ﬂuxes that would
emerge from the presence of eddies in reality. Furthermore, the PBL schemes are responsible
for determining the ﬂux proﬁles within the boundary layer. Atmospheric heating and ground
heat is constituted by the radiation schemes that are representing longwave and shortwave
radiation. Longwave radiation is including infrared and thermal radiation while shortwave
radiation is including the wavelengths of the solar spectrum. For the simulation, the model
also considers predicted distributions of water vapor including clouds and gas concentrations
in the atmosphere. Together with information about the properties of the surface of the
earth at the speciﬁed location, the land-surface models also use information from the other
physics schemes. These information are then used to update the state variables of the land
surface that encompass details about the surface temperature, soil temperature and soil
moisture proﬁles or snow cover [25].
Uncertainty of wind climate estimations by means of the WRF model

from the simulation (in case of the WRF simulation an hourly instantaneous wind speed
value) was matched with the temporally closest 10-min measurement value and the relative

It was shown that the wind climate generated by the WRF model is less aﬄicted by

of uncertainties depends on the model conﬁguration and also on the location for which the
data is modeled or more precisely on the regional wind climate and topography. Because
of the more complex topography that among others leads to diﬃculties in assigning the
regional surface roughness length and elevation in the model, the uncertainty in the modeled
onshore wind climate is generally higher than the one oﬀshore. For all oﬀshore sites included
in the analysis, the relative bias was smaller than 3.6 %. For the site of FINO1 the bias was
only 1 % with the wind speeds being slightly overestimated by the WRF model. The mean
wind speed for which this value was found is the average value of data from the period
between January 2006 and April 2011 [12].
It is believed that the coupling of the model to the sea surface temperatures is very important
for the ability of the model to accurately simulate the oﬀshore wind climate as temperature
diﬀerences between the lower atmosphere and the sea surface temperature have a great
inﬂuence on the atmospheric stability, especially in coastal areas [5]. However, the sensitivity
to the sea surface temperature also depends on the inﬂuence of thermal processes on the
wind climate in the respective region and is assumed to be larger in regions where eﬀects
like e.g. the sea breeze or ocean upwelling play a signiﬁcant role [12].
In the Baltic Sea but also in the North Sea the temperature diﬀerences between the air and
the sea surface are especially high in late spring and summer. If the wind now blows oﬀshore,
stable atmospheric stratiﬁcation is encouraged. In comparison to neutral or convective
stratiﬁcation stable situations implicate a lower turbulence and relatively low wind speeds
but also a less homogeneous wind ﬁeld. Due to the large gradients in the wind ﬁeld, the
WRF model has the largest diﬃculties with simulating these situations [5].

3.1. Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy
The ﬁrst prototype of the Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy has been developed in the
course of the project “Oﬀshore Messboje” and has been completed in 2013. An image of the
measurement system is shown in Figure 3.1. While this prototype is equipped with a pulsed
Windcube® v2 oﬀshore LiDAR device a second prototype that has been completed in 2014
is equipped with a cw ZephIR 300 LiDAR device [11]. The further analyses including a
performance veriﬁcation test and an uncertainty assessment that are described later in the
thesis (see Section 4.1 and Chapter 5) concentrate on the ﬁrst prototype. The further
description is therefore elaborating on this system as well although most of the features
also apply to the other device as the two ﬂoating LiDAR systems resemble each other.
Figure 3.1.: Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy (Photos: Caspar Sessler, Fraunhofer IWES)
The buoy itself is an adapted LT81 marine buoy, a type usually applied as navigational-light
buoy [10]. The buoy has a diameter of 2.55 m an overall height of 7.2 m, a weight of
4.7 t and can be utilized in water depths of at least 15 m. The Windcube® v2 oﬀshore is
able to measure wind speed and direction at twelve measuring heights from 40 m up to
about 250 m, depending on the atmospheric conditions. In order to protect the LiDAR
system it is encapsulated in a custom-made housing. In addition to the LiDAR system the
Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy is further equipped with a met. station to measure the

measurement system further serves for conducting oceanographic measurements. The

System (AHRS) additionally measures its movement by assessing the roll and pitch angles
of the buoy. This data is later used to correct the movement of the buoy caused by wave

The energy for the equipment is supplied by an autonomous system consisting of three

transferred through a wireless connection with a range of 300 m meaning that site visits are
necessary [9].
3.2. FINO1 Oﬀshore Met. Mast
The FINO1 research platform is located 45 km north-west of the island Borkum in the
North Sea at the location N 54◦00’ 53,5” E 6◦35’ 15,5” and has been in operation since
Autumn 2003. It was funded by the German Federal Ministry for the Environment, Nature
Conservation, Building and Nuclear Safety and constructed in order to investigate several
environmental parameters relevant for the construction of oﬀshore wind farms in the
German North Sea. The investigations carried out on the platform can be distinguished into
meteorological, oceanographic, technical and biological measurements; the latter include
among others the migration of birds and ﬁsh and underwater acoustics [8].
The meteorological data recorded by FINO1 includes measurements of the wind speed, wind
direction, air temperature, atmospheric pressure, atmospheric humidity and precipitation.
Most of the environmental parameters are simultaneously measured at diﬀerent heights.
The respective measurement sensors are mounted on a lattice met. mast with a height
of 80 m which is installed on the platform. The maximum measuring height thus is at
103 m above LAT. The measurement of oceanographic parameters is performed in order
to get information about the loads on oﬀshore structures that are induced by currents
and waves. Measured parameters are for example the sea conditions, i.e. wave height,
wavelength and wave direction, currents and the water temperature. Because meteorological
and oceanographic measurements are carried out simultaneously, it is also possible to
analyze reciprocal eﬀects of wind and wave induced loads [8]. Some more details about the
anemometers and the wind direction sensors can be found in Chapter 4.

The accuracy of the Fraunhofer IWES Lidar Buoy is assessed according to the procedure for
onshore veriﬁcation tests of ground-based RSDs speciﬁed in the standard IEC 61400-12-1

time the distance has to be far enough to avoid a mutual bias of the measurement data [16].

4.3. Simulated Data
4.3.1. Simulation Setup
The simulated data which will be used for ﬁlling gaps in the wind time series as described
in Chapter 7 has been generated by Christine Martens, Fraunhofer IWES by use of the
mesoscale numerical weather prediction model WRF 3. As input, Modern Era-Retrospective
Analysis for Research and Applications (MERRA) data is used to represent the atmospheric
conditions while Operational Sea Surface Temperature and Sea Ice Analysis (OSTIA) data is
additionally used for depicting the sea surface temperature. It has been decided to simulate
data for the year 2007 because out of the ﬁve year period from the complementation of
FINO1 until the installation of alpha ventus the input data was easily available for this
year. More speciﬁcally, data for the period from 2007-01-01 at 0:00 am until 2007-12-31 at
6:00 pm has been generated. The last six hours of the year are missing because unlike for
2007 the input data was not on hand for the year 2008 but would have been needed to
generate the last time steps in 2007. The simulation period was divided into four quarters
that correspond to the seasons as deﬁned in this thesis. Each quarter further has a lead
time of three days and a follow-up time of about two days. These periods are needed as
settling time for the model but are not used further on. The output from the simulation is
a wind time series with 10 minute intervals of both the wind speed and the wind direction.
The data has been interpolated to match the location of FINO1 at a height of 100 m. The
wind direction data had to be rotated by 180◦to correspond to the real wind conditions.
For the simulation, three domains centered at alpha ventus (N 54◦01’ E 6◦6’) have been
applied in the model and the respective domains are nested by the 2-way nesting method.
This means that the outer coarse grid and the ﬁner grid that lies inside of it interact in both
directions. For determining the lateral boundary conditions of the ﬁne grid the information
is passed from the coarse grid to the ﬁne grid and feedback is given back from the ﬁne
grid to the coarse grid at every time step of the coarse grid [25]. The size and position of
the domains are illustrated in Figure 4.10. Their spatial resolution is 18.9 km, 6.3 km and
2.1 km and their temporal resolution is 90 s, 30 s and 10 s, respectively.
The two outer domains are nudged at a height above 2500 m. By use of the nudging
boundary condition which is also referred to as relaxation or speciﬁed boundary condition it
is possible to specify the lateral boundary conditions of the grid of the chosen domain at all
sites. The speciﬁed lateral boundary of the coarse grid is comprised of a speciﬁed zone and
a relaxation zone with the latter being the zone in which the model is nudged or relaxed
towards the large-scale forecast. For the ﬁne grid, the speciﬁed lateral boundary conditions
are automatically chosen [25].
The vertical resolution of the simulation is deﬁned by the eta levels. In the present case, 62
eta levels are used. The lowest 20 of these levels are within the range up to 1 km from the
surface.
The physics schemes that are applied for the simulation are the Rapid Radiative Trans-
fer Model (RRTM) Rapid Radiative Transfer Model for longwave radiation, the Dudhia
parametrization for shortwave radiation, the Betts-Miller-Janjic scheme for the cumulus
parametrization and the Mellor-Yamada-Janjic (MYJ) scheme for the boundary layer or
turbulence parametrization.

5. Measurement Uncertainty of the
Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy
The uncertainty of the wind speed measurement of the Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy
is determined according to the approach described in Appendix L.4 of the standard IEC
61400-12-1 FDIS. The uncertainty components listed in the standard are the uncertainty that
results from the performance veriﬁcation test, the uncertainty resulting from the classiﬁcation
of the RSD, uncertainties due to a non-homogeneous ﬂow within the measurement volume,
uncertainties due to mounting eﬀects, uncertainties due to a variation in the ﬂow across
the test site and a possible uncertainty resulting from the system monitoring.
In accordance with [14] uncertainties due to a non-homogeneous ﬂow within the measurement
volume are disregarded. As the measurement system is installed oﬀshore it is assumed that
the terrain is homogeneous and that the ﬂow is not eﬀected by any obstacles or terrain
eﬀects. Also the uncertainties due to a variation in the ﬂow across the test site are not
considered. The standard IEC 61400-12-1 FDIS describes the assessment of power curve
measurements and the uncertainties that are associated with it. In the uncertainty analysis
of wind resource assessments horizontal and vertical variations of the ﬂow are looked at
separately. Because the system is a ﬂoating measurement system, the uncertainty due to
mounting eﬀects can likewise be neglected (also see 5.1.4) and also the uncertainties that
may result from the system monitoring are not considered in the following.
According to the standard the uncertainties have to be assessed bin-wise. For wind speed
measurements, bins with a width of 0.5 m/s are formed that are oriented towards the
measurement values of the reference sensor.
5.1. Uncertainty Resulting from the RSD Performance
Veriﬁcation Test
The components of the uncertainty that result from the performance veriﬁcation test consist
of the uncertainty of the reference sensor, the mean deviation of the reference sensor and
the RSD, the standard uncertainty of the measurement of the RSD, uncertainties due to
mounting eﬀects and uncertainties due to non-homogeneous ﬂow within the measurement
volume. However, like in the overall uncertainty the latter source of uncertainties is also
disregarded in the uncertainty of the performance veriﬁcation test. The measurement setup
of the veriﬁcation test and the wind speed data is described in Section 4.1.
5.1.1. Uncertainty of the Reference Sensor
In general, the standard uncertainty of a wind measurement sensor is composed of the
uncertainty due to the calibration and classiﬁcation of the sensor, mounting eﬀects, possible
corrections of mast shadowing eﬀects and uncertainties in the data acquisition system [16].
The information about the uncertainty of the anemometers and wind vanes that are installed

on the met. mast FINO1 are not known in detail. In the absence of speciﬁc values, the
uncertainty of the reference sensor is assumed to be 2 % of the determined mean wind speed

5.1.3. Standard Uncertainty of the RSD
The standard uncertainty of the remote sensing device is calculated by dividing the standard
deviation of the binned mean wind speed by the square root of the number of data present
in each respective bin. The results of doing this are shown in Figure 5.1.
5.1.4. Uncertainty due to Mounting Eﬀects
According to the standard IEC 61400-12-1 FDIS the uncertainty due to mounting eﬀects
is taken as a ﬁxed percentage of 0.5 % of the mean wind speed in each respective bin.
The movement of the buoy or more precisely oceanographic variables as the signiﬁcant
wave height, the peak wave period and the wave direction are considered in the sensitivity
analysis though. This means that mounting or movement eﬀects are already considered
in the uncertainty that results from the classiﬁcation of the measurement system and can
therefore be disregarded in the uncertainty due to the performance veriﬁcation test.
5.2. Uncertainty due to System Classiﬁcation
The uncertainty due to the system classiﬁcation reﬂects the uncertainty that might result
from diﬀerences of the meteorological conditions during the performance veriﬁcation test
and the deployment in the wind resource assessment. In addition to the environmental
parameters that are relevant for onshore conditions, oceanographic parameters that among
other things reﬂect the movement of the buoy have to be taken into consideration in the
sensibility analysis of a FLS.
The sensitivity analysis is performed by plotting the percentage wind speed deviation as a
function of the diﬀerent environmental parameters. The data (environmental parameter and
percentage wind speed deviation) is further sorted into bins depending on the respective
inﬂuence parameter. The bin widths are mostly given by IEC 61400-12-1 FDIS, Table L.1
but have been adapted if the given ranges resulted into very few bins. According to IEC
61400-12-1 FDIS, bins that do not include a suﬃcient amount of entries have to be sorted
out because they are not representative. An environmental variable is considered to be
relevant if a sensitivity (product of slope and standard deviation) of at least 0.5 is found or

6. Impact of Data Gaps on the
Uncertainty of the Wind Resource
Assessment
Data gaps emerge from failures of the measurement equipment or in the data transmission.
In the case of the Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy past data gaps have mainly been a
consequence of low battery voltage due to unfavorable weather situations or defects of the
energy supply system. To prevent deep discharge of the battery and to keep the voltage
level at a point that still allows sending status signals, the measurement equipment may be
switched oﬀin such a situation or it switches oﬀautomatically. The longest gaps occurred
in situations when the prevailing weather conditions furthermore complicated maintaining
the system, i.e. during stormy periods with high wave heights.
Up to now, data gaps with a duration of about one week have occurred several times.
After this period of time the voltage level had either recovered or the defect was remedied.
However, in the beginning of the year 2016 one of the Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoys
was inoperative for several weeks after the wind generators got damaged by breaking waves
and the weather conditions remained unsteady in the following weeks.
Another reason for large uncertainties may be insuﬃciently long measurement campaigns of
less than one year. In case of a failure and assuming that the measurement equipment is
not scheduled to be used in a diﬀerent measurement campaign, the measurement period
would be extended in such a situation. However, this only makes sense when the additional
measurement data can be assumed to suﬃciently represent the missing data, e.g. when it
is recorded in the same month so that seasonal eﬀects can be determined.
In this section it is explained how uncertainties that result from data gaps inﬂuence the
total uncertainty of the wind resource assessment in theory. Afterwards, the eﬀect of data
gaps on the error that is introduced on the wind statistics is investigated. This is done by
comparing typical characteristics of a wind time series in which parts of diﬀering lengths
are cut out to the ones of the complete wind time series with a duration of one year.
6.1. Uncertainties due to Missing Data
Uncertainties due to incomplete data or data availability are a part of uncertainties in the
wind measurement which in turn is a component of uncertainties in the wind resource
assessment as also illustrated in Table 2.1. According to the guidelines all uncertainty
components of the wind resource assessment are assumed to be independent from each
other and thus added in quadrature. However, it is reasonable that the uncertainty resulting
from data gaps also inﬂuences the uncertainty in data analysis and the uncertainty in the
long-term extrapolation. The extent and speciﬁc eﬀect of the dependencies are depending
on the amount of missing data and on the lengths of the existing gaps and are diﬃcult to
36 | 76
==PAGE:50==

determine in practice.
Uncertainties which are determined as uncertainties in the mean wind speed further have
to be multiplied by a factor which can be determined by means of a sensitivity analysis
in order to transfer them into uncertainties of the AEP. In the present case the factor
is approximately 1.2. In other cases it might also be somewhat higher. This means that
uncertainties from the wind resource assessment have a comparatively larger impact on the
total uncertainty of the net energy yield.
Because of the autocorrelative nature of the wind long coherent data gaps are in theory
introducing larger uncertainties on the wind statistics than shorter gaps which are distributed
over a certain period of time. This has to do with the fact that wind speeds that are
separated by one or two days can be regarded as being independent from each other [24].
In order to verify this theory, the eﬀect of diﬀerent numbers of randomly distributed gaps
with a duration between one hour and two weeks will be investigated ﬁrst. Subsequently,
coherent data gaps with a duration of up to 30 days are considered. If data from 30 days
is missing, the recovery rate of the wind time series is almost reduced to 0.9 which is the
minimum requirement to data completeness according to the MEASNET guideline.
The extent of the error caused by a gap varies largely depending on the location of
the data gap in the wind time series. Therefore, each analysis is repeated many times.
From the respective errors in comparison to the complete wind time series, the mean
absolute error (MAE) is derived. Among the root mean square error (RMSE) this measure
is commonly used in order to evaluate the prediction accuracy of forecasting methods

is low but this relationship is not always recognizable. A signiﬁcant inﬂuence of the wind

fairly well from data of other measurement sensors which are mounted on the same mast.

8. Discussion
The analysis about the inﬂuence of data gaps on the uncertainty in the annual characteristics
of the measured wind time series has shown that the extent of the error does not only
depend on the total availability of data, but also on the number of gaps, the respective
gap lengths and their location in the wind time series. Furthermore, diﬀerences of the error
have been observed in the considered years and it is likely that the extend of the error also
varies at diﬀerent measurement sites.
For the year 2006 a mean error of 1.4 % of the annual mean wind speed has been found
for a gap length of 30 days and an error of 1.3 % of the annual mean wind speed was
determined for the year 2007. If data of 30 days is missing, the overall data availability is a
little more than 90 % which is the data availability that is required in order to consider the
data as being complete according to the MEASNET guideline. However, the spread of the
error indicates that a particular error may be quite diﬀerent from the mean value of the
error.
Even though it is not possible to generalize the eﬀect of missing data in a certain season, it
has clearly shown that the extent of the error shows seasonal dependencies. One indicator
of the extent of the error is the diﬀerence between the annual and the seasonal value of a
parameter. The higher this diﬀerence is, the more likely it is that the MAPE is also high.
For a gap length of 30 days the highest mean error of the annual mean wind speed in
2006 with a value of 2.9 % was found if data of the autumn months October to December
were missing. In 2007 the highest MAPE was observed when data gaps were present in
spring. The MAPE of the annual mean wind speed was found to be 1.8 % here, which is a
considerably lower value than the maximum value observed in 2006. An eﬀect of the wind
direction on the extent of the error on the other hand was not seen.
Data availability is one inﬂuencing factor on the uncertainty in wind measurement. For FLS
other factors that add up to the total uncertainty in wind measurement are the uncertainty
due to the performance veriﬁcation test and the system classiﬁcation. Uncertainties due to
a non-homogeneous ﬂow in the measurement volume and due to mounting eﬀects can be
neglected for these kind of measurements. The ﬁrst, because it can be assumed that the
ﬂow oﬀshore is not disturbed by topographic eﬀects or obstacles and the second because
the uncertainty due to the mounting of the LiDAR system and the movement of the buoy
is already included in the uncertainty due to the system classiﬁcation through a sensitivity
analysis to the oceanographic parameters. In the exemplary analysis that was performed
in the thesis, the combined uncertainty due to the performance veriﬁcation test and the
classiﬁcation was found to be 2.3 % of the wind speed. However, it has to be mentioned that
the uncertainty of the reference sensor was probably assumed to be a bit to high. Taking
the MAPE of the whole year for a gap length of 30 days into account, the uncertainty due
to data gaps or data availability is a bit more than half of this value.
Filling the data gaps with data generated by the mesoscale NWP model WRF has shown to
be a good method to reduce the error of the wind speed measurement that resulted from
data gaps. After the gaps in the wind time series had been ﬁlled, the mean error of the

annual mean wind speed reduced by almost 90 %. Only in summer the simulated values did
not match the observations as well resulting in a reduction of the MAPE of only 67 %.
In order to improve the agreement of the simulated data and the observations it could
further be tested if it is useful to apply the analog ensemble (AnEn) method to the WRF
data when ﬁlling data gaps. This method is a relatively simple method used to correct
weather forecasts. Data from past forecasts and the matching observations are scanned in
order to ﬁnd situations that resemble the actual wind speed development. Good matches
are then used to determine a likely progression of the wind speed or to correct the predicted
trend. However, in order to apply the AnEn-method it is essential to have recourse to a
large data base from which matches can be drawn [27].
Looking at the generally already good agreement between the annual values of the wind
time series from the simulation and the measurement, one could argue that it is reasonable
to only rely on simulated data instead of accomplishing a one-year-long measurement
campaign. However, the coeﬃcient of determination between the two data sets was only
0.86 and the frequency of high wind speeds was underestimated by WRF. This might not
be that relevant when calculating the AEP of a WT or wind farm as wind speeds above a
certain value do not translate into an increased energy production anymore because the
WT reaches its rated power or even cuts oﬀ. This is also reﬂected in the MAPE of the
AEP. In the cases which have been looked at the mean error of the AEP was between 1 -
1.35 times higher than the one of the annual mean wind speed. The MAPE of the AEP
due to data gaps with a duration of 30 days was found to be 1.8 % in 2006 and 1.6 % in
2007. However, when it comes to estimating extreme winds and the loads that the WT
have to withstand during their operational life span it can be quite signiﬁcant that the local
wind speeds are well known. Moreover, the accuracy of the simulated wind time series does
depend on the site speciﬁc conditions and also on the simulation setup. Hahmann et al.,
2015 [12] have found a bias of 0.7 % up to 3.6 % between the long-term mean wind speeds
of the wind time series derived from WRF and measured at diﬀerent oﬀshore locations, with
the bias determined at FINO1 for the period from January 2006 until April 2011 being 1 %.
This means that the error that would have been determined at other locations than FINO1
might indeed be higher and that actual measurement campaigns should not be waived.

9. Summary and Conclusion
Oﬀshore measurements campaigns in which ﬂoating LiDAR systems are deployed hold
out the prospect of reduced costs and fewer restrictions regarding their deployment in
comparison to tall met. masts and reduced uncertainties in comparison to wind data which
is drawn from mesoscale NWP models or reanalysis data. The generally good agreement
between wind speed measurement data of oﬀshore met. masts and FLSs was demonstrated
by an exemplary veriﬁcation performance test of the Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy
which was performed near the research platform FINO1. The uncertainty of the wind speed
measurement of the Fraunhofer IWES Wind Lidar Buoy in which both the uncertainty due
to the veriﬁcation test and the uncertainty due to the system classiﬁcation were considered
resulted in a value of 2.3 % of the wind speed. The largest contribution to the total
uncertainty was the uncertainty of the reference sensor.
In comparison to met. mast measurements, missing data that results from system failures
cannot be reconstructed from measurement data of backup sensors. The approach to
dealing with data gaps thus is a major challenge. The extent of errors which result from
data gaps depend on various factors including the gap length, the number of gaps and their
distribution and position in the wind time series but also on the year and the location of
the measurement site. In the thesis, the eﬀect of data gaps with a length of up to 30 days
has been analyzed for data from the met. mast FINO1 of the years 2006 and 2007. In the
analysis it was focused on the eﬀect on the annual characteristics of the wind time series
that can be derived from wind speed measurements, i.e. the annual mean wind speed, the
Weibull parameters, the annual wind power density and the AEP. Data gaps with a duration
of 30 days where found to cause a mean error of 1.3 % of the annual mean wind speed in
the year 2007 and of 1,4 % in the year 2006. However, the error that was determined if
the data gaps were limited to a certain season was considerably higher in some cases. The
highest MAPE with a value of 2.9 % of the annual mean wind speed has been encountered
when data gaps have been introduced in autumn 2006.
The MAPE of both the annual wind power density and of the AEP generally exceed the
one of the annual mean wind speed. However, a high MAPE of the annual mean wind
speed does not necessarily coincide with a comparably high MAPE of the AEP. This is
because the wind speed frequency distribution was ﬁtted by a Weibull probability density
function but also because changes of the wind speed do not result in changes of the energy
production when the wind speeds exceed a certain value.
The comparison of the measured wind time series from FINO1 of the year 2007 and a wind
time series which has been generated by the mesoscale NWP model WRF for the same
period of time only showed a moderate correlation. Moreover, the WRF model revealed
tendencies to underestimate the occurrence of high wind speeds, which may be problematic
for determining wind induced loads or extreme winds. The annual values of the considered
characteristics on the other hand were matching the ones from FINO1 fairly well.
After having complemented the data of 2007 in which gaps had been cut before by means of
the simulated wind time series, the MAPE of the annual characteristics reduced signiﬁcantly.

9. Summary and Conclusion
While the MAPE of the annual mean wind speed decreased by more than 80 % for the
case that the whole wind time series was considered, it decreased by 67 % up to more than
90 % in the respective seasons. The smallest reduction of the error was generally observed
in summer. The reason for this is that stable atmospheric conditions are occurring more
often during this time of the year in comparison to the other seasons. Because of the large
gradients of the wind speed that might occur during stable atmospheric stratiﬁcation WRF
has the most diﬃculties with handling this situation.

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes 
organized into three main topics: Wind energy systems, Wind turbine technology and Basics for wind energy.
