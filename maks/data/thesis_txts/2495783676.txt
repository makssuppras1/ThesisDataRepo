Abstract
The European Organization for Nuclear Research (CERN) is responsible for
operating the largest particle accelerator in the world, the Large Hadron Collider
(LHC). At the LHC particles are accelerated up to almost the speed light and
collided with each other inside diﬀerent detectors, which allow scientist to study
particle physics.
The LHC will be upgraded in 2026 to increase the luminosity inside the accel-
erator.
The increased luminosity will result in an increased amount of collisions between
particles which will put the detectors under high load.
The ATLAS detector will need to be upgraded to operate after the luminosity
is increased. This thesis has been a part of the upgrade of the ATLAS Trigger
and Data Acquisition System (TDAQ) in the ATLAS.
The TDAQ performs an online ﬁltering of the data to remove noise and uninter-
esting measurements of particles. A key part of the ﬁltering is the track ﬁtting,
where the trajectory of the particles are reconstructed using the measurements
from the Inner Tracker in the ATLAS detector.
During the track ﬁtting the linearized χ2 is calculated of the trajectory, which
is used to determine if the data is relevant or not.
This thesis describes the design and implementation of the χ2 on a Stratix 10
MX FPGA board. The implementation is integrated with the rest of the track
ﬁtting operations into a Track Fitter Block. The χ2 calculator and the rest of
the Track Fitter Block are utilizing a high count of the DSP blocks available.
An analysis of the performance of the Stratix 10 MX has been done to determine
how the maximum frequency, power consumption and resource usage develops
==PAGE:4==
ii
as the count of utilized DSPs increases.
A design and implementation of the χ2 operation has been carried out. The
operation relies on vector dot products and a series of diﬀerent designs to per-
form vector dot product has been developed. The diﬀerent designs are balancing
the trade-oﬀbetween throughout, latency and resource/power consumption. A
simple implementation of each design has been done to evaluate the expected
performance on the Stratix 10.
A design was chosen with low latency, high throughput and a medium resource
usage for the ﬁnal implementation of the χ2. A test-bench was build to verify
the correctness of the implementation. The implementation managed to have a
high throughput, low latency and relative low resource/power usage
The χ2 implementation was integrated with the rest of the components in the
Track Fitter Block resulting in a complete implementation of the Track Fitter
Block.

Synopsis
I denne afhandling er komponenter til ATLAS detektoren i CERN blevet ud-
viklet.
CERN er en europæisk organisation med fokus på partikel fysik. Organisatio-
nens hovedansvar er at drive og vedligeholde verdens største partikel accelerator
(LHC). I acceleratoren bliver partiklerne accelereret op til tæt på lysets hastig-
hed, hvorefter at de kollideres i hinanden. Der er placeret ﬂere forskellige de-
tektorer omkring acceleratoren, som samler data om de nye partikler, der bliver
skabt ved kollisionen.
Det giver forskere muligheden for at studere partikel fysik, og acceleratoren gi-
ver uvurderlig vigtige observationer.
Denne afhandling har haft fokus på en af detektorerne, nærmere bestemt AT-
LAS detektoren.
Acceleratoren er ved at blive opgraderet igennem to faser, som i 2026 vil kunne
opnå et større energiniveau. Det betyder at langt ﬂere kollisioner vil forekomme
i sekundet. Dele af detektoren vil blive bygget om, og derfor skal dataopsam-
lings og ﬁltreringssystemerne i ATLAS også re-designes. Desuden vil de gamle
sorteringssystemer ikkekunne følge med den nye datarate, og derfor skal de op-
graderes.
Denne afhandling har bidraget med udvikling af komponenter til et af ﬁltre-
ringssystemerne, der hedder TDAQ.
TDAQ er ansvarlig for online ﬁltrering af data, hvor det ﬁltrer målinger væk
af uinteressante partikler. En af hovedkomponenterne for ﬁltreringen baseret
rekonstruktion af partiklernes baner.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) operated by the European Organization for
Nuclear Research (CERN) is the largest particle accelerator in the world, and
it has provided scientist with valuable observations that have been in state-of-
the-art research with-in particle physics. One of the detectors used to measure
the collisions is the ATLAS detector.
The LHC will undergo a series of upgrades within the next couple of years to
increase the luminosity in the accelerator. The high luminosity will increase the
rate of collisions happening in the ATLAS detector to a level where the current
Inner Detector and data acquisition system cannot keep up. The Inner Detector
and data acquisition system is therefor being redesigned and replaced.
This thesis has been a part of the development of the ATLAS Trigger and Data
Acquisition System (TDAQ), which is responsible for online ﬁltering of the data
from ATLAS. The ATLAS detector collects data at a high data-rate putting the
TDAQ under a great load.
To increase the performance of the TDAQ, parts of the system is implemented
on FPGA boards.
During this thesis a design and implementation of Track
Fitter Block (TFB) has been carried out.
The TFB is responsible of track
reconstruction and ﬁltering. During track ﬁltering an essential operation is the
χ2 calculation.
The χ2 calculation determines if a measured trajectory of a particle is interesting
==PAGE:20==
2
Introduction
for further processing. It is thereby used to reduce data moving forward in the
system hence reducing requirements to storage and processing capabilities in
the rest of the ATLAS computer system.
The χ2 component needs to have a low latency and a high throughput to be
able to keep up with the data-rate of incoming measurements from the ATLAS
detector. Many of the χ2 components will be operating on the ATLAS detector
hence the resource and power consumption is also of a concern.
During this thesis diﬀerent designs of χ2 component has been done and analysed.
The designs have been implemented to evaluate the balance between latency,
throughput and resource/power consumption. One of the designs were chosen
as the optimal design for the χ2 operation and a full implementation was carried
out. The implementation was integrated with the rest of the TFB.
The design will be implemented on an INTEL Stratix 10 MX FPGA board,
which is a state-of-the-art FPGA. An analysis of the expected performance of
the board under heavy load has been carried out.
During this thesis the χ2 was successfully designed. The design has been written
in VHDL and a test-bench has been developed to facilitate the functional testing
of the design. The latency, throughput, resource usage and power consumption
hace been met.
The design have successfully been integrated with the rest of the components
in the Track Fitter Block.

2.1
Introduction to CERN
2.1.1
LHC and ATLAS
The CERN is a joined European research organisation, which has the main re-
sponsibility of operating and maintaining a particle laboratory consisting of par-
ticle accelerators and detectors. CERN maintains and runs the largest particle
accelerator in the world; LHC. It is a circular accelerator with a 27 km circum-
ference consisting of superconducting magnets as well as accelerating structures
to increase the energy level in the LHC [1]. The particles inside the LHC travels
==PAGE:22==
4
Background - common work
in opposite direction in two separate tubes, where they travel in complete vac-
uum [2]. The particles are accelerated up to 99.9999991 % the speed of light and
guided into each other for collision. There are 4 diﬀerent detectors at the LHC:
ATLAS [3], ALICE [4], CMS [5] and LHCb [6], that carries out diﬀerent exper-
iments. The collision between the particles is highly controlled by the scientists
to happen inside one of the detectors, which then gathers data for research.
The LHC has lead to ground breaking physics discoveries, with the observation
of the Higgs boson in 2011 and 2012 as one of the most important ﬁndings by
both ATLAS [7] and CMS [8]. The Higgs boson was predicted by the standard
model, and it is necessary for the standard model to be correct. The observation
has thereby validated a part of the standard model, that had not been proved
before.
The LHC has proven to be capable of providing valuable scientiﬁc data, but
it is now reaching its limit of capabilities. To extend the possibilities of the
LHC, it has been decided to upgrade the LHC to increase the luminosity in the
accelerator. The project is called The High-Luminosity Large Hadron Collider
(HL-LHC), and it is expected to be operational mid 2026 [9].
The project aims to increase the luminosity with a factor of 10 beyond the
original design values of the LHC [1]. The increased luminosity will increase the
count of collisions that happens in a time slot. This result in larger quantities
of data being collected, which will increase the likely-hood of rare scientiﬁc
observations. But the increased data-rate causes a challenge to the detectors,
that needs to measure the collisions and process the data. The detectors will
therefore need to undergo diﬀerent upgrades and modiﬁcations to be operative
during HL-LHC. This master thesis is a part of the upgrade of the ATLAS
detector, which needs a new trigger system for online sorting and ﬁltering of the
data.
2.1.2
The ATLAS experiment
The ATLAS (A Toroidal LHC Apparatus) experiment is one of the four major
experiments connected to the LHC [3]. It is the largest detector at CERN, with
a length of 46 m, a diameter of 25 m and a weight of 7000 tonnes. It is a general-
purpose detector that consist of diﬀerent components to be able to exploit the
full potential of the LHC [10], which has lead to the size of the detector. Figure
2.1 shows an illustration of the detector.

The Inner Detector
The Inner Detector is the ATLAS component located closest to the collision
point. The current implementation of the Inner Detector will not be able to
handle the increased rate of interactions inside the detector, and will be damaged
by the increased radiation in the LHC. It is therefor being completely redesigned
and replaced.
Figure 2.2: View of ITk as implemented in sections [14]
The new design will be an all-silicon system called the "Inner Tracker" (ITk),
which will consist of two types of modules: pixel [15] and strip [16], that are able
to measure where a particle intersect the module. The pixel modules measure
the location of intersection point in two-dimension, while the strip modules
measure the intersection point in one-dimension.
Arrays of pixel an strip modules are placed on the ITk layers to allow the recon-
struction of charged particle trajectories. A visualisation of the ITk is shown in
ﬁgure 2.2. The modules are placed both in barrel layers, which are around the
collision point and in the end cap layers, which are placed perpendicular to the
LHC beam pipe in both ends of the detector. They are placed in this manner
to ensure that all possible tracks of the particles are measured correctly. Figure
2.3 shows the placement of the pixel and strip modules.
==PAGE:26==
8
Background - common work
Figure 2.3: A diagram showing the placement of the pixel and strip modules.
The red lines represent the pixel modules and the blue lines represent the strip
modules.
The LHC beam pipe is placed along the bottom axis.[17]
When a particle passes through the ITk each layer detects the intersection point
between the sensor and particle. The intersection point is referred to as a ’hit’.
The hits in each layer are used to reconstruct the trajectory of the particle,
and thereby analyse the type of particle. The particles are traveling in bunches
in the LHC which results in many collisions happening simultaneously. When
a collision happens many particles are shattered out through the detector. It
results in lots of hits detected, which is referred to as an ’Event’.
In ﬁgure 2.4 an expected collision is shown with the green lines representing the
tracks of particles send out from the collision point.
To be able to detect all the hits the ITk is build by many sectors, each measuring
the tracks of the particles. Each section has its own ﬁltering circuit to be able
to keep up with the high data-rate.

ATLAS Trigger and Data Acquisition
The TDAQ system performs a data reduction, rejecting uninteresting data mea-
sured by the detector. It consists of three parts: Level 0 (L0) Trigger, DAQ and
the Event Filter, as shown in ﬁgure 2.5.
The ﬁrst reduction of data is done in the L0 trigger, which consist of the L0Calo,
L0Muon, MUCTPI, Global Trigger and CTP[18] all colured with purple in ﬁgure
2.5.
The level 0 trigger is working on the data from the Calorimeters and the Muon
Spectrometer.
The Readout block uses the results from the Level 0 trigger to determine which
data from the ITk that is accepted.
The accepted data from the L0-trigger is sent to the Event Filter (EF) for further
processing. The Event Filter contains a CPU-farm, which run an algortihm for
event reconstruction and takes the ﬁnal decision, depending on the data coming
from the Hardware Track Trigger (HTT), which performs track reconstruction.

Stratix 10 MX
The implementation is based on a Stratix 10 MX 2100 FPGA [21] which is a
state of the art FPGA made for high performance computing. It consists of
8 GB of second generation High-Bandwidth DRAM Memory(HBM), 702.720
Adaptive logic Modules (ALM) and 3.960 variable-precision Digital Signal Pro-
cessing (DSP) blocks [22]. This makes it a very powerful FPGA for projects
with high memory requirements and computational requirements.
HBM2
The second generation HBM [23] on the Stratix 10 MX consist of an interface
divided into independent channels each having a 128-bit data bus operating at
DDR data rates. During the This makes it possible to achieve a transfer speed
of 512 GBps.
ALM
The ALM is a logic module on the Stratix 10 MX that consist of a LUT, a
two-bits full adder and four registers (see ﬁgure 2.8). This makes it possible
to implement logic functions, arithmetic functions, and register functions using
the ALM [24].

2.3.2
Constraints
In the ﬁnal implementation of the TDAQ each Stratix 10 will have 4 PRMs
implemented on them. There is a single TFB in each PRM resulting in, that
4 TFBs will be implemented on a single Stratix 10. The constrains have been
deﬁned pr. Stratix 10 unit, hence the constrains presented in the following will
be for 4 TFBs.
Timing constraints
Since the three computational blocks are part of a larger ﬁrmware design made
by the collaboration of scientist responsible for upgrading TDAQ, the design
constraints have been carefully chosen such that the entire board will perform
as desired.
The FPGA is going to be running with diﬀerent clocks for the diﬀerent sections.
They will range between 150 and 500 MHz depending on the sections of the
board. The TD, χ2 and HPC are predicted to run at a minimum of 200 MHz.
==PAGE:34==

The TFB is allowed latency of about 300 ns , which corresponds to 60 clock
cycles at 200 Mhz.
Power consumption
The power consumption of the FPGA have been estimated in a preliminary
research using Intel Early Power Estimator Tool[26]. It is based on an estimate
of the utilization of the diﬀerent building blocks of the FPGA, the input data
and the clock frequency.
The Current Best Estimates (CBE) of the PRM FPGA power consumption is
72 Watt of which the TFB consume 30 Watt including the static power con-
sumption of the FPGA. The implemented TFB design will need to stay within
this power budget.
Logic utilization
The ﬁrmware consists of many blocks with diﬀerent resource budgets. The TFB
is the only block that is estimated to utilize DSP blocks, thus all 3.960 DSPs
on the Stratix 10 MX will be available for the designs in this thesis. However
the DSPs have a large power consumption, and the utilization should be taken
into consideration when choosing a design.
The CBE for TFB logic utilization is at 10% of the available resources such as
registers and ALMs.

The data representation is a compromise between data resolution and memory
requirements.
Values stored with many bits contain more information, but
require more memory bandwidth.

The strip data consist of a 12 bit ﬁxed point value and a 5 bit overhead. The

undeﬁned, but this thesis assume 1 sign bit, 8 bit for exponent and 18 bit for the
mantissa, which are similar to the IEEE-754-2008 standard for ﬂoating-points
[27]. The diﬀerence between the IEEE Binary32 and the custom 27 bit ﬂoating
point format is the length of the mantissa. It results in that the custom ﬂoating
point has the same dynamic range as Binary32, but less precision.

3.5
Conclusion
This preliminary test of the Stratix 10 MX platform was done in preparation of
the actual project, and should thus be viewed as such. It helped us to under-
stand how the FPGA will behave under heavy DSP usage and under diﬀerent
timing constraints.
We believe the drastic decline in the clock frequency as a function of number of
DSPs is due to the interconnect wire delay. However we believe that this will
be less of a problem for the actual implementation, as it will use fewer DSPs
per sub-circuit, and can thus utilize the DSPs on the FPGA in a faster way.
There was a signiﬁcant diﬀerence of the maximum frequency between the two
test circuits, even though the circuits are similar. The diﬀerence between the
two circuits is the placement of the DSPs. In the HPC test circuit all DSPs
are independent of each other, while the DSPs are grouped in pairs in the χ2
test circuit. It suggests that dependencies between DSP blocks may cause an
issue in the design. It might be because the synthesis tool has more diﬃculties
performing an eﬃcient ﬂoor-planning. Thereby longer wires are used which is
known to have a big impact on the performance of an FPGA.
Research [31] shows that the interconnect delay causes 50% of the total delay in
some designs, which is not far from what we experience during the tests. How-
ever as the test designs has several thousands DSPs in parallel, it is somewhat
diﬀerent from what we will implement in the project. Therefor we do not expect
the problem to be this vast, however it should be taken into consideration in
the design process.
The logic utilized in the test was used primarily for the multiplexers that chooses
to output the result from one of the DSPs. They ensures that the synthesis tool
does not trim away some of the DSP blocks during the tests.
It was evident that the multiplexers were the main contributors to the logic uti-
lization because exactly half the logic was used in the χ2 tests compared to the
PC tests. In the χ2 test circuit two DSPs were used to generate a result, while
one DSP was used to generate a result in the HPC test. The χ2 test circuit had
therefor half as many outputs as the HPC at a certain count of utilized DSPs.
Hence half the count of multiplexers was required by the χ2 to chose a result
for output.
The tests also showed an approximated linear increase in compile time.
To

5.2
Mathematical operation of the χ2
The mathematically operation of the χ2 is based on a study done by H. Wind
[33]. Using the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) he showed that the vari-
ances of some coordinates are large, while others are very small and almost
insigniﬁcant. Any atypical point is much less likely to have as many negligibly
small new components [33]. The analysis showed that by determine atypical
tracks in a large sample of measurements, then they were able to deﬁne a con-
strain equation of each coordinate. Those equations can then be used to deter-
mine if a track is typical or not and thereby can be ﬁltered away.
The particles are moving in a helix after the collision inside of the ITk, which
is diﬃcult to reconstruct and results in complex constrains equations and cal-
culations. The analysis showed that the movement of the particles can be ap-
proximated using a linear calculation instead, because the ITk is suﬃciently
small. The linearization reduces the complexity of the calculations signiﬁcantly
without compromising the quality of the ﬁt. The equation of the χ2 is shown
in equation 5.1. The track is evaluated as an interesting track, if the χ2-value
is low[34] otherwise it is ﬁltered away.
The analysis also showed that it is important to take the environmental fac-
tors of the detector into account, because the placement of the sections and
the strength of the magnetic ﬁeld have an impact on the result of the analysis,
which therefor has to be included in the calculations. To be able to do the best
estimation of the usability of the track, the χ2 is calculated using the equation
showed in equation 5.1. The values Ai,j and ki are constants that takes the en-
vironmental factors into account and implements the constrain equations. Both
constants are determined by simulation to ﬁt the section of the detector, where
the HTT board is placed. The constants are saved into memory beforehand
allowing the χ2 to retrieve the relevant constants when needed.

5.4.2
Reduced Resources Design
The reduced resources design was developed to minimize the resource usage by
utilizing both the multiplier and adder in the DSP. It is designed to operate
in applications where the date-rate is to low to ﬁll a pipeline. The design that
aims to minimize the resource usage was developed to investigate the trade-oﬀ
between DSP usage and throughput / latency. The design uses a single DSP to
calculate the inner sum and a single DSP for the outer sum. The summations
are done by feeding back the result of the DSP to the input for addition. The
previous result is thereby added to the new multiplication. An overview of the
design is shown in ﬁgure 5.3.
The inner summation is done through 12 iterations. In the ﬁrst iteration Ai,0 and
x0 are multiplied and ki is added to the multiplications. The next 11 iterations
the result from the DSP is feedback to be added to multiplication of Ai,j and
xj with j running through the remaining 11 values. When the inner-summation
is complete the DSP performing the outer-summation calculates the square and
feeds back the result to the input of the DSP. The DSP for outer-summation
is then kept disabled until the next inner-summation is complete. When the
outer-summation has run 7 times the χ2 is complete and the output valid signal
goes high. The DSP’s are then reset to clear the current summation and be
ready for the next track.
The design reduces the DSP count, but it require control signals to operate
correctly. The following control signals are required:
• Enable signal to ensure that the outer-summation DSP only squares the
ﬁnished summation of the inner-summation
• Reset signals to clear the summations when the χ2 result is obtained
• Select signals to chose which input signals are used in the inner summation
The control logic requires counters to keep track of the summations hence logic
is needed.
The design minimizes the time where the DSPs are stalling, but it reduces the
throughput since it takes many iterations before the result is ready.
The circuit will have a latency of 12 ∗7 + 1 = 84 clock cycles, assuming that
the multiplication and addition takes a single clock cycle. The circuit is not
pipelined, hence it cannot take-in a new data-set before it has completed the
χ2 calculation. The design is optimized for applications where data is arriving
rarely, but the expected data-rate has shown to be high for the χ2, hence the
design is not well suited for the χ2.

The cascaded design is designed to be able to utilize both the ﬂoating point
multiplier and adder in the DSP block (the structure of the DSP is described

The inner-summation is calculated by creating a chain of 12 DSPs that feeds
the result into the next DSP. Each DSP calculates a multiplication between Ai,j
and xj and adds to result from the previous DSP the multiplication. The ﬁrst

summation. It results in that the circuit is not pipelined with regards to the
cluster data, and it needs to wait 7 clock cycles before a new cluster set can be
taken in.
The latency of the design is: 12 + 7 = 19 clock cycles.
One downside of the design is that pipeline registers are required to be used in
order for the design to function correctly. The Ai,j and xj need to be delayed
for same amount as the intermediate result is being delayed through the DSPs
in the chain. The data needs to be delayed j times resulting in the following
count of 32-bit registers:
2 · (1 + 2 + 3 + ... + 11) = 132 [32-bit registers]
The valid signal needs to be aligned with the result from the χ2 calculation.
It is therefor propagated through the same count of registers to ensure correct
timing.
The increased register count takes up area on the FPGA, but the Intel has
packed registers into all the routing segments on the Stratix 10, hence a high
count of registers is available, which can be placed very eﬃciently . The register
count may therefor not be an important design factor.

Comparison with Intel IP core
An alternative design will rely on the IP core from IntelcO. They have designed
an IP core "Floating Point Functions Intel FPGA IP" that can implement a
scalar product. It that allows the user to deﬁne the length of the vectors that
are to be used for the calculation. The IP core deﬁnes the DSP in three diﬀerent

settings allow the IP cores to build a structure similar to the design from the
parallel design described in section 5.4.1, but it utilizes both the multiplier and
adder in the DSPs. It uses the registers from the DSPs which minimizes the
resource usage of the design.
The IP core reduces the design and implementation eﬀort required for the arith-
metic part of the χ2, which makes it an attractive choice. But it reduces the
ﬂexibility and optimization potentials from the designer. Also it makes it very
diﬃcult to port the implementation for other devices other than Intel FPGA
boards.
The IP cores has a latency of 16 clock cycles at a clock frequency of 200 MHz.
The IP cores was not chosen for this implementation because a better latency
can be achieved with the cascaded design. Also it will not be possible to port
the implementation to another device provider, because the IP core is only to
be used if a license is obtained from Intel. It is therefor an advantage to avoid
the usage of large IP cores in the implementation.

5.5.1
Design of the χ2-calculator
A single χ2-calculator needs to both calculate the χ2 of the incoming track and
handle the ID’s and the cluster data of the track.
The χ2 is calculated using the Cascade Pipelined Design as explained in section
5.4.4. The design is pipelined enabling it to receive a new track each clock cycle.
The result from the χ2 is compared with the threshold value to test if the data
is accepted or if it needs to be discarded. In parallel the result is converted into
ﬁxed point format, which is needed in the later systems. If the data is accepted
by the comparison a valid signal conﬁrms that the data is correct and it can be
processed by the following system. Otherwise the valid signal shows that the
data is uninteresting and the data-set is dropped.
In the top level design it was decided to place the HPC after the χ2-calculator
to reduce the memory accesses. It requires that the HPC receive the data from
the χ2-calculator for the clusters that are accepted. Therefor the χ2-calculator
needs to store the cluster data in a buﬀer/memory to be able to send the data
to the HPC if the data-set is accepted by the χ2 calculations. The ID’s of the
data are also stored in a buﬀer to be send to the HPC.
Queuing of output values
The χ2 block needs to output a maximum of one track pr. clock cycle. On
average approximately 8% of the tracks are accepted by the χ2-calculator, but
it is possible that more of the parallel χ2 calculations result in an accepted track
simulations. It is therefor required to have a circuit, that can buﬀer and queue
the results from the χ2-blocks to supply a single stream of data to the HPC.
The HPC needs both the χ2 result, the cluster data and the ID’s.
The queuing circuit is designed to have a single level of buﬀers, that stores the
data of the accepted tracks from the χ2-blocks. The buﬀer layer is designed to
place a three buﬀers for each χ2-block. One buﬀer to store the IDs, one for the
clusters and one for the χ2 result. It will ensure that all blocks can write at the
same time.
A control circuit is designed to communicate with the HPC and to pull a set
of data from one buﬀer at a time. The order in which the tracks are being
processed is not important for the rest of the TDAQ system, as long as they are
all completed in the same event . The IDs are mapped to the tracks to ensure
that the corresponding data in the system can be retrieved correctly. Though it
is important that the buﬀers does not get ﬁlled and that no data-set is sitting

Arithmetic part
The χ2-arithmetic part is implemented according to the design of the cascaded

The DSP blocks are conﬁgured using the Intel IP core "Native Floating Point

The ﬂoating point DSP blocks on the Stratix 10 has several pipeline registers
(as mentioned in the section 2.3.1), which gives the DSPs a latency of 5 clock
cycles. To reduce the latency, all the registers except for the output register
have been disabled. It minimizes the latency, but it also reduces the maximum
frequency signiﬁcantly, because the critical path goes through both the adder
and multiplier, and it needs to travel between the DSPs causing a wire-delay.
The DSP blocks were able to run at approximately 230 MHz with the single
register activated which fulﬁlls the target frequency of 200 MHz. The frequency
was obtained when only a single χ2-block was implemented. The tests done on
the Stratix 10 in section 2.3.1 showed that the frequency may be reduced when
more DSP and logic is utilized on the Stratix 10 board.
The timing of the arithmetic part of the circuit was investigated before the
full implementation of the χ2-calculator was done. The test showed that the
arithmetic part of the χ2 was able to run at a maximum frequency of 227.
The full χ2-calculator consist of 4 parallel arithmetic parts, that each calculates
the χ2 of a track. When all four parts were instantiated in the full χ2-calculator
the maximum frequency dropped to 200 MHz. The tests that were carried out
on the Stratix 10 (see section 2.3.1) showed that it is expected that the maximum
frequency will decrease when more resources are utilized. As explained earlier 4
of the TFB will be implemented on a single the Stratix 10 board including the
other parts of the PRM. To ensure that the circuit will be running at a clock
frequency of 200 MHz the critical path was shorten to increase the maximum
clock frequency.
The critical path in the arithmetic part had become through a DSP in the outer-
summation, which suggests that the synthesis tool had diﬃculties performing
an eﬃcient ﬂoor-planing.
To reduce the critical path pipeline registers were
therefor placed between the chain of DSP’s in the outer-summation. It raised
the maximum frequency at the expense of increasing the latency by 7 clock
cycles.
Buﬀers
All the buﬀers in the design has been chosen to be implemented as FIFO. All
parts of the χ2-calculator rely on a ﬂow, where the oldest values gets processed
ﬁrst. It is therefor very natural to use FIFO’s, which also has a simple interface.
The FIFO’s are implemented using the IP core "FIFO Intel FPGA IP", which
allows an easy setup of the FIFOs. The FIFOs have 4 output signals: data, full,
empty, almost_full. The almost_full is used for the FIFOs implemented in the
queuing circuit to prioritize the FIFOs that need to be emptied before they run
out of memory. It is disabled for the FIFOs inside the χ2-blocks, because the
ﬂow of data is well known.
The FIFOs are implemented using RAM blocks, and they are small compared
to the available resources on the board.

During development and implementation of the χ2-calculator the input values
(Ai,j, xj and ki) were injected manually to ensure that the circuit functioned
correctly after changes were made. The implementation require 84 data inputs
for the A matrix, 7 data inputs for the k vector and 12 data inputs for the x
vector to calculate the χ2. In total 103 data inputs are required, which makes
it unfeasible to manually deﬁne them. Instead few numbers were chosen and
injected at multiple input ports to reduce the workload of forcing all the con-
stant values to unique values. It also made it easy to validate that the result
was correct as basic calculations could verify it.
When the same input values are used for multiple ports diﬀerent types of errors
may be hidden, such as wrong wiring. To ensure that the implementation works
correctly in all cases of input combinations a test-bench has been developed.
The required count of tests needed to test all possible values would be an im-
possible task. With 103 unique data values within the dynamic range of a 28 bit
ﬂoating point number the count of tests to do an exhaustive test of all possible
input values would be impossible.

6.1.1
Design
The TFB consists of the three components: The TD, the χ2-calculator and the
HPC, which all have been developed during our thesis.
During the development of the circuits it became evident that the TFB will have
==PAGE:90==
72
Integration - common work
to process data at a high data-rate. The components were therefor designed to
be pipelined, which ﬁts well to the high data-rate. The pipelined circuits also
make the integration between the parts more straight forward as each circuit
will be able to receive new data each clock cycle. It reduces the need of control
signals between the components.
The three blocks are integrated as shown in ﬁgure 6.1.
The track distributor consist of 4 parallel blocks to process the tracks at a suf-
ﬁcient rate. The χ2-calculator is also build by 4 parallel circuits that calculates
the χ2 for a track each in parallel. It allows a simple connection between the
two components as the tracks from each TD are connected directly to a corre-
sponding χ2-block. As both components are pipelined no control signals/ready
signals are required from the χ2-blocks to the TD-blocks.
The χ2-calculator calculates the χ2 of all 4 tracks in parallel. The HPC can
handle only a single track at a time hence a queuing circuit was designed as
part of the χ2-calculator.
The queuing circuit puts the track, the IDs related to the track and the χ2
result into a set of FIFOs. Only the tracks that passes the χ2 selection are
saved while the rest are discarded and not processed further. It reduces the
data-rate between the χ2-calculator and the HPC. The parameter calculator
is pipelined hence it can handle one new track each clock cycle. The queuing
circuit is therefor always providing a new track from the FIFOs to the parameter
calculator to be processed.
The HPC can process 1 track pr. clock cycle, but simulation shows that an
average of 3 clock cycles will occur between accepted tracks.
It is therefor
expected that the HPC is able to keep the number of tracks in the FIFO at a
minimum or empty. A valid signal is used to indicate if valid data is available
to the HPC.
Figure 6.1 shows the top level design of the entire TFB.
Both the χ2-calculator and the HPC require constants from memory to calculate
the results. It requires a HBM to retrieve the data, hence the constants are saved
in HBM blocks as explained in the 2.3.1.
The HBM interface will be developed by other people and we have therefor not
used it in our thesis. For our thesis, the constants are saved in a ﬁle, from where
the data can be retrieved.

Chapter 7
Discussion
High portability
The development done in this project is an upgrade of the previous track ﬁt-
ting implementation in the ATLAS detector (called FTK). The detector will be
changed which requires the TDAQ to be redesigned as explained in 2.2.1. To
be able to ﬁt the new design of the TDAQ the TFB is also redesigned, which
consist of the parts responsible for the χ2 and helix parameter calculations. The
FTK relied on a highly optimized FPGA and ASIC design which requires it to
be redesigned.
The arithmetic part of the χ2 calculation has been a part of the ﬁltering for a
long time and was developed for the ATLAS detector by H. Wind in 1982 [33].
It is therefor very likely that the χ2 will be a part of the ﬁltering for a long
time in the future. It is therefor an advantage to write VHDL code, that can
be easily ported to new devices when the TDAQ will be upgraded again. It will
also make it easier to use the design for other use cases if it may be suited.
It has been a priority for me to produce a design and implementation with a
relative high portability. When large IP cores are used in the development it
becomes diﬃcult to port the design between providers hence it would need a lot
of work.
In my design I have tried to reduce the usage of large IP cores, hence only 4 IP
cores has been used: "Native FLOATING POINT DSP", "FIFO", "Float com-
pare", ""Fixed to ﬂoat". The usage of the IP cores does not make it possible
==PAGE:98==
80
Discussion
to directly port the design to another provider, but they are small components,
that can be changed to ﬁt the new provider/device rather easily. The compo-
nents to ﬁt the DSP should be changed anyway to ﬁt the new device and it
implementation of DSP blocks.
However IP cores developed for a speciﬁc device are often highly optimized for
the device hence great performance can be achieved with a reduced design ef-
fort. The Intel Corporation have developed an IP core optimized to do vector
dot products, which has not been used in χ2-calculator. The IP core was not
used for two reasons: the IP core has a minimum latency of 16 clock cycles and
it cannot be ported easily to another device.
The latency between the IP core (16 clock cycles) and the implemented χ2-arith
(12 clock cycles) is 4 clock cycles hence the IP core would increase the latency
of the inner-summation with 33%. It is a signiﬁcantly diﬀerence in percentage,
but the reduction of 4 clock cycles may not be important for the overall design
of the system.
The IP core has an interface that makes it easy to optimize for a deﬁned target
frequency hence giving it a high ﬂexibility to ﬁt a new target frequency if it
changes during the design of the TDAQ. It is optimized for the DSPs on the
Stratix 10, hence it uses the registers more eﬃciently then the design of this
thesis.
Changing constrains
This master thesis has been a part of a large ongoing development project, which
is not to be operational before 2026. The long time frame of the project means
that parts of the system has not been developed yet which causes some constrains
to be unknown while others are changing during development of the project. It
has been exciting to work in an ongoing project, but it has also caused challenges
during the development process. When developing VHDL implementations on
FPGAs the design is optimized to ﬁt the constrains in the best manner. During
development of this project the focus has been on reducing the resource usage
and diﬀerent designs have been developed to do that.
However the designs
are sensitive to changes in the constrains and may become irrelevant when the
constrains change.
I should have investigated the constrains more before designing the arithmetic
part. It caused me to spend time on designs that were not relevant for the χ2-
calculator after all. For instance I spent time on developing and implementing
the reduced resources design, which is not suited for this application due to the
long latency. Instead I could have spend my time better to investigate high
performance designs. However the diﬀerent designs may be relevant for other
parts of the circuit or for other applications where vector dot product is used.

Reducing memory accesses from TFB
During the top-level design phase of this project it was considered beneﬁcial to
reduce the memory accesses as both the χ2 and the helix parameter calculations
relay on a high count of constants retrieved from memory. When all 4 FTBs, that
are implemented on the Stratix 10, are under high load, the memory bandwidth
may become an issue, which was shown in 4.2. Therefor the memory access
from the TFB was reduced by only calculating the helix parameters for the
tracks that have been accepted by the χ2. It reduces the memory bandwidth
needed for the HPC as it will be suﬃcient to calculate the parameters for a
single track pr. clock cycle. It comes at the cost of an increased latency that
can be tolerated.
The design requires that the χ2-calculator outputs a single track pr. clock and
that it also supplies the track information (clusters and the IDs). It has been
implemented using FIFOs as explained in section 5.5.2. When FIFOs and other
types of buﬀers are used in an implementation it is possible that a burst of data
causes the FIFOs to be ﬁlled and drop data. To minimize the risk, the buﬀers
are made large enough to handle the bursts of data in the expected cases.
The HPC is also designed to process data much faster then the expected rate
of accepted tracks. It is therefor very unlikely that the FIFOs will be ﬁlled, but
it cannot be guaranteed to never happen if unexpected large bursts occur. A
more reliable circuit can be obtained if the buﬀers are avoided.
The buﬀers used in the design of the χ2-calculator can be removed if the queu-
ing circuit is not needed. It can be removed if the HPC can retrieve enough
constants to calculate 4 tracks in parallel, however during the top-level design
it was not deemed feasible with the current memory bandwidth and number
format. It is not possible to increase the memory bandwidth, but instead it
may be possible to reduce the bit-width of the constants used in the χ2. It will
reduce the memory bandwidth required by the χ2-calculator which can be used
==PAGE:100==
82
Discussion
by the HPC instead.
In the current implementation, the results of the χ2 is used to classify the tracks
as either relevant or irrelevant, and it is not used for further calculations. There-
for high precision may not be necessary, at least not when the χ2 is far away
from the threshold.
In machine learning algorithms and deep neural networks data is also being
classiﬁed based on matrix multiplications hence the operations are similar to
the χ2.
Recent researches have shown that machine learning algorithms can be run
using a reduced bit-width without compromising the validity of the classiﬁca-
tions. A new ﬂoating-point, Bﬂoat16, has been developed by Google for high-
performance processing of Neural Networks [37][38]. It has the same bit-width
for the exponent, but it uses only 8 bits to represent the mantissa compared to
the IEEE single precision ﬂoating points. Using the Bﬂoat16 it has been shown
that the matrix-multiplications done in deep neural networks can be accelerated
allowing the network to be trained much more eﬃcient then it can be done using
the single precision ﬂoating point format.
It suggests that the bit-width of the constants may be reduced for the χ2 algo-
rithm. It will allow more memory bandwidth for the HPC hence reducing the
probability of data loss due to buﬀers even further. Though it will require a
careful analysis of the eﬀects for the χ2 before it can be implemented, which has
not been done during this thesis. If the memory bandwidth shows to be insuﬃ-
cient for the entire PRM implementation to function correctly it may be relevant
to study the possibilities of reducing the bit-width of the constant values.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis has been a part of the design and implementation of the TFB for
the new HTT, which are a key component in the TDAQ system that operates
on the data from the ATLAS detector at CERN.
The TFB will be implemented on the Intel Stratix 10 state-of-the-art FPGA.
Before the TFB was designed an investigation of the capabilities and expected
power consumption of the Stratix 10 was carried out. It showed that the per-
formance of the implementation may be reduced signiﬁcantly when many DSPs
are utilized. It is therefor important to be aware of it, when designing the cir-
cuit. It also showed that the DSP contributes to a high power consumption.
The results were expected but the actual power consumption and performance
of the DSPs were unknown.
It may also be valuable knowledge to be used by the ATLAS collaboration in
the further design process.
The top level of the TFB was designed to reduce the memory access to a low
level while keeping the latency at an acceptable level. The design reduces the
bandwidth required in the worst case scenario with 31% and the resource usage
for the helix parameter with 75% compared to the parallel top level design.
However it increases the latency of the implementation with approximately 23
clock cycles.

Conclusion
A key focus of the thesis has been to design and implement a circuit that cal-
culates the χ2 of a track. Diﬀerent designs have been developed for the χ2 that
prioritize resource usage, throughput and latency diﬀerently. The χ2 is based on
vector dot products and the designs may be used by other that has to implement
similar solutions.
The cascaded fully pipelined design was chosen for implementation as the through-
put was high, latency low and the DSPs were utilized well. It requires many
registers, but it was argued to be of little concern.
A full χ2-calculator was implemented that calculates the χ2 of four tracks in
parallel. The χ2-calculator was able to meet the target frequency of 200 MHz
with an accepted latency, resource and power usage.
The χ2-calculator was tested using random test vectors and was proven to be
working correct.
The χ2-calculator was integrated with the rest of the TFB with the usage of
buﬀers. The buﬀers have been scaled to ﬁt the expected data rates.
The ﬁnal product is a fully integrated TFB developed in collaboration with
Magnus Høegh Jensen.
The implementation of the TFB fulﬁll the requirements and is ready to be used
in the PRM, however pipeline registers may be needed to put in place if the
clock frequency drops further at full utilization of the Stratix 10
