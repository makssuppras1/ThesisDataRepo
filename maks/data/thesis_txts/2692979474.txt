Abstract
The Pearl-chain reinforcement (PCR) technology was invented at the Technical Uni-
versity of Denmark along with super-light (SL) elements by professor emeritus Kris-
tian Hertz and has been used for constructing arched bridges. The technology has
been recognized as an innovative and sustainable alternative state-of-the-art solution
to the traditional construction of concrete bridges used for traﬃc. The beneﬁts of
the technology have only been studied through projects on a large scale, where the
magnitude of loads and spans is large compared to domestic housing projects. The
following master thesis investigates the potential of applying PCR on tract housing
to develop a reproducible design as a competing alternative regarding environmental
impact.
The structural arch is described by type and shape and a calculation method is pro-
vided for the determination of internal forces.
The arch is represented statically
through common support conditions, where the strengths and weaknesses are high-
lighted for each. The static behavior of the arch is examined through an analytical
investigation of diﬀerent load arrangements, which has resulted in the identiﬁcation
of critical load scenarios. Furthermore, the relation of bending moments, axial forces,
and reactions are described as dependent on the rise-to-span ratio of the arch. The
load on arched domestic structures has been expressed in accordance with the Struc-
tural Eurocode Program for self-weight, imposed load, snow, wind, and active earth
pressure. With the obtained knowledge of the structural arch, a case study is inves-
tigated to establish an optimal shape of the tract house concept based on bending
moments from applying permanent loads alone. The optimal shape has been deﬁned
by minimizing the bending moments and optimizing the usable ﬂoor area with a cho-
sen free room height. With the optimal shape and applying PCR a design of both a
continuous closed arch and an open arch with recesses has been developed. The de-
sign is veriﬁed in the ultimate limit state for the identiﬁed critical load combinations
during usage and lifting. Furthermore, additional structural parts of high importance
are presented and explained why they must be considered in further veriﬁcation of
the ﬁnal structure.
The environmental impact of the developed designs is quantiﬁed through a life cycle
assessment (LCA) and compared with an extensive LCA study performed by the
State Building Institute (SBi). The LCA is based on the simpliﬁed LCA traditionally
used in Denmark, which includes processes from the production stage (A), usage stage
(B), and end-of-life stage (C).
The thesis concludes that applying PCR on domestic tract housing serves as a compet-
ing alternative to other single-family house designs when compared by environmental
impact. The global warming potential of the tract house design was determined at
3.50 kg CO2 eq. /m2/yr, which was below the average of the compared single-family
houses by 6%. The optimal arch shape has been established as circular with a rise of

1.
Introduction
1.1
Background
The concrete industry today is highly challenged [1], since the production of cement
alone accounts for approximately 5% of the global CO2 emissions [2]. Despite a large
amount of CO2 emissions, reinforced concrete structures have been widely used in
large parts of the world to design load-bearing structures, due to the versatility of
casting diﬀerent shapes.
The pearl chain reinforcement (PCR) technology is an invention of Professor Emeri-
tus Kristian Hertz [3], at the Technical University of Denmark and is an opportunity
to make reinforced concrete competitive in terms of constructability and environ-
mental impact [4].
The idea of utilizing PCR is to reverse the design process of
structures by shaping the structure and placing the pearl-chain to dictate the load
path. Together with Hertz’s other invention, super-light concrete structures [5], min-
imalistic structures can be designed to reduce material consumption, CO2 emissions,
and transportation costs [6]. PCR is seen previously applied in the test and design of
arched bridges with a span of 13m [7], where the magnitude of load and span requires
minimalistic solutions to lower the construction cost. The beneﬁts of applying PCR
on domestic housing have not previously been investigated and is the motivation of
this thesis. The PCR technology is applied along with super-light concrete structures
on a relevant case regarding a residential area of arched tract houses, where the con-
cept is to integrate the houses with nature and lowering the environmental impact as
much as possible, while at the same time designing an architectural appealing house
to challenge the tract housing norm.
1.2
Scope
This thesis seeks to investigate the possibilities to apply PCR in domestic tract hous-
ing through an optimized arch design approach. PCR has previously been applied
in the design and full-scale testing of bridges but remains to be utilized in structures
for domestic housing with an anticipated larger rise-to-span ratio. The investigation
is based on the ﬂexural strength, as the design limiting parameter, with the resulting
environmental impact of the design for comparison.
The overall hypothesis of the thesis related to the investigation of the case-study is:
• It is possible to apply pearl-chain reinforcement as a competing al-
ternative to the load-bearing elements in tract housing
The following objectives were identiﬁed based on the hypothesis:
• Investigate common structural arch shapes and types and determine a method
for calculation of reactions and internal forces.
1
==PAGE:17==
• Perform a static analysis of a simpliﬁed analytical model to identify strength
and weaknesses of arches through diﬀerent load scenarios.
• Determine critical loads and combinations related to arched tract houses.
• Propose an optimal arch shape based on permanent load and optimal utilization
of ﬂoor area.
• Design a practical PCR arch of straight elements and verify a design close to
failure.
• Investigate the advantage of PCR arched tract houses compared with common
single-family house designs in terms of global warming potential.
The following topics are considered outside the scope of the thesis:
• Serviceability calculations: Deﬂection and crack width control
• Instability/buckling and shear
Note: The investigation of shear capacity is deemed outside the scope of this
thesis as previous studies of PCR arches indicate the bending moment as the
critical internal force [7].
• Design of supporting structures
Note: The horizontal reactions are assumed supported by a concrete ground
slab, which is cast nonetheless in domestic housing.
• Detailed design of abutment elements
• Connections between PCR arches
• Dynamic analysis
• Detailed price calculations
Furthermore, the eﬀects of temperature changes are not investigated as the struc-
ture is placed on the inside of the building envelope, where insigniﬁcant change in
temperature is assumed.

2.1
History and Application
For a vast majority of engineers, the arched structures are considered intricate and
cumbersome to both design and construct, when compared to modern structural
beam-column and frame systems. From the early 1900s until today, straight lines
and simplicity in modernist (1900) and postmodernist (1972) architecture has been
the trending expression [8], which has largely aﬀected the ﬁeld of Civil engineering
in terms of designing economic, time-saving, steady, robust and sustainable struc-
tures. However, curvy architecture seems to be a rising trend in the 21st century
for consultants and entrepreneurs to express skill, and for building owners to express
wealth.
5
==PAGE:21==
Fig. 2. Single-family house in Gilleje, Denmark by BIG Architects [9]
The arched structure dates back to 3800 BC as an underground true keystone-arch
was revealed during excavations of the North temple and Sounding, in the ancient
Sumerian city Nippur [10]. The arch was discovered underground, to cover a water
drain running underneath the Temple of Bel. The Arch was laid in clay mortar and
stone bricks with a small height of 71cm a width of 33cm resulting in an elliptical
shape.
Fig. 3. True keystone-arch before 3800 BC in Nippur[10]
Arches were solely constructed underground for drains and other small tunnel-like
structures [11], as the horizontal thrust was obtained by vertical pressure of the above
and surrounding soil. It was not until the ancient Romans, the true arch was utilized
for structures above ground [12]. The Romans used the shape to build structures
such as aqueducts, domes, and vaults with large spans using materials only able to
carry loads in compression. The application of structural arches expanded widely and
6
==PAGE:22==
was used anywhere a structure had to span across doors, windows, bridges, cupolas,
rooﬁng, etc. The shape made it possible to build without the need for wood, which
unlike stone and bricks can be bend and tensioned.
Fig. 4. Roman aqueduct at Pont du Gard, France [13]
The arch solved the problem of possible bending in structures with the only limitation
being enough space. Today engineers design vertical load-carrying structures with
straight elements of reinforced concrete able to carry compression and tension to
withstand bending. Reinforcement in structures was invented by Joseph Monier in
1877, making ﬂowerpots and small garden bridges [14] and was later commercialized
by Gustav Wayss in 1884 [15]. With the rise of reinforced concrete and later the
industry of prefabricated concrete, the arch shape was considered outdated in terms
of production, cost, and construction as the price of manual labor was increased
and the material cost were reduced. However, with the invention of PCR [16] the
structural arch can be re-invented and presented as an optimized reinforced concrete
structure, which provides economical, sustainable, and constructional advantages [17].

parabolic, and catenary, which all have a geometrical representation in mathematics.
This thesis deals with simple arch shapes. The shape of the arch greatly inﬂuences the
distribution of internal forces as well as global reactions, which is discussed further
in section 2.5.

The complexity of calculating the behavior of the three arch types is very diﬀerent.
The two-hinged and ﬁxed arch are statically indeterminate, with indeterminacy n
of n = 1 and n = 3 respectively, and the three-hinged is determinate. The obvious
choice for designing an arch is the three-hinged arch, as the determination of reactions
is simple. The three-hinged arch is the least rigid of the types seen in ﬁg. 8, which can
be problematic in regards to settlements due to poor soil conditions in combination
with a low rise. Three hinged arches are widely constructed in steel and wood, where
hinged connections are simple to assemble. However, this is not the case for concrete
arches, even though engineers have approached concrete arches this way to simplify
calculations. The establishment of a hinge at the crown for concrete arches is rarely
seen, however, solutions exist as seen in [19].
The ﬁxed arch is the most cumbersome to design, as the degree of indeterminacy
results in several kinematic conditions to be fulﬁlled to determine the reactions [20].
Unlike the three-hinged arch, the added restraints result in a more rigid structure
overall, which in some cases is a favorable feature, e.g. when dynamically loaded.
Fixing the arch-ends to interact with the supporting substructure to transfer bending
moments can reduce the extreme values of the moment in the arch itself. At the same
time, the ﬁxed arch-ends introduce large stresses in the case of support settlements.
For a concrete substructure, a suﬃcient amount of reinforcement and dimensions
are required to obtain the bending moments. Even though a suﬃcient amount of
reinforcement and concrete are constructed at the springing, the design of obtaining
a fully ﬁxed condition is problematic due to soil conditions at the site. In the case of
exceeding the moment capacity, the static system of the arch is possibly changed to
that of a two-hinged. The two-hinged arch is the equivalent intermediate situation
of a ﬁxed arch and a three-hinged arch. It provides some stiﬀness compared to the
12
==PAGE:28==
three-hinged arch and some ﬂexibility compared to the ﬁxed arch. A two-hinged arch
can be constructed in a continuous matter such as concrete or other brick material
without the need for complicated solutions of constructing an internal hinge. The
hinges at the supports are simply obtained by placing the arch on the sub-structure
without ﬁxing it to the base with e.g reinforcement, bolts, anchors, etc.
Based on the previously established advantages and disadvantages, the two-hinged
arch is a natural choice for this speciﬁc project, where concrete elements with PCR
are assembled and lifted on-site as a scaleable and reproducible solution.

Internal Forces

5.5
Pearl-chain reinforcement
In Skovbuerne Rødding the main reinforcement in the arches is the PCR technology,
which is brieﬂy described along with the SL-elements.
5.5.1
Principle of Pearl-chain reinforcement
Utilizing post-tensioning in concrete structure, where a tensile force in a taut spring
is applied as compression to the tensioned structure after the concrete is cast, has
been widely used for more than a century [31]. In 2009 professor Kristian Hertz at
the Technical University of Denmark patented the invention of PCR [3]. The idea
of PCR is to reinforce segments of core elements of strong material in any shape, by
applying a compressive zone through the chain of elements.
Tension
pearl-chain
Compression zone
Core element
segment
Fig. 38. PCR principle
An example of an application of PCR is an arched concrete structure seen on ﬁg. 38,
where a total of 7 precast elements are held together by a post-tension cable drawn
through a curved duct in each element. The resulting compression in each element
makes the structure able to resist tensile forces as unloaded compression.
5.5.2
Beneﬁts
With the invention of PCR, constructing optimal structures in terms of statics by
designing arches or other curved structures is made easier and less time consuming.
This is due to the assembling of pearl-chains by pre-stressing, which makes it possible
to erect the structure as one whole body [4]. This beneﬁt means no scaﬀolding is
required, which otherwise would be resource- and time consuming. Together with
super-light (SL) structures, PCR can be applied to potentially lower the environmen-
tal impact in a cradle to cradle perspective [32].
5.6
Super-light Structures
5.6.1
Principle of Super-light Structures
The structure used to create the back arch of the tract house in Rødding is the
Super-light (SL) deck elements invented by professor Kristian Dahl Hertz [33] at
53
==PAGE:69==
the Technical University of Denmark. SL is referring to a structure which is lighter
compared to a structure with plain concrete [34]. The SL deck element consist of
three parts.
1. Strong concrete
2. Light aggregate concrete
3. Reinforcement steel
Fig. 39. SL deck element [35]
The strong concrete has a compressive strength of 55MPa and a density of 2400kg/m3.
The light aggregate concrete (LAC) has a lower compressive strength of only 3MPa
and a density of 700kg/m3. The reinforcement consists of 12.5mm prestressing 7-wire
strands with a cross-sectional area of 93mm2.
The principle of SL structures is to utilize the material properties more eﬃciently.
This is achieved by placing high-strength concrete in the zones, where the high
strength is beneﬁcial to sustain compressive forces, and encapsulating it with LAC
[16] [36]. The LAC ensures a transfer of the load to the compression and tensile zones
and also provides stabilisation of the strong concrete in compression [6]. To obtain
tensile forces steel reinforcement is placed along with PCR. The result is an optimized
minimal primary load-carrying structure, which reduces the overall self-weight of the
structure [5]. Hence, the purpose of the SL principle is to design structures that
govern the load path, rather than designing a traditional structure and subsequently
assessing the load distribution.
54
==PAGE:70==
Fig. 40. Principle of simply supported SL beam
The advantages of utilizing the SL principle compared to traditional concrete are
several, however the most prevailing are:
• Reduced self-weight (constructability) [37]
• Better ﬁre resistance of 4 hours, which is suﬃcient for a R30 requirement [38]
• Reduced sound reﬂection [39], which is highly relevant for arched structures,
where sounds are reﬂected similar to a satellite dish
• Lower environmental impact [40]
The enhanced ﬁre resistance is due to the insulating eﬀect of LAC [14]. The sound
insulation is due to the weight diﬀerence of light and heavy concrete.
The SL elements come in a large variety depending on speciﬁc requirements by the
builder. For instance a possibility of casting ducts for e.g ventilation is possible simply
by removing some of the LAC blocks, where the duct is drawn.

The shape of the arched tract house is discretized using straight elements. Choosing
the optimal length of the elements is based on approaching the curved shape, reducing
the amount of strong concrete and ﬁtting as many LAC blocks in the elements as
possible. The result is an element, which holds two blocks of LAC in the length
of the element, as this increases the overall number of possible LAC elements in
the structure. In the ends of each deck element, at least 200mm of massive regular
concrete is cast. This ensures an even pressure between the elements and the neutral
axis is shifted to the middle of the cross-section [7]. By choosing two LAC blocks
per element the overall self-weight is reduced since the number of massive concrete
sections in the ends is reduced.
The resulting total length of the deck elements is 1666mm, which divides the arch

increased to secure a suﬃcient cover layer for the post-tensioning duct at the middle
of each element. The deck element consists of two LAC blocks in the length and six

Lifting
The method for constructing and erecting the arches is described in [17]. The pre-
assembly work starts with carefully placing the segments of the arch on its sides in the
desired shape on a straight surface. Afterwards, formwork is constructed and joints
are cast. Post-tensioning is performed after suﬃcient curing time of the mortar joints
as described in [41]. Lastly, grouting of cable duct is performed after the desired arch
geometry is checked due to post-tensioning settlements. The arch is then ready for
lifting.
The lifting of the arch is done using chains attached to one of the edges of the arch.
Carefully lifting the arch in one side rotates the arch, where-upon the rest of the
chains are attached to the lifting points in the other side of the arch.

Alternative Design
In section 7.6 a concrete element with LAC blocks with a total height of 180mm was
found able to resist the external load. An alternative design of the chosen PCR slab-
arch layout in ﬁg. 46 is to reduce the amount of total required volume of concrete by
using pre-tensioned slabs of regular concrete only. A slab cast entirely with regular
concrete with suﬃcient capacity results in a 150mm slab height, pre-tensioned with
4xL12.5 in the bottom and top and post-tensioned with 13C15. However, [42] and
75
==PAGE:91==
[45] states that brittle failure of pre-stressed concrete elements must be prevented by
applying Method A in Denmark, which refers to a requirement of minimum reinforce-
ment. This means that the maximum spacing of primary reinforcement must be less
than or equal to 3 times the slab height or 400mm resulting in the double number
of pre-tensioned wires of 8xL12.5 in the top and bottom. Furthermore, in order for
the curved post-tensioning duct to have a suﬃcient cover layer in the middle of each
slab element, the height of the slab can be no less than 180mm. The resulting al-
ternative design of casting slabs entirely with regular concrete along with resistances
can be seen in appendix D, ﬁg. 82 and table 23. This design is more heavy and equal
in consumption of concrete in volume, which conﬁrms the SL elements to be more
beneﬁcial for the design of the massive arch in the case study.
The theoretical optimal slab design is a cross section with double positive moment
resistance compared to negative moment resistance as the relation of external loading
is Mpos = 0.5Mneg. Obtaining a theoretical optimal design with these properties is
diﬃcult to achieve based on geometrical challenges such as enough spacing for the
post-tensioning duct, adequate joint dimensions to avoid crushing for small cross-
sectional area and suﬃcient moment capacity for small heights all at the same time
to avoid brittle failure by placing required minimum tensile reinforcement.

the principle of SL elements is not utilized, as the cross section is cast entirely with
strong concrete in order for the structure to resist the external load and applied post-
tensioning. The bending stiﬀness of the PCR beams is substantially smaller compared
to the slab solutions, which causes the constructed arch to be more ﬂexible, which
results in larger deformations. The six individual supports also make this solution
more vulnerable to internal forces caused by relative settlements of the supports. The
consumption of concrete is dependent on the number of PCR beams. For the resulting
design, the optimal number of PCR beams is three, based on the required additional
concrete of each beam, if one beam is removed or if an additional beam is placed.
Furthermore, this design is more vulnerable to instability/buckling problems caused
by three individual cross-sections with signiﬁcantly lower ﬂexural stiﬀness.

Veriﬁcation of new and innovative tract housing applying PCR and arched SL-structures
are cumbersome. Therefore further static considerations have to be thought through.
As for most buildings, tract housing consist of several structural parts which have
their structural function. In this thesis, the structural arch serves both as roof and
as load bearing walls and as previously described in detail regarding loading, veriﬁca-
tion, and possible assembly. This thesis does not cover the whole structure from idea
to construction, because the focus is to determine the main geometry and design to
quantify environmental impact and economics. However, in this section, structural
parts of high importance are presented and explained why they must be considered
in further veriﬁcation of the ﬁnal structure.
To achieve the static properties of a two-hinged arch the thrust at the level of the
foundation has to be considered. There are diﬀerent approaches to achieve satis-
factory support conditions at the foundation. Based on the geographical placement
of Skovbuerne Rødding and the anticipated soil properties, three diﬀerent solutions
seem suitable:
1. Screw piles with inclination
2. Tension ties within the slab foundation
3. Post-tensioning of slab foundation
The screwed piles are an option to create equilibrium for the vertical and horizontal
forces. However, to achieve enough resistance for the horizontal reactions, further
103
==PAGE:119==
tests of the soil are necessary, as the arch is sensitive to soil settlements. To achieve
tolerable settlements may be problematic as the soil has to be compressed enough in
advance, to achieve suﬃcient stiﬀness for the relatively high thrust force. Therefore
it may be beneﬁcial to use tension ties within the concrete foundation slab, due to
the stiﬀness of reinforced concrete which will lead to small settlements compared to
soil see ﬁg. 75. Applying post tensioning may also be a favorable approach since the
thrust force will apply tension to the already compressed concrete, which will result
in an unloading of compression instead.
Fig. 75. Sketch of orthogonal saddle, abutment and ﬂoor slab connection
To carry the vertical forces screw piles will likely be chosen as the foundation system
due to the sustainable soil layer being located relatively deep. Initial investigations
of the soil with the chosen system by the stakeholders indicate suﬃcient soil capacity.
The veriﬁcation of the foundation is left as future work for the stakeholders with the
thrust and vertical reaction of 83.75kN and 101.61kN respectively.
11.1
Loss of Stability
The structural arch seeks to carry the external load in compression as described in
section 2.5. The compression of the PCR arches is a result of both external loads and
post-tensioning, which in theory could cause a loss of stability resulting in a collapsing
104
==PAGE:120==
mechanism. Instability is when the structure is disturbed from its initial equilibrium
[18], and is well described by the elastic theory and Euler’s formula for critical load
following a sinusoidal buckling curve [57]. According to [18] for shallow two hinged
uniform circular arches of rise-to-span ratios less than 0.2, the critical axial force is
approximately equal to the force determined by Euler’s critical load formula with
a buckling length of half the arch length. However, as reinforced concrete cracks
in order to activate the reinforcement and creeps during long-term compression, the
veriﬁcation of the PCR arches remains as future work since more detailed calculations
and modeling are necessary.
11.2
Abutment Elements
To utilize the PCR in order to dimension the arches, heavily reinforced abutment
elements must be designed carefully. The post-tensioning force is transferred to the
SL-elements through abutment elements, where reinforcement conﬁnes the concrete to
avoid bursting, splitting and crushing near the post-tensioning anchor. To design an
abutment element requires detailed modelling and static analysis and relies largely on
experience through testing, when declaring the required reinforcement. Furthermore,
the exact geometry of the abutment elements must be established by also considering
the required transfer length, which is the required longitudinal length of the element,
in which the post-tensioning force is distributed from the anchor to the whole cross
section evenly.
11.3
Longitudinal Connection of Arches
The houses of Skovbuerne Rødding are constructed by placing two structural arches,
connected in the longitudinal direction to ensure uniform bending behavior. This
connection must have a suﬃcient shear capacity, which can be designed as a rough
surface complimented with shear locks formed as hammerhead joints as shown in [7].
The veriﬁcation and design of the longitudinal connection of the arches are left for
future detailed veriﬁcation.

Conclusion
Through a case study, the PCR technology has proved to be a competing alternative
to the load-bearing elements in domestic tract housing, verifying the hypothesis of
the thesis.
During the master thesis studies, the arch as a structural shape has
been thoroughly investigated and applied together with PCR technology to minimize
material consumption of the load-bearing components in the building envelope.
General knowledge about arches was described to give an overview of the possible
approach to the usage of PCR applied to domestic tract housing. Given the soil
conditions on-site, the two-hinged arch solution was chosen as soil conditions may
vary on-site, which favors the required stiﬀness, ﬂexibility, and possible installation,
the two-hinged arch provides.
Based on the chosen static system weak points of the arched structure have been
identiﬁed as a ﬁrst step in developing an optimized design of the arch for the load
combinations present for domestic housing. It was concluded that loads applied along
the whole length of the arch introduce the largest thrust forces and insigniﬁcant
bending moments. However, it was also concluded that this behavior can be decreased
by increasing the rise-to-span ratio, which will lower the thrust force and maintain
the bending moments. Loads applied asymmetrically revealed the critical scenarios
in terms of bending moments for vertical loads at the speciﬁc positions: L/4 for point
loads and along half the span for uniformly distributed loads. For a horizontal point
load, the critical position was determined at h/2 for bending moments and at h for
the thrust force.
The possible loads for arched domestic housing were deﬁned and described in accor-
dance with the present Structural Eurocode Program. The total number of combina-
tions was determined with the critical situations as:
• Drifted Snow load dominating with live load applied along entire span and wind
resulted in the largest thrust, normal force and vertical reaction.
• Live load dominating applied along half the span with snow load drifted and
wind resulted in the largest negative bending moment
• Snow load drifted (additional arrangement) dominating with live load along half
the span and wind resulted in the largest positive bending moment
The dead load and earth pressure are included in all the above-mentioned load combinations.
The purpose, functional requirements, and geometry of the case study were presented
to highlight the inﬂuence of the speciﬁcations: Load, arch span, and rise, amount of
post-tensioning, geometry, and pre-stressing of elements, the number of elements and
geometry of abutment elements.
The optimal arch shape based on the case study was determined as circular with a
span of L = 21.25m and a rise of h = 4m. It was concluded that both the parabolic
106
==PAGE:122==
and circular arch introduced insigniﬁcant bending moments when only permanent
loads were applied. The circular shape was chosen due to scale-ability and the possi-
bility of mass production resulting in less construction cost. By expressing the usable
ﬂoor area, based on a free room height requirement of 2.1m, and comparing with the
bending moments, the optimal rise for the circular arch was found.
The actions on the arched tract house were calculated using an FE-model, where
the internal forces were determined based on the previously determined critical load
combinations. The related post-tensioning losses were determined to investigate the
eﬀects of short-term and long-term conditions on capacity veriﬁcation. Through an
iterative design process, the optimal cross-section was deﬁned for both arches in the
case study, which resulted in an SL slab with a height of 180mm. The elements were
veriﬁed in the ultimate limit state for bending moments as a result of both pre- and
post-tensioning and external loads. The utilization of the design indicates the joints
as the general weak points of the arch, even though a high strength mortar of 80 MPa
was used. The theoretical optimal design of the elements is not achievable as other
parameters such as the joint height and minimum reinforcement requirement in the
slabs is a limitation.
A suggestion for design reinforcement for the slabs with recesses in the open (front
arch) was provided based on a 3D FE-model, where expected behavior as a result of
stress concentrations was described and veriﬁed.
The assembly and lifting process from previous studies [17] was investigated in the
ultimate limit state in terms of bending moments. The applied dynamic factor of
2.0 provided by the Eurocode Program for a reference setup of a forklift vehicle was
questioned through a simpliﬁed dynamic analysis. The study was inconclusive since
further detailed modeling of the complete setup was required and is left for possible
future work.
The optimal lifting points were chosen as the position, which resulted in approxi-
mately equal global negative and positive bending moments concerning the joints.
The position of the lifting points was found to be at the middle of the abutment
elements.
The functional unit of the arched tract house was deﬁned to specify the environmental
impact of the designed arched tract house calculated through a simpliﬁed LCA to be
compared with 10 single-family houses. The comparison indicated the application of
PCR as a competing alternative for tract housing as the total GWP was below average
by 6%. The anticipated reduction of CO2 emissions by 20-50% [6], when applying
PCR with SL technology, could not be conﬁrmed as previously seen for projects on a
larger scale [58].

Future Work
Since this thesis does not cover all the relevant topics for constructing a PCR arched
tract house the following topics are left for possible future work:
• Design of supporting structures to carry thrust and vertical load.
• Determination of a calculation method for instability veriﬁcation of PCR arch.
• Detailed analysis and design of abutment elements in terms of failures related
to post-tensioning.
• Design and veriﬁcation of longitudinal connections between arches to transfer
shear speciﬁcally for PCR arched tract houses.
• Detailed dynamic analysis during lifting of PCR arch to possibly lower the
suggested ampliﬁcation factor found in the Eurocode Program.

E.
3D FE-model of Open Arch: Stress Maps
In the following the results obtained from a 3D FE-model of the open arch are shown.
The arch is modelled with regular concrete slabs with a Young’s modulus of E =
38GPa and 13 elements in total (2 abutment elements and 11 intermediate elements).
Fig. 87. FE-model of open arch in 3D
The mesh consists of elements with a size of 0.1m 3-node triangles and 4-node squares
with a total of 9 and 12 degrees of freedom respectively. The triangles are used around
the holes and along edges and the squares in the remaining slab elements.
The SL slabs and abutment elements are not modelled with a diﬀerent stiﬀness since
the purpose of the model is merely to show the local and global stress distribution in
tensile and compressive format to capture the static behavior of the open arch. This is
done to show that more careful and precise calculations are required for designing the
windowed elements in order to obtain the stress concentrations around the perimeter
of the holes.
The following stress maps relate to the local coordinates of the elements in the arch
as shown in ﬁg. 88.
