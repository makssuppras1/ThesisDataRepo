INTRODUCTION 
1.1. BACKGROUND 
Maintenance of good water quality is one of the necessary daily monitoring task in fish farming. 
Continuous replacement of used with new water is the most widely used method for water quality 
monitoring in extensive aquaculture such as pond and flow through production systems (O. I. 
Lekang, 2013). Rapid growth of aquaculture industry has increased water demand to satisfy 
expanded fish farming in order to meet market demand gap left as the result of decline of capture 
fisheries while the world population increases gradually (FAO, 2014). Recirculating aquaculture 
system (RAS) has been a solution towards water economization in fish farming allowing water 
“reuse” within the system, hence sustaining high stocking density that leads to increase in 
aquaculture productivity (Metaxa et al., 2006). Impurities in RAS water mainly comes from 
remaining of uneaten feeds, excretory and respiratory products as a result of increased stocking 
density, feeding, high degree of water recirculation and reduction in water replacement (Pedersen et 
al., 2012; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010).  
In order to restore water quality for reuse, RAS comprise of a series of water treatment units which 
are essential for physical, chemical, biological and microbiological water quality monitoring. Water 
treatment components in RAS includes; drum filter, biofilter, UV and Ozone for suspended solid, 
dissolved organic and inorganic and microbes removal respectively (O. Lekang, 2013; Morey, 
2009; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010; van Rijn, 2013). Biofilter is reported as one the most efficient 
technic for nitrogenous waste such removal such as ammonia and nitrite in RAS through 
nitrification process (Avnimelech, 2006; Crab et al., 2007; Guerdat et al., 2010; Gutierrez-Wing and

ABSTRACT 
Temperature is considered on the main factors affecting biofilter nitrification in recirculating 
aquaculture systems (RAS). Previous studies confirm the positive impact of temperature on 
ammonia removal while others reports nil/or negligible effects. The effects of temperature on the 
second step of the nitrification process, the nitrite removal is often overlooked in the current 
literatures. In this study, batch reactors were used to examine the acute effect of six temperatures (6, 
12, 18, 24, 30 and 36 C) on moving bed biofilter (MBB) nitrification using water from a steady-
state, experimental RAS  (18 - 20 °C). The average surface-specific total ammonia nitrogen 
removal (STR) and nitrite nitrogen removal (SNR) increased with temperature between 6 – 30 °C 
from 0.033 to 0.137; g NH4-N m-2 day-1 and 0.033 to 0.122; g NO2-N m-2 day-1 respectively. 
Nitrogen removal rates were decreased at 36 °C. Long-term experiments testing the effect of 
temperature at 6, 18 and 30 °C showed a reduction in STR and SNR over time and reduced removal 
rates compared to the acute experiments. Under limited substrate concentration, 1’ order 
nitrification had an increase in    for both ammonia and nitrite removal reaching maximum value 
of 0.664 and 0.229 m day-1 at 30 °C; equivalent to a 114.9% and 95.3% increase compared to the 
source temperature of approximately 18 °C. 
Key words: Temperature, Biofilter, Nitrification, Recirculating aquaculture system.

2.1. INTRODUCTION 
Biofilter in recirculating aquaculture system is the main component responsible for nitrogenous 
waste removal through nitrification process (Crab et al., 2007). Nitrification is a two-step microbial 
processes which involves oxidation of ammonia to nitrite and nitrite to nitrate (Ebeling et al., 2006; 
Lekang, 2013) . The process depends on biofilm formation which influence/facilitate growth of 
bacteria; Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter responsible for ammonia and nitrite oxidation respectively 
(Chen et al., 2006; Malone and Pfeiffer, 2006). The nitrification process is considered the main 
component for management of dissolved organic wastes below critical levels and allows “water 
reuse” in recirculating aquaculture system besides being an environmental sustainable production 
(Colt, 2006; Gutierrez-Wing and Malone, 2006; Martins et al., 2010).  
There are two main kind of biofilter that are widely used in recirculating aquaculture systems; fixed 
bed biofilter (FBB) and moving bed biofilter (MBB). The FBB consisting of submerged biofilter 
are designed in such a way that bioelements settles at the bottom of the biofilter (Eding et al., 2006), 
while in MBB media are kept in motion by aeration (Guerdat et al., 2010). Other types of biofilters 
include sand biofilter as alternative means of nitrifying bacteria colonization for ammonia and 
nitrite removal (Summerfelt, 2006) and trickling filters (Eding et al., 2006). For marine 
recirculating aquaculture system, seaweeds are also good candidates for ammonia removal  by 
converting them into biomass as nutrient sources for their growth unlike degradation in 
convectional biofilter nitrification (Cahill et al., 2010). Upon integration with other technologies 
such as ion-exchange or membrane filtration as hybrid filter system, biofilter shows elevated 
performance on ammonia removal though cost becomes the major limitation for large scale 
application (Miladinovic and Weatherley, 2008). 
Nitrification in a moving bed biofilter depends not only on complex interaction of chemical and 
biological condition of biofilm but also physical characteristics that involves motion and mixing of

the media. Like any other microbial processes, moving bed biofilter nitrification requires sufficient 
substrate; ammonia and nitrite concentration as a source of food (Pedersen et al., 2012; Vadivelu et

alkalinity for proper oxidation reaction (Chen et al., 2006; Hem et al., 1994). The biofilter 
hydrodynamics i.e. water flow, aeration degree and mixing have great influence on biofilm 
attachment and thickness to the media which determine the number and density of bacteria available 
for nitrification processes (Prehn et al., 2012).

Many researchers have addressed the effect of temperature on the first step of nitrification process, 
the ammonia removal. No significant effect of temperature on nitrification rate was reported, and 
negative impact emerged on further increase in temperature upon decrease in dissolved oxygen 
saturation decrease  (Zhu and Chen, 2002). Sin et al., (2008) and Hem et al., (1994) reported a 
positive effect of temperature on nitrification process in moving bed biofilm reactor, similar results 
were reported when using biodrum filter (Wortman and Wheaton, 1991).

Despite of the available studies, information on the effect of temperature on total nitrification; 
involving both ammonia removal and nitrite removal is still limited. On top of/ besides ammonia 
levels, nitrite accumulation in recirculating aquaculture system also has a great impact on fish 
welfare. Since ammonia oxidation causes formation of nitrite inhibited nitrite oxidation will lead to 
nitrite accumulation in system water affecting the carrying capacity of the blood hemoglobin in the 
fish which can results into brown-blood disease (Timmons and Ebeling, 2010), biofilter 
performance has to be examined for nitrite removal under difference temperature. 
The main objective of this study is to study the effect of temperature on both nitrification processes, 
in separate experiments at predefined temperatures covering cold and warm water recirculating 
aquaculture systems. Experimental water and media were taken from a recirculating aquaculture 
system at DTU Aqua Hirtshals operating at steady state at 18°C. Active carrier media from a system 
at steady state were added in a series of batch reactors set at different temperature levels.  At 
unlimited substrate concentration, biofilter media were studied on acute and prolonged exposure to 
temperature, and only acute effect of temperature was tested at limited substrate concentration. All 
reactors were operated at constant and similar water quality parameters such as aeration degree, 
alkalinity, and pH except for dissolved oxygen which its solubility depends on temperature. In 
addition this study will provide some potential useful information on performance and application 
of biofilter in tropical aquaculture industry, where water availability and treatment is the major 
limitation towards its growth and productivity.

2.2. MATERIAL AND METHODS 
2.2.1. Experimental RAS operation 
An experimental RAS at DTU Aqua, Section of Aquaculture in Hirtshals (described by Pedersen et 
al., (2015)) was the main source of water and active media for the experiment This system 
constantly in operation in a steady state at 18 °C. This RAS is made up of an 8.5 m3 rearing tank 
with rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss,), constantly fed 1 kg of feed per day. The water flow 
into the rearing tank (and through the system) was maintained at 6.8 m3 h-1, with the addition of 1 
m-3 of make-up water per day.  There are two FBB and two MBB (0.40 m3 each) filled with 0.20 m3 
of identical carrier media (RK BioElements® RK Plast, Denmark; Surface Specific Area = 750 
m2/m3). The MBB were supplied with 40 L min-1 of air to keep the carrier media in suspension. 
There was a daily addition of 150 g CaHCO3 to maintain pH between 7.6 - 7.8 and to keep the 
alkalinity > 1.5 meqv/L. 
2.2.2. Experimental setup 
2.2.2.1. Setup I: Temperature levels: 18-36°C 
A  reactor was connected to the RESUN Air Pump (RESUN® LP40, China) using a hose pipe 
sectioned with a control valve, New Flow air flow meter and a check valve. The air flow meter was 
set at 2.5 LPM using a control valve. The aquarium heater was inserted into the reactor to maintain 
the water temperature. HQ40d multimeter (HACH Lange, Germany) probes were also inserted in 
the reactor (See Figure. 2.1).

2.2.3. Experimental protocol 
2.2.3.1. Acute experiment 
Series of reactors in triplicates were set at 6 predefined temperature levels (6, 12, 18, 24, 30, and 36 
°C). Each reactor was filled 5 L of system water from the experimental RAS followed by addition 
of 2.5 liters of carrier media from the RAS’s MBB. The air blower set at 2.5 L min-1 was connected 
to each reactor, turned on to keep the media in suspension and for air supply. Heaters were inserted 
in each treatment to warm the water to the desired temperature. The set up was acclimatized for 24 
h before running the experiment. HQ40d multimeter (HACH Lange, Germany) was inserted in a 
reactor for each temperature levels to measure temperature, pH and dissolved oxygen throughout 
the experiment. Before the experiment, 200 mL of water from each reactor was taken for alkalinity 
analysis. In each reactor 25 mL of 1000 mg NH4-N/L were added to make the initial concentration 
of 5 mg TANN L-1. Using a syringe, 8 mL of water samples were taken after 10 min from each 
reactor, filtered through a 0.2 μm Sartorius filter and reserved in 12 mL test tubes which were 
stored at -5°C until later analysis. Similar sampling, filtration and storage was repeated after 15, 20, 
30, 40, 50, 60, 75, 90, 105, 120, and 150 min. Similar experimental procedures i.e. sampling, 
filtration and storage were adapted to study the acute effect of temperature on nitrite removal, this 
time the reactors were added with 25 ml of 1000 mg NO2-N L-1.. Samples were prepared for 
photometric analysis. 
 
2.2.3.2. Prolonged experiment 
Three sets of 5 L insulated reactors in duplicates were setup post-hoc for temperature levels that 
showed lowest and highest effects i.e. 6°C and 30 °C. These were tested against the 18 °C control 
for the prolonged temperature effects. The reactors were filled with 5 L of RAS system water 
followed by the addition of 2.5 L of carrier media from a moving bed biofilter. The air blower set at 
2.5 L min-1. Heaters were inserted to warm the water in reactors operating at 18 and 30 °C. Hach 
Launge HQ4 multimeter was inserted in each reactor at every respective temperature levels to 
measure temperature, pH and dissolved oxygen. The system was left to run for 4 weeks. During this 
period, a daily addition of 7.5 mL of 1000 mg TAN L-1 was done which corresponds to the 
experimental RAS daily addition of 1.5 mg TANL-1 day-1. 20% of water in each reactor was 
replaced by system from the experimental RAS after every two days. After 7 days of operation, 
==PAGE:28==
17 
 
each reactor was spiked with 25 mL of 1000 mg NH-N L-1. Using a syringe, 8 ml of water samples 
were taken from each reactor 10 min after spiking. Similar time intervals, filtration and storage of 
water samples were adapted like in the acute experiment. 24 h after ammonia spiking, 25 ml of 
1000 mg NO2-N L-1 was spiked in each reactor to test the effect of prolonged effect of temperature 
on nitrite removal. Spiking of ammonia-nitrogen and nitrite-nitrogen, sampling, filtration and 
storage of water samples from each reactor was repeated once per week for 4 weeks. 
2.2.3.3. First order nitrification experiment 
Three sets of 5 L insulated reactors in duplicate were set for three temperature levels i.e. 6, 18 and 
30 °C to determine the of nutrient limitation on biofilter performance. The same water and carrier 
media volumes were taken from experimental RAS as the previous experiment. Water quality 
parameter i.e. temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen were also monitored in the same way. The system 
was acclimated for 24 h before spiking. 5 ml of 1000 mg TAN/L was added to each reactor for 
ammonia-nitrogen removal experiment at low concentration. Using a syringe, 5 ml of water sample 
was taken from each reactor 5 minutes after spiking. The sample was filtered through a 0.2 μm 
Sartorius filter, reserved in a test tube. Samples were again taken after 10, 15, 20, 30, 40 and 50 min 
followed by the same filtration, reservation and then at -5°C until analysis. In a separate experiment 
the reactors with similar condition were spiked with 5 ml of 1000 mg NO-N/L stock solution, to 
study the influence of temperature on nitrite-nitrogen removal in a moving bed reactor. Sampling 
time intervals, filtration process, sample reservation and storage followed in low concentration 
spiking of ammonia-nitrogen were adapted. 
 
2.2.4. Water analysis  
Total ammonium-nitrogen, nitrite-nitrogen and nitrate-nitrogen in the water samples were analyzed 
using a DR 2800 Spectrophotometer (HACH®, Germany) according to a Danish standard 224 of 
1975, Danish Standard 223 (1991), and ISO 7890 (1986) respectively (Pedersen et al., 2015). Water 
temperature, dissolved oxygen, and PH were measured using HACH Lange HQ40d multimeter. 
Alkalinity was monitored throughout the experiment by the use of T50 Mettler Toledo titration 
machine and analyzed by LabX titration 2.6 software.

2.4. DISCUSSION 
Temperature has been a source of variation to biological reaction; refer to the van’t Hoff-Arrhenius 
equation (1) concept. Nitrification process a keystone in biofilter performance responds to this 
concept due to the increase in bacteria activities as temperature increase. This study potentially 
investigate the effects of temperature on the conversion rates for the two oxidation step involved in 
nitrification process in a separate experiment; ammonia to nitrite, and nitrite to nitrate in a separate 
experiment. In addition, the study will provide some useful information on biofilter performance in 
a wide range of climatic conditions from temperate region and if can possibly be adapted to tropical 
regions as an effective means of water treatment for an emerging aquaculture industry. Many 
researcher have reported small significance or no effects of temperature on nitrification rate (Zhu 
and Chen, 2002), without having a closer look in the second step of nitrite conversion to nitrate. In 
this study nitrification rate is measured in all two steps; ammonia to nitrite, and nitrite to nitrate 
using water from water and carrier media from an experimental RAS operating at steady state 
condition. 
 
2.4.1. Biofilter performance 
2.4.1.1. Ammonia removal 
Previous studies have shown high turn-over of ammonia in different biofilter design when subjected 
to an increase in temperature (Lekang, 2013; Mann et al., 1998; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010).  The 
design and setup of this study allowed investigation of temperature levels in both steps of the 
nitrification process by separate supplementation of either ammonia-nitrogen or nitrite –nitrogen 
stock solutions. In both types of reactor experiments, acute and long term trials, degradation data 
within a given temperature treatment was found to be highly reproducible/with limited variation. 
==PAGE:44==
33 
 
Reactors operating at 30°C showed higher STR than the rest temperature levels, while rectors 
operating at 6C had the least ammonia removal rate. Zhang et al., (2009) reported similar results; 
31°C as the optimal temperature for maximum ammonia removal rate being 4.7 mg NH4
+-N/L/h. 
The findings of this study agree on a direct relationship between temperature increase and STR as 
predicted by the van’t Hoff-Arrhenius equation (1) in his theory for the reactors operating between 
6°C to 30°C temperature levels (Kir, 2009), linear response of ammonia removal to increase in 
temperature was reported (Matthews et al., 2009). The increase of nitrification rate with temperature 
is mainly the temperature effect on bacteria activity. Ammonia diffusion on biofilm increases with 
increase in temperature (Gujer and Boller, 1986), that triggers rapid oxidation process which in 
theory lead to elevated ammonia removal. Nitrification process like any other biological reactions, 
depends on stable biomass and growth and on the abundance and activity of the nitrifies influenced 
by the chemical and physical property of water (Hagopian and Riley, 1998; Zhu and Chen, 2002) 
Apart from contribution of parameters on biofilter performance, temperature in both acute and long 
term trial showed a significance difference on ammonia removal. With ammonia loading being kept 
at constant addition in all batches of reactors, alkalinity, pH, and dissolved oxygen supply being 
maintained at a level for maximum removal, moving bed biofilter performance depends on the 
temperature level in which it operates (Sin et al., 2008). The decline of STR at 36°C in acute 
experiment and at 30°C for long term temperature exposure can be explained by decaying process 
as bacteria get denatured at extreme heating (Baer et al., 2014; Wong-Chong and Loehr, 1978). 
2.4.1.2. Nitrite removal  
Nitrite accumulation is not always considered a potential threat in in recirculating aquaculture 
system; however when it rises above optimal level, it impairs fish respiration process. Moving bed 
biofilter a reported to be poor in term of nitrite removal compared to fixed bed biofilter (Pedersen et 
al., 2015). Few studies have reported the effect water quality parameters in the second step of 
==PAGE:45==
34 
 
nitrification. For complete nitrification process Figure 6 reveals a linear relation between 
temperature increase and SNR reaching maximum rate at 30°C. However, further increase of 
temperature above 30°C resulted to decline in SNR due to deactivation of nitrite oxidizing bacteria 
as reported in (Wong-Chong and Loehr, 1978). On a long term effect, temperature showed impact 
on nitrite removal by maintaining a stable difference between temperature levels (Table. 2.3).  
Both STR and SNR showed a reduction in a prolonged experiment compared to the results in the 
acute experiments (Figure 2.4 vs Table. 2.2) and (Figure 2.6 vs Table. 2.3). A continuous exposure 
of the carrier media in extremely lower and higher temperature than the room temperature could be 
have resulted to denaturation of bacteria. On the other aeration as a mixing agent might have caused 
a physical damage, washout of the attached nitrifiers hence reducing their abundance for oxidation 
process. Decline of STR and SNR in a prolonged experiment could be described by rely on 
ammonia-nitrogen supplementation could also be reason for micronutrients mismatch on daily 
hence affecting growth of the bacteria. Hagopian and Riley, (1998) reported several inhabitants for 
both nitrition and nitration, spiking with high levels of both ammonia-nitrogen and nitrite-nitrogen 
higher allowed in the fish culture could slowdown nitrification process. 
2.4.1.3. 1’ order rates of nitrification constants 
The effects of temperature on the rates of nitrification constant was significance to the steps of 
nitrification process; ammonia to nitrite and nitrite to nitrate. An increase in temperature from 6 to 
30 resulted to great increase in ammonia and removal rate constants k1 from 0.039 to 0.664 m d-1 
and 0.027 to 0.229 m d-1 respectively. Despite of showing the same response to increase in 
temperature, the rate constant of ammonia removal was approximately 3 times higher to that of 
nitrite removal (Table. 2.6). This might be due temperature dependency on growth for both 
ammonia oxidizing bacteria and nitrite oxidizing bacteria, which lead to high nitrification rate for 
ammonia removal compared to that of nitrite at temperature above 20°C (Kaelin et al., 2009). 
==PAGE:46==
35 
 
Lower value of k1 in nitrite removal compared to ammonia removal shows high susceptibility of 
nitrite oxidizing bacteria compared to ammonia oxidizing bacteria at temperature and low 
concentration (Park and Bae, 2009). Charley et al., (1980) in report on nitrification kinetics in 
activated sludge, temperature increase resulted to elevation of maximum rate of reaction by 185.4% 
at 35°C for ammonia removal, while the 1’ order kinetics was observed to up 15 mg l-1. 
2.4.2. Water quality and operating condition 
In order to study the effect of temperature on nitrification process, reactors were operated with good 
water quality conditions for the bacteria to reach their maximum degradation and removal of both 
ammonia and nitrite at all temperature levels. Water and active Bioelement were taken from an 
experimental RAS moving bed biofilter running at steady state has similar water quality condition 
and organic loading (Pedersen et al., 2015). In order to archive/ensure maximum nitrification rate, 
the system water were kept under monitoring of pH, dissolved oxygen and alkalinity. 
Alkalinity levels were kept above 1.5 meqv/l in all experiments/reactor to ensure maximum 
nitrification rate (Gujer and Boller, 1986), by keeping the pH level in an optimal range for oxidation 
reaction for both ammonia oxidizing bacteria (AOB) and nitrite oxidizing bacteria (NOB) (Chen et 
al., 2006). In most cases, dissolved oxygen is always a limiting factor for bacteria activity on fixed 
biofilter, application of aeration in the moving bed insures sufficient supply of oxygen apart from 
keeping the media in motion. The range of dissolved oxygen measured in study (6.8 ± 0.1 to 12.48 
± 0.2321) mg/l was high enough to give maximum nitrification rate (Hem et al., 1994).

2.5. CONCLUSION 
This study investigated the effect of temperature on ammonia and nitrite removal in aquaculture 
biofilter in a standardized way.  Significant positive effects of temperature from 6 to 30°c were 
found for TAN and nitrite. In the 0’ order kinetics revealed an eight-fold increase in STR was found 
(from 0.03 to 0.25 g N m-2 d-1) and a four-fold increase from 0.033 to 0.122 g N m-2 d-1 SNR. For 
both TAN and nitrite removal, nitrification rates at 36 °C was lower than at 30°C indication optimal 
water temperature for aerobic nitrogen removal is around 30 °C. A decline in nitrification rates in 
prolonged experiment due extended exposure of AOB and NOB at extreme lower and high 
temperature that might have resulted to deactivation/denaturation. At limited substrate 
concentration, 1’order rates of nitrification constant were significantly high at 30°C for both 
ammonia and nitrite removal. Based on the data from this study, adaptation of biofilter technology 
to warm water aquaculture in tropical region could be feasible since nitrification performance at 
30°C has great efficiency.

ABSTRACT  
Moving bed biofilter performance depends on design, operation and hydraulic characteristics. This 
study investigated the potential influence of spontaneous application of different water flow and 
aeration degree levels on nitrification process in a batch experiment. Water flow between 30-120 
L/h and aeration degree from 0.25 – 2.5 LPM were tested using a Response Surface Method to 
estimate the surface specific ammonia removal (STR) and nitrite removal (SNR). Both STR and 
SNR increased significantly at higher water flow and medium aeration degree (120 :L/h vs 5.1 
:LPM) with values of 0.156 g NH4-N m-2 day-1 and 0.160 g NO2-N m-2 day-1 respectively, while 
aeration degree showed no effects. At static flow condition, an increase in aeration degree had a 
negative impact on STR. At 0.25 LPM, STR was 0.149 g NH-N m-2 day-1 compared to 0.123 g 
NH4-N m-2 day-1 at 2.5 LPM. Prolonged aeration (seven days) led to a decline in ammonia removal 
for both levels over time.

3.1. INTRODUCTION 
Recirculating aquaculture systems rely on water reuse through inline water treatment methods. 
Biofilter performance  via nitrification is the keystones on controlling the water quality for optimal 
fish productivity in recirculating aquaculture systems (Crab et al., 2007). Nitrification is a two-step 
biological reaction that involves conversion of potential harmful ammonia to nitrite and nitrite to 
less harmful nitrate (Lekang, 2013; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). Bacteria species; Nitrosomonas 
and Nitrobacter involved in nitrification, can grow attached on biofilter media as fixed film or 
freely suspended within the biofilter water column (Gutierrez-Wing and Malone, 2006) to 
maximize the surface area for ammonia and nitrite degradation. 
A number of biofilters have been designed to address high ammonia removal to satisfy the quality 
of water for reuse in recirculating aquaculture systems, however every design has its own pros and 
cons and therefore the choice remain on interests, capacity and economic feasibility. Fixed bed (i.e. 
submerged and trickling filter) and moving bed are the two common types of biofilters used in 
recirculating aquaculture systems (Guerdat et al., 2010; Gujer and Boller, 1986; Hem et al., 1994; 
Kir, 2009; Pedersen et al., 2015; Sin et al., 2008). Airlift and Sand fluidized, biodrum biofilter, 
constructed wetland, oyster and macroalgae culture are among other technics that are applied in 
removal of nitrogenous waste in recirculating aquaculture systems (Jones et al., 2002; Sánchez O. 
and Matsumoto, 2012; Summerfelt, 2006; Wortman and Wheaton, 1991). A continuous growth of 
filtration technology have emerged with hybridization of biofilter with other high tech system such 
membrane and ion exchange or electrochemical oxidation to maximize the removal of ammonia and 
other nitrogenous wastes. 
The efficiency of nitrification process depends mainly on the state of the ammonia and nitrite 
oxidizing bacteria as drivers of the process. The state of the oxidizing bacteria are mainly under the 
==PAGE:55==
44 
 
influence of; colonization, growth, decay, and oxidation capacity is basically determined by 
biological, chemical and physical characteristics of culture water (Badiola et al., 2012; Chen et al., 
2006; Gregory et al., 2010). Ammonia concentration substrate, like to any living organism is an 
important factor for autotrophic bacteria survival and growth, dissolved oxygen signifies the rate at 
which ammonia and nitrite are being oxidized by the bacteria (Suhr and Pedersen, 2010), and 
temperature, alkalinity, pH, and CO2 levels are other parameters that characterize biofilter 
performance (Chen et al., 2006). 
Hydrodynamic conditions are potentially affecting the nitrification process efficiency during design, 
operation and management of the biofilter. Water flow, turbulence, mixing and aeration degree fall 
in this category (Bansal, 1976; Chen et al., 2006; Ordaz et al., 2011; Sánchez O. and Matsumoto, 
2012; Sandu et al., 2002). Turbulence has been reported to affect nitrification process due to its

Aeration is the main mechanical mixing agent in a moving bed biofilter. It keeps the media in 
suspension to increase exposure of the surface area for maximum ammonia and nitrite degradation. 
Previous studies reported significant effects of aeration degrees on ammonia removal. Chui et 
al.(1996), Guo et al.(2009) and Wu et al.(2011) reported an increase in ammonia removal for

aeration degree and time. However, an increase in aeration leads to wash out of nitrite oxidizing 
bacteria (Guo et al., 2009). 
Besides the available information, the combined effect of water flow and aeration degrees; the 
management and operation of moving bed biofilter nitrification process can still be improved. 
Internal water and air flow raise concerns on economic feasibility due to their energy consumption. 
The aim of this study was to investigate the influence of these two factors on TAN and nitrite 
removal performance in moving bed biofilters. The finding of this study may provide important 
information on optimal levels of water flow and aeration to optimize running cost of moving bed 
biofilter and maximize nitrification performance in recirculating aquaculture systems.

3.2. MATERIAL AND METHODS 
3.2.1. Experimental RAS operation 
An experimental RAS at DTU Aqua, Section of Aquaculture in Hirtshals (described by Pedersen et 
al., (2015)) was the main source of water and active media for the experiment This system 
constantly in operation in a steady state at 18 °C. This RAS is made up of an 8.5 m3 rearing tank 
with rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss,), constantly fed 1 kg of feed per day. The water flow 
into the rearing tank (and through the system) was maintained at 6.8 m3 h-1, with the addition of 1 
m-3 of make-up water per day.  There are two FBB and two MBB (0.40 m3 each) filled with 0.20 m3 
of identical carrier media (RK BioElements® RK Plast, Denmark; Surface Specific Area = 750 
m2/m3). The MBB were supplied with 40 L min-1 of air to keep the carrier media in suspension. 
There was a daily addition of 150 g CaHCO3 to maintain pH between 7.6 - 7.8 and to keep the 
alkalinity > 1.5 meqv/L.

3.4. DISCUSSION 
3.4.1. Biofilter performance 
3.4.1.1. Ammonia removal  
Moving bed biofilter depends heavily on the hydrodynamics characteristic; mainly air flow to keep 
the media in motion while attributing media mixing and water flow an important physical property 
within the biofilter. Nitrification process may be altered according to changes in either water flow 
and/or airflow. 
The response surface method showed significant difference on ammonia removal when a moving 
bed biofilter operates in spontaneous supply of water flow and airflow. Increasing water flow had 
great impact on ammonia removal while aeration degree did not (Figure 3.3&3.4). An increase in 
water flow increases the replacement of oxidized water by new feed solution with more ammonia 
concentration hence giving the bacteria more substrate to oxidize. However the impact of air flow 
which brings about media mixing, and displacement doesn’t overcome water flow on ammonia 
removal. Increasing in water flow increases mass transport in the biofilm system, which in this case 
ammonia transport from the water into the biofilm (Prehn et al., 2012). On the other hand, an 
increase in water velocity inside a biofilter influence the oxygen transfer coefficient in the biofilm 
while dynamic gas hold up/aeration determine the specific area of the media which are the key 
requirement for nitrification rates (Leung et al., 2006). When applied together, water flow and 
aeration degree create turbulence in the reactor with moving bad biofilter which in return increase 
nitrification rates (Chen et al., 2006).  
In static water, where only air flow is applied, an extended aeration experiment show a negative 
impact of ammonia removal when a moving bed biofilter operates under the influence of increased 
aeration flow alone. Figure 3.9, shows high ammonia removal at 0.25 LPM aeration degree 
==PAGE:77==
66 
 
compared to 2.5 LPM air flow throughout the whole time of the experiment. An increase in aeration 
degree grinding to the bacteria in fixed biofilm attached to the media due to increase displacement 
and mixing that can results into clogging. The situation is different in fixed bed biofilter where 
aeration increase ammonia oxidation for bacteria attached on a fixed surface of the media. The 
finding from this study does not confirm with the results on the effect of aeration degree in the fixed 
bed biofilter which reported an increase in ammonia removal from 36.8 to 92.7% when air flow is 
increased from 1 to 5 LPM (Chui et al., 1996). Another study suggested aeration being influential 
on successful accumulation and growth of ammonia oxidizing bacteria that increase up 8.3 ± 1.1% 
of the total bacteria counts (Guo et al., 2009). 
3.4.1.2. Nitrite removal  
The overall nitrite removal in the biofilter depends upon the rate of ammonia converted into nitrite 
and nitrite that is primarily available in the system water. Like on the ammonia removal, nitrite 
removal in the moving bed biofilter is also influenced by both water flow that attributes continuous 
replacement of system water and aeration degree which keeps the media in suspension and mixing. 
Figure 3.5&3.6, show a significant increase in nitrite removal with increase in water flow, while 
aeration degree had no significance effect. One of the reasons for increase in nitrite removal at 
higher water flow is higher ammonia turn-over to nitrite in elevated water flow (Figure 3.3&3.4) 
which is taken into account in addition to the apparent nitrite removal. Watten and Sibrell, (2006) 
expressed a positive net change on nitrite-nitrogen when a product of ammonia conversion rate and 
hydraulic retention time decreases. Hence nitrite accumulation in the moving bed biofilter is still 
affected by water flow in the reactor apart from the ammonia oxidation. 
3.4.1.3. Nitrite accumulation 
Nitrite accumulation depends on the rate at which ammonia is converted to nitrite, Figure. 3.10A 
shows higher accumulation of nitrite at lower aeration degree compared to Figure. 3.10B at higher 
==PAGE:78==
67 
 
aeration degree. These results correspond to Figure. 3.8A and Figure. 3.8B, which represents 
quicker and slower degradation of ammonia concentration over time respectively. Guo et al., (2009) 
reported negative impacts of aeration degrees on nitrite oxidizing bacteria (NOB), a continuous 
aeration may reduce the density of NOB to zero. Nitrite accumulation in a moving bed biofilter 
which operates under the influence of aeration is reported due to the delay in oxidation as a result of 
physical erosion affecting growth on NOB communities when aeration time was increase from 4-12 
hours (Xu et al., 2014). On the other hand, nitrite accumulation in the moving bed biofilter can be 
prescribed by net-outward diffusion from the biofilm when higher ammonia oxidation is undergoing 
within a film layer (Pedersen et al., 2015).

3.5. CONCLUSION 
The study revealed the importance of hydraulic characteristics on moving bed biofilter nitrification 
process. An increase in water flow had a significant impact on both ammonia and nitrite removal 
while aeration showed no effect when acting at the same time with water flow. An increase in 
aeration degree on the other hand had a negative impact on ammonia removal at static flow. Nitrite 
accumulation and removal in the moving bed biofilter depended on the rate of ammonia conversion 
to nitrite in step one of the nitrification process. In order to optimize cost and moving bed biofilter 
application and efficiency, this study concludes usage of low aeration degree enough to keep the 
media in suspension and gentle mixing, while water flow and hydraulic retention can depend on the 
fish reared in the production system; though the higher water flow improves nitrification process.
