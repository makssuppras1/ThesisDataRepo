CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
In the era of the Internet of Things (IoT), low-power wireless networking is becoming
more and more ubiquitous as a variety of things are monitored using wireless sensor
devices. Several standards exist to allow multiple resource-constrained devices to com-
municate within a Personal Area Network (PAN).
For these networks to be feasible, the communication medium has to be shared be-
tween the different devices, by use of a Medium Access Control (MAC) protocol. A
recent addition to the IEEE 802.15.4 wireless standard [4], Time-Slotted Channel Hop-
ping (TSCH), has seen a lot of developments and research within recent years.
TSCH shares the radio spectrum by subdividing in time and frequency.
This is
elaborated on in Chapter 2. In doing so, it allows a number of devices to get a time and
space in the radio spectrum to transmit and receive messages. The way TSCH achieves
this subdivision comes with the necessity to keep time synchronized between devices.
In regular cases, where a node is deployed and stays in place, this synchronization does
not pose an issue. These nodes will join once and remain synchronized with the network
during regular operation. Due to these properties, the cost of joining presents itself
exactly once.
1.2
Mobility
The mobility issue presents itself in various use cases with regards to TSCH networks.
One such use case is SPHERE [2], which is a monitoring platform designed for res-
idential environments aimed at providing healthcare professionals with supplemental
information about their patient.
As opposed to regular deployment of nodes within a TSCH network, wearable nodes
have the potential to be physically removed from the network for extended periods of
time.
Once this occurs, the mobile node will lose synchronization with the network
beyond the slack that TSCH already provides for. This leads to the mobile node having
to join the network once again, at a large cost.
==PAGE:16==
2
1 Introduction
The cost of joining a TSCH network stems from the fact that the node has to stay
awake, scanning channels using its radio until a synchronization frame can be captured.
1.3
Contribution
The main goal of this thesis is first and foremost to reduce the TSCH joining time. As
noted in the previous sections, TSCH subdivides the radio spectrum in both frequency
and time.
Currently, the joining process has no concept of dealing with previously joined net-
works.
In theory, this means that the join (or rejoin) could be sped up by use of
memorization of crucial network parameters.
The proposed solution attempts to deal with the mobility problem by focusing on
the frequency domain. More specifically, the idea of making an informed search within
the space by taking into account that certain core parameters for the network don’t alter
drastically within a certain time frame.
1.4
Thesis Organization
The thesis is organized as follows:
Chapter 2 presents information about the TSCH joining mechanism and presents
possible solutions for reducing the joining time. Chapter 3 summarizes several research
articles related to TSCH. Chapter 4 documents experiments and their design. Finally,
Chapter 5 discusses the results and concludes the thesis with a perspective on possible
future work.

CHAPTER 2
TSCH and Joining
2.1
Time and Frequency Subdivision
As described in the previous Chapter, TSCH subdivides the radio spectrum by time and
frequency. It does this by making all devices that are part of the network adhere to a
global schedule.
This schedule allocates timeslots to a pair of devices within the PAN at a prede-
termined channel offset, as visualized in Figure 2.1.
The timeslots are ordered in a
slotframe, to form a repeating schedule.
Due to the fact that the schedule repeats, the nodes in the network know when they
are clear to transmit or receive messages, allowing them to run in low-power mode for
the rest of the schedule.
Figure 2.1: Minimal schedule with a slotframe of length 7
The frequency subdivision is based on a channel hopping strategy. Whenever the
==PAGE:18==
4
2 TSCH and Joining
schedule lists a channel offset, that is an offset from the current channel as dictated by a
Channel Hopping Sequence (CHS). The ASN is a continuous counter for timeslots that
helps with the repeat of the schedule and the selection of the current radio channel.
2.2
Time Synchronization
As described in the previous section, the schedule informs the nodes in the network with
regards to planned communication slots. Since there are strict timing constraints on the
transmission of MAC-layer frames, tight time synchronization is crucial. TSCH allows
for a degree of slack with regards to synchronization by incorporating a guard time. This
means that the node wakes up prior to the expected start of a timeslot, in order to not
miss the signaling deadlines.
Time synchronization is ultimately needed because every device will experience clock
drift, leaving two devices to desynchronize over time. There are two main causes to clock
drift [3], being manufacturing tolerances and a difference in operating temperature.
The way TSCH solves time synchronization is by the use of Enhanced Broadcast
(EB) packets at certain timeslots within the schedule. In Figure 2.1 the EB is marked
by the gray square.
This EB packet is what is captured during the joining process and synchronizes the
node to the coordinator’s clock. The node is furthermore also synchronized by other
communication with its designated time source.

CHAPTER 3
Related Work
This chapter briefly summarizes research articles surrounding TSCH. The research pre-
sented in these summaries is not directly related to the mobility problem as considered in
this thesis project, however, they touch on various aspects of TSCH network operation
that can be influential.
When it comes to running TSCH networks, configuration can either be static or
dynamic. Either way of configuring the network has its own challenges. Static config-
urations are usable in a scenario where you are in complete control of the network’s
environment. In this scenario, there are no devices that produce interference in the
2.4GHz band where TSCH networks operate. The configuration of a static network is
done once at deployment and may be reconfigured later if necessary.
For the majority of network operators however, this is simply not the case. Depending
on the site of deployment, all sorts of devices can interfere within the 2.4GHz band, such
as WiFi equipment or microwave ovens. It is this type of environment that provides us
with the necessity for dynamically governed networks within the 2.4GHz band.
Researchers have proposed several solutions to reduce communication interference.
One of these is channel management, which is the act of allowing or disallowing the use
of radio channels, in order to improve reliability.
3.1
Channel Management
Channel management can be vital in order to reduce communication interference within
a TSCH network. This is due to the fact that like IEEE 802.11 networks (WiFi), 802.15.4
networks operate within the 2.4GHz radio spectrum [8].
This overlap of channels is shown in Figure 3.1. The same Figure also shows several
channels that do not share an overlap with WiFi (15, 20, 25, 26). [6]

CHAPTER 4
Experiments
This chapter describes the experiments for this project. These experiments were done to
confirm that the memorization of channels would improve network joining time, given a
few scenarios.
Therefore we conduct experiments and measure the joining time to experimentally
confirm the hypothesis. Section 4.1 discusses design considerations and operating envi-
ronments during experimentation. Sections 4.2 to 4.4 discuss the experiments, which
are followed up by a description of a TSCH join time simulator.
The simulator was developed as a way to verify the results from the physical ex-
periments, while having more control over factors such as phase difference and channel
quality.
4.1
Design and Environment
The experiments are designed in such a way that every experiment represents a defined
scenario within the scope of a node joining a TSCH-coordinated network. For every
scenario there will be a single coordinator and a single leaf node, attempting to join the
network.
The scenarios, from the perspective of the joining node, are as follows.
1. It has no knowledge of the TSCH network.
2. It has memorized TSCH network parameters.
3. It has outdated knowledge of the network.
A network in operation typically has many nodes, for example in a Wireless Sensor
Network (WSN). However, for the purpose of these experiments, the interest is purely
in the joining sequence and the time it takes to associate. As such, a single coordinator
and joining node suﬀice.
4.1.1
Coordinator Operation
The coordinator is configured to monitor the 2.4GHz spectrum in order to avoid in-
terference with other wireless networks. We assume the coordinator runs a dynamic
==PAGE:22==
8
4 Experiments
black/white-listing channel algorithm, for instance [5]. Because of the dynamic config-
uration, a new node that joins the network for the first time will have absolutely no
knowledge of the channel hopping sequence.
This interference avoidance strategy is necessary because as Figure 3.1 shows, there
is an overlap within the 2.4GHz spectrum. In this case, the overlap is between IEEE
802.11- and IEEE 802.15.4-based networks.
4.1.2
Channel parameters
For every scenario listed above, three distinct CHS configurations have been used to
gather samples. The reasoning behind this is that the length and channel distribution
can be contributing factors to the overall association time for a node.
The CHS configurations are as follows:
• a 6-length hopping sequence consisting of 6 channels
• a 8-length hopping sequence consisting of 8 channels
• a 10-length hopping sequence consisting of 10 channels
All of these hopping sequences were generated using an implementation [7] of the
TSCH standard [4] hopping sequence generation scheme.
4.1.3
Hardware
The hardware used for these experiments consists of a pair of Texas Instruments (TI)
LaunchXL-CC1350 development kits, as seen in Figure 4.1.
These are evaluation kits for TI’s range of microcontrollers with built-in wireless
capabilities. As such, the boards come with everything needed to prototype your wireless
application, such as:
• On-board programming of the microcontroller
• General purpose I/O peripherals
• On-board radio antennas
What makes the CC1350 development kit suitable for our purposes is that it has an
on-board 2.4GHz RF transceiver which can be programmed to run the MAC protocol
of your choosing. Aside from that, the kit is fully supported by Contiki-NG [1] which
will be discussed further in Section 4.1.4.

4.1.4
Firmware
Since the experiments use microcontroller development boards, a choice had to be made
with regards to firmware.
For this purpose, the open source IoT Operating System
Contiki-NG [1] was chosen.
Contiki-NG has a highly configurable and resource eﬀicient networking stack which
allows the use of a variety of protocols, among which TSCH. These features along as
well as compatibility with the CC1350 development kit made it a good fit for this project.
For every distinct scenario outlined above, including their CHS length variations, a
pair of Contiki processes were defined. These can be found in Appendix A.1.
In order to have full control and only consider a given scenario, every Contiki process
was made to use a static TSCH configuration. Furthermore, to accurately get a measure
of joining time, the network stack is explicitly started by the applications themselves.
The actual measurement of join time is facilitated by overriding the default log print-
ing function and including a system time stamp in every log message Contiki generates.
Combined with a metric for how many clock ticks are in a second, it is possible to
compute the join time in seconds.
The criteria for determining when the association process succeeds is when one of
the following is true:
• Contiki tells us the node is associated

• The joining node has received an EB packet from the coordinator, valid or not
4.1.5
Experiment Conditions
Over the course of running these experiments, the boards are wired to a computer in
order to provide power data logging over serial connection. Due to this, the distance
between the two nodes is at most a meter.
To reduce possible interference, no other radio devices were operatedin the direct
vicinity of the development kits. However, since the experiments were run in a residential
area, they were subject to natural urban interference.
For every experiment described in this chapter, there are three permutations with
regards to the length of the CHS. This leads to a total of nine different scenarios to
execute. In order to generate enough data, each of these scenarios was executed on the
hardware 25 times, for a total of 225 experiment runs.
4.2
Experiment 1 – Baseline
The baseline experiment is meant to model a new node joining the TSCH network for
the first time. As such, it has no previous knowledge of the TSCH network in operation,
so it will try to scan all possible channels in order to associate.
The coordinator node has three distinct configurations built using Contiki, as de-
scribed in section 4.1.4. This is in contrast to the single configuration that the joining
node uses.
The joining node uses a 16-channel, 16-length Channel Hopping Sequence, as gen-
erated by [7]. This sequence is used because the joining node has no prior knowledge
about the channels in use by the TSCH network it intends to join. Therefore it iterates
over all available channels specified by the 802.15.4 standard.
4.2.1
Expectations
With no prior knowledge about the network, the joining node must do a blind search
on all 16 channels. The fact that it scans all 16 channels guarantees that an EB packet
will successfully be captured, regardless of the CHS of the network.
However this comes at a cost, since the joining node has to scan channels that the
network does not use. Therefore we would expect that the joining node will eventually
manage to join the network, but the time to do so would be long.
==PAGE:25==
4.3 Experiment 2 – Memorization
11
4.3
Experiment 2 – Memorization
This experiment models a node re-joining the network after being disassociated for a
short amount of time. The assumption is that, given the short duration of time, no
mutations occur within the coordinator’s CHS.
The joining node configurations use the coordinator’s CHS as its scanning sequence.
This models the fact that it memorized the hopping sequence from the previous associ-
ation with the network.
4.3.1
Expectations
In this case, the joining node has prior knowledge of the network. This effectively reduces
the search space to the exact channels that the coordinator utilizes.
Overall, this should significantly reduce joining time when compared to the Baseline
experiment.
4.4
Experiment 3 – Outdated Knowledge
This experiment represents the node being away from the network for a longer period of
time. In this case, due to blacklisting algorithms, the coordinator’s CHS will have been
mutated.
Since the joining node re-uses the scanning sequences from the previous experiment,
there is a mismatch in the network’s channel parameters.
4.4.1
Expectations
Given that the hopping sequences for the coordinator and node do not exactly match
up, a slight deterioration in join time can be expected when compared to Experiment
2. However, the overall increase in performance when compared to the Baseline should
still be substantial.
4.5
TSCH Join time Simulator
As described at the start of this Chapter, a TSCH Join time Simulator was developed
in order to verify the results from the physical experiments.
In the physical experiments, for every scenario including their permutations, 25 sam-
ples were gathered. This process was very time-consuming since there was no automated
testbed in place.

Along with the fact that the experiments were executed in a residential environment,
that is to say, with factors beyond our control, this lead to data that doesn’t possibly
tell the full story with regards to the hypothesis.
In order to acquire more data, where factors such as overall channel quality are within
our control, a numerical simulator was developed to model the process.
Using the simulator, it was trivial to generate 10000 results for each scenario including
its permutations. This leads to over 900000 join times to analyse.
4.5.1
Simulator Design
The simulator is designed such that it models the joining process by making comparisons
based on the overall system state and a packet reception probability.
This probability is a simplified measure of channel quality, in that it models the overall
quality of all channels rather than the quality of an individual channel.
For future
expansion, this could be implemented as a per-channel figure.

CHAPTER 5
Results & Discussion
5.1
Results
This chapter includes graphs for all the experiments and simulations that were executed
as part of the thesis. Section 5.1.1 discusses the physical experiments, where Section 5.1.2
discusses the simulations.
5.1.1
Physical Experiments
The results for the physical experiments are outlined in Figures 5.1 – 5.3. Every figure
provides results for all three of the experiments for a given CHS length. Based on these
graphs, we can observe that the Baseline case, as predicted, provides high join times.
Also be careful to note the high amount of variance.
If we then take a look at the Memorization experiment results, they show that the
median join time has dropped significantly compared to the Baseline. Alongside that,
the variance is also significantly lower. This would lead us to conclude that our prediction
for the Memorization case is true.
Finally, if we consider the Outdated Knowledge case, we can observe a decrease in
joining time and variance as compared to the Memorization case. Furthermore, we can
also observe rather odd results in Figure 5.3, where the Outdated Knowledge case shows
worse join times than the Baseline. This does not line up with our original prediction
for the Outdated Knowledge case.

5.1.2
Simulator Experiments
As described in Section 4.5, the simulator was developed as a way to verify the results
from the physical experiments while providing more control over operational parameters.
The outcomes for the Simulator Experiments are presented in Figure 5.4 – 5.8. Every
figure has a subfigure for one of the experiments, where each subfigure presents results
for increasing packet reception probabilities.
If we consider Figure 5.4a, we observe that the join time shows a downward trend as
the packet reception probability increases, which is exactly what we would expect. In
this aspect, the Simulator produces accurate results.
If we compare Figure 5.4b with Figure 5.4a, we can again observe a decrease in
join time. However, we can also observe that the performance increases significantly
as the packet reception probability increases.
In the original experiments, we were
not able to observe this as the channel quality was not a metric that we could readily
take into account and vary. Finally, if we then consider Figure 5.4c, it shows an even
bigger decrease in join time as compared to Figure 5.4b. This is not in line with our
expectations.
Figure 5.5 – 5.8 show similar patterns as to the ones described just now. There is
however one exception we can observe. Figure 5.6c, the 10-channel Outdated Knowledge
scenario shows similar amounts of variance across the whole range of packet reception
probabilities. After verifying the parameters in the simulator, it seems that this was an
unlucky occurrence due to chosen experiment parameters.
As a final remark with regards to the results of the simulator, we can observe that
the times reported by the simulator and the physical experiments are of the same order
of magnitude. This further increases our confidence that the simulator as implemented
is valid, and also validates the results of the physical experiments.
5.2
Conclusion
Based on the results gathered from the physical experiments and our simulated experi-
ments, we can say that Memorization as a measure works to narrow the search space in
the frequency domain.
The case where the coordinator’s CHS was mutated over time, does not align with
our initial expectations. Furthermore the simulator exhibits dissimilar results to the
physical experiments. On this basis, we cannot provide a successful conclusion on the
Outdated Knowledge experiment.

5.3
Future Work
As mentioned in Section 4.5, the simulator could be improved to take individual channel
qualities into account. This would allow the simulator to also be used for simulation of
blacklisting algorithms.
Furthermore, since this thesis put a heavy emphasis on reducing TSCH join time
while considering the frequency domain, more exploratory work can be done while con-
sidering the time domain or a combination of both.
