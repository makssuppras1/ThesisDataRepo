Abstract
This thesis focuses on exploring the benefits Software-Defined Networking (SDN) and
Network Virtualisation can bring to Optical Transport Networks (OTNs). Technologies
that integrate them are researched and potential new services are introduced. Those
services are: Bandwidth on Demand, dynamic restoration and Flexible traffic mapping.
The evaluation of these services with both qualitative and quantitative metrics is based
on real life OTN modelled in OPNET Modeller software. The resulting model allows for
static analysis of the processes that occur in the examined network while aforementioned
services are running. The thesis also evaluates how applicable Network Virtualisation
principles are to transport networks.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
A shift towards virtualised platforms has been steadily happening in the IT industry. The
idea to run virtual instances of devices performing certain functions originated in the
desire to fully utilise hardware resources and easily manage services. Instead of having
multiple hardware platforms, each running a single service and thus being utilised in
only a fraction of its capabilities, virtualisation enabled running multiple services on a
single machine, allowing for the optimal use of resources. Additionally, this shift was also
helped tremendously by the availability of tools allowing for fast and flexible deployment,
administration and orchestration of services. The scenario in which the utilisation of a
server belonging to a certain customer reached its limits, and automatically a new virtual
instance of this server is created to offload the first one is now a feature offered by the
providers.
The trend of virtualisation has also been slowly making its way to the networking in-
dustry. Services on a single device can be interconnected by a virtual network running
on that same device, effectively tricking the aforementioned services into thinking, that
they are connected by IP network. However, for the networks that interconnect many
physical devices this concept is impossible to apply. For this reason, network virtualisa-
tion that goes beyond a single device is based on a concept of slicing. Slicing is the ability
to create virtual networks (overlays) that are strongly decoupled from the underlying
hardware infrastructure[1, 2]. Those overlays perform different functions according to
the needs of a particular service they provide for and are logically separate. An exam-
ple of this concept are Virtual Local Area Networks (VLANs), which use one physical
infrastructure to support multitude of virtual networks. The idea is for the hardware
platforms to exclusively handle data forwarding, while other functions (such as access
control, load balancing, etc) are to be virtualised. This, in theory, should enable pro-
grammable and dynamic control over the network, without the need to interact directly
with hardware. All in all, the network virtualisation can be characterised by:
1. Decoupling of functions/services and hardware platforms
2. Network slicing
3. Use of open protocols and standards
4. Complexity abstraction (layered approach)
5. Focus on optimal resource utilisation
6. Automated deployment, administration and orchestration
==PAGE:12==
1 Introduction
2
By using standards and protocols that are open, network virtualisation tries to solve
the vendor lock-in problem. The operators are able to chose between competing manu-
facturers for each device on their network, which results in significant cost reductions.
Additionally, some network functions that do not require fast hardware execution can
be performed by Central Processing Units (CPUs) of Commodity-Of-The-Shelf (COTS)
hardware.
Network virtualisation is already present in the data-centre market, as Data Centres
(DCs) require a high degree of flexibility and adaptability[1]. In that space, the Open-
Stack[3] initiative provides a cloud ecosystem based on open-source projects, such as
Neutron and Tacker, enabling programmable control over the networks and orchestra-
tion of services. The situation is different however for geographically scattered optical
transport networks, which main function is to forward high data aggregates across long
distances. Those are historically static in nature, but already follow some virtualisation
paradigms, namely network slicing and complexity abstraction. From the point of view
of client networks, they provide only the end-to-end connectivity and the connections
that are set-up do not need to reflect the actual physical topology.
This thesis focuses on investigating the impact of applying the remaining virtualisation
paradigms to OTNs. The study is based on a real OTN located in Sweden. Enabling
flexible and programmable control over the network allows for implementation of new
services, such as Bandwidth on Demand and dynamic restoration. By bringing control of
client IP/MPLS networks together with control of underlying OTNs, the Unified Control
Plane (UCP) makes even more advanced services possible. The optimisation of resource
usage can now be done automatically by specialised applications. The key technology
allowing for this programmable control is SDN, which is described in more detail in
Section 2.2. The main products of this thesis are:
• High level designs of three services that are possible to implement with SDN based
control over OTNs. Those are:
– Bandwidth on Demand
– Dynamic restoration
– Flexible traffic mapping
• OTN model allowing for:
– Studying impact of failures
– Evaluating the impact of implementing dynamic restoration service with flex-
ible traffic mapping
– Comparing mapping scenarios defined in Section 5.1
• Comparison between downstream-on-demand and parallel control scheme with re-
gards to path set-up time

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical background
This chapter provides a theoretical overview of SDN and OTN, laying the foundation
for further analysis in the rest of the thesis.
2.1
Network planes
A fundamental concept necessary to understand why SDN is needed is a ”network plane”.
Network plane can be thought of as set of functions associated with a certain type of
operations. Three main types of network functions are [4]:
• Transport function - concerned with transporting user information from one end-
point to another (forwarding).
• Control function - concerned with identification and routing between endpoints in
the data plane.
• Management function - concerned with planning and design of data transfer func-
tion, maintenance of equipment, services etc.
Those functions are performed respectively in: data plane (aka.
forwarding plane),
control plane and management plane. Functions and interactions between them fully
describe network behaviour (Figure 2.1).
Figure 2.1: Network planes.
Management plane resides at the top of the stack and governs the behaviour of both
control plane and data plane. Monitoring and configuration of equipment and services,
==PAGE:15==
2.1 Network planes
5
together with modification of network devices’ state is performed here.
Those tasks
can either be done locally or remotely. An example the former would be configuring a
network device by Command Line Interface (CLI). Remote task on the other hand could
be a command to shut down a port on a specified device from an Network Management
System (NMS) (using e.g. a Transaction Layer 1 (TL1) interface). Network management
for more complex networks tends to be centralised, with some of the protocols enabling
it being Simple Network Management Protocol (SNMP) and Network Configuration
Protocol (NETCONF).
Control plane is concerned with routing tasks (call and connection control), topology
discovery and path fail-over mechanisms [5]. Routing protocols such as Open Shortest
Path First (OSPF), Intermediate System to Intermediate System (ISIS), Enhanced In-
terior Gateway Routing Protocol (EIGRP) and Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) would
fall in the control plane. Interaction between management and control plane is of par-
ticular importance, as some of their functions overlap. For example, in the IP/MPLS
stack the act of provisioning a Virtual Circuit could be done by adding labels manually
from the management system. Alternatively, the NMS could request the control plane
to set-up the connection and distribute the labels automatically. The third option is, (if
permitted by management plane) to request the Virtual Circuit directly from the control
plane, via defined User-Network Interface (UNI).
In short, it can be said that both management plane and control plane facilitate the
operations of data plane. Additionally, this division is purely logical, which means that
functions from different planes can be executed on a single device. What is more, data
plane traffic can be mixed with control and management traffic and traverse the same
physical links. This is known as in band control/management. If, on the contrary, data
plane traffic is separated from the other two, it is called out of band.
Difference between control and management
A useful way to distinguish between management and control is to look at their goals with
regard to timescale, persistence and locality [5]. Control plane updates are required at
a much higher frequency than those of management plane, which results in an emphasis
on performance when designing control plane interfaces. Secondly, changes made by
management plane are expected be more permanent than that made by control plane.
As far as locality is concerned, management was historically centralised, while control
was distributed.
Distributed and centralised control/management
As mentioned above, both management and control operations can be automated to a
certain degree. IP routing protocols are examples of automated Control Function (CF).
Two distinct approaches to performing automated control/management functions can be
distinguished, namely: distributed and centralised. To provide an example, distributed
control (Figure 2.2(a)) is native to Internet Protocol (IP) networks, where autonomous

Centralised control, (Figure 2.2(b)) aims to put one device in charge of the network and
leave rest with only basic control functions to perform (such as link establishment be-

this central device. As soon as a report about network change is received, an appropriate

replicating it and having back-ups ready for hot-swap.
2.2
Software Defined Networking
SDN is a technology that advocates strong separation (decoupling) of control/manage-
ment plane and data plane, as well as centralised control over the latter. As such, its
architecture would follow the one presented in Figure 2.2(b).
The reason for this is
the desire to be able to control the network ”programatically”, which means having the
capacity to initialise, control, change, and manage network behaviour dynamically via
open interfaces[5]. By abstracting the complexities of forwarding plane, SDN aims to
enable network engineers to program the network at a high level. A consolidated and
implementation agnostic view on SDN is presented in [5], where architecture shown in
Figure 2.3 is presented. This architecture extends the one presented in Section 2.1 by
additional planes, interfaces and abstraction layers necessary to reach the goals stated
above. The key elements are Device Abstraction Layer (DAL), Control Plane South-

OpenFlow
OpenFlow is an SDN framework developed by Open Networking Foundation (ONF).
Its Open Flow Switch specification[6] defines DAL in the form of OpenFlow Logical
Switch and CPSI in the form of OpenFlow protocol. DAL is based on the concept of
Flow Table, which in turn consist of flow entries. Flow entries define the data-path by
identifying flows using match fields and performing actions on them. OpenFlow protocol
enables remote control over the data-path, however it is heavily geared towards packet
networks. With the additions described in Section 2.6, it is however possible to utilise
it in connection-oriented circuit-switched networks.

NETCONF/YANG
NETCONF, defined in [7], was developed after SNMP’s failure as a comprehensive man-
agement protocol (although being successful in the monitoring space). As such, with a
suitable DAL it can be used as an MPSI. YANG modelling language [8] was developed
to enable abstraction of network devices for NETCONF.
Path Computation Element Architecture
Path Computation Element (PCE) is a defined as an entity (component, application, or
network node) that is capable of computing a network path or route based on a network
graph and applying computational constraints[9]. It is therefore an abstracted network
function whose purpose is to calculate paths, which can be embedded into network
nodes, be a part of an NMS, or function as a separate network entity. [10] specifies
PCE Communication Protocol (PCEP), which is used to communicate between Path
Computation Server (PCS) and Path Computation Client (PCC), allowing for remote
operations. With additional extensions [11, 12, 13, 14], an active Stateful PCE can initi-
ate Generalised Multi-Protocol Label Switching (GMPLS) label insertion on the Label
Switching Router (LSR), effectively acting similar to the SDN controller. The authors
of PCE go as far as to specify Application-Based Network Operations (ABNO)[15] ar-
chitecture, which is a more detailed (and thus less generalised) version of architecture
presented in Figure 2.3. The two approaches are compatible, and PCEP is supported by
some SDN controllers as a Southbound interface[16]. The network examined in Chap-
ter 3 supports it as a Control Plane Southbound Interface (instead of OpenFlow).
2.3
Optical WDM networks
The particular requirements of transport networks (high bit-rate, long transmission dis-
tance, high resilience and low Bit Error Rate (BER)) resulted in optical technologies
being dominant in this space. This is also due to the fact, that light propagating through
optical fibers is almost immune to electromagnetic interference and transmission loses
are low compared to other mediums (radio, copper). The other aspect of optical trans-
mission is, that as long as the fiber itself is of high quality, the capacity of the network
can be adjusted by replacing the terminal equipment, changing the modulation schemes
and channel widths. This in turn makes the upgrades easier, as they do not require
laying new infrastructure.
Optical Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) networks are concerned with creat-
ing connections (light-paths) between terminal equipment purely in the optical domain.
As the name suggests, multiplexing is done based on wavelengths and enables multiple
light-paths to exist in one physical fiber. More details about spectrum division available
in Section 2.3.3. Additionally, the spectrum within a light-path can be further shared
by utilising Time Division Multiplexing (TDM). The framing used to transmit data
for some time have been that of SONET/SDH, however as the capacity requirements

increased and packet traffic became more predominant, a new framing procedure was de-
veloped. The International Telecommunication Union (ITU) G.709[17] recommendation
defines a framing procedure known as a digital wrapper, which permits the transmission
of IP packets, Ethernet frames, ATM cells and SONET/SDH data over a synchronous
frame structure[18]. G.709[17], together with associated recommendations (overview of
which can be found in G.871[19]) defines what is commonly known as OTN - a future-
proof technology for long-distance, high-capacity transport networks. The OTN is log-
ically completely separate from the client network (eg. IP/MPLS) it serves. Therefore
this client network is only aware of the end-to-end connectivity provided. This section
provides a brief overview of OTN architecture and describes its functional elements.
2.3.1
OTN network architecture
Architecture of OTN is described in [20], while its functional elements in G.788.1[21].
The summary of layer division and network elements is presented in Figure 2.4. The
Optical Payload Unit (OPU), Optical Data Unit (ODU), and Optical Transport Unit
(OTU) reside in electrical domain, while all of the layers below (starting with Optical
Channel (OCh)) in the optical domain. Each of the electrical layers introduces its own
overhead information necessary to perform their functions, which are[22]:
• OPU encapsulates the client signal and does any rate justification that is needed.
This mapping is performed at the source, demapped at the sink, and not modified
by the network.
• ODU overhead is used for path and tandem connection monitoring, alarms and
maintenance messages,and Automatic Protection Switching (APS) coordination.
• OTU contains Forward Error Correction (FEC) and additional frame information.
After the OTU overhead is added, the signal is converted to optical domain. Some of
the devices presented in Figure 2.4 are:
• Network Termination Unit (NTU)
• Optical amplifier (also known as In-Line Amplifier (ILA))
• Optical Add-Drop Multiplexer (OADM)
• Optical Cross Connect (OXC)
NTU is a device which adapts client traffic (an action called also traffic grooming) to
ODUs and frames it accordingly. It would operate in all of the defined OTN layers.
The ILAs are responsible for regenerating the optical signal (Optical Transport Section
(OTS)) and operate only in OTS layer (although electro-optical regenerators are also
possible).

2.3.3
Frequency grid
An optical channel in the media layer can be defined by its central frequency and max-
imum spectral excursion[20] (the width of the channel). In Dense Wavelength Division
Multiplexing (DWDM) (which is used in OTN) the available spectrum in the optical
fiber is divided according to G.694.1[23]. The channel spacing commonly used in OTN is
50Ghz slots, with central frequencies of 193.1+n×0.05[THz]. With different modulation
schemes, either a 10G(Figure 2.7(a)), 40G(Figure 2.7(b)) or 100G(Figure 2.7(c)) signal
can be carried in each of those slots. Depending on which of the signals is carried, a pro-
portional part of the spectrum remains unused, with a 100G signal using it fully. Going
beyond 100G on a single optical channel with currently available modulation schemes re-
quires (depending on the scheme) either 75GHz(Figure 2.7(f)) or 150GHz(Figure 2.7(e))
slot.
A super-channel is a channel that consists of multiple sub-carrier slots, treated by the
network as a single entity. This idea is presented on Figures 2.7(e) and 2.7(f). Along
with a fixed spectrum described above, G.694.1[23] has also defined a so called flexible-
grid. The spectrum in the the fiber is divided into 12.5GHz slots (Figure 2.7(d)) with
central frequencies of 193.1 + n × 0.00625[THz]. Those slots can be flexibly assigned
to signals based on their required spectrum. This approach, when used correctly has
the potential to eliminate un-utilised spectrum, therefore improving spectral efficiency
of the transmission.
The modulation schemes presented in Figure 2.7 have varying
requirements regarding Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), and thus the maximum distances
that they can be used on before regeneration.

• Parallel reservation, where all of the Network Elements (NEs) reserve resources

• Hop-by-hop, where devices reserve resources in sequence (one after another). This

– Forward reservation (starting point is the request receiver)
– Backward reservation (starting point is the exit node of the connection)
The main advantage parallel reservation has over others is the connection set-up speed.
This aspect is studied further in Chapters 5 and 6.
2.4
Survivability in OTNs
Survivability is the ability of the network to continue operation in the face of failures.
It is especially important for core networks, as faults that occur there have considerable
impact. Some common failures are:
• Cable cuts
• Software bugs
• Hardware failures
• Power supply interruptions
==PAGE:25==
2.4 Survivability in OTNs
15
• Human errors
Majority of the failures can be attributed to physical cable cuts[35].
Methods of in-
creasing network survivability include, among the others, dual-homing and disjointness
of paths. One of the metrics describing survivability is a so-called availability, which
for any system can be calculated using Equation (2.1). Additionally, with A of each
network element along certain path known, the total availability can be calculated by
multiplying the As of all the elements (Equation (2.2)).
A = 1 −MTTR
MTBF
(2.1)
Atotal = A1 × A2 × ... × An
(2.2)
The first step of any survivability-enhancing scheme is to detect the presence of failure.
After that, actions are undertaken to mitigate that failure, usually starting at the lowest
possible layer (e.g. at OTS first). If, after a defined period the recovery is not successful,
the task is transferred to the higher layer. The reason for this behaviour is the fact, that
that failures at the lowest layer cause service interruptions all the way up to IP layer,
and successful low level recovery restores all network functions above.
The two classes of mechanisms used for failure mitigation are protection and restoration.
Protection uses pre-configured backup that is utilised as soon as failure is detected.
Restoration is a more reactive mechanism, that provisions backups only after failure
occurs. These required resources might be defined at the same time as the primary path,
in which case it is called pre-planned restoration. The main differences between the two
are in capacity usage and recovery speed, with protection being both faster and more
capacity-expensive. The set of metrics recognised for comparing recovery methods is[36]:
• Recovery percentage
• Operational complexity
• Recovery Speed
• Node resource usage
• Capacity usage
2.4.1
Protection schemes
Protection schemes can be divided as follows:
• Linear protection (a single span failure is mitigated by protecting that span)
– 1+1 protection, where traffic is sent simultaneously over two spans.
==PAGE:26==
2.5 Automatically Switched Optical Networks
16
– 1:1 protection, where a dedicated back-up span is predefined and used as
soon as a failure of primary span occurs. Backup span can be utilised by
other traffic while not required for protection.
– m:n protection - similar to the above, but m primary links can be configured
to use n backup paths (not dedicated).
• Ring topology offers additional possibilities for protection, such as:
– Optical unidirectional path sharing ring
– Two fiber optical bidirectional link sharing ring
– Four fiber optical bidirectional link sharing ring
• Mesh protection
– Span protection, where the last reachable node closest to the failure switches
to the backup span (Figure 4.4).
– Path protection, where the whole path between ingress and egress node is
recalculated (Figure 4.3).
– Local-to-egress protection, where path segment is recalculated from the node
closest to the failure to the egress node (Figure 4.5).
Although protection is faster and in some cases (1+1) safer to use, the desire to use the
most of the available capacity before upgrading the network (avoiding over-provisioning)
leads to increased interest in dynamic restoration schemes.

2.5.1
GMPLS as a control plane protocol in ASON
As mentioned above, the ASON architecture aims to be protocol and implementa-
tion independent.
There are different implementations available, the main one be-
ing GMPLS[39] based.
GMPLS is an extension of Multi-Protocol Label Switching
(MPLS)[40], that enables control of circuit switched OTN. The interfaces defined are:
1. Packet-switch capable
2. Time-division multiplex capable
3. Lambda-switch capable
4. Fiber-switch capable
With additional extensions defined in [41] call and connection control of OTNs becomes
possible. Those extensions enable switching of ODUs and OChs. GMPLS also requires a
signalling protocol - usually RSVP-TE or Constraint-based Routing Label Distribution
Protocol (CR-LDP) [18]. This suite of protocols fulfils the role of I-NNI[42] in the ASON.
Additional extensions defined in [30] and by Optical Internetworking Forum (OIF) made
it possible to also utilise it as UNI and E-NNI. GMPLS implies distributed control,
where every node stores a full link-state table of the network. This information is used
to set-up an explicit route using downstream on demand signalling scheme (which is in
fact backwards hop-by-hop reservation mentioned in Section 2.3.4). This process, (using
RSVP-TE) is presented in Figure 2.9.

2.6
SDN for OTN control
The centralised approach to control and management represented by SDN, together
with its emphasis on open interfaces resulted in an interest to adopt it to OTNs. PCEP
and OpenFlow have been at the forefront of those efforts, however their initial design
to handle packet switched networks had to be modified. PCEP has been extended to
support GMPLS (as mentioned in Section 2.2) and became capable of controlling circuit
switched networks. For OpenFlow, the main problems to solve were (after [45]):
1. Definition of a unified optical transport granularity
2. Design and implementation of an abstraction mechanism that hide intricacies of the
optical layer and present only the switching mechanism of the granularity defined
previously
3. Taking into account physical layer specific features of different optical transport
protocols
4. Cross technology constraints for bandwidth allocation and traffic mapping (groom-
ing)
A number of studies have been conducted over the past few years to investigate those
problems. Some of them will be summarised here.
Two distinct approaches can be taken in order to integrate SDN with OTN. One of them
is to employ DAL on every optical node that requires control, and expose its functionality
with either CPSI or MPSI. The other is to leave the detailed control of the optical layer
to the tools already present there and let the controller make request via some exposed
interface (e.g. UNI of the GMPLS ASON). The second approach had been investigated
in [46] and included an SDN controller extended with OpenFlow Gateway that interfaced
directly with UNI of the GMPLS controller. This approach enables use of SDN with
==PAGE:30==
2.6 SDN for OTN control
20
already established optical networks without the need of replacing/extending the optical
nodes with new technologies, which makes it suitable for brow-field deployments. The
drawback of leaving the legacy GMPLS control in place is the requirement of keeping
additional personnel to maintain it. Additionally, since the legacy control is distributed,
the time from submitting the request to the action being finished should be slower than
in the truly centralised system. What is more, the granularity of control available to the
SDN controller is limited to the parameters exposed by the UNI.
In contrast, exposing the functionality of each optical node is more in line with the SDN
architecture. For that to be possible, the CPSIs and MPSIs needed to be extended with
support of circuit switching (interfaces mentioned in Section 2.5.1) and each node in
the network needs to support those extentions. A discussion on possible extensions and
control scheme is presented in [43]. [45] is a very comprehensive study on UCP, which
investigates both integration possibilities presented in this chapter. It also proposes an
abstract way of integrating network nodes with OpenFlow, which consists of Hardware
Presentation Layer (HPL), Hardware Interface Layer (HIL) and OFAgent. HPL is re-
sponsible for presenting all of the hardware capabilities of the device to the HIL which
abstracts high-level features and information to the OFAgent. The implementation based
on that architecture (for both fixed and flexible-grid optical nodes) uses a custom-built
OFAgent and an SNMP interface that acts as HPL. The study concluded that pure
OpenFlow (OF) approach (without GMPLS) holds the advantage in term of path setup
time. This claim has been verified by this thesis in Chapter 6.
[47] proposes similar architecture and provides experimental results. The approach taken
is to use a virtual OpenFlow Switch as an OFAgent and translate OpenFlow messages
into TL1 messages in order to cross-connect optical nodes. Unfortunately, the exact
operations are left unexplained.
Since the studies presented above were conducted, the importance of achieving UCP
was recognised by Open Networking Foundation and the Open Transport project was
opened. It focuses on use of OF and SDN in transport networks and published a set of
documents concerning the topic, creating what is now known as T-SDN. This term is used
to describe an SDN architecture capable of controlling circuit-switched and connection-
oriented transport networks. The documents mentioned above are:
• [48] proposes the use scenarios for SDN with Optical Transport.
• [49] lists the requirements for integration of SDN based on OpenFlow with trans-
port networks.
• [50] presents the extensions to OF protocol based on the requirements listed in
[49].
• [51] and [52] propose the SDN system architecture for Transport Networks.
• [53] describes in detail what features should be made available by the Transport
API.

4.2
Dynamic restoration
As mentioned in Section 2.4, dynamic restoration is a reactive survivability technique,
trading (compared to protection) worse recovery times for better resource utilisation. In
a context of the given network, each protection scheme mentioned in Section 2.4 requires
up-front reservation of additional resources. With dynamic restoration it is possible to
calculate the backup path together with the primary path, but delay resource allocation
for the time it is actually needed. If the network is dynamic in its behaviour however,
each change would require a recalculation of all un-provisioned backup paths, to ensure
their availability when needed.
As the fiber-cuts are the most common cause for failures, this thesis considers only span
failures. For those, dynamic restoration can be based on the mesh protection schemes
enumerated in Section 2.4.1. All of the figures present in this section show the network
state before restoration on the left, and the state after on the right. Black colour is used
to indicate original paths, red to indicate which span failed. Other colours denote new
paths provisioned after restoration.

to as ROUTER ATS scenario.
4. Mapping is additionally permitted to the UPM zone of all router ODUs. Referred
to as ROUTER UPM scenario.
5. Mapping is additionally permitted to the CPM zone of all router ODUs. Referred
to as ROUTER CPM scenario.
Number of available TS is taken network-wide, meaning the sum of all available TS
across all ODUs is taken. Spectrum utilisation metric will show the difference between
maximally loaded node/link before and after restoration process. The restoration ratio
is the ratio of fixed service TS that were successfully restored to the number of fixed
service TS affected by failure. This is the same as dividing the number of Client ODUs
restored bt Client ODUs failed, with the weights depending on the ODU level. Additional
qualitative metrics estimated and used in Chapter 6 are:
• Restoration speed
• Complexity
• Predictability
• Optimum Capacity Utilisation
5.2
Assumptions and design choices
A number of assumptions was made in order to simplify the modelling process:
1. Links are unidirectional - links in the reverse direction are declared as a separate
objects.
==PAGE:45==
5.2 Assumptions and design choices
35
2. The failure of a link in one direction does not automatically fail the the link in
the reverse direction. This contradicts the architecture of network presented in
Chapter 3, where span failure would result in failure of both links. However since
all Links and Paths are reserved in a symmetrical way (a reverse direction object
will always be present and exactly reverse), the analysis of unidirectional failures
is still valid.
3. Dynamic restoration is considered in isolation, meaning no protection services are
implemented in the model.
4. The spectrum available at the fiber is unlimited. No blocking is possible at the
OCh level. It was verified, that for no scenarios the number of assigned 50GHz
slots in a single fiber is more than 80 (Chapter 3). This makes the obtained results
valid and the assumption unnecessary.
5. Power level adjustment for a single λ (see Section 5.3) can be conducted at the
ROADMs in parallel, if the signalling process supports it.
6. Only the fixed service pipes are restored during dynamic restoration process at
ODU level.
The reason for that is the fact, that ODU4 router tunnels would
always require a completely free OTU4/OCh trail (it is impossible to restore them
at ODU level).
7. During restoration, the fixed service ODU0/ODU1/ODU2 are mapped to available
router ODU TS as they are, without considering how much of their capacity is
currently used.
8. The link between GBG3 and ORE2 is considered non-existent (disabled).
9. The Maximum Permitted Rate (MPR) parameter is set to be equal to Maximum
Rate (MR).
10. Alarm Rate (AR) parameter is not implemented - no preemption and search for
alternative path occur.
11. Traffic Guard Margin is set to 0.
12. Link utilisation for all router ODUs is set hourly (Figure 5.1 derived from Fig-
ure 4.6).
13. The analysis for CPM zone is taken at the minimum, maximum and average value
of router ODU utilisation, respectively 0.1, 0.66 and 0.39.

5.3
Path set-up time process
As the metric used to evaluate Control Plane performance is time based, only the ele-
ments influencing time are included in the modelling process. The factors that contribute
to it are (from the time network receives the request are):
1. Path computation time
2. Signalling delay
• Serialisation delay on the DCN links
• Propagation delay on the DCN links
• Processing delay at the nodes
3. Resource reservation time
The resource reservation time is the main factor that differs between OCh and ODU
levels. At OCh level, the following processes take place:
1. Reconfiguration of WSS, which takes about 10ms.
2. Tuning of the wavelength source, which takes about 10s.
3. Power level adjustment along the new trail, which can take couple of minutes for
each ROADM traversed.
4. Global power level optimisation, which can take couple of hours, however can be
performed during service operation.
==PAGE:47==

The vendor of the network specifies, that the reconfiguration of each node at OCh level
(together with processing delay) takes less than 3s. At ODU level it would be assumed,
that reconfiguration will take less than 100ms, since no actions in the optical layer are
required. Without a detailed model of the DCN, the same signalling delays and path
computation times will be assumed. Additionally, power level adjustment is assumed
to be possible to be conducted in parallel. With that, the difference between GMPLS
and OpenFlow based control plane comes down to reservation schemes. The GMPLS
uses downstream-on-demand reservation, and its path set-up time can be expressed with
Equation (5.1). OpenFlow can use fully parallel reservation scheme, and its path set-up
time can be expressed with Equation (5.2).

5.4
OTN Network Model
Due to the packet-oriented focus of OPNET (limited support for circuit switching), the
OTN network presented in Section 3.1 was implemented in a single-node and single-
process OPNET project. The Universal Modelling Language (UML) Class diagram for
the model is presented in Figure 5.2. The four major classes are:
• Node - representing an OTN NTU with switching capabilities in both optical and
electrical layer.
• Link - representing a single fiber.
==PAGE:48==
5.4 OTN Network Model
38
• OCh - representing an optical channel with a specified spectrum utilisation.
• ODU - representing an end-to-end connection in the electrical layer.
Node objects are interconnected by Link objects. OCh objects represent light-paths that
are switched by the Nodes and traverse Link objects. ODU objects are assigned to OCh
objects. As a result the OTN Layer stack from Figure 2.4 and the mapping procedure
from Section 2.3.2 was simplified to Link/Node objects at the physical layer, OCh objects
at the optical layer, and ODU objects at the electrical layer. Furthermore, lower level
client ODU objects can be mapped to top level ODU objects. The detailed description
of each attribute and method used is available in the comments of the attached model
source code (see Appendix B). The current state of the network is represented as a set
of maps, that store all currently active (not failed) objects, namely:
• node_map, containing Node objects, with node.id being the key.
• link_map, containing Link objects, with a Cantor pair[55] of interconnected Nodes
being the key.
• och_map, containing the lists of OCh objects for each source - destination pair in
the network. Derived from Tables A.1 to A.3.
• odu_map, containing the lists of all ODU objects for each source - destination pair
in the network.
• client_odu_map, containing the lists of client ODU objects for each source - des-
tination pair in the network. Derived from Tables A.4 to A.9.
• router_odu_map, containing the lists of router ODU objects for each source - des-
tination pair in the network. Derived from Table A.10.
• top_level_odu_map, containing the lists of top level ODU objects for each source
- destination pair in the network. Derived from Tables A.1 to A.3.
The OTN model makes it possible to model the processes of failure and restoration,
and study them in a step-by-step fashion. The λ-routing is based on Dijsktra algorithm
running on graph from Figure 3.1 with Link objects as edges. Edge weight at this level
is set to 1, so the cost is always equal to hop-count. If multiple shortest paths exist,
predicting which one will be chosen is impossible. At ODU level Dijsktra algorithm
runs on graph from Figure 3.2, with primary ODU objects as the edges. Edge weight
is set to the inverse of Tributary Slots currently available for mapping at this ODU.
This behaviour models routing purely in electrical domain, without commissioning new
λ. ODU graph depends on the physical one, since a link failure will disable all of the
OChs (and thus primary ODUs) traversing that link.

5.5
Test cycle
With the network created, each Link goes through process described in Figure 5.3. All of
the OChs traversing the currently failed Link are removed from global maps mentioned
above, and also from Links and Nodes they traverse. Consequently, the primary ODU
(together with all of the client ODUs that are mapped to it) assigned to each of the
affected OChs is also removed from the global maps. This allows for accurate resource
utilisation calculations. The restoration procedure is different for each level of restoration
(either OCh or ODU), and is based on the process described in Section 4.2. In both
cases Dijsktra algorithm is used to find the alternative path. The reversion procedure
reverts the network to the state from before restoration procedure, destroying any objects
that were created during restoration. The recovery procedure returns the network to its
original state.
Figure 5.3: Test procedure for each link.
5.5.1
OCh restoration procedure
Graph from Figure 3.1 is used to find an alternative path at OCh level for each of the
OChs affected by the failure. The edge weight is set statically to 1, so the shortest path is
always the one with the least hop-count. In the event of multiple shortest paths existing,
the first one found is chosen (there is no way of predicting which one). New OCh objects
are created and the primary ODUs of the failed OChs are temporarily assigned to them
and reallocated in the network. This process is implemented in och_restoration()
function, and reverted by revert_och_restoration() function.
5.5.2
ODU restoration procedure
Graph from Figure 3.2 is used to find an alternative path at ODU level. This is done
for each client ODU affected by the failure, in the order of ODU2 →ODU1 →ODU0.
The edge weight is set to the inverse of currently available TS in order to maximise
the probability of the shortest-path being the one with the most available TS. With
the alternative path found, each top level ODU belonging to it is checked for sufficient
number of TS to accommodate considered client ODU, and if all of them pass, the
==PAGE:51==
5.6 Discrete Event Simulation Model
41
client ODU is temporarily assigned to new top level ODUs. Successfully restored ODUs
are reallocated. This process is implemented in odu_restoration_2() function, and
reverted by revert_odu_restoration() function.
5.6
Discrete Event Simulation Model
Discreet Event Simulation (DES) process model is the top level entity in OPNET, allow-
ing for event driven simulation in time. It is implemented as a state machine presented
in Figure 5.4. For the purpose of this thesis, the network model from Section 5.4 is fully
implemented in code, so the Node and Topology levels of OPNET Modeller are not used.
Most of the processes implemented are deterministic in nature, so it is not necessary to
use statistical analysis to obtain the results. As such, most of the results presented in
Chapter 6 were obtained by static analysis before actually exiting the INIT state of DES
model.
Nevertheless, DES model allows for both path set-up time computation (without actually
reserving any resources) and analysis of failure impact in simulation time. The only
parameter that changes in time is the utilisation of router ODUs, implemented as a
global utilisation percent for each hour in the day (as mentioned in Section 5.2). The
only scenario this is important for is ROUTER CPM, however even here static analysis
for minimum, maximum and average value of utilisation proved to be more illustrative.
As such, DES model can be treated as a ground for further improvements mentioned
in Section 5.9, especially for more exact calculation of restoration times and results of
feeding different traffic patterns. For those, the statistical analysis would be relevant.

5.9
Model shortcomings
The model presented in this chapter proved to be a useful tool for obtaining the desired
performance indicators. Due to some of its flaws however, some of results are not as
==PAGE:61==
5.9 Model shortcomings
51
precise as they could have been. This section describes those flaws and proposes possible
improvements.
For path set-up times, no exact DCN topology is available, so the signalling delays are
set to be static. With the addition of DCN to the model, the path set-up times would
be brought closer to their real values.
Another set of improvements would make the model more general and applicable to
different (bigger) OTNs. The assumption of unlimited spectrum in fiber is the first one
that should be removed. Although for the case study the limit of available spectrum (80×
50GHz) was not reached, a safety procedure checking that should be added. Additionally,
routing at OCh level could be modified to be similar to the one at ODU level, so the
shortest path will be the one with more available resources (spectrum). This however
adds an issue of deciding what an optimal path is. The choice is between three options:
the one with least hop-count, most resources or lowest delay. All of these can be added
as attributes to the model.
The results obtained with the assumption of unidirectional links are valid for the metrics
used. This is true, because a failure of a link in one direction triggers restoration proce-
dure in that direction, using defined resources. A failure of the reverse link triggers the
restoration procedure using the resources in the reverse direction. Modelling the span
failure as a bidirectional link failure would not change the results obtained in this thesis,
however it would reflect the real life behaviour of the system and might prove necessary
for other metrics.
There are also issues with the programming implementation of the model, such as the
simplification of mapping procedure from Section 2.3.2. The classes and methods used
can be further generalised and modularised, in order to enable easier changes to the
model. It is possible to do that by adding parent Network class with methods allowing
for management of the objects within. Additional TrafficGenerators could be used to
vary the traffic generation process, which is now done in a static way. Those changes
would allow for designing scenarios in simulation time, for which statistical analysis
would be desirable
The single most important improvement however would be the one allowing for obtaining
exact restoration times. This would require adding request queues in each node and
more precise definition of processing and reservation procedures. Exact model of the
DCN mentioned earlier would also be required here. With dynamic restoration times
the availability of the system could be calculated and compared between different levels
of restoration and to protection schemes.
In the end, the changes presented here would improve the quality of the model, however
most of them would not impact the results presented in the next chapter.

6.1
Path set-up times
Path set-up times are presented in Figure 6.1. It can be observed, that at both ODU and
OCh levels the benefits of parallel reservation scheme is a set-up time independent from
the number of nodes traversed. In contrast, with serial downstream on demand scheme
it depends on that number linearly. Additionally, the setup time at the ODU level is
on the order of tens of milliseconds, while at the OCh level on the order of hundreds
of seconds. This result confirms, that dynamic restoration is much better suited for the
ODU level, while at the OCh level fast protection schemes should be favoured. Since
the examined network would use PCEP/RSVP-TE/GMPLS stack for signalling, serial
set-up times are applicable to it. The results obtained here are a basis for estimating
restoration times in Section 6.4.3.

OCh Level
At OCh level, no traffic mapping is permitted. With the assumption of infinite avail-

to oslo5 Link raised the maximum utilisation of certain Node and Link by 350GHz.
Studying och_restoration_report.txt reveals, that it brought jon3 to stkm3 Link
from 1600GHz to 1950GHz, and stkm3 node from 4200GHz to 4550GHz. The maximum
observed link load (for all failures) is still within the limit of 80×50GHz set in Section 3.1,
so even with the assumption of unlimited spectrum the results presented here are valid.
It also has to be noted here, that the maximally loaded Link and Node do not have to
be the same before and after restoration.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
At the time of writing this thesis a significant effort has already been put into integrat-
ing Software-Defined Networking and Optical Transport Networks. Vendors continue to
expand the support for SDN features in their devices in order to enable Network Virtual-
isation as described in Chapter 1. They market devices as SDN ready and tout attractive
benefits and services, such as Bandwidth on Demand, centralised management and au-
tomation. The fundamental question this thesis tried to answer is, if with the static
nature of OTNs the initial investment of enabling SDN support is worth the potential
benefits. For that, it was necessary to quantify those benefits for a specific network and
express them in a way that is easy to understand. The model that is one of the main
products of this thesis allows for that in some limited capacity. The services presented
in Chapter 4 are possible to implement due to advancements in technologies presented
in Chapter 2. Fundamentally, this thesis proposes one service (Flexible traffic mapping)
that allows for making one underutilised resource (Tributary Slots) available for two
other presented services (BoD and dynamic restoration). It has to be emphasised, that
those are only two of many potential services that may use this newly freed capacity. It
is author’s conviction, that the results presented in Chapter 6 can be treated as an incen-
tive to enable SDN support in the examined network and put effort into implementing
presented services. The business viability of doing so is left unanswered.
Future work
There are three directions that can be taken in order built on the project presented in this
thesis. One of them is to concentrate on fixing model issues presented in Section 5.9 and
expand its capabilities in order to create a full fledged OTN model allowing for statistical
analysis of processes. The initial effort should be put into obtaining restoration times
(since the framework for that is already present in the DES model) and improving the
code-base to support easy modifications and use with other OTNs.
The second one is to implement the Flexible traffic mapping service with real devices
and software components.
The initial effort should be set on the less complex and
more predictable UPM mapping. Working implementation is very important for further
promotion of SDN and Network Virtualisation, since it proves that some of the promised
benefits are actually achievable.
The third and final one is a further research of the CPM mapping. Traffic prediction
techniques, optimal values for parameters defined in Section 4.3 and network behaviour
==PAGE:72==

62
in edge cases (e.g.
preemption) are all problems that should be investigated before
implementation of CPM mapping is possible.
Closing remarks
It is author’s belief, that it is best to view SDN and Network Virtualisation as enablers
for operators and developers. Different levels of abstraction make it possible for people
without an in-depth networking knowledge to design and implement services. A primary
example of that might be the modelling process conducted in this thesis. It was done at
the highest possible level of abstraction without the necessity of actually dealing with
signalling and complex configuration procedures of optical devices. Those were assumed
to be present, in the same way an SDN service level programmer would have access
to protocol implementations and information model.
The characteristics of Network
Virtualisation approach from Chapter 1 were:
1. Decoupling of functions/services and hardware platforms
2. Network slicing
3. Use of open protocols and standards
4. Complexity abstraction (layered approach)
5. Focus on optimal resource utilisation
6. Automated deployment, administration and orchestration
All of those were present in some capacity during project realisation. The service logic
(designed at the highest level of abstraction - 4) is expected to be executed on a cen-
tralised controller/orchestrator (1) and signalled to remote nodes, regardless of the actual
vendor of those nodes (1). The protocols and architectures used are publicly available (3)
and path calculations were conducted on two separate graphs, one of which was purely
virtual (2). The goal of the Flexible traffic mapping service itself was to optimise the use
of resources (5). Finally the BoD service is expected to automatically provision required
capacity and bill the client without need for human interaction (6). All of these prove,
that even though virtualisation (in traditional sense of the word) of transport networks
is impossible, the virtualisation principles are still applicable and very much useful for
them.
