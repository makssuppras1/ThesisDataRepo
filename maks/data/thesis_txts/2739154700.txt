Abstract
Microneedles have emerged as a promising technology for enhancing drug delivery systems.
This thesis explores the feasibility of fabricating hydrogel forming microneedles (HFMNs) using
blade coating. Each phase of the process was meticulously examined for enhancements and
optimization.
The study was conducted in six parts: fabrication of solid microneedles, negative mold fabrica-
tion and optimization, surface modification, Polyethylene Glycol Diacrylate (PEGDA) surface
tension calculations, drug loading in hydrogel, and hydrogel micromolding.
The fabrication of solid microneedles (SMNs) was done by microprinting, with a focus on needle
design to enhance penetration and make removal challenging, thereby extending therapeutic
duration and ensuring optimal drug release. During the printing process, various parameters
were systematically explored to achieve swift printing while maintaining the highest possible
resolution. The products were later used as master molds for the fabrication of HFMNs.
Efforts were directed towards optimizing the negative mold fabrication process to attain unifor-
mity, facilitating scale-up to pilot-scale production.
A brief study was conducted on surface modification parameters for the negative molds.
Additionally, an in-depth study was conducted on PEGDA hydrogel, covering its physical prop-
erties and eﬀicacy in drug delivery applications.
Ultimately, PEGDA was used for microneedle fabrication via blade coating, a rapid method
that does not require vacuum or centrifugation, showcasing a promising avenue for advancing
microneedle technology in drug delivery system.

1
Introduction
This chapter aims to provide insights into the obstacles faced by new-generation drugs in drug
delivery, the various delivery routes being explored, and the specific delivery route utilized
in this thesis. It will also discuss microneedles as a delivery mechanism. Subsequently, the
design parameters of microneedles will be examined. Finally, an overview of the microneedle
manufacturing process will be summarized, along with the project’s aims and objectives.
1.1
Challenges in drug delivery
This section provides an overview of the challenges faced by new-generation drugs, along with
traditional administration pathways and emerging non-invasive methods.
Macromolecular Drugs challenge.
Medications based on large molecules, also known as
macromolecules, are becoming increasingly crucial for the treatment of many diseases and con-
ditions due to their higher specificity in targeting and function. These large-molecule biophar-
maceutical products, which include proteins, peptides, and nucleic acids, are often referred to
as ”next generation” medicines [1].
The oral route is widely preferred for drug delivery; however, it faces significant challenges for
macromolecules. These include poor absorption through the epithelial membrane, susceptibility
to destruction by gastrointestinal acids and enzymes, and relative insolubility, all of which limit
bioavailability and targeting. To address these issues, innovative approaches such as nanopartic-
ulate formulations, biomimetic drugs, and microfabricated devices are being explored. However,
these methods are still in early stages and come with the downside of considerable cost increases
[2][3].
Consequently, large-molecule drugs are typically administered via the parenteral route through
injections, avoiding the first-pass metabolism.
This method, while effective, is painful and

1.2
Buccal mucosa
Among the various non-invasive routes under exploration, buccal drug delivery holds immense
potential. The buccal mucosa, lining the inner cheek, offers several advantages.
Unlike oral delivery, it bypasses the liver’s first-pass metabolism and the harsh gastrointestinal
environment, ensuring higher systemic bioavailability. Although its permeability is moderate
compared to sublingual delivery, the buccal mucosa boasts a larger surface area and longer
residence time. Moreover, its network of blood vessels facilitates rapid absorption directly into
the bloodstream, aided by high blood velocity [6].
Buccal formulations, like tablets or films, are non-invasive, patient-friendly, and easy to ad-
minister without pain, enhancing patient adherence and treatment outcomes. Consequently,
leveraging the buccal route for peptide and protein drug delivery presents a promising opportu-
nity to address the limitations of injections and improve therapeutic eﬀicacy [5][6].
The buccal route faces a significant limitation due to its narrow molecular weight range for
absorption. High molecular weight drugs frequently struggle to penetrate and are instead dis-
persed in saliva. Consequently, recent studies have pursued various strategies to address these
challenges, such as employing permeation enhancers or microneedles [8][9].
Moving forward, the exploration of microneedles for buccal drug delivery holds potential.

1.4.2
Shape
The influence of shape on the physical properties of microneedles (MN) has been extensively
studied by various researchers. Among the studied shapes, the conical microneedle is the most
commonly discussed in publications. Its popularity stems from its simplicity and stable form,
allowing it to be fabricated using multiple fabrication approaches. However, despite its popular-
ity, this shape presents drawbacks. Its tapered design poses challenges for penetration, lacking a
cutting blade to initiate the process. As a result, penetration begins with the incision of the tip,
followed by the dilation of the pore. This sequential procedure can be painful and regressive, as
the skin naturally tends to revert to its relaxed state. This means that for the patch to work it
must be pressed against the tissue [15][16].

1.4.4
Microneedle length
The length of microneedles (MNs) typically ranges from 150 to 2000 µm, ensuring direct pene-
tration through the epidermis layer to the capillary system of the skin in dermal tissue. However,
careful optimization of MN length is crucial. If the MNs are too long or fragile due to weak
materials, insertion forces may exceed the ultimate tensile forces, leading to MN breakage. MN
length must be at least 900 µm if they aim to reach the skin’s interstitial fluid [29].
1.4.5
Material
Ideal microneedle materials should exhibit biocompatibility, non-immunogenicity, suﬀicient me-
chanical strength, and the ability to carry large and complex drugs without causing damage.
Commonly used materials for HFMs include cross-linked polymers, especially those synthesized
via esterification reactions. These polymers display varying degrees of polymerization, leading to
diverse water absorption and swelling properties. This variability influences the rate of swelling
post-microneedle insertion into the skin, thereby affecting drug release profiles, including burst
and sustained release [30].
In previous projects within this research group, the material used for making HFMN was
polyethylene glycol diacrylate (PEGDA), which was crosslinked using 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropiophenone.
This material exhibited good drug loading and release properties, and tunable swelling [31][13].

2.1
Mastermold fabrication
Depending on the desired properties of the microneedles and the scalability of the process,
various techniques can be employed for the fabrication of the master mold.
Fancier needle

The most commonly used manufacturing methods for microneedles typically fall into the cat-

Additionally, there is also
another category called formative manufacturing, which includes techniques such as Blowing

2.1.1
Substractive Manufacturing
- Micromachining: This involves processes where material is removed from a workpiece to create
the desired shape. Techniques like micromilling, turning and drilling. It is challenging to achieve
extremely sharp tips with a diameter smaller than 10um using micromilling [33][34].
- Laser engraving: CO2 laser cutter can be used to engrave a negative mold on a PMMA sheet
that can be subsequently used to make a positive master mold [35].
2.1.2
Formative manufacturing
- Drawing lithography: Makes use of the tensile and elongating properties of viscous fluids.
Micro-needle structures are formed by contacting and stretching viscous polymer droplets in
some cases with the use of heat. The shape cannot be freely controlled and the mechanical
strength is weak [36].
- Centrifugal lithography: Polymer drops are dispensed onto an inner plate and subjected to
centrifugal force, elongating them towards an outer parallel plate, forming an hourglass shape.
Centrifugal evaporation then thins and solidifies the hourglass shape, allowing it to be separated
into two symmetrical needle patches [37].

These PVA microneedles are created in two steps. First, PVA solutions are blended with a
drug, and the mixture is molded and dried at 50°C to form the microneedle tip. Next, the outer
layer is molded using the same polymer composition without the drug. The needles are then
demolded and dried at room temperature. Various formulations include carboxymethyl cellulose

the rate and extent of water uptake. DMSO reduces viscosity and promotes the formation of a
porous structure, which increases the swelling ratio and release rate. Notably, higher molecular
weight PVA results in a lower swelling ratio [49].
2.6
Surface modification
In the context of micromolding on a PDMS surface, the contact angle is a crucial parameter
to consider. A high contact angle indicates poor wettability, making it diﬀicult to fill small
spaces because the liquid forms large, aggregated droplets. Therefore, it is essential to reduce
the contact angle between the substrate and the hydrogel to ensure proper filling and achieve
accurate molding results.
Considering a sessile drop on an ideal homogeneous solid surface as shown in Figure 2.5, three
different interfaces exist and they are described by the corresponding surface free energies: γSL
is the solid-liquid interfacial free energy, γSV is the solid-vapor interfacial free energy, and γLV
is the liquid-vapor interfacial free energy.
The liquid is attached to the solid surface at an
equilibrium contact angle, θ. Each of the interfaces describes the contact line in a manner so

that the corresponding interfacial area is minimized. By looking at the balance of forces along
the x-direction, we obtain the following relation:
γSV = γSL + γLV cos θ

The Young equation shows the relationship between the equilibrium contact angle with the
surface free energies. If water contact angle (WCA) < 90°, the surface is hydrophilic which
indicates that it has a wetting effect and if WCA > 90°, then the surface is hydrophobic which
indicates that it has a non-wetting effect.
Among the factors that affect the contact angle, the first one that can be controlled is the solid
surface free energy (γSV ). In general, surface properties are primarily determined by surface
chemistry and surface roughness. Surfaces with low surface energy are typically hydrophobic,
while surfaces with high surface energy are usually hydrophilic. Additionally, increasing sur-
face roughness tends to amplify these characteristics: hydrophobic surfaces become even more
hydrophobic, and hydrophilic surfaces become even more hydrophilic [50].
Chemical Composition: Typically what increases the γSV is the polarity of the surface The mold
has to match the properties of the substrate in terms of surface energy or polarity.
The PDMS surfaces can be activated, coated or chemically modified.

Design overview
This subsection introduces the various microneedle designs outlined in the thesis and their

Tower and obelisk shape needle.
Figure 3.3: Tower shape and obelisk shape needle designs on Fusion360
From previous studies carried out by Christina Richelsen Schulz [13], it was found that a cylin-
drical shape with a conical end named “Tower Shape” (TS) with a pitch of 1mm was the best
candidate in terms of penetration force and volume per needle, compared to a cylindrical or a
conical shape (Figure 3.3, left).
For this reason, it was decided to use this design. It is also best to try more complex structures
once a simple structure like the Tower Shape has been optimized.
Later in the thesis it was decided to add a conical base for the tower shape to add stability and
to ease the needle casting on the PDMS. A very similar design was developed by Kim et al. [33]
which they called circular obelisk (Figure 3.3, right).
Christmas Tree (CT) Design.
This design mimicked the no-return mechanism that characterizes some microneedles found in
nature [24]. This was done by adding to the design some lateral structures that act as barbs
(Figure 3.4). Additionally, this design incorporates a blade-like shape in the tip to facilitate the
penetration and potentially reduce pain. As explained before, the cutting blade on the needle
reduces the force needed to rupture the tissue by 100 times, which potentially reduces pain. In
previous studies, carried out by Li et al. [19], it was found out that the geometry of the needles
played a crucial role in long term retention in the skin.

Inspecting print for explanations
The needle elongation can be explained by the viscosity of the resin. Being an incompressible
liquid, the resin prevents the membrane from laying completely flat over the entire surface of
the design when attempting a 5 µm layer thickness. As mentioned before, the printer can be
overridden to achieve layers as thin as 5 µm. However, this is only feasible when the design has
a small printing section. As the printing section increases, the pressure the membrane can exert
decreases, making it more challenging to drain the viscous resin between the membrane and the
print as the platform ascends, which complicates layer slippage [54].
This particular design features an enclosed section with a projection area of 105mm2.
The
enclosed region effectively makes the area that needs to be flattened five times larger because
the excess resin must flow out of the enclosed region.
Consequently, this excess resin accumulation could contribute to the elongation of the needles.
To prove this hypothesis, a high-magnification SEM analysis is conducted over the layers at
different heights of the needle for the first print (Figure 4.2, left).

Materials
• Polyethylene Glycol diacrylate (PEGDA) (average 700 Da) and 2-Hydroxy-2-methylpropiophenone

Methods
A solution comprising 80% PEGDA, 15% water, and 5% 2-Hydroxy-2-methylpropiophenone was
formulated.
Subsequently, sample molds underwent a 15-minute treatment with oxygen plasma using both
pure oxygen and air. This was done by degassing the sample chamber to a pressure of 0.8 mbar
and after turning on the plasma, slowly adding a gas inlet to keep the pressure stable and the
plasma with a pink coloration. The inlet was either oxygen from a tank or atmospheric air.
Following the treatment, a droplet of the PEGDA solution was deposited onto the surfaces of
two treated molds and one untreated mold, after which the contact angle was measured.

method subsequently adopted for all future procedures. Contact angle was measured before and
after plasma treatment as a proof of concept. The angle was considerably reduced from 110º to
24º. The maximum droplet height was 49µm (Table 5.1).
From this viewpoint, it becomes feasible to fabricate needles with a diameter of 300 microme-
ters under atmospheric pressure provided the surface exhibits hydrophilicity. The subsequent
challenge lies in overcoming the surface tension of the hydrogel. Small droplets have a tendency
to aggregate, forming a large meniscus, making it considerably more diﬀicult to fill the confined
space without trapping air inside [56].

7.2
Methods
The experimental design was carefully crafted to extract valuable insights eﬀiciently. To expedite

prepared aqueous solutions. The mixture was homogenized with a pipette, and all components
were shielded with aluminum foil to protect them from UV light.
The solutions were then crosslinked for 30 seconds in a UV chamber using 30% of 365 nm and
405 nm light sources. Subsequently, a calibration curve of AFI in PBS solution was prepared
and introduced into a fluorescence 96-well plate.

Conclusions
This research explored the potential of UV-assisted micromolding for fabricating microneedle
patches intended for buccal drug delivery.
In the initial chapter, while designing and fabricating microneedles, it was observed that all
attempted needle configurations could be rapidly printed. However, during the optimization of
the print for the Box Shape mold, it became apparent that achieving an enclosed shape with
5um printing was unfeasible due to inadequate resin drainage, resulting in thicker and uneven
layers. Nonetheless, the reduction in the number of images compensated for the elongation. The
incorporation of round corners and spacing between models minimized the suction cup effect,
thus preventing layer rising and print failures.
Among the approaches investigated, the Wall-less mold emerged as the most promising.
It
effectively addressed issues related to layer thickness and facilitated the integration of separate
components, enabling PDMS casting with high precision and ensuring thickness uniformity, as
elucidated in the findings of the second chapter.
PDMS molding proved successful for the majority of microneedle designs (CT1-CT5), partic-
ularly those characterized by less sharp edges and minimal overhangs. However, the presence
of sharper features in specific designs (CT6) led to increased demolding resistance, resulting in
approximately 50% of needles fracturing at the base.
Following successful fabrication of PDMS molds, oxygen plasma treatment rendered them hy-
drophilic, enabling subsequent molding with PEGDA. However, surface tension within the
PEGDA hindered complete filling of small gaps, and attempts to mitigate this using surfac-
tants (PVA and Taurocholic acid) proved ineffective. Notably, the drug release profile from the
microneedles exhibited a constant rate, indicating adherence to first-order kinetics.
In conclusion, this research successfully fabricated hydrogel microneedles using PDMS molds
for a range of designs (CT1-CT5). While incorporating a drainage hole in the mold ensured
complete filling, it also resulted in a slight compromise in needle sharpness. Molding remained
feasible in the absence of a drainage hole for certain needles, albeit with the presence of gaps
between the tip and base attributed to trapped air bubbles. These findings offer valuable insights
for the continued development and optimization of microneedle fabrication techniques within the
context of buccal drug delivery applications techniques for buccal drug delivery applications.

Future Steps
The next steps in the project of creating functional HFMNs for buccal drug delivery would be:
Testing Microneedles: Use a texture analyzer to test the penetration and retrieval of the mi-
croneedles made in the first chapter on a hydrogel matrix and comparing them with results from
the previous student. The goal is to determine whether the new microneedle designs with a
blade shape improve penetration and if the barbed structure enhances retention in the tissue.
Ideally, the testing specimens would be made from PEGDA, as was done before in a compatible
setting [13]. Additionally, the microneedles could be tested for long-term retention in a hydrogel
tissue model to assess the impact of barb size on retention during swelling.
Skin Tissue Model Testing: Test the needle patches for penetration on pig skin models. This
testing will evaluate real-time drug diffusion in the tissue and provide microscopical images of
tissue sections after insertion [61][62].
Improving microneedles: Depending on the results from the microneedle tests, the shape of the
master mold could be potentially improved by wet or dry etching [63] to give sharper edges and
reduce the penetration force and potentially the pain.
Characterizing Micromolding: Fully characterize the micromolding of HFMNs using flat PDMS
mold with holes in the bottom. Find the optimal compromise to achieve the most accurate
reproduction of the master mold on the patches with the backing layer.
Drug Release Testing: Test the release of the AFI drug (or potentially other drugs) from hydrogel
microneedles into a PBS medium and compare the results to the cube model tested in Chapter
7. This comparison will provide insight into the effect of surface area on drug release kinetics.

Each heading is detailed to clarify its significance in the resin printing process:
• Image: Specifies the number of layers in the printing process.
• Exposure time (s): Indicates the duration of exposure to Ultraviolet (UV) light for
curing the resin. The first layer typically requires a longer exposure time of 3 seconds to
ensure proper adhesion, from subsequent layers, the thicker ones (10µm or 50µm) require
2 seconds of exposure the thinner ones (5µm) only require 1 second.
• Intensity: Specifies the strength or power of the UV light exposure. The initial layer
usually requires a higher intensity for better adhesion to the platform.
• Platform down (mm): Describes the distance the platform descends between printing
layers.
• De-1 (s): Denotes the duration the platform remains down after descending.
• Platform up (mm): Indicates the height of the platform after it rises following the drop
process. This parameter helps determine the thickness of each printed layer.
• De-2 (s): Specifies the standby time after the platform rises and engages with the mem-
brane. This interval allows the resin to drain from under the membrane and enables the
membrane to settle flatly, contributing to better print quality.
• Scra: Indicates the frequency of using the scraper between layers to remove potential
bubbles or excess resin from the membrane, ensuring smoother and more uniform prints.
