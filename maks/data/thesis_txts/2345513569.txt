Abstract
The Data Center Networks (DCNs) have gained great scientiﬁc and academic
interest at the recent years, mainly due to the increasing traﬃc volume of the web
applications. Furthermore, the advance of the social networking services and the need
of the cloud computing, for maintaining huge amounts of information from several
ﬁelds on the Internet, encouraged this trend [1, 2]. Recently, the research has been
focused on providing higher bandwidth in the DCNs, in order to support the increas-
ing traﬃc demands, and more eﬃcient ways in terms of the power consumption [3].
The optical technologies within the DataCenters (DCs) have arisen and implemented
as a promising solution for both the decreased energy consumption and the higher
bandwidth that can provide [3].
Taking into consideration the research trends for the DCNs that have been men-
tioned above, the present MSc thesis is focusing on the Optical DCNs. A review
of the DCNs, in general, is given at the beginning and the optical technologies are
thoroughly investigated afterwards.
Diﬀerent DCN architectures are investigated
through the simulation tool SP Guru Transport Planner, and the introduction of op-
tical switches at diﬀerent layers of these topologies is evaluated. Speciﬁcally, diﬀerent
scenarios have been created for each of the architectures, where the optical switches
are implemented at one or more layers of the DCN, that is the core, the aggregation
and the access. The scenarios of each architecture are investigated and compared
in terms of the power consumption, the reliability, the resource utilization and the
cost-eﬀectiveness.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The current DCNs should both sustain and process huge amounts of information, due
to the massive increase of the Internet traﬃc that has been observed the recent years
[3]. The rise of the web-based applications, the social networking services and the
cloud computing, has driven to an essential need for high bandwidth, high reliable,
high utilized and low power consuming DCs [3]. The research interest has now been
focused on providing new solutions for the intra-DCNs in order for the high bandwidth
to be supported in an energy eﬃcient way without additional cost [4]. Numerous
projects have been developed in the direction of providing new approaches regarding
the DCs. As an example, the COSIGN project is focusing on the combination of the
optical technologies with the Software Deﬁned Networks (SDN) [2].
1.1
Project Scope
The objectives of the present thesis and the project’s overall structure is presented and
analyzed at this section. In Ch.2, the basic theory behind the DCNs is presented, and
the parameters under current investigation are deﬁned. Speciﬁcally, an overview of
the current DC architectures is given, where the topologies are categorized into ﬁxed
and ﬂexible. The diﬀerent types of traﬃc that exist regarding the DCNs are presented
next. The reliability analysis of the DCN follows, where various parameters that aﬀect
the network’s reliability, and diﬀerent ways of measuring it, are investigated as well.
Furthermore, the resource utilization of the DCN is discussed, as it is a parameter
that aﬀects both the energy footprint and the eﬀectiveness of the network. Regarding
the utilization, the parameters that aﬀect it, as well as diﬀerent approaches towards a
higher utilised network are analyzed. Next, the power consumption of the DCN, that

directly aﬀects the cost eﬀectiveness of the DC, is investigated, and diﬀerent ways of
deploying an energy eﬃcient network are discussed.
As the primary focus of the present project is on the optical DCNs, diﬀerent optical
technologies, and speciﬁcally optical networking equipment that is nowadays deployed
inside the DCNs, will be investigated on Ch.3. At the beginning, the diﬀerent types of
optical the switching techniques will be investigated. Additionally, the various types
of the optical ﬁbers that are mostly deployed inside the DCNs are presented. The
optical transceivers with their corresponding data rates are discussed at the end of
that chapter.
The type of the DC, and more accurately the type of the applications that are
accommodated inside the DCN, signiﬁcantly inﬂuences the original architecture that
should be deployed for the DC and the technologies that should be used inside. As a
consequence, the type of the DCN has a considerable impact on the cost, the network
reliability, the resource utilization and the power consumption of the DCN, and as
such they are analyzed in Ch.4.
The methodology that has been followed in order for the diﬀerent architectures to
be simulated is analyzed in Ch.5.2. At the beginning, the global parameters that have
been set for the simulation are discussed. Next, the architectures under simulation,
namely the Current, the Fat tree, the BCube, the DCell and the Ring are introduced.
For each scenario, that has been created for the mentioned topologies, the overall
design, that is the type of the deployed switches, the data rates and the traﬃc, is
presented. In this chapter, also, the topology that has been created for the veriﬁcation
and validation purposes regarding the simulation setup is introduced.
The results that have been obtained by the simulations will be presented and thro-
roughly investigated in Ch.6. Speciﬁcally, the scenarios that have been created for
each architecture will be compared in terms of the power consumption, the network
reliability, the resource utilization and the cost eﬀectiveness. The Bill of Materials
(BoM) outcome of the simulation tool provides information regarding the deployed
switches and the number of ports of the switches. Upon the mentioned information
and through relevant speciﬁcation datasheets that have been used, the power con-
sumption of the topologies is calculated. Next, the reliability of the network, that is
expressed as a percentage, has been obtained for speciﬁc reliability parameters of the
deployed networking elements. Regarding the resource utilization, both the link and
the node utilization are considered for the present project, as they contribute to the
overall performance of the architectures. The link utilization illustrates the number

Current DataCenter Architectures
As it has already been mentioned, the DCs accommodate a great volume of informa-

necessary [1]. Thus, the deployed topology of the DCNs, that is the physical topology

architecture can be observed on Fig.2.1. The absence of the aggregation layer at the

The topologies that have been described above present some substantial drawbacks,

2.2
Traﬃc in the DataCenter Networks
The new applications that are nowadays accommodated inside the DCNs, and specif-
ically the rise of the social networking services and the cloud services that are hosted
there, create huge amount of network traﬃc inside the DCN [15]. The information
regarding the present network traﬃc inside the DCN is valuable for the future im-
provement of the DCNs towards a more sustainable, reliable, highly utilized and cost
eﬀective network. This is mainly the reason why both the academia and the research
community have been focused on the traﬃc that is created from speciﬁc applications.
The DCN traﬃc ﬂows can be categorized into three main categories [16] :
• Traﬃc that remains inside the DC
• Traﬃc that is directed from one DC to another DC
• Traﬃc that is directed from the DC to the end users through the Internet
As most of the traﬃc is created between the servers within the DC, the traﬃc
inside the DCN is signiﬁcantly higher than the other types of traﬃc [15]. Depending
on the destination of the traﬃc that originates from the rack, it can be categorized into
intra-rack traﬃc (traﬃc between the servers in the same rack) and inter-rack traﬃc
(traﬃc to servers outside the rack) [7]. As far as the inter-rack traﬃc is concerned,
this ranges between 10% and 80% [7]. Regarding the intra-rack traﬃc, this is highly
dependable on the type of the applications and the services that are hosted inside the
DCN. Speciﬁcally, for educational organizations and private enterprises, the traﬃc
ranges from 10% to 40% and for cloud data centers it is around 80% [7, 24].

2.3
Reliability Analysis
The main objectives of the deployed DCN topologies are to provide high bandwidth
and low latency communication accompanied by high reliable networking components
[8].
hese parameters have a signiﬁcant impact on the overall performance of the
network [8]. In this section, the reliability of the DCN is discussed, with great focus
on the parameters that inﬂuence the network’s reliability.
In general, the reliability of a telecommunication network refers to the probability
that the network will continue its initial operation without failure, or the failure will
not aﬀect its overall performance over a given time period [8, 18]. Alike a typical
telecommunication network, the links, the servers and the switches of a DCN are
prone to failure [8]. Hence, the reliability of a DCN refers to its ability to maintain
its operation regardless the failures that emerge either from some of the network’s
elements [8], that is the copper or the optical ﬁber and the racks, or from the me-
chanical elements, such as the power and the cooling [19]. At the present thesis only
the networking reliability is of concern, as the reliability regarding the mechanical
equipment is out of the networking scope that is studied here. There are diﬀerent
ways of measuring the reliability of a speciﬁc topology, such as causing a link failure
or a device failure [20]. The last can be achieved by failing some of the network’s
components, such as the edges between speciﬁc switches in a random manner [21].
In this way the speciﬁc pair of racks will be disconnected and the reliability of the
network can be observed [21].
2.4

The resource utilization of the DCN is among these parameters that can signiﬁcantly
aﬀect the power consumption of the network and as a consequence the overall cost.
Speciﬁcally, the utilization of the DCN is deﬁned as the servers’ usage and is expressed
as a percentage most of the times [22]. The resource utilization in many current DCs
has been proved to be signiﬁantly low resulting in wasted resources.
The reason
behind this low utilization of the available resources arise from the fact that some
servers are running without actually having applications that are processing at the
full capability of the deployed equipment [22]. Also, the low utilization results from
the erroneous selection of the network topology and from the low link utilization [23].
Current studies have shown that the link utilization of the core layer is rather high,
while the utilization of the aggregate layer and between the links inside the rack is

3.1
Optical Switching
The increasing traﬃc demands and the high energy consumption [7] of the modern
DCs have gradually led to the outspace of the traditional electronic interconnection
schemes [35]. The need for high throughput, low latency and energy eﬃcient networks

imposes new interconnection schemes [7, 35] that are accompanied by reasonable costs
[36]. The optical interconnects seem to be a promising solution that could satisfy the
mentioned DCs’ demands [35] and provide the beneﬁts that have already been noticed
in the telecommunication networks [7]. In order for the full capabilities of the optical
technologies to be exploited, the modern DCN architectures deploy either all optical

3.1.1
Overview of optical switching
At this point, it is interesting to investigate in a greater extent the diﬀerent types of
the optical switching techniques that exist, in general, namely Optical Circuit Switch-
ing (OCS), Optical Packet Switching (OPS) and Optical Burst Switching (OBS), and
then investigate how these switching techniques can be utilized in the DCs.
The basic principle behind the optical switching is the transmission of the data
from end-to-end through the optical ﬁbre in the optical domain [38]. In the hybrid
scenarios, the information undergoes E/O and O/E conversion at the commodity
electronic switches in order to be transmitted through the optical medium [37]. On
the contrary, in the case of the all-optical switching scenarios, the information does
not undergo this conversion from the optical to the electrical domain and vice versa,
and the information [37] is transmitted entirely in the optical domain [38]. As such,
the all-optical switching techniques can signiﬁcantly contibute to a network with
higher transmission speed, due to the lack of the time-consuming conversions at the
electronic nodes, and reduce the power consumption, as these conversions are power
hungry [37].
3.1.1.1
Optical Circuit Switching
The principle behind the OCS lies on the wavelength routing [39]. Speciﬁcally, the
data are transmitted entirely in the optical domain through an optical path [39]. Each
lightpath on a speciﬁc link is settled end-to-end, from the source to the destination,
by assigning a dedicated wavelength [39].
For a network that is based on circuit-switching, a connection, basically the circuit,
needs to be established in order for the communication to begin [39]. A dedicated
wavelength is then established for the entire time of the connection between the source
and the destination [39]. The main drawback of this switching technique is the lack
==PAGE:30==
CHAPTER 3. OPTICAL TECHNOLOGIES INSIDE THE DATACENTERS
Page 19
of ﬂexibility [40, 39] and as a consequence the poor bandwidth utilization [40]. This
derives from the fact that at least one wavelength per connection needs to be reserved
for the communication between two devices [41] and cannot be shared with other ﬂows
[40, 41] even if the circuit is idle . Adittionally, although it is easy for a circuit to be
implemented [39], the conﬁguration time for the circuit establishment and the circuit
teardown is considerable and can also lead to a low network connectivity [41].
3.1.1.2
Optical Packet Switching
The main principle behind the OPS is based on the transmission of single packets
on an optical Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM) network [41]. The header
of each packet, that holds the information regarding the forwarding of the packet
[37], can be processed either on the optical or the electronic domain [41] at each
intermediate node, while the playload waits on the optical delay line in order to be
transmitted in the following node [39]. The signiﬁcant ﬂexibility that can be oﬀered
by this technology lies on the fact that the packet streams can be multiplexed based
on some probability and in this way the available bandwidth can be better utilized
[37].
Though, the extensive use of the OPS technology is restricted due to some limi-
tations that have been set by the technology regarding the optical buﬀers [41]. As it
has already been mentioned, the data playload needs to be stored in the buﬀer, while
the headers are processed [39]. Currently, the optical buﬀers can accommodate only
a few tens of packets on the ﬁber delay lines and cannot cope with periods of packet
contention [39].
A packet-switched network can be either slotted or unsoltted depending on the
way that the packets are formatted and processed [37]. In slotted packet-switched
networks, the packets should have the same size and are framed on a speciﬁc time slot
together with their header [37]. The synchronization of the packets at the input ports
of the switch is a necessary procedure that has to be followed [37]. On the contrary,
in a slotted packet-switched network, the size of the packets can be diﬀerent and the
sychronization is not needed, as the packets can arrive at any time to the switch [37].
3.1.1.3
Optical Burst Switching
The OBS is a hybrid technology that its main principle lies between the OCS and the
OPS [40]. As such, the OBS can compensate for the drawbacks of the two mentioned
technologies. The driving force behind this switching technique is the transmission of
==PAGE:31==
CHAPTER 3. OPTICAL TECHNOLOGIES INSIDE THE DATACENTERS
Page 20
high volume of data on an optical network without the need of circuit establishment,
but preﬀerably on the deployment of optical cross-connects (OXCs) that do not rely
on the optical buﬀers [42]. The main diﬀerentiation of the OBS is that it makes better
resource utilization and improves the network connectivity, as the bandwidth can be
shared among the nodes that accommodate bursty traﬃc [41]. The OBS combines
the advantages of both the electronics and the optics [37, 41], as the management and
the control are achieved electronically [37], while the bursts are optically switched in
the data plane [42, 37].
Regarding an optical burst-switched network, the bursty data can be transmitted
to any of the available links [37]. Each transmission link can be composed of multiple
WDM channels, of which, at least one should be the control channel [37]. The control
channel is responsible to manage the assignment of the rest of the channels to the
data bursts [37].
3.2
Optical ﬁbers inside the DataCenter
The deployment of optical ﬁbers has become a dominant technology for the trans-
mission inside the DCs [43]. This trend has been arisen from the fact that there is
a signiﬁcant increase in the information that has to be stored and managed in the
current DCs [44]. This growth can be handled by the bandwidth increase, as more
information can be transmitted per time unit [44]. The advance of the optical tech-
nologies has made the use of the optical ﬁber inside the DCs the optimum candidate
for meeting this goal [44]. Though, the deployment of an all optical ﬁber DCN is
not really common compared to the combination of the optical ﬁber with the copper
cable. As such, the optical ﬁber is deployed for speciﬁc applications inside the DCN
in order for the optimum results to be achieved [45].
As diﬀerent types of optical ﬁbers exist, the speciﬁc type of the deployed optical
ﬁber is based on the desired data rate and the reach length that needs to be met [46].
In general, depending on the number of the propagation modes inside the ﬁber, the
Multimode Fibers (MMFs) and the Single Mode Fibers (SMFs) exist [47]. Regarding
the SMF, only one mode is propagating inside the ﬁber [47], while in the MMF
more than one modes are copropagating inside the ﬁber [46].
In this section the
two fundamental types of the optical ﬁbers, namely the SMFs and the MMFs, are
discussed for their potential use inside the DCs in terms of the data rates that can
be achieved.
==PAGE:32==
CHAPTER 3. OPTICAL TECHNOLOGIES INSIDE THE DATACENTERS
Page 21
3.2.1
Multimode Fibers
The large core size of the MMFs (typically 50.0-62.5 µm) allows for multiple modes
to propagate simultaneously inside the ﬁber [46]. The scattering is occured when the
light enters the optical ﬁber and in this way the numerous modes travel alongside
[46]. The main transmission windows for the SMFs are the 850nm and the 1300nm
[46].
The MMFs in combination with VCSELs are suitable for the deployment inside
the DCs [46]. The reach of this technology is relatively short, especially when the
data rate is increasing [46], as it is discussed in greater detail below.
3.2.2
Single Mode Fibers
The SMFs have been introduced in order to overcome the limitations that have been
set by the MMFs, and speciﬁcally the short reach length [46] and the small bandwidth
of those [48].
The characteristic small core size (typically around 9.0 µm) of the SMF allows
only one mode to propagate inside the ﬁber [46]. The SMFs are suitable in the DCs
when the Distributed Feedback Lasers (DFBs) laser are deployed [46], and they are
suitable for operation at the wavelengths 1310nm and 1550nm [43]. This combination
can provide longer reach distances, as these types of lasers are less prone to dispersion
due to their narrow spectrum width [46].
3.3
Optical Transceivers and Data Rates
The transceivers are an integral component of the DCNs, as they are an Integrated
Circuit (IC) which are used for the transmission and the reception of the data between
the network switches [43]. The optical transceivers that are deployed in the optical
DCNs, and will be discussed in this section, use optical ﬁbre technology in order
for the communication between the devices to be achieved and high bandwidth links
to be created [43]. The type of the optical technologies that are deployed for the
optical transceivers, speciﬁcally the laser source, depends on the desirable reach and
the bit rate that needs to be achieved [49]. The prevalent optical technologies that
are currently deployed in the DCs are the Vertical Cavity Surface Emitting Lasers
(VCSELs) that are based on MMF for the transmission [43, 50] and the DFB that
the transmission is achieved through SMF [43], as it has already been mentioned.

3.3.1
VSCELs in the DataCenters
The VCSELs have considered to be a great choice for use in the optical transceivers of
the DCs due to the advantages that they present [51]. These advantages are basically
based on the normal emission of the laser to the surface [52]. Speciﬁcally, some of the
main features that they present is the low cost in combination with the low power
consumption [51].
The VCESLs typically deploy MMFs as a transmission medium in the DCs due to
the direct coupling that can be achieved because of their large core diameter (typically
50m or 62.5m [53]) [43] and their low cost [55]. However, the MMFs are limited by the
modal dispersion, as multiple modes propagate in the core with diﬀerent velocities
[54], and they are most suitable for short range communication [53] in order for the
high data rates to be achieved [43].
Additionally, the operating wavelength is a parameter that is of crucial importance
for the desired transmission rate and the distance inside the DC. Speciﬁcally, the
VCSELs that are deployed in the current DCs are operating at 850nm [56]. The
combination of VCSELs operating at 850nm with MMFs is really typical for the DC,
basically due to the low cost of the deployed technology [56]. Though, the transmission
speed that can be achieved by the majority of such links is less than 10Gbit/s [56],
and speciﬁcally it ranges from 1Gbit/s to 10Gbit/s [57]. As the DCs’ structures are
becoming larger [56] in order for the huge information to be accommodated, they
demand for higher data rates [58]. Speciﬁcally, the current DCs upgrade their speeds
at the access and the aggregation layers to 10Gbit/s or even 25Gbit/s and 40Gbit/s
or even 100Gbit/s, respectively [58] [43]. However, as the data rates inside the DCs
increase, they face the limitation of the short transmission length [56] [59].
For
10Gbit/s the transmission length ranges from 300m to 550m [43] and for 25Gbit/s it
is currently only 100m [56]. Regarding the 40Gbit/s and 100Gbit/s, the reach length
is limited to 100m-150m [43]. In order for the limitation of the short reach length
to be overcome, diﬀerent wavelengths have been considered as promising candidates
and even SMFs instead of MMFs [43].
3.3.2
DFB in the DataCenters
In order for the distance limitations that arise from the VCSELs at high bit rates,
to be overcome, the DFB lasers in combination with SMFs have been considered as
a great alternative [43]. The DFB can operate at both the transmission wavelengths

has been chosen for use in the DCs when the data rate is 40Gbit/s and 100Gbit/s,

Although being more power hungry and more expensive, compared to the VCSELs

Types of DataCenters
Currently, the DCs are the main IT facilities for many enterprises [61]. The size
and the deployed architecture of the DC depend on various parameters. Primarily,
the type of the applications that are accommodated inside the DCs as long as the
complexity and the volume of the information that needs to be processed aﬀect the
choice regarding the DCN’s original infrastructure. The diﬀerent types of applications
that are managed by the DCNs may have diﬀerent needs in terms of the availability,
the capacity or even the speed that the information is processed and transmitted.
Accordingly, these applications may be accommodated either in Corporate DCs
(CDCs) or in Internet DCs (IDCs) depending on the actual needs and the original
purpose [62, 63]. The mentioned types of DCs are the two main categories that exist.
Although these DCs accommodate diﬀerent types of applications, they both share
the same objective in the direction of reducing the Total Cost of Ownership (TOC)
and provide high eﬃciency of the resources [64].
Speciﬁcally, the CDCs belong to enterprises in order to provide Web-based services
and data processing to their own organization or to their potential customers, and
they are, also, operated by them [63]. These enterprises can be private or public
companies, institutions or even government agencies [63].
Regarding the IDCs, they are predominantly owned by telecommunication com-
panies or service providers [62]. These can accommodate as well as maintain the
equipment of their clients in order for the necessary services to be provided [63].
These services are mainly Information Technology (IT) and can be accessed by dis-
tance through Internet connectivity [63]. Speciﬁcally, some of the services include
Internet access, storage networks and content distribution [63].

4.1
Cloud Services
A speciﬁc category of the applications that are hosted in the modern DCs, are the
Cloud-based applications. The Cloud has been considered as the migration from the
traditional DC applications, such as scientiﬁc and business, into applications that
need huge amounts of data to be handled and processed in a fast and secure way
[2]. The type of the Cloud-based applications include the Big Data - the volume of
the data that are generated, stored and processed by devices that are connected to
the Internet has been signiﬁcantly increased and this ﬁeld is focusing on new ways of
accessing diﬀerent storage locations- and the Storage Clouds - applications that the
users can store their data and access them when it is necessary, or even share them
with other users [2].
The Cloud Services include the applications that can be delivered through the
Internet as well as the software and hardware that should be run and deployed, ac-
cordingly, in the DCs [65]. At this point, a classiﬁcation regarding the deployment
models that exist should be made. The models’ categorization is made in terms of
who owns, manages and operates the Cloud as well as in terms of who has authen-
ticated access to it [2]. Speciﬁcally, the Could Services are categorized into: Private,
Community, Public and Hybrid [2].
Depending on the service capabilities that are provided to the user, three Cloud
Service models exist, namely the Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS), the Platform as
a Service (PaaS) and the Software as a Service (SaaS) [2]. A brief insight in these
models is given below.
The IaaS is the computing resources that compose the physical infrastructure
and provide services such as storage and processing to the users [65, 66]. In this
model the user is not responsible for the management and the control of the actual
infrastructure, but could handle the applications or the operating system [66].
Regarding the PaaS, the user can build and run applications that are using a
speciﬁc framework on the infrastructure [65, 66]. At this model, the user has only
the control of the custom applications [66].
May the most familiar model to the Internet users is that of the SaaS [66], as it
is this model that allows the users to access applications that run on the Cloud from
several devices [65]. The users in this model do not have any management or control
capabilities neither on the infrastructure nor on the applications, and can only handle
some of the application settings [66].

Chapter 5
Methodology
The present thesis, as it has already been mentioned, evaluates the power consump-
tion, the reliability, the utilization and the cost eﬀectivenes of diﬀerent DCN archi-
tectures after the introduction of the optics at diﬀerent layers. The topologies that
are investigated here are the following: Current, Fat tree, BCube, DCell and Ring.
Speciﬁcally, for each of the mentioned topologies, diﬀerent scenarios have been cre-
ated. The main scope of the diﬀerent scenarios is the evaluation of the introduction of
the optical switches at diﬀerent layers (i.e. core, aggregation, access) of the topology.
The optical switches are deployed in this way so as a logical network to be created.
Speciﬁcally, an electrical switch can only be connected with a switch that has an
electrical interface. In this way the optical switches are implemented at the ﬁrst layer
in the ﬁrst scenario, at the second layer in the second scenario, at both the ﬁrst and
the second layers in the third scenario and so on and so forth.
5.1
General explanation of setup
At this point, it is of outmost importance to refer to the global parameters, that
have been set for all the scenarios of the architectures, as well as to the networking
techniques that have been followed, such as the dimensioning and the grooming pro-
cesses. First of all, it should be mentioned that the opaque mode for the OCH layer
has been selected in order for all the scenarios to be created. This means that the
optical signals at the OCH layer, are regenerated at every intermediate node by the
LR transponders that exist on the links.
After, the actual design of the architectures is made, with the deployement of the
right switches -electrical, optical, electrooptical- at each layer. For the interconnection

of the nodes, the cables, i.e. optical ﬁbers, have been considered. The design for each
scenario of the architectures under investigation is presented below.
The line system for each scenario and the type of the switches has been deﬁned
next. For the no optical scenarios of all the architectures, no-WDM line systems
are deployed. In this way only one wavelength is deployed for each ﬁber and the

The deployed line system for the rest of the scenarios of each

Regarding the type of the deployed switches, these have been chosen to be Dis-

electrooptical nodes that have been implemented, these are composed by a DXC that
can switch SONET/SDH traﬃc and an OXC that can switch wavelengths. In this way
the OXC tributary ports are connected to the DXC trunk ports, as it shown below
in Fig.6.3, in order for the nodes to transport SONET/SDH traﬃc over WDM wave-

which signiﬁcantly aﬀects the results as it is discussed in the following chapter, is that
even if one extra port is needed for a speciﬁc switch in order to switch the traﬃc, the
type of the cross connect is upgraded to to the next available type where more ports
are available. In this way there is the chance of a signiﬁcant number of ports to be
wasted.
For all the scenarios of the architectures, uniform traﬃc has been created between
the nodes. The client traﬃc has been chosen to be 1GbE between all the servers, as
in this way it could be accommodated by the network. Depending on the architecture
and the layer that the optics are deployed, the client traﬃc is created either at the
digital (DCL) or the optical (OCH) layer. More details about this are given for each
scenario separately, below. Regarding the DCL traﬃc, the default value has been
selected for this layer, i.e. native traﬃc. For the OCH traﬃc, this has been set to
non-native in order for the logical link at the DCL layer to be created. In this way
the optical traﬃc can support SONET/SDH traﬃc.
The grooming is a necessary process in order for the digital traﬃc to be carried by
the optical channels. Link by link grooming has been selected for all the architectures
in order for the traﬃc to be groomed into the available optical channels whenever it
is possible, or new channels to be added. Speciﬁcally, at the link by link grooming
the traﬃc in terminated at every intermediate DXC and then it is regroomed in order

for the existing wavelengths to be highly utilised.
The dimensioning is a necessary process that has been followed for all the scenarios
of the architectures in order for the traﬃc at a speciﬁc layer to be accommodated.
Speciﬁcally, by dimensioning, the link and node equipment is deployed for the speciﬁc
type of traﬃc that has been originally created. No protection schemes have been
reserved for the topologies at this step.
For all the scenarios of the architectures, diverse routing has been selected. In this

that deﬁnes the cost of the paths, has been set to 2 for all the scenarios. This has

Utilization
It is really important the network resources to be highly utilized in order for an energy
eﬃcient and cost eﬀective DCN to be established. Thus, it is signiﬁcant to investigate
the architectures and the diﬀerent scenarios that have been created for those in terms
of the utilization. The interpretation of the utilization results for each topology is of
signiﬁcant importance, as it is the most important step in order to comprehend how
the resources that have been originally reserved are utilized and how each scenario is
aﬀected by the introduction of the optics. For each architecture, the link utilization
is presented ﬁrst and the node utilization afterwards.
Speciﬁcally for the node utilization of the DCL layer, it is important to state that
the number of the used ports as a function of the total ports is given in terms of the
STS-1 units that have been deployed for each node. This means that depending on
the type of the cross connect that has been deployed for a speciﬁc node, a number
of STS-1 ports is reserved, and depending on the supported data rate, a number
of STS-1 ports is utilized. For this reason the node utilization at the DCL layer is
not extensively analyzed for the scenarios. Though, an overall view is given for each
architecture.
Speciﬁcally, six types of OXCs and DXCs exist on the SP Guru Transport Planner.
This categorization is made regarding the number of the ports that are deployed for
==PAGE:69==
CHAPTER 6. SIMULATION RESULTS OF THE DCN ARCHITECTURES &
DISCUSSION
Page 58
each cross connect. Thus, the appropriate type of the cross connect for each node
is chosen by the simulation tool upon the number of the ports that needs to be
deployed. As the cross connects have been chosen to be discrete at the beginning of
each scenario, when more ports are needed for a node, the type of the cross connects
is upgraged to next type, where more ports are available. The deployment of discrete
cross connects signiﬁcanty aﬀects the utlization of the nodes. In order for this to be
clariﬁed, an OXC node may needs 34 ports in order to switch traﬃc -2 more ports
than those that are available at the Type 1 OXC. In this case the Type 2 cross connect
will be deployed for this node and a high number of ports will be not utilized.

Cost Eﬀectiveness
All the previous parameters are closely related to the cost eﬀectiveness of the DC
architectures. Speciﬁcally, the low power consumption can long term lead to a cost
eﬀective expenditure, and a balanced utilization of the newtork resources can lead
to a cost eﬀective DC, as well. Last, a highly reliable network can operate without
malfunctions of the basic networking elements that cost. At the last section of the
simulation results, the cost eﬀectiveness of each architecture is investigated. First,
the scenarios of all the architectures are compared in terms of the total network cost

that they present. It is really signiﬁcant, though, to investigate how the elements
that constitute the nodes and the links contribute to this cost. As a consequence, the

It is really important at this stage to discuss about the cost parameters that are
taken into consideration in order for the results to be obtained.
The cost of the
deployed networking elements and that of the total network cost is deﬁned by the
simulation tool SP Guru Transport Planner upon the parameters that have been
originally set. In this way the cost can be extracted through the comparison of the
scenarios and is illustrated unitless for each scenario.
As far as the node cost is concerned, the diﬀerentiation derives mainly from the
cost of the DXCs and the OXCs that are deployed for each scenario.
It is also
signiﬁcant to note that the DXCs and the OXCs do not have the same cost and their
exact cost is deﬁned by the simulation tool. This cost is mainly dependent on the
deployed type of the cross connect and speciﬁcally by the number of ports that are

7.1
Conclusions
For each architecture that has been investigated, the scenario with the optimum
performance is concluded here. Speciﬁcally, by optimum performance, the scenario
that presents low power consumption, high utlization and reliability, as well as it is
cost eﬀective is implied.
The switch-centric architectures, that is the Current and the Fat tree, present a
similar behaviour after the introduction of the optics at the diﬀerent layers. Regard-
ing these architectures, it seems that the introduction of the optics at the aggregation
layer or at both the core and the aggregation layers makes the topologies both power
and cost eﬀective. The no optical scenarios present signiﬁcantly higher power con-
sumption, and speciﬁcally for the Fat tree, the power consumption of this is double.
When the optics are introduced at the mentioned layers of these architectures, though,
the link utilization at the DCL and the OCH layers is low. This implies that less time
slots should have been reserved and less wavelengths should have been deployed for the
links of these architectures, respectively. The optical ports are highly utilized for the
optical aggregation and optical core&aggregation scenarios and as a consequence the
node utilization at the DCL layer is respectively high. Regarding the STS-1 ports that
have been reserved for these scenarios, these seem not to be highly utlized, though.
Regarding the reliability, for the Current architecture the optical core&aggregation
scenario presents the highest availability among the scenarios, while for the Fat tree
architecture, the optical aggregation scenario presents the highest availability. For
the mentioned architectures, it seems that the introduction of the optics at speciﬁc
layers has a positive impact on their overall performance.

The hybrid server-centric architectures that have been presented here, that is the
BCube and the DCell present some similar characteristics after the introduction of
the optics. As far as the BCube architecture is concerned, the introduction of the
optical switches at all the three layers presents the lowest power consumption, and
speciﬁcally half power consumption compared to the scenario where no optics are
introduced. The ﬁgure of the cost, though, is diﬀerentiated, as the scenario with
the optics at the mentioned layers presents higher cost than the one with no optics.
It seems that the wavelengths are highly utilized when the optics are introduced at
the BCube architecture and as a consequence the respective link utilization at the
OCH layer is the highest. However, the time slots that have been reserved for the all
optical scenario are signiﬁcantly more than these that are actually used and as result
the link utilization at the DCL layer is the lowest among the scenarios. The node
utilization at both the DCL amd the OCH layers of the all optical scenario is the
highest. Regarding the availability of the topology when the optics are introduced,
this is the second highest that is presented for this topology, after the no optical
scenario. Overall, for the BCube architecture the all optical scenario presents better
performance, especially regarding the power consumption. For the DCell architecture,
even though the power consumption of the scenario with the optics at the three layers
is lower than the scenario with no optics, the cost of the ﬁrst is signiﬁcantly higher,
almost three times higher. The link and the node utilization at the DCL and the OCH
layers present exactly the opposite behaviour. As it is expected, at the DCL layer
the scenario with no optics has better utilization while at the OCH layer the scenario
with the optics presents better utilization. Last, regarding the reliability of the DCell
architecture, after the introduction of the optics, the network presents signiﬁcantly
lower average availability. For the DCell architecture the optimum performance seems
to be a trade oﬀbetween the power consumption and the cost, while the introduction
of the optics results in an energy eﬃcient network with higher cost and lower reliability
compared to the no optical scenario.
Finally, for the Ring architecture, the introduction of the optical switches at the
core layer makes the topology both power eﬃcient and cost eﬀective.
The time
slots that have been reserved at the DCL layer for the optical core scenario are half
utilized, though, the link utilization of this scenario is the highest among the scenarios.
However, the introduction of the optics at the core layer makes the scenario poorly
utilized at the OCH layer, as only a small fraction of the deployed wavelengths are
actually utilized. Regarding the node utilization at the OCH layer, the optical core

7.2
Future work
As the traﬃc volume inside the DCNs is increasing with an alarming rate and the
energy footprint of these is increasing accordingly, the future research should be fo-
cused on deploying more energy eﬃcient techniques for the DCNs. These techniques
include both the selection of the right architecture, depending on the actual applica-
tions that are accommodated inside the DCN and the actual demands of the DCN,
as well as the introduction of optical switches at speciﬁc layers inside the DCN. The
research should, also, focus on the right combination of the optical components inside
the DCN, that is the type of the optical ﬁber and the type of the optical source,
depending on the actual traﬃc demands and the size of the DCN.
Upon the limitations that are stated at the beginning, the present architectures
could also be investigated in a larger scale, with a higher number of servers and
switches deployed at the layers, in order for a DCN to be simulated in a more realistic
way. Furthermore, it would have been interesting to evaluate the performance of the
architectures with higher data rate for the traﬃc that is created between the servers,
such as 10GbE, and also with diﬀerentiated traﬃc among them.
