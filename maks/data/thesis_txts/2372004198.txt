ABSTRACT 
ENGLISH 
Phosphor (P) is an essential element for all living organisms and cannot be replaced. With depleting P-
reserves, focus is shifted towards utilizing P-rich secondary resources from thermal treatments in the 
agricultural industry as P-rich fertilizers. XRD is suggested as an analytical method for the specification of 
phosphorous components in the ashes. In a literature study performed, it was found that previous 
investigations had sufficiently identified such components from similar samples (in varying degrees) with 
the addition of alternative analytical methods to characterize the samples. A treatment validation was 
performed to ensure proper sample treatment prior to XRD sampling which yielded satisfactory results. 
When performing the data matching methodology, an identification strategy was implemented which 
improve the speciation greatly. Combining this with a secondary analysis to establish sample restrictions, a 
total of 4 possible calcium metal phosphates were identified as well as five residual components (silicon 
oxide, iron oxide, copper potassium aluminum silicate, calcium sulfate & calcium carbonate). A large 
improvement over the initial two identifications from the basic methodology (calcium aluminum phosphate 
& iron oxide). Lastly, a study was performed to investigate further improvements towards obtainable data 
by improving SSA sample crystallinity through cooldown reduction. This was found to be ineffective, but 
gave an opportunity for applying the improved speciation method which coincided with the reduction of 
iron oxide observed. 
DANSK 
Fosfor (P) er et væsentligt grundstof for alle levende organismer og kan ikke erstattes af andre. Med 
udtømmende P-reserver, er der kommet forøjet fokus på at udnytte P-rige sekundære ressourcer fra 
termiske processer i landbrugsindustrien, såsom P-rige gødninger. XRD foreslås som en analytisk metode til 
speciering af fosforbestanddelen i asken fra de termiske processor. I et udført litteraturstudium blev det

forbedrede datatilpasningsmetoden, der faldt sammen med reduktionen af observeret jernoxid.

INTRODUCTION 
MAIN OBJECTIVES 
Phosphor is a limited resource, and recently there has been a growing focus on developing methods, in 
which phosphor can be recycled, in order to diminish its consumption from natural sources. These methods 
involve the use of ash from thermal conversion of P-rich biomass fuels in the agricultural industry such as 
for fertilizers and soil amendments. The available phosphor in such soil amendments is however heavily 
reliant on a number of factors such as soil moisture content, pH, particle size and specific phosphor 
chemistry. High focus has been set on the phosphor chemistry in the resulting ashes from the thermal fuel 
conversion processes (pyrolysis, combustion or gasification) at DTU Campus Risø. The goal is to optimize 
the conversion processes in order to produce ashes, with the optimal phosphorus characteristics for bio-
availability. 
This optimization process requires the necessary tools in order to specify the P-species present in the ashes 
from the thermal conversions. One method is X-ray powder diffraction (XRD), in which a sample consisting 
of various small randomly oriented crystals are exposed to X-ray waves in a two-dimensional setup, in order 
to observe the resulting wave diffractions from these crystals. Based on these diffractions, an estimate 
could be made regarding the crystalline species, which are present in the sample. Recent attempts at using 
the XRD method have shown to yield unclear or even false results, when using automated database search 
models. Whether this is due to the chemical/physical properties of the ash sample, an error in the sampling 
method, preparation of the sample or the use of the database search analytics, is something which has to 
be investigated further. 
The goal of this project is therefore to obtain fundamental insight into the chemical speciation and phase 
distribution of phosphor in thermal conversion ashes from biomass by use of XRD-analysis. To accomplish 
that, this report will focus on three main objectives. 
 
A literature study with focus on collecting and reporting previous experiences of XRD, especially for 
phosphor speciation in secondary resources. 
 
Establishing a methodology with which to treat, prepare and analyze ash samples in order to obtain 
the best results. 
 
Analyzing and adjusting the XRD data analytical method with sample ash in order to improve the 
resulting speciation. 
With these objectives in place, an evaluation should be possible concerning the application of XRD as an 
analytical method for ashes from biomass and the limitations, which are present.

PHOSPHOR RESOURCE & RECYCLE 
Phosphor is a chemical element of the periodic table, represented by the letter ‘P’. It has the atomic 
number of 15, and is part of the ‘none-metal’ group. It is a vital element in the function of biology for both 
plants and animals, as it is a crucial element for the structural development of cells, and, a key component 
in the storage of energy as ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate). In addition to this, it is also an essential key 
element in molecules such as DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) and RNA (Ribonucleic acid) (Elser, 2012). 
Phosphor can be seen as one of the basic building-blocks necessary for the preservation and eventual 
growth of all living matter. Therefore, it is a key element in the use of industrial fertilizers for the 
agricultural industry. 
In 2015, the consumption of phosphorous fertilizer in Europe reached 3.9 million tonnes, which was a 1.9% 
increase compared to 2013. It is projected that between 2014 and 2018, the consumption is estimated to 
increase by 2.3% (FAO, 2015). Phosphorus fertilizers are in great demand since they are vital for the growth 
of industrial crops via the soil phosphorus cycle. The soil phosphorus cycle is the cycle by which phosphorus 
enters the ecological environment and circulates throughout it. Entering this cycle can occur via natural 
means (such as animal or plant residues) or from fertilizers, municipal and industrial by-products that 
circulate this system. In this system, the soil phosphorus state, is heavily reliant on the soil pH. In acidic 
environments, the phosphorus reacts with iron (Fe) and aluminum (Al) to form insoluble phosphates. In 
alkaline environments, it can combine with calcium to also form insoluble phosphates (Mullins, 2009). 
Therefore, a large quantity of phosphorus which is added to the soil is rapidly bound by the soil minerals 
and is therefore subject to slow release, making the ‘soil-bound’ phosphorus concentration low. In 
conditions with neutral pH, there are P-bearing minerals, which dissolve to the soil solution P, largely 
     
  &     
  (Mullins, 2009). Besides crop uptake and removal, phosphorus can also leave the cycle via 
runoff, erosion or leaching. Soil is constantly subject to change due to environmental impact (e.g. 
weathering), which means that particulate and soluble phosphorus can be carried away from the system, 
and redirected into other environments, such as large bodies of water. This runoff, erosion or leaching of 
phosphorus into none-agricultural environments can present a large problem since increasing bioavailable 
phosphorus in environments which are not suited to this type of change can have devastating 
consequences. Worst case scenarios; include the increased biomass of plant life, which wipes out all 
residual animal life in the system. Therefore, the use of industrial fertilizers includes agricultural practices, 
in order to minimize the environmental impact of P. These practices can include, but are not limited to 
(Mullins, 2009): 
 
Controlling soil erosion at and around the agricultural fields 
 
Incorporating fertilizer directly into the soil 
 
Applying P to an actively growing crop 
 
Reducing the build-up of soil P via routing monitoring of soil samples 
Improving the P-cycle for the agricultural industry is vital, if the worldwide requirement for more P is to 
decline. In recent time, several methods have been investigated, in order to further the retention and 
utilization of P in agricultural soil. These methods include both the use of soil retention compounds or 
materials, in order to enable higher retention of phosphorus in the soil, or the use of recycled phosphorus 
comprised waste materials from other industrial applications. An example of these industrial applications 
could be the thermal conversion of biomass waste from agricultural production in gasification, combustion

THERMAL CONVERSION PROCESS 
The thermal conversion process refers to the act of treatment of organic materials, using high temperature 
with varying air/fuel ratios or steam depending, on the wanted conversion and fuel type. The purpose of 
this thermal conversion is yielding primary products such as heat, gases, char, tar or oils from these 
secondary organic materials resources. The process also causes the formation of varying phosphorous 
compounds (in P-rich materials) depending on the process conditions. There are numerous processes by 
which these thermal conversions can take place, and various technological manners by which to achieve 
this. However, principally, there are three types of thermal conversions. Namely combustion, gasification 
and pyrolysis. 
 
FIGURE 1 - THERMAL CONVERSION PROCESSES AND PRODUCTS (BRIDGWATER, 1994) 
Combustion refers to the thermal treatment of fuel in the presence of stoichiometrically high 
concentrations of oxygen (  ) relative to fuel. These oxidizing environments mean that the focus of 
combustion is the release of heat from the fuel source. The energy from this process is then utilized in 
order to generate electricity or heat to feed into the energy grid. The burning of fuel with excess oxygen 
results in its complete oxidation, and the release of the gaseous product, as well as the internal chemical 
energy. Part of the fuel composition (especially when dealing with biomass) is the non-combustible ash 
fraction. Some of the ash is caught in the gas suspension as fly-ash and is either caught by mechanical 
means or separated via a solid-gas separation process. These ash fractions can be used as mineral fertilizers 
since they contain some P-minerals, and other essential elements needed by plants, yet contain relatively 
low quantities of N elements. The high temperatures from combustion means that there is higher 
concentrations of     (nitrogen oxides) and     (sulphur oxides) in the off-gas (Roos, 2010). 
Gasification is the thermal conversion of fuel, in an environment with limited oxygen available. This means 
that there is insufficient oxygen necessary for the complete oxidation of the biomass fuel. This results in the 
production of a combustible gas, whose properties are dependent on the parameters from the gasification

process. These gas-like fuels include, but are not limited to, methane, hydrogen and carbon monoxide 
(Senneca, 2007). Gasification is often performed at lower temperatures than combustion, which means 
that there is not a large production of nitrogen and hydrogen sulfides (since these tend to form mainly at 
higher temperatures). 
Pyrolysis is the chemical degradation of fuel, using high heat with no available oxygen (or any oxidation 
agent). An example of pyrolysis would be the production of charcoal. The pyrolysis process does not 
necessarily yield energy, but instead produces combustible- gas, liquid and char. The composition and 
output of each of these products, is determined by the parameters set for the pyrolysis process and the 
fuel composition used. 
In this project, the focus will be on P-speciation from ashes, derived from combustion of sewage sludge, 
using XRD. Additionally, the goal is to conduct an investigation on real sewage sludge ash samples, in order 
to produce and analyze realistic data. Any variation in the samples will be controlled and monitored, in 
order to reflect these alterations in the data obtained, and thereby to achieve a reliable analysis of the XRD-
data. 
BIOMASS ASH & BIOCHAR 
The thermal conversion of biomass yield ashes with a specific content of trace elements, depending on the 
chemical composition and ash fraction of the fuels used. The most common elements found in these ashes 
include Na, Mg, Al, Si, P, S, Cl, K, Ca & Fe. All of these trace elements have a role in the ash formation. The 
oxides of these elements can be seen as the primary ash transformation reactions. It should not be 
assumed that these will necessarily take place in a combustion situation, but they can be considered 
hypothetical first steps (due to many main ash-forming elements usually being present as metal ions,

X-RAY DIFFRACTION CRYSTALLOGRAPHY 
X-ray diffraction (XRD) crystallography is the practice, in which electromagnetic radiation is used to 
determine the chemical composition and structure of a compound via diffraction. The practice was first 
applied 17 years after X-ray’s discovery by Max Theodor Felix von Laue at the University of Munich in 1912, 
who believed that X-rays had a wavelength similar to that of interatomic distances in crystals. Together 
with Walter Friedrich (research assistant) and Paul Knipping (PhD grad student), he performed the first 
diffraction experiment on a       crystals, which won him the Nobel Prize in physics, in 1914. The year 
after, 1915, the Nobel Prize in physics was given to Sir William Henry Bragg for determining the first mineral 
structure (    ), by use of XRD and developing the theory in which to determine crystal structure from 
diffraction pattern. This research and theory became the basis for Bragg’s Law, which is used to determine 
the lattice parameters from scattering data. 
In todays practice, XRD can be used to give a wide range of information pertaining to the crystalline sample 
such as particle sizes, size distributions, residual stress and strain. Commonly it is applied to gain specific 
crystal structure or qualitative chemical composition of the sample. The former is usually performed via 
single-crystal XRD, and the latter via powder XRD. The focus of this report will be the use of powder 
diffraction for the determination of P-containing species in biomass ashes.

Alternatively, to the use of X-rays for diffraction, other electromagnetic waves can also be utilized for 
creating diffraction patterns. These include both electron and neutron diffraction. Their size makes them 
ideal in the use of diffraction studies since even at relatively high energy, their size will only be a fraction of 
that for an interatomic bond. For electrons, the advantage is that it is easy to obtain an intense, 
monochromatic beam which allows for the analysis of gas sample in a very short time (roughly 20 seconds). 
The biggest issue with electron diffraction is that it is charged particles which interact very strongly with the 
given sample matter, and, do not penetrate solids or liquids very much. They also require the testing to 
take place in vacuum, due to their charge, which increases their experimental complexity. For these 
reasons, electron diffraction is usually not chosen for the diffraction analysis of solid compounds or 
elements (Waseda, 2011). 
Neutron diffraction is, like X-ray, very penetrating but has no atom-specific neutron scattering intensity 
based on the atomic number. In some cases the diffraction from heavier atoms can contribute no more to 
the total scattering diffraction pattern, than the light one. This does, however, make it very useful in certain 
studies such as hydrido ligands in transition-metal complexes. The largest issue with the use of neutrons, is 
the scarce availability of them. Nucleus reactors can be used or some spallation sources which are 
specifically designed to provide neutron beams (Waseda, 2011).

So due to the charged particle nature of electron beams, and the scarcity of neutron diffraction, X-ray 
becomes the primary possibility for the analysis of solid crystalline structures. The use of X-ray is however 
not ideal, since there are some disadvantages and issues with the use of X-rays. The recorded diffractions 
from X-ray are highly dependent on the atomic numbers of the scattering atoms. Since the recorded 
diffraction is a sum of all scattering contributions, the influence of lighter atoms is smaller than those of the 
heavier. In a component sample containing heavier atoms, presence of lighter atoms can be difficult to 
conclude. In circumstances with the presence of multiple heavier atoms, the presence of essential atoms 
such as carbon and oxygen can even be problematic to define precisely. The use of X-ray diffraction on solid 
crystalline samples also usually require several hours which makes the experimental procedure very time-
consuming. Lastly, it is important to note that the use of X-rays reveals information regarding the position 
of electron density since it interacts mainly with the electrons. This is different comparatively to diffraction 
at the atomic nuclei, since the electron distribution about the nuclei is aspherical (Rankin, 2013). However, 
due to the focus of this project being on the use of powder diffraction, which works on the principle of 
compound fingerprints that are achieved from previous analytical findings, the effect of electron diffraction 
rather than nuclei is minimal. 
POWDER XRD 
PRINCIPLE 
Powder diffraction works on similar principles as diffraction of single crystals, the difference being that a 
powder is composed of many smaller, randomly oriented crystallites. It is very similar to the use of electron 
diffraction in gas phase diffraction analysis, where there is also a large amount of randomly oriented 
molecules. So Bragg’s Law still applies, and each individual crystal behaves as previously described. The 
major difference is that the mass of randomly oriented crystal in the powder will reflect on the basis of a 
single projection angle as a cone, which yields a circular diffraction pattern. This means that the diffraction 
information from powder XRD is no longer three-dimensionally applicable. The information now is only 
applied for every one-dimensional radius of the circular pattern. Although this will mean that there is a 
reduction of information compared to a three-dimensional single crystal XRD, this powdered method has 
been very useful in order to analyze multi-component crystalline samples, through fingerprint association 
of the projection angle (  ) information available. Powder diffraction also allows for the assessment of 
phase purity and the polymorph nature of samples.

which is done on the crystalline sample. A common setup, which is also the same that will be used in the

uses the “Bragg-Brentano” geometry. 
This geometry is when the crushed crystalline sample is placed on a circular disk, that is then placed in a

apparatus (Rankin, 2013). The Bragg-Brentano methodology yields a reasonable scattering area of the 
sample surface for the same beam angle. Together with good intensity and instrument resolution, a high 
amount valuable data points can be achieved for the given sample.

THE RIETVELD METHOD 
The Rietveld method or ‘Rietveld refinement’ is the practice in which an entire powder profile is 
considered, in order to match with previous data. This is done by using a least-squares procedure on the 
diffraction data, in order refine a theoretical line profile, which can match a previously measured profile. 
This method of deconvoluting a powder diffraction data set, was first thought up in the late 1960’s by Hugo 
Rietveld by applying a whole pattern fitting structure refinement method on the diffraction patterns 
(Young, 1993). With this methodology, a powder diffraction pattern no longer needed to be deconvoluted 
in order to determine the individual intensities. Instead, all of the reflections were fit directly to the pattern 
which enabled the analysis of strongly overlapping reflections. 
It is important to remember that the Rietveld method requires a model of the crystal structure (without 
giving any possibilities for creating this structure from nothing). So the method is a good refinement and 
optimization tool which can also be used to find structural details (Pecharsky & Zavalij, 2005). This 
refinement method has been recently used in studies such as (Stemann, 2015) and (Peplinski, 2006) in 
order to produce semi-quantitative analysis on the phases present in SSA samples. The specific method 
used in these studies was the Rietveld fundamental parameters approach (FPA) utilized via the BGMN 
refinement software (Ortiz, 2000).  Since the analysis of the phase (P) quantification is outside the scope of 
this project, this methodology will not be applied in the experimental section.

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 
This literature study shows how several studies have utilized XRD as a tool for the speciation of ashes 
derived from secondary resources. Most studies focused on the determination of mineral phases and some 
focused especially on phosphorous component phases. The dominating secondary resource analyzed in this 
literature study was sewage sludge. Its high phosphorous content makes it a good candidate as a fertilizer 
for the agricultural industry. The phosphorous fraction in the SSA’s in the literature study was in the range 
of 5-10 wt%. 
The characterization of P-species, throughout the study, was found to be qualitative highly varying. Some 
studies were able to determine multiple phosphorous species adjacent to high component fractions of 
other sample species (Li et al., 2015) whilst some studies expressed difficulties with even identifying one 
phase additionally to the largest component fraction (Wang et al., 2012). From the basis of data gathered 
throughout the study, this is most likely due to a number of variables regarding the studies method for XRD 
analysis. These variables could include but are not limited to: 
1. Alternative analytical methods for specifying the SSA 
2. Sample preparation and XRD setup 
3. Manual evaluation of XRD patterns 
4. Suitability of ash 
The quality of the XRD data was influenced by the suitability of certain thermally treated ashes. In studies 
such as (Tsai, 2012) and (Wang, 2012) it is clear how the use of low temperature pyrolysis is problematic 
since this process does not enable the necessary crystallization of the amorphous phase. This absence of 
this phase for the residual studies have yielded high quality XRD peak results with clear indications of 
intensity and    positioning. This is perhaps an inherit issue with pyrolysis only due to the low temperature 
incineration method utilized in (Pedersen, 2010) did not encounter such issues. The possibility of none 
thermally treated sludge analysis was also presented in (Huang, 2004) , but here there were also substantial 
indications of amorphous areas coupled with wide diffraction peaks for the sample diffractions. The XRD’s 
patterns found with the highest quality were for high temperature treatment in gasification and 
combustion. 
Most studies relied heavily on the use of alternative analytical methods in order to determine speciation of 
the SSA samples which could aid the XRD analysis. These analytical methods are listed in Table 4, but the 
most prevalent ones were XRF and SEM. Studies, such as (Ksawery, 2010), utilized SEM as a basis for 
analyzing the correlating changes in XRD patterns, gave a satisfactory depiction of the crystalline phase 
composition. Additionally, like with the (Atienza, 2014) study, (Ksawery., 2010) does not exclude the 
probability of overlapping XRD species and specifies the possibility of multiple occurring phases for similar 
profiles. This is especially important since some studies have found the distinctive false identification of 
certain species in conjunction with overlapping data (Peplinski, 2013). 
The sample preparation disclosed in the studies was minimal to none existent, which makes it difficult to 
criticize the effect of this, however most studies did note the diffraction setup and process variables. The 
average setup was a D5000 or Panalytical diffractometer in a Bragg-Brentano geometry coupled with Cu-
   in a    range of roughly 0  to 75 . A noticeable parameter found was the diffraction steps and step

time. In studies such as (Morais, 2009) and (Wang, 2012) diffraction step times of 3 /min and   /min were

In conclusion, the studies show that use of XRD as a method for phosphorous speciation can be executed 
with proper alternative sample information from separate analytical methods, proper XRD setup and a

SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION 
This section in the experimental methodology achieved satisfying results concerning the evaluation of 
sample treatment, identifying issues for diffraction data analysis and the data matching approach. In the 
treatment evaluation it was found that the method used was suitable for the preparation of XRD samples. 
In addition, issues such as data shadowing and amorphous phases arose in the data. The data shadowing 
was problematic due to the reduction in data quality for identifying individual species. This information was 
however utilized in the data matching section for improving the analytical method of the matching 
software. 
The first data matching methods demonstrated how using default settings yielded inaccurate results (like 
those described in earlier attempts of SSA XRD analysis at Risø). The use of an identification strategy, 
coupled with the information regarding the data’s limitations and issues, resulted in additional component 
identification. Lastly, the strategy was further improved by the addition of the sample’s restrictions, that 
has been found previously in the (Thomsen, 2016) study. The resulting matching generated the estimation 
of multiple calcium metal phosphates as well as 5 other residual components in the sample. This is a large 
improvement over the initial 2-3 derived in the initial matching method. 
As mentioned in the literature study and treatment validation, the amorphous phase is an issue due to the 
reduction in XRD-available crystalline material as well possible shadowing of residual peaks from diffraction 
scattering. Noticeably, the amorphous area in the incineration SSA used in this report is larger compared to 
those analyzed in the literature at similar thermal treatment temperatures. A theory is that the fluid-bed 
incinerator setup utilized for the thermal treatment of this SSA has a very short retention time (5s) and 
==PAGE:63==

cooldown period for the SSA that could have inhibited the necessary formation of a proper crystalline 
phases. The goal is to investigate this further by varying the cooling rate for the SSA from its given 
formation temperature (840 ) to room temperature in an inert environment to study the effect on its 
crystallinity. 
REDUCED COOLING RATE VS CRYSTALLINITY 
With a basis established for proper sample preparation and diffraction setup in initial treatment validation, 
an analysis of the cooling rate can be initiated. In this part, the goal is to analyze the effect of elongated 
cooling on the crystallization of ash samples and if this can affect the XRD data analysis in a positive way. A 
basis for this was the reduction of amorphous component in order to achieve clearer sample peaks since 
industrial thermal treatment process rapidly cools the SSA (which might hinder the crystalline formation). 
The intention is to perform heating cycles with the SSA sample from initial treatment verification to mimic 
the conditions from when the ash was made and then adjusting the cooling rate in three varying setups. 
The heating cycles will be performed in an inert environment (  ) in order to ensure that the ash 
components remain unaltered. The sample will be heated at 10 /min from 30  to 840 , where it will 
remain for 60 min in order to ensure full thermal penetration. After this, the sample will be cooled at a 
given rate. Weight measurements for the samples before and after the thermal treatment are performed in 
order to note eventual loss of dry matter.

DISCUSSION & CONCLUSION 
Phosphor is a limited resource, which is steadily being drained from the world’s natural resources. In the 
past couple of years, there has been a heightened focus on the recycling of phosphorous rich waste 
materials back into the agricultural industry in order to mitigate future depletion of P resources. A method 
for doing this recycling is the utilization of ashes from thermal treatment processes of P-rich secondary 
resources. These P-rich resources have previously shown to be effective as industrial fertilizers.  One of the 
primary issues with the utilization of these is the speciation of phosphorous compounds inside the ash, 
which would determine the physical properties of it (quantification, release, binding properties). Recently, 
X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) has been suggested as a suitable analytical candidate for determining these 
phosphorus species due to the inherit crystalline properties of ash and since XRD is a readily available, low-
cost and fast sampling method. The main goal of this report was therefore to obtain fundamental insight 
into the chemical speciation of phosphor in thermally treated ash by use of XRD. This report included; a 
literature study with focus on collecting previous experiences with the use of XRD for phosphor speciation 
in ashes, establishing a methodology for the preparation of sample ash and the improvement of the 
analytical data matching method for greater speciation. In extension to this, a study was performed in 
order to investigate sample improvement through reduced cooling rate for the sample ashes. 
After establishing the basis theory for phosphorous chemistry and utilization in the agricultural industry as 
well as the theory behind XRD, a literature study was performed. This study found that multiple studies had 
previously successfully utilized XRD at a method for determining phosphorous compounds within their ash 
samples, with varying degrees of success. Studies that had a well-defined XRD setup, high sample 
crystallinity and supplementary sample chemistry data were able to clearly specify phosphorous 
compounds. This study, together with the introductory theory, became the basis for the treatment 
validation experiments and the diffraction data matching methodology. 
The treatment validation found that the method used was suitable for XRD sample preparation. The 
validation analysis also yielded additional properties in the SSA – phosphorous mixtures such as the data 
shadowing effect and issues regarding amorphous phases. However, these inherit properties were utilized 
in the data matching section for the improvement of the matching method. The initial method, with low 
speciation results, was representative of previous attempts made at Risø for the analysis of SSA. By 
implementing of an identification strategy and altering the scoring and group parameters (pattern shifting, 
unmatched intensity and semi-residual analysis), a suitably better result was achieved. Lastly, the known 
==PAGE:70==
DTU, Risø 
 
Christian Wolff Nygaard 
2017

Page 69 of 108 
 
properties of the SSA, which had been found via ICP-OES, were utilized as restrictions, which lead to the 
speciation of multiple calcium metal phosphates as well as 5 other residual components. This is a large 
improvement over the initial 2-3 components. To further improve on this component speciation, 
experimentation was performed to improve SSA crystallinity. 
This reduced cooling experimentation determined that the cooling rate did not affect the degree of 
crystallization in the SSA samples. The experiments additionally yielded an interesting effect, with the 
assumed reduction of iron oxide from the presence of residual    , carbon and atmospheric air inside of 
the aluminum oxide crucibles. This effect enabled the testing of the matching strategy from the initial 
testing. This yielded results that coincided with the visual analysis made. 
In conclusion, for this report, XRD was found to be effective in the speciation of multiple species in SSA’s 
(including multiple phosphorous-compounds). The speciation was made possible by establishing a 
theoretical and literature derived basis for the effects that have significant impacts on the determination 
method of matching reference powder patterns. By using this basis, the speciation was significantly 
improved, and aided in the characterization of the reformed samples in the reduced cooling 
experimentation. With the current setup established in this project for the XRD analysis of phosphorous 
compounds in SSA’s, future speciation’s should not be problematic. If proper sample treatment is 
performed and suitable XRD settings are utilized, then the resulting diffraction data should be sufficient to 
identify phosphorous compounds (even when dealing with smaller fractions of phosphor or larger 
amorphous phases). Utilizing additional analytical methods such as SEM, ICP, XRF or acidic leaching in order 
to determine chemical properties for the samples is beneficiary to establishing restrictions for the analytical 
methodology. A perfect scenario for the speciation of a SSA would be a full crystalline phase, low particle 
size <   m in a low particle distribution, high number of diffraction data points and an analysis of elements 
not present in the sample. 
To improve upon the use of XRD for future uses, methods that could potentially improve sample 
crystallinity inertly would be greatly beneficial for data matching precision. The goal for a future scenario 
would be to enable the analysis of SSA’s through XRD without the need for alternative analytical methods 
or manual interpretations. That with both a properly prepared sample and XRD setup that a sample can 
adequately characterized through a scoring system suited for SSA’s in order to sufficiently determine 
compound phases (notably phosphorous compounds). Until then, it is advisable that the Risø institute at 
the Technical University of Denmark continue its work on SSA speciation through XRD analysis.
