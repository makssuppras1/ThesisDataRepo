Abstract
Internet of Things (IoT) provide technology solutions for smart cities to healthcare
topics, through various of low-cost and energy eﬀicient devices. IoT as we know it
now is a new and very undiscovered field in computer science, based on that and the
complexity and heterogeneity of IoT networks and devices, challenges arise regarding
security concerns and mitigation techniques. Applications of IoT distributed networks
varies on different fields, based on the needs that we have we can decide how we will
develop our network and what device we will include. In order to keep the cost of
IoT solutions at the minimum prize, security goes at second place.
Keywords IoT; SDR; DoS; Jamming; Replay; Z-Wave; ZigBee; MQTT;

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivation
Internet of Things (Internet of Things (IoT)) has risen in the last two decades and
represents networks within devices that can connect and communicate with a million
things simultaneously. In terms of innovation, IoT consider the need for low-cost de-
vices that can continuously collect and gather information from the environment and
use this information for life improvement. The term IoT is inextricably intertwined
with the fourth industrial revolution, during the expansion of the internet to carry
more of the physical world by making the everyday object more sophisticated and
intelligent.
Nowadays, IoT is a part of the world as it is implemented in many domains such
as transportation, agriculture, healthcare and energy production. Based on the cost
of the devices and how easily everybody can implement a local IoT network to serve
any needs they can have, IoT can be found everywhere. Given the above facts, we
can say that IoT is an assistant that helps us in our daily life by using information
collected from our daily routines.
Looking at these domains, two basic security questions arise. In healthcare and
home automation, the devices can access a vast amount of private data and can be
responsible for providing services that have a crucial role in health-related issues.
Based on that, raise security and privacy concerns regarding the availability and
confidentiality in IoT networks.
The devices follow a client and server-centralised model and consist of three-layer
architecture. The first layer is the perception layer, where devices and sensors contin-
uously collect information from the environment. This information will be processed
through the network layer to gateways or other devices that are part of the network
and finally will conclude at the application layer, where all information will be avail-
able in a human-friendly version. The elements of this architecture are exposed to
specific attacks that threaten the security of an IoT network and devices. Distributed
Denial Of Service and Jamming attacks are two examples. These attacks are the most
widespread cyberattacks in the last decade. In these attacks, an attacker aims to in-
tercept the appropriate functionality in a network with several techniques. The real
challenge from a security perspective in IoT networks is the lack of knowledge regard-
ing these attacks through datasets or documentation and the lack of tools to simulate
and detect these attacks.
==PAGE:18==
4
Introduction
This thesis presents a simulation of a home automation IoT network using a
Raspberry Pi as a gateway that supports Z-Wave, Zigbee, and WiFi nodes.
The
network was configured with Home Assistant and Unify SDK, and various attacks
were launched against the MQTT broker and Z-Wave and Zigbee nodes. A dataset
was created from the collected traﬀic, including 5-second MQTT and Z-Wave traﬀic
flows. Machine learning algorithms such as KNN, Gaussian Naive Bayes, Decision
Trees, Random Forest, and MLP were used to evaluate the dataset based on accuracy
and confusion metrics. The thesis concludes that the datasets and evaluation results
are essential due to the need for existing datasets for malicious activity in IoT net-
works and a lack of knowledge about tools and techniques for capturing Z-Wave and
Zigbee traﬀic or launching attacks against these protocols. The main contribution
of this research is the generation of datasets that accurately represent standard and
malicious IoT network traﬀic, which can be used to train machine learning models to
detect and prevent such attacks.
1.1.1
Objectives
To define the objectives, it is essential to do background research to understand
techniques and methods that already exist. These include the sensor devices, their
communication standards in the IoT network, the network’s topology, attack concepts
and software-defined radio concepts.
A crucial goal of the thesis is to understand the vulnerabilities in IoT networks
and known techniques that can extract information regarding the topology and the
devices that constitute the network, which can lead to an attack chain.
After the attack simulation into the IoT network, the traﬀic will be analysed by
feature extraction techniques. The importance of these features will be evaluated
using various classificators. The thesis goal has been divided into smaller milestones
to achieve the desired results:
• Research on gateway solutions, ideally an implementation that can support
Z-Wave, ZigBee and WiFi devices. Configure the network
• Interact with the network and devices, examine information and behaviour that
can exploit in an attack.
• Research on Denial of Service (DoS) and jamming attack techniques using Soft-
ware Defined Radio.
• Implement tools that can automate the attacks and capture/collect traﬀic in
both IoT protocols Z-Wave, ZigBee and MQTT in a controlled environment.
• Examine the traﬀic, extract features and use models that can classify the dataset.
Analysis of the results and discussion.

1.3
Background
The wireless network is essential to the Internet of Things (IoT) applications. Differ-
ent groups and organisations cooperate to create and support wireless communication
standards related to IoT. Smart object communication has already significantly pro-
gressed in the vision of a global networking platform. A desirable IoT system should
support most of the IoT communication standards such as Z-Wave, ZigBee and WiFi.
However, configuring such a feasible and eﬀicient IoT system seems an uphill chal-
lenge. All sensor devices and objects require an identity management system, and the
standardisation of IoT is crucial to advanced interoperability. In addition, network
security and data confidentiality are significant concerns.
The IoT communication technologies have been the subject of several studies [1–4],
almost none of them deals with the IoT network and physical layer. Based on that,
all of these challenges in the physical and network layer need to be addressed.
1.3.1
Security
Once an IoT network is deployed, sensor devices are kept to interact within the
network more devices are added, some others are removed, and others could still be
in the network but in inactive or offline mode. The heterogeneity of these devices
and the way they work without any obvious sign for the human eye lead to black box
systems. Nevertheless, with appropriate hardware, we can reveal all these operations,
such as inclusion, removal, idle or interaction, and the data transmitted through
these processes. Moreover, to keep the low cost of these devices, most available in
the market lack security. The combination of Software Defined Radio and lack of
encryption or any other security measure exposes IoT systems to several Physical
layer attacks. Finally, we need to taxonomy the IoT technologies followed by the
vulnerabilities and the attacks that can exploit them. This thesis will focus on WiFi,
MQTT, Z-Wave and ZigBee.

1.4
MQTT
MQTT is used for messaging protocol. In this case, it is a broker between the gateway
and the devices. MQTT stands for Message Queue Telemetry Transport and functions
on top of Transport Control Protocol (TCP). It is a lightweight messaging protocol
based on publish/subscribe communication model. The protocol serves server-client
systems where a broker has the role of server. Broker holds the topics that the client
publishes the messages and subscribers of the topic receiving them, so the broker
is responsible for this communication between the publishers and subscribers.
A
topic can have more than a subscriber. Based on that, MQTT is an asynchronous
communication protocol, as all entities take place in parallel order. MQTT provide
reliable transfer through the Quality of Service [7].
Figure 1.2. MQTT centralised broker model
Each MQTT message consists of a fixed header, an optional variable header, a
message payload up to 256 MB, and a Quality Service payload (QoS). MQTT version 3
supports three levels of QoS. These levels determine how the MQTT protocol manages
the content.
• QoS 0: That means that is no guarantee of delivery. It is called fired and
forget as the message is transmitted from the client to the server but is no need
to acknowledge receipt, and the message is not retransmitted.
• QoS 1:
This type of message reveals that the sender sends the message and
is waiting for a PUBACK packet as an acknowledgement receipt. The sender
stores the message and retransmits the message. On this occasion, the message
could be sent or delivered successfully more than once.
• QoS 2:
This level guarantees that each message is received only once and is the
safest and slowest quality of service level. Similar to the three-way handshake of
Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) in this level can be said that it is a four-
way handshake. At this level, the sender and receiver use the original message’s
packet identifier to coordinate the delivery stage. When the receiver receives
==PAGE:23==
1.4 MQTT

a QoS 2, publish packet replies to the sender with a PUBREC packet which
is the acknowledgement receipt. If the sender does not receive this packet, it
sends the PUBLISH message again with a DUP flag. On the other hand, if the
sender receives the PUBREC replies with PUBREL, this packet is a signal to the
receiver to discard all stored states. Finally, the receiver sends the last packet,
PUBCOMP, which informs the sender that it can free the packet identifier.
Through this step, avoid processing the message for a second time.
Figure 1.3. MQTT Packet Structure [8]
MQTT session consists of four phases:
• Connection:
A client triggers a connection by creating a TCP/IP connection
with the broker on pre-defined port 1883 for clear text (unencrypted) commu-
nications or 8883 for encrypted SSL/TLS communications. From IoT perspec-
tives, since the low cost plays a key role most of the time, SSL/TLS communi-
cations are not an option.
• Authentication:
Users can agree to a username and password with the server.
After that can authenticate themselves by sharing in plain text the username
and password in a CONNECT packet, the server answers with a CONNACK
packet through which the server authenticates the client.
• Communication:
During communication, a client has four options, publish
a message, subscribe to a topic, unsubscribe from a topic or ping.
• Termination:
When a client wants to terminate an MQTT session, send a
DISCONNECT message to the broker and then the connection is terminated.
1.4.1
Security
As mentioned before, when a client wants to connect, send a CONNECT packet. In
this packet exist some flags. One is the clean_session flag that tells the broker if
the client wants to establish a persistent session. If this flag is false, the broker will
store the client session, but if this flag is true, the broker establishes a new session
with the client and any saved information is deleted. Another CONNECT flag with
security interest is the keep_alive flag. This parameter sets the period for the broker
and client to keep the session alive without any interaction.
These two parameters have a crucial role regarding the security in MQTT, as an
attacker can establish many sessions and set the keep_alive parameter to a high
period and, as a result, will lead to a DoS attack by exhausting the resources of the
broker.

1.5
Z-Wave
Z-Wave is a wireless radio frequency-based communication protocol. Z-Wave uses a
mesh network topology where nodes communicate through low-energy radio waves,
which operate in the Industrial Scientific and Medical frequency band (ISM). It trans-
mits signals in a frequency range from 865 MHz to 926 MHz. Z-Wave nodes can divide
into two types, controllers are responsible for the inclusion and removal of other nodes,
maintaining the routing table, and slaves, which are the actual end nodes, the smart
devices. As the Z-Wave relies on a mesh network, the nodes that are close to the
controller, most of the time the gateway, can interact directly with other nodes that
can not reach the gateway directly and use other nodes in the network to retransmit.
Each protocol controller has a unique 32-bit HomeID, and that is, to avoid network
overlapping, this ID changes only when the protocol controller is hard resetting. To
minimise battery consumption, nodes can turn to sleep mode, which will occur as of-
fline. Z-Wave consists of five layers application, logic link, adaptive, MAC and PHY
layer [9].
1.5.1
Protocol Stack
1.5.1.1
Physical Layer
The physical layer in Z-Wave is responsible for transmitting data between nodes in
the network and supports three data rates, 9.6, 40 and 100 kbps. The physical layer
consists of the Physical (PHY) protocol data with Physical Service Data Unit, header
and the End of Frame delimiter.
1.5.1.2
MAC Layer
The Medium Access Control (MAC) layer is responsible for managing the Radio
Frequency, and the MAC layer consists of the wireless hardware. For that reason,
Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance (CSMA/CA) is used to get
the medium that uses IEEE 802.11 RTS/CTS. Moreover, MAC layer is responsible
for acknowledgements of frames, data validation and retransmission. There are three
types: Singlecast, where there is only one and single destination address; Multicast,
where the frames are sent to more than one destination address and do not need
acknowledgement as an answer Acknowledgment is the answer of a device when re-
ceiving a singlecast frame [10].
1.5.1.3
Adaptive layer
As mentioned, the adaptive layer consists of three layers, through these layers can
handle the mesh network. NWK layer is responsible for routing. The SAR layer is
responsible for the segmentation and reassembly of datagrams, and the ENC layer

Through this process, the controller breaks the network into groups o nodes based on

1.6
ZigBee
ZigBee is a standard based on network protocol supported by the ZigBee alliance that
uses IEEE802.15.4 as a transport service. ZigBee works on 2.4 GHz with ISM band
up to 250kbps data rate. This wave band differs from the bands of other wireless
networks that operate at the same frequency, which guarantees that ZigBee does not
interfere with other wireless networks. ZigBee networks can support up to 653356
devices, with a distance between them up to 50 meters.
ZigBee devices can be divided into ZigBee physical devices and ZigBee logical
devices. Physical devices can be full-function devices that perform all operations,
including routing, coordinator and sensing tasks. Full-function devices are commonly
powered up through AC-fed, as these devices should always be powered on and ac-
tive. Moreover, physical devices can be reduced function devices that support specific
operations of the IEEE802.15.4 standard. Such devices they are not support routing
functions. They only do everyday tasks like recording and monitoring data or con-
trolling devices.
On the other hand, ZigBee logical devices consist of coordinator devices which are
the network’s root and can work as a bridge to other networks. Each ZigBee network
needs at least one coordinator as it is responsible for initiating the network. The
device chooses the radio frequency channel, the unique network ID and any necessary
operation parameters, such as security keys. Another logical device is the router. A
==PAGE:27==

ZigBee router is an intermediate node that delivers data from other ZigBee devices.
As the router can connect to existing networks of the routers, it works as an extender.
Last, end devices are low-power devices responsible for collecting information through
sensors or switches. Their only function is to transmit the information they collect
to coordinators or routers into the network. This type of device can turn into sleep
mode as they are not always functioning, and is no need to be always active.
ZigBee networks have three types of topologies. The first one is Star topology. In
this type of topology, an Full Function Device (FFD) node acts as the PAN coordi-
nator, and this topology is used for low-range and small networks for better energy
eﬀiciency. A ZigBee network can follow a tree-based topology where an FFD device
acts as a coordinator of subgroups of Reduced Function Device (RFD) that, most of
the time, are end devices. Every FFD in the tree topology network adds the PAN
coordinator as a parent. FFDs cannot communicate directly with each other, leading
to a cluster form of more extensive multicluster networks. On the other hand, in
Peer-to-peer topologies, FFDs devices can communicate with each other and take dif-
ferent routes to reach other FFD nodes that will be determined by a PAN coordinator,
which makes Peer-to-peer networks decentralised.

In this mode, similar to Z-Wave devices, access the channel through Carrier Sense

1.6.1.1
Physical Layer
In the 2.4GHz band, IEEE802.15.4 supports 16 channels and has a bandwidth of
2MHz with a 5MHz distance between each other. The physical layer is responsible
for activating and deactivating a radio transceiver and performs operations on packets
and channels, such as measurements and estimations. The PHY layer can determine
if a channel is busy through Energy detection of a received signal power.
1.6.1.2
MAC Layer
The MAC layer is the Data Link Layer between the Physical and Network layers. This
layer provides data services and management service interfacing to MAC sub-layer
management entity. MAC data service supports the transmission and reception of
MAC Protocol Data Units via Physical data service. Furthermore, it is responsible
for synchronising devices in the beacon-enabled mode by generating beacons and the
inclusion and removal process. It consists of four frames Beacon frame, data frame,
acknowledge frame, MAC command frame.
1.6.1.3
Network Layer
The network layer forms the network and is responsible for routing messages into the
network. Moreover, it is involved in the inclusion and removal process of nodes in

1.6.1.4
Application Layer
The ZigBee application layer is divided into three sub-layers. Application Objects
layer, which is responsible for controlling the protocol layers in ZigBee devices. Each
application object has its unique endpoint number that other application objects can
use to interact with. A single ZigBee device can have up to 240 application objects.
The profile of these objects defines the format of the messages and protocol that these
objects support. The second layer is the ZigBee device object, which is responsible
for discovery, security and binding.
Discovery operation relies on communication
with the MAC layer. Through this, the device object asks about the MAC addresses
of coordinators or routers through unicast messages and, through that process, can
find other nodes or services.
From a security perspective, ZigBee device objects
authenticate and derive keys for data encryption. Support the connection to existing
PAN or the creation of new PANs. In the binding process, the device object binds
nodes to resources, applications or channels. The last application sub-layer is the
support sub-layer that provides an interface between the network layer and application
layers, processes incoming and outgoing frames to secure the transaction of the frames
and establishes cryptographic keys [16].

3.1
Design Overview
Security and privacy concerns are critical aspects of IoT networks. As these networks
collect a large amount of personal and private information, it is important to protect
this data from unauthorised access. Ensuring the availability and confidentiality of
the network is also essential to prevent disruption of services.
IoT networks are exposed to certain attacks that threaten their security, such
as Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) and Jamming attacks. DDoS attacks are
designed to overwhelm the network with a flood of traﬀic, making it impossible for
legitimate traﬀic to pass through. Jamming attacks involve disrupting communication
between devices by broadcasting noise on the same frequency.
To defend against these attacks, ongoing research and development in the areas
of datasets, documentation, and tools for simulating and detecting attacks are neces-
sary. This includes implementing security protocols, such as secure communication
protocols, secure key management, and intrusion detection systems.
Additionally,
regular security audits and penetration testing should be conducted to identify and
address vulnerabilities in the network.
Furthermore, the problem that this thesis is discussing following the implemen-
tation below is the lack of knowledge around IoT attack techniques and tools for
traﬀic capture, which leads to the lack of resources in order to be able to detect those
attacks and find mitigation techniques to minimise the impact that could potentially
have against an IoT network. Without a doubt, Home IoT networks are the networks

the gateway should be available during the jamming attack. Moreover, the placement
of the attacker’s hardware, which will be used for sniﬀing, jamming and replay attacks,
is crucial.
The placement might vary based on the environment, and as a result,
the impact of those attacks will vary too. If the attacker has knowledge about the
network topology and the placement of the node and has access to a location close
to the gateway and the nodes, the possibility of success will dramatically increase.
In this thesis, the maximum distance from the gateway was up to 6 meters, and the
greater the distance, the less chance of success there was.
A necessary point to mention is that the DoS attacks against MQTT and the
Replay attack against Z-Wave are aggressive attacks. A goal of the adversary should
be to remain stealthy and avoid detection by launching the attacks in a more sophis-
ticated way and not continuously creating traﬀic on both occasions.
3.1.2
Scenarios
In order to analyse and examine the threats of a Home IoT network and propose tools
that can help to collect information that will be examined and analysed, we need to
take into consideration some attack scenarios that can simulate the threats in the real
world. Attack categorisation is the first and most important step in understanding
and underlying the threats to the Home IoT network.
• Replay attacks: One scenario would be to create a tool that can capture Z-
Wave or ZigBee network traﬀic and then replay the captured data to control a
specific device on the network. For example, the tool could capture the network
traﬀic of a Z-Wave door lock and then replay that traﬀic to unlock the door.
• Denial of Service (DoS) attacks: Another scenario would be to create a tool
that can launch DoS attacks against Z-Wave and ZigBee networks by flooding
the network with fake devices. For example, the tool could create hundreds of
fake Z-Wave devices and add them to the network, overwhelming the network
and causing it to become unavailable to legitimate devices. Moreover, create
and transmit a large amount of traﬀic against an MQTT broker.
• Jamming attacks: Another scenario would be to create a tool that can launch
jamming attacks against Z-Wave and ZigBee networks by broadcasting noise on
the same frequency as the network. For example, the tool could continuously
broadcast noise on the 868 MHz frequency used by Z-Wave devices and 2.4 GHz
used by ZigBee devices, causing communication between devices to be disrupted.
Emulate different devices: To test the security of the network, it’s a good
practice to emulate different types of devices (e.g. door locks, thermostats, light
==PAGE:43==
3.2 Testbed
29
bulbs) and check if the network is able to detect them and if it’s possible to
take control of them by using a tool that allows you to emulate these devices.
• Sniﬀing tools: The analysation and examination of those attacks need the
collection of traﬀic during normal behaviour and malicious behaviour.
This
will be achieved by capturing traﬀic in Z-Wave and ZigBee networks. Capture
network traﬀic using a wireless adapter that supports the same frequency as
the network (e.g. 868 MHz for Z-Wave, 2.4 GHz for Zigbee) and a software
tool like Wireshark to analyse the captured data. The tool should also be able
to decode the network traﬀic in a meaningful way and provide details such as
network topology, device types, and communication patterns.
• Datasets: To create datasets that can help to detect attacks, you could collect
network traﬀic data under normal and abnormal conditions, such as during a
replay, DoS or jamming attack. This data should include details such as network
device types and communication patterns and also the performance data of the
network during the attack, such as packet loss, delay or a large amount of traﬀic.
• Test with different network topologies: To test the security of the network,
it’s a good practice to test it with different network protocols, check if the
network is affected by the different number of devices, distances, and check if
the devices are still able to communicate by using a tool that allows you to
change the network topology.
Based on the attack categorisation above, we conclude with three different attack
scenarios.
The first one is DoS attack and Information Disclosure attack against
MQTT, and these two attacks are the beginning of the attack flow that will be
proposed below since it is the attack that can provide information for the Home IoT
network to an attacker. Jamming, Replay and DoS attack against Z-Wave network.
Replay attack against Z-Wave is a simulation of the unauthorised use of Z-Wave
devices in the network and DoS attack by emulating different Z-Wave devices. Finally,
a jamming attack against the ZigBee network. Those attack scenarios will lead to
the proposal of attack and sniﬀing tools and to the creation of datasets that will be
further examined.

3.6.1.2
Analysis
We can understand the inclusion, removal and interaction processes into a Z-Wave
network through the sniﬀing process. As mentioned in the IoT Smart Device section,
three out of four devices have no security methods. As a result, the frames were not
encrypted. This led to a clear frame that, with a few modifications, can be used as a
template where we can build similar frames.
Examining the traﬀic flow during inclusion and removal processes, as shown in
Figure 3.17, observed a pattern of frames on both occasions. Specifically, when the
protocol control turns into inclusion or removal mode, a node sends a request via
a Node Info frame, and after that, the controller replies with an Assign ID frame.
During the inclusion, the Assign ID frame is an informal frame that informs the node
that the controller assigned an ID to this node, and the node should be used in the
following frames. In the removal process, the Assign ID frame informs the node that
this ID will be removed from the network, and as a result, the node will not be able
to use it anymore. Finally, it assumed that if the same frames can be transmitted
into the network with other IDs, that can cause the inclusion of fake nodes or the
==PAGE:59==
3.6 Attacks against Z-Wave
45
removal of actual nodes. In the same logic, interaction frames can be analysed and
replayed, leading to the nodes’ unauthorised use. On the first occasion, the attacker
plays the role of a node that wants to be included or removed, and on the second
occasion, an attacker can play the role of controller that commands a node to take
action.
Figure 3.17. Traﬀic during inclusion (Top Image) and removal (Bottom Image) processes
Moreover, take into consideration that when an attacker transmits a Node Info
packet with the Source Node ID of an actual node during the removal process, the
actual node could reply with an Acknowledgement packet that will inform the con-
troller that it does not want to be removed from the network. Based on that, the RF
signal from the actual node should be intercepted to succeed in a malicious removal
process.
These attacks succeed after experiments and trial-error processes. The procedure
of those attacks can vary based on several parameters, such as the SDR that will be
used, the version and firmware of the SDR, the protocol controller and the legitimate
nodes used.

The jamming attack that is used is considered constant jamming. Constant jamming

channels being busy. Since CSMA/CA requires channel sensing before transmitting,
this type of jamming will prevent devices from transmitting since they will perceive
the channel as busy. Figure 3.22 shows the flowgraph that is used with the HackRF

before the transmission. When the jammer switched on, any attempt for communica-

5.3
Discussion
During the attack experiments, it was clear that the most effective attack in RF is
the replay attack in Z-Wave. This attack was able to crush the gateway’s Z-Wave
interface. The reason for that was the packet loss during the attack. The gateway
started an inclusion process with the first attempt of a node to be included in the
network by transmitting a Node Info automatically. The protocol controller turned
on a state where it was waiting for the rest of the packets. Since the rest of the
packets were dropped or the replay attack terminated, the inclusion process was never
completed. The controller was stuck in the inclusion state and could not execute any
other command.
Furthermore, the impact of the replay attack in Z-Wave with more research and
investigation can lead to more weaknesses. This case study successfully found a way
to include fake nodes into a network, but Z-Wave networks follow a mesh topology
==PAGE:84==
70
5 Results
where more than one protocol controller can handle nodes in the network. The next
step can be the inclusion of a fake controller.
Successful inclusion of a controller
will lead to a fake network expansion.
Moreover, replay attacks for unauthorized
manipulation of legitimate nodes of a network have a significant impact in any IoT
network, attacks where it can turn on and off devices randomly or can change the
state of a node into a network, for example, an IoT system that is responsible for
home safety, where binary sensors like door sensors or window sensors are responsible
for triggering on automation for turning on an alarm when a door or window opens,
can intercept these signals and unauthorized report to the gateway that everything
is closed.
Hopefully, as seen from the sections above, the detection methods for this type
of technique are rising, and it is easy to implement. More research and investigation
are needed about feature extraction and machine learning algorithms.
As an im-
provement, automation tools can actively monitor the RF networks and, in specific
intervals, classify the state of a network. Some of the scripts created for the needs
of this project are semi-automated and are also implemented in a way that is easily
adjustable for the needs of feature extraction for any traﬀic like it happened when the
same script with few modifications was able to turn from MQTT feature extractor to
Z-Wave feature extractor.
Moreover, the results after the evaluation of the datasets can evaluate by examin-
ing the reasons behind those results based on each machine learning algorithm.
• KNN: In this specific case, the performance of the KNN algorithm was sat-
isfactory, with an accuracy of 97.15 for the MQTT dataset and 82.76 for the
Z-Wave dataset.
However, it should be noted that KNN is sensitive to the
scale of the features, and it can be affected by irrelevant features and noise in
the data. Additionally, the choice of the value of k can also play a role in the
performance of the algorithm.
• MLP: With an accuracy of 97.40 for the MQTT Dataset and 85.77 for the
Z-Wave dataset. This can be attributed to the ability of the algorithm to learn
complex non-linear relationships between inputs and outputs.
Additionally,
MLP is robust to noise and can handle large amounts of data. A number of
tweaks can be made to the number of layers, the number of neurons in each
layer, as well as the learning rate in order to improve the performance of the
algorithm.
• Gaussian NB: The performance of the GNB algorithm was moderate, with
an accuracy of 71.44 for the MQTT dataset and 84.16 for the Z-Wave dataset.
This can be attributed to the assumption of independence between features,
which may not hold true in this specific case. Additionally, the algorithm also
assumes that the data follows a normal distribution, which may not be the case
for all features. While GNB is a simple and fast algorithm, its performance may
not be as good as other algorithms in cases where the independence assumption
is not met.

leaf node. Random Forest also generates a measure of feature importance, which
can be used to identify the most relevant features in the dataset.

5.4
Challenges and limitations
There have been significant obstacles during this project. Starting from the lack of
end devices, this project would not be able to achieve, and hopefully, as I had access
to some Z-Wave devices because of my student job, I could borrow the Z-Wave Sniffer,
protocol controller and all the Z-Wave end devices. Unfortunately, this was not the
case regarding ZigBee devices. The ZigBee devices that were used all used hard-coded
encryption, so they were not able to earn any information that could lead to similar
attacks as Z-Wave.
In addition, major time cost factors occurred in installing any tool used with
HackRF, like waving-z, GNU Radio and some other tools that, in the end, were
impossible to work with them. Specifically, the first scenario used the HackRFs, one
for receiving and the other for transmitting. In order to achieve that was assumed
that the first HackRF could be used in receiving mode with hackrf_transfer tool
and store the traﬀic in a file, after that a script would read the file with the traﬀic,
modify some parts of the traﬀic or even choose the parts that wanted for transmission
and create another file with the new traﬀic that would transmit through the second
HackRF. This whole process needed to be in real-time and faster as possible. The
waving-z tool was created six years ago and was not maintained at all for the last
four years is prolonged, and also it can be installed in a specific version of Ubuntu
since it needs libraries that also belong to the past. The second major problem was
the installation of GNU Radio, in order to use the modules that have been used in
the project, the only suitable version of GNU Radio is 3.7, and each module that is
used, for example, gr-ieee802-15-4 needs a separate installation. As a result, this led
to the search for a Linux distribution that could support the packets of three different
software. Fortunately, Ubuntu 18.04.6 LTS is the Linux distribution that can support
any necessary library.
Furthermore, the tools used and the HackRF operate very slowly, which causes
problems when we have to cope with RF world, devices in low-frequency are high-
speed and can transmit data in milliseconds. On the other hand, HackRF, most of
the time, could not capture or transmit this data at that speed. As a result, the traﬀic
that was captured was missing frames or was not correct. In TX mode, HackRF used
with hackrf_transfer tool was again slow, leading most of the time the gateway to
reach the time that can wait for a reply packet from the fake node. This significantly
impacted the attacks’ performance and occasions when the HackRF was used for
sniﬀing.
Future work regarding sniﬀing or attacks in radio frequencies can consider some
other SDR with more power and capabilities. For example, SDR produce a stronger
signal that can cause better jamming attacks or capture signals in a better way. Terms
of jamming attacks can be considered Reactive or Selective jamming attacks.
Overall, attacks against radio frequencies or that require external hardware that
documentation or tool is lacking have low possibilities of success in real-world scenar-
ios and need strong knowledge about this topic.

Conclusion and Future
Work
This thesis aims to investigate IoT networks and reveal traﬀic analysis and attack
detection methods. Topics around IoT networks are studied extensively, but based
on the low cost and low power characteristics, security topics are relegated and often
not researched due to the lack of hardware simulation approaches.
This project conducted methods and tools that can help for experiments and
further research around IoT security. Utilising techniques for sniﬀing traﬀic in low
frequencies and tools that help build a testbed for experiments successfully state a
way that can lead to further research. Moreover, proposing solutions to obstacles and
requirements arising in this type of project opens the way to a safe time in subsequent
attempts.
Currently, very few samples of IoT traﬀic dataset exist, making the study and
research for network analysis and detection techniques diﬀicult. Once the testbed
environment and the setup of the IoT network implement, it can experiment with
a different types of devices from both three communication protocols WiFi, Z-Wave
and ZigBee. Collected traﬀic can confirm the effectiveness of the implementation and
the methods used.
After analysing the IoT traﬀic, we have demonstrated the impact of the attacks
against the network. Furthermore, it is shown that by the evaluation of specific traﬀic
features can, such attacks can be detected with high accuracy.
Future work can address more eﬀicient methods and techniques for sniﬀing and
attack techniques, for example, through more sophisticated and powerful hardware
or improvements of existing tools like the ones mentioned in this report.
