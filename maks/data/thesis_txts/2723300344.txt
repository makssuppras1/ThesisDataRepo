Abstract
Exopolysaccharides, EPS, have rheological properties beneficial for a range of food prod-
ucts, especially in the dairy industry as they can act as viscosifying agents. Especially
EPS produced by LAB is of interest, as LAB is already widely used in the food industry
and have GRAS status. Understanding the mechanisms underlying EPS synthesis creates
a foundation for engineering EPS biosynthesis.
In this study, efforts were made to enable the non-EPS producing strain L. lactis MG1363
to produce EPS by creating plasmids containing eps genes from L. lactis AH1 isolated
from Finnish viili, a highly viscous fermented dairy product. As it is not known whether
all genes take active part in EPS synthesis, EPS was purified and the monosaccharide
composition was analyzed through HPAEC-PAD. Glucose, galactose, rhamnose, fucose,
xylose, mannose and arabinose were detected but only glucose were above quantification
limit. pCM1 was made to contain wzx, wzy, lytR and epsRXCDBE, genes known to be
essential for EPS synthesis.
pCM2, was made to contain epsH and epsFG, encoding
glycosyltransferases. These were chosen based on the gene cluster layout. Both plasmids
were assembled by Gibson assembly and transformed into E. coli DH5 alfa. No colonies
with the created plasmids were obtained, however transformation frequency of positive
controls was very low, indicating poor electrocompetent cells. Ruthenium red milk plates
were tested as a method for quickly determining if plasmids enabled L. lactis MG1363 to
produce EPS and deemed unsuitable.

1
Introduction
1.1
Aim of Study
The aim of this project is to examine the structure of EPS produced by the strains L.
lactis subsp. cremoris AH1 and L. lactis subsp. cremoris ES1. The EPS from L. lactis
ES1 is expected to have a structure similar to EPS produced by L. lactis subsp. cremoris
NIZO B40. Plasmids containing genes from L. lactis AH1 will be made, based on the
structure of EPS from this strain. These plasmids will be transformed into L. lactis subsp.
cremoris MG1363, a non-EPS-producing strain, to see if this strain then will be able to
produce EPS. In connection to this, a simple method for quick and easy detection of EPS
production will be developed to be used when playing around with genes to see which
will be essential for EPS production. This will lay a foundation for future engineering
efforts.
2
Theory
2.1
Benefits of Exopolysaccharides
Exopolysaccharides, EPS, are polymers consisting of monosaccharides which are bound
together by glycosidic linkages. EPS varies on a range of different parameters. It can
be a homopolysaccharide (HoPS) by consisting of only one type of monosaccharide or
it can be a heteropolysaccharide (HePS) consisting of several different monosaccharides.
Non-sugar decorations can appear on the monosaccharides such as phosphate, glycerol
and acetyl (Ruas-Madiedo et al. 2002). EPS also varies in degree of branching as it can
be linear or highly branched and the ratio and linkages of the different types of monosac-
charides also varies. Thus, EPS also varies in molecular weight, anomeric configuration
and conformation (Zeidan et al. 2017, Ruas-Madiedo et al. 2002).
The variation and combination of these parameters are said to be strain-specific, so each
strain is thought to produce a unique EPS. Heteropolymeric EPS almost always contain
D-galactose, D-glucose and L-rhamnose in different ratios but N-acetylglucosamine, N-
acetylgalactosamine and glucuronic acid are also present in some cases (De Vuyst and
Degeest 1999, Ruas-Madiedo et al. 2002).
1
==PAGE:8==
LAB is of high importance in the fermented dairy industry where LAB is used as starter
cultures for yoghurt, sour cream, cheese and products like cottage cheese and kefir. During
growth, the fermentation of lactose results in production of lactic acid, which acidifies the
milk and neutralizes the negative charge on milk proteins which leads to a coagulation
creating a desired texture for the products, as well as giving the product the pleasant
mildly acidic taste known in sour-milk products (Ruas-Madiedo et al. 2002). The lower-
ing in pH also prolongs shelf-life, as it is hard for spoilage organisms to grow at the low
pH conditions. LAB also converts other compounds (proteins, organic acids, sugars and
lipids) into characteristic flavour and aroma compounds (Ruas-Madiedo et al. 2002).
When it comes to fermented dairy products, consumers are looking for smooth, thick and
creamy products with no or very low syneresis (Buldo et al. 2016). These properties of the
fermented milk can be enhanced through additives. Dairy proteins and polysaccharides
like hydrocolloids, starch and pectin are often used as viscosifying and stabilizing agents
in dairy products (Zeidan et al. 2017). However, many of these are either chemically or
enzymatically modified, which puts a restriction on their use in food.
Microbial EPS can be used to increase viscosity instead due to their unique rheolog-
ical properties which will be elaborated in section 2.2.
Commercially available EPS
from microorganisms is already used in the industry. This includes xanthan produced
by Xanthomonas campestris and gellan produced by Pseudomonas elodea. However, as
X. campestris and P. elodea are plant pathogens, the use of these is also restricted and
requires labelling (JB Prajapat 2013). As most LAB have GRAS status and many have
been used in the food industry for years, LAB produced EPS would be a good alternative
providing a cleaner label. This is beneficial, as consumers are caring more and more about
the ingredients and additives in their food products are opting for clean-labelled products.
Increasing health awareness in society has led to consumers asking for low-fat products.
Milk fat plays an important role in the sensory experience of fermented dairy products,
as it is contributes to texture and creaminess, thus reduction of fat ultimately leads to
poorer sensory experience. The rheological properties of EPS results in a firmer body
and increased creaminess, making EPS ideal for use in low-fat products as the sensory
2
==PAGE:9==
experience is then kept (Lynch:2018). However, different EPS affect the milk matrix
differently, as certain characteristics is related to high viscosity, which will be discussed
in next section.
Besides important rheological properties EPS may also have secondary health-promoting
effects. Probiotics are defined as “live microorganisms which when administered in ad-
equate amounts confer a health benefit on the host” by the World Health Organization
(Consultation 2006). A prebiotic is defined as “a substrate that is selectively utilized by
host microorganisms conferring a health benefit” (Consensus:2017). Many LAB are
being used as probiotics, some of which also produces EPS. EPS has been suggested to
have prebiotic effects and be connected to the probiotic effects of these particular LAB,
as EPS is not digested by the host or the producing strain itself but has been suggested
to promote growth and activity of other beneficial bacteria in the gut (Patel et al. 2012).
EPS have also been suggested to have range of immunomodulatory effects. Examples of
this includes EPS being able to reduce or inhibit biofilms formed by pathogenic bacteria
and stimulating cytokine production and antigen-presenting cells (Jur´aˇskov´a et al. 2022;
Liu et al. 2011).
2.2
Relationship between structure and rheological effects
The rheological effects of EPS have been shown to be dependent of several factors. HePS
vs. HoPS, capsular polysaccharides (CPS) or excreted polysaccharides (EPS), ropy vs.
non-ropy strains along with structural factors of EPS as molecular mass, backbone flexi-
bility, charge and level of branching all influence the effects EPS pose in dairy products.
Hassan et al. 2002 studied rheological effects of milk fermented with ropy cultures, non-
ropy encapsulated cultures and unencapsulated cultures. Streptococcus thermophilus and
Lactobacillus delbrueckii ssp. Bulgaricus strains were used in the experiments. It was
found that both ropy polysaccharides and capsular polysaccharides increased viscosity,
but capsular polysaccharides resulted in low shear stress values while highest shear stress
values were obtained with ropy strains resulting in a more stretchable structure.
3
==PAGE:10==
Faber et al. 1998 highlighted the importance of molecular mass as they studied EPS with
same monosaccharide composition and branching but different molecular mass. S. ther-
mophilus Rs was shown to be non-ropy producing EPS with a molecular mass of 2.6 ∗103
kDa whereas S. thermophilus Sts was shown to be ropy producing EPS with a molecular
mass of 3.7 ∗103 kDa.
Gent`es et al. 2011 studied the effects of charge, degree of brancing and flexibility of back-
bone of EPS had on gel formation and rheological properties including firmness, apparent
viscosity and syneresis. Seven strains where studied -three S. thermiphilus strains and
four L. delbrueckii strains including a non-ropy control strain of each. The character-
istics of the EPS included the combinations: neutral, flexible, branched EPS, anionic,
stiff, linear EPS and neutral, stiff, branched EPS. The concentration of the EPS was also
taken into consideration, and it was found that the different rheological outputs were not
influenced by the concentration of the EPS, but rather the structural differences. It was
concluded that viscosity, firmness and syneresis were improved the most by EPS with a
high molecular weight, low degree of branching and a stiff backbone. It was also found
that negatively charged EPS contributed negatively to the syneresis.
In general EPS structure is deemed more important than concentration and EPS with
high molecular mass, stiff backbone and low branching level results in a viscous creamy
texture, while highly branched EPS with low molecular mass and flexible backbone results
in products with lower viscosity.
One of the best characterized EPS producers is L. lactis subs. cremoris NIZO B40. The
structure of this EPS has been determined by Van Casteren et al. 1998. The structure
is to be found in figure 1.
The EPS comprises four monosaccharides (glucose, galac-
tose, galactose-3-phosphate, and rhamnose) in a 2:1:1:1 ratio and contains a repeating
backbone unit of [-4)--D-glucose-(1,4)--D-galactose-3-phosphate-(1-], with two branches
at the galactose-3-phosphate moiety. These branches consist of α-1,2-L-rhamnose and
α-1,3-D-galactose, respectively.

2.3
Exopolysaccharide Biosynthesis
EPS can be synthesized via four different pathways. HoPS synthesis is simpler than HePS
synthethis and involves only one glucosyl transferase. Sucrose acts as a donor and enzymes
belonging to the glycosyl hydrolase (GH) family is then responsible for the polymeriza-
tion of the HoPS by transferring the resulting monosaccharide to the HoPS chain. The
specifics of this process depend on the specific HoPS (Torino et al. 2015). HePS can be
produced by either three ways: the synthase dependent pathway, where polymerization
and transport is performed by a single synthase complex, the ABC-transporter pathway
where the polysaccharide is anchored via an acid linker to the cytoplasmic space of the
inner membrane and is assembled by several glycosyl transferases before being exported
by an efflux pump consisting of ABC transporters lining the inner membrane, or the Wzy-
dependent pathway (Zeidan et al. 2017). As the Wzy-dependent pathway is the pathway
used by Lactococci, this pathway will be elaborated on further in the following.
First step in EPS synthesis via the Wzy-dependent pathway is synthesis of activated sugar
precursors, which takes place in the cytoplasm. The sugar precursors are synthesized
from glycolytic intermediates, typically glucose-6-phosphate and fructose-6-phosphate,
via multistep pathways. The ability to produce different sugar precursors relies on the
genes present in the EPS producing strain and this also determines the monosaccharides
present in the EPS (Zeidan et al. 2017). The second step is synthesis of the repeating unit
where the first sugar precursor is attached to an isoprenoid lipid carrier, undecaprenyl
phosphate (Und-P), found on the cyloplasmic membrane of the cell.
This first sugar
nucleotide is then catalyzed by the priming glycosyltransferase. This is followed by the
5
==PAGE:12==
sequential addition of sugar nucleotides performed by other glycosyltransferases (Nguyen
et al. 2020). The priming GT is a membrane-bound polyprenyl-P sugar-1-P transferase
and it catalyzes the formation of a phosphate bond when transferring a phosphorylated
monosaccharide from the sugar nucleotide to the Und-P. It is the energy stored in this
bond that is used for the sequential work of the other GTs as the membrane-bound Und-
P-P-sugar functions as substrate (Zeidan et al. 2017).

2.4
Exopolysaccharide Gene Clusters
Various gene clusters for EPS production have been found among LAB. The high diver-
sity among clusters is partly due to mobile genetic elements, as insertion sequence (IS)
elements, residing next to or within the gene clusters (Zeidan et al. 2017). Wzy-dependent
gene clusters have primarily been described in the three genera Streptococcus, Lactoccus
and Lactobacillus. These clusters have operon structures and can reside on either the chro-
mosome or a plasmid (Zeidan et al. 2017). Genes present in the cluster can be grouped
based on function and the modular gene organization is fairly conserved through the gen-
era. However, the nomenclature used for appointing the genes has varied between genera
and even strains, as the eps genes has usually been named by alphabetically order instead
of function. This means that genes having the same functions in different strains may be
named differently throughout the literature. Zeidan et al. 2017 proposed a nomenclature
adopted from Reeves et al. 1996 and based on the nomenclature for S. thermophilus. All
following is based on this nomenclature.
Figure 3, is depicting the eps gene clusters of L. lactis NIZO B40 and L. lactis ES1 with
the old annotation used in Van Kranenburg, Marugg, et al. 1997 and the new annotation
suggested by Zeidan et al. 2017. Genes in an eps gene cluster can be divided into mod-
ulatory genes, assembly genes and glycosyltransferases (GTs). In a generic eps cluster,
assembly machinery genes consist of epsE responsible for initiation, wzy encoding the
polymerase, wzx encoding the flippase and epsA responsible for attachment. Modulation
genes include the phosphoregulatory module epsBCD which is made up by a tyrosine-
kinase (epsC and epsD) and a phosphatase (epsB) (Nierop Groot and Kleerebezem 2007;
Zeidan et al. 2017). GTs are as mentioned responsible for the further phosphorylation

and assembly of the repeating unit and the number and specifics of these varies between
strains.

2.6
Gibson Assembly
Gibson assembly is a technique developed by Daniel G. Gibson which relies on matching
base pair overhangs and an exonuclease activity. This technique allows assembly of DNA
fragments regardless of end compatibility and size. The user ensures matching base pair
overhangs through strategic design of primers resulting in DNA fragments with 15-80 bp
overlaps. The DNA fragments are then mixed with Gibson Assembly Master Mix, which
contains a DNA exonuclease, DNA polymerase and DNA ligase in one single buffer. The
DNA exonuclease creates single stranded 3’ overhangs making ends of the overlapping
regions complementary, thus facilitating annealing of the DNA fragments.
The DNA
polymerase then fills in gaps of the annealed fragments and DNA ligase seals nicks leaving
the DNA fragments fully assembled to one fragment (Gibson et al. 2009 + NEB #2611).

3.4
EPS Purification
Following procedure for purification of EPS is based on protocol received from Paula Gas-
par at Chr. Hansen. All centrifugation steps were performed at 10000g for one hour at 4
◦C, unless otherwise is stated.
After to days of growth, growth media was poured into centrifugation tubes and cen-
trifuged. Pellet was discarded and trichloroacetic acid was added to the supernatant to
a final concentration of 20% and left for precipitation at 4 ◦C with agitation for two
hours. Resulting mixture was centrifuged. 2x volume of 96% ethanol was added to the
supernatant, which was left for overnight precipitation at 4 ◦C with agitation before being
centrifuged. Supernatant was then discarded and pellet was washed with 10-40 ml 50%
ethanol before being centrifuged. Washing step was repeated twice, making three steps in
total. The final EPS pellet was resuspended in 4 ml sterile water and pellet was dissolved
using a pipette tip. Resuspended pellet was then poured into 15 ml falcon tubes and
sonicated for 2x 20 minutes in an Emmi D20Q water bath sonicator.
The solution was then dialysed using a Pur-A-Lyzer Mega Dialysis Kit (PURG10010) and
protocol for this was followed. Columns were put in beakers containing approximately
800 ml sterile water and left at four degrees for agitation provided by a magnet stirrer.
Dialysis water was changed twice with 9-16 hours intervals. Samples were then divided
into Eppendorf tubes and lyophilized under centrifugation. Resulting product was kept
at -20 ◦until EPS structure analysis was performed.
3.5
Determination of EPS monosaccharide composition
Following was performed and written by Jesper Holck. Purified EPS was hydrolyzed ac-
cording to de Vuyst et al. (L. De Vuyst et al. 1998) with modifications. The purified
and lyophilized EPS was resuspended, divided in two aliquots for technical reproducibil-
ity purpose, and re-lyophilized in screw cap tubes (Sarstedt, Germany). The EPS was
hydrolyzed with 200 µL 6 M trifluoroacetic acid at 100 ◦C for 3 hours at 700 rpm agi-
tation. After hydrolysis, the samples were cooled to room temperature and dried under
a stream of nitrogen. The recovery for each monosaccharide was determined by hydrol-
ysis of a sample containing relevant monosaccharides.
Prior to analysis, the samples
18
==PAGE:25==
were resolubilized in milliQ water, diluted and filtered through a 0.22µ nylon filter. The
monosaccharide composition was determined by high performance anion-exchange chro-
matography with pulsed amperometric detection (HPAEC-PAD) on a Dionex ICS5000
system (Dionex, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) using a CarboPac PA1 column and post-column
addition of sodium hydroxide as described previously (Zeuner et al. 2018). The com-

monosaccharide in lowest abundance.

Discussion
5.1
EPS structure
By looking at the very similar EPS gene structures of L. lactis NIZO B40 and L. lactis
ES1, it would be expected that the EPS structures were very similar. The EPS from L.
lactis ES1 was determined in molar ratio, and can be compared with the EPS structure of
L. lactis NIZO B40 described by Van Casteren et al. 1998. Van Casteren et al. 1998 found
EPS B40 to have a molar ratio of 2:1:1:1 of glucose, galactose, galactose-3-phosphate and
rhamnose. The data of EPS from L. lactis ES1 showed a molar ratio of 2:1.15:1:0.1 of
33
==PAGE:40==
glucose, galactose, rhamnose and glucuronic acid. The amount of glucose, galactose and
rhamnose fits well with the ratio of EPS B40.
The behavior of potential galactose-3-phosphate during acid hydrolysis was uncertain,
but most likely the phosphate would be cleaved off, as it is in the case described by Van
Casteren et al. Phosphate determination was not performed in the experiments prior
this report. As phosphate would have a negative charge, it would be expected to elute
around the same time point as uronic acids in general. Our data showed peaks around the
uronic acids in the dionex chromatography, especially around glucuronic acid. Potential
phosphate could be eluted at that time point. It could therefore be possible, that some
of the detected galactose would have been a part of galactose-3-phosphate and phosphate
has been eluted instead of uronic acids, as glucuronic acid was detected for EPS from
L. lactis ES1 when no glucuronic acid has been determined for EPS B40. It should also
be kept in mind that this work has been based on few samples and this is not a 100%
thorough determination of the EPS structure of L. lactis ES1, as more tests should be
conducted.
While the samples from L. lactis ES1 behaved as regular samples for monosaccharide
detection, samples from L. lactis AH1 had a very low signal and was not fit for dilution.
During the EPS purification from growth in SA media, visible EPS pellets were formed in
the sample from L. lactis ES1, whereas pellets were almost non-existent for L. lactis AH1
samples. Growth in milk media displayed larger EPS pellets from both strains, however
these samples were not analyzed due to technical problems.
A range of different monosaccharides were detected in the analyzed samples from growth
from SA media, however, except for glucose, these were below quantification limits. De-
tected monosaccharides were comprised of glucose, galactose, rhamnose, fucose, xylose,
mannose and arabinose. When looking at the dionex chromatograms, attacthed in ap-
pendix, peaks also appeared at galacturonic acid and glucuronic acid. However, as these
peaks were inconsistent between samples, and also present in blank sample, these were
disregarded. As with samples from L. lactis ES1, phosphate determination was not per-
formed and some of the signal around uronic acids may have been due to phosphate.
34
==PAGE:41==
Unknown elements have also been detected in samples from both strains. As the concen-
tration in the L. lactis AH1 samples was so low, these were more sensitive for detection of
unknown elements. The presence of N-acetylgalactosamine (glcNac) has been reported for
other EPSs (Zeidan et al. 2017). glcNac monomer elution would take place after glucose
elution as shown by Cataldi et al. 1999. Peaks assigned unknown lie right after glucose in
all the dionex diagrams, suggesting that a fraction of undetermined monosaccharide may
be assigned to glcNac.
Though most were below quantification limit, presence of so many different types of
monosaccharides in EPS from L. lactis AH1 could suggest the need for many different
glycosyltransferases suggesting that many of the glycosyltransferases in the eps gene clus-
ter are active in the EPS production.
5.2
pCM1 and pCM2
As it is not known if all genes in the eps gene cluster of L. lactis AH1 (4) are active and
contribute to the production of EPS, this project set out to make plasmids containing
different combinations of the genes and see if these would enable the EPS non-producer
strain L. lactis MG1363 to produce EPS.
Biosynthesis of EPS through the Wzx/Wzy dependent pathway requires a priming glyco-
syltransferase (encoded by epsE), additional glycosyltransferases for sequential addition
of sugar nucleotides, a wzx flippase to flip the repeating unit to the other side of the
membrane and a wzy polymerase to couple the reapeating units. The generic modulation
module of epsCDB required as well. The eps gene cluster for L. lactis AH1 is shown in
figure 4. As epsR encodes a repressor and lytR is thought to play a role in attachment
and release of polysaccharides, these genes are thought to be active and important for
the production of EPS. The first plasmid to be made, pCM1, would contain the genes
epsRXCDBE, wzx, wzy and lytR, known to be involved in EPS production in L. lactis
AH1. This plasmid, displayed in figure 7, was first created in silico with the design of
primers with overhangs that would result in overlapping regions making Gibson Assem-
35
==PAGE:42==
bly possible. Though the layout of the L. lactis AH1 eps gene cluster suggest additional
promoters present, the promoter from which wzx and wzy is transcribed has not been
determined. Therefore, the primers were designed to change the direction of these genes,
so they would be transcribed from the epsR promoter as the rest of the EPS genes in
pCM1. The orientation of lytR was kept, as this orientation is consistent throughout EPS
gene clusters. pAK80 was chosen as vector, providing replication in both E. coli and L.
lactis along with a selective marker of erythromycin resistance.
To get an idea of how many glycosyltransferases would be needed to produce the EPS
of L. lactis AH1, the number of different monosaccharides present in the EPS was deter-
mined. As these results were obtained in the end of the project, the reasoning behind the
second plasmid was based of the layout of the gene cluster. As epsFG lies in extension
of epsRXCDBE, it would be likely that these genes are also transcribed from the epsR
promoter and be active and play a part in production. Another student (unpublished
data) found promoter activity in front of the epsH gene. This promoter is likely to tran-
scribe epsH and even wzx and thereby epsH would most likely also be active and take a
part. When making the in silico creation of the plasmid, see figure 11, the primers were
designed to flip the orientation of epsH and the promoter was also used for epsFG. The
plasmid pCI3340 was chosen as vector providing replication in both E. coli and L. lactis
along with a selective marker of chloramphenicol resistance.
The plasmids were transformed into E. coli DH5 and plated on selective plates with re-
spect to each plasmid. As no colonies were formed, PCRs were made to investigate if the
Gibson assembly of pCM1 had been successful. The results of these can be seen in figure
16. The lanes are generally quite smeared and a lot of different bands are present in the
different lanes. Based on the gel, it looks like a wanted band of wzx wzy is present (at
2500 bp), along with a wanted band of wzy lytR (at 2000 bp) in lane 2 and a wanted
band of lytR pAK80 (at 6000 bp). The overall tendency of the gel may be due to too high
template DNA concentration. According to NNajafov et al. 2017 too high template con-
centration can result in smeared bands and multiple PCR products. 1 µl Gibson assembly
was used as template for each PCR. Following calculations are to be found in appendix.
In total, 425 ng DNA was used for the Gibson assembly. The total volume of the Gibson
36
==PAGE:43==
assembly was 20 µl, meaning that in 1µl 21.25 ng DNA would be present. Najafov et al.
2017 suggest having a final template concentration below 10 ng/µl. With the total volume
of 50 µl in the PCR reaction, final concentration would be 0.425 which is way below the
limit. Other reasons may be too many cycles or too long extension times. Extension time
followed to be 15 seconds per kilobase which should be fitting, but number of cycles were
in the high end of 35 cycles. Another reason could be a high G/C content, but as most
of the DNA in pCM1 is of Lactococcus origin, which is known to be low in G/C content
this reason is not considered.
For both transformations, only 10-20 colonies were formed on the positive control plates.
Dower et al. 1988 obtained 109 to 1010 transformants per µg DNA in E. coli DH5 alpha
using electroporation. To estimate amount of DNA used, gels have been made with 1-2
µl of purified plasmids. These can be seen in figure 28 in appendix. From the gel 18
ng/µl purified pAK80 was estimated, making 0.00036 µg DNA in the two µl 10−2 pAK80
used for the electrocompetent cells. With this amount, a frequency around 105 colonies
would be expected. For pCI3340 37.5 ng/µl purified plasmid was estimated from the gel
resulting in 0.000375 µg DNA in the 2 µl used for the transformation, also resulting in
an expected frequency around 105 colonies. This suggests poor electrocompetent cells
have been used and the missing colonies with pCM1 and pCM2 is due to the state of
the electrocompetent cells rather than the plasmids not being assembled through Gibson
assembly.
5.3
Ruthenium red milk plates
Ruthenium red milk plates were tried out as a method for detecting EPS producing
strains. Ruthenium red stains the bacterial cell wall, producing red/pink colonies. As
EPS is surrounds the cells, it works as a protective layer thereby prevents ropy colonies
from turning red/pink (Stingele et al. 1996). Eventually, as EPS may be broken down, it
the cells can become coloured. The ruthenium red milk plates were made as according to
Stingele et al. 1996, which had success in distinguishing L. lactis MG1363 transformed
with pFS101 containing the entire eps gene cluster of S. thermophilus Sfi6 from L. lac-
tis MG1363 only harboring pJIM2279 without an eps gene cluster. Non-EPS-producing
37
==PAGE:44==
colonies were very clearly red, while colonies harboring pFS101 remained clearly white.
Two tendencies were observed in the experiments prior this report with either L. lactis
MG1363 turning pink first, followed by L. lactis ES1 a few days later or all three strains
turned pink fairly quickly. As the experiments showed inconsistent results, this method
is deemed non-applicable for determining if different inserts results in L. lactis MG1363
producing EPS. As the followed method was developed for S. thermophilus, this method
may not work as optimal for lactococci. Bouzar et al. 1996 also followed this method
working with Lactobacillus delbrueckii ssp. Bulgaricus where the method failed to distin-
guish between EPS producing strains and EPS-non-producing strains.
To assess EPS production other methods can be used. Growing strains in milk media and
afterwards measuring the flow-through time of the solution through a serological pipette
can be used to assess viscosity. The higher viscosity the longer flow-through time. By
comparing the flow-through timings of L. lactis MG1363 and L. lactis MG1363 with the
plasmids inserted it may be determined if addition of eps genes has led to EPS produc-
tion. A similar method has been used by Gent`es et al. 2011 where they created a plastic
funnel where pass through time was noted throughout the fermentation.
5.4
Future perspectives
Transformation of pCM1 and pCM2 was not successful, most likely due to poor electro-
competent E. coli cells. For a continuation of this project first step would be to obtain
different electrocompetent cells with higher transformation frequencies. This could be
done by only transforming pAK80 and pCI3340 at different concentrations to assess the
general transformation frequency. After confirming reasonably transformation frequency
a new transformation with pCM1 and pCM2, respectively, could be performed. After
obtaining colonies and confirming the plasmids, the plasmids should be purified from E.
coli cells and then transformed into L. lactis MG1363.
Meanwhile another method of EPS detection should be testet, as ruthenium red milk
plates showed to be unsuitable. Using a serological pipette to assess flow-through timings
38
==PAGE:45==
of L. lactis AH1, L. lactis ES1 and L. lactis MG1363 to compare if a difference is found
between the EPS producing strains and L. lactis MG1363, is suggested. If this method
is confirmed to give a good indication, growth can be performed with the cells containing
pCM1 and pCM2 and it can be assessed if the flow-through times for L. lactis MG1363
has changed.
It would be interesting to analyze more EPS samples to see if the ratio between monosac-
charides is consistent as well as obtaining samples with a higher signal from L. lactis AH1,
to see if the monosaccharides can reach above the quantification limit. It is suggested to
perform analysis on samples obtained from growth in milk media, as EPS production
seemed to be higher in milk. It would also be interesting to see if growth in different
media affect EPS production and composition.
As the EPS composition suggested presence of multiple monosaccharides additional plas-
mids containing more glycosyltransferases could be made and transformed into a strain
containing pCM1 and pCM2 to see if this changes the viscosity and ropiness further.
Given the time, strains with different combinations of plasmids could be grown and the
EPS from each could be purified and analyzed to get a more specific idea of the role of
the specific glycosyltransferases.
Petersen et al. 2013 made a tool for site-specific integration that allows multiple inte-
gration into the chromosome with removal of selective marker. This method utilizes the
phage TP901-1 integrase and the tool was demonstrated by integration xylose utilization
genes into L. lactis MG1363. If this method can be used for multiple rounds of inte-
gration, it could be used for adding glycosyltransferases one at a time. This can used
to assess the effect of the individual glycosyltransferases. Great knowledge about roles
of glycosyltransferases could be obtained by making such a library, that would be useful
for future engineering efforts. If it can be possible to define the structure of the EPS by
choosing which glycosyltransferases to add, it will be possible to engineer LAB to produce
EPS meant for a specific purpose. However, the function of a glycosyltransferase may also
rely on the function of a previous glycosyltransferase and therefore a “pick and choose”
approach is not possible.

6
Conclusion
EPS from L. lactis ES1 was shown to consist of glucose, galactose, rhamnose and glu-
curonic acid in the ratio 2:1:1:0.1. Most monosaccharides detected in EPS from L. lactis
AH1 were below quantification limit, but could suggest the presence of glucose, galactose,
rhamnose, fucose, xylose, mannose and arabinose.
Via PCR products it can be concluded that fragments for making the two plasmids, pCM1
and pCM2 were obtained. However, the transformation of these plasmids into E. coli was
unsuccessful, most likely because of the use of poor electrocompetent cells. By using elec-
trocompetent with higher transformation frequency it can be determined if the assembly
of pCM1 and pCM2 was successful. A PCR analysis of pCM1 was made, indicating that
this plasmid was assembled, no PCR analysis was made for pCM2.
Due to inconsistent results, the use of ruthenium red milk plates for detecting EPS pro-
duction was deemed unapplicable in this case. Measuring flow-through time of growth
media through a serological pipette is suggested instead.
