Abstract
The human brain, a masterpiece of nature’s design, orchestrates a dynamic network, excelling in
diverse tasks. Its intricate complexity spans scales, enabling dynamic interaction with the
surroundings and offering insights into consciousness, thought, and action. Diagnosing psychiatric
disorders is challenging due to complexity, heterogeneity, and subjective symptoms, further
complicated by genetic, environmental, and psychological factors. Structural and functional brain
alterations inform diagnostic approaches and therapies for disorders like schizophrenia. Functional
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) detects neural activity changes, which can reveal clusters of
synchronized brain regions, forming specialized functional units. These units, called functional
networks or modules, collaborate for cognitive or sensory functions. Statistical methods and advanced
algorithms construct comprehensive network maps from fMRI data, deepening our understanding of
brain function. The Functional Segregation and Integration Model (FSIM), an extended Gaussian
Mixture Model (GMM), aims to identify these cortical subdivisions and explore their intricacies across
populations.
This thesis investigates the utility of stochastic methods, specifically Stochastic Gradient Descent
(SGD) and stochastic EM algorithms applied with both voxels-wise sampling (sEM (v)) and
subjects-wise sampling (sEM (b)), as alternatives to the traditional EM algorithm for fitting the
parameters of the FSIM to large-scale datasets. These stochastic methods are thoroughly tested and
analyzed using simulated data and a subset of the fMRI data from the Decoded Neurofeedback
(DecNef) dataset, which includes both healthy individuals and patients with schizophrenia. Various
assessments are conducted to evaluate the stochastic methods’ ability to replicate original parameters
and produce dependable outcomes. Additionally, subject scalings derived from the FSIM are
employed in an attempt to classify the diagnosis (healthy/schizophrenia) using logistic regression and
Support Vector Machine (SVM).
Based on the analysis of simulated data, both sEM (v) and sEM (b) demonstrated performance on
par with the traditional EM algorithm while exhibiting robustness in relation to initialization.
However, the SGD algorithm struggled to converge towards meaningful model parameters, despite
sharing a similar log-likelihood with the other algorithms. Further investigation revealed that the use
of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) for reparameterizing the subject-specific parameter Ps in the
SGD caused this convergence issue. Moreover, we applied the stochastic EM algorithms to a subset of
50 subjects from the DecNef dataset, where sEM (b) failed to yield reliable results and attain the
same log-likelihood as the EM algorithm. In contrast, sEM (v) displayed performance akin to that of
the EM algorithm. When applying sEM (b) and sEM (v) on the complete DefNec dataset, the
classification results did not exhibit any discernible patterns in the obtained subject scalings.
Consequently, no classification with better performance than the zero-rule accuracy was identified. In
future research, it is advisable to investigate methods for achieving robust Singular Value
Decomposition (SVD) differentiation, and optimizing hyperparameters for the stochastic models.
Additionally, conducting a comparative analysis between the cluster parcellations generated by the
unsupervised model and established functional brain regions could serve as validation, verifying the
model’s accuracy in identifying these regions.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The human brain stands as a testament to nature’s intricate craftsmanship, forming an immensely
dynamic network that excels in a diverse range of tasks (Cohen and D’esposito 2016). Exhibiting a
notable level of complexity, the brain’s dynamics involve swift adjustments in network states,
facilitated by interactions among its constituents, aiming to optimize its temporal global progression.
Components spanning from micro to macro scales, neural cells, cluster formations, brain nuclei, and
regions, engage in intricate interactions to create an ever-changing, dynamic representation of the
brain, enabling its interaction with the surroundings (Van Den Heuvel et al. 2009). This intricate
complexity presents both a formidable challenge and an unique opportunity for academic exploration.
By methodically untangle the brain’s mysterious complexities, we might gain deep insights into areas
like thinking, actions, and the deep mystery of human consciousness.
Identifying Brain Networks
Numerous studies have concentrated on the task of identifying the brain’s network using functional
Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) due to its capability of detecting alterations in brain activity
(Rogers et al. 2007, Heuvel and Hulshoff Pol 2010). By capturing the variations in blood oxygenation
levels, fMRI provides insights into neural activity patterns. Analyzing these patterns allows
researchers to identify clusters of brain regions that exhibit synchronous activity, forming functional
units. These units, often referred to as functional networks or modules, represent specialized regions
that collaborate in performing specific cognitive or sensory functions (Honey et al. 2010). Through
statistical methods and advanced algorithms, researchers can establish the strength and specificity of
connections between these functional units. This enables the construction of a comprehensive network
map, showcasing how different brain regions intercommunicate and collaborate to execute complex
tasks (Cordes et al. 2000). By harnessing the information embedded within fMRI data, scientists gain
a nuanced understanding of the brain’s functional architecture and its dynamic connectivity, furthering
our comprehension of brain function and cognition. Both data-driven and model-driven approaches
have demonstrated their eﬀicacy in identifying these functional units (see review: Li et al. 2009).
A subset of these studies has emphasized the application of graph theory by representing the brain as
a network of nodes (regions) and edges (connections) to depict the connections between functional
regions and how they are interconnected. This methodological fusion of graph theory and fMRI data
not only enables the identification of key hubs within the brain network but also facilitates the
exploration of how these hubs interact, shedding light on the intricate dynamics that underlie
cognitive processes, making it an indispensable technique for modern neuroscience research (Heuvel,
Stam, et al. 2008, Salvador et al. 2005).
Psychiatric Disorders and Brain Functionality
Diagnosing psychiatric disorders presents a formidable challenge due to the inherent complexity and
heterogeneity of these conditions. Symptoms often manifest on a spectrum, and their subjective
nature can vary widely from person to person. Additionally, the absence of a singular underlying
cause and the interplay of genetic, environmental, and psychological factors further complicate
diagnosis. The overlap of symptoms across different disorders adds to the diagnostic ambiguity.

Moreover, the stigma associated with mental health issues can hinder accurate reporting and
disclosure, impeding the diagnostic process (Krystal and State 2014).
Recent studies have concentrated their efforts on discerning biomarkers to enhance diagnosis precision
and facilitate tailored psychiatric interventions. Nonetheless, a considerable degree of ambiguity
continues to enshroud these biomarkers and their implications within the clinical context (Glannon
2022). The psychiatric disorder schizophrenia have shown alterations in both the structural and
functional aspects of the brain. Structurally, individuals with schizophrenia often exhibit changes in
brain volume, particularly in regions responsible for cognitive and emotional processing. Functionally,
aberrant connectivity patterns between brain regions have been observed, leading to disruptions in the
synchronization and communication between neural circuits. These alterations manifest as impaired
coordination among different brain areas during tasks involving attention, memory, and
decision-making. Moreover, dysregulation in neurotransmitter systems contributes to the cognitive
and emotional symptoms characteristic of the disorder. Integrating these structural and functional
changes sheds light on the complex neurobiological underpinnings of schizophrenia, informing both
diagnostic approaches and potential therapeutic interventions (Karlsgodt et al. 2010, Shen et al. 2023).
The Functional Segregation and Integration Model
The Functional Segregation and Integration Model (FSIM) introduced by Churchill et al. 2016 offers a
potent data-driven tool for delineating cortical subdivisions and understanding their connectivity
patterns across a population using fMRI data. FSIM expands upon the conventional Gaussian
mixture model (GMM), incorporating the ability to learn subject- and voxel-specific scaling factors
within the network. This parametric characterization of connectivity structure enhances applicability
to graph theory analyses and has proven valuable for predicting subject characteristics like age.
However, FSIM possesses both beneficial attributes and certain limitations. While it boasts
advantageous features, such as subject-specific scaling profiles, it also exhibits drawbacks. Notably, its
generalization log-likelihood falls short of the standard GMM, and its computational demands are
significant. FSIM’s inclusion of voxel-, cluster-, and subject-specific parameters, including the noise
parameter, introduces scalability challenges when handling larger datasets. Moreover, FSIM’s lack of
robustness and reliability stems from its vulnerability to parameter initialization and its inability to
ensure convergence to a global optimum.
Despite its limitations, FSIM consistently showcases its capacity to yield meaningful spatial
parcellations and connectivity patterns through methodologies like data splits and bootstrapping. In
its original implementation, the model was fitted utilizing the Expectation-Maximization (EM)
algorithm, as outlined by Dempster et al. 1977. Differentiating between batch algorithms that require
all available data for inference and stochastic methods that only require a single sample, the batch
nature of the EM algorithm makes it unfeasible for sizeable datasets. Given the proliferation of openly
accessible large-scale datasets online, this limitation constitutes a substantial drawback, necessitating
extensive computational resources.
Batch versus Stochastic Optimization
Stochastic techniques have evolved into the norm for optimizing machine learning algorithms due to
the abundance of extensive data facilitated by cost-effective computational resources. The two
techniques offer different trade-offs; the batch technique will often have a smooth convergence as is it
benefits from consistent gradient estimates, though, at the cost of being computationally expensive
especially for large datasets. Furthermore, the algorithm might get stuck in suboptimal solutions,
especially in non-convex optimization problems (Allassonnière and Chevallier 2021). Stochastic
optimization processes data points one at a time or in small subsets, introducing randomness into the
gradient estimation. While this randomness might slow down the convergence rate, stochastic
methods are computationally more eﬀicient and can handle large datasets with ease. Additionally, the
==PAGE:12==

3
inherent noise in stochastic estimates can sometimes help escape local minima, leading to better
generalization and more reliable inference (Sabuncu 2020). However, the convergence path might be
erratic, and excessive noise can hinder convergence to the optimal solution (Bottou et al. 2016). A
compromise between these two extremes is the mini-batch approach, where a moderate-sized subset of
data is used for gradient estimation. This method leverages the advantages of both batch and
stochastic methods, providing a balance between computation and convergence stability.
This thesis will focus on the following research questions;
1. How can FSIM benefit from PyTorch implementation and GPU acceleration?
Employing GPU acceleration can significantly expedite matrix operations, enabling faster
experimentation and handling larger training datasets due to reduced training times.
2. What are the advantages of using stochastic optimization on FSIM? Implementing
both SGD and a stochastic EM algorithms for FSIM facilitates inference on extensive datasets.
Models will be tested on simulated data and Decoded NeuroFeedback (DecNef) fMRI data from
both healthy subjects and patients with schizophrenia, aiming for reliable inference.
3. To what extent can FSIM be used to characterize schizophrenia (SZ) using a large
resting-state fMRI dataset? Using machine learning algorithms like decision trees,
subject-specific parameters will be used as feature vectors to differentiate between healthy
subjects and those with SZ.
The thesis’ structure includes method descriptions, data details, results from simulated data, results
from fMRI data, discussion and perspectives on findings, and concluding remarks.

Methods
In this chapter, we will begin by explaining the fundamentals of classical Gaussian Mixture Models
and their potential expansion within the FSIM framework. This will be succeeded by an investigation
into the EM algorithm’s role in fitting parameters for the FSIM. Subsequently, we will examine the
application of stochastic algorithms, specifically SGD and a stochastic version of the EM algorithm.
These adaptations facilitate the optimization of large-scale datasets. Lastly, we will describe how we
can use the parameters obtained from the FSIM model to distinguish between healthy subjects and
subjects with schizophrenia.
2.1
Gaussian Mixture Model
Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) are probabilistic models used for representing probability
distributions of data points that may come from a mixture of multiple Gaussian distributions. It is a
generative model that can approximate complex data distributions by combining simpler components,
each represented by a Gaussian distribution. GMMs are commonly used in statistical modeling, data
analysis, and machine learning tasks like clustering, density estimation and data generation. In simple
terms, a GMM assumes that the data is generated by a mixture of several Gaussian distributions,
with each Gaussian representing a distinct cluster or mode in the data. The model finds the best
combination of these Gaussians and their associated weights to best describe the underlying data
distribution.

2.3
Expectation-Maximization Algorithm
The expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm is a widely used statistical method for estimating
parameters in models with incomplete or missing data. Introduced by Dempster et al. 1977, the
algorithm has since become a fundamental tool in various fields, such as computer science,
engineering, and biostatistics.
The EM algorithm is an iterative algorithm that seeks to maximize the likelihood of the observed data
by iteratively updating the parameters of the model. The algorithm operates in two steps: the
expectation step and the maximization step. In the expectation step, the algorithm computes the
expected value of the missing or unobserved data, given the current estimate of the model parameters.
In the maximization step, the algorithm updates the model parameters to maximize the expected
log-likelihood of the observed data, based on the expected values of the missing data computed in the
expectation step.
One of the main strengths of the EM algorithm is its ability to handle missing or incomplete data,
which includes the latent variables, a common problem in many real-world datasets. By modeling the
distribution of the missing data, the algorithm can estimate the parameters of the model more
accurately than traditional methods that simply discard incomplete cases. Another advantage of the
EM algorithm is its theoretical guarantees. Under certain assumptions, the algorithm is guaranteed to
converge to a stationary point of the likelihood function, which corresponds to a set of parameter
estimates that maximize the likelihood of the observed data (Haralick and Harpaz 2006)
When working with GMMs, initialization is important. Therefore, K-means clustering will be used to
initialize the means. This is done by running K-means clustering on the data, µinit ∈T × K × S. We
use the same method as Churchill et al. 2016 thus setting

2.4

Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) stands as a fundamental optimization algorithm in the realm of
machine learning and deep learning. Unlike its deterministic counterpart, SGD introduces a dynamic
and eﬀicient approach to iteratively minimizing the objective function of a model. In each iteration,
rather than computing the gradient using the entire dataset, SGD randomly selects a subset, often
referred to as a mini-batch, to approximate the gradient. This inherent randomness in selecting
mini-batches introduces a level of noise to the optimization process. While this noise might hinder the
convergence to an absolute minimum, it also allows SGD to escape local minima and navigate regions
of complex, non-convex optimization landscapes (Sabuncu 2020). The reduced computational burden,
owing to the use of smaller mini-batches, contributes to faster iterations, making SGD particularly
well-suited for handling extensive datasets. Furthermore, SGD’s adaptability has led to numerous
extensions and variations, such as learning rate schedules, momentum, and adaptive learning rate
algorithms, which collectively enhance its convergence properties and practical utility in different
machine learning models.

2.5
Stochastic EM optimization
The traditional EM algorithm is a great and somewhat simple optimization scheme suitable for many
clustering problems. However, the EM algorithm presents certain limitations and obstacles. A
primary concern revolves around the challenge of converging towards local maxima instead of global
maxima. This predicament becomes particularly pronounced in the case of intricate models, where the
log-likelihood function often possesses numerous local maxima. These local maxima may prove
inescapable during the optimization process, consequently leading to the convergence of the algorithm
towards suboptimal solutions. Such outcomes can introduce a degree of unreliability into the results
obtained. Another challenge is the computational complexity of the algorithm, which can be
prohibitively expensive for large datasets or complex models as it requires the whole dataset for
inference at each iteration. To address this, various extensions and modifications of the EM algorithm
have been proposed, such as the stochastic EM algorithm and the variational EM algorithm, which
can speed up convergence and reduce computational cost.
Consequently, we shall utilize a stochastic approximation technique within the framework of the EM
algorithm to optimize the parameters in the FSIM. The conceptual underpinning of employing
stochastic approximation was first introduced by Robbins and Monro 1951, subsequently evolving into
a well-established methodology for the optimization of machine learning models.
Various versions of stochastic, sometimes called online, EM algorithms have been explored. Both
versions where the E-step is changed to a stochastic step while the M-step remains unchanged (Cappé
and Moulines 2009) and cases where the E-step remains unchanged while the M-step incorporates
stochastic approximation of the gradients (Bishop 2006). The later version is typically called the
incremental EM.
The conversion to a stochastic model, introduces the learning rate parameters {αt}t≥1. This is a
decreasing sequence of positive numbers such that P∞

2.7
Classification of Schizophrenia
Utilizing the subject-specific scalings denoted as cks, our study aims to employ distinct classifiers for
the purpose of schizophrenia classification using these scalings as features. We use the classification
methods logistic regression and support vector machine (SVM), both implemented through the
scikit-learn library. Given the significant data imbalance (outlined in Table 3.1), we leverage the
class-weight functionality provided by scikit-learn within these implementations to balance the
sampling of the two classes in the train and test data. This approach assists in mitigating the
challenges associated with imbalanced data during classifier training. To validate the classification, we
use K-fold cross-validation. In light of the highly imbalanced nature of the dataset, we employ
accuracy and F1-score as our evaluation metrics. It is worth noting that accuracy can be misleading
in imbalanced scenarios, where a model might achieve high accuracy by simply assigning all instances
to the majority class. Given this concern, the F1-score proves more insightful, as it places emphasis on
true positives, thereby offering a better understanding of the model’s ability to accurately detect
signals (see Definition 2.5).

for each ”brain slice” for different signal-to-noise (SNR, average SNR across an entire brain slice)

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
With the empirical evidence presented in the previous chapters, the following discussion delves into a
comprehensive analysis of the empirical findings in relation to the research objectives, shedding light
on how introducing stochasticity can enable optimization on large-scale datasets. By comparing these
findings in the context of existing literature, a more profound comprehension of the topic comes to light,
casting fresh perspectives on the prevailing theories.
6.1
Reliable Inference
Not only has stochastic optimization methods proved to be able to do inference on a large-scale
dataset but it has also proved helpful in converging to a global, instead of local, optima. The inherent
noise in stochastic optimization methods, equips the algorithm with a unique ability to navigate the
landscape of potential solutions more effectively, and possibly sidestepping local optima (Sabuncu
2020). In the study conducted by Churchill et al. 2016, the EM algorithm was found to produce
unreliable cluster parcellations suggesting converging to a local maximum and not global. With this
context in mind, our investigation was designed to illuminate the comparative performance of the EM
algorithm and the stochastic algorithms.
6.1.1
Simulated Data
As depicted in Figure 4.1, all algorithms experienced a gradual increase in log-likelihood as the
number of clusters, denoted as K, increased. However, this upward trend did not culminate in the
expected peak corresponding to the ground truth value indicated by the dashed line. Although no
such peak was observed, the log-likelihood only experienced a slight increase as K transitioned from 6
to 10, compared to the change observed before reaching K = 6. In Figure 4.3, it was evident that all
three EM algorithms consistently achieved an NMI of 1 or close to it when K = 6. In contrast, the
SGD algorithm struggled to produce cluster parcellations resembling the original ones, as indicated by
the low NMI values. Upon visually examining the results, it became apparent that for values of K
exceeding 6, the EM algorithms could correctly classify most pixels, with only a few misclassified
pixels as the excess clusters, thus yielding a high NMI. The dissonance between the high log-likelihood
for the SGD algorithm and the low NMI may have stemmed from the algorithm’s failure to explicitly
incorporate responsibilities in the objective or loss function. This could have led the algorithm to
optimize parameter values that enhanced the log-likelihood but did not generate meaningful cluster
parcellations. This observation aligned with the visual findings presented in Figure 4.9. Figure 4.4 and
the simplified Figure 4.5 provided insight into the NMI between initializations. For values lower than
the ground truth number of clusters (K = 6), all algorithms exhibited relatively low mean NMIs along
with significant standard deviations, indicating a lack of reliable results. At K = 6, the stochastic EM
algorithms exhibited a pronounced peak with minimal standard deviation, signifying consistent and
reliable results for the correct number of clusters. Moreover, the NMI remained high and approached
1 for increasing values of K with these algorithms, indicating their reliability even as the model’s
flexibility increased. Conversely, the traditional EM algorithm generally displayed lower NMIs with
larger standard deviations for values of K greater than 6. Notably, for an SNR of 0.71, the standard
deviation for the EM algorithm was exceptionally small for these K values. On the contrary, the SGD
==PAGE:49==

algorithm exhibited a dip in NMI for K = 6, and its standard deviation was significantly large for
almost all K values, except at K = 6. This, coupled with the observations in Figure 4.3, underscored
the SGD algorithm’s struggle to produce meaningful or consistent results for this model.
6.1.1.1
Robustness
Together with testing the reliability of the algorithms, we also conducted a robustness test by
initializing the parameters randomly except for the means. Figure 4.6 showed that even with random
initialization, the stochastic EM algorithms still managed to converge towards a similar log-likelihood
as the EM algorithm. The results which can be seen in Figure 4.7 showed that even by initializing the
parameters randomly for the stochastic EM algorithms, they still managed to reproduce the clusters
and converge towards a high log-likelihood. Though, once again, the SGD did not produce any
meaningful cluster parcellations. Figure 4.8 showed that the stochastic EM algorithms maintained
their reliability for values of K higher than 6. Though the sEM (v) algorithm did show a slight
decrease in the pair-wise NMI compared to Figure 4.4.
Based on these findings, the decision was made to exclude the SGD algorithm from being applied to
the DefNec data as our initial investigations strongly indicated that it would not converge towards
meaningful results.
6.1.2
fMRI Data
Both the traditional and the stochastic EM algorithms were all evaluated on a consistent set of 50
subjects extracted from the dataset. This controlled comparison aims to ascertain how each algorithm
handles the intricacies of the dataset and whether stochastic approaches exhibit advantages in terms
of reliability and robustness on real fMRI data.
While the sEM (b) method exhibited promising results in the simulated data, Figure 5.1 revealed a
substantial discrepancy in log-likelihood compared to the EM algorithm when applied to real fMRI
data. Furthermore, Figure 5.2 showed that the sEM (b) algorithm was even less reliable than the EM
algorithm with a mean value for the pair-wise NMI across K of 0.32. This was a contrast to the
findings for the simulated data, where sEM (b) showed performance close to the EM algorithm. In
contrast, the sEM (v) method demonstrated log-likelihood values similar to the EM algorithm,
indicating its potential as an alternative for large datasets, albeit still not entirely reliable. The sEM
(v) had a mean pair-wise NMI across K of 0.61 which is comparable to the tradition EM algorithm’s
of 0.60.
To address this issue, we conducted an experiment using a subset of 15 subjects from the original 50,
initializing both sEM (b) and sEM (v) with the EM algorithm. The outcomes, as depicted in
Figure 5.3, demonstrated that this initialization strategy notably improved the log-likelihood for sEM
(b) when K ≥10, bringing it closer to the levels seen with the EM algorithm. For sEM (v), there was
also a slight increase in log-likelihood, nearly aligning it with the EM algorithm’s performance.
Examining pair-wise NMI in Figure 5.4, we observed a significant enhancement for sEM (b), reaching
levels closer to those achieved by the EM algorithm, with a mean value of 0.51 (compared to the EM
algorithm’s 0.60 mean value). In contrast, the pair-wise NMI for sEM (v) showed a slight decrease in
the NMI with a mean pair-wise NMI of 0.58. These finding contradicts the hypothesis that combining
the traditional EM algorithm with stochasticity would yield better and more reliable results.
Moreover, it enhances how important initialization is for the sEM (b) algorithm. Further exploration
into hyperparameter optimization, encompassing learning rate, batch size, and extended training,
might hold the key to enhancing the reliability of this initialization method.

6.1.3
Voxel-wise vs. Subject-wise Batching
In practical terms, when dealing with fMRI data, it’s more convenient to organize batches by subjects
rather than by individual voxels. This is because fMRI data is typically stored and structured by
subjects, and reorganizing it to access voxels individually can be a time-consuming and
preprocessing-intensive task. It is worth highlighting that while the sEM (v) model demonstrated
superior reliability, the sEM (s) model also achieved comparable results, especially when initialized
with the EM algorithm. This observation carries significance, particularly considering the sEM (s)
model’s strong performance on simulated data. It suggests that as the number of parameters in the
model increases, its stability tends to decrease, emphasizing the critical role of careful initialization.
When examining the log-likelihood obtained from the fMRI data, the sEM (v) algorithm consistently
outperforms sEM (b), displaying notably higher log-likelihood values across different values of K. In
terms of cluster parcellations, the sEM (v) algorithm produced less noisy results compared to the sEM
(b). Despite the distinct functionalities associated with the two brain hemispheres, e.g., left
hemisphere for language, right hemisphere for spatial skills (Hobson 1988), there is a significant degree
of symmetry between them, as evident from both algorithms’ cluster parcellations. It is important to
stress that finding an algorithm capable of consistently delivering reliable results for this unsupervised
model remains an ongoing research challenge.
6.2
Tackling the Noise Variance Parameter
The noise variance parameter is of interest as it is both voxel and subject specific. This means that we
can see the uncertainty of each cluster for each voxel and subject. As described earlier, the noise
variance parameter causes problems for large data sets and therefore different approaches in this
project was used to overcome this. Another way of handling this parameter is to factorize the noise
into R set of vectors of length K, V and S, respectively, thereby decomposing the tensor into a set of
rank-1 vectors. This can approximate the full K × V × S tensor but only requires (K + V + S)R
memory which significantly decreases the memory demand if we choose a low value for R. If we choose
an R of 1, we can access each σ2

6.3
GPU Acceleration and Automatic Gradients
Using GPU acceleration through the PyTorch library made it possible to do relatively quick inference
on a large datasets due to fast matrix computations. Fitting the models on the full dataset of 937
subjects took approximately 2.5 hours for the stochastic EM algorithms.
From Figure 4.9, it becomes evident that the SGD algorithm struggles to converge to meaningful
results, despite achieving a relatively high log-likelihood comparable to the EM and sEM algorithms.
Notably, the implementation of SGD using the PyTorch library leverages automatic differentiation
(AD), which introduces an element of black box optimization, where the inner workings are less
transparent. AD is especially useful for complex and large models like deep learning, simplifying
model implementation and optimization.
However, when employing algorithms such as the EM algorithm, we can derive closed-form
expressions for parameter gradients using the lower-bound. This provides a clear understanding of
parameter updates. Reparameterizing parameters using SVD in optimization schemes with AD can
introduce numerical instability which occurs when multiple eigenvalues are close to each other. This is
especially a problem occurring in large matrices (Wang et al. 2021). To investigate this, we conducted
an experiment by removing the PARAFAC2 factorization, leaving only πk, wkv, µtks, and σ2
kvs as
parameters, subject to constraints wkv ≥0 and σ2

successfully generated cluster parcellations and voxel scalings similar to the original parameters,

6.4
Classification of Schizophrenia
As outlined in the introduction, the search for biomarkers in psychiatric disorders has been a focal
point for researchers, as these biomarkers hold potential for aiding in the diagnosis of patients.
Previous studies have identified hypoactivation in specific brain regions, including the ventromedial
prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), left hippocampus, posterior cingulate cortex, lower precuneus, and the
precuneus, all of which are associated with functions like memory, self-reflection, and consciousness
(Kühn and Gallinat 2013).
Building on this knowledge, a hypothesis emerged that the subject scalings, which indicate the
intensity or activity of a cluster, might exhibit distinct patterns capable of distinguishing patients
from healthy controls. Table Table 5.1 presents the metrics obtained from testing two different
classifiers, logistic regression and SVM, trained using the subject scalings as features and the diagnosis
as the target variable. From the table, it is evident that none of the models even match the baseline
zero-rule accuracy of 84.4%, let alone surpass it. However, for most classifiers and algorithms, except
SVM and sEM (s), the metrics showed increased accuracy as the number of K (i.e., the number of
features) increased. The best-performing model for logistic regression achieved an accuracy of 75.2%
and an F1-score of 0.701, very close to the top-performing SVM model with an accuracy of 73.2% and
an F1-score of 0.708. Both of these models used subject scalings obtained from the sEM (v) algorithm
with K = 55. The F1-scores of approximately 0.7 indicate that the classifiers managed to capture
some patterns from the training set, although they still fell short of outperforming the baseline. When
visually examining the subject scalings used for training (see Figure 5.9), it becomes apparent that no
clear distinguishable pattern between healthy individuals and patients is visible to the naked eye.
Further investigation into the cluster with the highest weight in the classifier (cluster 11) reveals that
it contains voxels near the ventricles. The ventricles contain cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), and pulsating
changes in CSF have previously been shown to increase during resting-state. Despite implementing a
low-pass filtering step in the preprocessing, this physiological noise might still contribute to the
clustering. Nonetheless, future studies could explore whether the model captures functional units
previously linked to hypoactivity in schizophrenia, such as the vmPFC, and analyze the subject
scalings in relation to these regions.
6.5
Alternative Use of the PARAFAC2 Model
When employing the PARAFAC2 method as executed in this thesis, a static assumption is made
regarding the spatial clustering across subjects and time points. In the context of task-based fMRI
data, this spatial clustering framework usually remains unchanged, while variations manifest
predominantly in the functional connectivity among these spatial clusters as temporal dynamics
unfold during task execution. This focus on temporal dynamics introduces time as a critical factor.
Previous investigations, similar to the PARAFAC2 model used in the FSIM, have applied it to
task-based fMRI analyses. A notable study by Acar et al. 2022 underscores the importance of
studying temporal data like fMRI across both healthy and impaired subjects to unveil differences in
time-evolving functional connectivity patterns across distinct groups.
By orchestrating a reconfiguration of the tensor’s dimensions, denoted as S × V × T, the PARAFAC2
model demonstrates its capacity to discern disparities in the temporal attributes of voxels. As a result,
this method effectively discriminates between healthy individuals and patients. In this context, the
resulting component C emerges as a crucial revelation, uncovering spatial regions of interest

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In summary, this thesis has delved into the application of stochastic methods to extend the
unsupervised FSIM model’s usability for large-scale datasets. Additionally, it tackled the crucial
reliability challenge inherent in the EM algorithm, primarily the issue of converging to local maxima
rather than the global optimum. These methodologies underwent rigorous testing and analysis, first
on a simulated dataset and then on the DefNec resting-state fMRI (rs-fMRI) data. Subsequently, the
subject scalings derived from fitting the DefNec data were subjected to classification using logistic
regression and SVM.
In the first part, which concentrated on the findings from the simulated data, both stochastic EM
algorithms demonstrated their capability to achieve log-likelihood values similar to those of the EM
algorithm. They successfully recreated the original parameters and exhibited reliability across various
initializations when the number of clusters (K) exceeded the ground truth value. However, the SGD
algorithm, while yielding a comparable log-likelihood to the EM algorithm, fell short in reproducing
the original parameters and delivering reliable outcomes. Furthermore, an investigation into the
initialization process revealed that even when the parameters were initialized randomly (while
adhering to all constraints), the stochastic EM algorithms consistently managed to replicate the
original data and maintain reliability. Conversely, the SGD algorithm displayed even less similarity
between the original cluster parcellations and those obtained from the fitted model when randomly
initializing the parameters.
In the second part, the stochastic EM algorithms were test on a subset of 50 subjects from the DefNec
dataset. While the sEM (b) method showed promise in simulated data, the application to real fMRI
data revealed significant disparities. The log-likelihood results demonstrated a substantial difference
compared to the EM algorithm. Furthermore, the sEM (b) algorithm exhibited lower reliability than
the EM algorithm, as indicated by a mean pair-wise NMI across K of 0.32 which increased to 0.51
when initialized using the EM algorithm. This contrasted with the performance on simulated data,
where sEM (b) closely matched the EM algorithm independent of initialization. Conversely, the sEM
(v) method displayed log-likelihood values similar to the EM algorithm, suggesting its potential as an
alternative for large datasets, albeit still with some reliability concerns. The sEM (v) achieved a mean
pair-wise NMI across K of 0.61, comparable to the traditional EM algorithm’s 0.60. The findings
suggest that hyperparameter optimization, including learning rate, batch size, and extended training,
could enhance the reliability of this initialization method.
In the third and final part of this thesis, an attempt was made to classify schizophrenia based on the
subject scalings from the model. the results indicate that none of the models managed to match, let
alone surpass, the baseline zero-rule accuracy of 84.4%. Nonetheless, most classifiers and algorithms
demonstrated increased accuracy as the number of features (K) increased. The top-performing models,
logistic regression and SVM, achieved accuracies of 75.2% and 73.2%, respectively, both using subject
scalings from the sEM (v) algorithm with K = 55. The F1-scores of approximately 0.7 suggest that
the classifiers captured some training set patterns, although they did not outperform the baseline.
Visual examination of the subject scalings revealed no discernible pattern distinguishing healthy
individuals from patients. Further investigation into the cluster with the highest classifier weight,
cluster 11, revealed its proximity to the ventricles, which contain cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Pulsating
CSF changes during resting-state could contribute to clustering, despite implementing a low-pass

functional units associated with hypoactivity in schizophrenia, such as the vmPFC, and analyze
