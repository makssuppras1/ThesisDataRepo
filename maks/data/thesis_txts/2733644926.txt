Abstract
In this thesis, we dive into the world of algorithms and data structures, with the
goal of solving the following problem. Given a DAG (directed acyclic graph), we
want to design a space efficient data structure which support fast reachability
queries. Given two nodes u and v, answering a reachability query is to determine
if there exist a path from u to v in the DAG. We look into the existing solutions
of the 2-Hop and Chain cover with the aim of surveying and implementing the
algorithms along with two naive solutions. Furthermore, we experiment with a
relaxed extension of the 2-Hop where center graphs are assumed complete bipar-
tite graphs at all times thus allowing us to omit approximating the densest sub-
graph. To highlight and determine strengths and weaknesses we experimentally
compare all algorithms in terms of space consumption and time usage during
preprocessing along extensive query testing on a range of graph families. Finally
we conclude on the results and give our recommendations for when to use the
different algorithms.

Introduction
The study of reachability queries in graph theory has become increasingly rele-
vant in the world of complex networks. A study that is integrated in a variety
of applications in our everyday life. To determine if a path exists amongst two
nodes is essential in theoretical domains such analyzing social networks as well as
practical like navigating the street of our cities. In this paper we wish to explore
solutions to reachability queries and investigate their performance on networks
of varying sizes and complexities. Our main area of focus lies in the work of Co-
hen et al. [2] and their 2-Hop labeling scheme for answering reachability queries.
In the following sections, we will present a detailed analysis and methodologies
of a range of solutions, of which our experiments with modifications to the 2-
Hop labeling scheme. Furthermore, we will provide a comprehensive evalua-
tion of these approaches through extensive testing on various families of graphs,
thereby demonstrating their practicality and scalability in diverse settings.
0.0.1
Problem Description: Reachability
Given a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG), and two nodes u and v. The reachability
query Reach(u,v) are to determine however there exist a path in the graph going
from u to v.
In this thesis we focus on the static problem, where we have the option to pre-
process the data. We want to find a fast and space efficient way to solve the
reachability problem.

0.1
Preliminary
0.1.1
DAG
A DAG is a directed graph with no cycles. If it is possible to give the nodes unique
ids sorted in topological order such that no edge goes from a node with higher id
to a node with lower id, then there is no cycles, and hereby the graph is a DAG.
0.1.2
Transitive Closure
Many approaches to solve the reachability problem focus on storing the transitive
closure of the DAG, D. The transitive closure is a graph holding for every pair
of nodes (u,v) in D an edge from u to v if there in D exists a path from u to v.
In this paper we call edges in D for edges and edges in the transitive closure for
transitive edges.

and in the transitive closure.

Basic Reachability Solutions
There exist two naive solutions to the reachability problem, each exemplifying
opposite extremes. The first require no preprocessing and hereby no space us-
age, but at the cost of more time consuming queries. Conversely, is the second
substantial in terms of space utilization, which allows queries to be executed in
constant time.
1.1
BFS/DFS
To answer reachability queries for a given graph, one might naively traverse the
graph in a Breadth first or Depth first manner in order to find neighbouring nodes
for each node. Doing so, the question of reachability becomes a question of find-
ing the neighbour which is the designated node.
1.1.1
Design and Implementation
Breadth first search (BFS) and Depth first search (DFS) are two intuitive solutions
which utilizes the Queue and Stack datastructure respectively in order to provide
space efficient solutions as no preprocessing is performed. By iteratively visiting
neighbouring nodes of once neighbours and so on, it will thus be possible to
visit and cover an entire graph. Based on if a path from a given starting node to a
designated node exist, the answer of this becomes the response to the reachability
query. Both algorithms run in O(n + m) time where n is the number of nodes and
m is the number of edges in the graph.
1.2
Reachability Matrix
Another intuitive approach to answering reachability queries is via a table of
reachabilities, namely a Reachability Matrix which stores the entire transitive clo-

sure. This creates a time efficient but space inefficient solution, allowing constant
query time but using O(n2) space.
1.2.1
Design and Implementation
The reachability matrix is a matrix of size n × n. Each entry (i,j) are holding a
boolean telling whether there exist a path in the graph going from i to j. This
allows constant running time reachability queries which become the lookup for
the right entry in the matrix.

2
2-Hop Cover and Labeling
2.1
Design
The idea behind the 2-Hop cover builds on the same idea as the basic solution
using the reachability matrix. The idea is to store some information in each node,
we call that information a label. Reachability between two nodes should then be
determined by only using the labels of the two nodes. For the solution to be better
than the reachability matrix, we want the space used to store these labels to be
smaller than the space usage of the reachability matrix. For the 2-Hop solution
such a labeling is made by creating a 2-Hop cover of the graph.
2.1.1
Datastructure and Query Algorithm
The datastructure used to perform reachability queries with the 2-Hop solution
is a 2-Hop labeling. A 2-Hop labeling hold for each node a label. A label of a
node v holds two sets, an in-set and an out-set. The in-set holds a subset of the
nodes that can reach v (including itself) and the out-set holds a subset of all the
nodes that can be reached by v (including itself). The 2-Hop labeling is a repre-
sentation of a 2-Hop cover.
A 2-Hop cover is a subset of the transitive closure. For every pair of nodes (u,
v) where u can reach v there should exist a path from u to v using at most two
transitive edges. We call transitive edges in the 2-Hop cover hops. Note that the
entire transitive closure is a 2-Hop cover, but a 2-Hop cover can also be smaller
than the transitive closure. To make a space efficient solution the cover should be
as small as possible.
The nodes stored in the 2-Hop labeling represents hops in the 2-Hop cover. If a
node u is stored in the in-set of the label of v, then there is a hop from u to v in
the 2-Hop cover.

Having a 2-Hop labeling makes the reachability query very simple. If we want
to know if u can reach v, then we need to check for an intersection between the
out-set of u and the in-set of v. u can reach v if the intersection is not empty.
Retrieving the out-set of u and the in-set of v can be done in constant time, there-
fore the running time of the reachability algorithm must be the time used to find
the intersection between the two sets.
2.1.2
Preprosessing Algorithm
To understand how the cover is constructed we first need to understand the term
center graph. A center graph of some node v is a bipartite graph holding in one set
all the nodes that can reach v including itself, and in another set all nodes that
can be reached by v also including itself. Each node in the first set is connected
to every node in the other set resulting in a complete bipartite graph, CBG 1. In
other words, the center graph of v represents all paths in the original graph pass-
ing trough v. Edges in a center graph is a subset of the transitive closure, and the
union of all edges in all center graphs combined constitutes the transitive closure.
For the remainder we will use n′ to denote the amount of nodes in a given center
graph X. Furthermore we let Xout be the out-set, and Xin be the in-set of center
graph X.
Constructing the 2-Hop labeling
Initialising the algorithm: The first part of the algorithm is to construct the
reachability matrix. Having the reachability matrix we are now able to construct
center graphs for every node in the graph. Each center graph is associated with a
number d holding the density of the most dense subgraph of the center graph.
1A bipartite graph where all nodes in the first set are connected to all nodes in the second set.
6
==PAGE:12==
Running the algorithm:
1. Select the center graph associated with the greatest d. We call the center
node v.
2. Select the most dense subgraph of v and cover its edges using the following
rules:
• If the edge goes form v to v: Store v in the in-set and the out-set of v.
• If the edge goes form some node u , v to v: Store u in the in-set of v.
• If the edge goes form v to some node u , v: Store u in the out-set of v.
• If the edge goes form some node u , v to some node w , v: Store v in
the out-set of u and store v in the in-set of w.
3. Remove the covered edges from all center graphs and mark the center graphs
that has been modified.
4. Stop if there are no more edges left in any center graph.
5. Select the center graph associated with the greatest d, and let v = the center
node.
6. If the center graph is not marked, then continue from 2. Else recalculate d
and continue from 5.
In this paper we will use a 2 approximation algorithm to find the approximate
densest subgraph ADS of a graph. It works by iteratively removing the node
of lowest degree until no more nodes are left. For each iteration the density is
calculated, and the greatest is chosen at the end.

2.1.3
Argue of correctness
To determine the validity of the algorithm consider that the preprocessing al-
gorithm does not terminate before all center graph edges are covered in every
center graph. Since the union of edges from all center graphs are the transitive
closure, then we know that the algorithm does not terminate before the whole
transitive closure is covered. In the algorithm a transitive edge from u to v is
covered by either adding the transitive edge to the cover or by adding two other
transitive edges to the cover that together constitutes a path from u to v. Since
every transitive edge is represented by either a single or two hops we do have a
2-Hop cover.
2.1.4
Space usage
The size of the 2-Hop labeling is the sum of elements stored in all labels. The
problem of finding a minimum 2-Hop cover is shown by Cohen et al. to be NP-
hard [2]. The preprocessing algorithm explained here finds a cover with a loga-
rithmic approximation factor. We will show this by comparing the algorithm to
the greedy algorithm of finding a minimum set cover.
In the minimum set cover problem, we want to solve the following:
We are given a set T and a collection C of subsets of T , where the union of all
subsets in C is T . Each set E ∈C has a wight w(E). The problem is to find a a
subset L of C such that the union of the sets in L is T , and the sum of the weight of
all sets in L becomes as small at possible. The greedy algorithm runs in iterations.
Each iteration works by choosing a set from C and adding it to L. The algorithm
is keeping track of all the uncovered elements in T , we call that set T ′. Until T ′ is
empty the algorithm will keep running another iteration. The set chosen in each
iteration is the set that maximizes:
E ∩T ′
w(E)
We want to show that the preprocessing algorithm we use to construct the 2-Hop
cover is an instance of the greedy minimum set cover algorithm. We have all hops
in the transitive closure that we want to cover with a 2-Hop labeling. Let T be the
10
==PAGE:16==
transitive closure, and let C be the edges in all center graphs. A set in C is a set of
center graph edges, which covers a part of the transitive closure. The weight of
some set E is the number of elements added to the 2-Hop cover by covering the
edges in E. This means w(E) = |Ein| + |Eout|, where |Ein| and |Eout| is the number
of nodes in the in-set and out-set of E respectively. In this setup each iteration of
the greedy set cover algorithm chooses an E such that we maximize:
E ∩T ′
|Ein| + |Eout|
In the 2-Hop preprocessing algorithm we choose E to be the edges in the most
dense subgraph of all center graphs. That is we choose a subgraph of a center
graph which maximizes #edges
#nodes. In some arbitrary iteration of the preprocessing
algorithm, where some part of the center graph might already have been covered,
then maximizing #edges
#nodes corresponds to maximizing
E∩T ′
|Ein|+|Eout|. Knowing that the
2-Hop preprocessing algorithm is an instance of the greedy minimum set cover
algorithm, we know that the two algorithms have the same approximation fac-
tor. Hence we can translate the known log(n) approximation factor of the greedy
minimum set cover algorithm to the 2-Hop preprocessing algorithm.
An exemplary illustration of the foregoing is a graph of nodes directed sequen-
tially in a path. For such structure it is intuitive to see that the center graph which
maximizes #edges
#nodes would be the node at the center of the path. This creates an
equilibrium between Ein and Eout, splitting the graph in two equally sized parts.
Following the same reasoning then by continuously choosing center graphs that
divides the path in half results in O(n logn) hops (and of a single label of max-
imum size O(logn)). We will later dive into how the structure of other families
of graphs can affect the process and produce of the algorithm based on empirical
evidence.
2.2
Implementation
In order to implement the 2-Hop algorithm, we first choose to represent a graph
by an array of all the nodes. In each node we store the 2-Hop labels. The labels
are arrays holding the id of the nodes in sorted order. This allows us to imple-
ment the intersect function by using two index variables iteratively increasing
the lowest of the two. Either we meet a match on the way, or end up having tra-
versed both arrays with no match.
To represent a center graph we store an arraylist containing information about
its edges. The array consist of n + 1 entries to store all potential nodes that might
exist in a center graph’s in- and out-set. Each element is either null, for nodes not
in the sets, or a linkedlist representing edges related to a given node. Meaning
edges are represented twice, once for each node they connect. To simplify edge
removal each edge representation holds a reference to the other representation of
the same edge. Furthermore to tackle the dynamic problem of finding the ADS,
each center graph maintain its nodes in a min-heap with degree as key. Edges
from the edgelist will then reference their partnered node in the heap to make
11
==PAGE:17==
quick update of degree after edge removal. By utilizing a combination of these
two data structures we efficiently store edges while maintaining order to easily
remove the node with lowest degree.
Figure 2.2: Illustration of the Center Graph data structure. NOTE: The heap is
visualized as a tree even though it is implemented as an array.
The process of finding the ADS has been implemented as follows:
1. Store the density of the center graph as d and initialize an empty edgelist.
2. Pop the lowest degree node from the heap and save the node id.
3. Remove all edges connected to that node by:
• Find the node id in the arraylist and go trough the edges in the linked
list.
• For each edge remove the partner and decrease the partner’s heapitem
degree by 1, also adjusting the position of the heapitem in the heap.
• Remove all elements in the linked by setting the list to null.
4. Compare the current density with d. If d is greatest, continue from 5. If not
overwrite d with the current density and overwrite the stored edgelist with
an edgelist of the current graph.
5. If there are no more nodes to pop, the stored edgelist will then hold the
edges of the ADS and d will hold the density of the ADS. Else continue
from 2.
Thus to cover edges obtained from the above process, we take advantage of the
pre-computed reachability matrix. By modifying the design to instead contain
an edge list of references directly to edges in the arrayslist of center graphs. A
transitive edge in the graph will then be represented by the presence of an edge
list while the absence indicate the lack thereof.

center graphs.
To be able to find the center graph with highest ADS density we utilize a max-
heap to store the center graphs with density as key. The implementation of the
main algorithm then becomes:
1. Pop the center graph with the highest density and check condition:
• If marked, re-compute ADS, add item with new density to heap and
un-mark center graph.
• Else, cover edges of the ADS by traversing the edges and adding them
to the in- and out-labels. Hereafter remove covered edges from all
other center graphs the following way:
– Use the reachability matrix to look up the covered center graph
edge.
– Traverse the linked list removing the item in the arraylist reference
and the partner of that item.
– Set the edge reference to null removing the linked list.
2. Continue process from step 1 until heap is exhausted.
2.3
Space and Time complexity for preprocessing
To evaluate the space usage we look at the major structures stored; The reachabil-
ity matrix, the center graphs and the center graph heap. The primary allocation
of memory and time stems from the reachability matrix and the center graphs.
The (n×n) reachability matrix is utilized to store transitive edges which is neces-
sary throughout the entirety of the algorithm. Likewise is it necessary to store n
center graphs. The space usage of a center graph is the size of the arraylist plus
the size of the node heap. The size of the arraylist is n plus the amount of edges
in the center graph. The amount of edges in a center graph will maximally be
(n
2)2. Since in worst case the center graph will contain all nodes, and to make
most possible edges the amount of nodes in the in-set and out-set should be as
close to equal as possible. Hereby the size of a center graph becomes O(n2).

==PAGE:19==
The heap of size n storing center graphs, and the size n2 reachability matrix are
comparably small in contrast to the n center graphs of size O(n2). This gives us a
total space usage of O(n3).
To give a rough estimate of the running time of the preprocessing algorithm we
first want to find a bound for the number of iterations. We have two types of
iterations. One type is when we pop a marked node another when we pop an un-
marked node. First we look at the unmarked iterations. Each time an unmarked
node is popped some part of the transitive closure will get covered. This gives
us a rough upper bound for the number of unmarked iterations. The amount of
edges in the transitive closure is maximally (n−1)n
2
= O(n2). For each of the un-
marked nodes popped we might mark some other center graphs this amount can
be bounded by n. This gives us a rough bound for the number of marked itera-
tions to be O(n2 · n) = O(n3).
When a marked node is popped as the most dense center graph we perform ADS.
Due to our implementation of ADS, this process is bound to the copying of a
center graph along with the number of iterations needed to traverse its nodes to
find its most dense subgraph. As our structure builds on references, copying is a
necessity in order to avoid overwriting our center graphs. To copy a center graph
we have to iterate through the reachability matrix to find edges resulting in O(n2)
operations. The ADS will as suggested run in O(n′), where n′ is the amount of
nodes in the center graph, and updating the heap with the new ADS uses O(logn)
time. Meaning the time usage on marked iteration can be bounded by O(n5).
When an unmarked node is popped we are covering O(n2) edges. Each of the
edges we are to cover might exist in every other center graphs, which means that
we for each covered edge remove O(n) edges from other center graphs. This gives
a conservative upper bound for the time usage for each unmarked node popped
to be O(n3). Meaning the total preprocessing time can be bounded by O(n5).
2.4
Experimenting with 2- Hop
Since we are making n center graphs, lowering the space usage of each center
graph is crucial. The most space efficient way to store an arbitrary graph is to use
an edgelist. Using an edgelist would however make it difficult to implement the
ADS algorithm, since it works by removing nodes. Another way to improve our
implementation could be to remove the main array, and instead store the linked
list of edges directly in the nodeheap. This would especially be more efficient for
graphs where many of the center graphs initially have few nodes.
Thinking about compact ways to store the center graphs we got the idea to store
the center graphs as CBG. Center graphs are always CBG when initialized, but
they do not always stay CBG throughout the iterations of the preprocessing al-
gorithm. Although when running the preprocessing algorithm by hand, we ex-
perienced that for many test runs the center graphs stayed CBG throughout the
iterations. Therefore we thought to play with a variation of the algorithm where
14
==PAGE:20==
all center graphs are treated as CBGs. Storing the center graphs this way will
simplify the preprocessing algorithm in several ways. First of all we have a huge
space reduction since we no longer need to represent all edges. Instead we only
need to store two separate sets of nodes. Furthermore when the center graphs are
treated as CBGs then the center graph itself will always be its own ADS. There-
fore, we are able to skip all the ADS calculations.
This does however come with the limitation of not being able to remove a single
edge from the center graphs at a time. Instead we will use a subtract function:
subtract(A,B): Removes edges from graph A if they also exist in graph B. Fur-
thermore removes nodes from A if they have degree 0.
Subtracting a center graph from another will not always result in a CBG. We
will therefore perform a variation of the subtract function called roughSub-
tract(A,B). This variation always outputs a CBG although we are not guaranteed
that A ∩B = ∅. It works the following way:
roughSubtract(A,B) returns subtract(A,B) if subtract(A,B) outputs a CBG. Oth-
erwise A is returned unchanged.

preprocessing algorithm:
2.4.1
Our 2-Hop preprocessing Algorithm
As with the normal 2-Hop algorithm we start by creating the transitive closure,
in our case the reachability matrix. With the reachability matrix we create all the
center graphs. In this variation we also associate center graphs with a number d,
which is the density of the ADS, but in this case d is equal to the density of the
center graph.
Running the algorithm:
1. Select the center graph associated with the greatest d. We call the center
node v.
2. Cover the edges of the center graph using the following rules:
• If the edge goes form v to v: Store v in the in-set and the out-set of v.
• If the edge goes form some node u , v to v: Store u in the in-set of v.
• If the edge goes form v to some node u , v: Store u in the out-set of v.
• If the edge goes form some node u , v to some node w , v: Store v in
the out-set of u and store v in the in-set of w.
3. Subtract the center graph of v from every other center graph with rough-
Subtract, and mark the center graphs for which the roughSubtract caused
changes.
4. Stop if there are no more center graphs left to select.

5. Select the center graph associated with the greatest d, and let v = the center
node.
6. If the center graph is not marked, then continue from 2. Else recalculate d
and continue from 5.
To be able to perform roughSubtract(A,B) we need to look into when subtract(A,B)
outputs a CBG.
We claim the following:
Claim: Let A and B be complete bipartite graphs. A remains a complete bipartite
graph after subtraction with B if and only if one of following conditions
hold:
1. Aout ∩Bout = ∅
2. Ain ∩Bin = ∅
3. Aout ⊆Bout
4. Ain ⊆Bin
To show that the claim holds we first look at, condition 1 and 2. They state that if
there are no intersection on either the out-sets or the in-sets of A and B then the
result of the subtraction must be a complete bipartite graph. This is easy to see
since in this case none of the edges exists in both graphs, hence the subtraction
result in no change to A.
The 3. and 4. part of the claim states that if either the in- or out-set of A is a
subset of the corresponding set in B then A will stay complete after the subtrac-
tion. To prove that it holds imagine we have two complete bipartite graphs A and
B where Ain ⊆Bin. Say we do not have any knowledge of Aout and that Bout is
empty. Now if we add a node x to Bout along with the edges from every node in
Bin to x, such that B stays complete, then we will be in one of only two cases. Ei-
ther x exists in Aout or it does not. If x does not exist in Aout then subtract(A,B),
will not change A since no edges exists in both graphs. If x does exist in Aout
then, since Aout ⊆Bout, all edges in A ending in x will also exist in B. Hence x
is removed from Aout after the subtraction, leaving A a complete bipartite graph.
If we hereafter add some other node y to Bout, then we have the same two cases
again. Either y is in Aout or not. The cases here works the same way as with x
which holds for any node that could be added to Bout.
We have hence shown that we can not construct a complete bipartite graph B,
that if subtracted from A leaves A non-complete in the case Ain ⊆Bin. Similarly
it can be shown that we can not construct a complete bipartite graph B that after
being subtracted from A leaves A non complete if Aout ⊆Bout holds.
We now want to show that if none of the conditions are fulfilled, then we will not
get a CBG as output. Only the in-set of B can change the in-set of A and only the
out-set of B can change the out-set of A. This will simplify the task of considering
every case not covered by the conditions in the claim. Lets look at all possible

Implementation of Our 2-Hop preprocessing Algorithm
As stated above the key differences between our 2-Hop and the original is the

the underlying structure is interchangeable while reductions to the information

The subtraction of edges between center graphs has been implemented based on

the values of the two center graphs to see if an edge overlap. We then count each
overlapping edge in each case separately to determine the conditions from the
claim and for which it holds. Once a center graph has been subtracted from the
remaining, covering the center graph and adding labels is done in two steps. Be-
cause we have chosen to use arrays for the in- and out-sets, edges are not easily

2.4.3
Space and Time complexity for preprocessing
The time and space complexity of our modified 2-Hop algorithm bear a some
resemblance to that of the original version. Our experimentation lead to the
omission of ADS which impacts space and time complexity varying relative to
the given family of a graph.
The major advantage of our relaxed 2-Hop algorithm in terms of space comes
from our assumptions of CBGs. As a consequence ADS and the need for copying
center graphs is circumvented meaning the total space allocated is n2+n·2n+n =
O(n2) for the reachability matrix, the center graphs and the heap respectively.
The high space consumption is mainly due to storing the in- and out-set of the
center graphs as arrays of size n, where n is the amount of nodes in the DAG. The
center graph might not contain all nodes of the DAG and for some graph families
leads to a greater excess compared to others. One way to improve the space us-
age of the center graphs, without loosing the ability to perform fast search in the
node sets, could be to store the sets in hash tables.
For our 2-Hop variation we can make a tighter bound for the number of un-
marked iterations.
Since the entire center graph is being covered when cho-
sen, the amount of unmarked nodes being popped are bound to O(n). For each
popped unmarked node we subtract with every other center graph resulting in
O(n) subtractions which each uses O(n) time. In total we will perform O(n2) sub-
tractions of O(n) time across all iterations resulting in a total of O(n3) time.
Since the amount of unmarked iterations is O(n) we have a rough bound of
O(n2) marked iterations. The marked node iterations consist of calculating the
new density which is a constant time operation, and then updating the heap in
O(logn) time. Meaning the total preprocessing time can be bounded by O(n3).

3
Chain Cover
Having experimented with the 2-Hop cover variations we wanted to see how they
would compare to other existing ways of determining reachability. One such
method is the Chain cover method. Here the idea is to decompose the graph
into separate paths or so called chains and keep track of junctions where chains
connect and diverge.
3.1
Design
3.1.1
Data structure and query algorithm
The data structure used for this method mainly consist of a chain cover. A chain
cover is a collection of disjointed chains covering the entire graph. A chain is an
ordered collection of nodes for which it holds that a node can reach all nodes of
higher order and can be reached by all nodes of lower order. Each node in the
graph belongs to exactly one chain and the order of the node in the chain is stored
in the node. If there exist an edge in the graph (u,v) connecting two nodes that
belongs to different chains then v is called an entry point. For each node we store
the earliest entry point of to all other chains.
Let the width k be the number of chains in the graph. The space usage of storing
the data structure then becomes O(nk), since we store O(k) entry points in each
of the n nodes.
Having the chain cover makes the reachability query very simple. Asking if u in
chain a can reach v in chain b becomes looking at a′s entry point stored in v. If
the entry point exists and the order of the entry point is lower than the order of
v, then u can reach v and otherwise there is no reachability.
21
==PAGE:27==
Figure 3.1: Simple chain cover example. If we ask if node 2 can reach node 11.
we see that node 11 is on chain c1 and has order 3. We find node 2’s earliest entry
point in chain c1 to be node 4 which has order 1 in the chain. Since 1 ≤3 we know
that node 2 can reach node 11.
The query time is dependent on how the collection of entry point is stored in each
node. If we use arrays of length k, we can perform the query in constant time,
but then we use exactly nk space. If the entry points are stored in linked lists we
might use less space, since we do not need to store empty spaces, but then the
query time becomes bounded by the search time in the list.
3.1.2
Preprocessing Algorithm
A minimum chain decomposition can be constructed in linear time according to
[1]. In our paper we use a greedy algorithm to produce the chain decomposition.
We construct the collection of disjoint chains by processing the graph iteratively,
running through all nodes finding a longest path of nodes not yet associated with
a chain. This is done by giving nodes a score and linking each with a parent from
which the longest path can be drawn. The score symbolizes a node’s contribution
towards a path, which is important for creating each individual chain.
The process of creating chains and assigning nodes to each then becomes:
The chain cover is initialized with the following information stored for each node:
score: A score representing the distance from the initial node in a longest path,
initialized as null.
parent : The id of the parent node, initialized as null.
Furthermore we store the id of the node having the greatest score and its corre-
sponding score Ms.
22
==PAGE:28==
While every node has not been given a chain:
• For each node in topological ordered list select v:
– If the score of v is null, then if node is not associated with a chain, set
score to 1, if v has a chain assigned set score to 0.
– Go through each neighbour, u, of v and do the following:
∗If score of u is null or less than score of v, set score of u to the score
of v.
· If u is not associated with a chain add 1 to score of u.
· Set parent of u to v.
· Set Ms to score of u and set id to u.
• Construct the chain by backtracking the path from the node associated with
Ms using parents.
To answer reachability queries it is necessary to store the entry points as well.
Alike the previous brought up algorithms, the reachability matrix is used, here to
check reachability amongst nodes in different chains. For each chain go through
all it’s nodes comparing them to the remaining chains’ nodes to find the first
truthful entry using the reachability matrix. Thus each node will contain knowl-
edge about their first entry to all chains other than its own if any edge exist.

For the implementation of our chain cover we chose to use arrays of size k to store
the entry points, which allows fast query time.

3.3
Space and Time complexity for preprocessing
The chain cover is fundamentally different from that of the 2-Hop algorithms
and though they share the use of the reachability matrix they quickly diverge.
As the (n × n) reachability matrix is used to store transitive edges it is present at
all times in order to check for connectivity. The chain cover algorithm relies on
segmenting a graph into unique longest paths to create its cover. Thus it is bound
to the structure of the graph and how great the minimum number of chains are.
Furthermore because our implementation build segments iteratively and greed-
ily, the width is unknown at the beginning of the algorithm. Therefor chains
are stored in a linked list which can be prone to caching, affecting our empirical
data. Consequently, we convert the list to an array to allocate a chunk of mem-
ory for the entire object. Using the array chain-entries can then be stored at each
node to answer queries. Totaling an allocated space of n2+n+k·n = O(n2) respec-
tively for the reachability matrix and the chains as an array and the chain-entries.
To create a single longest chain we greedily sum contributing nodes while ex-
hausting all edges in the graph. This means the time complexity of covering the
entire graph in k longest path becomes O(k · (V + E)).
An immediate improvement also hinted at in the design section is to the stored
chain entries. As chain entries are stored as arrays asking reachability is fast but
at the cost of space allocated for empty values. By utilizing hashtables to store
chain entries in each node we can gain the benefits of fast queries without the
cost of empty entries.

5
Conclusion
After having performed tests on different graph types we can see that no algo-
rithm can be chosen as the best. The chain cover algorithm often performs the
smallest cover but for some graph types it notably exceeds the others with a O(n2)
growth. Graphs divisible into long segments of paths benefits the chain cover
while the 2-Hop variants are more suited for a wider graph. The 2-hop conse-
quently makes a small cover, but due to a very comprehensive preprocessing al-
gorithm, it might not be able to make the cover for larger graphs. The strength of
our relaxed 2-hop is that its preprocessing is less complex allowing us to process
larger graphs at the potential expensive of a larger cover. Our 2-hop is especially
good if the structure of a graph is known to be without diamond formations.
Given an unknown graph the best choice would be to use the 2-hop algorithm,
given one is not affected by computational limitations. However, our findings
on the GNP graph family suggest that the chain cover is effective on random
graphs. Meaning if one can accommodate the risk of polynomial increase in size,
the chain cover might be preferable as it also allows for preprocessing on larger
graphs with less computing resources needed.
