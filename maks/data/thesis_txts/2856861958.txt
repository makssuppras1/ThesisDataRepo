1.1 Intro  
In this report, I will present to you what I’ve been doing for the last few months. To begin with, I used an 
open LLM to mine arguments from Reddit posts and comments. These texts focused on sociopolitical issues, 
including climate change, gender issues, race issues in the US, and the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. Then, I 
classified these texts into two categories: claims and premises. The claim represents the main idea of the text 
and the premise the reason that supports it. I used a method called embeddings that transforms the text or 
vectors in multi-dimensional space (or simply numbers) and I clustered them using unsupervised machine 
learning algorithms to identify fine-grained sub-topics. I used different kinds of embeddings to achieve this 
and I picked the one that performed the best. Finally, I used these clusters and other features derived from the 
argument structure to predict post popularity. Why did I pursue this, aside from it being a requirement for 
completing my master's degree?

subreddit and across the platform.

Chapter 2 – Scientific literature review 
 
2.1 What is argumentation? 
Philosophers have been studying argumentation for some time now, with Aristotle’s Rhetoric being 
considered the best-known of the traditional body of work. Aristotle “sought to develop a general theory of 
persuasion and applied it to three types of speeches: the deliberative speech, which advices on a course of 
future action; the judicial speech, which accuses or defends someone whose past activities are to be judged; 
and the epideictic speech, which praises or blames a person of public interest.” Searching for more recent 
work, we find this definition by van Eemeren and Grootendorst [11]: 
“Argumentation is a verbal, social, and rational activity aimed at convincing a reasonable critic of the 
acceptability of a standpoint by putting forward a constellation of propositions justifying or refuting the 
proposition expressed in the standpoint.” 
We find it interesting how van Eemer brings together the different aspects of argumentation: the verbal, 
social, rational, the standpoint, and the constellation of propositions. This framework shows that 
argumentation is not just about logic but also about communication and social interaction. It highlights how 
people use reasoning to connect with others and justify their ideas in a clear and acceptable manner. 
For the purpose of the thesis, however, we will move to a more practical and better suited for natural language 
processing tasks [12]: 
“Argumentation can be defined as a process whereby arguments are constructed, exchanged and evaluated in 
light of their interactions with other arguments, each of which comprises a set of premises, pieces of evidence, 
offered in support of a claim. The claim is a proposition, an idea that is either true or false, put forward by 
somebody as true. The claim of an argument is normally called its conclusion. Argumentation may also 
involve chains of reasoning, where claims are used as premises for deriving further claims.” 
This definition highlights the close relationship between claim and premise.. The claim functions as the main 
idea or belief and the premise as the reason to support it.  
2.2. What is argumentation/argument mining? 
Argumentation mining thus, aims to automatically detect the argumentation of a document, i.e. detection of 
all the arguments involved in the argumentation process, their individual or local structure (rhetorical or 
argumentative relationships between their propositions), and the interactions between them (the global 
argumentation structure). Putting it more simply, argument mining is the action of automatically detecting 
argument elements and their relationship. We have only mentioned 2 argument elements or argument 
==PAGE:7==
 
6 
discourse units (ADUs) but depending on the argumentation model there are more. I will use the phrase 
argument elements instead of argument discourse units (ADUs) as it’s a more simple one. 
 
 
 
The general process of argument mining in its complete form is this (possibly and not necessarily in that 
order): [] 
1. Identify argumentative text  
2. Segment the text into argument elements   
 
3. Identify the central claim  
4. Identify the role/function of argument elements  
5. Identify relations between argument elements 
6. Build the overall structural representation  
7. Identify the type and the quality of the argumentation  
 
Since text is not necessarily argumentative, we need to apply step 1 to extract only the argumentative parts. 
We can use an argumentativity classification technique for the whole text and/or sections of it. In step 2, we 
identify sentences, or groups of them as argument elements of the argumentative structure. We can complete 
this step independently or in conjunction with some of the other tasks. In step 3, we identify the main claim 
of the text which is a crucial task for any argumentation mining process. Similarly, in step 4 we categorize the 
function of the argument elements in the argument, including whether they are evidence or potential 
objections. Step 5 creates relationships between the argument elements, such as "Support" and "Attack." 
Often though, simpler versions of this process will do. Instead of classifying all the different argument 
elements (for some argumentation models there are more than 6), we will identify an argument as “a set of 
premises that back up a conclusion” which reduces step 4 to detecting premises, eliminating step 5 (as the 
Support relation then is implicitly given). For this thesis, we will also use this simpler structure of identifying 
claims and supporting premises. That means we will also not use step 6 as we do not need to build the full 
structure as it is implied that the claim is supported by the premises.  
Finally, step 7 allows us to identify other characteristics of the argument, such as the particular type of 
argument from a range of predefined classes or the underlying reasoning pattern. It also allows us to evaluate 
the argument's overall quality, determining whether it is convincing and coherent. We will also not include 
this step in our analysis.

(use text-to-text generation papers, automated annotation using LLM papers)

The researchers investigated mining arguments from podcast transcripts using GPT-4. Two methods were 
employed: one for smaller components (claims, premises, and positions) and another for sophisticated 
argument structures (primary claims, premises, counterarguments, and rebuttals). To accommodate GPT-4, 
they divided transcripts into manageable chunks and used keywords to verify the accuracy of the responses. 
Despite these input constraints and challenges, the results show strong potential for using AI in argument 
analysis and suggest future research on multilingual data and open-source tools. 
 
2.4 Modified Toulmin’s model 
The researchers adapted Toulmin’s Model of Argumentation to mine argumentation elements from user-
generated web discourse, resulting in a modified model with five components: claim, premise, backing, 
rebuttal, and refutation. They made several key adjustments to fit the nuances of the data. The qualifier was 
excluded because the authors did not indicate the degree of cogency, and they removed the warrant as well 
since it was rarely stated explicitly and often overlapped with other components. They introduced a new 
component, refutation, to address instances where rebuttals were countered. As claims could be implicit, 
they allow annotators to infer the author’s stance, as well as classify multiple arguments within a single 
document without creating complex hierarchies.. The researchers clarified terminology by equating 
Toulmin’s grounds to premises, aligning with existing argumentation literature. They expanded the backing 
to include additional evidence that supports the argument broadly rather than acting as reasoning for a 
claim. They also allowed annotators to distinguish between premises, which provide general reasons, and 
backing, which often includes personal experiences or factual statements enhancing credibility. This 
approach offers more flexibility and practical application for argument mining tasks than the original model 
while preserving the fundamental structure of  Toulmin’s argumentation theory. 
2.5 Argument clustering (based on embeddings) 
The authors assessed how well two different types of embeddings cluster arguments by sub-topics within a 
given topic. They compared word-based TF-IDF and sentence-based BERT and used unsupervised 
clustering techniques to both embedding types, namely HDBSCAN, and k-means. they also apply 
dimensionality techniques like UMAP further improving clustering quality. 
They reported that TF-IDF embeddings when combined with HDBSCAN outperformed sentence-based 
BERT embeddings achieving a higher ARI. Additionally, dimensionality reduction improved clustering 
with TF-IDF embeddings outperforming again the sentence embeddings. While BERT embeddings 
captured richer semantic information they were not as effective as the word embeddings in this task. 
Overall, they conclude that their results confirm that argument clustering based on topical aspects is non-
trivial and high evaluation results are still hard to achieve in real-world settings.

Chapter 3 - Methodology  
3.1Embeddings  
 
Transformer models are a type of deep learning model first introduced in 2017 by Vaswani et al. in the paper 
"Attention Is All You Need". While designed to process sequential data like Recurrent Neural Networks 
(RNNs), transformers differ in that they do not process data in a specific order. This makes them particularly 
effective for NLP tasks, as they capture the contextual meaning of each word in the input. They achieve this 
using a self-attention mechanism, which focuses more on specific, relevant parts of the data by analyzing the 
contextual relationships between inputs. This architecture has demonstrated immense potential and serves as 
the foundation for numerous models, including BERT, and GPT-3. 
 
3.1.1 Sentence embeddings  
Sentence embeddings are dense vector representations of sentences that capture their semantic meaning, 
enabling machines to process and compare text effectively. Unlike word embeddings, which focus on 
individual words, sentence embeddings consider the full context of a sentence, making them particularly 
useful for tasks such as semantic search, clustering, paraphrase detection, and question answering (Reimers 
& Gurevych, 2019). 
Modern sentence embedding models, such as “sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2”, are based on 
transformer architectures like BERT or MiniLM and are designed for sentence-level tasks (Wang et al., 2020). 
These models encode sentences into vectors that position semantically similar sentences closer together in the 
vector space, enabling efficient similarity comparisons. 
 
3.1.2 Word embeddings 
TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency) is a statistical method used to represent the 
importance of words in a document relative to a corpus (Ramos, 2003). When applied to word embeddings, 
TF-IDF assigns weights to words based on their frequency in a document and their rarity in the entire corpus. 
These embeddings are sparse and interpretable, emphasizing the significance of specific terms. 
It combines two components: 
 
Term Frequency (TF) which measures how often a term t appears in a document d, normalized to account 
for document length. 
Inverse Document Frequency (IDF) which assesses how unique or rare a term is across a corpus of 
documents  
TF-IDF Score combines TF and IDF to determine the relevance of a term within the document. This score 
highlights terms that are frequent in a document but rare in the corpus, helping identify key topics.

3.2.1K-Means Clustering 
K-means is a clustering algorithm that partitions data into kkk clusters by minimizing the variance within 
each cluster. It works iteratively by first initializing kkk centroids, assigning each data point to the nearest 
centroid based on distance, and then recalculating the centroids as the mean of all points assigned to each 
cluster. This process repeats until the centroids stabilize or the assignments no longer change. 
 
3.2.2 Silhouette  
 
The silhouette score is a metric used to evaluate the quality of clustering by measuring how similar 
an object is to its own cluster compared to other clusters. It provides a measure of how well-separated 
and cohesive the clusters are. The Silhouette coefficient for a data point i is: 
Generally, Silhouette scores in the range of 0.7-1.0 indicate excellent clustering with strong 
cohesion and clear separation between clusters. Values between 0.4-0.7 represent “reasonable” 
clustering with some overlap between clusters, scores less than 0.4 suggest weak or poorly separated 
clusters and negative scores indicate misclassified points closer to other clusters than their own. 
 
3.3 Dimensionality reduction  
Dimensionality reduction is a process that transforms high-dimensional data into a more 
manageable low-dimensional representation while preserving its intrinsic structure. As Van Der 
Maaten et al. (2009) describe, this is particularly important for tasks such as visualization, where 
human interpretability is limited to two or three dimensions, or for preprocessing, where reducing 
dimensionality can improve computational efficiency and mitigate the curse of dimensionality. 
Techniques like Principal Component Analysis (PCA) focus on maximizing variance to retain 
global structures, while non-linear methods like t-SNE aim to preserve local relationships between 
data points, often revealing clusters and patterns that may otherwise remain obscured in high-
dimensional spaces. The choice of method depends on the specific goals, whether they involve 
uncovering patterns, improving model performance, or visualizing complex data. 
 
3.3.1 T-SNE 
 
t-SNE (t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding) is a dimensionality reduction algorithm that projects 
high-dimensional data into a lower-dimensional space, typically two or three dimensions. It minimizes the 
divergence between pairwise similarities in the original and reduced spaces, preserving local structure. By 
focusing on neighborhoods rather than global geometry, t-SNE effectively visualizes data clusters and 
relationships in a compact, interpretable manner.

3.4.1 Decision trees 
Decision trees are a supervised learning algorithm for classification and regression. They partition the 
dataset into subsets through a series of binary splits based on feature values, forming a tree structure. 
Each node represents a decision rule, and each leaf corresponds to a final prediction. The algorithm 
evaluates splits using criteria like Gini impurity or information gain to ensure the resulting subsets are as 
homogeneous as possible. Decision trees are interpretable and handle both numerical and categorical data 
well. However, they are prone to overfitting, especially with complex datasets, which can be mitigated by 
techniques like pruning or ensemble methods (e.g., random forests).

4.2 Argument mining and topic modeling as text-to-text generation   
 
We follow a process similar to that described in the paper [...], with adjustments to suit our specific 
needs. Instead of using ChatGPT, which requires a paid API, we utilize Mistral 7B, an open LLM 
with seven billion parameters, accessed through its API. We instruct Mistral 7B to process text from 
posts and comments and produce a structured JSON output. This output includes the name of each 
argument element and the corresponding section of the input text. 
Additionally, we ask the model to identify the topic of the text—choosing from a predefined list: 
Climate Change, Gender Issues, Racial Issues, and Other, using no more than two words. Posts and 
comments labeled as “Other” are discarded. The model is also instructed to label whether the 
identified topic is dominant or non-dominant in the text and to generate claims, even if they are not 
explicitly present in the input text. 
After evaluating a small sample of the output, we chose to discard posts and comments labeled as 
non-dominant, as they lacked relevance to the analysis. This decision reflects insights from the 
authors of paper [...], who emphasize the importance of designing effective prompts as a critical 
component of achieving accurate and meaningful results. 
4.2.1 Prompt engineering  
The prompt engineering task consists of two key components: First, instructing the LLM to apply 
the modified Toulmin model argument model as described by the authors [], and second, formatting 
the results into a structured output. On the technical side, we relied heavily on Roman’s 
experimental work, which explores the use of Mistral 7B for structured information extraction from

We identify potential reasons for this by reviewing the relevant literature: 
 
Semantic Overlap 
Daxenberger et al. (2020) and Färber and Steyer (2021) found that arguments often shared overlapping 
semantic content, which made it challenging for clustering algorithms to distinguish between them 
effectively. They highlighted that nuanced differences between arguments addressing similar subtopics were 
particularly difficult to capture. This overlap frequently led to clusters that lacked clear separation, as the 
embeddings struggled to differentiate arguments discussing overlapping themes. 
Embedding Limitations 
Reimers et al. (2019) and Färber and Steyer (2021) demonstrated that advanced embeddings, such as 
BERT-AVG, BERT-CLS, and ELMo, struggled to fully capture the context of multi-sentence arguments. 
Their findings showed that these embeddings often failed to model broader relationships within arguments 
spanning multiple sentences. Additionally, both studies noted that simpler embeddings like TF-IDF, used 
in systems such as ArgumenText and the Argument Search Framework, produced clusters based on surface-
level lexical features rather than capturing the deeper structure of arguments. 
Impact of Noise 
Daxenberger et al. (2020) and Färber and Steyer (2021) identified noise from heterogeneous data sources, 
such as web crawls and informal texts, as a significant factor in reducing clustering performance. They 
noted that algorithms like HDBSCAN categorized much of this irrelevant or poorly structured data as 
noise. However, even with such filtering, the noise diminished the clarity of the resulting clusters, posing a 
substantial challenge in systems relying on noisy, real-world datasets. 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
Challenges with Multi-Faceted Arguments 
Färber and Steyer (2021) reported that many arguments inherently addressed multiple facets of a topic, 
making traditional clustering algorithms such as k-means and HDBSCAN inadequate. They observed that 
these methods failed to account for arguments that could belong to multiple clusters simultaneously. This 
lack of flexibility in clustering overlooked the complexity of multi-faceted arguments, leading to less optimal 
results. 
Dataset and Data Quality Issues 
Reimers et al. (2019) and Färber and Steyer (2021) pointed out that poor-quality datasets, especially those 
containing informal or poorly structured language, further compounded clustering difficulties. They noted 
that datasets with inconsistent annotations or a lack of argument-specific cues led to weaker embeddings, 
reducing clustering accuracy. For example, in debate.org datasets, they observed that arguments often lacked 
clear structural markers, making them more challenging to cluster effectively. 
Summary 
Our work in argument clustering has likely been affected by several of these challenges. First, the presence of 
multi-sentence claims and premises in our dataset would have required embeddings to capture complex 
relationships across sentences, a task many existing embeddings like BERT-AVG or TF-IDF struggle with. 
Second, the informal and noisy nature of Reddit data introduced irrelevant or poorly structured arguments, 
further complicating the clustering process and potentially causing misclassification or reduced cluster clarity. 
Additionally, the potentially multi-faceted nature of many Reddit arguments, addressing multiple subtopics 
within a single comment, challenged the rigid structures of clustering algorithms like k-means and 
HDBSCAN, which are not well-suited to handle overlapping or multi-dimensional data. Together, these 
factors likely contributed to clustering difficulties, echoing the findings in related studies. We find that these 
factors likely played a significant role in the clustering challenges observed, aligning with relevant literature.

• 
Need for a New Approach: A single Decision Tree is likely insufficient for handling the 
observed complexity in the data. Exploring more advanced machine learning models, such as 
Random Forests, XGBoost for more complex datasets, may significantly improve performance. 
Additionally, incorporating richer feature engineering, such as NLP-derived metrics (e.g., 
semantic similarity, argument quality), could enhance the model's understanding of the data. 
After encountering these challenges, we attempted to address them by experimenting with 
alternative classification models. Unfortunately, these models performed worse than the Decision 
Tree, highlighting that the issues may not solely stem from the model's complexity but also from 
limitations in the dataset or the predictive power of the features themselves. This reinforces the 
need for potentially gathering additional data to improve overall performance. 
 
Summary  
 
Concluding our analysis, we saw that the average premise length was the most important feature of the 
model, with longer premises playing a significant role in determining the popularity of a post, potentially 
because they reflect more detailed or nuanced arguments. The attribute of how many premises were 
clustered with the corresponding claim also played a big role in predicting post popularity. On the other 
hand, the topics and the clusters representing sub-topics did not appear to have a significant impact on the 
model's performance. This suggests that the content and structure of individual claims and premises were 
more critical in influencing post popularity than the broader thematic context. The lack of significance in 
sub-topic clusters may indicate that the model struggles to capture the relationship between argument topics 
and popularity or that these clusters were not well-defined enough to contribute meaningfully. These 
findings highlight the importance of focusing on the quality and composition of arguments when predicting 
engagement metrics like popularity. Future work could explore refining sub-topic clustering or integrating 
additional features that better capture thematic relevance.

Chapter 5 - Conclusion 
 
This research underscores the complex interplay between argument structures, semantic embeddings, and 
clustering techniques when analyzing textual data. Our results reveal a nuanced picture of both the promise 
and limitations of argument mining and clustering tasks, especially when applied to heterogeneous and 
informal datasets such as Reddit posts. 
We began by obtaining a dataset of Reddit posts and comments from the "changemyview" subreddit, which 
is known for its argument-rich content. The data was preprocessed to focus on dominant topics—climate 
change, race issues, and gender issues—and underwent argument mining using a modified Toulmin model. 
By simplifying the argument structure to focus on claims and premises, we enhanced the clarity of outputs 
but encountered challenges with implicit claims and model-generated artifacts, echoing findings by 
Dusmanu et al. (2017) on mining arguments in informal online discussions 
The clustering analysis of extracted claims and premises revealed significant limitations. Across multiple 
methods (e.g., K-means, HDBSCAN, DBSCAN), we consistently observed poor clustering results, with 
Silhouette scores frequently below 0.01. The notable exception was dimensionality reduction techniques 
(e.g., t-SNE), which improved clustering performance slightly, consistent with findings by Färber and Steyer 
(2021). Despite these improvements, the clusters lacked semantic separation, likely due to overlapping 
argument content, embedding limitations, and noise in the data. 
In predicting post popularity, the average premise length emerged as the most critical feature, suggesting 
that longer, more detailed premises correlate with higher popularity. Features such as claim proximity to 
cluster centroids and premise clustering also played roles, emphasizing the importance of argument 
structure. However, sub-topic clusters failed to contribute significantly, indicating that broader thematic 
elements may not directly influence popularity. 
From a methodological perspective, our reliance on sentence embeddings and simplified clustering 
algorithms highlights the need for more advanced techniques to capture the complexity of real-world 
argumentation. The inclusion of richer semantic features, coupled with ensemble methods or neural 
approaches, could address some of the observed limitations 
Broader Implications 
This study reflects the potential of argument mining to distill meaningful insights from vast online 
dialogues, as Swanson et al. (2015) emphasize in their work on argument extraction from forums. 
However, it also demonstrates the inherent challenges in clustering and classifying nuanced argument 
structures. Future research should prioritize refining data annotation processes, exploring more 
sophisticated embeddings, and integrating domain-specific knowledge into the models. This approach will 
enhance the robustness and interpretability of both argument clustering and popularity prediction tasks.
