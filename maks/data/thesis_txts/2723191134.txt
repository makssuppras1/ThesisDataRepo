Abstract
The purpose of this master’s thesis project was to design a new low­power MAC layer
for an Internet of Things (IoT) sensor network. The thesis has been done in collaboration
with an external company for the specific application of data gathering in datacenters.
The main objective was reducing the power consumption by adapting the whole design
to this application. The plan was to implement a working prove of concept, to decide on
the feasibility of developing a complete protocol for the mentioned application.
The first part of the thesis involved investigating and understanding the different wireless
solutions available today. The research has focused on the ISM 2.4 GHz band tech­
nologies, and more specifically, in the Bluetooth Low Energy, Bluetooth Mesh and IEEE
802.15.4 standards­based protocols.
The second part starts with a clear definition of the requirements for the specific appli­
cation at hand. Then, there has been an evaluation of the available physical layers to
select the best one for the project. The bulk of this part was designing the MAC layer
by combining ideas from the researched protocols. The design has been based on the
Time­Slotted Channel Hopping (TSCH) channel access method defined in the 80215.4
standard. However, it has introduced modifications such a bidirectional, three­message
timeslot and a completely new join procedure, customizing the solution for the described
application and its requirements.
In the next part, the previous design has been implemented in a specific platform, the
Nordic Semiconductor nRF52840. Developing the whole MAC layer involves a lot of dif­
ferent functionality, the most important parts being described with more detail. There has
been an extra effort in the implementation of the synchronization, an essential component
for TSCH, in which the final approach using the low frequency oscillator to measure time
has been able to significantly reduce the energy used while idle.
In the last part, a series of test measuring the power consumption have been carried out to
analyze the result and impact of some design and implementation decisions: the chosen
physical layer, the efficiency of the low frequency oscillator against the high frequency one
and the modified timeslot structure. Finally, the developed protocol has been compared
to existing implementations, evaluating the results.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation and objectives
This master’s thesis project has been carried out in collaboration with an external com­
pany, TycheTools. TycheTools is a startup company developing a platform to help with
climate monitoring and cooling operation in datacenters. One of the most important part
of the solution is to collect telemetry data and, to that end, TycheTools has developed a
wireless sensor network.
However, the current implementation, which uses Bluetooth Mesh, present some impor­
tant drawbacks, among which the power usage is the main one. The different alternatives
that solve some of them have some other issues.
There are some viable alternatives, like ZigBee, Thread or Z­Wave, each of them with
different advantages and disadvantages, but the main problem of the power usage is
present in all of them.
Faced with this situation, it was proposed to develop a new wireless communication proto­
col from scratch, tailored to this specific use­case, with efficiency as the main goal. Due to
the effort involved in this kind of developments, this master’s thesis project will research,
design and implement a proof of concept, focused on the MAC layer. It results will help
decide on the feasibility of the project.
Therefore, the main objectives of this master’s thesis project are:
• Research the existing wireless technologies, including both commercial as well as
state­of­the­art solutions.
• Design a new MAC layer after defining some requirements, the main one being
reducing energy usage.
• Implement the new design in existing hardware.
• Analyze and evaluate the results.

2
Background
This chapter introduces the necessary background knowledge for existing wireless tech­
nologies. As explained in Section 4.1, the research is focus in wireless protocols working
in the 2.4 GHz ISM band. The decision leaves out other interesting protocols, such as
LoRa or Sigfox, which work in the sub­gigahertz radio frequency and are focused on long­
range communication.
The following sections delve into Bluetooth Low Energy, Bluetooth Mesh and the IEEE
802.15.4 standard. The 802.15.4 standard is a special case as it does not feature a
complete communication protocol. It only defines the physical and MAC layers. However,
it is implemented by popular solutions like ZigBee, or Thread, built on top of 6LoWPAN
(IPv6 over Low­Power Wireless Personal Area Network).
2.1
Bluetooth Low Energy
Bluetooth [1] is a wireless communication protocol introduced in 1998, managed by the
Bluetooth Special Interest Group (SIG). It operates in the unlicensed ISM (2.4 GHz) band.
Version 4 of the standard introduced Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE). This new and indepen­
dent technology isn’t compatible with the previous versions of the protocol, now denomi­
nated Classic Bluetooth. As the name suggest, BLE main focus was to reduce the power
consumption of communications. This made Bluetooth suitable for a whole new range of
low power devices, such as sensors, beacons, wearables or healthcare devices, among
others.
BLE uses 40 channels in the 2.4 GHz band, with a distance of 2 MHz between centers
of adjacent channels. Channels 0 to 36 are called data channels, while channels 37, 38
and 39 are called advertising channels. As shown in Figure 2.1, the advertising channels
are spread about the whole range. They have been placed in these specific frequencies
to avoid interference with Wi­Fi.
Figure 2.1: BLE Channels
Similar to these two types of channel, BLE has two modes of communication. The first
one is advertisement. In this mode, a device with the advertiser role broadcasts packets
periodically in at least one of the advertising channels. Any other device can assume the
scanner role, listening in these channels to receive those packets. The communication is
one­way only, from the advertiser to the scanner. This mode uses the advertising channels
to avoid packet collision with the other one. The maximum advertising payload is 37 bytes.

For the second communication mode, the two devices must first connect. To initiate a
connection, the advertising mode is used: the peripheral (slave) device must send con­
nectable advertising packets. The central (master) device scans the advertising channels,
and when it receives this packet, it sends back a connection request. Once connected, the
central and the peripheral communicate at regular intervals, called connection events. For
the connection events they use the data channels. This packets have a maximum payload
of 39 bytes. Every connection event they hop to a new channel, following a pattern only
know by the two of them. This helps keeping interference to a minimum, even in noisy
environments. Between connection events, the devices can turn off the radio, achieving
a low power usage.
Version 4.2 added a new feature called LE Data Packet Length Extension, which allowed
packets sent over data channels to has a 257 bytes maximum payload. This feature
theoretically increases the data­rate, as it allows to send more data per packet, while the
physical layer overhead is kept the same. However, in practice this is not always so, as
it depends on how noisy the environment is. The larger a packet is, the longer time it
needs on­air, giving more possibilities for a collision with other packets or to be corrupted
by noise. If due to this most packet are lost, the effective data rate can be even lower.
Version 5 of the protocol evolved and improved BLE. The most interesting feature is the
addition of new physical layers. Besides the original BLE physical layer at 1 Mbps (LE 1M
PHY), this revision added three more: A 2 Mbps for increase data­rate but and expected
shorter range (LE 2M PHY), and two coded physical layers which increase resistance two
noise and range, at the cost of speed, working at 500 and 125 Kbps (LE Coded PHY, S=2
and S=8, respectively).
BLE provides encryption an authentication for the connection oriented communication
mode. If security is required while using the advertising mode, it should be implemented
by the application.
2.2
Bluetooth Mesh
Bluetooth Mesh [2] is a new protocol released by the Bluetooth SIG in 2017, which allows
many­to­many communication in a mesh topology network.
The protocol is structured in four layers, which seat atop the BLE stack. These layers,
from top to bottom, are:
• The access layer is the interface between the application and the rest of the stack. It
exposes how data is encrypted and sent in a standard way. It also forwards received
data to the application.
• The transport layer is divided in two. The upper transport layer controls how appli­
cation data is encrypted and authenticated with the application key. The lower trans­
port layer defines how packets are segmented before being sent, and reassembled
upon reception.
• The network layer controls how messages are relayed across the network, deciding
when to forward or when to drop packets. It also controls network level encryption
and authentication.
• The bearer layer defines how packets are sent between devices. There are two
bearers, corresponding to the two modes of BLE communication: advertising bearer
and GATT bearer. The advertising bearer is the most common one. The GATT
bearer allow external devices that do not support the advertising bearer to connect to
4
Low Power IoT Mesh Network
==PAGE:14==
the network through proxy nodes (nodes that support both bearers), using a regular
BLE connection.
The advertising layer is the most common one because it allows devices to broadcast
messages, so they can be received by any other node in range. To increase its robustness
against noise, each message is sent in in the three advertising channels, at the cost of
tripling the power used for each transmission. In general, while a node is not sending
anything, it should have its radio always on, in receiver mode, since the other nodes send
their data at random times. Nodes in receiver mode change the channel they listen with
a given period.
To reach nodes outside of direct range, messages are forwarded through the network
using a strategy called managed flooding. This strategy does not provide any actual
routing. Instead, every message is relayed in every direction, reaching every node in the
network. Nodes that are not supposed to receive a message drop it after it has been
relayed. To avoid having every message traveling back and forth through the network,
which would render it unusable, each node maintains a short cache of relayed messages.
A message is only relayed the first time it has been received.
As explained before, BLE advertising packets have no security, and thus Mesh has to
define its own encryption and authentication schemes. To be able to decide whether to
relay, drop, or pass up the stack a message when its received, every node in a network
needs to be able to decrypt that message. But if it was not meant for that device, it can
be a security vulnerability than that node can actually read the application data. The
solution to this defined by Bluetooth Mesh is to have two levels of security: network and
application. Every set of nodes exchanging messages for a certain purpose or application
shared a common application key. They use it to encrypt the application data in the upper
layer. This already encrypted data is forwarded to the network layer, where is added the
network related data (f.e. the source and destination address) and encrypted again with
the network key. A device belongs to a certain network if it has the key associated to that
network.
There is a third type of key, called device key, which is unique to each device, and usually
its only known to that device and a central, more powerful device controlling the network.
This key is supposed to be used only when configuring the device.
As explained before, nodes belonging to a Mesh network are expected to have the radio
turned on all the time. This is completely unaffordable by low power nodes, as it uses
a lot of power. To solve this problem, the Mesh standard provides the Friend and Low
Power Node (LPN) features. A LPN is a node that can turn off its radio most of the time,
and thus reduce greatly the power consumption. To avoid missing any messages, it must
first bond with another node, called Friend. Friend nodes can have many bonded LPNs,
and they keep a queue of messages for each of them. When a LPN wakes up, it sends
a message to its Friend requesting any pending messages, which are sent back by the
Friend. LPNs must be in direct range of its Friend.
In order to join a network, a device needs at least a network key, and probably, some
application keys too. These can be given to devices by and out­of­band method like, for
example, from a phone, or hardcoded when the device is programmed. However, the
Mesh protocol offers a method as well, called provisioning. The network needs at least
one Provisioner, which is a node capable of adding new devices. When a device wants
to join a network, it starts sending provisioning beacons, again, in the three advertising
channels. When a Provisioner node receives this beacons, it can decide to provision the

device by sending to him a Link Open message. If the device also wants to join that
network, it replays with a Link ACK, and then the provisioning process takes place. It
involves assigning the new node a unique address and keys, such as the network or the
device key. When the process ends, the new device becomes a node, part of the network.
2.3
IEEE 802.15.4
The IEEE 802.15.4 [3] is an wireless communication standard released in 2003, main­
tained by the IEEE 802.15 working group. It was created for low­rate wireless personal
area networks (LR­WPAN), and it targets low­power, low­data­rate devices.
Unlike Bluetooth, this standard is not a full communication standard. It just defines the
physical (PHY) and medium access control (MAC) layers. The PHY layer specifies the
radio frequency, modulation, and coding used by the devices, while the MAC layer defines
the procedures for accessing the shared wireless channel and transmitting data.
The first version of the standard, release in 2003, defined one physical layer, called O­
QPSK, which could use the 868/915 MHz and the 2450 MHz bands. The MAC layer
included a beacon enabled approach, with a superframe structure to synchronize the
network. Each superframe had a contention­based period during which any device can
try to send data, using slotted CSMA/CA to avoid packet collision. After the contention­
based period, there is a contention­free period, with a limited number of guaranteed time
slots (GTS) that could be assigned to specific nodes. The MAC layer also defined a
more simple, non­beacon mode. It has some similarities with how Bluetooth Mesh works,
sending a packet without any kind of order or synchronization, as soon as it is available.
However, unlike Mesh, this mode also used CSMA/CA for accessing the medium to try to
avoid collisions.
The standard also defines two distinct network topologies: the star topology, characterized
by a central node known as the coordinator, to which all other nodes are connected, and
the mesh topology, in which every node has the capability to communicate with any other
node through the relay of packets across the network.
Security wise, the standard does not enforce any kind of security, but it provides guidelines
to implement it if needed. The standard supports the following security features: data
confidentiality, data authenticity and replay protection.
The standard has rapidly evolved with new revisions every few years, the last one in
2020. It has been extended to include new physical layers and MAC modes. Some of
these new approaches target some specific sectors, like the RCCN for rail communication
and control or the MBAN for medical devices. However, it also includes general modes
that improved on reliability and robustness, such as DSME or TSCH. The first defined
MAC layer had some limitations that these extensions were trying to solve:
• This mode only uses one channel, making it very vulnerable to external interference.
Moreover, as more nodes join the network, the probability of an on­air collision rises.
The result is that in dense networks, it is also affected by internal interference.
• The latency is unbounded. The limited number of GTSs (7 at maximum) makes the
contention­based period the primary communication method for most nodes in the
network, and this method could fail indefinitely due to collisions. As in the previous
case, this effect worsens as more nodes join the network.
• While the star topology works well with this mode, the unreliability of the contention­
based communication and the limited amount of GTSs makes it difficult to design

multi­hop mesh topologies.
The TSCH (Time Slotted Channel Hopping) mode is particularly interesting. TSCH was
added as an amendment in 2012, and to the main standard in 2015. It solves with the
above­mentioned limitations, and it will be a key concept during this project.
TSCH introduces a new medium access mechanism, which divides time into regular,
fixed­length timeslots that repeat periodically in what is referred to as a slotframe. During
each timeslot, a link can be scheduled, enabling a node to transmit or receive a packet and
optionally receive or transmit an acknowledgement (ACK). The channel used for commu­
nication is also changed in accordance with a predetermined pattern through a technique
known as channel hopping. This approach not only mitigates the impact of interference,
but also enhances reliability while allowing multiple links to occur simultaneously within
the same timeslot.
TSCH defines the absolute slot number, which is the number of timeslots since the start
of the network. It is tracked by every network in the network, and it allows new nodes to
synchronize, and to compute the channel for any given link. It is also used as the frame
counter, allowing time­dependent security.
TSCH establishes the concept of the absolute slot number (ASN), a measure of the num­
ber of timeslots since the start of the network, and it is followed by every node within the
network. The ASN has three main purposes:
• When broadcasted in beacons announcing the network, it allow new devices to
synchronize the network.
• It is used by nodes to calculate the channel for any given link.
• It serves as the frame counter, providing the foundation for time­dependent security
mechanisms.
The Figure 2.2 shows an TSCH communication example with three timeslots per slotframe
and four nodes (A, B, C, D). The first timeslot contains two links (A ­> B and C ­> D), the
second, one link (B ­> A) and the third another two (A ­> D and B ­> C). The slotframe
repeats over time, it can be seen how the channels used for communication change in
each of them. The ASN keep track of the total number of timeslots elapsed.

Requirements
As briefly described in the introduction, the specific use­case is data gathering inside
datacenters. The most common devices for this task are the sensors, which are battery­
powered. They are hooked to the datacenter servers, to both the cold aisle front, called
the inlet, and the hot aisle front, called the outlet.
The main purpose of the network is to carry the telemetry data from the sensors, to the
network coordinator. The latter acts as a the network gateway, sending the data to the
cloud via TCP/IP. The telemetry data covers a large portion of all the information that
is going to be sent over the network. However, there are also messages flowing from
the coordinator to the sensors, like, for example, to configure the data acquisition period.
Moreover, the network can also contain actuators that control the cooling system, which
adds a restriction on the network latency of up to a couple of minutes.
Datacenters are big enough so that sensor can be out of the range of the gateway, so, a
star topology is not an option. The network needs to be able to relay messages across
nodes, using a mesh topology. While the relay functionality is usually implemented in the
network layer and this project is focused on the MAC layer, it still needs to support it. To
this end, the use main powered relay nodes in the network is allowed, but obviously, the
fewer, the better.
Another important aspect of a datacenter environment is that it has little interference.
Rooms are usually well isolated, due to the fact that it helps with the cooling. Furthermore,
the majority of them do not have any kind of wireless network. When they do have one,
like for example a Wi­Fi network, is seldom used, as usually there is nobody there unless
they are fixing or improving the room. The only possible source of interference are packet
collisions with other nodes of the network. Depending on the specifics of the required
service, the amount of nodes and data rate can vary a lot. When both of them are high,
the network needs to support the communication with as little retransmissions as possible.
A big drawback of the current implementation using Bluetooth Mesh is the effective pay­
load for custom messages, only 8 bytes long. It makes nodes having to split data in
various frames, with the power increase it implies. It also makes it almost impossible to
implement Over­the­Air updates.
On the other hand, a good advantage of Mesh is the short time needed for joining the
network. This is useful for quick deployments, or for relocation of nodes to problematic
areas, to gather more detailed data there. The new design should also try to implement
a fast joining procedure.
Trying to design a new general purpose wireless protocol from scratch is a big and com­
plex task. And probably pointless, since already available options like Bluetooth and the
802.15.4­based protocols are very mature technologies, already supporting a wide range
of different applications. The aim of this project is not to replace them, but to design and
implement a MAC layer explicitly tailored to the use­case described above, having energy
efficiency as the major goal. Therefore, it only needs to support the necessary features
to cover the functionality described above.

• Bidirectional communication.
• Bounded latency under a couple of minutes.
• Support for mesh networking.
• Relatively noiseless environment.

• Fast joining.

4.1
Physical layer
Altough not part of the MAC layer, the first thing to do is to decide which physical layer
to use. The decision has been limited to the 2.4 GHz Industrial, Scientific, and Medical
(ISM) frequency band, an worldwide unlicensed band. Another option could be a lower
frequency physical layer, like the 800­900 MHz, which could provide an extended range
However, it is not of much use for this specific use­case. as nodes are always close
enough from each other. On other hand, it will provide a lower bandwidth and data­rate,
resulting in more time to transmit the same data. The main options considered are the
BLE LE 1M physical layer, and the 802.15.4 O­QPSK physical layer.
The LE 1M PHY is the main BLE physical layer, released as part of the Bluetooth 4.0
specification. It has a 1 Mbps data­rate and 40 channels, spaced by 2 MHz. Originally,
it had a 39­byte maximum payload, which was increased to 257 in Bluetooth 4.2. The
payload includes a 2­byte header at the beginning, so the effective payload is 255 bytes.
Figure 4.2 shows the structure of a packet. Each packet has a 1­byte preamble, a 4­byte
access address field, the 2­byte header, the payload and finally a 3­byte CRC. The total
overhead is 10 bytes.
Figure 4.2: LE 1M PHY
Low Power IoT Mesh Network
11
==PAGE:21==
The O­QPSK PHY was part of the 802.15.4 standard since its first release. It supports
various bands, but this project will focus in its 2.4 GHz band version, which has a data­rate
of 250 Kbps. It has 16 channels, spaced by 5 MHz. The maximum payload is 127 bytes
long, which includes a 2­byte long CRC at the end, so the effective payload is 125 bytes.
Figure 4.3 shows the structure of a packet, which has a 4­byte preamble, a 1­byte SFD
(start­of­packet delimiter), a 1­byte long PHR (PHY header, which includes the payload
length), the payload and the final 2­byte CRC. The total overhead is 8 bytes.
Figure 4.3: 802.15.4 O­QPSK PHY
In general, a higher data­rate requires less energy simply because it expends less time
with the radio active to transmit the same amount of data. Here, the advantage is to
LE 1M, which has a four times higher data­rate, so it uses a fourth of the energy per
packet. However, a lower data­rate usually comes with a more robust encoding, which
provides more resistance to external interference and an extended range. This is exactly
the case of the 802.15.4 O­QPSK PHY. In a noisy environment, packets can be lost due to
interference, requiring retransmission. If the amount of retransmissions is large enough,
it can be more energy­efficient to use to robust lower data­rate option.
The relatively noiseless environment described in the requirements makes LE 1M PHY
a more energy­efficient solution. Moreover, the node density is high enough to make no
use of the O­QPSK PHY extended range.
When using TSCH, the main source of interference is mainly internal, due to packet colli­
sion. The only way to avoid them during multiple communications in the same timeslot is
by using different channels, due to the strict synchronization during transmissions. This
becomes a problem in highly dense networks, where the chance of communicating in
the same timeslot increases. The advantage is again for LE 1M PHY, with 40 channels
against 802.15.4 O­QPSK PHY 26.
[4] and [5] provide empirical results verify the reasoning made above. Therefore, taking
into account the requirements for this project, it is clear that the LE 1M PHY is a better
option.
Finally, the LE 1M PHY 255­byte maximum payload is not needed for the data­rate re­
quirements of this application, and it increases unnecessarily the timeslot duration. The
chosen maximum payload is 125­byte long, a fair compromise. It makes MAC layer de­
sign and implementation compatible with the 802.15.4 O­QPSK physical layer, in case it
is needed to support it in new environments where noise is more relevant.

4.3
Timeslot structure
The timeslot structure presented in the 802.15.4 standard is unidirectional: There is one
node sending data and another one receiving it, and optionally sending and acknowledg­
ment back, in each timeslot. In this protocol, the timeslot structure has been redesigned
to provide a bidirectional communication. It is achieved by exchanging three frames dur­
ing each time slot. The fist two, Data and Data Rsp, can carry up to 119 bytes of data,
while the last ACK, up to 7 bytes, as shown in Figure 4.5. The Data Rsp frame also acts
as an acknowledgement for the first Data frame, and so both data frames are acknowl­
edge, making the communication reliable. In this example Node A is the node starting
the communication, and will be referred to as the sender (even though the communica­
tion is bidirectional), while Node B starts in reception mode, and will be referred to as the
receiver.
The 802.15.4 timeslots include the option to perform a clear channel assessment (CCA) at
the beginning. In TSCH, CCA only helps against external interference, due to the fact that
transmissions are closely synchronized so the collision avoidance mechanism would not
detect other frames from the same network. The requirements for this design establish
that the external noise is low, so CCA does not provide any benefits. Thus, the option has
been removed, making the timeslots shorter.

4.3.1
Time synchronization
Time synchronization is a key aspect of TSCH. It is what allows nodes to have the radio
powered off most of the time. On the other hand, the tight synchronization adds significant
complexity to the communication process. Whichever method used to track time, it always
adds some error. Thus, over time, clocks from different nodes can drift apart, so they will
end up with different notions of the current time.
That explains the need for the big first reception window. It has to be long enough to
tolerate the drift since the last communication. If both nodes were perfectly synchronized,
the beginning of the frame should arrive exactly in the middle of the reception window.
The drift, called time correction can be measured in the receiver as the distance from the
the middle of the window. Figure 4.7 illustrates this method.
Figure 4.7: Timeslot synchronization
To maintain the synchronization, nodes must adjust its time according to the measured
drift. The one correcting its time is called time receiver, while the other is called time
source. The 802.15.4 defines that if a node have multiple time source neighbors, it should
synchronize to all of them, in an implementation­dependent manner. The available imple­
mentations of this feature [6] shows that it adds significant complexity. The new design is
very centralized, with data flowing to and from the coordinator. To avoid the complexity,
in this protocol, each node is only allowed to have one time source neighbor: the one
closest to the coordinator. The network coordinator time is considered to be the ”real”
time. Figure 4.8 shows how time is relayed across the network.
Figure 4.8: Timeslot flow

The easiest implementation would be to make the time source always be the sender
node too, and the time receiver the receiver node, since it is where the time correction
is measured. However, receivers usually use more power due to the first, big receiving
window during which the radio is on. If main powered relays are used, they will end up as
time source for most sensors, which are low­power nodes. This will make the latter end
up as receiver nodes, the role that uses more power.
Thus, a reverse time synchronization scheme has been designed in which low­power
nodes are always the senders if their time source is a main powered node. The time
correction is measured in the main powered device, and send back inside the Data Rsp
frame. When the timeslot is over, the low­power device corrects its time accordingly. This
ensures the maximum energy efficiency in low­power nodes.
4.3.2
Avoidable last ACK
As described in the requirements, in this specific use­case most data is telemetry, going
from the sensor nodes to the coordinator. Moreover, the data acquisition period can be
much larger than the slotframe period (5 seconds). This can result in a significant amount
timeslots not exchanging any data at all, and the ones doing it, containing mostly data
from the sensors to for the coordinator. However, frames must be exchanged even if
there is no data to be sent, to maintain synchronization.
Sensor nodes are also low­power nodes. Then, assuming the reverse time synchroniza­
tion method describe in Section 4.3.1, the Data Rsp frame will be empty a significant
amount of times. In these cases, the node sending the Data Rsp should set the ACK
required bit field of the frame control to zero, as described in 4.2.2, since there is no need
to acknowledge an empty frame. Then, both nodes skip the last ACK frame, ending the
timeslot there. The result is a timeslot as energy efficient as the original 802.15.4 when the
communication flows from low­power nodes to main powered ones, with only two frames
per timeslot, but without losing the bidirectional communication when needed.
4.4
Joining procedure
In TSCH, there are two steps needed to join an existing network. The first one is syn­
chronizing with the network, to be able to communicate its nodes. After that, the joining
node needs to be properly configured in a process that this project calls provisioning. The
802.15.4 standard defines the first step, but leaves the second implementation dependant.
This project will implement an original solution.
4.4.1
Synchronization with the network
The network must be advertised in order to allow new nodes to discover it. Nodes an­
nouncing the network and accepting joining requests are called provisioners, and will
typically be main powered. This nodes advertise the network by sending beacon frames.
The 802.15.4 standard also defines beacon frames (although they are different from the
ones designed here). A joining node needs to scan on random channels until it receives it.
Depending on the number of provisioner nodes, it can take a while to receive one. More­
over, you typically want to receive various of them, to choose the best node to connect to.
The process can take up to several minutes, all of them with the radio active, using lots
of energy.
Some research has been done in this area. For example, [7] proposes a join method by
including information about where to find the next beacons in every frame. In another
one, [8], the beacon frames follow an independent channel­hopping scheme that repeats
over time and can be predicted by new nodes. Another example is [9], which describes a
18
Low Power IoT Mesh Network
==PAGE:28==
method for a faster re­synchronizing, after synchronization has been lost, specially suited
for non­static nodes.
However, by taking advantage of the low noise environment, a faster and more energy
efficient method has been devised in this project. Beacon frames are always sent in the
same channel, which is reserved for this operation. The selected channel is BLE channel
39, the one with fewer interferences with Wi­Fi (only with channel 13). This solution is
obviously less robust against interference and noise. However, it provides a couple of big
advantages. First and foremost, joining nodes know which channel to scan. Since the
slotframe period is 5 seconds, that period becomes the upper bound to receive a beacon.
Moreover, if a scan is performed over the whole period, the joining node can be sure
to have received beacons from all provisioners in its range, allowing it to take the best
decision about which one to connect to.
Beacon frames are sent in the first transmission slot during a timeslot the one also used
by the Data frame in regular connections. Knowing this, joining nodes can synchronize
with the network, upon reception by calculating when that timeslot started.
After the node is synchronized, it has to be configured. In order to chose one provisioner
device, and when to communicate with it, the beacon frame includes the following fields:
• The provisioner MAC address, identifies it.
• The number of child nodes that this provisioner already have.
• The distance to the network coordinator, in hops.
• The Absolute slot number (ASN), used to compute channels as explained in 4.5.
• The provisioning timeslots, a list containing, for each provisioning timeslot, its
timeslot number and its channel offset. Described in Section 4.4.2.

4.5
Channel hopping
Channel hopping is the second key feature of TSCH. It provides robustness against in­
terference by using a different channel in each communication timeslot. More valuable
for this project, it also allows multiple nodes to communicate in the same timeslot without
20
Low Power IoT Mesh Network
==PAGE:30==
packet collision by doing so in different channels. This effectively increases the available
bandwidth, allowing denser networks.
A connection between two nodes is defined by the timeslot number and the channel offset.
The actual channel used during an specific timeslot is computed as follows:
ch = ch_list[(ASN + channel_offset)%ch_list_length]
where ch is the computed channel, ch_list is an array of the available physical channels,
the ch_list_length is the length of that array, ASN is the absolute slot number and the
channel_offset is a number between 0 and ch_list_length, which prevents every con­
nection from following the same hopping sequence.
BLE has 40 channels, numbered from 0 to 39. The last one, channel 39, is reserved
for announcing the network with beacons, as described in Section 4.4.1. This leaves 39
channels for regular communication. The channel list refereed in the previous paragraph
contains them in a pseudo­random order. The method used to order them is described in
section 6.2.10 of the 802.15.4­2020 standard.

5.1.1
The nRF52840 SoC
The nRF52840 is a 64 MHz ARM Cortex­M4 based System­on­Chip developed by Nordic

timing and power control functionality, among other things. It takes control of the related
peripherals, blocking the application from being able to use them.
There is also the option of programming the controller directly via registers, which are
documented in extend in the datasheet. As explained before, complete control of the

hardware was deemed necessary to reduce the power consumption. Thus, this has been
the approach followed for the development, without using the Nordic libraries.
The nRF54840 includes a variety of peripherals, to support all kind of applications. The
following list contains the most important ones used in this project:
• The radio peripheral allows the device to transmit and receive data. It operates
in the 2.4 GHz band and it supports the four BLE physical layers, the 802.15.4 O­
QPSK and two Nordic proprietary ones. In order for it to work, it needs a 64 MHz
external crystal oscillator.
• The timer peripheral is used to measure time. It uses a 64 MHz clock source, which
can be the CPU internal one, or an external crystal oscillator. Its high frequency
gives this peripheral a high resolution, suited for when time requirements are tight.
The nRF52840 has five independent instances of this peripheral.
• The RTC (Real Time Counter) peripheral is also used to measure time. In contrast
to the timer peripheral, the RTC uses a 32 KHz clock source, which can be internally
synthesized by the chip (with a significant high error) or taken from a 32.768 KHz
external crystal oscillator. Its lower frequency provides less resolution, but it also
uses less power.
• The PPI (Programmable Peripheral Interconnect) is a peripheral that connects other
peripherals When a peripheral completes an action or produces an event, it can
activate a task in another one, without the need for the CPU to wake up.
• The UART peripheral is used to communicate the coordinator with a computer.
• The GPIO peripheral is used to measure the execution time of synchronization and
time sensitive functions.
5.2
Synchronization
The synchronization has been one of the toughest parts to implement, due to the variety
of factors that affect it.
5.2.1
Timer module
The timer module implements the timing functionality. The module uses the timer and
RTC peripherals to allow the user to schedule a callback function to be executed in a
given time. It provides a microsecond precision. The callback is executed inside the
interrupt context of those peripherals, and the CPU can go to sleep in the meantime.
The first implementation only used the timer peripheral.
It was configured with a 64
prescaler to work at 1 MHz, obtaining the desired resolution of 1 microsecond. This ver­
sion was used during almost the whole the project. With its higher precision, it was easier
to develop and debug the synchronization functionality.
Then, the timer module was improved to reduce the power usage by using the RTC when
possible. When the scheduled time was bigger than the RTC resolution, the timer module
will switch off the high frequency (HF) oscillator and use the RTC for measuring time. If
the RTC resolution is not an exact divider of the scheduled time, the remaining time is
measured with the timer peripheral, to maintain the precision. The RTC and the timer are
connected through the PPI, to avoid waking up the CPU.
In that situation, the HF oscillator must be switched back on. However, it needs some time
to stabilize so that it can be used by the radio and the timer peripherals. This time depends
Low Power IoT Mesh Network
23
==PAGE:33==
on the specific crystal oscillator being used, but in the Nordic datasheet they recommend
it to be of at least 1.5 ms to support with most crystals. Considering this, the RTC was
configured with a 64 prescaler to reduce its frequency to 512 KHz, resulting in an RTC
resolution of 1953.125 μs, which leaves enough time for the HF crystal to stabilize. The
HF crystal is turned on just one RTC period before the timer is used, as shown in Figure
5.2.
Figure 5.2: Measuring time with RTC and timer peripherals
Finally, the timer module also implements functions for measuring time, used mainly dur­
ing timeslots to calculate the remaining time until its end when the communication is done.
For this purpose, the module uses a second timer peripheral instance.
5.2.2
Synchronization module
The synchronization module uses the timer module (Section 5.2.1) and the radio module
(Section 5.3.1) to implement the timeslot design described in Section 4.3.
It performs most of the time sensitive functionality: synchronizes the device for the first
time after receiving a beacon frame, starts the timeslots, fetches pending data from the
connection queues, sends and receives that data using the radio and finishes the timeslot
by scheduling the next active one with the timer module.
It also calculates the time correction upon reception of the first Data frame, and keeps
it or sends it back depending on the time source configuration for that connection. The
correction is applied at the end of the timeslot.
To ensure compliance with the timeslot communication timings, it takes into account the
different software and hardware delays. The following list briefly describes them:
• The radio ramp­up time, of 40 μs, is the time needed for the radio before it can start
receiving or transmitting. Taken from the nRF52840 datasheet.
• The radio TX­to­RX overhead, of 10 μs. This is an undocumented delay of the
radio peripheral due to the fact that data processing on the receiver side is clocked
at the same speed as the radio bit­rate. It was discovered by a Nordic DevZone
user, Fmag, and subsequently confirmed by Nordic [10].
• The interrupt overhead, of 4 μs, is the time that elapses since a peripheral gener­
ates an event and the associated interrupt to that event is executed.
• The radio radio END­to­DISABLE delay, of 21 μs. The radio is configured to disable
automatically after receiving or transmitting.
• The start of timeslot function execution time, which have different delays depend­
ing if the node is a receiver (4 μs) or a sender. The sender delay also depends on
which type of frame is being send: 11 μs for Beacon frames, 15 μs for Provisioning
frames and 5 μs for Data frames. The payload for the first two is generated right
24
Low Power IoT Mesh Network
==PAGE:34==
there, and that is why they take more time. The Data payload is fetched from the
connection queue later.
• The Data Rsp frame send function execution time, of 5 μs, exactly the same as the
Data frame.
• The end of timeslot function execution time, of 2 μs.
Except for the first two, the rest of them could only be measured experimentally. To that
end, two new macros, GPIO_DEBUG_SET and GPIO_DEBUG_CLR, were introduced in
the code. They set and clear a predefined GPIO pin, respectively. Then, another board
was flashed with a simple firmware developed for this task. This firmware just starts
measuring time when the GPIO state transitions to ’1’ and concludes when it changes
back to ’0’. The measurement was then transmitted via UART to a computer, where a
Python script received and displayed the time on the screen.
5.2.3
HF and LF oscillator
Both external oscillators have a precision of 10 ppm, which is theory is good enough for the
defined reception window width and the maximum timeslot period of 5 seconds in Section
4.3. However, this precision is only ensured at a given temperature, usually around 25­30
ºC. How the error changes with the temperature is a complex topic, but in general, the
further away from that temperature, the greater the error, either increasing or decreasing
the nominal oscillator frequency [11].
As described in Section 5.1, in the development platform the HF oscillator is enclosed in­
side the Laird module, at around a more or less constant 33 ºC, according to the datasheet.
Meanwhile, the LF oscillator is mounted on the board, open to the air. Inside a datacenter,
its temperature is going to vary a lot between devices in the cold aisle and the hot aisle,
affecting the LF oscillator.
Moreover, according to the nRF52840 datasheet, the RTC hardware works at 32.768 KHz,
compared to the 64 MHz CPU. This creates a start delay of 15­46 μs.
The end result is that the RTC and the timer measure time differently. To mitigate this
effect, an RTC calibration method was devised. In this method, the HF crystal used by
the timer peripheral was considered the reliable clock source, as its temperature varies
less. The calibration procedure starts both the RTC and the timer at the same time. Every
three RTC periods, both are measured. This is done by default during 45 RTC periods,
obtaining a total of 15 time samples. Finally, a linear regression is calculated taking as
reference the time measured by the timer. The correction is applied to the scheduled time
every time the RTC is used.
5.3
Other modules
Implementing the design of this protocol from scratch required many more modules and
functionality to be developed. This section describes some of the most relevant ones.
5.3.1
Radio module
The radio module implements the radio behavior. It provides the basic functions to send
and receive frames. The radio automatically powers itself off when a transmission or a
reception are completed. It also calculates the CRC for outgoing frames, and checks it
for received ones.
The radio module is configured and used primarily by the synchronization module, which
receives event from it through an event callback. The following list contains the possible
radio events:
Low Power IoT Mesh Network
25
==PAGE:35==
• RADIO_TX: A frame has been sent.
• RADIO_RX_START: Event indicating that a new frame is being received. This event
is generated after the preamble has been received. It is used by the synchronization
module to calculate the time correction and adjust the clock, as described in 4.3.1.
• RADIO_RX_OK: A frame has been received, and the CRC is correct.
• RADIO_RX_ERROR: A frame has been received, but the CRC is incorrect.
• RADIO_DISABLE: The radio has been disable, either manually or automatically.
This module implements also provide the means to change the transmission power and
the frequency channel. It also includes an RSSI indicator to every received frame.
5.3.2
Scheduler module
Due to its tight timing requirements, most of the protocol is implemented inside interrup­
tion routines, either from the timer/RTC or the radio. However, interruptions should be
fast to execute, to avoid blocking the CPU for too long. The scheduler module allows
to schedule functions anywhere, including from inside interruptions, to be executed later.
The scheduler module then executes them inside the main loop.
This module is specially useful when passing any of the events described in Section 5.3.4
to the application. The events are always generated inside interruptions. However, since
the callback function receiving them is implemented by the application, there is no guar­
antee that its code is short enough to be executed inside them. As an example, in this
prove of concept, events generated in the coordinator are sent through UART, which is
a relatively slow protocol that can take a significant amount of time. Instead of executing
the callback right away, is scheduled to be executed in the main loop.
5.3.3
Connections module and message queues
The connection module controls the scheduled connections.
For each connection, it
keeps track of its timeslot number and channel offset, which node starts it and if the con­
nection involves time synchronization, and in which direction.
Moreover, in TSCH, messages can not be sent right away when the application generates
them. Any message must be stored and it is actually sent in the next timeslot connection
to the target node. The connection module implements individual queues for every con­
nection, which provides this functionality. Each queue can store multiple messages, and
when the associated timeslot is reached, as many messages as possible are fitted in the
data frame.

Conclusion
The main objective of this master’s thesis project was to design and implement a MAC
layer for wireless sensor network. The reason to develop a new one instead of taking
an existing technology was to improve the power consumption as much as possible for a
very specific application: data gathering inside datacenters.
A further analysis of the application at hand has resulted in some well define set of re­
quirements to use as a guide during the development of the project. The final design is
heavily based on the Time­Slotted Channel Hopping channel access method described in
the 802.15.4 standard. However, some modifications an extensions have been included
to tailor it to the defined requirements.
The combination of the chosen physical layer, the BLE LE 1M PHY, and TSCH increases
efficiency substantially. It effectively reduces the transmission time, and therefore the
power consumption, the timeslot duration and the latency. Moreover, a larger number of
channels also provides a better performance in dense networks, where packet collision
becomes an issue.
The modified timeslot structure provides bidirectional communication and a potential higher
data­rate, by introducing two data frames, followed with a final acknowledgement. The
possible drawback of a larger energy consumption per timeslot has been successfully
mitigated by the reverse time synchronization and avoidable last ACK. This two features
have prove to reduce the power usage in experimental tests.
The devised joining procedure results in a fast and time­bounded method for new nodes
to join the network. It also ensures that joining nodes have all the possible information to
decide which node to connect to. The major drawback is that it uses a single, predefined
channel for announcing the network, which is not suited for noisy environments.
Implementing everything from scratch has been a tough task. However, it has been worth
it, since it allows a fine­tuned control over the chip and its peripherals. An aspect to
highlight is the combination of the RTC and timer peripherals for keeping track of time.
It was challenging to implement, but it has proved to highly reduce the overall power
consumption. The negative side is that the implementation is too tied to the specific chip.
It makes it difficult to port it to new devices, as the most complex parts are very hardware
dependant.
The comparison against Nordic’s implementation of Bluetooth Mesh and Contiki’s 802.15.4
TSCH mode roughy shows how efficient the final result is from an energy point of view. It
provides a better performance than Mesh in terms of bidirectional communication, latency
and data­rate thanks to the TSCH design, and a significant less power usage than Contiki
due to a more efficient implementation, focused in a specific hardware.
The final conclusion is that the prove of concept developed during this project meets all
the requirements while also achieving a notably low power consumption, both during each
communication timeslot and in between them. The result looks very promising, for this
specific application, but it still missing some important features to be ready for actual use.
To that end, the following section provides some future lines of work.

7.1
Future work
The following improvements are proposed as possible continuations of the project in the
future:
• Security. This project was a prove of concept focused on reducing the power con­
sumption, and therefore security have been left out. However, for a real application,
security is a very important aspect. The MAC layer should support at least encryp­
tion and data authenticity.
• Network layer. The network layer provides the means by which a node can com­
municate with other nodes outside of its range, an key feature for mesh topologies.
Its implementation is crucial to obtain a full communication protocol. However, it
is also a very challenging feature. Some research is needed in order to make a
decision about how to route and relay messages across the network, using existing
solutions or developing a new one.
• More flexible timeslots. The proposed timeslot structure is great for low­power
bidirectional communication, enough for the scope of this project. However, de­
pending on how the network layer is implemented, new needs may arise. For ex­
ample, it can require an improved data­rate for one­way communication, or the im­
plementation of shared timeslots where various nodes receive the packets.
