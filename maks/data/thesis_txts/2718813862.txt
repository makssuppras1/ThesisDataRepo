Abstract
The Danish hospitals experience a great problem with the nursing shortage. Experts and
nurses point out, among other things, that better work conditions and workloads are a
part of the solution to get the nurses back to work at the hospitals. Roster quality has a
huge impact on the nurses well-being and satisfaction. Nurse Rostering Problem (NRP)
is a well-studied Operation Research problem due to the problem’s complexity. Many
solution approaches have been developed in the past 50 years, where Mixed Integer Pro-
gramming (MIP) models have increased in popularity the recent years. This thesis studies
the development of a MIP model in the Capital Region of Denmark (CRD) as a case study
of the Intensive Therapy Unit (ITU) at Hvidovre Hospital. The thesis seeks to develop
a MIP model design that fits the planning software Optima used in the CRD to increase
the model’s scalability and quality. The developed MIP model is solved by a weighted
sum objective function that maximizes nurses’ wishes and minimizes objectives such as
using over- and undertime and substitutes. This thesis’s main body review, analyze, and
discuss the model’s solution approaches, results, and quality. The conclusion is that the
MIP model is a great solution approach to solving the NRP at ITU. The thesis refers to
continuing to automate the data management process, testing the model on similar and
other hospital wards within the CRD, and improving the focus on the implementation
part of the problem.
Keywords: Nurse Rostering Problem, Mixed Integer Programming, MIP, Weighted sum
Objective Function, Lexicographic Optimization, Implementation

1
Introduction
Danish hospitals have increasing diﬀiculties retaining nurses. We have seen this before,
but never to the extent that we are witnessing now. The problem of the nursing shortage
is increased by the demographics with more elderly people and a declining labor supply,
and because the nurses are really tired and frustrated. The nurses are traditionally loyal
to our public hospital system, but this solidarity is challenged, and nurses’ patience is
exhausted. Around 5,000 nursing positions nationwide are unfilled, and 2,400 nurses left
public hospitals from May 2021 to May 2022. That is almost six percent of all nurses
employed in the regions in one year [2]. The lack of nurses causes problems with rostering,
hospitals must close beds and operating theaters, and many inconveniences for patients
who have their treatments postponed. The total number of nurses in Denmark is 60,000.
This means that the workforce is available, but a way must be found to get them back to
work at the hospitals [3].
In the election campaign for the parliamentary elections in 2022, there were several pro-
posed solutions from the politicians. Most have a short-term perspective with emergency
packages, retention bonuses, support staff who can relieve the nurses, etc. These interven-
tions are probably not enough to solve the problem in the long run. The nurses point to
lasting improvements such as a higher salary, better working conditions, and workloads,
better work-life balance, and recognition of their efforts [4].
A roster has a huge effect on the nurses well being. A high-quality roster can lead to an
evenly balanced workload among nurses and an attempt to satisfy personnel preferences,
creating a more contented and, thus, more effective workforce. But creating a good nurse
roster with eﬀicient utilization of time and effort is a complex scheduling problem. There-
fore, the need for quality software solutions to solve the Nurse Rostering Problem (NRP)
has never been more in demand. Many solution approaches have been developed since the
early 1970s, ranging from operations research techniques to metaheuristics and artificial
intelligence methods [5].
The mathematical programming technique Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) has re-
cently gained significant interest and popularity in operations research. This is due to
the model’s flexibility and the improvements in MIP algorithms used in the commercials
solvers and computational hardware [6]. The motivation to use MIP models for NRP is
their relatively simple structure, resulting in less maintenance and easier implementation
than extended solution approaches such as Metaheuristics. The MIP model’s flexibility
also makes the model more agile to be transformed into several different wards within a
hospital.
1.1
Problem Statement
A new employee scheduling software system, Optima[7], started being implemented in the
Capital Region of Denmark (CRD) from 2021-2022. Naturally, the list of requirements is
long for what a new planning solution should meet. Still, all requirements share the com-
mon purpose of allowing for better digital support in planning and being more transparent
and flexible. Several off-the-shelf employee scheduling software solutions promise to live
up to these requirements. Unfortunately, it is known that the complexity of an organiza-
tion such as a public hospital makes optimal solutions almost impossible. Traditions in
planning, local schemes, and collective agreement rules are some of the issues that impair
the intended impact of a new employee planning solution.

Henrik Foss Jørgensen as, a Ph.D. student at DTU Management, started in December
2021 the Ph.D. project called Manpower Planning In Region Hovedstaden. This Ph.D.

(DTU Management). The purpose of the Ph.D. project was to apply modern operations
research methods to practical manpower planning problems in the Danish healthcare sector
that could support the employee scheduling software system, Optima.
This thesis is based on collocation with Henrik Foss Jørgensen and the Ph.D. project as a
case study of the Intensive Therapy Unit at Hvidovre Hospital (ITU) within the CRD. In
collaboration with the planner at ITU, we decided to scope the project to nurse rostering
due to the other employees working Monday to Friday in a standard day shift, and they
are not too complicated to plan for. The purpose of the thesis is to develop a strong MIP
model that by the model itself can support a better nurse planning process for the planner
at ITU. The aim is to develop a MIP model that can be implemented into Optima’s design
and seek to be scalable to extend to other departments or hospitals in The CRD. However,
the model’s scalability will not be tested within this thesis’s scope.
Henrik resigned from his Ph.D. in August 2022. As Henrik has access to Optima, he shared
data from Optima before he left DTU. This thesis project is therefore solved with data
from Optima. Henrik’s resignation doesn’t impact this thesis’s purpose but regulates the
implementation possibilities in Optima due to the lack of access. We seek to generate a
solution design that can translate into Optima for pointing out the usefulness of our MIP
model automatic roster planning.

Literature Review
Healthcare optimization problems have received significant attention to providing more
appropriate services at a lower cost. Healthcare optimization problems are typically seg-
regated into two types; personnel and resources [8].
Depending on the planning horizon, the personal optimization problem can be categorized
into three further sub-problems staﬀing, rostering, and re-planning. The staﬀing problems
are the long-term decisions that cover the qualifications and size needed in the workforce.
The rostering problems are the mid-term decisions concerning the roster, also called a
schedule. Typically planned for a group of staff within the same department. The re-
planning problem covers the short-term planning with adjustments needed to handle, for
example, staff illness, demand fluctuations, etc. A scheduling plan will typically span from
one week to a few months and cover details of each staff’s daily shift assignments [9]. This
project studies the rostering category for the staff category nurses.
The resource optimization problems cover Operating room scheduling and Patient Admis-
sion Scheduling. The Operating room scheduling problem requires a unique combination
of personnel such as surgeons, nurses, anesthetists, etc., and equipment such as operating
rooms, recovery rooms, intensive care units, etc. Every surgery requires preparation be-
fore and after the surgery. This means that the operating room problem consists of two
parts; the preoperative and the postoperative. The Operating room scheduling problem
is extremely diﬀicult due to the problem constraints, stakeholders’ preferences, resource
limitations, and increased demand for surgery services. The Patient Admission Schedul-
ing problem (PASP) assigns patients to rooms in the hospital within a given time hori-
zon. PASP supports the decision makers on three different levels; long-term (strategic
level), med-term (tactical level), and short-term (operational level). These levels deter-
mine whether the hospital’s resources are ready to accept patients through satisfactory
services [8].
The NRP is the most studied personal optimization problem. The goal of the problem
is to organize a timetable that satisfies the demand of every nurse without conflict [8].
It is a complex and highly constrained optimization problem with various hard and soft
constraints such as legal regulations, personnel policies, nurses’ preferences, and many
other requirements that are hospital specific. NRP constraints will vary from one hospital
to another, and the objectives of the rostering problem will also vary. Due to the significant
variation in NRP models, a wide range of solution approaches has been developed [10].
For the NRP, there are two types of schedules; cyclic and non-cyclic. For cyclic scheduling,
each nurse works in a repeated pattern in consecutive scheduling periods. For non-cyclic
scheduling, a new schedule is generated for every scheduling period.
Due to the low
computational requirements, cyclic scheduling was used/applied for the first time in the
early 1970s. R. Barker [1974][11] presented a simple algorithm there was easy to implement
for a cyclic schedule model for a work week with two days off for each person. Rosenbloom
et al. [1987][12] present a simple cyclic schedule model for hospital nurses implemented
eﬀiciently on a microcomputer. This time the NRP was formulated as an integer problem
and produced a solution. Warner et al. [1997][13] presented one of the first non-cyclic
studies for NRP formulated as a large-scale mixed-integer quadratic programming problem
that requires a mainframe computer. These early scheduling studies only focus on days,
resulting in a simplified problem by excluding shifts, etc. The cyclic scheduling model

does not require much computational effort but has low adaptability regarding changes.
The development of computational possibilities impacted that non-cyclic scheduling began
to be the main application to solve NRP to optimality. Millar et al. [1998][14] studied
the two types of scheduling for NRP using a network programming method with 12 shifts.
Millar et al. showed that the non-cyclic scheduling used up to ten times the computational
effort of the cyclic method but returned significantly better solutions.
Jaumard et al. [1998][15] presented a generalized 0-1 column generation model with a
resource-constrained shortest path auxiliary problem for NRP. This method covered most
of the restrictions needed for a real-world problem. The optimal solution was obtained by
applying branch-and-bound, but this approach makes it hard to find feasible solutions in
practice.
Another solution approach is the multi-objective model, which is more realistic and flexible
in weighting objectives by priority. Berrade et al. [1996][16] formulated the NRP as a
multi-objective combined with tabu search. Berrade et al. solution approach produced
satisfactory results in tests on real-world problems. This study was one of the first to
apply metaheuristics to address different goals and makes an exciting contribution to
further studies.
Since the late 1990s, metaheuristics have increasingly been implemented to obtain bet-
ter solutions to NRP, with a sub-goal of shortening computational effort. Burke et al.
[2001][17] used a memetic algorithm with tabu search on a complex NRP problem in a
Belgian Hospital, which was the current fastest computational time. Burke et al. [2010][18]
implemented a hybrid solution approach with integer programming solving the soft con-
straint as a subset and a decomposed model using variable neighborhood search to solve
the excluded constraints. This decomposed model significantly shortened the computa-
tional time compared to including all initial constraints.
In 2010 the First International Nurse Rostering Competition (INRC) was held to develop
further interest in the general area of rostering and timetabling while providing researchers
with models of the problems faced, which incorporate many real-world constraints [19].
This kind of competition benefits the optimization community by being inspired by the
design of many search techniques, which leads to the comparison and cross-fertilization
of different paradigms. The main aim of the competition is not only to find the most
promising techniques but also to lead to the development of new hybrid techniques [20].
INCR-I from 2010 was about assigning nurses to shifts in a fixed planning horizon subject to
many soft and hard constraints. The competitions had 15 submissions in three categories
(sprint-, medium-, and long-tracks). The research groups produced remarkable results
and found optimal solutions and new best solutions. The sprint track category requires
solutions within 10 seconds, given 30 instances of 10 nurses and a planning horizon of 28
days. The winning team was Velouxis et al. [2012][21], which presented the method that
partitions the original problem into sub-problems. The sub-problems were solved using
Mathematical Programming in a computationally manageable size. The medium-distance
track category requires solutions within a few minutes, given 15 instances of 31 nurses
and a planning horizon of 28 days. The winning team was again Velouxis et al. using the
same method as for the sprint track. The Long distance track category requires solutions
in about 10 hours of run time, given 15 instances of 50 nurses and a planning horizon of
28 days. There were two winning teams in this category, Velouxis et al. [2012][21] and
Burke et al. [2010][22]. Velouxis et al. applied the same method in the sprint and medium
track distance categories. Burke et al. applied a branch and price algorithm with an
ejection chain-based variable depth search. Besides finding the winner, other lessons were

learned from this competition. Here are some highlights; dedication helps, hybridization
and decomposition are effective, modeling power is essential, the local search method is
an intelligent restart mechanism, and hyperheuristics works very well when more time is

The second International Nurse Rostering Competition (INRC-II) was held in 2015. This
time the problem formulation was different from INRC-I. Instead of a fixed planning
horizon subject to many soft and hard constraints, INRC-II proposed a smaller set of
constraint types but introduced a multi-stage formulation of the problem. The researchers
had to deal with a sequence of cases, referring to consecutive weeks of a longer planning
horizon (4 or 8 weeks).
They need to solve a single stage (one week) of the problem
where some information called history is carried over between consecutive weeks, such that
information coming from the previous week has to be taken into account by the solver.
INRC-II is the first problem in rostering and timetabling that involves a certain degree
of uncertainty in the objective function.
The problem formulation has been extended
based on the academic literature to balance the representation of the actual cases and
simplicity. The competition had 15 participants there, all supplied their solutions for the
28 instances. The winning team was Römer et al. [2016][23] reformulated the problem
as a network flow-based Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP), where each nurse
associates with a directed acyclic graph in which arcs correspond to shift and days off
assignments; a flow from the source to the sink is a roster. The second-best team was
Legrain et al.
[2019][24], who modeled a MIP and solved it using column generation.
The best two teams’ solution approaches for this problem resulted in quite a significant
advantage compared to the solvers using metaheuristics, even though previous timetabling
and NRP competitions had been won using metaheuristics. The analysis on this was that
a short length of each stage made using IP-based techniques more practicable, whether
in longer stages metaheuristics might have gained the advantage. The 15 submissions
delivered high-quality solutions, and it will be hard to outperform in the future [20].
Petrovic S. [2016][25] focuses on implementing the personal scheduling problems solution
approaches in practice. Petrovic states that the available software does not use the devel-
oped models and methods. The personnel scheduling research community could enhance
the implementation of research results in practice to decrease the gap between research
and planners and software developers.
For an overview of the operations research done within the healthcare sector, personnel
scheduling and rostering, and NRP, different papers of research reviews are referred to.
Cheang et al. [2003][10] present a brief overview in a bibliographic survey of the recent
models and methodologies available to solve the NRP. Cheang et al. briefly present math-
ematical programming methods, different kinds of constraint programming techniques,
and metaheuristics. Unfortunately, the paper does not attempt to evaluate the methods
critically. Ernst et al. [2004][26] present a widespread overview of the literature for staff
scheduling and rostering for staff in general. Ernst et al. discuss the personnel schedul-
ing problem in detail with an extensive list of terminology and problem characteristics
across the application areas. Ernst et al. consider crew scheduling and rostering as the
best-covered application domain. Ernst et al. point out that mathematical programming
and metaheuristics approaches are the most investigated methods of personnel scheduling,
and especially metaheuristics are promising for real-world problems where optimal solu-
tions cannot be obtained with exact approaches. Burke et al. [2004][5] present a critical
evaluation of the solution approaches to the NRP for about 40 years, spanning operation
research techniques to artificial intelligence methods. Burke et al. highlight that few de-

Nurse Rostering Problem
It is essential to clarify the NRP because it is our main focus. As studied in Chapter 2

complexity, we have visualized the planning process in a model:
Figure 3.2: Visualization of a Nurse Rostering Problem
Figure 3.2 shows the different components that the planner should be able to handle to

influence which shifts the nurse can be allocated to. Some nurses are employed as substi-

wish to have time off.
The Agreement component represents all the collective agreements the planner needs to

3.2
Optima
It is essential to know what Optima is, its purpose, and how it works to create a roster
that complements Optima’s workflow and design.
Optima is the shift planning system developed for communes and regions. It supports
planning in hospitals, nursing homes, and home care.
Optima is based on the tested
British system, Allocate Optima, which is used by more than 800,000 users globally in
public hospitals, municipalities, and institutions. The purpose of Optima is to make it
simple for the shift planner to handle the complexity of cross-cutting planning. Optima
supports decentralized shift planning for every planner in the organization and central
planning for the organization’s management. One unique feature about Optima is that it
is up-to-date with applicable and collective agreements from the public sector and gives
information to the planner if an agreement has been violated. The solution is modular so
that the individual commune or region can tailor it to their needs. Optima has a build
in automatic shift planning function that can help the planner save time. [7] The design
of Optima is visualized in a simplistic model in Figure 3.3. A column represents a day,
and a row represents a nurse in a department. At the beginning of a planning process, all
shifts are placed in a field for unplaced shifts. The planner’s assignment is to drag and
drop every shift to a nurse with mouse clicks until all shifts are placed in the plan.
Figure 3.3: Visualization of an Example of Optima Interface
In late summer 2021, the first part of the roll-out of Optima in the Capital Region started,
replacing the previous shift planning system ”Tjenestetid”. In contrast to ”Tjenestetid”,
Optima has a closer link to finances, employee wishes, and a higher degree of automatic
planning. Optima also has apps that make it easy to involve employees. The apps allow
the employee to see the shift schedule integrated into the employee’s calendar and share
it with the family etc. It is also possible for the employee to know who is on shift at the
same time and see colleagues’ shift schedules. [27]

Intensive Therapy Unit at Hvidovre Hospital
The Intensive Therapy Unit (ITU) is an independent unit at Hvidovre Hospital.
The

hospital. The patient’s condition requires constant monitoring, and nurses and doctors
must have special education with insight into physiological conditions regarding circula-

the hospital’s other departments and dispatches the Mobile Acute Team (MAT) to acutely

ITU has associated various position categories, such as physicians, nurses, porters, So-

3.3.1
Nurse Rostering Problem at ITU
There are different challenging parts of NRP at ITU, and we are reviewing the main ones.
In general, nurses work day, evening, and night fully manning from Monday to Thursday
in a three-shift plan, where every shift is about eight hours long. Friday through Sunday
are scheduled in a two-shift plan, where every shift is about 12 hours. Furthermore, it
is also possible for nurses having eight hours shifts on the weekend. This leads to many
daily shifts requiring a number of nurses allocated. There are restrictions that on each
shift, there must be at least two nurses with the ”MAT” skill and at least one with the
”responsible” skill. It is also crucial that every collective agreement rule, such as 11 hours
of rest between shifts, must be met when making the roster. All collective agreement rules
will be thoroughly described in Section 3.6. ITU is also responsible for educating specially
trained intensive treatment nurses, which leads to nurses under education needing a Mentor

All these requirements make this NRP a complex problem for a planner like Tina. It is
hard to make a legal roster regarding all rules and requirements and a good roster that
accommodates the nurse’s wishes. Tina spends five full-time working days every month

3.3.2
Optima at ITU
Tina uses Optima for the whole planning process. We will clarify some of her experiences
and challenges with Optima from dialogue with Tina. The first thing Tina highlights is
that Optima supports the financial management system, which requires many mouse clicks
from the planner. Employees who should have disbursed their salary must be allocated to
a shift in the system, including nurses on maternity leave, absence, or internship in other
hospitals. Therefore, specific shift types are made for the employees on maternity leave,
absence, or internship, where each of these shift types has no demand. Tina must also
ensure that all day, evening, and night shifts with demand are covered. Besides the many
mouse clicks, it also creates the uncertainty of making wrong entries for each month’s
roster.
ITU was one of the first departments in the CRD to transition to Optima in 2021 as a
pilot project. However, many functionalities have developed in Optima since Tina started
using it. Functionalities include automatic planning, where the software makes a legal, but
not necessarily a good, roster. To make a good roster that meets the employee’s wishes,
Tina does not use automatic planning. Tina does the planning manually from scratch for
each month by dragging and dropping shifts to employees. Tina finds Optima to be slow
and not user-friendly. She also highlights that it is hard to get an overview of the plan,
and it is impossible to check if it reaches the employee’s contracted hours.
Tina wants help to ease her workflow to create a good roster for the nurses in the ITU.
She wants a good and legal roster that is automatic and optimized for the nurses to
fulfill their wishes. It is also essential to minimize the use of substitutes because they
are more expensive than permanent employees. However, due to the employee shortage,
Tina cannot make a roster without using substitutes. In addition, the roster Tina wants
should work as decision support, as there will always be unforeseeable circumstances, such
as employee illness and shift changes, that need manual correcting. The main focus is for
our model to produce desirable rosters of work shifts, and to help Tina, we want those
rosters to be translatable into Optima. Additionally, we wish to make the model scalable
to handle salary shifts such as maternity, absence, or internship shifts.

3.7
The Good Roster
The roster planning varies from ward to ward and hospital to hospital. There can be a
difference in shift categories, required nurses, skills, and local rules. The definition of what
characterizes a good roster can also vary between wards. However, most wards seem to
agree that a minimum requirement for a good roster is that the demand requirements, the
nurses’ contracts, the local rules, and the global collective agreement rules are met.
The quality level of a roster is often measured by the number of nurses’ disadvantages,
where a disadvantage could be the unfair distribution of unpopular shifts, for example,
the night shifts. Usually, the night shifts are the least popular, so a roster where all night
shifts are assigned to a few nurses while the rest are assigned to all the popular shifts is
not a good roster. Another example could be the distribution of weekend shifts equally
between the nurses and ensuring that nurses working on weekends have shifts on both
Saturday and Sunday. In other words, avoid nurses working only a single shift on the
weekends. Preparing a roster that includes shifts on both Saturday and Sunday for the
nurses who have to work on a weekend means that as few nurses are bothered by weekend
work as possible. Finally would, shift wishes distributed equally between the nurses also
increase the quality level of a roster.

4
Mathematical Formulation
We are creating a MIP model to solve the problem described in Chapter 3 Problem Speci-
fication. We are choosing the MIP model due to its great popularity in operation research
and the MIP model’s significant result for NRP achieved in the INRC-II (see Chapter 2).
We have divided the development of the MIP model into a Base model, a MVP model,
and a Fairness model.
The Base model is done first and seeks to include all constraints related to each nurse’s
contract information. The Base model also consists of the demand for each shift, such
that we have enough nurses at work at any given time. Further, the Base model ensures
the general constraints, such as nurses only having one shift at a time and nurses fixed to
shift getting the specific shift.
MVP modeling is developing a model with just enough constraints to solve the minimum
requirement for a good roster but needs feedback for future development. The MVP model
is an extended version of the Base model and covers all necessary constraints for the planner
at ITU to make a feasible roster. It includes local and global collective agreement rules
and ITU-specific constraints.
The Fairness model is an extension of the MVP model, where more nurse’s beneficial
considerations are covered to increase the quality of the roster. It covers a fair distribution
of wishes between the nurses, gives all nurses enough protected days off, and ensures that
nurses either have a whole weekend off or an entire working weekend.
All constraints included in the Base model can be used for nurses in all the wards in
all hospitals in the CRD. Nurses employed in the CRD have the same standard contract
parameters, and every ward has a specific demand for nurses. The MVP model will include
more specific constraints for the ITU, though many are considered transferable to other
wards. The global collective agreement rules in the MVP model and the Fairness model’s
constraints can also be used for all the wards if demanded.
The Base model, the MVP model, and the Fairness model focus on making a roster that
can solve the classical NRP problem for nurses at work (see section 3.1). Therefore we
ignore nurses on maternity and shifts related to salaries such as maternity, internship, and
absence. In section 7.2, we will discuss how the model could extend to handle the salary
shifts to fit better into Optima.
The Base model, the MVP model, and the Fairness model are described separately in the
sections 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3

Fairness to Weekend Work
The quality of the roster increases for nurses if they don’t have any work shifts on the
weekends, and if they have to work, nurses prefer to work the entire weekend. Therefore, a
penalty parameter ρw1 is multiplied with the auxiliary variable w1n,e to punish nurses that

with no work shifts.
Fairness on Night Shifts
It is a more fair roster if the number of night shifts is distributed equally between nurses.
Therefore this model will seek to minimize this gap between the value of the nurse(s)
having the most night shift and the nurse(s) having the lowest number of night shifts.

Improvement with Cliques
For many planning problems, cliques can significantly reduce the number of rows in the
model and make the model faster to set up and pre-solve [30]. We need some theory to
understand how cliques can be applied to a MIP model.
In a simple undirected graph, a clique is a set of vertices where each vertex is adjacent
to all other vertices. This means that a clique is a subgraph of the original graph that is
complete [31].
A maximal clique is a clique that can not be extended to include more vertices, as adding
a new vertex would mean that all vertices in the set would no longer be adjacent to each
other. Figure 5.2 shows a graph where the vertices in blue frames are examples of cliques
in the graph, and the vertices in red frames are maximal cliques. Notice that there are
many more cliques in the graph than indicated in blue, but the red frames show only two
maximal cliques.

6.6
Lexicographic Optimization
Another approach to solving multi-objective MIP models is the lexicografic solution ap-
proach, where we solve for each objective in order of importance. We do this on Instance
1 to compare the results to the weighted sum objective function.
The Fairness model has defined 16 different objectives. The model seeks to maximize
wishes and minimize over- and undertime and substitutes. Furthermore, it seeks to in-
crease fairness by maximizing weekends without work and minimizing weekends with only
one work shift for every nurse. The many objectives are solved by using the lexicographic
approach.
First, we minimize the four unavoidable slack variables; then, we maximize the granted
VETO wishes. Next, we minimize the use of substitutes and the use of over and undertime.
When the objective is to minimize undertime, we find that the solution time increases
drastically, and the solver cannot find an optimal solution within a reasonable time.
Due to the long solution time, we do not find this approach applicable to this MIP model.

Perspectives and Outlooks
This chapter will give a perspective and outlook on the problem this thesis is based on. The
discussion concerns data structure and quality, model quality, model sensitivity, solution
approaches, and project realization.
7.1
Data Structure and Data Quality
The data management process aims to keep data as close to the data structure within
Optima and make the data as simple as possible but complex enough to accommodate
changes in contracts, demand, skills, local regulations, etc. The chaotic amount of files
extracted from Optima forced us to do some transformation and cleaning of the data to
be able to use the data for modeling.
One of the main data considerations was how to structure the demand data, where we
merged rows describing a too-specific demand on the same shift category within the same
day. The simplification of the demand information makes it easier for the model to solve
and maintain the demand. Still, when complexity is removed from the data, the complexity
of the model can increase to maintain the same functionalities. The assumption of the
demand structure was a decision we made from a modeling perspective. We think the
demand structure should be reconsidered due to several circumstances. The first reason
is that it would be advantageous if the demand data could be used directly from Optima
without lots of reshaping and cleaning where several error sources can occur. The second
and main reason is that Tina does not plan demand on a shift category basis but seeks
to cover the demand on a time basis across the different shift categories. We did not
find the best way to transform the demand data to fit Tina’s demand perspective. In the
Benchmark and Fairness model demand comparison (Figure C.10), we saw a gap in the
number of nurses on different shift categories, especially on weekends when 8-hour and 12-
hour shifts occur simultaneously. The bridge-building from the demand data in Optima
to the more floating demand on the time level is challenging for the modeling process.
Still, we believe this challenge must be resolved in future work. The benchmark demand
overview also showed us that the Fairness Model used the D(N) →N shift combination
many times compared to Tina’s planning (Figure C.10). Whether the many two shifts in a
day are unwanted is unknown. If Tina wants less use of the D(N) →N shift combination,
we can handle it by minimizing the use of D(N) in the objective function.
The overall impression of the data used in this thesis model is that the data quality is un-
certain. Data extraction from Optima contains risk due to the lack of certain information
and explanations. We highlight two uncertain occurrences of attributes from the demand
data frame; wishes and fixed. As mentioned in the data selection step within the data
management process, we interpreted a solution to merge the wishes list into the demand
data frame (see Section 3.4). This procedure was our best approach to comparing the
information, but the procedure could be a source of misinterpretations, and wishes could
be placed incorrectly. We point to a further investigation of how the data of nurses’ wishes
are extracted from Optima. We recommend finding the key between demand and wishes
data such that the connection is made automatically so we can avoid a situation of inter-
pretation. The greatest error source in this thesis must be the information about nurses
being fixed to shifts. We experienced nurses fixed to shifts that violated the collective
agreements, all nurses fixed to protected days off on specific weekends, and nurses being
fixed to too many work shifts that exceeded the nurse’s contracted hours. Also, according
52
Nurse Rostering at The Capital Region of Denmark
==PAGE:65==
to Tina, there is a lack of information for nurses that are fixed to shifts permanently, which
leads to an incorrect and unusable roster. We find the many errors within the attribute
fixed regrettable because it makes it hard to verify the quality of the roster truly. It would
be a great improvement for our model to investigate the fixed data directly in Optima, to
point to courses for all these errors, whether the incorrect or missing data is due to the
extraction process.
Henrik’s resignation has an impact on the quality assurance of the model. Henrik had
access to Optima and could extract new data, meaning many questions and reflections
on the data structure and quality could have been answered or solved.
We find the
constructive dialog with Tina a great help in answering several questions. Most of the
decisions in the data management process are based on Tina’s information which results in
specific data cleaning mostly related to ITU. It would have been interesting to develop our
model in collaboration with Henrik and to increase the connection to his Ph.D. project,
Manpower Planning In Region Hovedstaden, which could make our data management
process even more automatic and general so that data for modeling across the CRD were
handled the same way.
7.2
Extend Model with Salary shifts
Salary shifts were handled in the data management process to extend the MIP model with
the salary shifts problem, but due to the thesis scope, we focused on the isolated NRP
problem. It would be a great value for a planner using Optima to allocate all salary shifts
automatically. The isolated salary shift problem is relatively easy to solve because the
salary shifts have no demand. Meaning that the problem can be divided into a problem
for each nurse without crossing constraints. The hard part of extending our MIP model
with salary shift is the increase of model size as they add several more rows while the set
S increases. Maybe the best way to solve the salary shift problem would be to set up a
parallel model to solve the NRP and salary shift. We recommend further investigation
into how the NRP and salary shift problem can be solved in parallel or merged into a
combined MIP model.
7.3
Model Quality
From the theoretical perspective, the Fairness model finds a solution with a tolerance gap
of less than 1% within ten minutes for Instance 1, indicating that we have developed a
MIP model that can find acceptable solutions. It is diﬀicult for the model to find the
optimal solution. By running the model without the tolerance Gap limit of 1%, Gurubi
continues running. We tested the model by making Gurubi run for 72 hours and the gap
was 0.11%. We point to the large solution space as the reason for the diﬀiculties in finding
optimality.
We experienced that improvements in the three largest constraints were valuable for the
model’s setup time, greatly increasing the model’s quality. However, it is up to further
investigation to check whether it is possible to rewrite or improve other constraints that
could reduce the model’s size even more. To further increase the model’s quality, we would
point to the implementation of cliques on the two large constraints covering the global rest
time rules. Hopefully, implementing improved constraints with cliques would decrease the
model’s setup time.
The four unavoidable slack variables are modeled so that nurses can potentially use them,
even though they do not have violation reasons to do it. We recommend rethinking how
the slack variables are modeled so only the nurses with the wrongly entered data can use
them. One approach could be creating new subsets for the nurses with illegal data.

From a scalability perspective, our MIP model has a relatively high quality. We have
grouped the constraints into six groups; general constraints, contract constraints, demand
constraints, ITU-specific constraints, collective agreement rules constraints, and fairness
constraints, which seek to be modeled as generally as possible. We aspire that the general,
contract, and demand constraint groups, which constitute the Base model, can be used for
any hospital ward at the CRD, insinuating the Base model is high-quality-scalable. The
ITU-specific constraints are modeled on forms that can easily be transformed to fit other
hospital wards’ requirements. The global parts of the collective agreement rules might be
general for all hospital wards in the CRD; therefore, they can be used as they are. The
local collective agreement rules need some editing to fit other hospital wards. All Fairness
constraints are general and can be turned on/off if required. An overall impression of our
MIP model is that it is relatively scalable and transferable with minor corrections and
additions.
7.4
Model Sensitivity
A sensitivity analysis should be made on the final MIP model to ensure the robustness of
the results. In a sensitivity analysis, each reward and penalty parameter would be tested in
a given interval. In contrast, other parameters are fixed to determine which parameters are
the most sensitive to result fluctuations. This analysis result would increase our awareness
of the most sensitive parameters and be included in the weights decision-making.
The way we have chosen the parameter weight for the weighted sum objective function
solution approach is somewhat arbitrary. Or, we have discussed the weight internally based
on information from Tina and the MIP model results that we find acceptable. But still,
we want to challenge our approach to choosing the weights because we experienced great
sensitivity in the models runtime and the roster quality when we changed the weights.
It would be more correct to include the decision maker, in our case, Tina, in choosing the
weights so that the roster fits her priorities. An approach could be using decision-making
techniques to help the decision-maker prioritize the objectives to be sure that the roster fits

7.5
Solution Approaches
One of the reasons a MIP model is a good approach for solving the complex NRP is the
simplicity of the model. MIP model’s modeling process is relatively straightforward as the
complexity is broken down into constraints. It is easier to adjust each constraint to fit
the planner’s needs, and it is easy to add new agreement rules or specifications, making
it simple to maintain.
In our case, the MIP model has a fast solution time due to our MIP model’s quality of the
constraints. It is hard to find arguments for adding extended solution approaches to solve
our problem while the MIP model performs so well. Whether this would be the case for
hospital wards with a larger number of nurses is yet unknown. A hypothesis would be that
the problem would be significantly larger and, therefore, the solution running time would
also be larger. This leads us to discuss how much time would be acceptable as solving
time. Currently, planners use many days to make rosters. Therefore, a MIP model might
still be a good approach even though it takes some hours or even days to solve. Because
of the MIP model’s simplicity isolated, it has a high value compared to other solution
approaches.
If the run time is not acceptable for larger wards, then examining other solution approaches
to the MIP model would be interesting. As we studied in the literature review (Chapter 2)
the MIP models were the great winners at the INRC-II. However, metaheuristics could be
a better approach when solving larger problems. It can be hard to point to the MIP model
limit, where the NRP is big enough for metaheuristics to be preferable. Another argument
for using metaheuristics is that NRP is a natural heuristic, as true optimality cannot be
defined due to the large number of humanistic elements to the problem. However, we have
several reasons why we don’t find metaheuristics as a promising research direction for our
NRP. The great problem complexity, implementing even a local search is challenging be-
cause so many illegal moves exist. The main reason is that maintaining the metaheuristics
is significantly harder compared to MIP models because additions and corrections require
complicated changes in algorithms. The hospital wards’ size in connection with the MIP
model should be investigated further before we can conclude whether MIP models would
be enough to solve the manpower problem for the CRD since we have shown that they
apply to the ITU at Hvidovre.
Furthermore, the usability of MIP models is highly dependent on the solver used. Based
on the solver used, solving time varies a lot. For this project, the commercial solver Gurobi
is used primarily, but the solver setting still impacts the results. Without applying the
NodefileStart parameter to the setting of Gurubi, we cannot solve our MIP model as
we reach the memory limit. Knowing the different settings when choosing a solver and
solving the model is therefore important. For a comparison to Gurobi, we tried out the
open-source solver HiGHS. The result for HiGHS, not surprisingly, was not as quick as
Gurubi, but it found the same solution bounds. A cost-benefit analysis must investigate
whether the open-source solver HiGHS is a better choice for solving the NRP at CRD.
The weighted sum objective function is preferable to the lexicographic solution approach
as the solution time is much faster. As we have seen, the choice of weights when solving
the weighted sum greatly impacts the running time and optimality gap. Likewise, the

7.6
Realisation of the Project
The motivation for this thesis was, among other things, to investigate NRP as a part of the
solution to help avoid the emigration of nurses from danish hospitals. Better automatic
rosters cannot solve this nurse relinquishment crisis alone, but they can lead to a better
sustainable workload for nurses and planners. A planner uses 25% of their time on roster
planning work, whereas, with automatic rosters, the planner could spend more time on
patients and care.
The model developed in this thesis is decision support for a planner. There will be changes
to the roster in all situations due to special agreements during a planning period. Our
model cannot recalculate a new feasible roster due to changes. But if our model is imple-
mented into Optima, a re-planning problem (described in Chapter 2) must be developed
to strengthen the value of the model and time saving for a planner.
Petrovic S. [2016][25] points to the problematic part of implementing NRP, where there
is a lack of cooperation between research, planners, and software developers (described in
Chapter 2). To implement automatic rosters in the CRD, we raise, as researchers, a close
relationship with the planners in the CRD and the software developer Optima. Every ward
has individual needs, which must be accounted for when creating a roster. There is a lot
of work in software implementation if we want to fit the MIP model with Optima’s data
structure and MIP solvers. All this great work is a part of a larger development project
for which money must be raised from the public. Politicians in the Danish Parliament
have to make the necessary decisions to initiate such a project with the right people.

8
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to develop a strong MIP model that could generate nurse rosters that
provide decision support for the planner at the ITU at Hvidovre Hospital. The MIP model
should be developed to fit into the scheduling software Optima’s design and be scalable
to extend to other departments or hospitals within the CRD.
The MIP model was solved by the weighted sum objective function that maximizes nurses’
wishes and minimizes objectives such as using over- and undertime, and substitutes. To
develop the MIP model, great work preparing data extracted from Optima was required
to reach a suﬀicient data structure and quality. The developed output based on the MIP
model’s results was an automatic roster printed in a static excel interface covering the
final rosters describing which nurses are assigned to which shifts.
The MIP model’s size constitutes, to begin with, ∼37 Mio. rows which results in a set-up
time of more than a half hour. We improve the three largest constraints and implement
cliques techniques on one constraint. With these improvements, we reduced the model size
87% to ∼4, 8 Mio. rows. The improved Fairness model gave us a solution with a tolerance
gap of less than 1% within ten minutes and a model setup time of five minutes using the
commercial solver Gurobi 10.0.0. These results were acceptable for decision support to
the NRP at ITU at Hvidovre Hospital.
For comparison reasons, an open-source solver, HiGHS 1.4.0, was tested against Gurobi.
HiGHS was reaching the same solution bounds but within three hours, which was far
slower.
The general quality of the model shows a high quality on almost all quality parameters.
What was remarkable was the unfortunate incorrect number of nurses being fixed to the
wrong shifts due to wrongly entered data in the Fixed-data extracted from Optima.
The main reflections based on the thesis analysis are how to automate the data man-
agement process and choose the weights for the weighted sum. Furthermore, testing the
model on different wards and hospitals and data from non-vacation periods is needed to
improve the model. It was also recommended to increase the focus on implementing the
NRP in the CRD and Optima.
The time the planner at ITU uses making rosters manually is five full working days. Using
the roster generated by the MIP model reduces the time to 15 minutes, including setup
time and solution running time. The MIP solved by the weighted sum objective function
is a great approach to solving the NRP at ITU at Hvidovre Hospital. These improvements
to the planning process can potentially increase the nurse’s and planners’ work conditions
and workloads, decreasing the nurse shortage.

A.1
Preparing Data for Modeling
Most of the attributes in the three data frames can be used directly as vector parameters.
But some attributes need further data processing. This section describes how we handle
these attributes such that we can use them for modeling.
The information about the nurses’ skills is stored in a list of skills described in Table 3.2.
We define two binary parameters telling whether the nurse has the responsible skill or the
MAT skill if the nurse has the skill, then 1 otherwise, 0.
The information about nurses’ absence is stored in a list of absence type, start-, and end
date in the employee data frame described in Table 3.2. We define a parameter that tells
which days a specific nurse is absent. We are doing some data processing to use this data
as a parameter. We split the list of absences into new rows for each absence and split each
absence into three new columns defining absence type, start day, and end day. Then we
clean the data such that if an absence period is outside the planning period, the absence
is removed. If the start- and end-day are on both sides of the planning period, set the
start day as 1 and the end day as 28. If the start day or end day is outside the planning
period set, the start day to day 1, or the end day to day 28. We remove all absence types
that are neither ’Ferie’, ’Kursus/møde’, or ’Andetfravær’. As the data is collected after
ending the planning period, we cannot tell if an absence related to illness is a planned
illness or occurred during the period. We cannot plan for illnesses that occurred during
the planning period. We are storing the cleaned absence data in a binary matrix where
each row represents a nurse, and each column represents a day within the planning period.
If a nurse is absent on a day, it is shown by placing a 1 in the absence matrix, while it is
0 otherwise.
The information about nurses fixed to a shift is stored in a list of optima id in the demand
data frame described in Table 3.5. We are doing some data processing to use this data as
a parameter. The fixed data are stored in a binary matrix where each row represents a
nurse, and each column represents a shift within the planning period. If a nurse is fixed
to a shift, it is shown by placing a 1 in the fixed matrix, while it is 0 otherwise.
Like the information about nurses fixed to a shift, data for nurses wishing for a shift is
stored in a list of optima id in the demand data frame described in Table 3.5. We do
the same for wish data as we did for the fixed data and store the wish data in a binary
matrix. If a nurse wishes a shift, it is shown by placing a 1 in the wish matrix, while it is
0 otherwise.
To keep track of which shifts are placed on which days, a day-shift matrix is constructed.
The rows of the matrix correspond to days, and the columns shifts. If a shift is placed
on a given day, the matrix entrance is set to 1 and 0 otherwise. A ”category shift”-shift
matrix is constructed to keep track of which shifts are placed on which days. The rows
of the matrix correspond to category shifts and the columns shifts. If a shift is a given
category shift, the matrix entrance is set to 1 and 0 otherwise.
To model the global rule G1 11-hour rest rule. We need a parameter that is a binary
matrix comparing all shifts in demand. If there are 11 hours or more rest between the
shifts, then place a 1 in the matrix, while it is 0 otherwise. Likewise, we need a matrix
for G4 that describes a protected day off. If there is a whole day and 11 hours between
