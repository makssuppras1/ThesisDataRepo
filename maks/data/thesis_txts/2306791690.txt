Abstract 
The aim of this project was to investigate the electrochemical impedance response of porous lithium 
manganese nickel oxide (LMNO) cathodes in a three electrode setup followed by the development of an 
equivalent circuit model, to characterize and propose performance limiting mechanisms. 
An oxygen deficient structure of LMNO was synthesized through a solid state synthesis and used to 
produce porous electrodes, containing a network of percolated conductive carbon black. A discharge 
capacity of 114.7 mAh/g with Coulombic and voltage efficiency of 1.00 and 0.94, respectively, was 
determined at 1C. 
Two interfacial processes was detected using potentiostatic electrochemical impedance spectroscopy on 
cycled cells. The high frequency process was ascribed to the charge transfer from the electrode to the 
current collector, based on a stable capacitance observed, consistent with a carbon interface, when 
normalized to the geometric area. The low frequency process and a diffusive tail were only detectable at 
low and high state of charge, at potentials where the LMNO exhibited solid state behavior. The 
electrochemical impedance response of the low frequency process was modelled with a transmission line 
model in which the single particle impedance included a finite spherical diffusion element to describe the 
solid phase diffusion of lithium-ions.  
The charge transfer resistance of the high frequency process was found to strongly depend on the porosity 
of the electrode, decreasing to the point where the capacity dropped due to insufficient electrolyte 
diffusion. Similar behavior was observed for increasing particle size, and expected to be due to improved 
contact at the electrode/current collector interface.  
Cycling and storage revealed that the main cause of capacity loss was the increasing charge transfer 
resistance at the current collector/electrode interface, expected to be a result of electrolyte oxidation at 
high potentials. Storage and cycling both resulted in an increased charge transfer resistance at high 
frequencies, increasing linearly with operated cycles, but stabilizing at low potentials during storage. 
Statistical analysis of experiments performed at similar conditions displayed some deviation on the high 
frequency process, which was believed to originate from different handling during assembly and small 
variations in porosity, while a good reproducibility was found for the low frequency processes.

Resumé 
Formålet med dette projekt var studiet af den elektrokemiske impedans for porøse katoder baseret på en 
litium-mangan-nikkel-oxid spinel struktur (LMNO), i et tre-elektrode setup. Karakterisering af ændringer i 
impedansen blev gennemført med en ”ækvivalent kredsløbs model”, for at identificere de mekanismer der 
begrænser materialets ydeevne. 
Iltfattig LMNO blev syntetiseret via en simpel fast stof metode, og anvendt til at fremstille porøse 
elektroder med et netværk af ledende kulstof (Carbon Black). En afladnings kapacitet på 114.7 mAh/g blev 
målt ved 1C, med en strøm og spændings effektivitet på henholdsvis 1.00 og 0.94. 
To overflade processer var observeret med elektrokemisk impedans ved potentiostatiske målinger af 
cyklede celler. Den højfrekvente proces var tilskrevet overførsel af ladning fra katoden til strømaftageren, 
baseret på den stabile kapacitans, som var tilsvarende forventede værdier for en carbon grænseflade ved 
normalisering med det geometriske areal. Den lavfrekvente proces og en diffusiv hale var kun observeret 
ved lav og høj lade tilstand, hvor LMNO udviste potentiale ændringer tilsvarende en enkelt fase. Den 
lavfrekvente proces blev modelleret efter en transmissions linje model, som tog højde for tredimensionel 
diffusion med reflekterende grænser. 
Det blev vist at modstanden for ladnings overførslen for den højfrekvente proces afhang direkte af 
porøsiteten af elektroden, og faldt til et punkt hvor den interne modstand steg, formodentligt forårsaget af 
begrænset diffusion af elektrolytten igennem elektroden. Tilsvarende effekt blev påvist for en stigende 
partikelstørrelse, hvilket forventes skyldes forbedret kontakt til strømaftageren ved katodens grænseflade. 
Cykling og opbevaring i en ladet tilstand viste at den primære årsag til et tab i kapacitet var forårsaget af en 
stigende modstand for ladningsoverførsel fra elektroden til strømaftageren, formodentligt forårsaget af 
oxidation af elektrolytten ved høje potentialer. En lineært stigende modstand blev observeret ved gentaget 
cykling af batteriet, hvorimod modstanden stabiliserede ved lave potentialer under opbevaring.  
Statistisk analyse af udførte eksperimenter viste en afvigelse for den højfrekvente modstand, hvilket 
forventes at stamme fra små variationer under samlingen af celler, eller små variationer i porøsitet, 
hvorimod meget små variationer blev observeret for den lavfrekvente proces.

1. Introduction - Why do we need lithium-ion batteries? 
The global demand for energy is steadily increasing accompanied by a growing environmental concern 
about the sustainability of our future. Renewable energy sources represent an increasing share of the 
world’s energy market as the technologies and awareness improves [1].  Better wind turbines and cheaper 
photo voltaic cells are making their impact on the market, reflected in an increasing interest, both from a 
political view and private investors. New markets meet the growing demand for energy, especially 
developing countries and their need to access modern technology; Technology that requires thinner, 
lighter, and flexible battery materials with a high energy density. Mobile devices, such as phones, laptops, 
and other consumer goods, show ever growing possibilities with high performance, accompanied by an 
increasing energy demand. Electric Vehicles (EV) have restricted mobility due to limited capacity of the 
available battery technologies, and new materials are a necessity to utilize the growing production of 
sustainable energy [2]. Interest in industrial scale storage facilities are sought to solve the issue of 
sustainable energy peak production, deviating from the peak demand. New applications are coming forth 
as well, such as large batteries targeted private household to store excess energy  [3], or electric cooking 
stoves for developing countries for people living far from the grid  [1].  
There are three different battery types; Primary, which are assembled in a charged state, and can only be 
discharged once; Secondary, which are reversible and can be charged to initial conditions; Tertiary which 
requires continuous refueling. Examples could be Lithium-Air, Li-ion and fuel cells respectively  [4][5].   
Lithium ion batteries (LIBs) are a promising technology with high energy density and design flexibility, 
facilitated by the high electronegativity of lithium [6]. Safety, energy density, power, efficiency, 
temperature range, cost of fabrication and environmental constraints are critical parameters in the search 
for sustainable solutions [2][7].   
The breakthrough for LIBs was in 1978 with the development of insertion electrodes, materials being able 
to reversibly insert and extract Li-ions at various valance states [8][9]. The early batteries used metallic 
lithium for the high capacity, but dendrite formation leading to short circuits, and thermal runaway 
reactions restricted the applications. A graphite anode, capable of intercalating the lithium ions during 
charge avoids the problem of dendrite formation, but lead to formation of resistive surface films. Replacing 
the aqueous electrolyte with an organic liquid carbonate increased the energy density sufficiently for 
mobile applications [7].  In 1991, Sony introduced the “lithium-ion battery”, with a graphite sink anode, and 
lithium cobalt oxide (LCO) as the cathode [2][9]. Due to the early commercialization, LCO is the most 
common battery chemistry in small mobile devices, though other materials are available, as can be seen 
from Figure 1:

From Figure 1, it is apparent the capacity limitation is posed by the positive materials. Many issues face 
new materials; heightened safety requirements, environmentally benign production and high energy 
density, to name a few. Cobalt is a common metal used to stabilize the structure of the insertion electrode 
and provides good conductivity. However, it is toxic and expensive, rendering room for new materials to 
compete.  
The goal set forth by the United States Advanced Battery Council (USABC) from 2002 was a demand for EV 
battery systems with 10 years of calendar-life with 80% capacity retention after 1000 cycles [10]. Modern 
batteries additionally set a requirement for high rate capabilities [2], high energy density and stable 
electrolytes necessary for good cycle life at high potentials [7][10]. 
Many of the new materials come with a promise of low costs, safety, similar capacities and benign 
environmental constraints. However, many suffer from kinetically limited low rates, setting large demands 
on the manufacturing and application. Lithium manganese nickel oxide (LMNO) have high rates, good 
capacity and operate at a nominal value of 4.68 V [11]. The downside is a limited cycle life due to 
degeneration reactions.

3. Lithium Ion Batteries 
A Li-ion battery contains four elements besides its container: a positive and negative electrode, a separator, 
and an electrolyte. The separator prevents short circuit from contact between the positive and negative 
electrode, while being permeable to ionic transport. The electrolyte facilitates ionic transport between the 
electrodes, while acting as an insulator to electrons. The ideal electrolyte has high ionic conductivity, and 
good thermal and chemical stability.  
During galvanic operation of a cell (Discharge), oxidation occurs at the negative electrode (anode), and 
reduction at the positive (cathode) electrode. Electrolytic operation (charging) reverses the reactions and 
thereby changes the positive electrode to the anode and negative to the cathode. The anode and cathode 
annotation is often used instead of the terms negative and positive electrode, defined during galvanic 
operation. 
The reversible potential of the cell, the electromotive driving force (EMF), is defined as the difference 
between the chemical potentials of the electrochemical reaction at each electrode. The net charge of a 
battery is neutral, which requires simultaneous balance of charge. Equation { 1 } and { 2 } are examples of 
half-cell reactions governing the potential at each electrode, for a Li-ion battery during galvanic operation :

Many conditions influence the performance of a Li-ion battery, such as temperature, rate of discharge, 
crystal morphology, chemical, and electrochemical side reactions. In the following section a review of the 
known electrochemistry of LMNO is covered. 
3.2 The electrochemistry of LMNO 
The electrochemistry of the electrodes determines the voltage range of the cell, in which it is 
electrochemically active, however, the changes to the crystal structure of the insertion material often 
changes as a function of Li-concentration, which can affect the shape of the voltage profile [28]. 
Stoichiometric LMNO (𝐿𝑖𝑀𝑛1.5𝑁𝑖0.5𝑂4) is a well-ordered cubic spinel structure (𝑃4432 𝑜𝑟 𝑃4132) [29][30] 
with a lattice parameter of 𝑎= 8.167 Å, in which nickel is located on the octahedral 4(b) sites, manganese 
on the 12(d) sites and lithium ions on the 8(c) sites in a cubic-close packed oxygen array consisting of the 
8(c) and 24(e) sites [31]. 
Most Li-ion batteries undergo significant structural change during a charge/discharge cycle, which is often 
accompanied by a phase change, which can cause degradation of the insertion material [32]. This is the 
main reason commercial LCO batteries have such a low practical capacity compared to the theoretical, as 
they are only discharged to ~50% to avoid the structural change occurring at further discharge, see Table 1. 
LMNO however, shows only a small change in lattice parameters when compared to the fully delithiated 
MNO, of around 2.1-3.37% [30][33], which is less than predicted by DFT at 5% [29].  In the ordered 𝑃4332 
structure, all manganese is 𝑀𝑛4+, which is electrochemically inactive above 3 V vs 𝐿𝑖0/+ [31], and the exact

reduction must be balanced, and as LMNO is stoichiometric (Li =1) at 3.5 V, additional Li-ions has to be

different electrochemical paths [33]. Charging below 0.1 V will plate the anode with lithium [43], and 
possibly reduce the electrolyte [44]. It is reasonable to only discharge LMNO to 3.5 V as this will cover the 
electroactive region of 𝑀𝑛3+/4+ and 𝑁𝑖2+/4+ redox couples without signigicant structural change. The 
upper limit is more delicate. In theory, all lithium can be extracted at 4.7 V, however, this is so close to the

extracted. 4.7-5 V are reported to be necessary to extract the final lithium [32], however at high potentials 
oxidation of electrolyte occurs at the cathode [24]. High potential does not have a significant influence on 
the LMNO, however; few available electrolytes are stable in this region.  How fast LMNO can be 
charged/discharged is governed by the rates of the different processes occurring in the cell during 
operation, which are dependent on a several parameters, especially temperature and particle size.

3.3 The processes of a LMNO cell in operation 
Conductivity of charged particles is a key phenomenon in electrochemical systems [20]. Getting lithium 
from the negative electrode inserted into the positive involves the conductivity through several elements. 
Figure 5 illustrates the transport pathways of ions and electrons at the positive electrode:

Metallic lithium at the negative electrode is oxidized and transported through the electrolyte, stabilized by 
the large negatively charged hexaflourophosphate (PF6
-) ions. The ions have to diffuse through the porous 
electrode and cross the interphase of the active material, which in order to maintain charge neutrality, 
require reduction of the active material, facilitated by electrons. The electrons are conducted mainly 
through the percolated carbon network from the current collector. Once the Li-ion is inside the active 
material, it will have to diffuse further, as the next Li-ion cannot move through it, which requires 
conductivity of electrons inside the active material [46]. 
The different paths have different conductivities, and it is key to optimize all in order to obtain the full 
capacity of the material [47]. An electronically conducting network is often employed in Li-ion batteries, as 
materials like graphite or carbon black have high electronic conductivity compared to LMNO. Getting 
electrons into the negative electrode, whether it is metallic lithium or lithium intercalated in graphite is 
negligible compared to the conductivity of the active material, though it is usually ionic diffusion that is the 
limiting factor [20]. 
Lithium intercalated in graphite is the most common anode in conventional Li-ion batteries due to the 
improved safety by preventing dendrite growth [14]. Electrochemical reactions between the electrolyte 
and carbon at the negative electrode forms a layer called the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI). The SEI layer 
is a complex mix of different species formed mainly from components in degenerated solvent upon contact 
with active material or carbon [20]. The exact composition of the SEI is complex and varies with electrolyte, 
anode material, temperature, and time [16][48]. Alkyl carbonates, esters, and ethers are the most common 
electrolyte liquids as they have a high oxidation potential against metal-oxide cathodes [14].  
SEI film growth can be significant contribution to degradation in the battery. The films formed increases 
internal resistance and limits lithium-ion transportation, which results in capacity fading and reduced 
capabilities [25][49][50]. Film thickness and composition depends on the system, and the exact 
composition can change during operation [48]. Increased temperatures yields a thicker SEI layer, that is not 
diminished when battery operated at lower temperatures [16][48]. Some of the initially formed 
components may not be stable when a current is drawn through the SEI layer, and more resistive species 
are formed [38]. LiF is a common component in the SEI layer when using fluorinated salts in the electrolyte. 
LiF is continually formed, even during storage, and results in an increasing impedance [16][25], though it is 
not necessarily deposited as a film, as it has been observed to form crystallites [48].

benefits.  The formed layer is insulating to electronic current, but conducts Li-ions and inhibit the insertion

this, it is usually mixed with other carbonates, typically diethyl or dimethyl carbonbate (DEC, DMC) to 
reduce viscosity [14]. 
 The conductivity of the SEI layer is very complex as continuous phase changes (Li in graphite) and changes 
in the SEI layer occurs as a function of lithiation. The SEI layer is sometimes referred to as “junk”, as its 
composition is mixed and very difficult to determine in-situ, and examinations ex-situ does not necessarily 
reflect the composition during operation [51].

In addition to transferring ions through EDL, the particles may have to pass through the Cathode Electrolyte

structure degenerates, limiting the access to parts of the active material, or if the surface is covered in a

film of low ionic conductivity. Improving conductivity of electrons and ions in the host materials is primarily 
done through doping, surface treatment, reducing particle size or changing the crystallinity [20].  
Electronic conductivity depends on the material but very different values are reported in the literature,

necessary to have a homogenous mixing and a good contact interface between the additive and current 
collector  [59]. The kinetics of cathode materials without added carbon black degenerate faster than with 
[16], however, carbon black can agglomerate after continuous cycling, reducing the surface contact, 
thereby the available area, in the end affecting the rate performance of the cell [17]. 
3.4 Rate performance 
High rate performance is an electrochemical systems ability to retain high capacity when discharged fast, 
and is directly related to the diffusivity.  High diffusivity yields a better rate performance [20]. Particle size is 
an important parameter when improving rate performance of a battery. By decreasing the particle size, the 
relative surface area is increased and the diffusion path is shortened. This give less interfacial resistance 
which improves the rate capabilities [37]. The trade-off is the increased reactivity, especially if the particles 
are in the nano-size region, which are more prone to form CEI layers that potentially impede diffusion into 
the particles [20], and when using an additive as electrical conductor, it can be difficult to get sufficient 
contact. 
Oxygen deficient LMNO (Fd-3m) is reported to have better capacity retention at high rates, than the 
ordered (P4332), as the ordered structure have lower ionic and electronic conductivity, due to lower energy 
barriers for lithium diffusion in the disordered structure [33]. At low rates, both structures regain pristine 
capacity [23]. 
High potential, good rate capability, decent capacity, materials benign to the environment – Why have 
LMNO not replaced all expensive cobalt containing batteries yet? The issue of degradation of the various 
components in the cell is covered in the following section.

on the degradation reactions in LMNO batteries, but many of the capacity losses are due to reactions found 
in most Li-ion based insertion batteries. An overview of all the known cathode degradation processes is

A reaction that is specific to manganese spinel batteries, such as LMO or LMNO is the dissolution of 
manganese(II) [24]. In LMO a dissociation of the spinel structure can take place: 
2𝐿𝑖𝑀𝑛2𝑂4(𝑠) →3𝜆𝑀𝑛𝑂2(𝑠) + 𝑀𝑛𝑂(𝑠) + 𝐿𝑖2𝑂(𝑠)

inactive components. Nickel prevents this reaction as the manganese is forced to a tetravalent structure 
(𝑀𝑛4+), at stoichiometric conditions. The downside is the reduced electric conductivity [38]. This is 
especially important if no additives are used to improve electrical conductivity [19].  
Lithium is present everywhere in a Li-ion battery, and reacts violently with water and to some degree with

These are chemical reactions, and occur as soon as the different components come into contact. Due to 
this, reactions with electrolyte impurities often occur directly on the surface. Carbondioxide react with 
lithium to form electrochemically irreversible lithiumcarbonate (𝐿𝑖2𝐶𝑂3), while any hydrogen fluoride (HF)

reacts with the lithium salt forming lithium fluoride (LiF) [24]. This is the base of the formation of surface 
layers, the SEI and CEI (Cathode Electrolyte Interphase). 
Most of the SEI layer is formed as the electrolyte is reduced by carbon surfaces at the negative electrode 
[20], however oxidation is also possible due to the high potential LMNO is operated at. One of the most 
stable common electrolytes, EC:DMC is stable up to 4.85 V [60][61], and charging to higher potential will 
lead to a drop in battery capacity [24]. One course of degradation due to oxidation residuals, are 
components with an acidic nature, which as mentioned can form HF, which dissociates the LMNO material, 
leading to manganese dissolution and loss of active material, both insertion material and available lithium 
[24][25]. In high voltage batteries, degradation of electrolyte outside its stable potential range should be 
considered as a major degeneration [43]. 
Formation of an SEI layer is not solely a detrimental reaction, as it can provide countermeasure to other 
forms of degeneration. Especially EC containing electrolyte tends to form a layer that is electronically 
insulating, but conducts lithium, leading to improved cycle ability [31]. This also prevents insertion of 
unintended species, mostly solvated lithium or residual components of the electrolyte, which can occur in 
graphite, often irreversible [10]. Additionally the SEI layer prevent further reduction/oxidation of 
electrolyte in contact with carbon and it prevents the dissolution of manganese [16][41]. Structural 
damage, caused by fast charge/discharge rates can further reduce capacity, as it can expose new surface 
for parasitic reactions with electrolyte, but also yield phases not in contact with the current collector [25]. 
Surface treatment or coatings can alter the formation of the SEI layer, such as acid treatment, which forms 
a layer that is resistant against HF and improves cycle life at elevated temperatures, reducing the usual 
increase in impedance growth with continuous operation [26]. Coating the particles can equally provide 
resistance against HF, such as a zinc oxide (ZnO), which removes HF from the electrolyte, without a 
significant loss in capacity [62]. 
As mentioned, discharging LMNO below 3 V induces a structural change with a volume expansion, which if 
done at high rates can form micro cracks, which may create areas without contact to the current collector 
[10].  
Metallic lithium anodes, tend to form dendrites upon repeated cycling [14]. If the dendrite connects the 
anode to the cathode, the battery short circuit, which can pose safety issues as thermal runaway reactions, 
which in worst case can cause the cell to explode [20].

Solid State 
A simple approach where stoichiometric quantities of Ni and Mn are mixed thoroughly in a ball mill with a 
suitable solvent, like acetone, dried and heat treated at 600-900°C for 3 hours. Adding stoichiometric Li, the 
wet ball milling procedure is repeated  [18]. The dried residue is then annealed at 350-900°. Increasing 
annealing temperatures show crystallite growth. Annealing below 800°C revealed non-spinel phases of 
NiMnO3 and Mn2O3, while Ni6MnO8 and LixNi1-xO2 was observed above, as oxygen and lithium loss distorted 
the spinel structure. High annealing temperatures (>800°C), improved the cycle life and capacity [18].  
Hydrothermal 
Soluble salts of the precursors are dissolved in deionized water and precipitated by adding an acidic 
solution slowly. The pH is adjusted and the particles are dried, heated, and annealed. Hu et al. [63] 
dissolved acetate salts of Li, Ni, and Mn and used a solution of oxalic acid to precipitate. After the heat 
treatment (550°C), the particles were grounded. The annealing was done in two steps, fast heating to 900°C 
directly followed by cooling to 600°C, which was held for 8 hours to improve crystallinity, yielding sub-
micrometer particles [63]. The hydrothermal process can be used to coat the particles, which was shown to 
improve capacity and rate capabilities [63]. 
Sol-Gel 
A gel network is formed through hydrolysis-condensation reactions which can be controlled through a 
composition of the solvent, typically alkoxides [64]. Lee & Kim [35] synthesized LMNO through acetate salts 
in methoxyethanol with ethanolamine at 200°C. The post annealing showed crystallite growth with 
increasing temperature along with a transition from P4332 structure to Fd-3m around 700°C, which shifts 
the average potential towards 4 V. 
(Modified) Pechini Process 
Similar to the sol-gel and precipitation process, the Pechini process occurs at low temperatures. It is based 
on weak acids ability to form polybasic acid chelates which through a polyestrification process can yield 
resin structures with a very even distribution cation[65]. Kunduraci & Amatucci [66] dissolved 
stoichiometric amounts of Li, Ni and Mn nitrate salts in de-ionized water, which was added dropwise to a 
citric acid and ethylene glycol solution at 90°C. After an hour to allow chelation, the temperature was 
increased to 140°C to induce esterification. Afterwards the mass was heated to 180°C for the 
polymerization. The polymer was then burned away in air, and the material heated to 400°C for 4 hours, 
followed by annealing at 500-800°C for 15 hours. The surface area decreased with increasing annealing 
temperature, consistent with crystallite growth, and Fd-3m was formed at high annealing temperatures. 
Precipitation 
Precipitation of a stoichiometric (1:3 Ni-Mn) spinel is formed by dissolving nitrate salts in ammonia during 
continuous stirring. The structure is lithiated with stoichiometric lithium hydroxide, followed by thermal 
treatment in air and annealing. Axmann et al. [23] investigated the effect of cooling rate post annealing, 
and found that annealing at 800°C yielding NiO impurities and the Fd-3m structure, despite being cooled 
slowly or quenched. Annealing at 700°C for 48 hours yielded the P4332 structure.

4.3.7 Physical-Chemical models 
Physical-chemical models are based on the physical and chemical properties of a reaction, like changes in 
concentration, potential, temperature, diffusion different phases, or specific geometry. They serve as an 
extension of the equivalent circuits, and model specific properties that allow extraction of fundamental 
properties. These models are typically derived in steps and finally extended to complete expression for 
impedance for the particle or system [6][8][82].  
A thorough understanding of the phenomena occurring in the system is necessary to create a model that 
can be used to interpret real systems. Park et al. [20] divides resistance into three categories; ionic, 
electrical, and interfacial. The ionic resistance is the transport of ions through the electrolyte  and the solid, 
thus expected to be of different magnitude, similar to the transmission line models [56]. Electrical 
resistance is in the solid phase of the active material, and through the conductive network from the current 
collector [20]. The interfacial resistances is the resistance involved with any interfaces, like 
electrolyte/electrode, active material/conductive network, active material/current collector or from 
additives to the current collector [20].

4.4 Criteria for impedance measurements 
So far it has been assumed the impedance measurements behave as described by the model, however 
impedance is a sensitive technique, and several factors influence the measurements. There are three main 
requirements a system is required to fulfil in order to use impedance measurements [84]: 
1. Causality – Response only related to excitation signal 
2. Linearity  – Only first order terms in the response signal 
3. Stability   – System is unchanged with time 
Causality is the most important of these requirements as it is the foundation of the impedance technique. 
For a closed system where either the current or potential is held, this is usually not a problem, given the 
system is at a relaxed state.  
==PAGE:39==
37 
 
Linearity requires the linear response of a system, e.g. if the overpotential is doubled, twice the current 
density is expected. According to the Butler-Volmer equation ({ 29 }), the current density depends on  
exponential function of the overpotential. At small values of x, the exponential function, 𝑒𝑥 can be 
approximated as 1 + 𝑥 [51]. This is referred to as a “pseudo-linear” area, which achieves close a linear 
response at small excitation amplitudes. Non-linear systems will display harmonics or drift if the excitation 
signal exceeds the pseudo-linear region [70]. 
A stable system does not change with time, nor oscillate after the excitation signal is removed [84]. When a 
cell is left at OCV after a period of current bias, it will relax towards a stable potential. The change in 
potential when left outside a stable region is expected to be due to reorganization of the crystal structure 
to a stable configuration. As CEI/SEI formation can occur without a current being drawn, the system will 
take longer to stabilize. Cycling the battery a few times will accelerate the chemical processes leaving the 
cell at more stable conditions during further operation [85], though the impedance usually increases with 
the formation of the CEI/SEI layers [27][38][58] 
As the formation of CEI/SEI increases the impedance, the system is not stable, due to the increase in 
internal resistance, referred to as system stationarity. This sets a limit for the time a measurement can take 
to keep the changes in the system negligible[68]. This can be a problem at low frequencies, which require a 
rapidly increasing amount of time when approaching zero. If the intent is to measure processes at very low 
frequencies alternative techniques such as step excitation are available and can go to a few µHz [73]. For a 
well-known system, data from a single frequency may be sufficient to obtain specific information [86].

4.4.2 Impedance – Parameters and Pitfalls 
The impedance of a system depends on several different controllable parameters. This section includes a 
list with most and a brief description of the significance and appearance in impedance measurements.  
1. Electrode material – Content, composition and microstructure 
Regarding insertion materials, it is especially the diffusive behavior that can change with 
different materials. LiCoO2 only diffuses in on plane, whereas LNMO has 3-dimensionel 
diffusion, which in theory is significantly faster. An insertion material that has a significant 
structure change is expected to change more at different state of charge. 
Cathodes usually consist of 4 components, a foil coated with a mix of insertion material, 
==PAGE:40==
38 
 
binder and a conductive material, such as carbon black. The higher amount of insertion 
material, the smaller the impedance is expected. This is highly dependent of the 
microstructure. If the insertion material agglomerates in the coat, the impedance may 
increase, and poor rate capabilities are expected. 
 
2. Particle size 
Reducing particle size increases surface area, increasing insertion/desertion which 
improves rate capabilities, but too small particles (nano scale) degenerate faster [37]. The 
imaginary peak frequency decreases with increasing particle size[87]. 
 
3. Porosity  
The impedance response relates a lot to microstructure. At very low porosity, the 
electrolyte diffusion is hindered. At very high porosity, the contact area between insertion 
material, carbon black and the foil is low, and the first impedance arc is large. 
 
4. Electrolyte – Type and amount 
The ideal electrolyte conducts solely the intended ions and is thermally and chemically 
stable within the operated potentials. This is rarely the case, and decomposing electrolyte 
forms the SEI and CEI layers [4][48]. 
 
5. Pressure (during measurement) 
Physical pressure on the cell during operation can improve contact area, which improves 
conductivity [74]. Too much pressure will short circuit the system if the positive and 
negative electrode come into direct contact. 
 
6. Geometric effects 
3-electrode setups are sensitive to electrode arrangement and can cause distortion, 
induction loops and scaling effects [88]. As a rule of thumb, the anode is larger when the 
cathode is investigated. 
 
Stray capacitance is unavoidable in 3-electrode setups. Paired with resistances, stray 
capacitance can appear as inductance, but it depends on magnitude of capacitance and 
resistances as well as frequency [88]. 
 
7. Cell age – Cycles & Storage 
An ideally stable system would not change with cycling. All systems display some 
degradation, due to side reactions or film formation, which will increase the impedance 
[17]. 
 
8. Temperature 
Reducing temperature slows kinetics and increase the impedance [89]. High temperatures 
may also lead to accelerated degradation depending on the thermal stability of the 
different components in the cell. 
 
9. Separator – Type and thickness 
==PAGE:41==
39 
 
The main use of separators in Li-ion batteries is to impede dendrite growth. Tight woven or 
porous polymer based separators are typically thinner than glass fiber, though more 
expensive and can provide more resistance through the electrolyte. 
 
10. State of Charge 
During charge/discharge the structure of the insertion material changes. Structural change 
can results in crucial changes to conductivity, diffusivity and electrochemistry.  
 
11. Noise 
Outside interference is a significant issue which can be greatly reduced by using proper 
cables and a faraday cage. Springs, reference electrode, drift and corrosion can cause 
inductive or negative capacitance behavior [49]. If an AC oven is used as faraday cage, it is 
important that the measured frequencies are different from 50 Hz, which is the frequency 
supplied in Denmark. Using short and proper cables reduce inductance [69].

expected. In the tape test, a piece of tape is stuck to the coat surface and pulled. Judging the tape test is 
difficult, as it depends on the tape, how it is stuck and how it is pulled. It is not a good sign if the coat is 
flaky or comes off in chunks. Both tests are purely guiding in the approximation of the coat’s quality.

the tape test. A drop in capacity is expected, as the electrode might have poor contact area.

To minimize scaling effects in the cell, it is important that the reference is placed with equal distance of the 
anode and cathode. As the lithium anode is 100 µm, a single separator (122µm) is insufficient to get good 
reference placement. A spacer can be used to elevate the lithium anode, but to avoid additional contact 
surfaces, several separators were employed instead. In a commercial cell, this is not viable, as it greatly 
increases the volume of the cell. It was assumed that lithium transport had a negligible decrease through 
the separators compared to the electrolyte, which seemingly wet without problems. This is seen in relation 
to wetting polymer based separators, which have much smaller pores. 
With the separators in place, the dry cathode was inserted. It is crucial that the cathode is placed above the 
anode to minimize geometric effects. It helps somewhat that the anode is slightly larger than the cathode. 
If the cathode is bending, as occurs if it has been pressed, a separator can be placed on either side before it 
is bent in shape.  
50 µL electrolyte (1:1 EC:DMC with 1M LiPF6 from Solvionic [91]) was added per separator. This amount was 
sufficient to have contact between all the electrodes, and consistent with the procedure at Haldor Topsoe 
A/S.  
The current collector piston was added, after assuring the cathode was not bent, by pressing it gently down 
the sleeve. At this point the cell was tested, to see if it had short circuited, with a multimeter. A working cell 
has a potential difference of 3.2-3.6 V, whereas it is 2.8-3 V if the cell has been short circuited. If no 
difference is detected between the WE and RE, the current collector piston was pressed gently, and after a 
few minutes electrolyte wetting was usually sufficient to measure at the RE.

If the lock was added at this point it could short circuit the cell. A PE seal was placed, the large spring and 
the lid. The lid was pushed straight down, and held in place until the cell was secured in the bracket. If the 
lid touches the bracket, the cell short circuited. 
The cell was transported in a nitrile glove when moving it out of the glovebox through the antechamber, as 
contact with the surfaces could short circuit the cell. It is crucial not to apply vacuum to the cell when 
moving it out of the antechamber, as this will breach the PE seal and short circuit the cell. 
 
5.3.1 Cleaning 
This cleaning procedure was adapted from ref. [92]. After a completed series of measurements, or in case 
of short circuited cells, the cell was removed from the bracket in a fumehood void of all easily flammable 
objects. The cell parts were disassembled and Li was quenched in a large beaker (>400 ml) filled with cold 
water.. 
All cell pieces were sonicated 20 minutes in soap water. All residual soap was rinsed of with warm water 
and flushed with demineralized water. All cell parts were then washed three times with ethanol and three 
times with acetone to supplant all water and finally dried at 120°C for at least 3 hours, usually overnight.

at the reference.  Figure 23 shows the cable setup for a three electrode cell on a multichannel potentiostat

6.1.3 Electrochemical behavior 
The capacity of LMNO is reported close to the theoretical maximal value (when only intercalating a single 
lithium ion). It was shown by Wu et al. [33] that it is possible to quadruple the capacity, but at significantly 
lowering the average potential, which decreases another important parameter of a battery, power. The 
power is calculated as the product of current and potential and is a measure of how fast energy can be 
drawn from the cell. High power is desirable in many modern applications to power processors which can 
have fluctuating power consumption depending on the task.  
Formation of CEI layers consume some capacity during initial cycles as can be seen from Figure 25 by the

6.2 Impedance measurements 
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy is a very sensitive technique, capable of distinguishing the 
processes occurring at a different electrochemical rate in a battery. Interpretation of the impedance can 
yield crucial information regarding different phenomena and the changes occurring in the cell as a function 
of many different parameters. A list of the adjustable parameters that influence the impedance of a system 
is given in section 3. 
In this section, Experimental measurements are described with respect to the sections following where 
different electrode parameters have been investigated.  
6.2.1 Cell assembly 
The ideal reference electrode placement is at half the total distance between the anode and cathode. As 
the position of the anode and RE was fixed, adjusting the number of separators was one solution to adjust 
the relative position of the cathode. To determine the number of separators needed for the best 
positioning of the RE, a series of experiments with varying the number of separators was carried out.  In 
order to determine the position of the reference electrode the series resistance was measured between 
the reference electrode and either the cathode or the anode, where it is expected that an equal series 
resistance on either side corresponds to a position of the reference electrode that is equal to half the 
height of the stack of separators and the results are shown in the results shown in Figure 29:

7. Conclusion  
The aim of this project was to investigate the electrochemical impedance response of porous LMNO 
cathodes in a three electrode setup followed by the development of an equivalent circuit model, to 
characterize and propose performance limiting mechanisms. 
LMNO was synthesized through a solid state synthesis and annealed at 900°C for 10 hr. The product was 
92.2% LMNO, with some rock salt impurities. The secondary particle diameter was estimated from SEM 
images around 2 µm. It was determined to be the oxygen deficient, disordered Fd-3m structure, based on 
the observed discharge capacity of the 𝑀𝑛3+/4+ redox couple between 3.8-4.3 V, compared to the 
𝑁𝑖2+/3+/4+ redox couples at 4.4-4.85 V in a cyclic voltammogram. A small, unidentified, partly reversible, 
phase was observed at 4.9-5 V.  
A discharge capacity of 123.7 mAh/g for the oxygen-deficient LMNO was determined at C/10. The 
Coulombic efficiency was 1.00 at 1C, but decreased at low C-rates due to electrolyte oxidation contributing 
to the charge capacity. Electrolyte oxidation was observed to occur from 4 V, but accelerate significantly 
around 4.5 V. 
Potentiostatic electrochemical impedance spectroscopy was utilized to investigate the processes occurring 
at different rates. The electrochemical impedance response of aged LMNO, at low state of charge revealed 
two interfacial processes occurring different rates.  
The high frequency process was ascribed to the charging of the electric double layer, as the effective 
capacitance for the high frequency process was consistent with a carbon interphase when normalized to 
the geometric area. A decrease in porosity by mechanically pressing the electrodes significantly reduced 
the resistance of the high frequency process, which was believed to be due to an improved interfacial 
contact between current collector and the conducting CB network. At low porosities, a rise in electrolyte 
resistance was observed along with significantly reduced capacity; limited electrolyte diffusion through the 
electrode seemed probable. Reducing the CB content to 4wt% with an equivalent amount of PvDF binder 
reduced the resistance of the high frequency process by a factor 5, but further reduction of CB content to 
2wt% was insufficient to produce a homogenous electrode coat, and a sharp drop in capacity was observed 
and believed to be caused by active material without sufficient contact to the current collector.  
The low frequency arc and the diffusive tail were modelled with a transmission line model adapted to finite 
spherical diffusion. The model failed to adequately determine the electronic and ionic resistivity of the 
electrode, which left the remaining parameters of questionable validity. Based on what was observed from 
Nyquist and Bode plots, the interfacial contact did not appear to have a direct influence on the transport 
behavior, though reducing the resistance of the high frequency process, revealed that the processes at low 
state of charge did not overlap.  Reduction of CB content from 8 to 4wt% decreased the transport related 
resistivity considerably, which is expected to be due to reduced ionic resistance through the electrode, 
though it could not be confirmed by the model. 
Electrodes stored in a charged state self-discharge, which is expected to be facilitated by oxidation of 
electrolyte. A initial linear increase in the high frequency charge transfer resistance was observed at self-
discharge from 4.5 to 4.3 V, after which the resistance stabilized, consistent with the observed stability of 
the electrolyte. As the potential decreased a significant change in the impedance response at low 
frequencies was observed, which could be due transformation from a two phase system to a solid solution 
or due to different electrochemical pathways, as the 𝑀𝑛3+/4+ redox couple dominate the LMNO potential 
at low potentials. Between 10 and 95% SOC the LMNO behaves mostly as a two phase system with a stable 
==PAGE:84==
82 
 
potential around 4.7 V, with a small step at approximately 50% SOC, which could be a transition between 
two different two phase systems. At 4.7 V negligible impedance of the mass transfer at low frequency was 
observed, as the rates appeared comparable. 
Due to the increasing resistance of the low frequency charge transfer at low state of charge, complete 
lithiation was not observed at a discharge rate of C/10. Repeated attempted discharge of a cell at low SOC 
changed the impedance response to resemble the pristine cathode, though with a significant increase to 
the high frequency charge transfer resistance. At low frequencies for the repeatedly discharged cell, no 
mass transfer was observed, which is interpreted as nearly complete lithiation. This contributes to a 
reduction of the capacity compared to the theoretical value. Similar behavior was observed at high SOC, 
though the reactions in the cell occurred significantly faster, relaxing or self-discharging to 4.7 V when left 
at OCV. 
Repeated cycling yielded a linear increase in the high frequency resistance between the 20th and 100th 
cycle. The deviation from pristine conditions is interpreted as the initial formation of CEI or polarization. As 
the capacitance was found to be stable at 3 µF/cm2, the main contribution to the increasing impedance at 
high frequency was the charge transfer resistance at current collector-electrode interface. 
Comparison of four sets of impedance data from experiments at similar conditions was used to quantify the 
reproducibility. A deviation of 15.1% was found for the high frequency resistance, which is expected to be 
caused by deviation during assembly or small variations in porosity. The capacitance deviated 29.1%, but 
within 2.4-5.1 µF/cm2, which are values consistent with the capacitance of expected of a carbon interface. 
LMNO is a fast material, with decent capacity which can be synthesized and coated by simple means. The 
main advantage is also the main downside. The high potential of 4.7 V gives good power density, but due to 
electrolyte oxidation, the system is subjected to increased resistance at continuous operation or storage in 
a charged state.

8. Future outlooks 
A relevant addition to this project would be continuous development of the model, with the aim to 
qualitatively assess the conductivity of ions and electrons through the electrode. Nara et al. [89] adds a CPE 
element at the end of the equivalent circuit model to better reflect on the capacitive behavior at complete 
lithiation – that could be an initial attempt, especially for data at low frequencies. Alternatively, other 
means of measuring the impedance could be investigated, as very low frequencies could give a better idea 
of the diffusive properties with less risk of the system drifting. 
Replicating some experiments, with LMNO from another synthesis with improved purity and without the 
unidentified phase at high potentials would be another interesting inquiry, along with the identification of 
the partly reversible reaction observed above 4.8 V. 
Additional experiments with less CB content would be very interesting, as significantly reduced resistances 
for every process in the battery was observed, which may be an indication that the composition of the 
composite electrode could be optimized further. This was observed based on only one experiment, and 
reproducing that result should take priority before a full scale investigation. However, if the resistances 
scale linearly, this could potentially extend the cycle and calendar life significantly. 
A line of experiments with a graphite anode would give a more realistic comparison to commercial cells. A 
reference electrode mesh could be used to give a more realistic idea of the cell, in case of uneven current. 
Investigation on changes to the impedance of the anode would equally be relevant. 
Comparing the three electrode cell to a two electrode cell would be the first step towards development of 
a good state of health test, which could be used to assess the remaining life time of a commercial battery. 
Signs of EC:DMC (1:1) electrolyte decomposition was observed above 4.5 V, which is below the stable 
plateau of 4.68 V, obtained for most of the battery operation. Cycling to a lower potential would likely 
extend the cycle life. A future improvement would be the implementation of an electrolyte with a stability 
window extending to higher potentials.
