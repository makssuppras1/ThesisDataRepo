Abstract
Change Management is an integral part of construction projects. Changes are inevitable
and a fact-of-life at all phases of a project. This master thesis was chosen to focus on
a speciﬁc causing factor, the client-initiated design changes, referring also as Variation
Orders, and the need to constantly monitor and manage them to avoid escalation, cost
overruns, time delays and disputes. If a change cannot be avoided an efﬁcient system
has to be developed in order to alleviate the impacts. The contribution of technology
and particularly Building Information Modeling with the view to streamline the process,
assist decision making, reinforce trust and transparency among the parties and minimize
the risks for the contractor will be investigated.
Real case information was acquired from two large scale construction projects operated
under different project delivery methods. Both projects share the same particularity of in-
troducing BIM along the way and not as part of the initial agreement. Several interviews
were conducted revealing the factors inhibiting the change management process from be-
ing efﬁcient. Challenges that were pointed out were, the efﬁciency on the information and
documents ﬂow, lack of visualization during design change communication, lack of time
to follow step by step the deﬁned processes as the perception that production is more im-
portant is prevalent, inefﬁcient initial planning, the assessment of impacts is mainly based
on the subjective experts’ opinion and manual review of drawings, contractual limitations
and the human nature and its tendency to stick to familiar approaches.
Focusing on the aforementioned identiﬁed sources of complications the contribution of
BIM was discussed. Different ﬁelds of enchantment can be provided by BIM utilization
that span from cost estimation of change, version-based model analysis, linking client
requirements with the model, analyzing the ripple effect of a change to quantifying the
impacts. A process is described based on a BIM-based visualization Model developed by
Moayeri, Moselhi, and Zhu, 2017 with an addition to support the identiﬁed challenge of
linking the construction status in the Design Change Management impact assessment.

1.1
Introduction
¨τὰπάντα ῥεῖκαὶοὐδὲν μένει’ - Heraclitus
"Everything ﬂows and nothing remains still", is one of the most notable sayings, at-
tributed to the Greek philosopher Heraclitus. This phrase highlights that everything in
life changes. It is an idea that ﬁnds its application not only in nature’s evolution but also
in everything that entails people’s involvement.
Architecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry can be a representative ex-
ample, since project changes are inevitable and a fact-of-life at all stages of a construction
project. Nowadays, a signiﬁcant contradiction stems from the fact that although the evo-
lution of technology is rapid, construction industry is still suffering from insufﬁcient com-
munication and misinterpreted or lost information. The industry remains very fragmented
with inadequate connections between information and processes. The U.S Department of
Commerce, Bureau of Labor Statistics, states that all the major industries report an in-
crease in productivity as opposed to construction, that experiences a decrease.
Since construction is a project-based industry and Change Management (CM) is an inte-
gral part of it, the Triple Constrain of the project management model, scope, cost and time
that is illustrated in Figure 1.1, can be easily disrupted by a change. It is commonly ac-
cepted that change management is crucial for the project’s success. The Engineering and
Physical Sciences Research Council in UK (EPSRC), states that " More than a third of
major clients are dissatisﬁed with contractors performance in keeping to the quoted price
and to time, resolving defects, and delivering a ﬁnal product of the required quality." (Q. ;

In a construction project, the reason for a change request varies. Design mistakes, changes
to the scope from the owners side and changes due to unforeseen problems are only some
of the most common reasons. Despite the fact that change requests directly affect not
only the schedule of a project but also the budget, are still considered a big challenge for
the project manager. However, it is a common belief that if a change cannot be avoided,
the only way to alleviate the impacts is to efﬁciently manage it. This is where technology
can contribute the most.
BIM, which stands for Building Information Modeling, is a revolutionized process that
promises enhancement in the whole AEC Industry. BIM is more than a tool, is a process
that represents a new collaborative way of working designed to facilitate all stages of a
building’s life cycle. It can be used as a project management tool, an efﬁcient means
for communication and at the same time as an accurate tool for various types of simula-
tions. The full utilization of BIM guaranties an improvement in terms of productivity and
quality. Its tools may also streamline the process of, minimizing, tracking and manag-
ing changes. Consequently, it can be leveraged to create and deliver an effective change
management process.

1.2
Scope of Thesis
This MSc thesis will explore how construction change management can be optimized. In
terms of optimization an attempt to clarify how an efﬁcient design change management
process should be organized in order to assist decision making. The aim is to make the
process clear and transparent between the client and the contractor and identify existing
challenges; with the view to address them in order to maximize efﬁciency and productiv-
ity, minimize cost overruns for the contractor and time delays. The purpose that technol-
ogy serves on that will be investigated focusing on the potential incorporation of BIM.
The project examines the topic from the contractor’s perspective and ﬁrst an extensive
literature review regarding change management processes and their potential association
with BIM tools will be conducted. After identifying processes and theories that are sug-
gested from a scientiﬁc point of you, real case change management processes will be
examined. For that purpose, data will be acquired for two projects of two different con-
tractors. Both projects are in the last phase of construction but operate through different
type of contracts that directly affect their CM processes. This particularity let for an in-
teresting and multidimensional assessment. One case of every project will be analyzed
in order to evaluate to what extent the theory is put into practise. Evaluating real-case
data, opportunities for improvement will be identiﬁed on the speciﬁc CM processes that
are followed by the two contractors.
1.3
Delimitation
Change management is a broad topic that may be examined from various different per-
spectives. On this particular study the scope will be limited due to time constrains and
data availability. The literature review will cover the background of Construction Change
Management giving a special focus on design changes initiated by the client, referring
also here as Variation Orders (VO) . The two assigned projects will be investigated with
regards to design changes by the client as well. In addition, apart from the limited time to
perform the study, there are conﬁdential restrictions in the provided real-case data. Given
the fact that in a change management process multiple stakeholders are involved, from
the client to various subcontractors, the acquired data and interviews conducted, come
primarily from the contractors perspective and secondarily from the client.
Both cases are part of a large scale projects and it should be taken into consideration that
more challenges and opportunities for improvements may exist that this project do not

regarding Qualitative Research Design. It promotes sufﬁcient and successful functioning
focusing on the components’ close interrelation. Figure 1.2 illustrates the aforementioned
concept. As it is stated by Maxwell (2013), any component of the design may need to
be modiﬁed or redeﬁned during the research process due to unforeseen data or results.
The hypothesis, the research questions, the collection and analysis of data, validity and
threats are developed almost concurrently, inﬂuencing each other. In other words, it is a

(Maxwell, 2013).

1.4.1
Disposition
Figure 1.4 illustrates the Chapter sequence of the project. Chapter 1 establishes the con-
text of the work that will be reported answering questions such as "What is the the purpose
of the project?", and What has been reported? , determining the scope and its relevance
with the nowadays circumstances of the AEC industry. With Chapter 2, the Conceptual
Framework part starts, where general information regarding BIM are provided followed
by a particular focus in BIM and its utilization by contractors. General processes of the
three major phases of a construction project, (Tender, Pre-Construction and Construction)
are presented incorporating BIM technology. Advantages, challenges and opportunities
are identiﬁed considering the contractors’ perspective.
Figure 1.4: Structure of thesis.
Chapter 3 introduces all the aspects of construction change management, deﬁnes what a
change concerns in a construction project and methods to classify them in order to assist

Building Information Modelling
This chapter provides an introduction to Building Information Modelling key theories.
BIM deﬁnition along with its seven dimensional capabilities are described. Further fo-
cus is made in terms of processes in the three main phases of a project, Tender, Pre-
construction and Construction where the position and opportunities of BIM for the con-
tractor are determined. Beneﬁts and challenges are identiﬁed and presented as well.
2.1
Deﬁnition
Currently, AEC industry goes thought a new era with regards building modeling and
managing processes. It shifts from "2D-based documentation and staged delivery pro-
cesses to a digital prototype and collaborative work-ﬂow" (Eastman et al., 2008). A
closer connection of simulation and analysis tools is achieved with the view to provide
adequate and effective monitor, control, coordination and maintenance in construction
projects throughout its life cycle (Shourangiz et al., 2011).
Several deﬁnitions for BIM exists with the prevailing by National Building Information
Model Standard Project (NBIMS) Committee , claiming that "Building Information Mod-
eling is a digital representation of physical and functional characteristics of a facility. A
BIM is a shared knowledge resource for information about a facility forming a reliable
basis for decisions during its life-cycle; deﬁned as existing from earliest conception to
demolition" (NBIMS, 2018). More speciﬁcally, BIM technology, is considered a commu-
nication and collaboration tool and its utilization in construction has signiﬁcant positive
effects, saving time and money.
The promise of BIM is to create a model, simulating the actual structure in detail prior
its construction in reality (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014). Consequently, the people

2.3
Level of Development
The abbreviation LOD was ﬁrst introduced by the American Institute of Architects (AIA)
meaning Level of Detail (Treldal, Vestergaard, and Karlshøj, 2016). After the advent of
BIM, the acronym LOD has changed to Level of Development. The difference between
the two deﬁnitions speciﬁed by the Level of Development Speciﬁcation Guide, is that
the ﬁrst term is used to describe how much detail is included in the model element in
terms of graphical data (BIMForum, 2017). On the other hand the second and prevalent
term, speciﬁes the content requirements (non-graphical) of objects that are included in the
models during the design and construction process (Treldal, Vestergaard, and Karlshøj,
==PAGE:36==
18
CHAPTER 2. BUILDING INFORMATION MODELLING
2016). In other words, it is a common language that enables the project stakeholders
to use BIM models as a tool of communicating object-oriented and property speciﬁc
requirements (Treldal, Vestergaard, and Karlshøj, 2016). According to AIA, there are
six LOD levels that facilitate teams on deﬁning the required information and provide
standards where the contracts can refer to. The LOD levels are developed as follow
(BIMForum, 2017) (AIA, 2013):
• LOD 100: A conceptual approach of the building with a graphical-generic repre-
sentation that is not responding to the actual dimensions. Non-graphic information
may be attached to the elements, but shapes sizes and precise locations are consid-
ered approximate.
• LOD 200: The design is developed including the model element as generic place-
holders. They maybe recognizable as the components that represent but shapes
sizes and precise locations are considered approximate. Non-graphic information
may be attached to the elements.
• LOD 300: The model element are accurately represented in terms of quantity, size,
shape, location and orientation. Non-graphic information may be attached to the
elements.
• LOD 350: The model element are accurately represented in terms of quantity, size,
shape, location and orientation. Interfaces with other building systems is included
such as supports and connections. Non-graphic information may be attached to the
elements.
• LOD 400: The elements are modelled with sufﬁcient details for fabrication, as-
sembly and installation information. Non-graphic information may be attached to
the elements.
• LOD 500: The elements can support operation and maintenance with geometry
and data in an As-built and ﬁeld veriﬁed status.
2.4
Conclusion
Building Information Modelling plays a more and more signiﬁcant role in AEC industry.
It provides physical (geometry, materials, dimensions) and virtual information (logical
relationships, connectivity) that assist practitioners and contractors to perform multiple
activities throughout buildings life-cycle. Cost estimation, scheduling, clash detection
and transparency are some of the key beneﬁts that utilized in the construction processes
after the advent of BIM. However, it is an imperative need for further technological and
mentality progress to be made in order to minimize BIM limitations and fully exploit its
potentials.

Conclusion
Change management is an inevitable part of every project. Given the fact that projects are
unique, every change need to be assessed one by one in terms of impacts with the view
to further assist decision makers. However, the beneﬁts that may derive from changes
should not be neglected from the stakeholders; but it is proved that most of them, if not
carefully managed through a formalized change management process, may even cause
severe time and cost implications, or contract disputes risking project success (Q. Hao
et al., 2008).
Reviewing the available literature the conclusion that is reached is, that ﬁrst the most
common type of change, responsible for time and cost implication, is design change and
especially the ones initiated by the client. Integrated solutions are missing from the in-
dustry while the existing ones offer a partial solution of the issue. It is a multidimensional
task where the right combination of technological/technical solution with management
processes is required.

4.2
Preventing Management
To begin with the building model, as the design starts developing through BIM tools, the
contractor is possible to get involved in the process in an earlier phase than usual without
waiting for the architect or the engineer to ﬁrst ﬁnish the design (Lee, 2008). As a result,
constructability issues can be identiﬁed in the design phase where the changes are still
easier to be applied with less time and cost impacts to the project. The same applies for
the owner as well, having the opportunity to visualize in three dimensional models how
the project looks like, facilitates the process. The owner, is able even to "walk thought"
the building making sure that his desires are aligned to the design and possible changes
or improvements in scope can be implemented on time.
Drawing errors can be minimized with BIM utilization since the efﬁcient coordination
of the drawings is applicable. The multiple stakeholders are working with an aggregate
building model, making sure that they are always updated and there are not inconsis-
tencies between the different model versions. Inaccuracies in speciﬁcations can also be
discerned on time. The information is stored in one model giving the ability to the users
to reuse and exploit the information (Lee, 2008). Finally, another contribution area is
the material substitution. More and more companies tent to rely in a BIM-oriented Con-
struction Supply Chain that brings together different material suppliers with the view to
optimize lead time management (Avsatthi, 2017).

4.3
Managing Change with BIM
When changes cannot be prevented, an efﬁcient management can diminish their impacts.
When the BIM model has also integrated 4D and 5D BIM, tracking the cost and the
time consequences of a changes becomes tangible. Cloud-based platforms have been de-

Literature of design change management processes
with BIM utilization
A research that was conducted regarding BIM and its ﬂexibility in design change, revealed
that BIM performs inadequately against design change impact assessment (Shourangiz et
al., 2011). During the research 170 BIM expert’s opinion was asked and among others it
was also concluded that there is lack of artiﬁcial intelligence in order for BIM tools to an-
alyze/offer alternatives to design changes, inability to export a comparable design change
report and unreliability due to interoperability issues (Shourangiz et al., 2011). Solutions
to some of the identiﬁed problems of this research and the ones identiﬁed in Section 3.4
were given by different researcher. A brief literature review is presented and three ap-
proaches are further investigated and evaluated against the identiﬁed requirements. The
selection of the three approaches is based on the year of publication and on the type of
changes that the system-tool is designed for. The time frame is set to be within the last
two years; taking into consideration the evolution of technology and the fact that older
technological solutions may be obsolete or further developed. As far as the type of change
is concerned, design changes have been decided to be the primary matter of interest.
To begin with the brief description of CM processes with technology incorporation. Isaac
and Navon (2009) developed a proactive CM approach automatizing the identiﬁcation of
possible impacts of a proposed change (cost, time and performance) prior its application.
It is an integrative, adaptive, stochastic, graph-based model that relies on the utilization of
available sources of the project information that are inter-depended. In later publications
of these authors the project information regarding the building’s elements and their depen-
dencies are extracted by BIM and Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) (Isaac and Navon,
2013). However, only the spatial dependencies are taken into consideration. A concep-
tual approach to track design changes is made by Pilehchian, Staub-French, and Nepal
(2015b) developing a BIM-based ontology of design changes. Six classes are composing
the taxonomy of design changes, deﬁning its type, the changed attributes of the altered
component, the dependencies between the components, the severity of impacts on the
other components, the timing, cost and scheduling implications. This is another graph-

4.5
Conclusion
BIM contributes in two ways in CM, ﬁrst in preventive management, identifying the prob-
lematic areas before a high impact change occurs, and second optimizing the managing
process of an existing change. It is of paramount importance to note that a change is pos-

CHAPTER 5
Project Delivery Methods
This chapter describes the delivery methods that are currently used in construction indus-
try. Three of them will be further analyzed based on the frequency of use and their efﬁ-
ciency. In addition the integration of BIM with each one of the analyzed delivery methods
will be examined. It is proven that the contractual agreement under which a construction
project is running is directly pertinent with the change management formation. What are
the beneﬁts and challenges of the different contractual forms and how these affect change
management it is also a matter of interest of this chapter.
5.1
Delivery Methods
A project delivery method is a system under which the owner collaborates with the de-
signer and the builder, based on legal agreements (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014). The
type of contract is one of the ﬁrst decisions that the owner has to make, taking into con-
sideration various aspects. Such aspects are, the type of building, the risk and complexity,
the experience of the possible parties involved in conjunction with the project time and
cost restrictions if any. Furthermore, the answers in the following three questions play a
vital role to determine the type of delivery as well (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014):
– Do the owner want the contractor to be involved in the design phase?
– Can the contractor and the designer be legally contracted under one agreement?
– How do we want to share the risks among the contractor, designer and owner?
Answering the above mentioned questions the owner can choose among others systems a
Design-Bid-Build (DBB) , a Design-Build (DB), an Integrating Project Delivery (IPD), a
Construction Manager at Risk or a Public-Private Partnership. This chapter will further

analyze the ﬁrst three types of delivery methods since they appear to be the most widely
used or promising one.
5.1.1
Design-Bid-Build
Design-Bid-Build is the most traditional type of delivery approach (B. Hardin and D. Mc-
Cool., 2014). The responsibilities are segregated and two separate contracts are managed
by the owner. The one legal agreement is with the Architectural ﬁrm and the other one
with the contractor (ACCM, 2016). DBB can be described as a linear process where the
processes and services are not overlapping. The phases of the delivery process are ﬁrst the
completion of the design by the architect, then the bide time where the contractors make
their offers based on the ﬁnalized detailed design and then the construction phase (Figure
5.2 shows the phases in relation to time). The owner awards the construction contract to
the contractor with the lowest price (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014).
Using that system, the risks of the project are undertaken by the owner who has to handle
and coordinate two contracts. Usually such a delivery method is chosen when the owner
wants to retain the control over design decisions (ACCM, 2016). However, it is proven
that many implications could arise due to the rigid lines and non-integrated processes.
The communication is limited between the designer and the contractor during the design
phase and cost overruns may occur due to constructability issues that has not been taken
into account. Hence, an increased number of RFIs and change orders initiated by both the
owner and the contractor may create delays and friction between the parties (B. Hardin
and D. McCool., 2014). The owner’s risk is associated with all the possible errors, deﬁ-
ciencies in the design or gaps that may arise in the construction phase. Being the mediator
of the two parties and responsible for compensation in case of changes in the design, the
owner’s reason for still choosing this system is the human nature and its tendency to stick
to familiar approaches (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014).
DBB contract system limits BIM’s full utilization and exploitation of its beneﬁts mainly
due to the fact that contractors are not involved in the design phase. MacLeamy Curve
(Figure 5.1) of project effort and impact clearly depicts the cost of change thought projects
life-cycle in relation to DBB and integrated delivery methods. However, BIM can still add
value on DBB delivery method, assisting the contractor with the coordination of different
systems, cost estimation, increasing the clarity and enabling prefabrication (B. Hardin
and D. McCool., 2014).

5.1.2
Design-Build
The Design-Build approach is an integrated delivery method where the owner manages
one contract for both the design and construction. Collaboration is being promoted since
the Architects, Engineers and Contractor are part of the same team. The risk now is
transferred from the owner to the contractor (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014). The tra-
ditional roles and responsibilities are altered and collaboration based on trust takes their
place. The owner sets the rules such as project requirements, budget and time frame and
the contractor with the architects are design within the limits and budget. It is an approach
that gains more and more ground according to the SmartMarket Report byMcGraw Hill
Construction (2014) mainly with a Contractor-Led agreement.
The beneﬁts of this delivery method can be summarized on the following according to B.
Hardin and D. McCool. (2014), worry-free owners, owners that are not playing the role
of a mediator, faster delivery with faster decisions that are reached within the same team,
potential for zero change orders and additional costs and a collaborative way of working
and thinking is encouraged. However, a mentality change is required to fully exploit its
beneﬁts. As far as the involvement of BIM is concerned, DB can boost its potentials,
Having constructable models during the design phase, changes and issues can be solved
early minimizing cost as MacLeamy Curve also suggests.

5.1.3
Integrated Project Delivery
Integrated Project Delivery method shares the same principles with Design-Build deliv-
ery, with a major difference the allocation of risk (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014). In
IPD the risk and awards are shared among the owner, architect and contractor. The pre-
vailing deﬁnition for what IPD is, came from the American Institute of Architects (AIA)
claiming that " IPD is a project delivery approach that integrates people, systems, busi-
ness structures and practices into a process that collaboratively harnesses the talents and
insights of all participants to optimize project results, increase value to the owner, reduce
waste, and maximize efﬁciency through all phases of design, fabrication, and construc-
tion." (AIA, 2007). As opposed to the conventional contractual approaches that suffocate
innovation and perpetuate the egocentric character of construction services the IPD pro-
motes a thinking "outside of the box" (O’Connor Jr., 2009).
Common incentives bring mutual effort for the common good. The success of the archi-
tect in terms of innovation for instance, will reward the owner and the contractor. It is
an approach that aims to minimize and even break away the conﬂict of interests among
the stakeholders (O’Connor Jr., 2009). The conventional contracts focus on deﬁning pro-
cesses and products, roles, responsibilities and the desired outcomes. However, IPD also
describes culture (O’Connor Jr., 2009). Consequently, the right decision of a partner is of
major importance. It worth to note that a strong downside is the lack of acceptance as a
contractual aggreement from the public agency yet (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014).
Although integrated project delivery is a different concept from building information
modeling, AIA deﬁnes the ﬁrst as a process while the latter as a tool. The greatest ben-
eﬁts could be accomplished through their simultaneous use (AIA, 2007). Together are
supporting the collaborative way of working, sharing information, and data, reinforcing
transparency and allowing changes in the models to be identiﬁed during design iterations
(J. Ma, Z. Ma, and Li, 2017). Consequently, their combination is proved to a great asset in
change management as well. It offers zero potentials of changes orders and the contrac-
tor’s criteria upon decision are not only based on cost but on quantitative and qualitative
criteria as well (B. Hardin and D. McCool., 2014).

5.2
Impact of delivery system in change orders
Project owners dislike changes orders due to cost overruns, so as the contractors. How-
ever, there is a prevalent perception that contractors are better to bid at a low price and
claim for high compensation although it is difﬁcult to acquire it (J. Ma, Z. Ma, and Li,
2017). This is an approach fostered by DBB and DB delivery methods as opposed to
IPD where a common incentive mechanism is taking place. A research made by McGraw
Hill Construction (2014) revealed that 51% of the contractors who have worked in an DB
system believe that this is the best delivery method in terms of change orders. Only 10%
voted for DBB, 17% for Construction Manager at Risk and the 17% argues that none
of the the three are appropriate for achieving fewer change orders. Examining the issue
from the owners perspective, 76% of the owners involving in an IPD project, observed a
reduced change order number.
5.3
Conclusion
Project delivery methods have a major share of responsibility for the current problems of
productivity in AEC industry. The Design-Bid-Build method has rigid lines inhospitable
for new innovating ideas, limits communication and collaborative practises that may in-
crease litigation. However, it is still widely used because it is common accepted and com-
prehended by all stakeholders. Design-Build is a more integrated approach giving space
to innovation and design creativity, increases trust among the parties and there is poten-
tial to zero change orders with the early involvement of the contractor in design phase.
Finally, the promising Integrating Project Delivery incentivize innovation, promotes trust
and collaboration including the owner, there is potential for zero change orders while the
risks and rewards are shared among all the parties. Why the industry ﬁnds it difﬁcult
to adopt a more collaborative delivery method is deeply rooted in the fragmented nature
of the parties involved (O’Connor Jr., 2009). The perception that at the end of the day
everyone works for himself is time consuming to be changed and currently the contracts
focus on the remedies and penalties for noncompliance rather that mutual commitments.

8.2
Challenges and BIM contribution
The main inhibitors of a successful change management as were identiﬁed through the
interviews and the available material of each project will be hereafter categorized. The
contribution of BIM in order to alleviate the challenges will be discussed. It is worth
mentioning that during the interviews the participants focused more on the general chal-
lenges that AEC industry addresses and as a consequence affect their change management
processes.
Communication
It is a common phenomenon in Construction industry people to focus more in production
than on processes. As it was stressed on the interviews people do not always communi-
cate the change on the right person or just omit to document all the related decisions and
information. The participants reported that many times when drawings were exchanged
regarding design changes the information was not always clearly stated or highlighted.
Particularly for a Design-Bid-Build contract that the contractor directly receives the ﬁnal
revised drawings, the efﬁcient communication of a change saves a lot of time. In a Design
and Build contract the case is slightly different because the contractor prepares the ﬁnal
design. However, the more explicit is described the better. In terms of organizing the
information, as it has already been mentioned, input from several disciplines is usually
needed for the assessment of the change. The traditional mediums, e-mails or phone calls
increase the possibility of missing who and why took a decision or under what circum-
stances.
The contribution of BIM in communication is already widely accepted. Speciﬁcally for
change management, communication protocols based on collaboration platforms will sig-
niﬁcantly assist the process. Consequently, a more active engagement of collaboration
platforms may result in managing information and documents ﬂow more efﬁcient. The
two projects are using collaboration platforms but for example in Project B the input is
not given directly there and the correspondence is not classiﬁed and archived on the cloud.
In addition, another suggestion would be to use BIM extracts in order to visualize the
change. The discipline managers will receive a change that can directly associate it with
the place in the project and understand to what extend it affects them. Misunderstandings
or wrong interpretation of the change will be eliminated.

Trust and Transparency
Trust and transparency are directly pertinent with the form that the information is ex-
changed. For instance, referring to Project’s B VO it is was concluded that one of the
reasons resulted in a late client’s approval was also the level of transparency of the impact
assessment documents. The cost breakdown analysis was made without presenting unit
rates or quantities but only description of what is affected. This prevents the client from
validating the estimates. The more complex the project the more time consuming to mind
the details of a change. Utilizing BIM for the accurate extraction of quantities it deﬁnitely
reinforces transparency. Generally, communication under BIM philosophy, contributes to
development of trust and transparency.
Time Management
The lack of time was identiﬁed as the main inhibitor of a successful change management.
It was intensely reported that time limitations impeded the process focusing on two as-
pects. First in the Design-Bid-Build contractual agreement (Project A) the fact that the
client requested design changes after the ﬁnal approval of the project design, caused de-
lays and cost overruns. Some of the interviewees supported that any extra time spending
at the initial planning it will be saved at the production. The signiﬁcant number of VO
initiated by the client resulted in 40% to 50% additional cost, a lot of rework and almost a
year of time extension. It is hard to suggest that design should be ﬁrst 100% ﬁnalized prior
the construction phase since the industry is dynamic in its nature and changes is a fact of
life. However, a better planning at the beginning in conjunction with the involvement
of the contractor as earlier as possible will for sure minimize the amount of requested
changes. On a Design and Build contract the case is different, it requires a well coordi-
nated system both internally and externally ensuring that contractor aligns with client’s
expectation.
With regards to the second aspect, as it has already been mentioned, there was not always
enough time in order for the design change to go through all steps as they were deﬁned
on the documents. In both projects commercial agreement was usually regulated in par-
allel with the implementation of the change or under a general settlement at a later stage.
Again this is a way to facilitate production focusing on the big picture and the ﬁnal goal.
However, this practise still conceals risks for the contractor in terms of time and cost in a
sense that the client may reject or modify the design once more after all the necessary im-
pact information is available. BIM incorporation improves the exchange of information
and reinforces transparency. As stated before, a change management system by utiliz-
ing BIM, tends to become quantitative according to Moayeri, Moselhi, and Zhu (2017).

Meaning that design change impacts can be quantiﬁed in less time, affected disciplines
are easier to be identiﬁed and client’s validation can take place faster.
Decision Making
Design changes once they requested require a combination of experts experience and
technological tools in order to evaluate their impacts. In both projects it was expresses
that the initial cost and time assessment is based on experts’ experience or assumptions.
Taking into consideration the limitations that the two projects had with regards to BIM
adoption since the initial contracts didn’t foresee BIM incorporation, the BIM models
were not detailed enough in order to support 4D and 5D BIM. Project A exploit to some
extend BIM capabilities making clash detection and comparing the revised models in the
pre-construction phase but it was pointed out that during construction the models were
not used to track the consequences of design changes. The example of the VO in Project
B conﬁrmed that only the manual review of drawings can be inadequate sometimes. The
raise of installations affected the height of the respective holes on the walls that allows
cable trays to pass through them. The provided and approved by the client cost break-
down estimation didn’t include this cost on the already constructed walls.
BIM technology is developing more and more and now it is feasible to see the ripple
effect of a design change before making a decision. It contributes to a more proactive
approach giving the possibility not only to minimize the inaccurate impact estimates but
also to analyze alternatives. On time identiﬁed limitations can also be sent to the client for
approval before they become clear on site. In the two particular projects even though the
cost and time estimation couldn’t directly extracted from the models a 3D extraction was
feasible. A suggestion for facilitating decision making in the design change management
of the projects would be to utilize in a more active way extractions of the models. One
can easily see what the change is and which discipline is affected with a well developed
visualization tool. In addition, the opportunity of BIM models to be used as testing tools
was recognized by the managers in the interviews but time restrictions was a limiting fac-
tor. However, a small team could be dedicated for modelling and testing design changes
and using the model to identify the downstream impacts of a change.
Mentality
"Process works, people don’t follow the process", was a statement that reported in most of
the interviews. It is an undeniable fact that even if the best process has been deﬁned, hu-
man nature and its tendency to stick to familiar approaches is difﬁcult to be overcome. It

requires individual drive towards change in order to step out of the comfort zone. Even if
the companies establish innovative change management processes the participants should
ﬁrst have the competence and second the willingness to adapt on it.
Training the employees ensuring the mutual understanding towards the goals and beneﬁts
of BIM in change management is the only way to proceed. The education should address
different perspectives, not only the BIM skills and beneﬁts but also develop their com-
mercial perception. The more understanding on the commercial impacts of a change the
more multidimensional will be their approach and behaviour.
Speciﬁcations
Another challenge that was identiﬁed is with regards to the well deﬁned scope and the
reﬂection of the design change impacts against the client’s requirements. The client may
introduce a design change and since the commercial assessment takes place in parallel
with the implementation, misalignment may occur in terms of weather or not a reimburse-
ment should be made. Both the client and the contractor should spend time to determine
if the change is withing the signed scope or according to the speciﬁcations.
Isaac and Navon (2009) stated that there is a need for an integrative model that combines
the currently fragmented information of requirements, design, planning and risks in one
model. At the moment design and requirements are not integrated at all. Achieving to
link these two parameters saves time for both parties that have to look for the information
among several documents. It is essential, that the client requirements are taken into ac-
count in the evaluation of the design changes impacts (Isaac and Navon, 2009). This is a
feasible approach that according to the interviews the right input brings the right output.
The consequences of a change can be tracked and reﬂected upon the initial contractual
speciﬁcations creating a strong change control system.
In order to further reinforce the linkage between client speciﬁcations and the contractors’
provided solutions a requirement traceability matrix can be developed. In conjunction
with the integration of speciﬁcation in the BIM model, the traceability matrix where the
contractual requirements are listed and proof is provided on how each one of them are ad-
dressed is a valuable tool for information management and quality assurance. Therefore,
when a design change is introduced by the client, the contractor can easily look back and
verify that the new design still fulﬁlls the requirements or that a change is not under the
agreed scope of work.

Contractual and Legal
The results of both Project A and Project B afﬁrmed once more that the type of contract is
of great importance in Construction Projects. The strong relationship between the parties
and the creation of common interests is directly pertinent to the contractual agreement
made in the early phase of the projects. The manager of Project A stressed that in the tra-
ditional Design-Bid-Build contract everyone has a rock solid line that cannot cross. This
adversarial contractual set up, makes people loosing time by being defensive rather than
concentrating in the real target. Therefore, Design and Build contracts establish a more
effective level of communication, less prone to changes since the contractor involves ear-
lier in the project and the design. However, construction industry continuous developing
and even the more integrative Design and Build approach can be inefﬁcient since the
client and the contractor are still in opposite sides. Collaboration and industry fragmenta-
tion are still challenges that need to be drastically addressed improving the overall project
performance.
Shifting to an innovative and revolutionary approach as Integrated project Delivery is,
would be a major importance step. IPD promotes a thinking outside of the box where ac-
cording to Fischer et al., 2017, "companies become business partners rather than entities
that cooperate when it serves their interests". The principles of IPD is to integrate people,
systems, business structures and practices into a process where risk and awards are evenly
allocated among the parties. Building Information Modeling can be considered an inte-
gral tool of IPD. How IPD affects Design Change Management? Beneﬁts are associated
to all the abovementioned challenges. The common incentives support the collaborative
way of working, transparent information exchange, trust and decision making. Decisions
are not only based on cost but on qualitative criteria as well. Finally, there is a potential
of zero or at least minimum number of VO.
In terms of legal implications, concerns were expressed in regards to management of
design changes under a BIM oriented Change Control system. When the model or model
extractions are used to document changes, directly become facts in case of legal disputes.
Despite the fact that engineers are familiar with BIM models, lawyers are not. It is an
imperative need to educate accordingly all parties involved against the new technological
developments in AEC industry in order to eliminate confusion on handling the respective
legal cases.

8.3
Suggested Design Change Management Model
Given the analysis and discussion conducted on the two Projects, it can be concluded
that for a successful Design Change Management process with BIM incorporation to be
achieved, a different initial planning with a list of prerequisites that has to be fulﬁlled,
would be recommended. Although the two projects were varying in terms of team struc-
ture, BIM level of implementation, and the type of contract, the common challenges were
identiﬁed. Consequently the suggested DCM model, which is a compilation of informa-
tion acquired by literature review, interviews and individual recommendations, proposes
an approach to tackle some of these challenges in a more uniform way. It is divided in
two categories the Prerequisites and the Process & Implementation. The Prerequisites
part refers to the BIM implementation within a company’s setup which is not the primary
purpose of this project thesis. Therefore, an in-depth analysis will not be conducted.
Prerequisites
In order to develop a DCM process plan taking into consideration the identiﬁed chal-
lenges, companies should establish a BIM Management Team including mid-layer man-
agers who have a better and more holistic overview of the project’s needs and company’s
capabilities combining all different aspects for delivering a successful project. In addi-
tion, team members are expected to have a good knowledge of what the capabilities of
BIM are and being convinced for the value that can bring on to the various processes.
Four are the key contribution aspects:
• Perform a study quantifying the total cost and time overruns due to design changes
in the project. The results will be presented to the company and will aim to convince
senior management for the necessity of developing a new DCM process utilizing
innovative tools.
• Raise awareness on BIM both on a strategic and operational level, attempting to
achieve mutual understanding and alignment towards utilization of the capabilities
of this technology. Training the employees in order to develop their competences
with regards to BIM is essential but also support them to evolve from a cultural
perspective towards a new way of working. According to Autodesk (2012) three to
four months are required for an employee to achieve the same level of productivity
as the one already has with CAD software.
• Highlight necessity for setting up more collaborative agreements with the involved
parties. Meetings with representatives of all parties at an early stage should aim to
align all different stakeholders towards the same goal. The expectations in using

BIM should be contractually established. BIM capabilities span from cost estima-
tion, sequencing simulation, version-based model analysis, to design updated and

• Develop a BIM execution plan. Such plan, according to The Pennsylvania State

Conclusion
The purpose of this chapter is to highlight the most important ﬁndings of this projects. A

9.1
Conclusion
This project has investigated how Design Change Management (DCM) process can be
optimized identifying ﬁrst the challenges that need to be addressed with the view to max-
imize efﬁciency and productivity. A closer look was attempted on Building Information
Modeling (BIM) contribution to design change management focusing on client initiated
changes or else Variation Orders. The artifacts investigated are the change management
processes in two construction projects and the BIM opportunities.
More speciﬁcally, the investigation included two large scale construction projects oper-
ated by two different contractual agreements, Design-Bid-Build and Design-Build that
directly affect their Change Management processes. Based on the available documents
the contractual change management procedures were described and two cases of Varia-
tion Orders, one for each project, were utilized in order to evaluate to what extend the
theory is put in practice. Due to privacy limitations the assessment of Project A couldn’t
be supported to the same extend as for Project B. The analysis was performed based on
qualitative results acquired by interviews, the formal change management procedure and
correspondence associated with the particular Variation Order. It worth mentioning that
both projects share the same particularity of introducing BIM along the way and not since
projects’ initiation.

CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSION
The Variation Orders assessment revealed that the steps of the DCM may alter in practice.
Time limitations were mainly led to either implement the change prior the commercial
agreement or to let them regulated in parallel. This result was conﬁrmed by the inter-
views held while more challenges were identiﬁed. The main inhibitors of a successful
DCM were classiﬁed on the following categories: communication, trust and transparency,
time, decision making, mentality, speciﬁcations, contractual and legal and technology. On
top of that it was noticed that none of the projects had a detailed DCM process described
in the formal procedures.
The capabilities of BIM revealed that there is a lot of room for enhancement. It was con-
cluded that organizing properly the use of collaboration platform with communication
protocols that deﬁnes how and what information should be exchange will ensure track-
ing, monitoring, follow up and archiving all the information and decisions associated with
a Variation. In addition, linking the client speciﬁcations with the BIM models reinforces
traceability. It minimizes the risk and the time required ﬁrst to determine if the change
is within the signed scoped and second to verify that after the change the design still
complies with the requirements. Targeting on decision making and communication, BIM
extractions reinforce visualization and reduce the level of assumptions. Furthermore,
currently software let for model comparisons and analysis of the inter-dependencies of
the changed components, creating reports for visualization purposes and quantifying the
impacts. More and more tools promise a semi-automatic evaluation of design changes
contributing to a more proactive DCM. Subjective experts opinion and experience will
not be the only available method of evaluation, minimizing change-associated risk in
terms of cost and time. Finally, The use of the model to quantify the impacts reinforces
transparency and assisting the client to validate the information on time.
Based on the identiﬁed challenges derived by the analysis of the two projects in con-
junction with the literature review a suggestion for a Design Change Management Model
was made. The proposal considers four aspects regarding BIM implementation in the
companies as prerequisites. First, performing a study quantifying the total cost and time
overruns due to design changes in the projects; second, raising the awareness on BIM;
third, highlighting the necessity for setting up a more collaborative agreement with the
involved parties and last developing a BIM execution plan. The proposal was based on a
model developed by Moayeri, Moselhi, and Zhu (2017) with an additional linkage of con-
struction status on the Design Change Management process. However, further research,
validation and monitoring of the implementation of the model need to be made in order
to quantify its contribution.

9.2
Further Studies
Several intriguing topics were encountered during the execution of the project. However,
not being withing the scope or due to the time limits of the master thesis were not in depth
investigated. Therefore are presented hereafter as suggestions for further research:
• Further investigation of Design Change Management considering the perspective of
clients, contractor and subcontractor in conjunction with a higher number of Varia-
tion Orders for assessment will capture the topic in various aspects and dimensions.
• The capability of BIM to analyze change management alternatives.
• The legal aspects with regards BIM in design changes.
• The possibility to incorporate design, construction and procurement status in the
model elements so as to create a strong change management tool that identiﬁes the
impacts of a design change in real time.
• Evaluation of a Design Change Management process of a project that is initially
organized under the BIM principles and potentially operates under IPD contractual
