Abstract
By leveraging the unique combination of sensitivity and time-resolution oﬀered by a newly de-
veloped type of in-situ electrochemical mass spectrometry (EC-MS), we investigate a transient
peak of high methane production at the onset of a constant potential step on Cu-nanoparticles
samples deposited on glassy carbon supports. The methane production can be divided in two
components: a short lived dynamic contribution at the beginning of the potential step followed
by a steady state production. Once quantiﬁed, the dynamic and steady state methane produc-
tion show diﬀerent potential dependence, suggesting the they follow diﬀerent catalytic pathways.
A similar behaviour is also observed for ethylene.
The dependence of this phenomenon on experimental parameters is studied systematically, and
measurements on polycrystalline copper and on a Cu-sputter deposited chip are carrier out to
gain insight in its causes. A possible explanation in proposed for the dynamic production of
methane with the aid of mechanistic simulations, pointing to the role played by oxygen atoms
decorating undercoordinates surface sites of the copper catalyst.
We also report the observation of a secondary HER peak during cyclic voltammetry measure-
ments, which is explained as hydrogen intercalation in the copper surface.
The results demonstrate the capabilities of the setup and open the possibility of improving cat-
alysts’ performance and tuning their selectivity by tweaking experimental parameters in order
to stabilize metastable reaction pathways.

Foreword
The project
This report is the conclusion of my master program here at DTU. Choosing a master topic
was not easy for me, since I essentially wanted to study all facets of the energy challenge, and
perform the equivalent of several Phd projects. After a long reﬂection, I chose to bet on the
seemingly inﬁnite possibilities oﬀered by electrochemistry and add my energy to the boundless
optimism of two guys that wanted to save the world in six months.
In the past months my mind has been tested, stretched and ﬁlled with knowledge in what has
been one of the most challenging, most motivating and most rewarding experiences of my life,
and I couldn’t be happier with my choice.
I am aware that it might come across as naive, but I strongly believe that reshaping society
to avoid a global-scale catastrophe is the mission of my generation. We not only are conscious
of the looming danger and of the limited time at our disposal, but we are also equipped with
powerful and unprecedented tools, that allow us to come up with solutions that might have been
unthinkable in the past.
Whether we choose to call it global warming, climate change or some other fancy expression,
what we are facing now is so much bigger than virtually any other challenge humanity has
encountered before, and its magnitude is potentially discouraging. However, one simply needs
to open a scientiﬁc journal or even just go online to be amazed by the sheer brilliance of the
people that are ﬁghting the good ﬁght and working to, quite literally, save the world.
CO2 electroreduction is possibly the most important reaction in the world, with the potential
to reinvent the way we store our energy and produce the chemicals that are essential to modern
society. This project, that focuses on a very short peak of very high catalytic activity observed
on Cu nanoparticles, aims at bringing it closer to real world application.

and similar expressions), and to occasionlly use contractions like ”didn’t” and ”can’t”, since
that’s not only the way people talk, but also the style used in the vast majority of written

1.
Background and Introduction
In this chapter I will give an overview of the current challenge we are facing, making a brief
introduction to the global energy scenario and green house gases emissions to highlight the
need for cheap and eﬃcient means of energy storage and the advantages of the elctrochemical
reduction of carbon dioxide (CO2RR).
Then I will perform a review of the current state of the art of CO2RR, to illustrate what
is already known and the remaining challenges, with a focus on nanostructured copper based
catalysts. Finally, I will present a few key electrochemical concepts used in this report.
1.1
Challenging times
If we look back at the 20th century, it is hard not to be impressed by how eventful it was. Two
world wars, the clash of two great ideologies, the space race and the invention of the Internet have
irreversibly changed mankind and the planet. The massive implementation of technology into
society has lifted an unprecedented number of people above the threshold of poverty, allowing
them to enjoy a level of living that would have been accessible only to an extremely restricted
minority just two centuries ago.
However, despite all of these reasons for optimism, humanity as a whole is currently facing one
of its greatest threats yet in global warming, challenging us to rethink our very way of life. Fossil
fuels have been essential for this new prosperity we as a species are enjoying, as they are used
to produce the energy we consume, to fertilize the land that grows our food and are also used in
the production of essential materials such as plastics and steel. All of these sectors, essential to
current society, result in the emissions of CO2 into the atmosphere, as can be seen in ﬁgure 1.1
World population has been increasing at an unprecedented rate in the last 70 years, going from
about 3 billion in the ﬁfties to over 7 billion now, due the the recent development of medicine,
living conditions and farming techniques. Studies show that the rate of growth will slow down,
but it is still expected that world population will exceed 10 billion by the end of the 21st
century.62 This growth in population is largely due to developing countries, while nations in the
so called ﬁrst world have much slower growth rates.
As the living conditions in developing countries improve, so will do their energy consumption
and their emission of green house gases, both as a country and as individuals. Interestingly,
in ﬁrst world countries that were industrialized in the the past centuries, GHG emissions per
capita have remained more or less stable in recent years despite an increase in GDP, because new

go a long way towards winning the energy challenge, but having access to such a plentiful
supply would possibly pose a challenge of its own: how to best channel it to beneﬁt humanity?
Electrochemistry might hold the answer.
The electrochemical production of commonly used chemicals is still in its early stages, but it is
a fast developing ﬁeld. An example is HPnow, a DTU startup, that is developing a technology
to electrochemically produce hydrogen peroxide at viable concentrations for food industry and
disinfection applications [http://hpnow.dk]. In the future, it might be possible to obtain other
substances, even ones as vitally important as ammonia, which is used for the production of
the fertilizers that are feeding the world, in the same way. Again, the fact electrocatalysis can
operate at ambient conditions means that it is ideally suited to meet the output of sustainable

power plants, and it can usher in a reality where the delocalized, low cost production of chemicals
using renewable energy is the norm.
1.1.1
The need for viable CO2 sinks
At the moment, we produce about 11 Gton of CO2 per year,8 and even if all major players
made a concerted eﬀort to abandon fossil resources wherever possible, about 0.8 Gton are from
production of concrete, fertilizers, metals and other sources that would be arduous to quit. In
the Paris agreement, a lot of emphasis is put on the importance of obtaining an overall zero net
emission of anthropogenic GHG, which means we need to develop eﬃcient and cost eﬀecttive
”sinks” that will remove GHG from the atmosphere. For carbon dioxide, there are essentially
two ways to do so: capturing it from air and point sources and then store it underground, or
else turn it into useful products.
There are currently several sites worldwide where CO2 is being sequestered underground, in
salt mines and similar sites, and more are projected to open in the next years. Their combined
capacity, projected around 11 Mton per year, will be a minor fraction of our current Co2 emis-
sions, but studies show that there are enough suitable sites to store several centuries of human
CO2 emissions.9 However, this would hardly be the perfect global scale solution, since there
would always be an inherent risk of leakage and, possibly more importantly, it is not economi-
cally sustainable as no value is produced. With current technology, capturing and storing a ton
of CO2 from a coal power plant ﬂue gas has been estimated to cost around 60 USD,10 which
would result in an increase of the cost of electricity for the end consumer by about 50%, with
repercussions of prices at all levels.
Bio-based solutions, where plants are used to remove CO2 from the air and convert it to fuels
that are then burned to produce energy, and the emissions are then captured and stored under-
ground, cannot be applied on a global scale since they would take up too much farm land.35
An approach that might be viable is using electrocatalysis to turn carbon dioxide into valuable
products such as hydrocarbons and alcohols, so that the closing the anthropogenic CO2 cycle
may become proﬁtable and viable even in developing countries without governmental funding.

1.2
A debate on solar fuels
As I was reading material to gain an overview of the current world energy situation, I asked
associate professor Peter Vesborg if he had any articles to suggest, since I had seen him give an
excellent lecture on a related subject as part of one of my courses. He brought to my attention a
very interesting debate that happened on the Viewpoint section of ACS Energy letters between
Bruce Parkinson from University of Wyoming, Frank E. Osterloh from University of California
and Arnaud Tatin, Julien Bonin and Marc Rober from the Universit´e Paris Diderot, which I’d like
to brieﬂy outline here, and then express my opinion.1315Parkinson starts by acknowledging the
need for energy storage to complement renewable energy sources and the great promise held by
solar fuels, then makes the case for the advantages of water splitting over direct CO2 reduction,
First, he states that if the goal of CO2 reduction is to control global CO2 levels, it would need to
be implemented on a unprecedented scale, given the massive amount of CO2 released annually
in the atmosphere, and that the ﬁnal price of the produced fuels would still need to be cheap
enough to be aﬀordable without massive subsidization, and that the fuels produced would be
burned in engines still limited by the Carnot cycle low eﬃciency and would, upon combustion,
release again CO2 in the atmosphere. Then he argues that using CO2 reduction to mitigate the
emissions of a coal burning plant is not feasible, since the expenses of cleaning up coal burning
gas streams and then disposing of harmful waste products must be taken into account when
evaluating EROEI (energy return on energy investment), and that directly replacing the energy
production of the coal plant with renewables would be simpler and cheaper. He also predicts
that production of more valuable chemicals, such as polymers, will still be based on fossil sources
for the foreseeable future. Then he lists the advantages water splitting over CO2 reduction, such
as the lower number of protons and electrons involved, the high concentration of water and
the lack of the mass transport limitations that aﬀect CO2 reduction, and concludes that the
hydrogen thus produced could in any case be used in the water-gas shift reaction to obtain CO,
Syngas and from there methanol in case liquid fuels from CO2 as feedstock are incentivized.
Osterloh adds that it makes little sense to reinvent our society and base it on the use solar fuels
obtained from CO2 reduction, since they would release the CO2 in the atmosphere and it would
prolong our reliance on CO2 emitting coal plants and cement factories. We would need to close
the CO2 loop by recapturing all emitted CO2, but capturing CO2 from the air or at the exhaust
pipe of automobile is very energy intensive ( 440-600 kJ per mole of CO2, from air).
On the other side of the discussion Tatin, Bonin and Rober16 defend the usefulness of keeping
investigating CO2 and nitrogen based electrofuels. They start by observing that electrochemical
reductions of such elements is in general very attractive, since it can be used both as energy
storage or as feedstock for high value compounds, and then move on to defend CO2 reduction.
8
==PAGE:21==
They observe that hydrogen is as of now also unable to meet the GW-scale energy problem
we are facing, and that there are promising research lines that could solve the current density
limitation of CO2 reduction, and stress how much easier it is to store CO2 and CO2RR product
than to store H2 with high volumetric energy densities. They argue that current electrolysis
stack are unable to meet the required scale form implementation in an industrial reactor for
water shift reactor, and could not then be used in the creation of CO2 based fuels as claimed
by Parkinson. They state that CO2 electrofuel production would realistically not aim to oﬀset
the global CO2 emissions, which would require capture from air and automobiles, but to ease
the switch to renewables by absorbing the CO2 emissions from large concentrated sources such

refute the argument that the production of high value products via fossil carbon sources would
be the preferable way. They conclude by saying that there is no need to choose now, on the

would become feasible in the future, economical factors will determine the winner.
Now, I do not feel qualiﬁed to pick a winner in the debate, though I feel that Parkinson and
Osterloh explain their points more clearly. I too think that a society based on the production of
electrofuels through CO2 reduction is a losing proposition, but I deﬁnitely share the view that
CO2 reduction could be essential for producing carbon based chemicals and as a bridge solution
to ease the switch to renewable, and I get the idea that a large part of the community seems aware
of that, given the focus they put on obtaining multi carbon CO2RR products. Furthermore,
my admittedly unsubstantiated gut feeling is that batteries, by choosing the chemistry best
suited for each job, could end up winning the ”energy storage war” in most applications, and
solar fuels could be limited to applications such as air transport, that require extremely high
energy densities. I am a strong believer in combining solar energy with household scale energy
storage to make most buildings independent, and batteries are in my opinion more likely to
oﬀer a solution simple enough for the everyday user, one that just works. Tesla is very close to
bringing such a solution to market, and several companies are oﬀering competing technologies.67
Redox-ﬂow batteries, while deﬁnitely still in need of research and further development, could
oﬀer a very attractive solution for grid scale storage. Metal-air batteries, if they solve the many
issues that plague them, could potentially be a viable solution for the automotive industries, and
even current lithium based batteries oﬀer attractive range to electrical cars, though they are ill
suited to replace all internal combustion cars due to the long recharging time and limitations to
their scalability caused by relative material scarcity.6 If this were to happen, I would argue that
investigating CO2 reduction would actually be preferable, since it would allow us to produce
essential chemicals such as plastic and ammonia in a sustainable way.

1.3
CO2RR literature review
Finding an economically proﬁtable way to oﬀset the anthropogenic emissions of CO2 in the
atmosphere would be an important step towards mitigating global warming. Several possible
approaches are being considered, among which heterogeneous catalysis to hydrogenate CO2 to
syngas and then methanol on a copper oxide and zinc catalyst, bio-based techniques such as
intensive cultivation of algae, that would capture CO2 from the atmosphere and then be turned
into fuels, and one needs only to open the ”climate engineering” page of wikipedia to ﬁnd several
more radical ideas.17 Our approach is based on electrochemistry, and uses electrical energy, that
can come from renewable sources, to convert CO2 and water to useful products, such as methane
ethylene and other hydrocarbons or alcohols. In an electrochemical setup, the application of
a high enough potential diﬀerence will result in the oxidation of water at the anode, releasing
electrons and protons following 2H2O −−→O2 + 4 H+ + 4 e– that are then used to reduce CO2
into hydrocarbon. Some of the reactions that may happen are:
• CO2 + 8 H+ + 8 e– −−→CH4 + 2 H2O, E0 = 0.169 V vs RHE
• 2 CO2 + 12 H+ + 12 e– −−→C2H4 + 4 H2O, E0 = 0.079 V vs RHE
• CO2 + 2 H+ + 2 e– −−→CO + H2O, E0=-0.103 V vs RHE
• CO2 + 6 H+ + 6 e– −−→CH3OH + 2 H2O, E0=-0.03 V vs RHE
• CO2 + H+ + e– −−→HCOO–, E0=-0.225 V vs RHE
• 2 H+ + 2 e– −−→H2, E0=0 vs RHE
where E0 is their equilibrium potential.
CO2RR to alcohols and hydrocarbons is a potentially very rewarding but extremely challenging
process, since it is a complex reactions with several intermediate steps involving a high number
electron transfers and requiring a high overpotential.
Ideally, we would be able to develop a set of catalysts that, in addition to being cheap and envi-
ronmentally compatible, proved to be highly active, stable and with a high degree of selectivity
for a particular product.
Since the process happens at negative potentials vs RHE, CO2RR is accompanied by HER, so
an ideal material would also be a very poor catalyst for HER.
CO2RR on copper was pioneered by Yoshio Hori, who discovered the process and then performed
several rigorous studies to explore it. In 2008 he also wrote an excellent review delineating the
state of the art.18

acidic pH and base is not an option, since CO2 would react with OH– to form HCO3

• Ag, Au, Zn Pd and Ga prevalently form CO,
• Fe, Pt, Ti and Ni show very little activity for CO2 reduction, and are instead rather active
for HER.

1.3.1
Deactivation of electrocatalytic activity
Several articles2214 report catalyst deactivation over the course of an experiment. Hori18 showed
that the deactivation is due to metal impurities contained in the electrolyte, which are ca-
thodically reduced during CO2 reduction and deposit on the surface of the electrode, altering
its properties. Typically, HER increases after the electrode has been contaminated, and the
production of methane and other CO2RR diminishes strongly. Hori and coworkers, in partic-
ular, performed a thorough study to demonstrate that metal impurities are to blame for this
widely reported loss of electrode activity. They prepared several KHCO3 electrolyte solutions
according to recipes from articles where signiﬁcant deactivation had been reported, and were
able to reproduce the loss activity. They too observed that the activity could be temporar-
ily restored by anodic striping voltammetry and anodic polarization Then, they showed that
preelectrolysis with a Pt black cathode in Ar atmosphere is really eﬀective at removing the
metal impurities from the electrolyte thus preventing deactivation. To further prove the point,
they deliberately reintroduced 0.1 uM of Fe++ in an electrolyte that had been puriﬁed with
prelelctrolysis, and again they observed electrode deactivation18 Another solution to remove
metal impurities is the use of Chelex, or similar products, as described Wuttig.23 There, the
use of solid-supported iminodiace-tate resin was shown to be very eﬀective at preventing elec-
trode deactivation. Dewulf22 and Wasmus14report that the electrode was found to be covered
in carbonaceous substance after running CO2RR for relatively short amounts of time (from 10
to 30 minutes), which diminished the production of methane and ethylene with a corresponding
increase in HER.

Single crystals
Using single crystals is an excellent way to study the catalytic performance of copper, and it
makes it possible to isolate the contribution of the diﬀerent surfaces to the catalytic properties
of copper. Working with single crystals in an electrochemical environment can be extremely
challenging, as they are very sensitive to impurities and their surface can easily be altered,
introducing defects that change their properties. Cu single crystals are usually obtained by
electropolishing in sulfuric acid, instead of the thermal annealing used for Pt and Au ones, and
this results in narrower terraces.30 However, they are still a precious tool, since they act as model
systems, and the results obtained by studying often gives a unique insight into the functioning of
other catalysts such as nanostructured Cu. A study by Frese31 showed that Cu(111) is the most
active for methane production, followed by Cu(110) and Cu(100), and several studies report
that Cu(100) is the most selective for ethylene among low index planes.32 Hori et al.33 also
studied Cu(S)-[n(100) x (111)] and Cu(S)-[n(100) x (110)] electrodes, to determine the eﬀects
of introducing (111) and (110) steps in a (100) surface.
They performed electrolysis at a constant current density of 5mA/cm2 and analyzed the gas
products with gas chromatography and soluble ones with ion chromatography and a gas chromatograph-
mass spectrometer. They conﬁrmed that product distribution in heavily inﬂuenced by the crystal
orientation, with Cu(111) .reaching a CE close to 50% for methane at highly negative potentials,
in comparison to a FE below 10% for ethylene. They also explained the relatively high forma-
tion of CO observed for Cu (111) as due to the fact that fcc Cu (111) has the lowest density of
dangling bonds, and therefore it does not interact strongly with CO. Cu (100) again is shown to
favour ethylene, with a CE of 40.7% vs 19.8% for methane and a C2H4/CH4 ratio of 2.1. The
introduction of a Cu(111) step has a strong eﬀect on the product distributions: we can see that
the C2H4/CH4 ratio reaches 13.6 for a (711) crystal, and the selectivity towards C2+ products
increases from the 58% obtained for (100) to more than 70% for the stepped surfaces. The
introductions of a (110) step had a similar eﬀect, which led to the conclusion that the inclusion
of step atoms in a Cu (100) surface increases the selectivity for ethylene while suppressing the
one for methane.
Several studies focused on the mechanisms behind the diﬀerent selectivity of Cu (100) and Cu
(111). An explanation could be that (111) facilitates the protonation of CO, which has been
identiﬁed as a key step in the formation of methane, while Cu 100 promotes the dimerization of
carbon monoxide, leading to the production of ethylene28.34

1.3.3
Nanostructured copper
One of the most important innovation in the ﬁeld of catalysis in recent years has been the
advent of innovative nanostructured catalysts, whose unique electronic and morphological prop-
erties results in behaviours that are often remarkably diﬀerent from bulk materials. The advent
of new synthesis methods has allowed the creation of novel structures, whose properties are then
studied with better than ever characterization techniques, and the results are then interpreted
with the help of increasingly reﬁned theoretical and computational models. The structure is
then modiﬁed on the basis of the acquired knowledge and the process is repeated. The synergic
use of these three elements has led to the rapid development of ”rationally designed” catalyst
that have shown new and often surprising properties.37
Several factors play a role in determining the activity and selectivity of a nanostructured cata-
lysts, including the presence of grain boundaries, the oxidation state of the surface, its atomic
coordination and the dispersion of the catalyst on the support.. Sen et al38 showed a porous
copper nanofoam with a high selectivity for HCOOH, with a FE reaching 26% at -1.1V vs RHE
and as high 37% at -1.5V, a marked increase from the value obtained for smooth polycrystalline
copper (less than 1% at -1.1 V vs RHE) and a suppression of the production of CO, CH4 and
C2H4. The nanofoam also showed small FE for C2 and C3 product such as ethane and, the
ﬁrst catalyst to do so, propylene. This could be due to its porous structure, that would increase
the residence time of key intermediates allowing for the formation of multi carbon products.
A similar phenomenon could also explain the characteristics of the the porous nanostructured
gold-copper catalyst presented by Jia et al.,39 which showed activity towards alcohols such as
ethanol and methanol with FE that dependent on the composition and nanostructure of the
alloyed ﬁlms.
They demonstrated a FE for alcohols ranging from 11.5% to 28%, a marked
improvement from the one measured for bulk Cu (4.8%) and nanostructured Cu (9.7%).
A powerful way of controlling the properties of a nanostructured catalyst is by the creation
of a metal overlayer on a substrate, This can aﬀect the electronic structure of the surface, and
therefore its catalytic properties, through two mechanisms, known as ligand and strain eﬀects. In
the strain eﬀect, the interatomic distance of the surface is changed due to the lattice mismatch
between the overlayer and the substrate. By choosing a substrate with the correct unit cell
parameters, it is possible to force the metal overlayer to increase its interatomic distance. This
will aﬀect the d band of the catalyst, raising it, making the surface more likely to interact
with adsorbates, which will in turn result in an increase of the adsorption energy of reactive
adsorbates on the surface. An opposite eﬀect can be obtained by choosing a substrate with
a lattice constant slightly smaller than the metal overlayer, in order to slightly compress the
metal overlayer. Ligand eﬀects are given by the electronic interaction between the overlayer
and substrate atoms, which will aﬀect the surface’s electronic structure and again change the
20
==PAGE:33==
adsorption energy of reaction intermediates. This techniques have been used by Reske et al,40
who deposited Cu overlayer with various atomic scale thicknesses on a Pt substrate and tested
their performance for CO2 reductions.
They showed that the thickest of the Cu layers (15
nm) had a bulk like behaviour, with a high selectivity for hydrocarbons. With the decrease of
the layer thickness, hydrocarbon formation decreased, with methane doing so much faster than
ethylene. They attributed their results to a combination of strain and ligand eﬀects, and noticed
that while ligand eﬀects are short range, strain eﬀects can aﬀect multiple layers of Cu.
Varela et al.30 investigated the performance for CO2 electrochemical reduction of copper over-
layers on Pt(111) and Pt(211) surfaces, in order to gain insight on strain eﬀects and study the
role of steps in the process. Given that it has been reported in literature that bimetallic catalyst
can be altered by CO under reaction conditions,41 they also used EC-STEM to monitor in-situ
the surface, to determine its stability and understand how eventual changes would aﬀect the
catalysis. They registered a marked decrease in the selectivity towards hydrocarbons and a cor-
responding increase in the selectivity towards HER. The EC-STM measurements showed that
the presence of CO forced copper atoms to move closer together, thus exposing the underlying
Pt, which was also conﬁrmed by means of AR-XPS (angle resolved XPS). They then concluded
that the surface is best considered as a Pt surface partially covered with polycrystalline copper,
which explains their high activity for HER. This also shows the importance of taking the stabil-
ity of the catalyst into account, and would suggest that the low selectivity towards hydrocarbons
for the thinner ﬁlms of the Reske study could be caused by a similar eﬀect and not be related
to the Cu-CO binding energy.
Oxide-derived copper is another kind of nanostructured catalyst that has received considerable
attention in recent years, due to its remarkable selectivity for multicarbon products. As early as
1989 a study by Koga et al described a copper electrode with a roughness factor of more than
100, obtained by an oxidation treatment in air at 350 C and a reducing tratment in H2. The
catalyst had a FE for ethane of about 1%18 . Years later, Li and Kanan42 showed a somewhat
similar catalyst, synthesized by thermal annealing, with a roughness factor above 400 and with
a FE of about 45% for CO2 conversion at low potentials (-0.3V vs RHE). This was seen as
a remarkable achievement, and sparked a lot of interest in oxide-derived Cu as a new class of
catalysts with high activity at low overpotentials. Koper and colleagues showed that the initial
thickness of the oxide layer is important. Increasing the thickness diminished the selectivity
for ethylene, while increasing the one towards ethane.43 Diminishing the thickness of the oxide
layer also increased the selectivity towards methane.
Verdaguer-Casadeval et al.44 have used temperature programmed desorption (TPD) to show the
presence of a site on oxide-derived copper that is able to bind carbon monoxide more strongly
than what has been reported for the sites on polycrystalline copper. This might stabilize an
21
==PAGE:34==
intermediate on the C2 pathway, or raise the chance of C-C coupling as a result of a higher
coverage of CO at the ground boundaries. Bertheussen et al45 showed that oxide-derived copper
also is able to produce acetaldehyde during CO reduction to ethanol, which later gets reduced
to ethanol and observed that this shows the existence of a pathway to ethanol with acetaldehyde
as an intermediate that is not present on polycrystalline copper. Several hypothesis have been
made to explain the performance of oxide-derived copper. Kanan and Li suggested that it could
be due to the presence of grain boundaries that would stabilize highly active sites. Kim et al46
have proposed that, contrary to what is commonly believed, the oxide-derived catalyst is not
completely reduced during the reaction, and used ex-situ Auger measurement to argue for the
presence of oxide species. Eilert et al.47 used ambient pressure XPS and quasi in situ EELS in
a TEM to reveal the presence of residual oxygen and no copper oxide in OD-Cu, and propose
that the subsurface oxygen alters the catalyst’s electronic structure creating sites that bind CO
strongly, which might explain the properties of oxide-derived copper.

1.3.5
Eﬀects of the electrolyte
The selectivity of a catalyst, and even more so for a nanostructured one, can be deeply inﬂuenced
by the electrolyte used in the electrochemical reaction and by its pH. The selectivity between
HER methane and ethylene can be severely changed by using a diﬀerent electrolyte, a fact
that has been attributed to the kind of ions present in the solution,55 and there are several
studies of how cationic species can inﬂuence the methane to ethylene, as for instance done by
Hori.56 HER was favoured by the presence of Li+, while CO2 reduction was promoted by Na+,
K+ and Cs+ ions, and they reported that larger cations favour ethylene formation more than
methane. They explained this behaviour by the inﬂuence of the cationic species on the outer
Helmholtz plane potential. Due to the diﬃculties of changing the pH when performing CO2
electroreduction experiments, it is a common practice to perform CO reduction instead, since
as discussed above CO is widely accepted as a key intermediate in CO2RR. This has allowed to
highlight the pH dependence of methane formation, which indicates that it happens by through
a coupled proton-electron transfer (CPET).57 Ethylene formation, on the other hand, does not
show a dependence on the pH of the electrolyte solution, and therefore CH4 will be favoured
in acid or neutral electrolyte, while C2H4 will be favoured over CH4 at alkaline pH.18 During
reaction, the pH close to the electrode surface can be signiﬁcantly higher than bulk value, due to
the consumption of protons, and this can have marked eﬀects on the selectivity and can explain
the very high production of multi carbon products, such as in the case of the cited Cu nanofoam
catalyst, This eﬀect is in fact augmented in the case of nanostructured copper catalysts, whose
surface morphology can lead to extremely high local pH concentrations, a phenomenon that
needs to be further investigated in order to develop more better and more active catalysts,
and tune their selectivity. This phenomenon also happens in carbon dioxide electroreduction
studies conducted at neutral pH. Gupta et al estimated that the pH at the electrode-electrolyte

for methane.

1.4.3
Three electrode setup and potential scales
When running a reaction in an electrochemical cell, two electrodes would be suﬃcient to apply
a potential or run a current trough the system but, if we want to be able to precisely follow the
potential at the working electrode (WE), a three electrode setup is preferable. In this solution,
the current runs between the WE and another electrode, known as the counter electrode (CE).
As such, the potential of the CE will vary over the course of the experiment, which is the reason
the potential is instead measured between the WE and the last electrode, known as the reference
electrode (RE). The reference is built to have a stable and well known electrode which remains
constant over the course of the experiment, since no current is run trough it, giving us a reliable
measurement of the potential of the WE.
A CE should be able to withstand the ﬂow of electrons for the duration of the experiment.
Typically, they are not damaged by the current, but in some cases sacriﬁcial CE are used, which
are consumed in the reaction.
A RE should be easy to construct and should be able to mantain a stable potential even if small
amounts of currents are passed through it. Typically, inside a RE a reversible electrochemical
reaction is happening, and a glass frit at one end of the RE provides electrycal contact with the
external cell electrolyte. Common examples are the calomel RE and the Ag/AgCl RE.
Reversible half reactions can be used to creates scales. The two most used scales in CO2RR
are the ones based on the standard hydrogen electrode and the reversible hydrogen electrode.
Hori, whose studies pioneered CO2 electroreduction on copper, used SHE in most of his early,
groundbreaking work, but most recent publications in the community use RHE, which has grown
popular because of its widespread adoption in fuel cells studies.

2.1
Mass spectrometry technologies
In electrochemistry, it is often not suﬃcient to monitor the current and potential as the reaction
proceeds, and it is crucial to analyze the products to properly understand what is happening
and where the current as a function of time, in order to determine important properties of
our catalysts such as selectivity and FE and monitor how they change over the course of the
experiment.
2.1.1
Current MS technologies
There are two main types of technologies being current employed to study chemical species dis-
solved in liquids. MIMS (membrane inlet mass spectrometry) uses a permeable membrane, that
can be made for example of 0.5-1.0 mm dimethyl silicone, to separate the mass spectrometer
vacuum chamber from the liquid. The solvent and the solute will then diﬀuse through the mem-
brane and be sensed by the MS. The ﬂux is controlled by the solubility of the species and by the
pressure diﬀerence, and the use of a relatively thick membrane limits the ﬂux to a point where
the vacuum chamber in the MS is at a low enough pressure and the instrument can function
without problems. While this system is highly sensitive, the low ﬂux through the membrane
means that it also has a very slow time response, making it impossible to have a quasi-real time
picture of the behaviour of the system. Also, given that each species has a diﬀerent solubility
and diﬀusivity, the systems will not be equally sensitive towards all substances.
If a faster time response is needed, DEMS and related techniques are currently the best choice.
Here, a much thinner membrane, made of dimethyl silicone or a hydrophobic and porous mate-
rial such as Gore Tex is used, supported by a steel or glass frit. Since it’s much thinner, a higher
amount of solvent and solute passes through, with a much shorter transit time, resulting in a
faster time response and avoiding the preferential selectivity described for MIMS. However, such
a high ﬂux would prevent the proper functioning of the MS, and would end up damaging its ﬁla-
ment, which is both costly and time consuming to repair. Therefore, a diﬀerential pumping stage
30
==PAGE:43==
is used between the MS and the membrane, to bring down the pressure to below 10-6 mbar. This
unfortunately means that about 99.9 % of the total species that made it through the membrane
are pumped away, and only the remaining 0.1 % actually reaches the mass spectrometer, which
considerably reduces the sensitivity of the setup when compared to MIMS. Water evaporation
also can be a problem since it leads to a high cracking signal of molecular hydrogen and oxygen
in the MS due to cracking of the water molecule, which makes the setup less sensitive towards
reactions such as HER and OER. The high gas ﬂux can also signiﬁcantly reduce the tempera-
ture in the proximity of the membrane, causing the inlet to freeze as a result of water evaporation.
The current state of the art does not oﬀer a solution that combines a fast time response and a
high sensitivity, a problem that our setup aims to solve.
2.2
The EC-MS setup
2.2.1
The EC-MS chip
Our setup uses a novel technology that was developed here at DTU by Daniel Trimarco et
al.59
The heart of our EC-MS setup is a vacuum inlet fabricated on a silicon microchip in
the cleanroom facilities at DTU Danchip, which aims at combining fast time response and a
high sensitivity in a way that traditional techniques such as gas chromatography and diﬀeren-
tial electrochemical mass spectrometry are unable to do. It is currently being made available
commercially by SpectroInlets, a DTU spinoﬀcompany.60
A carrier gas, with a gas ﬂow of about 1-10 ml/min, is inserted via the carrier gas inlet, 40 mm
long and 100 wide and 200 um tall, ﬂows under the chip, where it is split into two streams. The
larger stream ﬂows out through a similar outlet channel and a pressure controller and ends up
in the buﬀer volume situated in front of the turbo pump of our setup.
The second one is channeled in an inlet capillary, having a length of 1.5 µm, a width of 100 µm
wide and a height of 3 µm. At this point it is lead in 300 µm through-hole with a diameter of
100 µm and then it ﬂows into a low volume gas chamber with a height of 3 µm and a diameter
of 2 mm. The small section of the inlet capillary causes an increase in the ﬂow velocity of the
gas, thus preventing back diﬀusion. The chamber has a perforated and hydrophobic membrane,
with holes of 3 microns placed in a honeycomb mesh, that supports a large liquid gas interface.
All surfaces of the chip are covered in a FDTS coating, including the holes in the membrane.
The pressure in the chamber can be regulated with a pressure controller, and it is usually set
at ambient pressure in order to prevent a collapse of the interface. Volatile species are thus
able to diﬀuse across the membrane into the carrier gas, that is therefore saturated with solvent
31
==PAGE:44==
and solute species from the chamber. The carrier gas then ﬂows through a narrow outlet capil-
lary, that reduces the pressure to below 10−−6 mbar, into the MS. The small volume of the gas
chamber and the channels is about 31 nl, and the ﬂow rate in the capillary to the MS is about
1.5 10−15 molecules per second at atmospheric pressure. The residence time can be estimated
at about 0.5 seconds, and this determines the time response of the chip. The gas interface can
withstand a pressure diﬀerence of about 33 kPa before breaching.
In59 the system is assumed to be about 1000 times more sensitive than DEMS while maintaining
a similar time response. The chip was then further developed and reﬁned by Trimarco and Scott
as part of the latter’s master thesis,61 where the time response of the system is studied with the
aid of simulations, comparing it to a typical DEMS setup.
The model for a stagnant system is based on Fick’s laws is one dimension He considered a
diﬀusion limited case, where evaporation is fast compared to diﬀusion, where the model show
that evaporation limitation is important for our setup even for very volatile products, resulting
is a slightly longer response time than DEMS, that is however oﬀset by a much higher signal.
An evaporation limited case is also considered, showing that our setup performs considerably
worse than DEMS for products with low volatility.
For a ﬂow system, the model is slightly more reﬁned, as the continuity equation for the electrolyte
needs to be taken into account. In a ﬂow system the time response of our setup improves for
products with low or zero vapour pressure, since the ﬂow will set the time response, but that
happens at the expense of sensitivity, since products are carried away by the electrolyte ﬂow.
All the simulations point to the crucial role played by Henry’s constant in determining the
sensitivity of the setup. Our setup is shown to be about three orders of magnitude more sensitive
than DEMS for insoluble gases, and 30 times less sensitive for those analytes whose volatility is
comparable to that of the solvent. This can be a problem, since recent literature suggests that
nanoparticles as those we are investigating might be very active towards ethanol production, and
it would be amazing to be able to investigate such phenomenon with the same setup sensitivity
we have for other CO2RR products, especially since ethanol is a relatively pricey chemical with
a wide range of applications and a high global annual production.63

and the resting potential is varied in a ladder-like fashion.

4.2
Experimental procedures and common issues
The EC-MS we used over the course of this project is still being developed and, while it is more
reﬁned than it was just a year ago, it is far from a polished product.
Using a consistent and meticulous procedure to ensure the cleanliness of the setup components
has been shown to be essential to increase the success rate of our experiments.
In this section I will give a description of the procedures to remove impurities from the setup
parts and to prepare the electrolyte solutions.
Then, I will present an overview of the most common we encountered during our measurement,
and the measures we implemented to solve them.
4.2.1
Cleaning procedures
Before each set of experiments, the components of the setup were cleaned by leaving them
overnight in 3:1 solution of 98% sulfuric acid and 30% hydrogen peroxide, commonly referred to
as piranha solution.
This is extremely eﬀective at removing organic impurities and non precious metals.
If, for
any reason, it was suspected the presence of precious metals such as platinum, a 3:1 solution
of 37% hydrocloric acid and 69% nitric acid, known as aqua regia, was used instead.
The
components were then rinsed 5 times with ultrapure water to remove the solvent and any residual
contaminations.
4.2.2
Electrolyte preparation
CO is kinetically inert, so it is possible to perform experiments at any pH, which is one distinctive
advantage of starting from CO instead of CO2. Given that a high pH promotes C-C coupling,
it was chosen to focus on CORR studies in alkali environment.
Initially, CO2 saturated 0.1 M KOH with pH=13 was the electrolyte of choice, due to its wide
use in CORR studies45 , but once it became clear that this caused stability problems to the
EC-MS chip, which would often breach in an hour, we adopted a 1.0 M carbonate buﬀer at
pH=12. The decrease in pH extended the lifetime of a chip by about a factor of ten, while
increasing the onset of CO reduction vs HER by about 60 mV.
A phosphate buﬀer was also considered, but we eventually discarded since it has been reported
to suppress CORR.24
The electrolyte was prepared by saturating 200 ml of 1.0 M KOH with CO2, with a pH value of
7.8, This was then titrated to pH 12 using 213.5 ml 1.0 M KOH. Quantities can, of course, be
48
==PAGE:61==
scaled to prepare diﬀerent amounts of solution.
4.2.3
Gas bubbles
One of the major issues of the setup is that its relatively easy to introduce a bubble inside it by
mistake, and it is then very time consuming to remove it.
Assembling the setup is a particularly delicate phase, as there are several steps where a bubble
could be created, especially when introducing the electrolyte and connecting the electrodes to
the cell, and it is important to take all the steps to reduce the chance of it happening. The
glass frits of the electrode chambers can also generate bubbles, as the air trapped into the frits
degasses into the cell. This can be avoided by keeping the narrow end of the chambers immersed
in electrolyte between experiments.
Another way of creating bubbles is by going to very negative potential while running an exper-
iment. This often results in an extremely high production of hydrogen which, via a mechanism
that we do no fully understand yet, in turn seems to ”suck in” an air bubble in the setup.
This has proven particularly annoying, as it has eﬀectively prevented us from running our usual
experiments on all but very low nanoparticles loading, as in the case of the polycrystalline Cu
sample.
Having a gas bubble in the setup is highly undesirable, as depending on its position it can make
it impossible to get meaningful data. If a large bubble is positioned at the entrance to one of
the channels connected to the reference and working electrode, it can prevent proper electrical
contact. A bubble placed at the entrance of the outlet channel can eﬀectively obstruct it, making
it impossible to insert fresh electrolyte in the cell. A large bubble close to the same channels
can result in a very high and often unstable ohmic resistance, while having one in contact with
the sample can cut oﬀpart of the catalyst from the electrolyte, altering its performance, and
can also cause irregular electrical contact. An air or oxygen bubble in contact with the working
electrode can also be reduced if the potential is negative enough, diverting current away from
the reactions we intend to study and preventing us to hit the sharp potential steps we need to
study the dynamic behaviour of methane. Furthermore, they can also frustrate all our eﬀorts
to perform O2 free experiments.
For all this reason, it is crucial to take all possible precaution to prevent bubble formation. If
a bubble does get appear in the system, it sometimes can be removed by ﬂowing electrolyte
for a long time, thus ﬂowing it out of the cell. Another solution is to lower the pressure below
the chip to 0.95 or even 0.90 bar to try and suck it through the chip membrane, though it is
important to be careful not to breach the chip in the process. If the bubble is positioned at
the electrodes channel, we can close the outlet channel with a plug, remove the glass chambers
and ﬂow electrolyte through the electrode channel until it gets out. Oxygen bubbles can also
49
==PAGE:62==
removed by going to negative potential until all the gas has been reduced to water.
None of these methods are guaranteed to work, though, and it can also be challenging to un-
derstand the location of the bubble, so unmounting the setup, drying every component and
assembling it again it’s often the only solution, but that’s also undesirable as the sample is often
less active afterwards Its of course best to just avoid bubbles in the ﬁrst place
4.2.4
Breaching of the chip membrane
The hydrophobic membrane of the EC-MS chip is essentially a 15 µm thick silicon layer with
3 µm wide channels going through it. The surfaces of the membrane and of the channels are
coated with FDTS(perﬂuorodecyltrichlorosilane) to make them hydrophobic.
The liquid-gas interface can withstand a diﬀerence in pressure of 33 mbar,59 above which the
membrane breaches and the electrolyte ﬂows in the chip chamber, . When this happens, the
water signal in the QMS shoots up to about 10 −5 mbar and then the safety valve shuts down
to protect the QMS ﬁlament. It is then necessary to deassemble the setup, dry the components
and pump down again to UHV.
Often, the chip breaches as a result of human error, which causes and excessive pressure, but
it can also be caused as a result of the corrosion of the membrane by the electrolyte. In the
past, 0.1 M KOH with a pH value of 13 was used, which would lead to the chip breaching in
about an hour. Since that was hardly ideal, a carbonate buﬀer electrolyte was adopted instead,
with a pH value of 12.2, when correctly prepared, and this made it possible to measure for over
ten hours without problems. Interestingly, rinsing the chip in milli-Q water at the end of a
measurement day prolongs it lifetime, and on several occasion a chip has lasted us for over 20
hours of measurements spread over multiple days.
4.2.5
Faulty electrical contact and noise
Given how important it is to precisely control the potential and the current applied to the
sample, it is not surprising that anything that results in poor electrical contact in the setup is
problematic.
In the ﬁrst months of my project, the reference electrode was the most common cause of noise,
sometimes in ways that we did not fully understand. On some occasions, we noticed a source
of noise with a frequency of 50hz, that we could not locate. Given its frequency we thought it
50
==PAGE:63==
was somehow caused by the electrical grid, a suspect that was strengthened when a assembling
a makeshift Faraday cage around the setup reduced the noise by a factor of two, but we could
not pin down what the issue was. This problematic would happen on an irregular base, possibly
because the RE junction would occasionally dry up or clog, and it was only permanently solved
by the switch to a full size reference electrode.
We also learned that it is important to ensure that the Teﬂon ucup is uniformly hydrophilic
along its edge, otherwise in the hydrophobic parts it will repel the electrolyte, preventing proper
electrical contact and causing a large ohmic drop in the system.
Finally, during the last months of the experiments we kept having persistent electrical noise
measurements, and after some tinkering we noticed that the metal rod holding the sample was
the cause, probably because its gold tip had dulled with use and was no longer providing good
electrical contact with the glassy carbon disk. We used a multimeter to measure the resistance
between the sample and the back end of a metal rod and noticed that it changed irregularly
between 0 and several MΩ, while the use og a new metal rod led to a value of a few Ω.
4.2.6
Particle deactivation
As described in the literature review, there are many reports of catalysts deactivating over the
course of an experiment due to impurities in the electrolyte,18 which is a big limitation for us
since experiments 4b and 6b can easily take an hour, and we often needed to perform several of
those to acquire relevant data.
Therefore, throughout the project we were looking for ways to improve the cleanliness of our
setup, which for example led us to tweaking over time the procedure to piranha clean the parts
of the setup. From the ﬁrst moment, though, we suspected that one of the major sources of
impurities was the electrolyte used during our experiments, especially since in the beginning,
we were only using semiconductor grade KOH to prepare our carbonate buﬀer.
To remove the metal impurities we tried using Chelex as described by Wuttig et al.,23 but it
did not lead to signiﬁcant improvements, and when ICP-MS performed by Thomas Vagn Hogg
showed that using Chelex on suprapure KOH actually caused an increase in the amount of
detected impurities, we simply switched to using suprapure KOH, reasoning that its advantage
over Chelex-puriﬁed semiconductor grade KOH would be even larger.
This did not completely stop sample deactivation, as was to be expected given that not only it
has also been reported for ultrapure electrolyte solutions,18 but also because despite our best
eﬀorts it’s hard to completely eliminate impurities in such a process. To reactivate our samples
we can either use anodic potential pulses, which could be explained by the fact that we are
oxidizing the metals deposited on the electrode surface, dissolving them back in the solution.
Another method we tried is to ﬂush for a prolonged way of time, which we suspect has the same
51
==PAGE:64==
eﬀect, due to the oxygen contained in the electrolyte.
Neither of these methods has a 100% success rate, and most of the time it is only possible to
reactivate the steady state production of methane and ethylene, while the dynamic behavior
proved much harder to restore.

4.3.5
The importance of oxygen: suppressing the transient methane peak.
At this point, we suspected that the dynamic peak of methane production was caused by the
oxygen dissolved in the electrolyte solution.
In order to prove it, we decided to repeat our
experiments using oxygen-free electrolyte, hoping that this would make it impossible to obtain
the transient, thus proving our hypothesis.
The signal registered by the QMS for m
z = 32 includes a background level caused the cracking
of water molecules, so it is impossible to lower the measured amount of oxygen to zero, but we
set out to try and reduce as much as possible all experimental sources of O2. To remove the
O2 from the electrolyte we saturated it with argon. We also left the cell and the luer ﬁttings
overnight in a beaker ﬁlled with milli-Q water at a temperature of 70 degrees, with Argon
bubbling continuously through it, in an attempt to purge the PCTFE material from the O2
stored in its pores.
Despite our eﬀorts, we did not manage to ”kill” the transient. Studying our data, we realized
that while the O2 level during the resting potential were lower than in days were the electrolyte
had not been purged with argon, when we inserted fresh electrolyte the height of the spike in
the O2 signal was almost unchanged, suggesting that either we did not manage to remove the
dissolved O2 from the newly inserted solution or that oxygen was somehow being sucked in the
cell while performing a ﬂush. After examining the components of the setup, we realized that the
electrolyte situated below the valve of the bubbler and above the cell, the one contained in the
bubbler tip and in the inlet tube, was cut oﬀfrom the Argon gas and it was not being constantly
purged. On the contrary, it was being replenished with oxygen that was diﬀusing through the
tube walls, a fact that would explain the observed peak in the m
z = 32 signal.
To solve this problem, we designed a new experiment, schematically represented in ﬁgure 4.13,
that would sum up what we had learned so far and prove the key role played by the oxygen
dissolved in the electrolyte.
In the ﬁrst segment of the experiment we do a 1 minute long step at 0 V vs RHE (a potential
value that we will call ”active rest”, since it triggers the transient), introducing fresh electrolyte
in the 25-30 s interval, and then go to a potential that we know from experiment 4b would trigger
the dynamic behaviour of the sample. Since the electrolyte inserted had been oxygenating in
the tube, we expect this would indeed activate the transient.
Then, in the second part, we perform a 1 minute long step at a resting potential too negative
to activate the transient (”passive rest”), as learned from experiment 6b, ﬂushing again at the
25 seconds mark, and then repeating the step at working potential as above.
The last part of the experiment consists of a long ﬂush at the same passive rest potential used
in step two, to get rid of the electrolyte that got oxygenated in the tube. While still ﬂushing,
64
==PAGE:77==
we switch to a 35 second long step at 0 V vs RHE, that we know causes a dynamic peak when
paired with oxygen-containing electrolyte, closing the bubbler’s valve after 5 second, and then
repeat the working potential step for the last time. This way the electrolyte introduced in the
cell while at 0 V vs RHE is going to be the one that has been Argon purged in the bubbler, and
it should not cause the transient.
We also decided that we would perform the experiment three times: once with aerated elec-
trolyte, where we would observe the transient in the third part, once with argon-bubbled elec-
trolyte, where hopefully the transient would be absent, and one last time in oxygenated solution,
where the transient would manifest again, to strengthen the message that it was the removal of
oxygen that suppressed the dynamic peak of methane production.

5.2
Transient methane production
5.2.1
Data interpretation
We can sum up the knowledge gained from the chronoamperometry measurements described in
the previous section as follows:
• Exposing the catalyst to oxygen during a resting potential step at a potential E ⩾−0.2
vs RHE, and then performing a working potential step at a potential E ⩽−0.7 V vs RHE
results in a brief peak of high methane production, that has been referred to as ”transient”
or ”dynamic”, at the beginning of the step.
• Longer exposure to oxygenated electrolyte causes larger transient peak up to ﬂush duration
of about 15s, after which a plateau is reached.
• The process can not be explained by mass transport, since if the decrease in methane
production after the ﬁrst instant was caused by CO scarcity near the the surface, the
ethylene signal would also be aﬀected.
• The phenomenon does not disappear in phosphate buﬀer, proving that it is not caused by
the speciﬁc electrolyte used, and it is not killed by using a lower percentage of CO in the
carrier gas. It is also possible to increase the duration of the resting potential step without
losing the dynamic behaviour.
• The phenomenon can’t be explained by a malfunctioning of the potentiostat, which would
overshoot the potential at the beginning of a pulse, since running the same CA experiment
without inserting fresh electrolyte does not trigger the transient, and the same increased
production would also be observed for other reaction products.
• pH eﬀects are also not the cause, since they would also aﬀect the H2 signal in a similar
way.
• Oxygen activation can be performed either by insertion of aerated electrolyte or through
the carrier gas, though this has not been observed on samples that also show dynamic
ethylene.
• Inserting oxygen-free electrolyte leads to the almost complete disappearance of the tran-
sient methane peak on samples that do not show transient ethylene.
• Samples that show transient methane but not transient ethylene also have a high C2H4/CH4
ratio, and the transient peaks die oﬀextremely quickly .
84
==PAGE:97==
All of this points to oxygen as the responsible for the increased production. The eﬀects of oxy-
gen during CO2RR catalysis have been investigated by several studies in recent years, especially
when trying to explain the properties of OD-Cu, as described in the next section.
There are two points that are worth making at this stage. First, the brief duration of the high
methane production suggests that the presence of oxygen on our sample is short lived. Second,
in OD-Cu studies, the presence of oxide species or subsurface oxygen is used to explain the
increased selectivity towards ethylene2666,68 as it is often thought to cause an increase in the
bonding energy of CO. In our case, on the other hand, the presence of oxygen is instead linked
to a vast increase in the production of methane. This, however, is not incompatible with what
reported in literature as our hypothesis considers the eﬀects of surface oxygen on a reduced sur-
face, and the presence of subsurface oxygen could indeed be the reason for the high C2H4/CH4
ratio.
In the next section, a possible explanation based on DFT simulations, performed by another
group collaborating with this project, is proposed, but this does not mean that it can be ac-
cepted until it is corroborated by further studies, that could for example be performed on single
crystals. While it is not straightforward to incorporate single crystals in our setup, as they need
to be handled carefully in order to preserve their surface, they could allow us to isolate the
contributions of individual surface sites, whose eﬀects are instead convoluted in the NP and PC
samples we have used so far.
5.2.2
Atomic scale simulations
As a way of gaining some insight into the causes of transient methane, we asked Alexander Bag-
ger, a Phd at Jan Rossmeils’ group at Københavns Universitet, to perform DFT calculations
to investigate the eﬀects of adsorbed oxygen on the CO binding strength on diﬀerent surface
sites. Two surfaces were simulated: a 211 stepped surface and a kink model created by removing
atoms on a 211 surface. Another set of calculations was used to create the Wulﬀconstruction
of a nanoparticles of approximately 5 nm in diameter, in order to determine the fraction of
speciﬁc surface sites having a certain coordination number. In ﬁg 5.1 we can see three Wulﬀ
constructions for nanoparticles having a diameter of 4.3 nm, 5.7 m, and 6.8 nm, formed by a
number of atoms ranging from 4000 to 16000, while ﬁgure 5.1 shows the portion of surface sites
with a determined coordination number, normalized to the number of surface sites, as a function
of the number of atoms forming the particle.

turbation to the system that opens up additional pathways for the production of methane, so

5.3
Transient ethylene
Five of our NP samples showed a dynamic behaviour for methane and ethylene, and in all cases
they also possesses a lower C2H4/CH4 ratio than samples that only show dynamic methane.
The transient peaks also look less sharp than on the other typology of samples. The Cu-sputtered
chip also showed dynamic ethylene, but its peaks had a shape similar to the ones of the samples
that did not show transient C2H4. We did not manage to study dynamic ethylene in the same
systematic way adopted for methane, as it was present on less samples and also because we did
not actively design experiments to investigate it, since our focus, especially in the ﬁnal part of
the project, was on understanding the mechanism leading to dynamic CH4.
However, we have seen that it is activated at the same potentials as dynamic methane, and it
also requires the insertion of fresh electrolyte in the cell. We did not so far manage to acti-
vate it through O2 in the carrier gas, and further study will be needed to determine if that’s
possible.
Reconciling the measurements on the NP with those on the sputtered chip is not
straightforward, since the chip had diﬀerent shape of the dynamic peaks. Also, for the chip we
have observed a diﬀerent O2 signal during the resting potential steps preceding the dynamic
peak, which might suggest that on its polycrystalline Cu surface there is a diﬀerent mechanism
leading to the dynamic behaviour of the sample.
Our data may suggest that the pathway for transient ethylene shares a rate-limiting step with
methane, which would mean it is formed by CH2-CH2 coupling. The DFT simulations suggest
that kink sites are the location where the transient methane peak happens and, given that sev-
eral samples showed only dynamic methane, it is unlikely that the same sites are also involved
in dynamic C2H4. As such, we can expect other surface sites to be responsible for this phe-
nomenon.
The behaviour of these sample is not similar to the one oxide-derived copper: while OD-Cu
shows a suppression of methane in favor of high selectivity for steady state ethylene , these NP
samples actually have lower C2H4 /CH4 ratio than those that do not exhibit dynamic C2H4. Still
we can look at the mechanisms proposed in literature to gain inspiration. As already stated, we
think that the dynamic behavior is not caused by a pH eﬀect, as proposed by Nie28 for Od-Cu,
since that would also aﬀect the hydrogen and methane signals.
Mistry et al.26 have detected the presence of oxide species on plasma oxidized catalysts that
showed high selectivity towards ethylene. If, by exposing the surface to oxygen at -0.2 V vs RHE
or above we managed to create a small portion of such species this could explain the increase of
ethylene production, though the fact that there is no corresponding decrease in methane again
is a point against this theory, as it would require the oxidized species to be creates in sites that
are not anyway active for methane. Also, the species described by Mistry were still present
after 1 hour of reaction at -0.9 V vs RHE, which is in contrast with the transient nature of the
88
==PAGE:101==
phenomenon we observe.
Eilert et al.66 have suggested that the unique properties of OD-Cu may be explained by the
presence of sub-surface oxygen in absence of oxidized species, as it would lead to stronger bind-
ing of CO, but this explanation too does not explain the short duration of the dynamic ethylene
peak, and we are more inclined to think that subsurface oxygen is what is causing the high
activity for ethylene in samples that do not show transient ethylene.
In order to explain the dynamic behavior of C2H4 we will probably need to identify the reason
these samples have such diﬀerent properties than those shown by the rest of our NP samples.
From there we can hope to understand the surface sites proportion, oxidation state and mor-
phology of their nanoparticles, at which point we could perform single crystal studies and DFT
simulations geared to isolate their unique features. It is interesting that in the month of January
we observed several samples showing transient ethylene, which could mean that something has
changed in the way the samples are prepared or treated without us noticing. If that’s the case,
ﬁnding the diﬀerence would point us in the right direction.
At the moment, in conclusion we do not have an explanation for the dynamic behaviour of
ethylene, but it does certainly look worthy of further investigation, both from a fundamental
science point of view and from an economical one.

5.4
Secondary H2
Leveraging the fast time response and high sensitivity of our setup we have been able to identify
the presence of a secondary hydrogen peak at cathodic potentials on a polycristalline copper
sample. The peak:
• depends only weakly on the size of the primary HER peak .
• is not poisoned by CO.
• it is not a caused by delayed detection of a portion of the primary HER peak due to a
setup problem, since it is only detected at anodic potentials, and it can be ”put on hold”
by keeping the potential at 0 vs RHE after going to cathodic potentials, where the primary
HER peak is observed.
• it is not observed if we do not ﬁrst go HER potentials, meaning that it is not actually
produced at anodic potentials.
A possible explanation is that we are intercalating hydrogen in the catalyst’s surface after having
gone to cathodic potential to perform HER. Hydrogen then stays in the surface until it is released
at anodic potential, where it is picked up by our detector.
A way to conﬁrm this would be to expose the surface to hydrogen at cathodic potential where
HER does not take place, and then going to the anodic potential where the secondary peak has
been observed. If our hypothesis is correct, varying the cathodic potential it would be possible to
ﬁnd the value where hydrogen is intercalated in the surface. While it is hard to ﬁnd a industrial
application for this phenomenon, it is a powerful demonstration of the capabilities of our setup.

5.5
Conclusion and future work
Over the course of this project we leveraged the unique capabilities of our setup to investigate
a brief peak of high methane and ethylene production on diﬀerent nanostructured copper cata-
lysts.
By combining the experimental data with DFT simulations we proposed an explanation for
the transient production of methane. While we found correlation between the presence of the
dynamic ethylene peak and the C2H4 CH4 ratio, we were unable to identify its causes, meaning
that further studies will be needed.
We also observed a secondary H2 peak at anodic potential during CV measurements on poly-
crystalline copper, that can be explain as hydrogen intercalating in the catalyst surface at HER
potential.
In the future we will try to understand the causes of transient ethylene, and attempt to replicate
both phenomena on other kinds of copper based catalysts including single crystals, to further our
understanding of the process and see if it is possible to tune the dynamic behaviour of the catalyst
toward ethylene. Then we will investigate the possibility of stabilizing the transient behaviour,
as this could potentially be a major step forward towards the goal of making electrochemical
CO2RR an industrially viable process.
Further work will also be geared at developing the EC-MS setup, streamlining it and making it
easier to operate in order to increase the success rate of the experiments.
A new generation of chips is also being developed, which will oﬀer several improvements over
the current one, such as a larger membrane area and the capability of withstanding a larger
diﬀerence in pressure without breaching.

B.
EC-MS quantiﬁcation
In this section I will brieﬂy describe the procedure used to quantify the reaction products in our
experiments. Note that while Scott and I performed the experimental measurements described
in this appendix together, the coding work was done entirely by him. Given the extremely thin
layer of stagnant electrolyte used in our setup, we can assume that all volatile products are
collected by the membrane, so that the QMS signal is directly proportional to the amount of
the speciﬁc substance produced. Therefore, if we can produce a known amount of a molecule,
we can calculate the calibration factor of the QMS.
We decided that a performing electrolysis, CO2 oxidation and CO stripping experiments on a
platinum catalyst would be ideally suited for that goal, so we sputter deposited Pt on a glassy
carbon disc as used it as our working electrode. To perform a Co stripping, we exposed the
catalyst surface to CO at a potential where it would not get oxidized, using CO as carrier gas.
97
==PAGE:110==
Then, we switched the carrier gas to He, so that the only CO present in the system would be a
monolayer adsorbed on the electrode. At that point we we went oxidized the CO and detected
the CO2 produced. From the reaction CO·+H2O −−→Co2(g)+2 H+ +2 e– using the integrated
measured current we can calculate the amount of moles produced dividing by 2F, where F is
the charge of a mole of electrons, and derive the calibration factor. This was repeated for several
sputtered deposited Pt samples, and the data thus collected is shown in ﬁgure B.1. We called
this an ”internal calibration” point.
Figure B.1
Since we could not perform a similar process for ethylene, methane and other CO2RR products,
we used the Relative Sensitivity Factors (RSF) reported by Hiden Analytical2 and combined
it with the calibration we had obtained for carbon dioxide. For m
z values where this was not
possible, we used the electron impact ionization spectra provided by NIST,51 to perform a ﬁrst
adjustment to the RSF. By plotting the calibration factors we had obtained vs the RSF values
reported by Hiden (ﬁg B.2), we saw that there was a spread in proportionality equal to about
a factor of two, which we took as measure of the precision of the quantiﬁcations method used
in the report. In the future we believe it is possible to improve on this method, by inserting a
methane line in the carrier gas system to obtain an internal calibration for CH4.
