Abstract
Natural Language Processing and Machine Learning can be utilized to limit the amount
of human input, and work, necessary when analyzing large text documents. The Food
and Agriculture Organization (FAO) is a specialized agency of the United Nations that
leads international efforts to defeat hunger. This work will be carried out together with the
Digital Public Goods team, which analyses all FAO Projects with digital/IT components.
Every approved Project, whether Regional-National or Global, is described in a PDF/Word
document where all aspects of the effort being made are documented. Projects are then
classified based on the scope and key information extracted through a partially manual
process to compile a yearly FAO Digital Portfolio (FDP) of Software Products. This re-
search aims to aid the DPG team in data retrieval, extraction, and processing. To do so
an assessment of which aspects of the process can be automated, through the use of
Natural Language Processing and Machine Learning techniques, will be carried out, as
well as a deep analysis of the underlying data.

1
Introduction
Nowadays the digital world possesses a great wealth of data [1], in about all aspects
of everyday life. It becomes increasingly possible to analyze these data and develop
automated applications [1] through Machine Learning techniques.
The work in this paper has been carried out with the Food and Agriculture Organization
(FAO). FAO is a specialized agency of the United Nations that leads international efforts to
defeat hunger [2], with projects in over 130 countries worldwide. To sustain such a large
organization a significant amount of data is processed, and part of this work is performed
by the Digital Public Goods (DPG) Team which analyses all FAO Projects with digital/IT
components. Projects are classified based on the scope and key information extracted
through a partially manual process to compile a yearly FAO Digital Portfolio (FDP) of
Software Products.
This research aims to aid the DPG team in data retrieval, extraction, and processing. As
all Projects provide a Project Document, which outlines the purpose and efforts made,
the initial assumption is that it can be utilized to retrieve most, if not all, information nec-
essary. Project Documents, for the most part, follow a standard format, however, no
standard template is provided. This means that Title and general information are present
however, not in standardized locations. Moreover, at times a complete Project Document
is unavailable, with small informative documents taking its place.
Three main Project features are of most interest, namely:
• Business Themes: aims to define the focus area of the Project
• Digital Innovation Themes: explains what digital components are utilized
• FAO Products used: lists all FAO-provided products utilized by the Project
The Project’s business theme is central; through this classification, it is possible to have
an overview of every single Project’s scope as well as global knowledge regarding all
current initiatives. There are 15 Business Themes and they are very heterogeneous, just
like FAO Projects; some examples are Fishery Sustainability and Management, Water
Sustainability and Management, Trade Markets and Social Protection.
On the other hand, the digital innovation theme is to be considered slightly more propri-
etary to the DPG team, as it represents all digital components utilized. There are 17 in
total, examples are Drones, E-commerce, and Cyber-Security.
Finally, FAO provides access to over 400 Digital Products which consist of applications,
databases, and platforms, to aid executives and employees in reaching their project goals.
These range from visualization tools, Hand in Hand, for example, satellite imagery pro-
cessing software like ArcGIS and many, many more.
All 3 of these features consist of examples of what in Machine Learning is known as
Multi-Label Classification, as a project may be assigned multiple Themes and Products.
Throughout this research, we will analyze the data at our disposal, which is not to be
considered perfect, and identify critical areas in the process. Automation techniques will
be developed where possible, and critical insights into the underlying data will be provided
to improve the process as a whole. The goal is not to create a finished product but an aid
NLP for FAO Projects

==PAGE:11==
to humans. In the future, with more, and better, data at our disposal, this may prove to be
a more objective and time-sensitive method to extract information.
This research is divided into seven chapters, including this Introduction. Contrary to most
papers, the second and third chapters show the data retrieval process and consequent
data exploration. This choice stems from the need to understand further our problems
in this area and what solutions may be necessary. Following, we will detail the most

this specific use case. Finally, the last two chapters analyze the results obtained and
provide critical insights into how the process could be improved in the future.

Data Retrieval and Pre-Processing
2.1
Document Download
To work on following aspects of this research, it was first necessary to retrieve all Project
Documents. At our disposal were three main resources that we could use in any way we
deemed fit:
• An excel file of 1300 entities containing all Projects for our training set, with a Unique
Entity Number, made up of 5 or 6 digits
• A dated backup containing over 2000 Project Documents, not all relative to the Dig-
ital aspect of FAO
• A website where inserting an Entity Number, the user can download the Project
Document
There was no guarantee that the backup folder would contain all Projects in the training
set, as it was unclear what day the backup had been generated. Therefore to maximize
the number of Projects available in the training set, I decided to use the website to retrieve
all Project Documents.
The first step, therefore, consisted in cleaning the Excel file of the training set of any
impurities, namely:
• Remove any entries with a missing Entity Number
• Remove any non-unique Entity Number codes, keeping the most complete entry
However, these steps were not sufficient, as there was still the possibility that correctly
formatted Entity Numbers would not correspond to a Project, rendering the document
download impossible. This was simply solved in the next step of the data download.
Manually downloading each project would be possible but highly time-consuming, given
the size of the training set. That being the case, I developed an automatic web scraper
that was designed using Selenium [3], a web-driver package for Python. Manual analysis
of the process verified that while the website was old and missing some key XPATH (rep-
resents the location of a particular object on the page) and the key-strokes for download
were not consistent throughout each project, some similarities were found and made it
possible to write code which would download a majority of the Project Documents. Uti-
lizing this methodology 780 Project Documents were downloaded, with the remaining
being manually searched for on the website. This way, it was also possible to find sub-
sequent errors in the entity numbers and verify that sometimes the Documentation was
not present. Combining the two processes, 1095 Project Documents were downloaded,
as seen in Figure 2.1.

2.2
Text extraction
It was finally necessary to extract all text from the documents, which was stored in .txt
files named with the unique code of the entity number of the relative Project. I did this to
facilitate and significantly speed up future model development, testing, and training. As
NLP for FAO Projects
5
==PAGE:15==
can be seen in Figure 2.4 at the end of the download process, there are four formats of
Project Document:
• DOC obsolete version of Word Documents
• DOCX standard Word Document format
• PDF both scanned and not scanned versions of the document
• TIF scanned version of paper Project Document in image format
The documents themselves do not follow a strict format; hence, all text in the documents
had to be extracted to train further models. The methodology, packages, and logic nec-
essary to extract text from these formats vary drastically [4], therefore, I developed 4 main
functions to address each individually.
2.2.1
DOC and DOCX
All .DOC files were transformed, utilizing Libre Office [5], into .DOCX files, as it represents
the more advanced and newer version of the file format. Also, extracting text from .DOCX
files in Python is relatively simple by utilizing the docx package [6], as all aspects and
parts of the document can be accessed. In this manner, all text in paragraphs and cells
was extracted and stored in a .txt file.
2.2.2
PDF
When it comes to PDF format, there were one of two possibilities.
• The document had been exported as a PDF starting from a word format
• The pages of the PDF consist of scanned images of the document
For exported PDFs, extraction tends to be fairly straight-forward through the use of a
package named pyPDF [7]; however, extracting texts from images tends to be slightly
more tricky. Text extraction for this type of PDFs follows that of .TIF format files you can
see below.
2.2.3
TIF
TIF stands for Tag Image File Format, it was created primarily by imaging developers of
input and output devices such as printers, monitors, and scanners [8]. To address images
Optimal Character Recognition (OCR) is considered state of the art, primarily through the
use of Tesseract [9] and the developed python wrapper for use in a Python environment
PyTesseract [10]. Using PyTesseract, all text in the images was retrieved and stored in a
.txt file for the necessary pre-processing. The process did not present any issues after a
manual inspection, however the validity will be analyzed further on in Chapter 6
2.3
Final corrections and downloads
As there was no manual inspection of the documents downloaded from the scraper except
for the previously shown steps, it was necessary to verify that the document contained
enough information once the text had been extracted. Therefore after cleaning the text
extracted, I verified whether it was sufficient, based on the amount of information con-
tained in all other documents. A threshold for minimum document length was determined
and all documents where the text extracted was insufficient were manually checked to
verify their integrity and whether they were sufficiently explanatory. The flow can be seen
in Figure 2.5 below, portrayed for one Project Document.

Data Exploration
3.1
Description of Context
All FAO Projects with a digital / IT component are analyzed by the Digital Public Goods
(DPG) team. Projects are classified based on the scope and critical information extracted
through a manual process to compile and publish a yearly FAO Digital Portfolio (FDP) of
software products.
Three main Project features required the most time, namely:
• Business theme
• Digital Innovation theme
• FAO Products utilized
Challenges faced vary based on the target features to predict, as the three values differ
for the number of training examples and labels present in the Multi-Label classification
problem. Instances which lacked assignment of the target feature were not, through-out
this research, considered. Processing of the Projects is not always completed uniformly,
therefore the feature being empty is not to be seen as an assignment, but as a missing
target variable.
To better understand how to develop the work, it was necessary to analyze the underlying
data to comprehend challenges and issues. The following sections will show trends in
data and how these affected model development.
3.2
Business Themes
3.2.1
Pre-Processing
Our target feature is, as already stated, that of Business Themes, and our input variable
is text extracted from the Document available for a specific project. As a training set, we
are utilizing the already processed and categorized project excel sheet used in the data
download and pre-processing chapter. An extract of this excel sheet was created after
the downloading process that consists of all Projects previously downloaded.
Subsequently, all entries where no Business Themes were assigned, i.e., the column was
empty, were removed as not valid for our model training. The reasoning behind this step
is that all Programmes and Projects delve into at least one Business Theme, therefore,
if none are attributed, it is due either to an error in the analysis or it has not yet been
processed. Below in Figure 3.1 we can see that a large percentage of our documents
have Business Themes assigned, over 90%. This will be our training set for subsequent
work in this chapter.

All three tasks defined in the introduction represent examples of Multi-Label classification.
Jesse Read et al. perfectly describe how multi-label classification differs from single-
label classification [11]: ”The traditional data mining task of single-label classification,
also known as multi-class classification, associates an instance x with a single label l
from a previously known finite set of labels L. A single-label dataset D is composed of
n examples (x1, l1), (x2, l2), ..., (xn, ln). The multi-label classification task associates a
subset of labels S ⊆L with each instance. A multi-label dataset D is therefore composed
of n examples (x1, S1), (x2, S2), ∙∙∙, (xn, Sn)”.
Multi-label classification tends to be more challenging to tackle than single-label classifi-
cation due to the nature of the problem itself. It is utilized for various problems such as
music categorization [12], proteomics-genomics [13], and most importantly for our use
case text categorization[14].
The definition of multi-label though limits itself to stating that a specific entity can ”belong”
to one or more classes. It is important to note how much a problem is multi-label, [15] as
applications can vary from having a set of labels L larger than our training set examples
N to L << N. The amount of labels present is hugely relevant when it comes to the
choice of which model to adopt in a real-life scenario.
In this chapter, we will see which methods are currently studied in literature for NLP Multi-
Label Classification problems, as well as the most common metrics adopted in the litera-
ture.
4.1
Machine Learning Methods for Multi-Label classification
According to Grigorios Tsoumakas et al. ”existing methods for multi-label classification
can be grouped into two main categories problem transformation methods and algorithm
adaptation methods”[15].
In their paper Multi-label Problem Transformation Methods: a Case Study Everton et
al. [16] provide the following definition for problem transformation methods: ”problem
transformation methods map the multi-label learning task into one or more single-label
learning tasks. When the problem is mapped into more than one single-label problem,
the multi-label problem is decomposed into several independent binary classification prob-
lems, one for each label which participates in the multi-label problem.” One of these meth-
ods is Binary Relevance.
Algorithm adaptation methods on the other hand, consist in adapting single-label clas-
sifiers to allow their use in multi-label problems [17]. Most machine learning classifiers
cannot be adapted to deal with multi-label classification, however, some exceptions have
been utilized in the literature.
• KNN K Nearest Neighbours [18]
• Neural Networks [19]
• Decision Trees [20]

This research focused on using Problem Transformation Methods due to the specific struc-

constant. An approach with single classifiers allows tailoring methods to the labels, with-
out hindering the performance as a whole. With this in mind, two Problem Transformation
Methods will be analyzed in the remainder of this section, Binary Relevance, and Classi-

Binary Relevance (BR)
In Binary Relevance, L classifiers are trained, and the output consists of aggregating the
labels positively predicted by all independent binary classifiers[15].
Figure 4.1: Binary Relevance Model, image taken from [22]
The main draw-back in problem transformation methods, specifically the Binary Method,
is that L models, one per label, have to be trained, which requires resources and time if
the number of labels present is great. However, for an application like Business Theme
prediction, it seems feasible to use Problem Transformation Methods as the number of
labels is small compared to the number of examples present in our training set. Another
issue is that we tend to lose relationships between the various labels by utilizing this
method[15]. On the other hand, the method is simpler and more intuitive and can be
relevant in applications where label relationships may change over time[11].
Given the relatively small amount of labels in our application, training a single classifier
for Business Theme is not a deal-breaker. An improvement on Binary Relevance can
be implemented to address label dependence and relationships, and that is Classifier
Chains.
Classifier Chains (CC)
The Classifier Chain model is described by Read et al. as follows 4.2: ”The Classifier
Chains model (CC) involves L binary transformations—one for each label— as in BR. In
this sense, CC is also a binary relevance method, but it is different from BR in that the
attribute space for each binary model is extended with the 0/1 label predictions of select,

or all previous classifiers, thus forming a classifier chain.” Utilizing this model, the Nth
classifier can base its predictions not only on the input variable but also on potentially all
previous N −1 predictions.
Figure 4.2: Classifier Chains, image taken froom [23]
In Figure 4.2 we see the two phases of a Machine Learning Model’s development, train-
ing, and testing. In classifier chains, every single model is trained not only on the input
features, which in our case is the text, but also on the true assignments of the previous
model’s target values. In prediction, on the other hand, predicted values from the previous
classifiers are utilized.
The Classifier Chain architecture proved to be able to compete with other modern multi-
label methods in terms of predictive performance [11]. However, the ordering of the labels
in the chain has been proven to affect accuracy, as stated by both Read et al. [11] and
Cheng et al. [24]. An apparent issue with chain ordering is error propagation [25], as
classifiers are trained on real target values and tested on classifier predictions. Therefore
errors at a part of the chain propagate down the chain causing further miss-classifications.
Chain order is, unfortunately, a question of combinatorial complexity as the number of
possible orders equates to L!, where L indicates the number of labels present in our
Multi-Label Problem.
Many approaches for determining the optimal ordering of the labels have been proposed.
It was expected that ordering classifiers in a descending fashion based on their single
performance, to mitigate the effect of error propagation would improve performance; how-
ever, the results were not satisfactory. Subsequently, other approaches were advanced,
for example, Genetic Algorithms [26] and Probabilistic Classifier Chains (PCC)
However, given the time and computational constraints of this research, one approach, in
particular, proposed by Zaragoza et al. [27] proved to be the most applicable. Most re-
search promotes structuring the chain by individuating label dependencies discovered in
the data [11]. Following this reasoning in their paper Bayesian Chain Classifiers for Mul-
tidimensional Classification Zaragoza et al. propose a method composed of two phases:

• Creation of a Bayesian Network that expresses dependencies between labels
• Creation of multiple chain classifiers, consistent with the order portrayed by the
Bayesian Network
As David Heckerman defines it in his paper A Tutorial on Learning with Bayesian Net-
works: ”A Bayesian Network is a graphical model that encodes probabilistic relationships
among variables of interest [28], finding causal relationships between them.”
This method builds a non-fully connected Chain Classifier, i.e., not all N −1 previous pre-
dictions are used as input features for the Nth classifier. In doing so, label dependencies
are taken into consideration, although at the same time disregarding labels that can be
considered to be independent one from the other. This provides a simpler architecture to
the model, both in terms of comprehension and development.

Text Cleaning
Our features consist of unstructured data, text, which must be converted into a structured
feature space when using mathematical modeling as part of a classifier [34] . As a first
step data needs to be cleaned so that feature extraction can be applied. However, some
of our documents were not in English (the language used for over 80% of the documents).
While there are models trained to classify multilingual text [35], I decided to translate text
by identifying the language with the package langdetect [36] and translating it using the
google-translate-api [37]. This choice stems from the basic idea that, while translation is
not perfect for sentence structure, single words are translated efficiently.
As a starting point for our data cleaning and pre-processing, we retrieve the .txt files
created in Section 2.2.3. Alper Kursat Uysal et al. [38] analyze the impact of the three
most common pre-processing steps of text classification, all of which are used in this
paper, they are:
1. Stop-Word removal In The importance of stop word removal on recall values in
text categorization by Silva et al. [39] stopwords are defined as ”the great number
of non-informative words, such as articles, prepositions, and conjunctions”. Through
the removal of stop-words, which don’t by themselves characterize the text, the ac-
curacy of the text mining should not decrease. At the same time, the dimensionality
of the text feature space is significantly reduced [40]. The stop word removal was
accomplished with the NLTK package in Python [41].
2. Lowercase conversion All words in the text are turned into lower case words, and
according to Uysal et al. [42] this improves accuracy and brings further dimension-
ality reduction
3. Symbol removal Every character other than letters and digits is removed as not
relevant to the purposes of our research. [43]
4. Stemming/Lemmatization
Stemming is a process by which word endings or other affixes are removed or mod-
ified so that word forms that differ in non-relevant ways may be merged and treated
as equivalent [44].
Lemmatization (or lemmatization) in linguistics is the process of grouping together
the inflected forms of a word so they can be analyzed as a single item, identified by
the word’s lemma or dictionary form [45].
The two differ as stemming removes the last few characters of the word in a some-
what arbitrary sense. In contrast, lemmatization considers the context and converts
it to the root of the word itself.

24

SMOTE [52]. This method balances class distribution by randomly duplicating examples

from the training set. This makes our training set overall more balanced. Also, by using

5.3.1
Order Determination
As previously stated label dependency was evaluated using the BNLearn Package. A
Structure Learning function is provided, through which a DAG that captures dependencies
between classes can be evaluated. DAG stands for directed acyclic graph through which
distributions are inferred [66]. The function presents an option of imposing a maximum
in-degree, meaning each class is ”fathered/correlated” by only one n other.
The DAG structure can be stored as a series of directed lines between two vertices,
sources, and targets. Starting from this representation of the DAG it is possible to de-
termine the order through the use of Algorithm 2. We can see the reasoning for limiting
the DAG to one father, as with multiple fathers possible, finding a starting label, with no
correlations, would be close to impossible. Therefore no starting point for the chain could
be found. This issue is solved in literature, and the reference paper[24], by picking a
label as a starting point for the DAG evaluation. Multiple structures, and classifiers, are
therefore evaluated, to form a voting ensemble, where each model contributes to the final
classification. However, due to the computational and time constraints of this work, this
was not possible.

NER for Product Classification
This section will analyze what methods were utilized for Product Classification. Feasibility
of the three main methods for NER, from Section 4.2, was analyzed. As we can see from
Figure 3.9 most Products are not assigned more than a handful of times, therefore it was
not possible to train my own models for Machine Learning-based NER. Nonetheless, state
of the art pre-trained models were tested, Spacy [67] and Stanford CRFClassifier [68], with
results that were not encouraging. Products utilized were not seen as entities by the pre-
trained models, likely because these words were not present in any of the instances used
for training.
I was also faced with two issues which I was informed of through continuous communi-
cation with the DPG team:
• Products utilized are not always stated in the Project Document, due to updates to
the Project structure throughout its life cycle. Products are then displayed in sub-
sequent documents, Budget allocations, for example. In this case, certain products
could be assigned in the training set; however, classification from the Project Doc-
ument is impossible due to a lack of the necessary information.
• Some Products are utilized in close to all Projects. Because of this, at times, they
are not assigned even though they are deployed in the Project
Mentioning a product name in the Project Document can be seen as an implicit assign-
ment. Therefore, a simple Dictionary-based method was employed. Two versions of the
text were extracted, one completely lower-cased and one where the casing of words was
maintained.
Each document was hard searched for the Product name (in the un-cased text) or the
Acronym as a self-standing word (minding casing). However, there were challenging
cases, which can be seen in some examples below:
• FIRMS (Fishery Resources Monitoring System)
• SMR - Sistema de Monitoreo de Restauracion (Panama)
• Digital Services Portfolio (DSP) - AgriMarketPlace
• Digital Services Portfolio (DSP) - Cure and Feed your Livestock
Three main challenges were individuated with a manual inspection of the Product list,
namely:
• Some Product Acronyms consist of words that have a meaning in the English lan-
guage. This would lead our algorithm to miss-classify many documents due to the
use of the word in the text in a context completely unrelated to the Product itself.
An example of this is FIRMS which stands for Fishery Resources Monitoring Sys-
tem. This represents the reason why Acronyms are matched, keeping casing into
consideration.
• A few Products are employed in multiple countries and are to be identified depending
on the country of use. SMR in the table above is an example of this
• Finally, in some cases, a broad category of Products is available, with certain as-
pects considered to be self-standing Products. The Digital Services Portfolio, for
example, provides products that deal with a wide range of issues; two of the over
ten examples can be seen in the examples above; AgriMarketPlace and LiveStock.

Future Work and Conclusions
This research is aimed at improving Data Processing procedures for the Food and Agri-
culture Organization Digital Public Goods team through the use of Information Extraction
techniques and Machine-Learning. Projects with Digital components are processed and
key information extracted from Project Documents which outline the purpose and efforts
to be made. Three features were analyzed in depth, Business Themes, which define the
scope of the Project, Digital Innovation Themes which state the Digital Components em-
ployed, and Products, where all FAO provided Products used in the Project Life cycle are
stated.
The work was carried out in two main areas.
Firstly, an extensive exploration of the
underlying data identifying key issues was carried out to evaluate applicable solutions.
Secondly, solutions ready for integration in the data pipeline were developed where pos-
sible. While this does not represent a finished product and should always be validated
by human supervision, it represents a clear aid to the DPG Team. Using these methods,
not only is processing time reduced, but a more objective classification can be obtained.
Defining the scope of a Project, for example, is extremely subjective , as multiple scopes
are likely to be present at the same time, with some representing primary objectives and
others being secondary. As there are no clear guidelines at present, hopefully, by provid-
ing suggestions for Project classification, a more objective classification will be possible,
further improving the model itself.
While the main objectives of the research have been met, I believe the proposed methods
can be improved, mainly through the acquisition of further data and a more objective
evaluation of the target variables on behalf of the DPG Team.
A great deal of research is nowadays focused on using Deep Learning methods in Nat-
ural Language Processing. Over time the training set at our disposal will roughly double
annually, with Neural Networks becoming a feasible option for both text vectorization and
classification in this application. Pre-trained models for Named Entity Recognition could
be tailored for Product Assignment as well as state-of-the-art Deep Learning Text Classi-
fication Models for Business and Digital Innovation Theme prediction.
The Classifier Chain structure analyzed throughout this work is greatly simplified due to
the challenges of applying it with limited data and the computational resources needed.
However, I believe that this venue, as well, could be further analyzed in the future, as
through acquiring more data, label dependencies could become a more significant factor
in Project Classification.
Finally, in my opinion, processing could also be improved by providing all Project Orga-
nizers with a standard format first page outline for the Project Document. Thus, essential
information could be retrieved from standardized locations, further speeding up the pro-
cess. Examples of helpful information for the task analyzed in this work could be a list of
FAO Products utilized as well as a summary of the efforts being made. At the moment
the entire document is being used for our classification; however, being able to work with
a more defined and clearer summary would greatly decrease both training and prediction
time.
At this time no other applications of Machine Learning techniques are present at any level
of the Food and Agriculture Organization. I believe that this thesis represents a starting
48
NLP for FAO Projects

point for the use of these methods as a core component of the data processing life cycle.
Hopefully, the proof of concept portrayed in this work, will showcase the added value of
automation techniques, sparking an organization wide evaluation of possible use cases.
In doing so crucial time can be saved, leaving more resources for the key objective of the
organization, defeating world hunger.
