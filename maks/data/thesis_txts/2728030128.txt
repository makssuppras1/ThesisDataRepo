Abstract
One promising avenue for effectively addressing the challenges of escalating costs and deterio­
rating outcomes of healthcare systems involves leveraging the potential of Artificial Intelligence
(AI). The ability to process vast amounts of data efficiently and accurately opens up new pos­
sibilities for early disease detection, precise diagnoses, and personalized treatment plans. AI
has the potential to bring about significant improvements in healthcare, but it also raises various
ethical, legal, and regulatory concerns. The AI community has been advocating for policies to
address these issues, as there is a growing concern about legal uncertainty in areas impacted by
technological advancements.
This study focuses on the sociotechnical aspects of AI in healthcare and explores the potential
risks and challenges that come with its implementation. The study specifically examines how
the EU AI Act, the world’s first large­scale initiative to regulate AI, addresses these challenges
and ensures the trustworthy use of AI in healthcare. The EU AI Act aims to reduce risks related
to ethics, society, and safety in AI applications by emphasizing the need for trustworthy AI to
protect fundamental rights. Based on the findings of this study, it cannot be expected that the EU
AI Act will guarantee an entirely trustworthy implementation of AI in healthcare. Fundamental
shortcomings lie in the lack of trustworthy requirements, accountability, and explainability in
the proposal, which are crucial for fostering trust in AI in healthcare.

Introduction
The escalating costs and deteriorating outcomes of healthcare systems worldwide pose a com­
plex and significant challenge to society. As the global population ages, countries face the
pressing need to adapt their health and social systems to meet the demands of this demographic
shift. According to World Health Organization (WHO) [1], all nations face great challenges in
preparing for the increasing number of elderly individuals in their populations. The aging popu­
lation presents a multifaceted challenge that impacts various aspects of healthcare systems. First,
the elderly tend to have a higher prevalence of chronic diseases and complex health conditions,
leading to increased utilization of healthcare and associated costs. In light of these challenges,
the exploration of innovative solutions has become imperative to ensure the sustainability and
effectiveness of healthcare services.
One promising avenue to address these challenges involves exploiting the power of AI, par­
ticularly through the application of machine learning techniques [2]. AI has demonstrated the
potential to transform healthcare by augmenting the capabilities of medical professionals and
enhancing the quality of care provided to patients. Different AI techniques enable computers to
learn from and analyze vast amounts of data, assisting healthcare professionals in the decision­
making process [2]. The ability to process this data efficiently and accurately opens up new
possibilities for early disease detection, precise diagnoses, and personalized treatment plans tai­
lored to individual patients. Through the analysis of comprehensive patient data, AI can aid
medical professionals in identifying subtle disease markers and trends that might otherwise go
unnoticed. As a result, patients may experience improved health outcomes and a higher quality
of life.
The integration of AI systems into clinical practice has sparked considerable discussion sur­
rounding the concept of trustworthiness. While AI holds great promise in advancing healthcare,
it also raises important ethical challenges that demand attention. Concerns about data privacy,
security, and ownership arise when using sensitive patient data for training and testing AI sys­
tems. Safeguarding patient confidentiality and ensuring that data is used responsibly and se­
curely is paramount to building public trust in AI­driven healthcare. Furthermore, deploying
AI in healthcare should not exacerbate existing health disparities or create new ones. Efforts
should be made to ensure that AI technologies are accessible to all, regardless of socioeconomic
status or demographic factors. Examples of bias and unfairness in AI systems have heated the
discussion about the trustworthy implementation of AI in healthcare.
Gender disparities can be exacerbated by unbalanced algorithms. A popular example of gender
bias in healthcare is the immense misdiagnosing of heart attacks in women [3]. Prediction mod­

els for cardiovascular disease, which purport to predict heart attacks years before they occur,
are often trained on datasets that are predominantly by males. Given that cardiovascular dis­
ease presents differently in women and men, models trained on male­dominated data sets may
yield less accuracy in diagnosing women. Another notable example pertains to an algorithm
developed by Optum that helps hospital networks identify patients requiring additional care [4].
However, significant racial bias has been found in this algorithm. The algorithm used health
costs to predict and rank which patients would benefit from supplementary care. Nevertheless,
researchers claim that using healthcare costs as a proxy for health needs yields biased results,
as black patients frequently incur lower healthcare expenditures than White patients [4]. There­
fore, the algorithm erroneously determined that black patients were healthier than equally sick
white patients.
The examples above address some of the potential risks and challenges of AI in healthcare.
While AI in healthcare has the potential to revolutionize healthcare it also presents different eth­
ical, legal, and regulatory challenges. The AI community has consistently advocated for policy
intervention concerning AI, and there is a voice of criticism regarding the expanding legal void
in every domain impacted by technology advancements [5, 6]. The founder of Tesla, SpaceX,
Neuralink, and The Boring Company, Elon Musk, supports AI regulation and emphasizes the
importance of being proactive:
“AI is the rare case where I think we need to be proactive in regulation instead of
reactive. Because I think by the time we are reactive in AI regulation, it’ll be too
late. (...) AI is a fundamental risk to the existence of human civilization.” [7]
Policymakers around the world are beginning to discuss AI policy challenges [5]. Major powers
such as China, the US, and the European Union (EU) have launched important strategies to pro­
mote the responsible development of AI while maintaining sustained economic competitiveness
[5, 8]. While China and the US have released national strategies and guidelines for AI [9, 10],
the European Commission proposes a legislative proposal that addresses the risk of AI and aims
for Europe to play a leading role globally [11]. This legislation is also known as the European
Union Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act) or Artificial Intelligence Act (AIA).
The purpose of the regulatory proposal is to provide developers, deployers, and users with pre­
cise requirements and obligations regarding specific uses of AI [12], including AI in healthcare.
They are aiming to ensure trustworthy AI, which is AI that is lawful, ethical, and robust. Ad­
ditionally, it needs to respect fundamental rights and values. The regulatory framework aims
to promote innovation while ensuring patient safety and privacy is protected. The regulatory
framework proposes a risk­based approach to the development and deployment of AI. Higher­
risk AI models would be subject to more stringent requirements such as third­party conformity
assessments and data quality.

1.1
Scope of the Thesis
In this thesis, I wish to investigate socio­technical aspects of AI in healthcare including aspects
that relate to both technical­scientific elements and how society and its stakeholder perceive
and react to technology and its uses. The primary stakeholder, in this case, includes citizens and
patients/patient organizations, politicians and regulators, developers, and industry.
Additionally, I wish to investigate how the world’s first large­scale initiative to control the risks
of AI, the EU AI Act on AI, will cope with the threats and challenges that developments and
applications of AI in healthcare are posing or will be posing. My scope will be on selected
key sociotechnical aspects of the planned EU regulation, namely its attempt to establish the
acceptability of AI applications on elements of trustworthiness. Outside the chosen scope will
be other important aspects of the AI applications in healthcare such as legal issues about privacy
and security issues, commercial and financial issues, or the use of algorithms in social media.
More precisely, the goals of this thesis are to 1) identify and describe the main socio­technical
challenges of developing and applying AI in healthcare, 2) to explore stakeholders’ perspectives
on the planned EU regulation of AI, the EU AI Act, and 3) investigate whether the EU AI Act
can be expected to ensure trustworthy implementation of AI applications in healthcare.
The EU AI Act is still under development and amendments to the original proposal[12], are
still being made. Therefore, the findings of this study could inform healthcare policymakers
and stakeholders about the opportunities and challenges of the EU AI Act in healthcare and
contribute to the ongoing discourse on the ethical and responsible use of AI technologies in
healthcare.
1.1.1
Research Questions
1. What are the key socio­technical challenges of developing and implementing AI in health­
care?
2. Given that trustworthiness forms the cornerstone of the EU AI Act, are there additional
requirements of trustworthiness that have not been duly considered?
3. To what extent is the EU AI Act ensuring the trustworthiness of AI applications in health­
care?

1.3
Outline of the Thesis
The first part of this study delves into the literature on AI and machine learning, with a particular
focus on their applications in the healthcare domain. The first part of this research aims to
elucidate the growing significance of trustworthiness in AI. The following is to understand the
EU AI Act, how this framework will regulate AI, and how this will ensure trustworthiness.
Understanding this, the EU AI Act is analyzed based on stakeholders’ perspectives, but also
in relation to the concept of trustworthiness. Finally, I discuss the proposal’s ability to provide
trustworthy AI implementation in healthcare.

Methodology
In the following chapter, the methodologies used in the compilation of this thesis are presented
and described. The structure of this chapter follows the layers of the research onion [13]; re­
search purpose and philosophy, approaches, strategies, choices, and data collection methods.
The aim of this chapter is to provide the reader with an understanding of the methodological
choices made in this thesis as well as an understanding of the thesis process.
2.1
The Research Onion
The research onion is a framework used to illustrate the various stages of the research process
and was developed by Saunders et al. [13]. The research onion consists of six main layers;
Philosophy, Approach, Strategy, Time Horizon, Data Collection, and Data Analysis. Each of
the layers represents a different aspect of the research process.
2.1.1
Purpose and Philosophy
The first layer of the onion is the research philosophy, which is the foundation of the study. In
this thesis, the philosophy of pragmatism is used. As the research questions include different
aspects and purposes, this pragmatism philosophy was found appropriate.
There exist three different types of research that are common: descriptive, exploratory, and
explanatory [13]. In this thesis, a combination of descriptive and exploratory methods has been
used. Part of the thesis seeks to describe AI in healthcare together with the associated challenges
and risks. The aim is to provide a comprehensive description of AI applications in the healthcare
domain, including their benefits, challenges, and potential risks. Furthermore, a part of this
thesis explores the concept of trustworthy AI in healthcare care, which is still in its early research
stages. Exploring trustworthiness seeks to gain insight and develop a better understanding of the
subject according to healthcare. This exploratory research includes a literature study to explore
different dimensions of trustworthy AI, identifying key challenges and concerns. Finally, a part
of this thesis is exploring the regulatory framework on AI, the EU AI Act, with a focus on the
healthcare domain. I examine how the high­risk AI system requirements relate to the healthcare
sector and their relation to trustworthiness.
This combination of research purposes allows for gaining a comprehensive understanding of
the EU AI Act’s impact on healthcare while providing theoretical insights into the principles of
trustworthy AI.
2.1.2
Research Approach and Strategy
The second layer of the research onion is the research approach which involves selecting the
overall approach to conducting the research, which can be deductive or inductive. For this thesis,

The third layer of the research onion is the strategy of the research. This layer focuses on the

2.1.3
Choice of Methods
The fourth layer of the research onion focuses on the selection of appropriate methods for gath­
ering data. This layer is about choosing between using qualitative or quantitative methods to
collect data. In my research, I have partly used qualitative methods in the form of interviews,
to collect and analyze data. Interviews are particularly suited for exploring the experiences,
perspectives, and insights of individuals involved in the EU AI Act.
Additionally, a sociotechnical method is used to compare the high­risk AI requirements in the EU
AI Act with scientific articles and methods inside trust in technology. This approach considers
both social and technical factors when examining systems or technologies. It acknowledges
that technology and society are deeply interconnected, and any AI solution must be understood
within its social content. This was deemed important as the goal of the EU AI Act is to foster
trust in AI while promoting innovation. This aim was to investigate to what extent the EU AI
Act complies with existing literature on trustworthy AI. Lastly, in my analysis of explainability
as a cornerstone of trust in AI I apply conceptual analysis.
2.1.4
Time Horizon
The fifth layer of the research onion is the time horizon for the research. The time horizon of my
research was constrained, meaning that I had limitations in terms of the duration of the study.
Therefore, I adopted a cross­sectional time horizon for qualitative data collection in this thesis.
A cross­sectional time horizon involves collecting data at a specific point in time. In my case, I
conducted interviews and collected qualitative data during a specific period, rather than over an
extended period of time. By using a cross­sectional time horizon, I aimed to capture a picture
of the participant’s perspectives and experiences related to the EU AI Act. With the proposal
still under development, this approach allowed me to gain insights into the current state of af­
fairs, opinions, and experiences. However, the choice of approach and the legislation are still in
progress, this does not allow for observing changes or developments after the implementation
of the regulation.

3.1.1
Difference between AI and Machine Learning
As the definition of AI in section 3.1 supported, AI is the broad term that refers to the use of
technologies to build systems that can imitate human intelligence. Machine learning is a subset
of AI that automatically make a system capable to learn and improve from past experiences.
Machine learning uses algorithms to analyze vast amounts of data, learn from data and make
decisions based on these learnings. Machine learning algorithms also have the ability to improve
their performance concurrently with time as they are trained and exposed to more data. [18]
There exist classic approaches to AI, also known as rule­based AI. Rule­based AI is based on
the idea that a hand­crafted model of the domain is necessary in order for the system to make
sense of the input data and generate any useful results [15]. It relies on explicit programming
and predefined rules to solve a specific problem. These systems are difficult to develop since
they require domain expertise and do not have the ability to adapt to changing environments.
The classic approach to AI does, however, provide a higher level of transparency in the decision­
making and explanatory capability compared to machine learning models [15].
On the other hand, machine learning is based on algorithms and statistical models which learn
from data and make predictions without any explicit programming. It is expected to derive a
model and learn how to perform a specific task [15]. Through training processes, the model
learns patterns and relationships in the data and adjusts the model accordingly.
In healthcare, we will see examples of both classic AI and machine learning. However, ma­
chine learning has gained significant popularity due to its ability to handle large and complex

3.2.1
Unsupervised Machine Learning Algorithms
Unsupervised machine learning algorithms are used to analyze large amounts of data, which is
unlabelled. The idea of unsupervised learning is to group data into smaller groups, which is also
known as clustering. Clustering is a data mining technique that groups data based on similarities
or differences. The literature distinguishes between soft clustering and hard clustering [23].
Hard clustering is when data points belong to only one cluster, whereas data points belonging to
more clusters are called soft clustering. Some popular unsupervised learning algorithms are K­
Means, K­Medoids, and Hierarchical Clustering. These three algorithms are referred to as hard
clustering algorithms. Some popular soft clustering algorithms can be Fuzzy c­Means, Gaussian
Mixture Model, and Hidden Markov Model. In healthcare, these algorithms have been used to
predict heart disease, mental health prediction, analysis of patient satisfaction perception, etc.
[23].
3.2.2
Supervised Machine Learning Algorithms
Supervised learning trains a model to make predictions based on this labeled training data. The
training data consists of input examples (features) and their corresponding desired output (tar­
gets). The goal is to make the model able to map new and unseen inputs to their correct outputs
[24, 23]. In healthcare classical supervised machine learning application includes prediction of
heart attack, medical image analysis, and speech recognition [23]. Some of the most well­known
classification algorithms are K­Nearest Neighbors [25], Naïve Bayes [26], Decision Tree [27],
and Support Vector Machines [28]. These algorithms have been utilized in healthcare for heart
disease prediction, hear disease prediction, skin disease detection, heart disease detection, and
heart disease diagnosis [23].
Another approach to supervised learning is regression. Regression algorithms are able to fore­
cast an outcome’s specific value instead of classifying the data [23]. As in classification, the data
is split into testing and training. The target outcome is predicted by some selected algorithms
such as linear regression, logistic regression, ensemble methods, and support vector regression
(SVR). Examples of these algorithms used in healthcare include linear regression to optimize
and utilize healthcare resources and logistic regression to predict health­related behavior[23].
Ensemble methods have been used to predict patients’ weekly average expenditures on certain
pain medications, and SVR has been used to visualize and predict the COVID­19 outbreak [23].

3.3.1
Seven Risk of Machine Learning in Medicine and Healthcare
Based on existing literature The European Parliamentary Research Service (EPRS) has identified

1. Patient harm due to AI errors: Algorithm errors can lead to different problems such as
false negatives in the form of missed diagnoses of life­threatening diseases, unnecessary
treatments due to false positives where healthy persons are incorrectly classified as dis­

prioritization of interventions in emergency departments.
The study describes 3 causes for AI errors leading to the above medical consequences,
which include noise and artifacts in AI’s clinical inputs and measurements, data shift
between AI training and real­world data, and unexpected variations in clinical context
and environments. Misclassification of patients may appear due to dataset shift. It is a
common problem in machine learning when the statistical distribution of the data used in
clinical practice is shifted from the original distribution used to train the model.
2. The misuse of medical AI tools: AI tools are dependent on their correct usage of them in
practice by clinicians, healthcare professionals, and patients. Using the tools in an incor­
rect way can result in incorrect medical assessment and decision­making which have po­
tential harm to the patient. The potential causes of misuse of AI applications in healthcare
are due to the limited involvement of clinicians and citizens in the development process,

4.2
Trustworthiness in Artificial Intelligence
In recent years, words like trust, trustworthiness, and trustworthy have gained significant pop­
ularity in discussions of AI. Today’s systems are very efficient and complex due to the vast
amount of data available, advanced algorithms, and extreme computer power, and it has be­
come more difficult to interpret the logic of the systems [35]. This limitation of explainable and
understanding of the systems results in ethical issues and challenges. But what does it mean for
a system to be trustworthy? And is it even possible to trust AI?
Trust and trustworthiness are often used interchangeably, but categorically the two words are
distinct [36]. Trust is an attitude, whereas trustworthiness is a quality in the object of this attitude
that satisfies it and helps make it appropriate [36]. According to Cambridge Dictionary, trust is
defined as follows: ”To believe that someone is good and honest and will not harm you, or that
something is safe and reliable” [37]. And trustworthiness is defined as: ”The quality or fact of
being trustworthy ­ able to be trusted” [37].
The concept of trustworthy AI is not a monolithic concept [35], and not a single definition ex­
ists. The interpretation, relevance, and implementation of trustworthy AI depend on the domain
where the AI system is applied and used. Researchers, organizations, and government agencies
have proposed various guidelines and frameworks to develop trustworthy AI [38, 39, 40]. To
avert inconsistency a study from 2019 found there to be an emerging convergence around five
ethical principles: transparency, justice, and fairness, non­maleficence, responsibility, and pri­
vacy [33, 34]. I have selected the EU framework of Trustworthy AI [38] that includes all of
these five principles. The AI HLEG set by the EC produced ethics guidelines for trustworthy
AI in April 2019 [38]. The framework is summarized in figure 4.1.
According to AI HLEG, trustworthy AI exists of three components:
Lawful ­ respecting all applicable laws and regulations
Ethical ­ respecting ethical principles and values
Robust ­ both from a technical perspective while taking into account its social environment
Based on fundamental rights AI HLEG identified and defined four ethical principles consisting
of 1) Respect for human autonomy, 2) prevention of harm, 3) Fairness, and 4) Explicability.
These ethical principles must be respected to ensure that AI systems are developed, deployed,
and used in a trustworthy way [38]. The first principle regarding respect for human autonomy is
ensuring that the AI system is complementing humans and not replacing them. The principle of
prevention of harm makes sure that the AI system performs as intended and does not cause any

damage to society. The principle of fairness ensures that all social groups are treated equally
without any discrimination. The last principle, explicability, is ensuring that the AI system is
transparent, interpretable and explainable.
Further, AI HLEG put forward a set of seven requirements that AI systems should fulfill in order

1. Human agency and oversight

4.2.1
Respect for Human Control
The AI HLEG has proposed human agency and oversight as one of the seven primary require­
ments for making AI trustworthy. Humans should be able to override decisions made by the
Master Thesis

==PAGE:29==
AI system, set limits for performance, and flag and correct errors [35]. The combination of hu­
mans’ cognitive ability and immense computing power is entering a new era of AI. Including
a human­centered approach in the requirements of trustworthy AI ensures that AI systems are
developed to empower human beings instead of replacing them. Throughout an AI system’s life
cycle, different types of users or stakeholders exist. The paper ”Trustworthy Artificial Intelli­
gence: A review” [35] describes five different types of stakeholders involved in the AI system
which satisfy different purposes:
• Type 1: Data Scientists, Researchers, and Developers. They are involved in the design
and development, and improving the system by increasing performance, correcting errors,
and adding new functionalities.
• Type 2: Users who are affected by the decision made by the AI system. This type should
be able to understand the reasoning that led to a specific decision.
• Type 3: Domain experts. This could be doctors at a hospital using the AI system to assist
their decision­making process.
• Type 4: Policymakers and regulatory bodies. This type is ensuring the system complies
with ethical and moral principles.
• Type 5: Managers and company board members. They oversee AI system development
and asses its use and application in the real world.
All of these five different types of stakeholders are involved in different levels of the life cycle.
The AI HLEG has proposed human participation in three phases to manage the involvement of
the different types of humans and make AI trustworthy [38]: human­on­the­loop, human­in­the­
loop, and human­in­command:
• Human­on­the­loop refers to the capability for human intervention during the design
phase of the life cycle. Humans are involved in planning, designing, and making require­
ments for the system. The types of human involved in this phase could be developers,
domain experts, policymakers, and users.
• Human­in­the­loop refers to the capability for human intervention in the development
phase of the life cycle. Humans are involved in data collection, model development, test­
ing, and deployment of the system. Data scientists and developers often do data collection,
preprocessing, model development, and model evaluation.
• Human­in­command refers to the capability to oversee the overall activity of the system
and the ability to decide when and how to use the system in any particular situation. Users
or domain experts give feedback and override the decisions when needed. Policymakers
make policies to govern the working of AI systems.

By incorporating these three phases of human involvement, AI HLEG recognizes the importance
of striking a balance between human oversight and AI technologies. This approach ensures
that humans remain in control and maintain accountability. It ensures that the development
and deployment of AI systems align with human values, promote transparency, and foster trust
between humans and AI.
4.2.2
Prevention of Harm
Privacy and data governance are closely linked to the principle of prevention of harm. Privacy
is a fundamental right that is particularly affected by AI systems [38]. The requirement of data
governance covers the quality and integrity of the data used in AI systems. The systems must
guarantee privacy and data protection including the information initially provided by the user
and the information generated about the user over the course of their interaction with the system
[38]. This requirement is outside of the chosen scope, and will not be described in greater detail.
4.2.3
Fairness
Empowering AI systems in society and the growing impact on our daily lives it has become
more important than ever to ensure the systems used are fair. A system should be free of bias
and discrimination. The system is fair if it does not harm or discriminate against any individual
or group in society.
The concept of fairness is very complex and multifaceted, and there exist more than 21 defini­
tions in the literature [41]. Fairness can be interpreted differently depending on social beliefs,
culture, and individual perspectives which leads to various definitions of fairness. In the scope
of this thesis, the approach to fairness will be from an algorithmic perspective. The nascent
field of algorithm fairness in healthcare aims to ensure the decision­making process is non­
discriminatory and equitable.
Proposed definitions for fairness are typically divided into either individual fairness or group
fairness. The principle of individual fairness is treating similar individuals similarly. Any two
individuals who are similar with respect to a specific task should get similar predictions [42,
35]. Popular definitions referring to individual fairness are ’fairness through awareness [43]’
and ’fairness through unawareness’[44]. Group fairness ensures that different groups in society
are treated equally and without any discrimination [42]. Group fairness is explained in greater
detail in the following section.
Group fairness
There exist various definitions and approaches dealing with group fairness: Demographic parity
[45], Statistical parity [43], Equalized odds [46], Equalized opportunity [45], and Predictive
parity [47] are among many other definitions dealing with group fairness.

Fairness in machine learning is a critical concern, aiming to ensure equitable outcomes for all
individuals. However, achieving fairness requires addressing the presence of bias in the data, al­
gorithms, and decision­making processes. Bias refers to the systematic deviations or prejudices
that can lead to unfair treatment or discriminatory outcomes.
Types of Harm According to Bias
Friedman and Nissenbaum define bias in computer systems as a system ”that systematically
and unfairly discriminate against certain individuals or groups of individuals in favor of others”
[48]. The literature often distinguishes between statistical and social bias [49]. Statistical bias
is characterized by situations in which the distribution of the data set utilized to train a machine­
learning model does not accurately reflect the true distribution of the population. This might lead
to an over­representation or under­representation of certain groups within the model. Social bias
refers to inequalities caused by systematic favoritism or discrimination of a certain group based
on race, gender, age, etc. Social bias can manifest within algorithms if the data used for training
machine learning models reflect societal biases such as discriminatory patterns, these biases can
be perpetuated and amplified by the algorithms. This is called algorithmic bias, see definition
4.2.3.
Definition 4.2.1: Algorithmic Bias
In the context of AI and healthcare, algorithmic bias is defined as; the instances when the
application of an algorithm compounds existing inequities in socioeconomic status, race,
ethnic background, religion, gender, disability, or sexual orientation to amplify them and
adversely impact inequities in health systems. [50]
Flawed outcomes from AI systems can have roots in many different problems. These flawed
outcomes or incorrect results can result in discrimination against certain groups of patients.
This section will describe three key problems: 1) Measurement Errors or Measurement Bias, 2)
Selection Bias, and 3) Feedback Loop Bias [51].
Measurement errors can be defined as ”the difference between the actual quantity of interest
and the measured value” [51]. Measurements errors are often a result of poor data quality. The
data there is used to train machine learning models can have missing and incorrect information
and this can lead to inferior performance of the model. Minorities and economically disadvan­
taged individuals are more likely to be vulnerable to incomplete datasets [51].
Selection bias is the bias that can occur if the subset of individuals represented in the training
data is not representative of the target population. It arises when the data used for training
Master Thesis
26
==PAGE:35==
the model comes from a health system that only serves a particular population (e.g. white and
wealthy people). The predictions made by the model might not be generalizable to all patients
of interest then. [51]
Feedback loop bias also referred to as historical bias has the ability to arise in machine learning
even if the data used for training is perfectly measured and sampled. Even if the model reflects
the real world perfectly it could prevent harm to society and patients [30]. For example, some
patients may receive less health care because of their demographic characteristics instead of
their medical needs [51].
Existing methods to mitigate bias
Bias is unavoidable, but the goal is to mitigate bias to minimize the presence of it within datasets
and in models. Different methods and open­source toolkits have been developed and published.
IBM has published a toolkit that can help to examine, report, and mitigate discrimination and
bias in machine learning models throughout the lifecycle [52]. The toolkit is an open source
Python toolkit for algorithmic fairness called AI Fairness 360. The goal of AI 360 is to support
a deeper understanding of fairness metrics and mitigation techniques [52]. The toolkit includes
an extensive set of metrics for datasets and models to test for biases and algorithms to mitigate
bias. The toolkit also includes a description of the metrics. Microsoft has also developed a tool
called Fairlearn [53], where the goal is to empower data scientists and developers to assess and
improve the fairness of their AI systems.
4.2.4
Explicability
Explicability means that processes need to be transparent, the capabilities and purpose of AI sys­
tems openly communicated, and decisions, to the extent possible ­ explainable to those, directly
and indirectly, affected [38].
Explainability
Toward trustworthy AI in healthcare, a reasonable step is to develop transparent and explain­
able AI systems [54, 38]. Despite the fact that AI systems have outperformed human beings
in analytical tasks, the lack of explainability of these systems prevents them from gaining trust
[55]. The lack of explainability and transparency within AI systems is also known as the ’black
box problem’ [56]. The black­box problem in AI refers to the problem that occurs whenever
a system’s behavior cannot be explained. And this ’black­box problem’ is a huge debate point
when discussing whether AI must be explainable to be applied and used in medicine [57]. There
exists no distinct technical definition of explainability, but the General Data Protection Regula­
tion laws in the EU express that all individuals have the right to ”meaningful information about
the logic behind automated decisions using their data” [57, 58]
Explainability is defined, according to the paper [59]:, as ”An AI system is explainable if the
task model is intrinsically interpretable (here the AI system is the task model) or if the non­
interpretable task model is complemented with an interpretable and faithful explanation (here
Master Thesis
27
==PAGE:36==
the AI system also contains a post­hoc explanation)”. While interpretability is defined as the
ability to explain or present AI systems in an understandable way to humans [59].
In the literature, existing explainable AI methods can be categorized in two terms [57]: 1) Post­
hoc explainability and 2) Ante­hoc explainability. Post­hoc is a method to explain the black
box of a model. It is a retrospective analysis and interpretation of a trained AI model. The
approach extracts meaningful explanations of the outputs after completion of a task [57]. Ante­
hoc seeding explainability into the model from the start, and this requires building a new learning
model. However, most of the current AI systems are too complex to be understandable. This
includes systems capable of analyzing text, image, and sound [57].

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act
The previous chapters provided comprehensive knowledge within AI, machine learning in gen­
eral, but also with respect to the healthcare domain. The aim of the previous chapters was to
investigate why AI should be regulated with an explicit focus on healthcare. This chapter will
highlight the most important details of the regulation, the EU AI Act. Subsequently, the ele­
ments that has an impact on healthcare will be highlighted as well.
5.1
Highlights of the EU AI Act
President von der Leyen announced in her political guidelines for the 2019­2024 Commission
that the European Commission would put forward legislation for a coordinated European ap­
proach to the ethical implications of AI [12]. In conjunction with this announcement, on 19
February 2020, the Commission published the ”White Paper on AI ­ A European Approach to
Excellence and Trust” [63], which sets out policy options on how to achieve the twin objective:
1) promoting the uptake of AI, 2) while addressing the risk associated with AI. Recognizing the
legal regulatory framework is adequate this thesis will present and highlight the most essential
parts of the proposal.
The EU AI ACT proposal aims to implement the second objective, which is risks, in order to
develop an ecosystem that is trusted [12]. Therefore this proposal, which is based on EU values
and fundamental rights, aims to propose a legal framework to achieve trustworthy AI. The
regulation applies to a) providers placing on the market or putting into service AI systems in
the Union, irrespective of whether those providers are established within the Union or in a third
country, b) users of AI systems located within the Union, and c) providers and users of AI
systems that are located in a third country, where the output produced by the system is used in
the Union.
With the proposed regulatory framework on AI the Commission stated the following specific
objectives [12]:
• Ensure that AI system placed on the Union market and used are safe and respect existing
law on fundamental rights and Union values.
• Ensure legal certainty to facilitate investment and innovation in AI.
• Enhance governance and effective enforcement of existing law on fundamental rights and
safety requirements applicable to AI systems.
• Facilitate the development of a single market for lawful, safe, and trustworthy AI appli­
cations and prevent market fragmentation.

The Commission states that the proposal requires full consistency with existing Union legis­
lation. Further, consistency is also ensured with the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights and
the Union legislation on data protection, consumer protection, non­discrimination, and gender
equality. The commission also emphasizes that this regulatory framework complements exist­

5.1.1
A Risk­based Approach to Regulation
The European Commission proposed a risk­based approach to regulation on AI. The regulatory
framework classifies AI system into different risk categories based on their potential impact and
associated risks. The varying level of risk and thereby the level of requirements and obligations
depend on the specific AI system.

The EU AI Act established four risk categories for AI systems: 1) unacceptable risk, 2) high
risk, 3) limited risk, and 4) minimal risk, also depicted in figure 5.1. AI system that contradicts
EU values is prohibited including subliminal manipulation resulting in physical/psychological
harm, exploitation of children or mentally disabled person resulting in physical/psycholog­
ical harm, general purpose social scoring such as classifying people based on their social be­

The goal of the risk­based approach is to ensure that machine learning and other AI applications

5.1.2
High­Risk AI Systems
The main focus of the proposal is AI System that is classified as high­risk. The AI Act focuses
on imposing stricter requirements that underlie this classification. According to the classifica­
tion rules for high­risk AI systems should be considered high risk where both of the following
conditions are fulfilled [12]:
1. The AI system is intended to be used as a safety component of a product, or is itself a
product, covered by the Union harmonization legislation listed in Annex II (see appendix
A.1).
2. The product whose safety component is the AI system itself as a product, is required to
undergo a third­party conformity assessment with a view to the placing on the market or
putting into service of that product pursuant to the Union harmonization legislation listed
in Annex II.
Further, the AI systems listed in Annex III (see appendix A.1) should also be considered high­
risk.
High­risk AI systems will be subject to strict obligations before they can be put on the market in­
cluding adequate risk assessment and mitigation systems, high­quality datasets used for training
to minimize risks and discriminatory outcomes, logging activity to ensure traceability of results,
detailed documentation providing all information necessary on the system and its purpose for
authorities to assess its compliance, clear and adequate information to the user, appropriate hu­
man oversight measures to minimize risk, and have a high level of robustness, security, and
accuracy. [12]
”Those requirements are necessary to effectively mitigate the risks for health, safety, and fun­
damental rights, as applicable in the light of the intended purpose of the system, and no other
less trade restrictive measures are reasonably available, thus avoiding unjustified restrictions
to trade” [12].
Further, the proposal emphasizes that a conformity assessment is necessary for ensuring a high
level of trustworthiness of high­risk systems [12]. Conformity assessment refers to the process
of evaluating and verifying whether a product, system, or process meets the requirements set

Patient Harm due to AI Errors
EPRS suggests three measures for mitigating the risk of patient harm [32]:
1. Comprehensive multi­center evaluation studies to identify instabilities
2. Assistive AI solutions that maintain the clinician part of the workflow
3. Traceable and dynamic AI solution that continues to improve over time
The report emphasizes that AI systems should be designed and implemented as assistive tools to
prevent patient harm. This means, that clinicians remain part of the data processing workflow
to detect and report potential errors and contextual changes. Future AI solutions should be able
to continue to learn from new scenarios and mistakes as they are identified in practice. In the
high­risk AI system requirements, I have identified the three requirements; human oversight,
record­keeping, and accuracy as appropriate mitigation measures for the risk of patient harm.
Human oversight is an important requirement to ensure that clinicians remain an integral part
of the data processing workflow. By involving clinicians in the AI system’s operation, they can
actively detect and report potential errors and contextual changes. This involvement facilitates
ongoing monitoring and intervention by humans enhancing patient safety and prevention of
harm. The requirement of record­keeping further support the prevention of harm to patients.
Article 12, pt. 3 in the proposal [12], requires that:
”logging capabilities shall enable the monitoring of the operating of the high­risk AI system
with respect to the occurrence of situations that may result in the AI system presenting a risk
(...)”.
By maintaining detailed records of AI systems it is possible to ensure a level of traceability.
Moreover, this documentation allows for the identification of mistakes and learning from new
scenarios, enabling continuous improvement of the system. This requirement of record­keeping
suits well with mitigation measure number 3: traceable and dynamic AI solution that continues
to improve over time. Lastly, accuracy is another crucial aspect addressed by the high­risk
AI system requirements in Article 15 [12], including accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity.
According to Article 15, pt. 3:
”high­risk AI systems shall be resilient as regards errors, faults or inconsistencies that may
occur within the system or the environment in which the system operates, in particular, due to
their interaction with natural persons or other systems”.
AI algorithm errors can lead to missed diagnoses of life­threatening diseases, and with the re­

Misuse of Medical AI Tools
EPRS suggests four mitigation measure to the risk of misusing medical AI tools:
1. User­centered design and extensive usability test for the AI algorithms
2. Future integration of AI education and training in medical schools
3. New literacy programs to increase medical AI knowledge in society
4. Better regulation and information on emerging AI tools
In greater detail, the report [32] describes that the end­users such as healthcare professionals,
specialists, technicians, or patients should be closely involved in the design and development of
AI solutions to ensure their points of view and preferences are implemented into the deployed
and used AI systems. According to EPRS, this will help reduce human error and incorrect use of
future medical AI solutions [32]. Remembering the interview with Danske Patienter, section 6.4,
this viewpoint of integrating end­users in the development process was also of high importance.
Additionally, EPRS also points at education and literacy programs on AI to increase the knowl­
edge and skills of future AI end­users and reduce human error [32].
I have identified one requirement in the EU AI Act; human oversight as an appropriate mitiga­
tion measure for the risk of misuse of medical AI tools. As described multiple times, human
oversight can help the active involvement of healthcare professionals and end­users throughout
the lifecycle of the AI system. However, the EU AI Act, does not include any educational re­
quirement of end­users since the requirements in the proposal are established for developers of
high­risk AI systems.
In summary, the connection between the risk of the misuse of medical AI tools and the high­risk
AI requirement of human oversight lies in the need for active involvement and supervision of
healthcare professionals. It is my evaluation that by incorporating human oversight, healthcare
experts can provide necessary checks and balances to prevent incorrect usage of AI systems.
However, there might be a lack in the focusing of end­users, and there still exists a potential risk
of misuse of medical AI tools.

2. Interdisciplinary approaches in medical AI involving social scientist
3. Promotion of more diversity and inclusion in the field of medical AI
I have identified two main requirements in the EU AI Act that try to mitigate the risk of bias

Ensuring that the data used to train AI models is diverse and inclusive, reduces the risk of per­

Transparency
The requirement of transparency is present in both sets of requirements. Analyzing the Trustwor­
thy AI requirements of transparency consists of three elements: traceability, explainability, and
communication. Traceability requires the data sets and the processes that yield the AI system’s
decision should be documented to the best possible standard. While traceability is described un­
der transparency in the Trustworthy AI requirements, it is described under the Record Keeping
requirement in high­risk AI systems:
1) ”High­risk AI systems shall be designed and developed with capabilities enabling the auto­
matic recording of events (‘logs’) while the high­risk AI systems are operating. Those logging
capabilities shall conform to recognized standards or common specifications” and 2) The log­
ging capabilities shall ensure a level of traceability of the AI system’s functioning throughout its
lifecycle that is appropriate to the intended purpose of the system” [12].
Explainability, as also described in section 4.2.4, concerns the ability to explain both the techni­
cal processes of an AI system and the related human decisions. Technical explainability requires
that the decisions made by an AI system can be understood and traced by human beings [38].
It might be necessary to prioritize enhancing a system’s explainability which might reduce the
system’s accuracy. The requirement of explainability is not mentioned in the EU AI Act’s re­
quirements for high­risk AI systems. Especially for health­related AI systems explainability is
important because their output can affect people’s health. Further, the history of bias in the med­
ical field also has an enormous impact on the importance of the requirement of explainability.
As aforementioned in section 4.2.3, throughout history, racial and ethnic bias had a significant
impact on healthcare where certain populations have been excluded from research studies. Fol­
lowing, medical research and clinical practices have primarily focused on male participants,
historically. Explainability will provide information about how decisions are made, and which
information is being used leading individuals to make informed choices on the utility of the
system’s recommendations and would help to identify and mitigate errors and biases.
Lastly, is the Trustworthy AI requirement of communication. This requirement state that AI
systems should not represent themselves as humans to users. It requires that humans have the
right to be informed that they are interacting with an AI system. The original proposal of the EU
AI Act does not require that the user is informed that it is interacting with an AI system. How­
ever, to ensure a human­centric and ethical development of AI in Europe, MEPs endorsed new
transparency rules for AI systems. In amendments to the original proposal, generative founda­
tion models, like ChatGPT, would have to comply with additional transparency requirements
including disclosing that the content was generated by AI, designing the model to prevent it from
generating illegal content, and publishing summaries of copyrighted data used for training.

8.1
Navigating the Multifaceted Challenges of Trustworthy AI
The concept of trustworthy AI is complex and includes multifaceted sociotechnical challenges
that encompass various technical, ethical, legal, and societal aspects. Moreover, the trustwor­
thy AI is not an easy concept to define, as emphasized in section 4.2, and there exist multiple
interpretations of trustworthy AI. However, there is an emerging convergence around the princi­
ples of transparency, justice and fairness, non­maleficence, responsibility, and privacy. Section
4.2 describes one of many, frameworks for trustworthy AI, namely the framework developed
by AI HLEG. The framework provides principles of what a trustworthy framework should in­
clude, herein three guidelines, four ethical principles, and seven requirements. However, the
framework does not provide specific solutions to attain them.
In the following sections, I will discuss the findings of the challenges of establishing trustworthy
AI. These challenges can be broadly classified into technical and societal challenges. However,
it is important to note that many of these challenges are interconnected and cannot be confined
to a single category alone.
8.1.1
Technical Challenges
Transparency, explainability, and interoperability are among the most important technical chal­
lenges when discussing trustworthy AI in healthcare, specified in section 4.2.4. As aforemen­
tioned, the lack of transparency in complex AI models can lead to potential risks and limita­
tions. Complex machine learning models, such as deep learning, are often preferred because of
their accurate predictive capabilities. However, there is an inherent trade­off between providing
clear explanations and prediction accuracy in machine learning models, as described in 4.2.4.
As these models grow in complexity, their decision­making processes become increasingly dif­
ficult to understand and interpret. This complexity arises when the model uses a large number
of interconnected neurons or features.
In addition to acknowledging the significance of explainability in fostering trust within the
healthcare domain, it is imperative to recognize the technical challenges that must be surmounted
to achieve explainable AI. There exists a challenge in integrating explainable AI into existing
Master Thesis
61
==PAGE:70==
machine learning frameworks. Machine learning models are developed on different frameworks
and architectures, making it difficult to develop unified explainable AI solutions. While research
in the field of explainable AI has gained significant attention in recent years, there is still a lack
of applicable methods. Moreover, to enhance trust in healthcare including both healthcare pro­
fessionals and patients, I see a difficult task in presenting these explanations in a human­readable
format. This will be extremely important for the healthcare professionals to trust the system, and
the patients to trust the healthcare professionals.
Imagine a perfect world where the technical challenge of explainable AI has been successfully
resolved, and AI models can provide transparent and understandable explanations for their deci­
sions, new ethical concerns may emerge. Ethical concerns such as data and data privacy. While
the goal of explainable AI is to enhance transparency and build trust in the AI system, there is
a risk that these systems may accidentally reveal sensitive data during the explanation process.
Existing explainable AI methods often extract information for a prediction using features, or
patterns present in the input data, explained in section 4.2.4. In this feature extraction process,
sensitive data that have been used during the model’s training may be exposed. This poses a sig­
nificant privacy concern, particularly when dealing with personal data such as medical records.
Ensuring fairness and non­discrimination in AI is a challenge that straddles both technical and
societal aspects. It involves a combination of developing AI algorithms that minimize biases
and promotes equitable outcomes, along with addressing the concept of fairness and discrimi­
nation. There exists a technical challenge in identifying and mitigating bias in AI models, briefly
described in section 4.2.3 and section 4.2.3. Further, there exist a technical challenge in ensur­
ing algorithmic fairness, which is also described in section 4.2.3. Additionally, there exists a
societal challenge in determining what constitutes fairness and non­discrimination, and as sec­
tion 4.2.3 also encompasses, fairness can be interpreted differently depending on social beliefs,
culture, and individual perspectives, which lead to various definitions of fairness.
8.1.2
Societal Challenges
Beyond technical challenges, AI also faces societal challenges. Addressing societal challenges
related to AI in healthcare requires a collaborative effort involving many different stakeholders
including governments, industry, healthcare professionals, patients, and citizens. Moreover, the
big challenge is to strike a balance between innovation and responsible implementation, ensuring
that AI technologies contribute positively to society while minimizing risks.
Applying AI in healthcare raises ethical considerations, as mentioned multiple times throughout
this report, due to the potential impact on individual lives and fundamental rights. Moreover, as
described in section 3.3.1, using AI in healthcare may raise risks and ethical concerns such as
privacy and security issues, bias, and accountability. Another societal challenge is the percep­
tion of existing inequities, also mentioned in 3.3.1, which refers to, among others, the disparity
Master Thesis
62
==PAGE:71==
between different demographic groups’ access to and utilization of technology including AI.
There is a societal concern of those with limited access to technology might be left behind,
exacerbating existing social and economic inequalities.
Trustworthy AI is a multifaceted and evolving concept that requires a comprehensive approach
to address its complexities. Ensuring trustworthy AI in healthcare demands not only technical
excellence but also ethical considerations, legal clarity, societal acceptance, and trust. As the
technology continues to evolve, there is a need for ongoing collaboration between the different
stakeholders to foster trustworthy AI in healthcare.

As countries compete to attain technological dominance in AI, they are also striving to develop
trustworthy AI applications that inspire confidence and trust. In establishing trustworthiness, the
EU proposed this EU AI Act, discussed and mentioned multiple times throughout this report.
Further, as mentioned in section 4.2, there is a need for AI to be trusted if it is to be widely
adopted including in healthcare. With the regulatory framework, the EU AI Act acknowledges
that there is a need for AI to be trusted. However, the more interesting question is whether the
EU AI Act is finally ensures trust in AI?
Establishing trust in AI is a complicated task since trust cannot be created on command, nor
with a regulatory framework emphasizing the importance. As reflected in section 4.2, trust and
trustworthiness are categorically two distinct words. As described in the same section, 4.2, trust
is an attitude, whereas trustworthiness is a quality in the object of this attitude that satisfies it
and helps make it appropriate [36]. With the EU AI Act, the EU is signaling trustworthiness,
and this is an important step for creating initial trust and changing attitudes, which is needed for
the uptake of AI in general and in healthcare.
The EU AI Act may lay the groundwork for creating initial trust by signaling the importance of
trustworthiness. The regulatory framework can play a vital role in setting guidelines, standards,
and accountability measures for AI systems, all of which contribute to establishing trust. The
EU AI Act is a step forward in changing attitudes towards AI. By promoting trustworthiness
and establishing statutory requirements for high­risk AI systems, it forces developers and orga­
nizations to prioritize responsible AI practices. This, in turn, can influence public perception
and acceptance of AI systems. The more individuals perceive AI as reliable and safe, the more
likely they are to accept new technologies.
”(...) We need trust from society and AI in general. This does not just go for health,
but particularly for health, right, because it’s not just like you’re buying a car or
something. It is for your, you know, for your own personal health”, Simone Morhs,
MedTech Europe

The quote is from the interview with Simone Morhs from MedTech Europe, which highlights
the significance of gaining society’s trust in the application of AI. In the healthcare sector, there
is already a regulation concerning healthcare applications, known as MDR. However, MedTech
Europe firmly believes that by adhering to supplementary regulatory standards laid out in the
EU AI Act, it demonstrates a commitment to achieving the highest level of certification possible
and earning the trust of society.
The question of whether we will finally have trust in AI through the implementation of the regu­
latory framework, specifically the EU AI Act, is a complex and multifaceted one. Furthermore,
given that the proposal remains in its developmental phase, only the passage of time can ascer­
tain its ultimate outcome and impact. However, the proposal certainly plays a crucial role in
addressing concerns related to AI’s ethical, societal, and safety aspects. With the legislation, the
EU undoubtedly signalizes that trustworthy AI is of high priority.
8.3
Need additional trustworthy requirements in the proposal?
Nevertheless, the concept of trustworthiness is complex and comprises various dimensions, as
elaborated in section 8.1. Focusing only on AI in healthcare, explainability in AI is especially
important for several reasons; patient safety, ethical and legal concerns, and bias and fairness, all
of which directly impact trust in AI systems. If an AI system produces unexplainable results, it
becomes challenging to justify or be held accountable for its decisions, which may raise ethical
and legal concerns. However, as found in the comparisons in section 7.2, the need for explain­
ability is not explicitly mentioned in the proposal. Without a requirement for explainability, the
EU AI Act may result in unreliable, unaccountable, and ethically questionable systems, espe­
cially when dealing with AI in healthcare. Incorporating explainability as a core requirement
has the ability to address the risk described in section 3.3.1, and better be able to promote respon­
sible and trustworthy use of AI in healthcare. However, as previously described and discussed
in section 8.1, there exists a major technical challenge in solving the black­box problem and
providing explainable AI systems.
The proposal aims to strike a balance between innovation and the protection of fundamental
rights and adding explainability as a requirement might hinder innovation. A possible con­
cern with adding explainability as a prerequisite for high­risk AI systems is that it may force
researchers and developers to adopt simpler, less, advanced models, which could potentially
compromise the system’s performance and efficacy. This will become necessary as long as
there exists no fully adequate technical solution to explainability. Striking a balance between
innovation and explainability becomes a challenging task, as there might be trade­offs between
the two objectives. While the EU AI Act does not require explainability, the proposal requires
transparency, which focuses on providing evidence and documentation for the system’s deci­
sions and actions. The proposal, therefore, requires some explanation of the AI system.

In section 3.3.1, explaining the seven risks of artificial intelligence in healthcare, there exist gaps
in accountability. There exist legal gaps in current regulations, which still do not allow for a
clear definition of accountability in AI used in healthcare. This results in difficulties in defining
the roles and responsibilities of the multiple actors involved in AI in healthcare. Moreover, the

Accountability is important for establishing trustworthy AI. While the absence of a specific

and risk management implying the necessity of clear lines and responsibility for the behavior
and impact of the AI system.

Conclusion
My ambition for this study was to investigate sociotechnical aspects of AI in healthcare. Furter,
I wished to investigate how the worlds first large scale initiative to control the risk of AI, the
EU AI Act, will cope with the threats and challenges that applications of AI in healthcare are
posing or will be posing in the future.
On top level, the concept of trustworthy AI is multifaceted and encompasses various challenges
including technical and societal challenges. Focusing only on healthcare, they key sociotech­
nical challenges of developing and implementing AI in healthcare includes fairness, bias and
non­discrimination, explainability and accountability. Explainable AI is essential for fostering
trust, but there remain an yet unsolved technical challenge in providing explainable AI systems
presenting explanations in a human­readable format.
The EU AI Act is addressing risks and challenges related to the ethical, societal, and safety as­
pects of AI, and with the proposal, the EU shows the great importance of developing trustworthy
AI application in order to safeguard fundamental rights. Based on the findings in this study, it
cannot be expected the EU AI Act to guarantee a completely trustworthy implementation of AI
in healthcare. This is primarily because of the lack of trustworthy requirements; accountability
and explainability in the proposal, which are especially important for fostering trust in health­
care. Moreover, establishing trust on command is not possible, as it requires time to effectuate
changes in attitudes. Nonetheless, the EU AI Act is acknowledging the importance of trustwor­
thy AI, which is an important step for establishing initial trust and changing attitudes and the
uptake of AI applications in healthcare.
Finally, there exists great consensus between stakeholders that this regulation is a welcomed step
forward in ensuring ethical, responsible, and trustworthy AI regardless of domain. Further, the
use of risk­based approach and regulation on a specific field of application instead of technical
specification is also evaluated as the correct approach by the stakeholders. However, in the field
of healthcare, there is a wish to make the requirements stricter when applying AI in healthcare
to ensure patients’ rights.
As a closing remark; only time can truly reveal the impact of this world’s first large­scale leg­
islation and its ability to address the threats and challenges posed by the applications of AI in
healthcare.

requirements referred to in point 3.2 or whether a reassessment is necessary.
