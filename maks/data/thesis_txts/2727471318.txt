Summary
This thesis focuses on the enhancement of self-suﬀiciency in a hybrid microgrid lo-
cated on Reunion Island. The concept of self-suﬀiciency in this context refers to the
ability of the microgrid to fulfill its energy demands solely using its own internal re-
sources, without relying on external grid support. To ensure accuracy, the calculation
of self-suﬀiciency considers the dissymmetry of the load throughout the year.
The study combines real data obtained from the Université de La Réunion regard-
ing on-site load demand and photovoltaic (PV) production, along with simulated data
for wind and PV production. These datasets are utilized to analyze various possibil-
ities and scenarios aimed at achieving a minimum self-suﬀiciency target of 80%. In
addition, the research assesses and compares different modelling tools to identify the
most suitable ones for accurately simulating and evaluating the microgrid.
Multiple scenarios, including wind and solar, as well as a sole PV scenario, are
simulated and carefully examined throughout the research. The investigation con-
cludes by presenting the advantages and disadvantages of each scenario, enabling the
proposal of the optimal solution for the university to enhance its self-suﬀiciency. By
implementing the recommended approach, the university can effectively develop and
increase its self-suﬀiciency capabilities.

Reunion Island
Reunion Island is a French overseas department located in the Indian Ocean, located
approximately 700 kilometers east of Madagascar and 200 kilometers southwest of
Mauritius, Reunion Island is an integral part of the Mascarene Archipelago. It covers
a land area of nearly 2,511m2 and has a diverse topography that includes mountains,
volcanoes, and coastal plains.
Figure 1.1: Reunion Island geographical location
Its population has experienced significant demographic changes over the years.
The island’s population is approximately 851 thousand inhabitants [4]. Reunion Is-
land is renowned for its ethnic diversity, with a fusion of cultures stemming from
European, African, Indian, and Chinese origins. The island’s population comprises
various ethnic groups, including Creoles, Indo-Mauritians, Tamils, and Chinese, which
contribute to the island’s rich cultural heritage.

1.3
Self-Suﬀiciency in microgrids
Self-suﬀiciency in a microgrid connected to the grid can be defined as the ability of a
microgrid to meet its energy demands using only its own internal resources, without
relying on external grid support. A self-suﬀicient microgrid can operate independently
from the larger utility grid, but it can also be connected to the grid to exchange en-
ergy when necessary. This is particularly important in situations where the microgrid
is located in an area with variable renewable energy resources or where the demand
for energy fluctuates over time.[18]
In order to calculate the self-suﬀiciency of a microgrid is important to consider
the dissymmetry of the load throughout the year, as this may not be constant. For
this is necessary to calculate the total amount of energy that was consumed directly
by the load and was provided from the generators plus what was provided from the
battery in each time step and then sum the total for the year. Once the total for a
whole year is calculated then this is divided by the total sum of the load throughout
the year.

CHAPTER 2
Introduction
2.1
Motivation
The motivation for this thesis stems from the increasing need for sustainable and
resilient energy systems. Microgrids, which are small-scale energy systems that can
operate independently or in conjunction with the main grid, have emerged as a promis-
ing solution to this need. By maximizing energy self-suﬀiciency, microgrids can reduce
dependence on fossil fuels and decrease greenhouse gas emissions.
The university campus microgrid of Terre Sainte on Reunion Island presents an
ideal case study for this research as it is a hybrid microgrid system consisting of
rooftop solar PV, energy storage and electrical and thermal loads. The unique char-
acteristics of the microgrid and its location on the island make it an interesting and
challenging case to study.
The objective of this thesis is to develop an accurate energy flow model of the
Terre Sainte microgrid and use it to investigate possible solar PV generation and
storage expansion. By doing so, the thesis aims to increase the self-suﬀiciency of the
microgrid to 80%. The results of the thesis will be used to make recommendations
on the future expansion of the Terre Sainte microgrid.
This research contributes to the field of microgrid modelling and optimization by
providing a comprehensive study of a hybrid microgrid system and its potential for
increasing energy self-suﬀiciency. The findings of this research will be used to propose
a solution that will increase the self-suﬀiciency of the Terre Sainte University campus
microgrid.

Research question and objective
The research question of this thesis is:
• How can a hybrid microgrid system be modelled and optimized for to increase
its energy self-suﬀiciency?

2.3
Methodology and Contribution
The methodology for this thesis will involve a multi-step process that includes data
collection and analysis, microgrid simulation, optimization, and validation. The first
step of the process was data collection. Technical specifications of the existing micro-
grid and user load measurements were collected from the Université de La Réunion.
These data will be analyzed using statistical and data analysis techniques to model
the current system. This information will be used to inform the development of the
energy flow model and to identify potential expansion scenarios that could be imple-
mented to increase the self-suﬀiciency of the microgrid to a minimum 80%.
Once the data is collected and analyzed, the next step will be to choose the
most suitable microgrid simulation software for modelling the Terre Sainte microgrid.
This selection will be based on the data collected and analyzed.
The simulation
model will be developed based on the technical specifications of the existing microgrid.
The simulation will be used to investigate possible solar PV generation and storage
expansion with the aim of increasing the energy self-suﬀiciency of the microgrid to
80%.
The simulation model will then be optimized using optimization techniques to
minimize the battery capacity required, while still achieving a minimum 80% self-
suﬀiciency. This will involve finding the right sizing of PV systems for the available
area as well as the correct sizing of the battery.

The scope of this thesis will focus on the modelling and optimization of a hybrid
microgrid system for increasing energy self-suﬀiciency to a targeted 80%. The case
study will be the university campus microgrid of Terre Sainte on Reunion Island,
which consists of rooftop solar PV, and electrical and thermal loads. The research will
investigate the most suitable microgrid simulation software and develop an accurate
energy flow model of the microgrid. The simulation model will be used to investigate
possible solar PV, wind turbine generation and battery storage expansion with the
aim of increasing the energy self-suﬀiciency of the microgrid to 80%. The thesis will
also provide recommendations on the future expansion of the Terre Sainte microgrid.
Analysis of the transformers and their capability to handle the increase in energy
generated are not analyzed and it is assumed they are capable to handle it.
The limitations of this research include that the accuracy of the data collected
is assumed, the microgrid simulation software chosen may have errors or limitations,
and the economic and financial aspects of the microgrid expansion are considered
only for the investment part but not throughout the operation and maintenance
phase. Finally, the available area of the roofs is estimated using satellite pictures
and may not be accurate, as well as all roofs are assumed to be fully free unless an
obstruction is clearly seen in the satellite picture.

CHAPTER 3
Literature Review
3.1
Overview of microgrid systems
Definition of a microgrid: A microgrid is a small-scale power system that can operate
either connected to or disconnected from the main power grid. Microgrids are charac-
terized by their ability to generate, distribute, and manage power locally, while also
being able to exchange power with the main grid[9].
Microgrid systems can be classified into three main categories: stand-alone, grid-
connected and hybrid. Stand-alone microgrids operate independently of the main grid
and rely on local energy sources such as photovoltaic (PV) systems, wind turbines,
and diesel generators. Grid-connected microgrids are connected to the main grid and
rely on a mix of local and external energy sources. This type of microgrid system is
able to sell any excess energy back to the main grid and buy energy from the grid
when the local generation is insuﬀicient[11].
A hybrid microgrid is a localized energy system that combines multiple sources
of power generation, energy storage, and distribution. It integrates renewable energy
sources like solar and wind with conventional power sources like diesel generators,
along with energy storage technologies such as batteries. The main goal is to op-
timize energy utilization while ensuring a stable power supply. Hybrid microgrids
reduce greenhouse gas emissions, promote clean energy use, and enhance energy re-
silience. They operate autonomously or in conjunction with the main power grid,
employing advanced control systems to manage energy flow and optimize eﬀiciency.
Overall, hybrid microgrids provide a sustainable, reliable, and resilient power infras-
tructure by integrating diverse energy sources and storage technologies. [11].
Microgrid systems typically consist of various components such as energy gener-
ation sources, energy storage systems, and loads (such as buildings, appliances and
electric vehicles) that consume energy. Energy generation sources can include solar
PV systems, wind turbines, and diesel generators. Energy storage systems can in-
clude batteries, thermal energy storage, and flywheels. Loads can be divided into
two categories: electrical loads and thermal loads. Electrical loads include lighting,
appliances, and electric vehicles. Thermal loads include heating and cooling systems,
and hot water systems, as shown in figure 3.1.[11].
==PAGE:22==

3 Literature Review
Microgrids offer several benefits compared to traditional centralized power sys-
tems, such as improved energy security, reliability, and resilience, as well as increased
use of renewable energy sources. They also offer the ability to reduce greenhouse gas
emissions and improve the integration of renewable energy sources. However, micro-
grid systems also present several challenges such as high costs, technical complexity,
and regulatory challenges[9].
For instance, a building with a PV and storage system is designed to meet the
energy needs of a single building or facility, incorporating solar panels for electricity
generation and a storage system for excess energy. It remains connected to the main
power grid and typically does not have the capability to operate independently. On
the other hand, a microgrid is a comprehensive energy system that serves several
buildings within a specific area. It is composed of various energy sources, storage
systems, and control mechanisms to provide a reliable and resilient power supply.
Unlike a building with a PV and storage system, a microgrid can operate indepen-
dently from the main grid during grid outages or in remote areas. This increases its
resilience and allows for localized power generation and distribution.
Figure 3.1: Microgird model
Current trends and future developments in microgrid systems: Microgrids are be-
coming increasingly important as the world moves towards a more decentralized and
sustainable energy system. Advances in technology, such as the development of en-
ergy storage systems and advanced control systems, are making microgrids more cost-

such as electric vehicles and demand response systems is becoming increasingly impor-

3.2
Microgrid simulation software
One of the key aspects of developing an accurate energy flow model of a microgrid is
the selection of appropriate simulation software. There are various microgrid simula-
tion software packages available, each with its own capabilities and limitations. Some
of the most widely used simulation software packages for microgrid modelling include
GridLab-D, PSCAD, MATLAB/Simulink, Homer Pro, and Python-based libraries
like PyPower, PyMgrid and pandapower.
GridLab-D is an open-source software package developed by the National Renew-
able Energy Laboratory (NREL) that is specifically designed for modelling microgrids
and distribution systems. It uses a combination of physics-based models and data-
driven models to simulate the dynamic behaviour of microgrids. One of the main
advantages of GridLab-D is its ability to model the interactions between different
components and subsystems of a microgrid in a highly detailed and accurate man-
ner[17].
PSCAD is a commercial simulation software package developed by Manitoba
HVDC Research Center. It is widely used for modelling power systems, including
microgrids. PSCAD uses both steady-state and dynamic models to simulate the be-
haviour of microgrids. One of the main advantages of PSCAD is its ability to model
the interactions between power electronics-based components and the grid in a highly
detailed and accurate manner[16].
MATLAB/Simulink is a widely-used commercial simulation software package de-
veloped by Mathworks.
It is used for a wide range of engineering and scientific
applications, including microgrid modelling. MATLAB/Simulink allows for the cre-
ation of custom models using a graphical block-diagramming approach. One of the
main advantages of MATLAB/Simulink is its wide range of toolboxes and add-ons,
which can be used to extend its capabilities for microgrid modeling[19].
Homer Pro is a commercial software that is widely used for the design and opti-
mization of microgrids with renewable energy sources such as solar PV and wind. It
offers a wide range of features and functionalities for simulating and analyzing micro-
grids including advanced optimization algorithms, detailed simulation of power elec-
tronics and electrical systems, support for a wide range of renewable energy sources,
advanced load modelling capabilities, detailed financial analysis, advanced visualiza-

PyPower is an open-source library that provides a set of functions for simulating

There are various optimization techniques and approaches that have been pro-

(LP), mixed-integer linear programming (MILP), nonlinear programming (NLP), dy-

monly used for the optimization of microgrid systems with discrete variables, such

used for the optimization of microgrid systems with continuous variables, such as the
power output of renewable energy sources. SP is commonly used for the optimization
of microgrid systems with uncertain variables, such as the availability of renewable
energy sources.[11].
One of the most commonly used optimization approaches in microgrid literature
is the use of particle swarm optimization (PSO) and genetic algorithm (GA) as they
are known to provide good results in terms of accuracy and computation time. Other
techniques such as Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) and Fuzzy logic (FL) have also
been proposed in the literature for microgrid optimization. [21][13][6]

Solar Resource Data:
PVlib relies on solar resource data, which includes parameters such as solar irradiance
and meteorological conditions. Solar irradiance is the amount of solar radiation per
unit area that reaches the Earth’s surface. It is measured in watts per square meter
(W/m2).
PVlib uses different models to estimate the solar irradiance on the PV
modules based on the position of the sun, atmospheric conditions, shading effects,
and reflections.
PVlib calculates the position of the sun based on the location and time of interest.
This involves determining the solar zenith and azimuth angles, which are crucial for
==PAGE:39==
4.4 Simulation of solar PV generation and storage expansion scenarios
27
estimating the angle at which sunlight strikes the PV panels. The solar zenith angle
(￿z) is the angle between the vertical and the line from the observer to the sun, while
the solar azimuth angle (￿a) is the angle between the observer’s south direction and
the projection of the line from the observer to the sun on the horizontal plane.
PVlib utilizes mathematical models to estimate the incident solar irradiance on the
PV modules. The most commonly used model is the Perez model, which is based on
empirical data and takes into account the angle of incidence, atmospheric conditions,
and shading effects. The model is given by:
G = Gb ∗(B1 + B2 ∗e(−B3/cos(z)))∗(1+B4∗(cos(a))2)
(4.11)
where:
• G is the global horizontal irradiance
• Gb is the beam irradiance
• B1 to B4 are empirical coeﬀicients
• and ￿z and ￿a are the solar zenith and azimuth angles
4.4.1.2
Module Performance Modeling and system configuration:
PVlib incorporates models to simulate the electrical behaviour of PV modules. The
most commonly used model is the single-diode model, which is based on the basic
principles of semiconductor physics. The model is given by:
I = Iph −I0 ∗(exp((V + Rs ∗I)/(a ∗Vt)) −1) −(V + Rs ∗I)/Rsh
(4.12)
Where:
• I is the output current
• Iph is the photocurrent
• I0 is the reverse saturation current
• V is the output voltage
• Rs is the series resistance
• Rsh is the shunt resistance
• a is the diode ideal factor
• Vt is the thermal voltage
PVlib allows users to define the configuration of their PV system, including the
number and arrangement of modules, inverter specifications, and system losses. This
information is necessary for accurately predicting the system’s overall performance.

4.4.2
Inputs into the model
In order to run the model and simulate the power output of a PV system throughout
a full year, it is necessary to provide the model with inputs from the desired PV
module, the number of modules and inverter size and the number of inverters that
compose the system.
4.4.2.1
PV system in available area calculation and PV module inputs
The first step is to identify the available area for the system to be installed, regardless
if it’s a roof or ground area. The total area can be measured or obtained from building
blueprints or estimated with the use of software such as Helioscope or google maps,
both of which use satellite photographs to estimate the available area or distance
between objects once a line is marked, as shown in figures 5.3 and 5.4. Once the
available area is known the number of modules that fit is calculated with the following
formula:
Modules = Roundown[Area/[(L + SM) × (W + SM + SR)]]
(4.13)
Where:
• Area = available area
• L = length of the desired PV module
• W = width of the desired PV module
• SM = space between modules
• SR = space between rows
The length and width of the PV module can be found in the data sheets as seen in
Appendix A, the space between modules and between rows can vary and is normally
between 0 and 15cm for the space between modules, this helps to avoid heating which
can damage the performance of the system. Fr the row spacing it can be between
50cm to more than 1m and is normally considered to avoid shading between the rows.
In order to simulate the system with the chosen module it will be required to
extract some information from its data sheets, as referenced above in appendixes A
and B. The inputs required are listed below:
1. Cell type
2. Max power (P−max)
3. Max power voltage (Vmp)
4. Max power current (Imp)
5. Open circuit power voltage (VOC)
==PAGE:41==

29
6. Open circuit power current (ISC)
7. Temperature coeﬀicient of P−max
8. Temperature coeﬀicient of VOC
9. Temperature coeﬀicient of ISC
10. Number of cells in series
11. Reference temperature
The choice of solar cell type has a great impact on the production of solar modules.
Monocrystalline cells are known for their high eﬀiciency and output, making them
ideal for installations where space is limited. These cells are made of a single crys-
tal structure (usually silicon), which allows for better electron flow and lower losses.
Polycrystalline cells, on the other hand, are slightly less eﬀicient than monocrystalline
cells but cheaper, it has multiple crystals within the cell structure, which can increase
electron scattering and slightly degrade performance. However, advances in technol-
ogy have closed the eﬀiciency gap between monocrystalline and polycrystalline, and
the affordability of polycrystalline cells has made them a popular choice for residen-
tial and commercial applications.
VOC refers to the maximum output voltage of a PV module when no load is con-
nected. It represents the voltage produced under ideal conditions, such as when the
cell is fully exposed to sunlight. VOC is primarily affected by the bandgap energy of
the cell’s semiconductor material and temperature. Materials with higher bandgap
energies lead to higher VOC levels, indicating better performance in high temperature
and low light conditions.
ISC represents the maximum output current of a PV module when the terminals
are shorted. Measurements are taken when the voltage on the terminals is 0 and the
module is exposed to maximum solar radiation. ISC depends on factors such as cell
size, incident light intensity, and temperature. The larger the cell area and the higher
the light intensity, the higher the ISC value.
Vmp is the voltage at which PV modules operate to produce maximum output
power. This occurs at a point where both current and voltage are optimal, thereby
maximizing power transfer.
Vmp is affected by several factors such as cell type,
temperature and shadowing. Temperature changes can cause the Vmp to drift, and
shading on the module can cause multiple Vmp points, reducing the overall power
output. Similarly, Imp represents the current at which PV modules operate to achieve
maximum power output.
This occurs at the sweet spot where power transfer is
maximized.
Imp is also affected by cell type, temperature and shade.
Similar to
Vmp, temperature changes and tint can affect his Imp point and reduce overall power
output.

4.4.2.2
Inverter sizing and inverter inputs
Once the maximum number of the desired module is obtained the total power output
is calculated, this will determine the size of the inverter that is required:
Poweroutput = Modules × P −max
(4.14)
After the power output of all the modules is calculated, the maximum number of
PV modules per string based on the inverter’s maximum DC input power is calculated
and rounded down to the nearest hole:
Maxmodules = InvertermaxDC/P −max
(4.15)
Once the number is obtained the total number of modules is divided by the number
of strings the inverter has to define the number of modules per string the system will
have, if the number exceeds the maximum number of modules per string the inverter
can have, a different inverter should be selected or the system should be designed
with fewer modules. The next step is to calculate the operating voltage the string
will be working on, to ensure it falls under the voltage range specified in the inverter
data sheet as shown in the appendix B. To calculate the voltage range of the string,
the number of modules per string is multiplied times the VOC of the module + a
safety margin (usually 5%):
Vstring = Modluesstring × (VOC + Safetymargin)
(4.16)
If the voltage per string falls in between the operating voltage range of the inverter
the inverter can be used to design and operate the system, otherwise, a new inverter
has to be selected or fewer modules would have to be used for the system.
Finally, after sizing the inverter adequately for the system, the data can be input
into the system:
1. Number of strings in the inverter
2. Modules per string (this will define the total number of modules in the system)
3. Maximum power of the inverter
4. Nominal eﬀiciency
5. Reference eﬀiciency
Once all the data from the chosen module and inverter are added to the model,
the surface tilt (10-15◦for most buildings) and surface azimuth are added, and the
model is ready to simulate the output of the designed PV system.

Proving the model with real data
The weather data used for the modelling of the system is acquired from the university

as well as separate days for each season to compare the reliability and accuracy.
4.4.3
Modelling storage system
Battery sizing is a critical step in designing a microgrid to achieve optimal self-
suﬀiciency and reliable power supply. Several factors need to be considered to ac-
curately determine the appropriate battery capacity. The following step-by-step ap-
proach can be used:
1. Gather data: the load profile and total load for the microgrid over a full year,
as well as data on wind and solar energy generation profiles.
2. Calculate the total energy consumed from renewable sources and calculate the
excess of energy produced and identify the periods when its produced.
3. Calculate the energy deficit: Subtract the expected energy generation from the
self-suﬀiciency target to determine the energy deficit that needs to be covered
by the battery system.
4. Size the battery system: Divide the adjusted energy deficit by the chosen depth
of discharge (DoD) to calculate the required battery capacity.
The model will calculate the total load for the full year as well as the total energy
produced, it will then calculate on a time-step basis the energy consumed by the grid
and sum it to have the total energy provided form the PV and wind systems that are
being directly consumed by the grid.
It will then calculate both the excess produced as well as the load missing to be
met. By inputting the minimum desired self-suﬀiciency to be obtained the system
will then calculate the ideal battery capacity depending on the objective function in
place, either to minimize the battery capacity or to minimize the excess of power at
the end of the year, on which the excess will be accounted as the power that was
produced but not used directly by the grid or to charge the battery system.

The research question addressed in this study was: how can a hybrid microgrid sys-

two sub-questions: What is the Current state of the Terre Sainte microgrid? and
how can self-suﬀiciency in the Terre Sainte microgrid be increased while minimizing
battery capacity? The objectives of the study are to model the Terre Sainte microgrid
and to increase its self-suﬀiciency to a minimum of 80%. In the following discussion,
we will examine the findings in relation to these research questions and objectives.
The main findings of this study are that the objective can be achieved with both
PV modules and the available area for PV, however, it is necessary to include ground
area and not only utilize the roof areas, also a combination of PV and wind can be
used in order to reduce the required battery capacity and increase the direct sup-
ply during the night, however, this will imply a higher investment. These findings
indicate that the current micro-gird of the Terre Sainte University campus can be
upgraded and optimized to fulfil the desired self-suﬀiciency as it has suﬀicient avail-
able roof and ground area for the needed installations for PV, on the wind turbine
side, the location would still have to be addressed and evaluated, but the wind profile

the battery capacity as it provides power during the night and thus diminishing the
dependency of the battery.

6.2
Comparison between Wind+Solar vs Solar systems
When determining the optimal solution, it is crucial to carefully consider the advan-
tages and disadvantages of solar PV systems with battery storage connected to the
grid versus hybrid microgrids with battery storage (combining solar PV and wind).
Hybrid microgrids offer several benefits compared to solar PV-only systems. The
combination of solar PV and wind power sources improves reliability by mitigating
the intermittent nature of these renewable resources. This combination ensures a
more consistent power output, reducing the risk of energy shortages during periods of
low solar irradiance or wind speeds. Moreover, the integration of multiple generation
sources in hybrid microgrids enhances stability, eﬀiciency, and energy availability,
while also reducing dependence on grid electricity.
The complementary nature of
solar and wind power enables load balancing and peak shaving, leading to potential
cost savings through reduced grid reliance and optimized energy management.
However, hybrid microgrids also come with their own challenges. The incorpora-
tion of multiple renewable energy sources increases the complexity of system design,
operation, and control. Eﬀicient coordination of intermittent generation profiles from
solar and wind resources requires real-time monitoring and sophisticated algorithms.
This complexity arises from the need to manage the variability and intermittency of
solar and wind resources, ensuring smooth integration and optimal utilization of both
sources.
Real-time monitoring is crucial for tracking the generation profiles of solar and
wind resources and making timely adjustments to balance supply and demand. Ad-
vanced control systems are needed to manage the power flow between different gen-
eration sources, storage systems, and the grid. These control systems must be able
to handle dynamic changes in generation and load, making decisions in real-time to
maintain stability and reliability.
On the other hand, solar PV systems with battery storage connected to the grid
offers advantages such as increased energy self-consumption, grid backup during out-
ages, and scalability. These systems optimize the utilization of generated electricity
for self-consumption and provide a reliable backup power source in the event of grid
outages. Furthermore, the scalability and flexibility of solar PV systems make them
suitable for various energy demands, and battery storage can be expanded to accom-
modate future needs.
Nevertheless, solar PV systems with battery storage also have limitations. They
rely solely on solar energy and are subject to weather conditions and daylight avail-
==PAGE:65==

53
ability, resulting in reduced energy generation during periods of low solar irradiance.
Dependence on the grid for backup power during such periods may lead to increased
costs and reduced energy independence in regions with limited or unreliable grid con-
nectivity.
In conclusion, the choice between hybrid microgrids with battery storage and
solar PV systems with battery storage connected to the grid depends on specific
requirements and circumstances. Hybrid microgrids offer benefits in terms of reliabil-
ity, energy yield, diversification, load balancing, and potential cost savings, but they
come with increased upfront costs and complexity. Solar PV systems with battery
storage connected to the grid provide advantages such as energy self-consumption,
grid backup, scalability, and flexibility, but they are subject to solar dependency and
potential grid dependence. Ultimately, the decision should consider factors such as
project scope, budget, energy demand patterns, local resource availability, and grid
reliability.

Upon further analysis, it is evident that if the focus is solely on CAPEX, the optimal
choice is to pursue the only PV approach. Both the 395Wp and 445Wp modules meet

beyond CAPEX, such as system resilience.
The addition of a wind turbine generator significantly enhances the system’s re-

system, resulting in a smaller battery capacity. Although the wind-solar combination

turbine on campus needs to be assessed, as it may not be possible or practical to be

6.4
Suggestions for further research
To further expand on the research in the case study the following points can be
included:
1. Viability of Current Transformers: A detailed analysis should be conducted
to evaluate the capacity of the existing transformers in the microgrid. This
assessment will determine if the transformers can support the proposed PV
systems and battery storage without any issues.
2. Financial Assessment: A detailed financial analysis should be carried out to as-
sess the economic feasibility of the proposed expansion. This assessment should
not only consider the CAPEX but fully include the operation and maintenance
for the whole life of the system. It should also consider the potential savings
in energy costs and the return on investment over the project’s lifecycle. Sen-
sitivity analyses can be conducted to explore different scenarios and assess the
financial viability under different market conditions, E.g. electricity prices be-
fore and after the Ukrainian war.
3. Life Cycle Assessment: In addition to the financial evaluation, a life cycle as-
sessment should be conducted to evaluate the environmental impacts associated
with the proposed expansion. It should consider the energy consumption, emis-
sions, and resource utilization throughout the life cycle of the PV systems,
battery storage, and other components. It will provide a full view of the en-
vironmental footprint of the microgrid expansion and help identify areas for
improvement in terms of sustainability.
By conducting these further research approaches, a fully comprehensive analysis
of the proposed microgrid expansion, considering technical, financial, environmental,
and sustainability aspects can be obtained.
The findings from these assessments
will contribute to the overall understanding of the project’s viability, benefits, and
potential for future growth and implementation.
