Abstract
Evaluating fracture toughness in composite structures is challenging when material properties are
unknown and residual stresses are present, such as aged or repurposed wind turbine blades. This
thesis validates stiffness- and curvature-based analytical J-integral formulations for double cantilever
beam (DCB) specimens under these conditions. The analytical methods compute the J-integral along
the external boundaries, and their accuracy was assessed by comparison with local J-integral values
evaluated around the crack tip in finite element models. A surface-based bilinear cohesive zone was
implemented in three finite element models: a single-material baseline, a multilayer configuration, and
a multilayer model incorporating residual stresses.
The analytical methods showed strong agreement with local J-integral results across a range of load
cases, with errors typically below 2% of the fracture toughness. Increased stiffness in the multilayer
model lengthened the cohesive zone and improved accuracy by aligning more closely with the analytical
assumptions. In the residual stress model, the auxiliary problem, free of residual stresses, accurately
reproduced the real problem’s crack plane opening profiles for variations in temperature, stiffness,
thermal expansion coefficient, and mechanical loading. Validating the analytical expressions for the
auxiliary case–combined with opening profile consistency and the assumption of a potential-function-
based J-integral–validated their applicability to the real problem.
These results confirm that the analytical J-integral formulations can accurately estimate the energy
release rate when material properties are unknown and residual stresses are present, enabling reliable
structural integrity assessments of composite structures.

Introduction
Wind turbine blades (WTBs) are intricate structures made of multilayered composites and are subjected
to complex loading conditions, including aerodynamic, gravitational, and centrifugal forces. These
blades are primarily constructed from glass-fiber reinforced polymers (GFRPs) due to their high
strength-to-weight ratio. Although approximately 94% of the total mass of a wind turbine is recyclable,
WTBs pose a major sustainability challenge due to their low recyclability [1]. By 2050, the global mass of
decommissioned WTBs is expected to reach 43 million tonnes [2]. According to the waste hierarchy [3],
repurposing is the most favourable strategy to manage WTB waste. However, effective repurposing
requires a thorough damage assessment to ensure structural integrity and safe reuse of components [4].
This process is inherently complex and technically demanding.
Delamination is one of the most critical failure mechanisms in composite materials, significantly
influencing their structural strength. A key challenge in assessing delamination-driven fractures is
the difficulty in obtaining accurate material properties such as layer stiffness, interface properties, and
residual stresses, particularly in aged WTBs. Traditional fracture mechanics methods, including the
J-integral, typically rely on well-defined material data to accurately predict crack growth [5]. In the
context of circular economy, the inability to evaluate residual strength without material characterization
remains a limiting factor in the repurposing of decommissioned blades.
Residual stresses in composite materials originate from mismatches in thermal and mechanical properties
between layers, as well as from curing and manufacturing processes [6]. These inelastic stresses exist
in the structure even in the absence of external loading. Their presence complicates fracture analysis,
particularly when using energy-based approaches such as the J-integral. Although the J-integral is
formally defined as a path-independent measure of the energy release rate in linear elastic materials [5],
this path-independence breaks down in the presence of residual stresses or plastic deformation.
Specifically, when residual stresses are significant, the calculated J-integral becomes path-dependent [7],
leading to inconsistencies and numerical challenges in finite element modelling. Addressing this issue
requires alternative formulations that can account for these residual stresses.
This research addresses the need for a method to analyze delamination in multilayer composite structures
when material properties are unknown and residual stresses are present. The objective is to validate
the analytical expressions for the J-integral based on curvature and strain measurements derived by
Toftegaard and Sørensen [8], particularly when residual stresses are present. Through this study, the
aim is to contribute towards a robust tool for assessing fracture toughness in aged composite structures
without requiring full material characterization.
To achieve this, finite element modeling (FEM) of a double cantilever beam (DCB) specimen will be
performed using cohesive zone modeling (CZM). Mechanical and thermal loads will be applied to
simulate conditions with induced residual stresses. The local J-integral around the fracture process
zone, Jloc, will be calculated from the area under the traction-separation curve by measuring the normal
and tangential crack-tip opening displacements (CTODs). The external J-integral, Jext, will be computed
using the analytical expressions developed by Toftegaard and Sørensen [8]. Since the J-integral is
theoretically path-independent, agreement between Jloc and Jext will serve as a validation of the analytical
formulation.
In the presence of residual stresses, Toftegaard and Sørensen [8] propose a framework that separates
the problem into three conceptual cases: real problem, thought experiment, and auxiliary problem.
The thought experiment and auxiliary problem provide alternative methods that account for residual
stresses while working towards enabling path-independent J-integral expressions. In this work, all three
1
==PAGE:12==
2
cases will be modeled using finite element simulations: the residual stress field will be incorporated as
equivalent loads in the auxiliary problem, and compared to the full thermal-mechanical real problem
and thought experiment. The behavior of the crack plane displacement field and agreement of J-integral
values will serve to validate the residual stress approaches proposed by Toftegaard and Sørensen [8].
The project is grounded in the theoretical framework outlined in chapter 2. It builds upon existing
analytical and numerical approaches, which are reviewed in chapter 3. Based on this foundation, the
research questions are formulated in chapter 4. The model setup and implementation are described
in chapter 5. Finally, the results and validation of the model are presented in chapter 6, followed by a
discussion of the findings in chapter 7.

Theoretical Background
Fracture mechanics involves studying the behavior of cracked materials under stress in order to predict
failure. By quantifying how cracks propagate in relation to different material properties, geometries,
and loading conditions, fracture mechanics enables the design of structures with optimal safety margins,
helps assess the lifespan of components, and supports decisions regarding the feasibility of repairs [9].
2.1. Fracture Modes
In fracture mechanics, crack propagation is typically classified into three fundamental loading modes:
mode I, mode II, and mode III, as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Fracture modes that can be applied to a crack.
• Mode I is the opening mode, where the crack faces are pulled directly apart by normal tensile
stresses perpendicular to the crack plane. This is the most common and critical mode in composite
materials, as they generally have the lowest resistance to crack opening under tensile loading. The
crack faces undergo opening displacements relative to one another in the normal direction as the
crack propagates. [10]
• Mode II is the sliding mode (in-plane shear), where the crack faces slide relative to each other in the
plane of the crack and perpendicular to the crack front. This cracking mode is less common and
critical than the crack opening tensile mode due to the higher fracture toughness of composites in
Mode II. It still relevant to be studied for structures subjected to shear and/or bending loads. [10]
• Mode III is the tearing mode (out-of-plane shear), characterized by shear stresses acting parallel to
the crack plane and the crack front, causing one crack face to slide over the other in an out-of-plane
direction. This mode is less common compared to mode I and II but can be significant in complex
loading scenarios or at free edges. [10]
In practical engineering applications, cracks often experience a combination of these loading conditions
referred to as mixed-mode loading. Predicting crack growth under mixed-mode conditions is more
complex due to the interaction between normal and shear stress fields at the crack tip, especially in
layered materials like composites.

2.2. Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM)
LEFM is a theoretical framework that assumes materials behave in a linearly elastic manner up to the
point of fracture. This means that stress and strain remain proportional (Hooke’s Law holds), and any
plastic deformation near the crack tip is small enough to be neglected. LEFM is particularly suitable
for brittle materials or structures where the plastic zone around the crack tip is small compared to the
dimensions of the structure or the crack length.
The primary development of LEFM began with Griffith’s energy balance approach [11]. Griffith
proposed that a crack will propagate when the energy available for crack growth exceeds the energy
required to create new surfaces. The energy release rate, 𝐺is defined as follows:
𝐺= 𝑑𝑈
𝑑𝐴
where 𝑈is the total potential energy of the system, and 𝐴is the crack surface area. The critical condition
for crack growth is reached when 𝐺≥𝐺𝑐, where 𝐺𝑐is the fracture toughness of the material. The
fracture toughness can be interpreted as the energy required to create a unit area of new crack surface.
LEFM employs a stress-based approach focused on the stress intensity factor, K. Near the crack tip, the
stress field can be expressed in terms of K, leading to the definition of a K-dominated zone, where the
stress field is governed by the stress intensity factor regardless of loading configuration or geometry.
However, this formulation introduces a mathematical singularity: the theoretical stress at the crack tip
approaches infinity. In reality, this is not physically accurate, as materials exhibit finite strength.
2.2.1. Assumptions and Limitations of LEFM
The main assumptions of LEFM are as follows:
• The material behaves elastically up to the point of fracture, following Hooke’s law.
• The crack tip is modeled as a mathematical point with zero radius, resulting in a stress singularity
as the distance to the tip approaches zero.
• The plastic or damage zone around the crack tip is small compared to the crack length and
specimen dimensions, so the surrounding material remains elastic.
Despite its usefulness, LEFM has the following key limitations:
• It is not applicable to ductile materials where the plastic zone is large compared to the crack size
or specimen dimensions.
• It fails to accurately describe fracture behaviour in materials with large fracture process zones,
such as composites or toughened polymers.
In such cases, the LEFM-based prediction of crack growth and fracture resistance becomes inaccurate.
This lead to the development of Elastic-Plastic Fracture Mechanics (EPFM).
2.3. Elastic-Plastic Fracture Mechanics (EPFM)
EPFM extends LEFM to materials that undergo significant plastic deformation ahead of the crack tip
prior to fracture. This is particularly important for ductile materials, where the plastic zone cannot
be ignored. To improve on Griffith’s original energy balance theory, Irwin introduced a plastic zone
correction [12]. Irwin proposed that the effective crack length, 𝑎eff includes the physical crack length, 𝑎
plus a plastic zone extension, 𝑙𝑝𝑧, such that:
𝑎eff = 𝑎+ 𝑙𝑝𝑧
This accounts for the additional energy consumed to form a plastic zone ahead of the crack tip.

In EPFM, the energy release rate 𝐺is adjusted as follows:
𝐺= 𝐺𝑠+ 𝐺𝑝

2.3.1. Assumptions and Limitations of EPFM
The main assumptions of EPFM are as follows:
• The material exhibits elastic-plastic behavior, with plastic deformation localized near the crack tip.
• The plastic zone near the crack tip is assumed to be small compared to the crack length and
specimen dimensions, such that the surrounding material remains predominantly elastic. This
condition is known as small-scale yielding.
• The crack tip remains sharp, and modeling of the crack tip blunting or cohesive process is not
included.
Despite its broader applicability compared to LEFM, EPFM has the following limitations:
• The formulation assumes a sharp crack and is not well-suited for materials with a large fracture
process zone (FPZ), such as fiber-reinforced composites.
• It does not explicitly model progressive damage, crack initiation, or microstructural failure
mechanisms such as fiber breakage or matrix cracking.
These limitations become especially relevant for composite materials, where the fracture behavior
involves damage mechanisms like delamination, fiber bridging, and interface debonding. In such cases,
the Cohesive Zone Model (CZM) offers a more suitable approach.
2.4. Cohesive Zone Model (CZM)
The Cohesive Zone Model (CZM), based on the works of Dugdale [13] and Barenblatt [14], introduces an
approach to fracture that accounts for gradual material separation at the crack tip rather than modeling
crack propagation as an instantaneous event. CZM introduces a fracture process zone in which material
points separate progressively under cohesive tractions. The fundamental idea is that the material
continues to transfer stress across the fracture surfaces until a critical separation is reached, beyond
which full decohesion occurs.
CZM is particularly suitable for materials such as fiber-reinforced composites, where complex failure
mechanisms (such as delamination, fiber bridging, or matrix cracking) are not captured by traditional
linear or elastic-plastic fracture mechanics. In CZM, fracture behavior is characterized using a traction-
separation law that defines the relationship between interfacial traction, 𝜏and the opening displacement,
𝛿across the interface.
Similarly to EPFM, the CZM energy release rate has an additional term to account for the energy
required to separate the cohesive surfaces. The total energy release rate is expressed as follows:
𝐺= 𝐺𝑠+ 𝐺𝑐𝑧
where 𝐺𝑐𝑧is the energy dissipated within the cohesive zone.

2.6. Finite Element Modelling (FEM)
This section outlines the key theoretical concepts of FEM relevant to this study, based on the formulations
presented by Cook et al. [16]. These principles guide the modeling decisions made in chapter 5.
2.6.1. Solver Type: Implicit vs. Explicit
Finite element solvers used in structural and fracture mechanics can be broadly classified into implicit
and explicit types, based on how they solve the governing equilibrium equations at each time or load
increment.
In implicit methods, the solution enforces force equilibrium through the system of equations:
K Δu = ΔF
(2.9)
where K is the global stiffness matrix, Δu is the nodal displacement increment, and ΔF is the externally
applied load increment.
For non-linear problems due to material behaviour, large deformations, or contact, the stiffness matrix,
K changes with deformation. In such cases, iterative procedures, such as the Newton-Raphson method,
are used:
K(𝑖) Δu(𝑖) = R(𝑖)
(2.10)
where R(𝑖) is the residual force vector and K(𝑖) is the tangent stiffness matrix in iteration 𝑖. The solution
iterates until the residual falls below a specified convergence tolerance.
==PAGE:19==
2.6. Finite Element Modelling (FEM)
9
Explicit solvers, in contrast, solve the dynamic equilibrium equations directly:
M ¥u = Fext −Fint
(2.11)
where M is the mass matrix, ¥u is the nodal acceleration vector, Fext is the external force, and Fint is
the internal force. This method avoids solving a global system of equations, making each time step
computationally inexpensive.
Implicit solvers are considered unconditionally stable because they incorporate future state information
(displacement, velocity, or acceleration) when enforcing equilibrium. This allows stable solutions even
with large time steps, particularly in linear problems. However, large time steps may lead to poor
accuracy and failure to capture non-linear behaviour. In contrast, explicit solvers are only conditionally
stable, meaning that their time step must be smaller than a critical value governed by the smallest
element size and wave speed. If this critical step size is exceeded, numerical instability can occur.
In general, implicit solvers are best suited for quasi-static or slowly evolving processes where accuracy
and convergence are critical. Explicit solvers are favoured for highly dynamic, impact-driven, or
failure-dominated problems where extreme non-linearity or rapid events are present.
2.6.2. Element Type
Linear vs. Quadratic
The choice of element order determines how the displacement field is interpolated within each finite
element.
In two-dimensional analysis, linear elements (4-node quadrilaterals) use bilinear interpolation. Each
corner node has two translational degrees of freedom (DOFs), resulting in 8 DOFs per element. These
elements are computationally efficient but can be inaccurate in problems with stress gradients, high
curvature, or crack tip singularities, especially when the mesh is not sufficiently fine.
Quadratic elements (8-node quadrilaterals) include four additional mid-side nodes, allowing for second-
order interpolation of the displacement field. This increases the total DOFs to 16 per element. The
higher-order interpolation significantly improves accuracy in capturing bending behavior and localized
phenomena such as stress concentrations or fracture process zones, without requiring an extremely fine
mesh.
Figure 2.3: Schematic of nodes and integration points in (a) linear and (b) quadratic elements for full
and reduced integration. Adapted from Burlayenko and Sadowski [17].
A key issue with linear elements is shear locking, which occurs in bending-dominated problems when
the element becomes overly stiff due to the inability of linear shape functions to represent transverse
shear deformation accurately. To address this, the following techniques are commonly used:
• Reduced integration uses fewer integration points than the full element formulation, which helps
to reduce the excessive stiffness that causes shear locking. However, it can sometimes lead to a
==PAGE:20==
2.6. Finite Element Modelling (FEM)
10
phenomenon called hourglassing, where the element deforms in unrealistic shapes that do not
reflect the actual physics. These artificial deformation patterns do not carry energy and can distort
the solution.
• Incompatible modes enrich the displacement field by adding higher-order strain modes within
the element, which do not interfere with nodal compatibility. It improves bending performance
and alleviates locking without under-integrating the stiffness matrix. However, the added modes
must be carefully formulated to avoid introducing spurious oscillations or mesh sensitivity.
Quadratic elements naturally overcome shear locking due to their ability to represent both bending and
shear deformation more accurately. As a result, they are generally preferred in applications that require
precise stress prediction.
Plane Strain vs. Plane Stress
In two-dimensional modelling, assumptions must be made regarding the behaviour in the out-of-plane,
𝑧-direction. The two most common simplifications are plane strain and plane stress models.
Plane strain assumes that the body is constrained in the out-of-plane direction, i.e. 𝜀𝑧𝑧= 0. This
condition applies to thick geometries where deformation along the 𝑧-axis is negligible. It is valid for
interior regions of thick plates and large fracture specimens. Plane strain leads to higher levels of
constraint and more brittle behavior near the crack tip, leading to more conservative estimates of crack
growth. The effective elastic stiffness increases, with 𝐸eff = 𝐸/(1 −𝜈2).
Plane stress assumes negligible stress in the out-of-plane direction, i.e.
𝜎𝑧𝑧= 0. This condition
is appropriate for thin structures where the surfaces are free to expand or contract. Compared to
plane strain, the plane stress condition allows more deformation near the crack tip and predicts lower
crack-driving forces.
The choice between plane strain and plane stress is not inherently critical, but it must be taken into
account in the analytical computations.

A literature review has been conducted to understand the current state of research in fiber-bridged
delamination and residual stress analysis in composite materials. This review first discusses the
mechanism and effects of fiber bridging on fracture resistance. Then it outlines the origins and impact
of residual stresses in composites and their effect on the path-dependency of the J-integral. Lastly, it

Finally, it states the role of this thesis in advancing the current body of knowledge on delamination in

3.1. Fiber Bridging in Composites
Fiber bridging is a toughening mechanism observed during delamination in fiber-reinforced polymer
composites, where intact fibers span across the crack surfaces behind the delamination front. As the crack
propagates, some fibers remain unbroken and continue to transmit load between the separated faces.
This bridging effect occurs because the fibers are typically stiffer and stronger than the surrounding
matrix and resist full separation, particularly if they are well-bonded to the matrix or misaligned
relative to the crack path. In the literature review by Khan [18], it is highlighted that bridging tends to
begin when the matrix fractures or debonds while the embedded fibers maintain continuity across the
delaminating interface, effectively shielding the crack tip and reducing the local stress intensity. This
mechanism is most pronounced in unidirectional composites where fiber continuity is aligned with the
crack growth direction, allowing the fibers to act as mechanical ligaments that carry load even after
matrix failure.
Yao et al. [19] provide direct in-situ visual evidence of this phenomenon through high-resolution SEM
imaging. Figure 3.1 captures the progressive stages of fiber bridging: initial bending of the bridging
fibers as the crack opens, followed by debonding from the matrix and progressive peeling, and finally,
fiber breakage as the crack opening increases. These observations reveal that fiber bridging is not an
abrupt event but a gradual process that depends on both the local stress state and the fiber-matrix
interaction.

3.2. Residual Stresses in Composites
Residual stresses in composite laminates are internal stresses that remain after manufacturing, even
without external loads. Fernlund et al. [6] categorize their sources into two main groups: intrinsic
(related to the material system and lay-up) and extrinsic (related to processing conditions and tooling).
As illustrated in Figure 3.4, intrinsic sources have the strongest influence at the fiber-matrix level,

while extrinsic sources more strongly affect the overall laminate structure. These residual stresses can
significantly impact both the strength and the final shape of the composite part.
Figure 3.4: Sources of residual stresses in composite laminates. Thicker arrows indicate a stronger
relationship. Source: Fernlund et al. [6].
To better understand how these stresses develop, Safarabadi & Shokrieh [22] describe residual stress
formation at both the micro- and macro-mechanical levels. At the micro-mechanical level, the mismatch
in thermal expansion between the fiber and the matrix is the main driver. During cooling, the matrix
shrinks more than the stiffer fibers, generating compressive stresses in the fibers and tensile stresses in
the matrix, as shown in Figure 3.5a. Fernlund et al. [6] note that the matrix initially behaves as a viscous
fluid and in this state, it does not contribute to the build-up of stress. However, as it transitions into a
visco-elastic state during cooling, it starts to exert stresses on the fibers.
At the macro-mechanical level, residual stresses develop due to differences in thermal contraction across
plies with different orientations and positions in the laminate. Although the laminate cools uniformly as
a whole, each ply experiences different in-plane contractions. To maintain strain compatibility through
the thickness, internal stress gradients form between the layers. These result in inter-laminar tension
and compression, often leading to out-of-plane deformation such as warping or curvature, as shown in
Figure 3.5b. This observation aligns with Fernlund et al. [6], who report geometric changes such as
spring-in (mainly due to intrinsic effects) and warpage (driven by tool-part interaction).

3.2.1. Path Dependence of the J-Integral under Residual Stresses
In classical Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM), the J-integral is path-independent and represents
the energy release rate associated with crack growth. This path independence relies on assumptions
such as elastic material behavior, absence of body forces, and no internal stress fields. However, the
presence of residual or thermal stresses violates these conditions and leads to path dependence in the
J-integral evaluation.

approach, particularly for capturing the entire crack evolution from initiation to propagation, not just
the steady-state phase.
3.4. Finite Element Modelling of Delamination
Delamination in composite structures can be modelled using several finite element techniques. Sharma
et al. [15] compare three commonly used approaches: Virtual Crack Closure Technique (VCCT), Cohesive
Zone Models (CZM), and Extended Finite Element Method (XFEM) implemented with both VCCT and
CZM formulations.
VCCT is based on the assumption that the energy required to close a crack equals the energy needed to
propagate it. While it can predict crack propagation when the crack path is predefined, it cannot predict
crack initiation. VCCT also requires a fine mesh near the crack front to maintain accuracy.
CZM allows for modelling the initiation and propagation of delamination and can be implemented in
two ways: surface-based and element-based. The surface-based CZM uses cohesive interactions defined
between laminate interfaces, as shown in Figure 3.9a. In contrast, element-based CZM inserts cohesive
elements between plies, as shown in Figure 3.9b. CZM requires the definition of cohesive parameters
that govern the traction-separation behaviour, which significantly affect the model response.

3.5. Role of this Thesis
This thesis aims to build on the work of Toftegaard and Sørensen [8], by simulating delamination in
a composite double-cantilever beam, particularly with the inclusion of residual stresses. While the
stiffness and curvature formulations have been analytically validated, the extension to include residual
stresses requires further verification. Using a surface-based cohesive zone model, this work performs
numerical simulations of composite DCB specimens and compares Jext and Jloc to validate Equation 3.4,
Equation 3.6, and Equation 3.8.
The outcome of this study supports the development of a reliable method for evaluating fracture
resistance without requiring detailed knowledge of the material’s elastic properties. It also enables
assessment of energy release rates in the presence of residual stresses, which is essential for understanding
fracture behaviour in realistic composite structures.

How can the J-integral be accurately modelled for multilayered DCB specimens with unknown

The sub-questions that will structure the research and contribute toward the primary research question
are the following:
• What are the effects of varying the stiffness of the layers on the fracture process?

• To what extent can the analytical J-integral derived from strain and curvature measurements

5.1. Problem Definition
The primary objective of this thesis is to validate a generalized approach that specifically focuses on:
1. J-integral evaluation with unknown material properties: Develop and assess a method to compute
Jext using the stiffness-based (Equation 3.4) and curvature-based (Equation 3.6) formulations, and
compare the results to the local J-integral (Jloc) evaluated around the fracture process zone (FPZ).
2. J-integral evaluation under residual stress: Extend the analysis to include thermally induced
residual stresses using the framework proposed by Toftegaard and Sørensen [8]. This involves
simulating the real problem, the thought experiment, and the auxiliary problem to assess
crack-propagation behaviour and validate the path-independence of the proposed J-integral
formulations, Equation 3.7 and 3.8.
To ensure robustness, these methods were tested in the following load cases:
• Pure Moment Loading
– Mode I-dominated
– Mode II-dominated
– Mixed-mode (three cases with varying phase angles)
• Pure Force Loading
– Tangential sliding (equal and opposite axial forces)
• Combined Moment and Force Loading
– Mode I-dominated moments with equal axial forces
– Mode II-dominated moments with equal axial forces
– Mode I-dominated moments with equal and opposite axial forces
– Mode II-dominated moments with equal and opposite axial forces
5.1.1. Model Requirements
The models were guided by the analytical framework and assumptions presented by Toftegaard and
Sørensen [8]. To ensure compatibility with their derivation of the J-integral, the FEM models were
designed with the following key requirements:
• The specimen geometry must be large enough such that the distance from the crack tip to both
ends exceeds several times the DCB height. This helps satisfy the assumptions of Euler-Bernoulli
beam theory and ensures that a uniform bending state is established away from the crack tip and
boundary.
• The cohesive traction-separation law should be derivable from a potential function. For the
selected truss-like cohesive law, this implies that the fracture resistance must be independent of
the phase angle of separation [30]. In other words, the effective traction vector must depend solely
on the magnitude of the opening, not on its direction.
• The material is assumed to behave linear-elastically outside the fracture process zone.
23
==PAGE:34==
5.2. Methodology
24
• Crack growth is assumed to be stable and self-similar, maintaining consistent conditions at the
crack tip during propagation.
• Axial forces must be applied to the elastic centres of the beams to prevent unintended bending or
curvature.
• The J-integral remains path-independent for functionally graded materials when property variation
is confined to the height direction. Accordingly, beams with constant cross-sectional properties
along the length were used.
• The moments and forces are applied at the ends of the beams inducing a stress field corresponding
to uniform stretching and pure bending.
The rest of the boundaries are considered to be
traction-free.
These assumptions ensure consistency between the analytical and numerical computations of the
J-integral and were used to guide both model construction and post-processing.
5.2. Methodology
The experimentation in this project was conducted using 2-dimensional FEM models built in Abaqus
and executed on the DTU Wind Energy cluster, which operates on a Linux/SLURM system. Abaqus
is a well-established FEM solver widely used in both academia and industry due to its robustness
in capturing complex phenomena such as delamination, crack propagation, fiber bridging, and the
development of residual stresses.
To ensure the reliability of the model and build confidence in the numerical results, modelling was
carried out in a staged manner. The progression of models follows this sequence:
• Single Material Model
• Multilayer Model
• Residual Stress Model
5.2.1. Finite Element Modelling Choices
A static implicit solver was used for all simulations. Since the aim was to capture quasi-static delamination
behavior, the implicit approach ensured stable convergence without excessive computational cost.
Geometric non-linearity was not included because the analytical J-integral expressions were derived
under the assumption of linear geometry.
The model was defined in 2D with a plane stress assumption. This is justified by the assumed specimen
width of 1 mm and the fact that deformation is primarily in-plane, making out-of-plane stresses
negligible.
Quadratic elements were selected to better resolve stress and strain gradients, to improve accuracy of
the analytical strain and curvature formulation. A finer mesh was applied in the cohesive zone region
to accurately capture the traction-separation behaviour and minimize mesh-sensitivity.

5.2.2. Data Collection and Post-Processing
The simulation output files (.odb) were exported from Abaqus and processed using Python scripts.
The local J-integral, Jloc, was calculated from the traction-separation law defined in the cohesive zone.
The relative displacements between the crack tip nodes of beam 1 and 2 were extracted to obtain the
normal and tangential separations, 𝛿𝑛and 𝛿𝑡, respectively. Then, the energy release rate was determined
from the total separation, 𝛿. For residual stress simulations, the full crack-plane displacement field was
also extracted to compare real, thought, and auxiliary problems.
The external J-integral, Jext, was calculated from the applied mechanical loads and the resulting
deformation. Axial loads at beams 1 and 2 were applied through displacement rate conditions at the
reference points. Moments were applied via prescribed rotation rates. The loads were extracted from the
corresponding reaction forces and moments at these reference points. Due to the boundary conditions
applied to beam 3, its loading was not directly prescribed but determined through the equilibrium of
forces and moments. Axial strains and stresses were extracted along each beam from the output at
the integration points and interpolated to the nodal locations. The axial strains at the top and bottom
surfaces were used to calculate the elastic center strain and curvature. These outputs were used in
Equation 3.7 and Equation 3.8 to calculate Jext.

5.3.3. Residual Stress Model
The residual stress model builds upon the multilayer model by introducing residual stresses arising
from hygrothermal effects. These stresses were induced by simulating the thermal changes experienced
during the curing process of composite laminates using a predefined temperature field. Due to the
mismatch in thermal expansion coefficients between Al 2024-T3 and GFRP, the two materials would
naturally deform differently under thermal loading. However, because the materials are bonded
together, they cannot deform freely, which leads to internal residual stresses in the specimen.
As discussed in subsection 3.3.3, the standard J-integral methodology used in the previous models is
no longer valid in the presence of residual stresses. To address this, the methodology proposed by
Toftegaard and Sørensen [8] was adopted. It involves a conceptual framework consisting of three stages:
the real problem, the thought experiment, and the auxiliary problem.
The Real Problem
The real problem represents the actual physical situation. A multilayer specimen is first exposed
to hygrothermal effects (temperature and/or moisture changes), which generate inelastic strains.
Mechanical loads are applied on top of this residual stress field, in the next step. The schematic of this
approach can be seen in Figure C.1 (a-b).
In this approach, the local J-integral can be computed from the bilinear cohesive law, since the internal
stress field does not invalidate that the energy release rate is derivable from a potential function.
However, the standard stiffness and curvature J-integral formulations are not valid due to the presence
of residual stress.
The Thought Experiment
The thought experiment is a conceptual approach used to separate the influence of residual stresses
from that of mechanical loading. The load steps for this approach are defined as follows:
1. Load Step 1: Hygrothermal conditions are applied while constraining the end displacements of
the specimen, as illustrated in situation (c) of Figure C.1. Alternatively, the same state can be
achieved by applying mechanical loads that counter the hygrothermal deformations, as shown in
situation (d).
2. Load Step 2: Mechanical loads equivalent to those induced by the hygrothermal conditions in the
real problem are applied. Additional mechanical loads can be applied to propagate the crack,
depicted as situation (e) in Figure C.1.
==PAGE:43==
5.3. Models
33
In Load Step 1, the constrained boundaries prevent any end opening, meaning that the crack opening
observed in Load Step 2 is solely due to the applied mechanical loads. This decouples the residual stress
effects from the crack-opening behaviour. However, since residual stresses are still present in Load Step
2, the J-integral remains path-dependent.
The Auxiliary Problem
The auxiliary problem replicates Load Step 2 of the thought experiment, but begins in a stress-free state,
as shown in situations (f) and (g) of Figure C.1. By the end of Load Step 1, both the thought experiment
and the auxiliary problem exhibit zero end openings. In Load Step 2, the same mechanical loads are
applied in both cases, resulting in identical cohesive zone openings. However, since no hygrothermal
conditions are applied in the auxiliary problem, the specimen remains free of residual stresses, thus
meeting the path independence criterion of the J-integral.
Application In This Work
The objective of the residual stress model in this study was to validate the J-integral formulations with
hygrothermal loading, by following the conceptual framework proposed by Toftegaard and Sørensen [8].
To do so, the framework was decomposed to validate the following criteria:
1. Equivalence of Situation (c) and (d): These must produce identical results in terms of zero
displacements and equivalent internal stress states. This step is critical, as only situation (d) can
be extended into Load Step 2 due to its unconstrained boundary conditions.
2. Opening Profile Consistency: The normal and tangential opening profiles at the end of Load
Step 2 should be identical in the real problem, the thought experiment, and the auxiliary problem.
3. J-Integral Validation: In the auxiliary problem, the extended J-integral formulations accounting
for hygrothermal effects, (Equation 3.7 and 3.8) must match Jloc, computed from the cohesive zone.
If these criteria are satisfied, the extended Jext formulations are validated to accurately determine the
energy release rate in the real residual stress problem.

6.1.1. Cohesive Parameter Calibration
The bilinear cohesive zone model is defined by three key parameters: fracture toughness, 𝐺𝑐, maximum
traction, 𝜏𝑐, and penalty stiffness, 𝐾. The first two parameters are intrinsic to the material, and for GFRP,

non-dimensional scaling parameter, 𝛼.

appropriate viscous regularization. The regularization coefficient, 𝜇(or 𝜂vr) was calibrated to prevent
convergence issues due to softening, as discussed in subsection 2.4.2, while ensuring that viscous
dissipation remained negligible compared to the total internal energy.

The introduction of tensile axial forces in LCM1N1 is expected to have no influence on the applied bending

In LCM2N1, the same displacement rate was applied to both beams, but the resulting axial forces
became asymmetric (𝑁1 = 2343.09N, 𝑁2 = 2676.80N). This was not the intended behaviour, as equal
displacement rates were expected to produce equal axial forces. The asymmetry arises from the
superposition of rotational and translational boundary conditions. In the LCM2 case, the prescribed
rotations cause the beam tips to move in the 𝑥1 direction by −0.0475 mm for beam 1 and +0.0475 mm
for beam 2. When a 2 mm axial displacement rate is applied to both beams (LCM2N1), the measured
total axial displacements at the end of the simulation is 2 mm for each beam. This means that the
axial forces must compensate for the rotation-induced offsets, contributing an additional 2.0475 mm
of displacement in beam 1 and only 1.9525 mm in beam 2. As a result, unequal axial reaction forces
develop to enforce the prescribed rates.
This imbalance in axial force contributes to additional end sliding in the same direction as the applied
moments. As a result, the required bending moments are slightly reduced in LCM2N1 compared to
LCM2, with 𝑀1 = 1744.12 N·mm and 𝑀2 = 1820.61 N·mm. The curvature in beam 3 also decreases,
with 𝜅0
3 = 0.189m−1 at 𝛿= 0.25mm. This reduction occurs because the axial forces induce a curvature in
beam 3 that counteracts the one caused by the bending moments.
In LCM1N2, the addition of tangential displacements to the Mode I baseline case (LCM1) reduces
the bending moment magnitudes significantly and introduces asymmetry between them (𝑀1 =
−163.24 N·mm, 𝑀2 = 1463.67 N·mm). The reduction in moment can be explained by the contribution of
opposing axial forces (𝑁1 = −829.52 N, 𝑁2 = +829.48N), which contribute in driving crack separation,
especially in the tangential direction. However, the mismatch between the moment magnitudes can
again be attributed to the way the prescribed rotation and displacement rates are enforced. The imposed
displacement rates leads to uneven rotations in beam 1 and 2, therefore, resulting in different moments
on each beam. This mismatch along with the axial forces 𝑁1 = −829.52 N and 𝑁2 = +829.48 N contribute
similarly to a significant reaction moment and curvature in Beam 3 (𝜅0
3 = −0.452 m−1).
LCM2N2 exhibits the intended behavior of Mode II-dominant loading, with symmetric moments and
equal but opposite axial forces. At 𝛿= 0.25 mm, the bending moments are lower than those in LCM2
(𝑀1 = 942.30 N·mm, 𝑀2 = 951.37 N·mm), and the axial forces are also reduced compared to LCN2
(𝑁1 = −788.64 N, 𝑁2 = +788.59N). In this case, both the moments and axial forces contribute towards
tangential separation in the same direction, enforcing mode II dominated fracture. Note that the
resulting curvature in beam 3 reaches 𝜅0
3 = −0.374 m−1, indicating that the applied moments and axial
forces induce counteracting curvature effects.

differences remain below 27 J/m2. At final separation and in the steady-state region, the percentage
errors in Jext remain below 3% in all cases except for LCN2, where the elevated errors are explained by
its distinct fracture behaviour and boundary interactions. The curvature-based formulation exhibits
similar accuracy to the stiffness-based one throughout all single material model tests. Therefore, these

Mode I and Mode II 𝐿ss values in the multilayer model is nearly twice as large as in the single material

The influence of tangential forces is again evident when comparing load cases such as LCM1 vs.
LCM1N2 and LCM2 vs. LCM2N2. LCM1N2 shows a phase angle of 28.61° (compared to 0.01° in LCM1),

trends suggest: the phase angle decreases as separation increases. The actual reason for the lower phase
angle in the multilayer model is that the chosen displacement rates are tuned to optimise convergence.
This means there is a smaller contribution to the tangential separations, making the fracture process
slightly more mode I.

Discussion of Results
7.1. Answers to Research Questions
This thesis has successfully addressed the research questions posed in chapter 4. The central research
question—How can the J-integral be accurately modelled for multilayered DCB specimens with
unknown material properties and residual stresses?—was answered by addressing the sub-questions
related to stiffness effects, residual stress behaviour, and analytical estimation methods.
What are the effects of varying the stiffness of the layers on the fracture process?
One of the goals of this work was to understand how increasing the stiffness of the layers affects the
fracture process in a DCB specimen. This was investigated by comparing the single material model with
the multilayer model, where aluminium layers were added to increase both extensional and bending
stiffness. The results showed that as the structure becomes stiffer, the cohesive zone also becomes longer.
This trend is consistent with the analytical scaling relationships given in Equation A.12 for mode I and
Equation A.21 for mode II. The increase in cohesive zone length was more pronounced in mode II,
growing from 13.0 mm to 15.0 mm, compared to a smaller increase in mode I, from 4.5 mm to 5.7 mm.
This difference is expected, since the fracture process zone (FPZ) scales with stiffness as 𝐸1/4 in mode I,
but as 𝐸1/2 in mode II. Therefore, increasing the layer stiffness leads to a longer cohesive zone, with a
stronger effect on mode II.
In load case LCN2, the change in stiffness had a clear effect on the tangential separation behaviour.
In the single material model, the applied axial forces were transmitted along the entire DCB length,
causing sliding even ahead of the crack tip. Because the beams were relatively compliant, this led to
pre-opening of the cohesive interface before the crack reached that region. As a result, the analytical
J-integral initially under-predicted the energy release rate, since less energy was required to advance the
crack. During steady-state propagation, the energy release rate was over-predicted by both the stiffness
and curvature formulations, suggesting that the pre-opening was interacting with the boundary of
beam 3.
In the multilayer model, the tangential opening ahead of the crack tip was nearly zero throughout
propagation. This suppression of pre-opening is not only due to higher overall stiffness, but also
because of the layered stiffness distribution. The aluminium face sheets provide high axial and bending
stiffness and resist global sliding. This concentrates the deformations around the crack tip and prevents
premature separation ahead of the crack front. As a result, the traction conditions at the crack plane
more closely match those assumed in the analytical J-integral, leading to improved accuracy in both
transient and steady-state phases.
The stiffer multilayer structure also required higher loads to reach the same crack opening. For example,
in LCM1 at 𝛿= 0.25 mm, the applied moment increased from around 1725 N·mm in the single material
model to over 2135 N·mm in the multilayer model. Despite the higher applied loads, the resulting axial
strains and curvatures were lower in the multilayer model. This indicates that the deformation behaviour
is closer to the assumptions of Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, such as small deflections and linear strain
distributions. As a result, both the stiffness-based and curvature-based J-integral formulations showed
lower errors in the multilayer model. This confirms that increasing layer stiffness improves the accuracy
of analytical Jext predictions.

How do residual stresses inﬂuence the behaviour of the cohesive zone and the J-integral?
Residual stresses influence both the behaviour of the cohesive zone and the accuracy of the J-integral
in several ways. The parametric studies in subsection 6.3.2 isolate the effects of temperature change,
stiffness mismatch, and thermal expansion mismatch, under pure hygrothermal loading. The sensitivity
plots in Figure 6.25 summarise their influence on total separation and phase angle.
For the temperature variation, the total separation 𝛿increases linearly with Δ𝑇because the hygrothermal
loads scale directly with temperature.
The thermal expansion coefficient study shows a similar
monotonic increase in 𝛿with 𝛼, as seen from Equation 3.9 where Δ𝑇and 𝛼appear in the same term
and therefore influence the loads identically. In contrast, the stiffness variation produces only small
changes in 𝛿because the compliance varies inversely with stiffness, offsetting the effect of changing
hygrothermal loads. At large deviations from the reference stiffness, the total separation decreases due
to 𝑀HT
3
causing mode I crack closure.
The phase angle trends in Figure 6.25b highlight the different sensitivities of the three studies. The
temperature variation produces 𝜙≈0◦for all cases, since the thermal loading is applied uniformly
and no mismatch is introduced between beams 1 and 2. In contrast, both the stiffness and thermal
expansion coefficient studies show increasing |𝜙| with greater percentage deviation from the reference.
The direction of the tangential opening is based on whether beam 1 is stiffer/more compliant or has a
higher/lower 𝛼than beam 2. The phase angle is most sensitive to changes in 𝛼because variations in 𝛼
alter the hygrothermal load mismatch without affecting compliance.
In the full load case study, the application of a prescribed temperature change generates hygrothermal
loads prior to mechanical loading. This superposed residual state influences the resulting mode mixity
in cases with Mode II components. Comparing the residual stress model to the multilayer model shows
that the added pure mode I moment shifts the phase angle toward lower values. This effect is especially
visible in LCN2, where the phase angle decreases from 89.91° in the multilayer model to 75.5° in the
residual stress model, indicating a shift toward more mixed-mode fracture.
The presence of high compressive axial forces also alters the mechanical response of the structure. These
forces increase the apparent stiffness, resulting in lower strains and curvatures for a given load. This
behaviour improves agreement with the assumptions of Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, enhancing the
accuracy of analytical J-integral estimates. For example, in LCM1, the residual stress model exhibited
smaller errors in both stiffness-based and curvature-based J-integrals compared to the multilayer case.
However, these high compressive loads introduce the risk of local buckling. In LCN2, the axial force
exceeds the critical buckling limit for a fixed-free beam, leading to convergence failure at higher CTOD
values. While these compressive loads can improve accuracy by bring the model closer to ideal analytical
conditions, they also create stability challenges that must be monitored.
To what extent can the analytical J-integral derived from strain and curvature measurements re-
liably estimate the energy release rate in materials with unknown material properties and resid-
ual stresses?
The analytical J-integral derived from strain and curvature measurements, Jcurv
ext , provides reliable
estimates of the energy release rate across all models and load cases considered in this study. Its
performance is comparable to the stiffness-based formulation, with the maximum absolute error
remaining below 40 J/m2 (or 2% of the fracture toughness) in all cases. To maintain this level of accuracy,
strain and curvature must be measured at locations sufficiently far from the crack tip. Measurements
taken too close to the fracture process zone may be affected by localized stress concentrations (referred
to as “stress bubbles”), which can lead to significant error in the prediction of the J-integral. This issue
does not affect the stiffness-based formulation, as it only relies on force and moment data.
Additionally, boundary interactions can distort the strain field and introduce further error if not
accounted for. These interactions influence both J-integral formulations, as the reaction forces and
bending moments reflect the influence of stiff support conditions.
It should also be noted that the residual stress model still requires some knowledge of the material
properties and the applied temperature change in order to accurately compute the hygrothermal loads.

Therefore, while the curvature-based J-integral is robust and widely applicable, its reliability depends
on careful placement of measurement points and a good understanding of the hygrothermal loading in
the DCB specimen.
7.2. Potential Sources of Error
Several sources of error and limitations were identified throughout the modelling and simulation
process. One important factor is the choice of viscous regularization. Increasing the regularization
value improves numerical stability and convergence, as seen in cases like LCM1N2, but it also delays
the onset of steady-state crack growth. This delay artificially increases the steady-state separation and
energy release rate values, making the interpretation of the results more difficult.
The deviations observed in the residual stress model for the LCM2N2 case require further investigation.
After final separation, the loading of the real problem showed noticeable differences compared to
both the thought experiment and the auxiliary problem. This behaviour is unexpected, as equivalent
hygrothermal loads were applied across all models, and the prescribed displacement and rotation rates
were identical.
The LCM1N2 case for the multilayer model exhibited convergence failure during steady-state propagation.
This failure cannot be attributed to compressive loads exceeding the critical buckling limit, as higher
compressive forces were observed in the LCN2 case, which still converged successfully. A more likely
explanation is that the high mode-mixity loading condition in LCM1N2 may be causing unstable crack
growth. However, this hypothesis is weakened by the fact that the same load case in the single material
model still achieved convergence. Therefore, the cause of this failure remains uncertain and warrants
further investigation.
Lastly, it is important to recognize the limitations of the analytical models used in this work. All
expressions rely on assumptions such as Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, small deformations, and uniform
material properties along the beam length. While these assumptions generally hold for most load cases
considered, their validity should always be verified, especially in cases involving large strains and/or
significant mode-mixity.

Conclusion
In this thesis, analytical formulations for the J-integral based on stiffness and curvature were validated
for double cantilever beam (DCB) specimens, with the aim of allowing fracture toughness evaluation
when material properties are unknown and residual stresses are present. The method was applied to
single material, multilayer, and residual stress model for a range of loading conditions. Finite element
simulations were performed using a surface-based bilinear cohesive zone model to assess the accuracy
of the analytical approach to predict the energy release rate.
In chapter 3, a comprehensive review of fracture mechanics concepts, cohesive zone modelling, residual
stress effects, and existing J-integral formulations was presented. This highlighted the need to further
validate the application of stiffness- and curvature-based J-integral methods to multilayer systems with
residual stresses. Based on this, the role of the thesis was established and in chapter 4, the essential
equations that guided the thesis were introduced.
In chapter 5, the modelling approach was defined in detail, including the assumptions made about
material behaviour, loading conditions, and boundary constraints. The FEM implementation was
described for the single material, multilayer, and residual stress models. This covered geometry
definition, mesh construction, and load application. Also, the post-processing routines to extract the
local and external J-integral values were presented.
In chapter 6, the single material model was used to calibrate the cohesive zone parameters and perform
mesh convergence studies around the cohesive interface. The calibrated model was then used to
evaluate multiple load cases, with the results showing strong agreement between the analytical and local
J-integral values for all conditions. The multilayer model showed that increasing stiffness extended the
cohesive zone length and improved analytical accuracy by aligning more closely with Euler-Bernoulli
beam theory assumptions. For the residual stress model, the opening profiles from the auxiliary
problem were found to match those of the real problem across variations in temperature, stiffness,
thermal expansion coefficient, and mechanical loading. This confirmed that the auxiliary problem (free
from residual stresses) could accurately represent the local fracture process zone of the real problem,
allowing validation of the analytical residual stress formulations.
In chapter 7, the results were interpreted in relation to the research questions. The discussion focused
on stiffness variations, residual stresses, and influence of measurement location on the reliability of the
curvature-based J-integral formulation. Key limitations, such as the influence of viscous regularization,
boundary interactions, and stability issues were identified.
This work validates the analytical J-integral equations for DCB specimens with unknown material
properties and residual stresses.
This is particularly relevant for aged or repurposed composite
structures, such as wind turbine blades, where material properties are unknown and residual stresses
can significantly influence fracture behaviour.
Future work should focus on extending the method to 3-dimensional models with more complex
geometries, such as asymmetric beam configurations.
Experimental validation using DCB tests
equipped with strain and curvature sensors would provide direct verification of the analytical equations
under real loading conditions. In addition, developing a residual stress formulation that eliminates
the need for material property data in hygrothermal load computation would make the analytical
expressions entirely independent of material properties. These advancements would broaden the
applicability of the framework to a wider range of composite structures, enabling more accurate and
efficient structural integrity assessments.

A.3. Limitations and Applicability of the Derivation
80
A.3. Limitations and Applicability of the Derivation
The analytical derivations for 𝐿ss in mode I and mode II rely on simplifying assumptions derived
from Euler-Bernoulli beam theory. Therefore, the above expressions can represent scaling laws rather
than exact computation of the cohesive zone length in real tests or simulations due to the following
limitations:
• No shear deformation: Beam theory ignores shear deformation, which can be significant in mode
II-dominated cases. This leads to an underestimation of the cohesive FPZ length.
• Homogeneous and isotropic: The derivation assumes that each beam has uniform, isotropic elastic
properties. In layered or anisotropic laminates, the bending stiffness, 𝐸𝐼should be replaced with
an effective stiffness calculated from laminate theory.
• Cross-section behaviour: Beam theory assumes that cross-sections remain plane and perpendicular
to the neutral axis, which may not hold near the cohesive zone and, especially in mode II, uneven
deformation across the thickness of the beam is prevalent.
• Cohesive law compliance: The final separation 𝛿𝑓is assumed to be known from the traction-
separation law in the cohesive model.
• Neglect of coupling effects: Bending-stretching or bending-twisting couplings are not considered.
These effects can be significant in unsymmetrical laminates.
Hence, the analytical 𝐿ss should be used as a tool to understand the scaling behaviour with geometry
and material parameters, rather than as a precise predictor of the cohesive FPZ length.
