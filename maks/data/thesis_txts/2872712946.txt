Project formulation 
As the healthcare industry aims to reduce its environmental footprint, biodegradable 
materials, such as polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs), have a large potential. These materials 
are derived from renewable resources, biodegradable, and versatile, making them 
promising candidates to replace fossil-based plastics. However, their adoption is slowed 
down by challenges, such as limited mechanical strength, sensitivity to environmental 
conditions, and variability in properties based on composition. In addition to the need 
for predictable degradation behaviour, these issues require further in-depth research. 
By adjusting blends and optimising formulations, PHAs can better meet the diverse 
demands of pharmaceutical delivery systems. This project addresses these challenges, 
using PHA’s potential while ensuring its suitability for applications, such as subcutaneous 
injection pens and others. The project’s objective is to develop a range of PHA-based 
materials. It will focus on different PHA blends with varying compositions to balance 
biodegradability and mechanical properties. For example, polylactic acid (PLA), 
polycaprolactone (PCL), or other derivatives can be excellent candidates. By 
systematically analysing these blends, a performance spectrum will be created, which 
ranges from materials with high degradability and low mechanical strength to blends 
with high mechanical properties, but limited biodegradability. This spectrum will help 
define the application span. The objective of the study is to identify the optimal blend 
composition for pharmaceutical delivery systems, focusing on biodegradability. The 
findings will help in the selection of the best PHA materials for subcutaneous injection 
pens and other applications. 
The study will be divided into two parts. First, a literature review will be performed, it will 
focus on studying current PHA blends in various applications by targeting compositions 
similar to the materials available for this project. The review will identify tested blends, 
evaluate their mechanical and biodegradability properties, as well as to analyse

hydrolysis data. In general, the review will concentrate on higher PHA content, 
specifically ranging from 50% to 80%. These are more challenging to obtain but are more 
relevant in substituting fossil-based plastics as they biodegrade faster and are fully 
biobased. A consideration in the literature review will be the manufacturability and 
scalability of the selected PHA blends. Additionally, the review will analyse which part of 
subcutaneous injection pens and oral delivery systems will benefit most from the use of 
biodegradable plastics.  
For the second part, the research will focus on material selection, material synthesis, and 
property testing of the selected PHA blends. Several formulations will be tested based 
on the literature findings by considering biodegradability and mechanical properties. 
The blends will undergo mechanical testing, including tensile strength, compression 
tests and other suitable tests and surface property testing, to evaluate their 
performance for pharmaceutical applications. Additionally, chemical hydrolysis will be 
done to analyse the potential biodegradability and stability of the blends. All the results 
will be used to optimise the blends and identify the best one for further development. If 
time permits, research into PHA from yeast waste or testing of other characteristics, like 
barrier properties for oral delivery systems, will be carried out.

Abstract 
This work investigates suitable polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) blends to use in Novo 
Nordisk’s injection pen application. Five PHA blends were analysed, each with varying 
ratios of PHAs, including poly-3-hydroxybutyrate-co-3-hydroxyvalerate (PHBV), poly-3-
hydroxybutyrate-co-3-hydroxyhexanoate (PHBH350), and poly-3-hydroxybutyrate-co-4-
hydroxybutyrate (P3HB4HB), with compositions ranging from 70:30 to 55:45 with or 
without talc. The study examined crystallisation kinetics, secondary crystallisation and 
mechanical properties through differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) and tensile 
testing. A life cycle analysis (LCA) was performed to evaluate carbon emissions, and the 
influence of different crystallisation rates on processing cycle time was studied. 
The Avrami model was applied to isothermal crystallisation of the blend and revealed 
that an equilibrated balance between semi-crystalline and amorphous PHAs, particularly 
with shorter hydrocarbon chains and a small talc addition, accelerated crystallisation. 
Tensile tests indicated a higher tensile strength and low creep behaviour for blends have 
a ratio close to 1:1. In addition, blends containing PHBH350 exhibited lower tensile 
strains and toughness but a larger Young’s Modulus. LCA results demonstrated a carbon 
footprint reduction of 3% to 5% with talc addition and optimised crystallisation rates. 
Finally, the blend containing 55 wt% PHBV and 45 wt% PHBH350 with a low amount of 
talc exhibited the best results in all measured parameters and was successfully moulded 
into a high precision component.

1. Introduction 
Over the last 25 years, the global demand for plastic has increased at an unprecedented 
rate, reaching 414 million tons in 2023 [1]. If the current consumption trend persists, 
production is projected to surpass one billion tons annually by 2050, posing significant 
environmental and economic challenges [2]. While plastics offer many advantages, such 
as low price and ease of fabrication, they are also responsible for long-lasting 
environmental effects and the release of microplastics, directly impacting the 
environment and human health [3].  
The pharmaceutical sector significantly relies on single-use or disposable plastic in 
medical devices, most of which end up in landfills. Therefore, given the large 
environmental problem associated with fossil-based plastic, a focus on sustainable 
materials 
is 
needed. 
Biobased 
and/or 
biodegradable 
polymers, 
such 
as 
polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) are promising candidates [4]. PHAs are biobased, non-
toxic and biodegradable, making them a great option for sustainable medical devices. 
However, many drawbacks remain and need to be addressed, such as their low 
processing window, their brittleness, high production cost and large amount of waste 
involved in the production [5]. While many studies have explored PHA blends with other 
biopolymers using solvent casting, only limited research has been performed on 100% 
PHA blends processed via injection moulding.  
The thesis aims to identify, through characterisation, a suitable blend for pharmaceutical 
applications that balances processability and mechanical performance while being 
sustainable.

2. Literature Review 
2.1. Overview of PHAs and their potential  
Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHAs) have been the focus of extensive research for several 
decades due to their potential to replace fossil-based plastics [6]. With most industries 
transitioning to more sustainable practices, there has been a growing emphasis on 
biopolymers, particularly PHAs. They are a class of renewable thermoplastic polyester 
and are synthesised by microorganisms. Under optimised conditions, with a high carbon 
concentration and low oxygen and nitrogen content, certain bacteria will produce PHAs 
as an energy reserve. Depending on the energy source, the strain of the microorganism 
and the fermentation type, the type of PHA will differ [7]. The main advantages of PHAs 
are their ability to be engineered to enhance specific properties, their non-toxicity and 
biodegradability. This allows them to be used in a large panel of applications, ranging 
from medical applications, such as drug delivery and implants, to clothing or packaging 
[8]. It must be noted that biodegradation is a system property, if not properly managed, 
biopolymers also have end-of-life issues. Therefore, the right environment with 
microorganisms, moisture and the right temperature is required [9]. 
In general, PHAs are showing a high stereoregularity, slow crystallisation rate, and 
formation of large-sized spherulites, which are crystals that are radiating from a 
common centre. In addition, secondary crystallisation is common and is one of the most 
consequential drawbacks of PHAs. This process occurs after the primary crystallisation, 
and other crystals are formed, which have a lower quality and result in higher density 
and more shrinkage. This ageing process lowers the mechanical properties of the 
polymer and limits its storage at room temperature [10]. Therefore, the application span 
is limited to basic applications, which do not require high mechanical properties [11]. To 
address their limitations concerning mechanical properties, crystallisation behaviour, 
==PAGE:14==

Page | 4  
 
and thermal stability, blending with other types of PHA, synthesis of co-polymers or 
incorporating additives or fillers is a commonly used solution [12]. 
Most of the PHAs can be divided into three main categories depending on their chain 
length. First, short-chain length PHAs (scl-PHAs), which consist of 3 to 5 carbons and are 
similar to Polypropylene (PP) in terms of crystallinity and melting point. Medium chain 
length PHAs (mcl-PHAs), which are 6 to 14 carbon long and have lower tensile strength, 
melting point, and higher elongation [8]. Lastly, long chain length PHAs (lcl-PHAs), which 
are rarer but more flexible, consist of 17 to 18 carbons [13]. With more than 150 different 
types of PHA, the most important and researched are listed below. Figure 1 and Table 
1 display the structure and chemical name of the main types of PHA, respectively [12]. 
They are separated into two categories, homo-polymers, which are more brittle and 
have a higher crystallisation rate and co-polymers.  
The latter exhibit enhanced flexibility, elongation break, and toughness, but require a 
higher degree of control in the synthesis process and additional steps. From the list in 
Table 1, the main homo-polymers and co-polymers, PHB, PHV, PHO, PHBV, PHBHHx and 
P3HB4HB will be discussed further [12]. These types of PHA have been the most 
researched, are sold on a global scale, and possess a straightforward synthesis process, 
making them more promising for replacing fossil-based plastics [14] [8].

2.1.1. PHA homo-polymers (PHB, PHV and PHO) 
Homo-polymers consist of the repetition of the same monomer unit [15]. The most 
researched PHA homopolymer is poly(3-hydroxybutyrate) (P3HB or PHB), a scl-PHA that 
is classified as a semi-crystalline polyester with similar properties to PP. It can be found 
as part of a co-polymer or as a virgin material. In addition, it has better barrier properties 
than PP and has lower flexibility [16]. Due to the strong intermolecular interaction from 
the methyl functional group and the rigid ester linkage, the material possesses a 
remarkably high degree of crystallinity, reaching up to 70%. This characteristic also 
contributes to its hydrophobic properties [17]. However, this high purity leads to high 
brittleness with a low strain at break at around 5% [12]. One of its main disadvantages is 
the narrow margin between the degradation temperature, approximately 220°C, and 
==PAGE:16==
Evaluation of Polyhydroxyalkanoates-Based Blends for Sustainable Pharmaceutical Delivery Systems 
 
Page | 6  
 
the melting temperature, approximately 180°C, resulting in a challenging processing 
window [18].  
Poly(3-hydroxyvalerate) (P3HV or PHV), in contrast to PHB, has received less research 
attention as a virgin homopolymer material. Nonetheless, its compatibility in blends with 
other PHAs has been the subject of research and will be further explored in Section 
2.1.2. and Section 2.2.1, which discusses the modification of PHAs.  
Poly(3-hydroxyoctanoate) (P3HO or PHO), an mcl-PHA, was reported to have a lower 
crystallinity than PHB, accounting for 37.5% [19]. Due to its longer side chain, the polymer 
exhibits elastomeric properties, up to 1000% extension to break. Moreover, it is semi-
crystalline and has a moderate hydrophobicity [20]. Other characteristics that make PHO 
interesting are its thermal properties, such as a melting temperature around 60 °C. 
Considering the thermal decomposition temperature of 300 °C, its processing window 
is much wider than PHB [21].  
2.1.2. PHA co-polymers (PHBV, PHBHHx and P3HB4HB) 
Co-polymers consist of the repetition of multiple, at least two different, monomer units 
[15]. In general, they have a higher potential to be used for advanced applications as their 
properties can be adapted by varying the content of each monomer. The main PHA 
copolymers include PHB, along with another monomer. PHB is the most common 
material produced by cells, making it one of the most studied PHAs. Therefore, co-
polymerisation to improve PHB properties leads to an optimised material, such as 
flexibility, easy to process, and has excellent barrier properties. 
One of the most researched PHA co-polymer is Poly(3-hydroxybutyrate-co-3-
hydroxyvalerate) (PHBV), it is a scl-PHA that includes PHV units into PHB in a repeating 
order. The combination of both monomers improved the flexibility and strength of the 
PHB [22]. A higher content of PHV will lead to a lower crystallinity content, a higher

elongation at break and a lower melting temperature [23]. Most of the commercially 
available PHBV grades have a mol% of 3HV units between 5% and 15% to ensure 
optimised properties. PHBV has great potential as it exhibits high barrier properties, 
non-toxicity, and lower brittleness than PHB while keeping a high tensile strength. It also 
has a lower melting temperature than PHB, which improves its processing efficiency. 
However, it still has a high crystalline content, which leads to brittleness and low impact 
resistance [24].  
Similarly, 
poly 
(3-hydroxybutyrate-co-3-hydroxyhexanoate) 
(PHBHHx) 
is 
the 
incorporation of 3HHx monomers into PHB. This results in a lower degree of crystallinity 
and melting temperature, which ultimately broadens the processing window. The 
incorporation of elastomeric units from PHHx in the crystalline polymer PHB, similarly to 
PHBV, enables the possibility to fine-tune the final properties. Similarly to PHBV, while 
the content of the incorporated chains is increasing, the flexibility and the elongation at 
break are increasing, but the tensile strength and the modulus are decreasing [25].  
Lastly, poly(3-hydroxybutyrate-co-4-hydroxybutyrate) (P3HB4HB) is the result of 4HB 
units that were incorporated into P3HB. At a low content of 4HB units, it can be 
considered semi-crystalline, while at a high content, it is considered an amorphous 
rubber. However, this decrease in crystallinity results in lower barrier properties 
compared to PHB [26].   
2.2. PHAs blend and their potential 
2.2.1. Strategies for modifying PHA properties  
As previously discussed, PHA co-polymers have enhanced properties compared to 
homo-polymers. However, several other strategies can further improve chemical, 
mechanical and biodegradable characteristics of PHAs. Techniques such as plasticiser, 
nanocomposite reinforcement, post-processing techniques, and blending have been 
==PAGE:18==
Evaluation of Polyhydroxyalkanoates-Based Blends for Sustainable Pharmaceutical Delivery Systems 
 
Page | 8  
 
widely used to improve material properties [27]. This research examines the possibilities 
and impact of blending as a method to optimise PHA properties. Polymer blends are a 
mixture of two or more homo- or co-polymers [15]. PHA blends can be divided into two 
categories: blends with other bioplastics such as PLA or PCL, and blends including two 
types of PHA. This research aims to investigate blends that are composed totally of PHAs, 
as it favours biodegradability and has been less researched, while having a large 
potential. To assess the quality and feasibility of the blends, their miscibility, thermal and 
mechanical properties, and chemical stability have to be analysed.   
In the following part, the most common blends found in the literature are reviewed. It is 
important to note that due to limited literature on blend composition prepared through 
injection moulding and extrusion, research was expanded, and many reviewed blends 
have been prepared through solvent casting. It is a method that consists of dissolving 
the polymers into chloroform at 50°C to  70°C [28]. This method offers many advantages, 
such as ease of dissolution and preparation on a laboratory scale and lower 
temperature, which decreases the degradation. Therefore, it leads to fast analysis and 
evaluation of blends. However, the focus of this research remains on different 
processing methods that are industrially scalable, such as extrusion and injection 
moulding, which is not the case of solvent casting. 
The blend of PHBV and an amorphous P3HB4HB is highly regarded for its combination 
of a more crystalline and brittle PHA with an amorphous and flexible PHA, making it a 
very promising option. Wang et al. determined that blending P3HB4HB with 19.2 mol% 
of 4HB and PHBV with 5.7 mol% of 3HV through solvent-casting, led to complete 
miscibility of the polymers. It was also found that the more P3HB4HB was added, the 
more the melting point was reduced, until 178 °C. This was to be expected, as a more 
amorphous content slows down the isothermal crystallisation rate and decreases the 
interaction between both polymers. In terms of mechanical properties, when increasing 
==PAGE:19==
Evaluation of Polyhydroxyalkanoates-Based Blends for Sustainable Pharmaceutical Delivery Systems 
 
Page | 9  
 
the P3HB4HB content, no trend was observed for tensile strength, while the elastic 
modulus dropped considerably, and the elongation at break and ductility increased [28]. 
Another combination involves blending PHBV and PHBHHx, which similarly combines a 
more crystalline and brittle PHA with a more amorphous and ductile PHA. This blend 
exhibits a high modulus of elasticity and yield strength with a low elongation at break. It 
was seen that the crystal content dominates in this blend and that the toughening effect 
was lower than expected. However, the processing window of the blend was greatly 
improved when the content of PHBH was increased [29] [30]. Tsui and Frank confirmed the 
miscibility across all compositions of the two PHAs, as a single Tg, using PHBV with 5 
mol% of 3HV and PHBHHx with 7 mol% of 3HH  [31]. 
The third combination of co-polymer consists of PHBHHx and P3HB4HB. In the study of 
Luo et al., PHBHHx with 12 mol% of 3HHx and P3HB4HB with 12 mol% of 4HB were 
blended through solvent-casting. It was found that the PHBHHx/P3HB4HB blend with a 
1:2 weight ratio was the best candidate for higher strain and strength. Regarding the 
former, it had a decomposition temperature of 280°C, a glass transition temperature of 
-3°C and a melting temperature of 123°C. This large window between Td and Tm is 
favourable as it eases the processing of the blend. Regarding mechanical properties, the 
material's tensile strength was just 50% that of polypropylene, and its Young's modulus 
was one-third that of PP, indicating its weakness [32].

As it was shown, a lot of the discussed blends were prepared by solvent casting. It was

2.2.2. Processing techniques  
The objective of this research is to focus on techniques that are primarily utilised in the 
industry. Such techniques involve extrusion or injection moulding. In addition, melt 
blending is often coupled with these techniques to ensure that the added polymer is 
finely dispersed into the polymer matrix. A perfect mixing and blending are important 
steps as they aim to avoid thermal decomposition later. Therefore, it is important to set 
a temperature that exceeds both melting points to ensure sufficient melt flowability, 
while remaining below their degradation temperature. This temperature sensitivity for 
the processing of the blends is one of the largest challenges [34].

The first technique, which is one of the most used in the plastic industry, is extrusion. 
The polymers are melted at high temperatures and compressed by the helical screw to 
ensure homogeneity and removal of air. The speed, size, temperature, and type of screw 
are important parameters to optimise the final properties of the material [35]. Elodie et al. 
advises to use a single screw extruder, to reduce shear stress, at a temperature ranging 
from 145°C to 170°C with a 4 mm die and a screw speed of 20 RPM and cooled into water 
to create pellets [36].  
Injection moulding is another common processing technique. It consists of a reciprocal 
screw that melts the polymer and bring it into a mould for it to cool down and solidify. 
In this method, the cooling time is one of the crucial parts as it will have a direct impact 
on the crystallisation. Lee et al. listed several recommendations for the injection 
moulding of PHAs. For the processing temperature, it should range from 160°C at the 
nozzle to 176°C at the feeding zone. The screw speed should be set between 50 RPM and 
150 RPM. Lastly, the mould temperature should be between 49°C and 60°C before 
cooling, as it will optimise the crystallisation process [35]. If a temperature lower than 
160°C is used, it is beneficial to use a low speed screw as the viscosity will be higher 
compared to higher temperatures [36].  
As presented, PHAs can be processed via these methods. However, as limited research 
has been done on the processing of 100% PHA polymer blends via injection moulding 
and extrusion, the following section will focus on PHA blends with other biopolymers.

(PLA). While there are different methods to process this blend, all methods require 
drying the polymer. The first method is via injection moulding; the polymers were mixed 
and injection moulded. From the hopper end to the nozzle, the temperature ranges from 
140°C to 170°C, with one zone at 180°C. The mould temperature was set at 20°C [37]. The

2.2.3.  Role of crystallisation 
Processing techniques, parameters and blend composition were discussed in depth 
previously. Their aim is to obtain optimal mechanical properties for certain applications. 
Another parameter that has a high impact on mechanical properties is crystallisation. It 
corresponds to the degree of structural order in which the polymer chains are arranged 
regularly. Depending on processing and composition, crystallisation will vary, higher 
crystallisation leads to higher tensile strength and rigidity but lower flexibility.  
In general, crystallisation occurs through nucleation and growth processes and can be 
divided into two stages, primary and secondary crystallisation. The first occurs relatively 
fast during the cooling, spherulites are growing radially at a fast rate until they impinge 
on each other, and the growth rate then decreases drastically. As there is no more space, 
secondary crystallisation or post-crystallisation happens, which is the reorganisation of 
the amorphous phase and often occurs later, such as during storage. Its mechanism 
involves lamellar thickening and lamella infill, which is referred to as imperfect 
crystallisation and leads to more brittleness and lowers the final mechanical 
properties[45].  
The characterisation method that is mainly used for crystallisation kinetics is differential 
scanning calorimetry (DSC), which analyses heat flow and temperature changes related 
to thermal transitions in the material. The polymer sample is first heated above its 
melting point and below its decomposition temperature to erase its thermal history and 
is followed by being cooled down to the desired crystallisation temperature. Another 
protocol is to perform two heating cycles from the glass transition temperature to a 
temperature above its melting point to analyse all the thermal transitions. Heating and 
cooling rate, temperature range and isothermal protocols need to be adapted to the 
polymer.  
==PAGE:26==

point. Near the melting temperature, chain mobility is very high, which limits a stable 
nucleus formation. Near the glass transition temperature, chain mobility is very low and 
results in a large number of small spherulites. Therefore, the optimal crystallisation is at 
a compromise between both [46]. For PHAs, it occurs between -10°C and 170°C and can 
be analysed via DSC studies.

can be calculated by measuring the enthalpy of fusion of the polymer via DSC and using

Post-crystallisation is an important consideration as it occurs in a later stage, where the 
environment isn’t controlled and, in most cases, weakens the polymer. Robbins explains

temperature is also responsible. Higher storage temperature leads to higher secondary 
crystallisation as the polymer is in its rubbery state above its Tg. Under its Tg, in the glassy 
state, secondary crystallisation will diminish but its brittleness might increase.  This

Robbins studied the secondary crystallisation by monitoring the change of properties 
over time at different temperatures via DSC and mechanical testing. Concluding that 
higher temperature and storage time increase post-crystallinity and stiffness [46]. 
2.3. Research gaps 
The majority of the literature related to PHA blends and their processing was presented. 
Therefore, it is now possible to identify the main research gaps. Firstly, most research 
regarding PHA blends has been done on blends of PHA with another bioplastic. Research 
on pure PHA blend hasn’t been done on a large scale and offers many opportunities. 
Secondly, as we discussed, most of the 100% PHA blends are processed via solvent 
casting, while the method offers many advantages at a laboratory scale, it doesn’t apply 
to an industrial production scale. Some studies showed the possibility of processing high 
content PHA blends with other bioplastics, and a few with 100% PHA, via extrusion and 
injection moulding. Therefore, studies on 100% PHA blends that were processed via 
extrusion or injection moulding have a large potential. Another gap in the research is 
degradation studies. Some research has been done on PHA with other bioplastics or on 
neat PHA. However, there aren’t many studies on the biodegradability of 100% PHA 
blends. In addition, analysing conditions in which the blends have a different 
degradation rate, such as humidity, UV radiation and aquatic environment, would be 
beneficial as most plastics end up in landfills or oceans. Another point that can be 
addressed is the crystallisation of the polymers, while it is a studied field, the 
optimisation of crystallisation is challenging to achieve. Especially regarding 
crystallisation temperatures, degree of crystallisation and post-crystallisation. The latter 
leads to a decrease in mechanical properties over time, and regarding 100% PHA blends, 
==PAGE:29==
Evaluation of Polyhydroxyalkanoates-Based Blends for Sustainable Pharmaceutical Delivery Systems 
 
Page | 19  
 
only a few studies have been performed. As brittleness is one of the largest challenges 
for PHA blends, addressing crystallisation will directly offer opportunities to improve 
mechanical properties. In general, this literature review gave an overview of PHA blends, 
and it was often discussed that optimising mechanical properties is very important but 
also difficult. As the ductility and flexibility are enhanced, it leads to a loss of strength 
and Young's modulus. Most of the PHAs with high strength are very brittle. As was seen 
previously, this can be optimised via different processing parameters. Regarding PHA 
blends, few studies have been performed, although it is an important subject. 
2.4. Opportunity 
After identifying the main research gaps, it is possible to identify the research 
opportunities that will complement current literature. Firstly, it is important to select the 
PHA blends that have the highest potential, PHBV/PHBH and PHBV/P3HB4HB will be 
studied in priority. As the exact composition wasn’t studied, analysing different 
proportions and compositions of the blends would be interesting. Regarding processing 
techniques, injection moulding offers the best opportunity as it is used at an industrial 
scale and has shown promising results on other blends.  
As discussed, crystallinity has a direct impact on the mechanical properties of the PHA 
blend and puts it at the centre of attention in order to have a polymer that can be used 
in industry. Therefore, crystallisation studies offer a large opportunity for the 
understanding of mechanical properties. In addition, optimising crystallisation time, 
cooling time, temperature, and minimising post-crystallisation will lead to optimising 
processing at an industrial scale and have a positive, sustainable and economic impact. 
DSC studies combined with mechanical testing would allow for a better understanding 
of the crystallisation process, thermal behaviour and ageing impact on the different 
selected polymer blends.

The observed curves are divided into several phases. First, the primary nucleation is 
initiated, and the transformation is slow. The next stage occurs when there is a rapid 
increase in the growth rate of the crystal until it reaches a critical point. The last part is a 
strong decrease in the rate as the portion in a melt state is very small and slowly hinders 
further crystallisation [46]. For the temperatures 124 °C and 126 °C, phase 1 is very short

The results corresponded to the trend seen in literature, with an increase in the n factor 
and a decrease in the rate with higher temperatures [46].  In addition, most of the n values 
are around 3, which aligns with PHB studies with the Avrami model. These n values 
indicate a three-dimensional spherulitic growth process that started by heterogeneous 
nucleation.  Additionally, a mixed growth with surface nucleation and a two-step 
crystallisation is present [57]. It suggests a complex crystallinity structure with structural 
irregularities with varying crystal size. This could lead to an increase in mechanical 
strength but an increase in brittleness. The double peak on the DSC thermograph in 
Figure 7 aligns with this theory, while also indicating that this irregularity is not 
significant.

Talc is known for its acceleration of crystallisation and increase in crystallinity as it 
provides heterogeneous nucleation sites, leading to faster creation of smaller crystals 
[59]. From the different rates measured at 132 °C, it can be observed that when comparing 
blend F1 and F2, talc is slowing down the crystallisation rate. Its effect on the 
crystallisation rate will depend on the blend composition. Battegazzore et al. and Qui et 
al. report an acceleration of the crystallisation, as talc acts as a nucleation agent with PLA 
and PP [59, 60]. Aminishahsavarani et al. also found that the crystallisation rate increases 
when talc is added to PHBV [61]. When comparing the blends F3 and F11, the 
crystallisation rate is faster for the latter. While the blends have a different ratio, a lower 
amount of talc could accelerate the crystallisation, as seen in the literature. Due to the 
unavailability of a material containing a 55:45 ratio of PHBV/PHBH350 without talc, 
quantifying the influence of talc isn’t possible. However, it can be presumed that it 
accelerated the crystallisation. The measurement displaying a reduction in crystallisation 
rate with talc could be explained by too large amount. Blends with varying amounts of 
talc would be interesting to study, as a lower amount could be beneficial for the 
crystallisation, and a higher amount could slow down the crystallisation.  
Another parameter that was changed was the type of amorphous PHA used in the blend. 
When comparing the blend F1 and F3, it can be seen that the crystallisation occurs faster 
with P3HB4HB than with PHBH350. This can be explained by looking at the chemical 
structure, PHBH350 contains hexanoate units, which are longer hydrocarbon chains 
compared to the butyrate units in P3HB4HB. PHBH350 reduces the kinetics of PHBV but 
doesn’t reduce the final degree of crystallinity. The blend F3 has a lower crystallinity due 
to the longer chains, which are more flexible and are longer to crystallise. Another 
possibility is the compatibility of the two polymers. However, this contradicts the fast 
crystallisation rate of the blend F11. Therefore, another option could be the nucleation 
==PAGE:47==
Evaluation of Polyhydroxyalkanoates-Based Blends for Sustainable Pharmaceutical Delivery Systems 
 
Page | 37  
 
rate. The PHBV/PHBH350 blend has a lower nucleation rate due to fewer nucleating sites, 
which is overcome when the correct amount of talc is added.  
The last parameter that has an influence on the crystallisation rate is the semi-crystalline 
to amorphous PHA ratio. When comparing F1 and F4, it can be observed that a ratio with 
an amount of PHBV that is too high (70) leads to a slower crystallisation. When also 
observing the blend F11, it can be seen that it is the fastest by having a 55:45 ratio of 
PHBV/PHBH350. Therefore, having a more equilibrated ratio between the 2 virgin 
polymers leads to better compatibility and more effective molecular interaction to 
enhance the crystallisation rate. The blends F1 and F4 have similar crystallinity but 
different crystallisation rates. The blend F1 may have a higher concentration of PHBV, 
which might restrict the mobility of the PHBH chains, slowing down crystallisation. 
From this comparison, a low amount of talc, an equilibrated ratio and the P3HB4HB 
virgin polymer are the best options to accelerate the crystallisation rate. Therefore, it 
would be interesting to process a blend composed of a 55:45 ratio of PHBV/P3HB4HB 
with 2.5 eq of talc.

compared to the more amorphous regions. The blends F1, F2 and F3 have similar tensile 
stresses, around 20 MPa. However, for the 3 blends, their tensile strain is positively 
correlated to their crystallinity. This is an interesting result as a higher amorphous 
content usually leads to a higher elongation. In this case, the blend F1 has the highest 
crystallinity, 42%, and the highest elongation, while the blend F3 has the lowest 
crystallinity, 32%, and the lowest elongation. It is interesting to point out that blends 
containing PHBH350 have the lowest tensile strain. Therefore, the more flexible 
properties of PHBH350 compared to P3HB4HB do not translate into the final properties 
of the blend. 
The increase in Young's modulus from F1 to F11 suggests a transition from more ductile 
materials to stiffer ones. This supports what was explained in the tensile test curves 
previously. As Young’s modulus increases, the material becomes less able to deform 
elastically under stress. Therefore, the blends like F11 would be better suited for 
applications requiring rigidity and structural integrity. If a blend with a higher toughness 
and higher strain is needed, while keeping a high tensile stress and young modulus, the 
blend F4 would be a good candidate as a middle ground. In addition, it was the second 
fastest to crystallise. 
Blends composed of P3HB4HB have a larger tensile strain and flexibility compared to 
PHBH. In addition, blends with ratios that are more equilibrated exhibit higher tensile 
stress and Young's modulus but lower toughness. Regarding talc, it acts as a 
reinforcement agent which improves the stress distribution, restricts polymer chain 
mobility and improves the energy absorption. These characteristics are translated into 
a lower strain and higher toughness when comparing the blends F1 and F2.

4.5. Life cycle analysis 
A life cycle analysis (LCA) was performed to analyse the sustainable impact of the 
transition from PP components to PHA components. This LCA study followed the 
standard methodology outlined in the ISO 14040 series by the International 
Organisation for Standardisation (ISO). The LCA methodology is divided into four main 
stages: defining the goal and scope, conducting an inventory analysis, performing an 
impact assessment, and interpreting the results. The analysis was done in EcoInvent. 
The LCA was performed for a cartridge holder for one of Novo Nordisk’s devices. 
4.5.1. Goal and Scope 
The goal of the LCA can be divided into two objectives. First, a life cycle analysis was 
performed to assess the projected sustainability impact of the PHA blend compared to 
PHA currently and PP, POM and PC/PBT, the current materials used in most of Novo 
Nordisk’s devices or in development. The LCA analysis covered hot spots of the PHA 
component production from the end of life. Furthermore, the environmental 
performance of the 5 blends based on their different crystallisation time and talc 
addition was performed. The different crystallisation times will influence the overall cycle 
time during injection moulding. As a longer cycle time will require more energy, the 
sustainability impact will vary depending on the blends. 
The functional unit was set as the production of 1,000 PHA cartridge holder parts 
globally. It is intended for one-time use, and it is disposed of in landfills. 
The key assumptions and limitations used in the analysis are the following. 
a. Blend composition. When comparing the different PHA blends, the virgin PHAs 
weren’t taken into account, only the energy requirement during injection 
moulding and the talc were changed. 
==PAGE:55==
Evaluation of Polyhydroxyalkanoates-Based Blends for Sustainable Pharmaceutical Delivery Systems 
 
Page | 45  
 
b. Crystallisation time. The blend F11 was used as a baseline for the injection 
moulding energy, as data was only recorded for this blend. The other blends were 
compared based on their isothermal crystallisation time recorded previously. A 
translation of 1:1 will be performed by considering the factor at which the other 
blends crystallised more slowly at 132 °C. 
c. Impact categories. This study focuses on global warming potential (GWP) (kg 
CO2-eq). Other environmental impact categories are outside the defined scope. 
d. Mass allocation. The comparison of the different materials used by Novo Nordisk 
was done via mass allocation.

product. However, it doesn’t have the same mechanical properties.

4.5.4. Interpretation 
Injection moulding is responsible for 40% of the PHA life cycle, making it the largest 
process in terms of carbon footprint. Therefore, increasing the crystallisation rate and 
reducing the cycle time will directly influence one of the carbon emission hotspots. 
It can be inferred from the result that the talc influences the final carbon emissions, as 
its incorporation diminishes the carbon emissions. This can be observed with the blends 
F2 and F3, while using similar energy, talc reduces its carbon footprint by 3% to 5%. As 
==PAGE:62==

Page | 52  
 
the production of talc is less carbon-emitting than PHA, adding talc as a filler will reduce 
the carbon footprint and act as a nucleating agent if added in the right proportions.  
It needs to be noted that even if the blends F2 and F4 have one of the lowest carbon 
emissions, they also have a slower cycle time, which will slow down the efficiency and 
reduce their attractiveness at an industrial scale. From a cost perspective, F4 will have 
the lowest price per piece in terms of material due to the addition of talc. However, due 
to a longer cycle time, it will not be profitable. Therefore, an addition of talc will reduce 
the price only if the crystallisation time is lowered or similar without talc. 
When changing the ratio used for the variable electricity to 2:1 instead of 1:1, the trend 
persists, with F11 as the lowest carbon-emitting blend and F3 being the highest. Results 
can be seen in Appendix C. 
From the processing of PHA parts compared to PP, it was recorded that the cycle time 
was 17% faster with the blend F11. In addition, as the temperature is lower (approx. 40 
°C to 50 °C), the blend F11 requires 25% less energy than PP.  
Lastly, an environmental risk assessment (ERA) study was performed to evaluate the 
human health and ecological risk associated with a PHA cartridge finishing in a landfill. 
Anaerobic degradation was studied, and it was concluded that there are no harmful 
effects on aquatic environments and human health. All the PEC/PNEC ratios were below 
1, indicating no interference with their environment. The study can be found in

5. Conclusion 
The thesis evaluated various polyhydroxyalkanoate blends for their potential application 
in sustainable pharmaceutical devices with an aim to injection mould complex 
components of injection pens. Through the analysis of the 5 different PHA blends, it was 
shown that blend composition significantly influences the crystallisation kinetics, 
mechanical properties and slightly influences carbon emissions. The Avrami model 
applied to isothermal crystallisation showed which blend would increase the processing 
efficiency and gave insight into the effect of the different changes in composition. 
compared to F11. In addition, the crystallisation mechanism was identified as a three-
dimensional spherulitic growth process that started by heterogeneous nucleation. 
The blend F11 (55 wt% PHBV and 45 wt% PHBH350 with talc) demonstrated good overall 
performance with a balance of fast crystallisation, highest tensile strength and low 
creep, and a low carbon footprint. Even though it had a high Tg and was the most brittle, 
it was chosen as the material to be injection moulded and showed high dimensional 
accuracy during processing. In comparison to PP, the blend F11 has comparable tensile 
strength, Young modulus and melting temperature. However, its tensile strain, 
toughness and resistance to degradation differ substantially. From the composition 
comparison, a new blend containing 55 wt% PHBV and 45 wt% P3HB4HB with a low 
amount of talc could lead to an increase in ductility and crystallisation rate. This would 
ultimately lead to a reduced processing cycle time with higher strain and toughness. 
This research establishes a foundation for future studies on accelerated ageing or 
robustness studies. As it was proven to be injection moulded into a detailed component, 
it also opens the door for further optimisation of material properties by focusing on the 
addition of nucleating agents to accelerate crystallisation, reduce the glass transition 
temperature, or enhance flexibility. In addition, a deeper end-of-life analysis is required.

attained 70% of the yield stress recorded previously. The stress was kept constant for 20 
minutes, and the elongation was recorded.

8.4. Appendix D 
In order to complement the LCA of PHAs, an environmental risk assessment (ERA) was 
performed to assess the possible effects of the product on the environment. It involves 
the collection and analysis of data on potential environmental consequences, assessing 
the likelihood and severity of impacts and implementing strategies to mitigate or 
prevent those risks [68]. According to the European Chemicals Agency, the ERA for 
chemical substances relies on comparing the predicted environmental concentration 
(PEC) with the predicted no-effect concentration (PNEC) [69]. The ecological and human 
health risks were determined using the ERA methodology described in Figure A5.
