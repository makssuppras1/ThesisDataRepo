Abstract
The use of remote sensing for Earth observation is an ever-growing area in the com-
mercialized satellite technology industry, with optical and radar payloads expected to
be used in the majority of these markets. CubeSats are a key component in developing
new space technology for this purpose.
To offer a low-cost, quick-to-market solution, this project presents a preliminary
design of a CubeSat with a radar payload for flexible Earth observation tasks. The
design, developed through systems engineering methods, integrates a SAR payload
in a GOMspace 12U CubeSat platform with required components.
This master’s thesis examines the development of a small yet complex satellite
mission for Earth observation using systems engineering techniques. The iterative
process is used to establish the mission’s requirements, based on simulations and
analyses of individual subsystems.
The proposed mission aims to detect dark ships around Greenland using a 12kg
CubeSat equipped with a Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) in a limited water body
area. Though lacking analyses in some engineering disciplines, the proposed space-
craft is detailed down to the subsystem level and deemed feasible for the task. Despite
their specialization, CubeSat-sized spacecraft are now developed enough to support
complex Earth observation missions, thanks to NewSpace.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The maritime industry is facing numerous challenges including the threat of illegal
activities such as smuggling, piracy, and illegal fishing by dark ships that disable
their automatic identification system transponders to avoid detection. The presence
of dark ships is a major challenge in detecting and preventing illegal activities in
the maritime industry. These illegal activities pose a significant threat to national
security and the economy of coast shall states. They endanger the safety and security
of ships and crews and undermine the maritime industry’s crucial role in global trade
transportation and economic growth.
Ensuring the safety and security of the maritime industry is a vital aspect of the
global economy and international security. In addition to illegal activities, natural
disasters and accidents also create a risk to the industry. Using satellite technology
which provides real-time information would effectively help to enhance immediate
emergency response. This could be used as well on predicting the weather ocean
currents and other environmental elements that may affect the safety of ships and
crews. All those benefits can be obtained by using satellites.
To address this issue, satellite-based systems have emerged as a promising solution.
The use of earth observation technology, with the help of satellites, offers global
coverage and enables the taking of identical measurements over vast areas. Satellite
images are a popular choice for ship detection and classification purposes due to
their ability to operate in various conditions and provide a wide range of imagery.
However, using some satellite imagery has its limitations, such as diﬀiculties and
complex scenes like harbour areas with dense ships due to interference from land.
additionally complying with the demand for high-velocity and high-accuracy detection
poses challenges. Traditional methods using data and statistics have been used for
ship detection. Still, lately, machine learning models have developed more to solve
some of these problems.
Earth observation (EO) has benefited greatly from the use of satellites, which
offer global coverage and enable the taking of identical measurements over wide areas.
The two main EO techniques are optical and radar instruments, with radar payloads
offering significant advantages over optical payloads due to their ability to penetrate
clouds and measure in the absence of sunlight.
The advancements in satellite technology have managed to develop a new type
of small affordable satellite famous as CubeSats. Satellites used for similar types of
missions usually are equipped with a variety of sensors and instruments including

1.1
Science case
Law enforcement organizations, environmentalists, and the fishing industry have all
long expressed concern about the existence of dark vessels in the world’s oceans.
These vessels engage in illegal activities like smuggling, traﬀicking, and illegal fishing
and are recognized for their lack of identification markings. Dark ships are a serious
threat to national security, global fisheries, and marine ecosystems. The Arctic is
especially exposed to the threat posed by dark vessels because of its isolation and
lack of surveillance resources.
Illegal fishing is one of the main dangers posed by dark vessels.
These boats
frequently engage in destructive fishing practices, like bottom trawling, which can
destroy delicate marine ecosystems. The FAO of the United Nations estimates that
up to 26 million tons of fish, worth up to 23 billion US Dollars, are caught each
year as a result of IUU (illegal, unreported, and unregulated) fishing. Fish stocks
are depleted due to IUU fishing, but legitimate fishing operations are also threatened,
which causes economic harm to communities that depend on legal fishing.[1]
Smuggling creates real threats. They support organized crime and endanger na-
tional security because they are used to transport illegal goods like drugs, weapons,
and people. Dark vessels occasionally transport people and practice traﬀicking and
frequently operate in isolated locations where they are diﬀicult to detect. With an
estimated 40 million people living in modern slavery worldwide, this is an interna-
tional issue. These people are frequently transported in inhumane conditions, and
their human rights are gravely violated when they are transported on dark vessels,
which is a serious concern.[2]
Additionally, because dark vessels frequently break environmental laws, harmful
substances like wastewater and oil spills end up polluting marine environments. This
pollution may negatively impact the environment and marine ecosystems for a long
time. It may cause habitat damage and ultimately a disruption in the equilibrium of
marine ecosystems.

2.1
Systems engineering
The main function of systems engineering is to guide the engineering of complicated
systems by focusing on the system, evaluating its operation from various viewpoints,
and including interactions outside the system. This includes identifying customer
needs, system operational environment, interfacing systems, capabilities of operators,
logistic support requirements, and similar factors that must be represented in the
system requirements and design. Systems engineering also brings together various
engineering disciplines to design a complex system where each subsystem element
must function properly in combination with other subsystems. Systems engineering
aims to find the answer that best balances every stakeholder’s interest in a system.
The system life cycle consists of concept development, engineering development
and post-development, which is a sequential process. In space missions, the post-
development phase is divided into separate phases for production and operations
due to their long operational periods. The four phases of a space mission are con-
cept exploration, in-depth development, production and employment, and operations
and support. Each phase is further divided into smaller faces depending on the or-
ganisation. During the production and deployment phase, the satellite is launched,
hardware, and software used on the ground during the flight are produced. [3]
Different system life cycle models are made in various organisations, but they all
have similar phases. Systems engineering at ESA transects with fields like project

Sustainment (Phase E).

Program/Project Life Cycle. During this phase, the initial project concept is for-

2.2
Energy range study
The Earth’s atmosphere shields our planet from a variety of higher energy waves,
which protects life from dangerous radiation. As result, the entire electromagnetic
spectrum does not reach the Earth’s surface. Specific forms of electromagnetic ra-
diation cannot pass through the atmosphere due to atmospheric absorption. This
phenomenon is called the ”atmospheric window”. Therefore, Sensors used in remote
sensing are designed to detect certain spectral bands.[4]
Atmospheric windows are crucial for Earth mapping satellites Because they enable
the collection of high-quality data about the Earth’s surface without interference
from the atmosphere. Each of the layers that make up the Earth’s atmosphere has
unique characteristics that can influence how electromagnetic radiation interacts with
it.
For instance, the upper atmosphere’s ozone layer absorbs a lot of ultraviolet
light, making it challenging to take high-quality pictures in this wavelength range.
There are, however, areas of the spectrum where the atmosphere is more transparent,
enabling the capture of more explicit images.[4]
Several gases in the Earth’s atmosphere, including water vapour, carbon dioxide,
and ozone, primarily reflect or absorb electromagnetic radiation. The atmosphere
blocks particular wavelengths, such as most of the ultraviolet light, gamma rays, and

to the absorption bands. [4]

The most well-known atmospheric window is the visible window because it includes
the wavelengths that are visible to the human eye. High-quality visible light images
can be taken using this window, which is situated between 400 and 700 nanometers.
Visible light is one type of radiation that mostly passes or is transmitted through the
atmosphere which allows our eyes to view the features of the Earth. Atmospheric
windows are mostly defined as those parts of the spectrum with wavelengths that can
penetrate the atmosphere. Because some microwaves can even penetrate clouds, they
are the best wavelength for sending signals for satellite communication. Meanwhile,
the region of the electromagnetic spectrum that is inhibited is known as an absorption
band.[4]
Another crucial window used for mapping the Earth is the near-infrared window,
which is present between 700 and 1300 nanometers. The health of the vegetation
and other surface features can be determined using this spectrum of wavelengths.
Meanwhile thermal infrared atmospheric window lies in the range of 8000 to 14000
nanometers. This range of wavelengths can be used to identify temperature variations
on the surface of the Earth, which can reveal the location of hotspots like wildfires
or volcanic activity. The microwave window, which measures features on the Earth’s
surface like soil moisture and sea surface temperature, is useful for doing so. Its range
is roughly between 1 millimetre and 1 meter.[4]

Radio wave window atmospheric window is also used for mapping the Earth. It
overlaps the microwave window at a distance of between 1 millimetre and 1 meter.
As radio waves can easily and without much attenuation pass through the Earth’s
atmosphere at these wavelengths, they are crucial for satellite communications. Typi-
cally, radio waves used by satellites for communication operate at frequencies between
1 and 30 GHz, or wavelengths between roughly 1 and 10 centimetres. The Earth’s
atmosphere can be easily penetrated by these frequencies, enabling dependable com-
munication between ground stations and satellites.[4]
Radar imaging benefits from the radio wave window in addition to communication.
Radar systems use radio waves to identify and measure the characteristics of objects
on the surface of the planet. Scientists can calculate the distance to an object and
produce 3D images of the Earth’s surface by timing how long it takes a radar signal
to leave an object and return to the radar system.[4]

4.2
Structure
Every spacecraft has a platform that supports all the subsystems. The platform needs
to control the thermals because the onboard electronics are temperature sensitive,
some more so than others. The platform also needs to shield its internal components
from radiation and other harsh space conditions. Together, these three components
make up the platform, which serves only to support the other systems. It is essential
to control thermal conditions to ensure that electronics operate correctly. While some
subsystems may need active heating, others may produce a significant amount of heat
that needs to be dissipated. The subsystem has a significant impact on thermal man-
agement. In addition to mounting subsystems, offering radiation shielding, ensuring
thermal isolation, and preserving structural stability, the platform also serves other
purposes. Compared to stationary components, mechanisms that involve movement
necessitate extra care. These components, along with solar panels and deployable
antennas, fall under the mechanism domain. This includes moving parts like springs,
hinges, and others. A mechanism’s failure could lead to the malfunction of crucial sub-

Safe mode
Safe mode is a mode of operation for unmanned spacecraft. All non-essential systems
are shut down in this mode, and only essential functions like thermal management,
radio reception, and attitude control are active. Safe mode is triggered automatically
when a predefined operating condition or event is detected, which may indicate a loss

systems can also cause a trigger event, with central processor electronics being par-

window can cause a trigger event due to hardware failures or the miss-programming of
the spacecraft. The process of entering safe mode involves taking immediate physical
actions to prevent damage or complete loss. Non-essential subsystems are powered
down, and regaining attitude control is the highest priority, as it is crucial for main-

4.4.1
Theory
The strip mode is a type of imaging mode in SAR that is frequently used for remote
sensing applications. In this mode, the SAR platform’s radar instrument continuously
beams electromagnetic radiation pulses toward the ground as it flies over a patch of
terrain. The radar pulses bounce off the surface features and are picked up by the
instrument’s antenna, which transforms them into digital signals. The SAR system
processes these signals to create a detailed image of the terrain below.
SAR’s strip mode enables quick imaging of large terrain areas, making it ideal for
tasks like mapping, land-use analysis, and environmental monitoring. It is frequently
employed for both oil spill detection and maritime surveillance. However, variations
in the topography and other surface features, as well as atmospheric effects, can lead
to distortions in strip mode SAR images. Advanced SAR data processing methods
like multi-looking, speckle filtering, and terrain correction can be used to reduce these
distortions. In general, the strip mode in SAR is a crucial tool for remote sensing
and offers insightful data for a variety of applications.
Fundamentally, Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) is a type of radar which uses
high-resolution imaging that is installed on a moving platform such as a satellite or
an aircraft. One of the most important features of SAR is its coherent radar nature,
which requires a stable oscillator to ensure that the phase between transmitted pulses
and received echoes is comparable. This stability is necessary to generate high-quality
images.
The simplest form of viewing geometry for SAR is called the strip map,

4.4.3
Image distortion
SAR can suffer from image distortions that affect the quality of the final image. Here
are some of the common SAR image distortions:
• Foreshortening: is a common phenomenon in SAR images, where an object
appears shorter in the image than its actual length due to its orientation relative
to the radar antenna. This occurs because the radar signal measures only the
projection of the object onto the ground, rather than its true shape. Objects
that are oriented perpendicular to the radar antenna appear at their true length,
while those at an angle appear foreshortened. This effect can be corrected using
techniques like multi-looking or interferometry. One example of foreshortening
occurs when a hill is observed in a way that makes the closest bottom and the
top point appear closer to each other than they actually are in ground range.
This is due to the wavefront only detecting a small difference between the closest
bottom and the top point, while the furthest bottom point appears further away.
Figure 4.5 illustrates this phenomenon in SAR images.
• Layover: When the top point of the terrain appears to be closer than the
bottom point, this is an extreme case of fore-shortening. Layover is a type of
geometric distortion that can occur in SAR images. Layover occurs when an
object appears to be taller than it actually is because it is located on a slope or
steep terrain. This happens because the radar signal only measures the projec-
tion of the object onto the ground, rather than its true shape as demonstrated

in figure 4.5. As a result, objects that are located on steep slopes or terrain
will appear to be taller in the image than they actually are. Layover can be
corrected using various techniques, such as multi-looking or interferometry
• Shadowing: In this case no direct path echo returns because the terrain top
obscures some of the terrain. causing some areas to appear dark. Shadowing is
a type of geometric distortion that can occur in SAR images. Shadowing occurs
when an object is located in a shadowed area and does not reflect enough radar
energy back to the antenna to be detected. This happens because the radar
signal only measures the amount of energy that is reflected back to the antenna,
and objects that are located in shadowed areas do not reflect enough energy to
be detected. As a result, these areas appear dark or black in the SAR image.

• Speckle: Speckle is a type of noise that can occur in Synthetic Aperture Radar
(SAR) images, caused by the coherent sum of many elemental scatterers within
a resolution cell. It appears in areas with distributed scatterers where the radar
wavelength is comparable to the surface roughness. Although it is referred to
as noise, speckle cannot be reduced by increasing the transmit signal power,
since it has a multiplicative character, meaning its variance increases with its
intensity.
To mitigate speckle, a technique known as multi-look is utilized,
which is a non-coherent averaging of the intensity image. Multi-look causes
a degradation in the image resolution, but it improves the interpretability of
the SAR image. In extended targets like fields, water, or forests, the echoes
can be thought of as a superposition of numerous distinct reflection echoes.

4.5.3
Frequancey
The selection of the communication frequency, or frequencies, between a ground sta-
tion and a spacecraft, is influenced by several factors. A brief overview of the effects
of frequency in space communication is necessary. As the radio spectrum is a shared
channel, it must be divided into separate bands for various services. The International
Telecommunication Union (ITU) is responsible for regulating the different telecommu-
nication bands and has made recommendations regarding frequencies used in space
missions. The ITU recommends the SHF band, or 3 GHz to 30 GHz. The ITU rec-
ommendations organize the spectrum to prevent services from colliding. VHF and
UHF bands, 30 MHz to 300 MHz and 300 MHz to 3 GHz respectively, are also highly
used for satellite communication. However, SHF is becoming more popular in space
missions due to its high data rate and because the band is less occupied by other ser-
vices. Higher data rates require higher transmission bandwidth, making it diﬀicult to
allocate high bandwidth applications in VHF and UHF bands due to high occupation
by other services. In contrast, high bandwidth applications can be allocated in SHF
bands because the band is not as busy as lower frequency bands. Antenna size is
another important factor in selecting a communication band. Antenna size is directly
related to wavelength, which is inversely proportional to frequency.
For example,
VHF antennas are in the order of meters, while SHF antennas are in the order of
centimetres. Small antennas are useful in satellites because they are easier to fit in
spacecraft. Atmospheric conditions also play an important role in space communica-
tion. In HF bands, the ionosphere reflects radio waves making it diﬀicult to perform
space communication. When the frequency is increased, atmospheric losses occur due
to molecular absorption of oxygen and water vapor. High attenuation occurs when
the wavelength is near 3 cm, which is near the size of water drops. Increasing the
frequency leads to more atmospheric losses. Free-space path loss, which results from
the receiving antenna’s capture area and the line-of-sight path through free space,
is the major cause of losses in space communication. Free-space loss increases with
distance between antennas and decreases with wavelength. Radio wave intensity de-
creases with the square of distance from the transmitting antenna due to spreading
of the electromagnetic energy in space.
Due to the platform’s capabilities, only S-band and X-band will be considered as
viable communication band options.
4.5.3.1
Modulation
Modulation is a technique of altering one or more characteristics of a carrier signal,
such as amplitude, frequency, or phase, with a separate signal containing information
to be transmitted, known as the modulation signal. Analog modulation techniques
include AM, FM, and PM, whereas digital modulation techniques comprise ASK,
FSK, PSK, and QAM.
AM modulation results in a wastage of around 50% of the transmitted power
in the carrier signal. Therefore, it is unsuitable for power-limited systems such as
==PAGE:54==
42
4 System Analysis
satellites. ASK modulation is simpler than AM modulation, with pulse transmission
indicating bit values. However, it is not optimal in the presence of noise, which is
why it is discarded for satellite communication.
PSK modulation offers excellent spectral eﬀiciency, but with an increase in the
number of symbols, it becomes more susceptible to noise, which elevates the Bit Error
Ratio (BER). BPSK is a type of PSK modulation that uses two phases separated by
180 degrees, making it the most robust of all PSKs. However, it can only modulate
at 1 bit per symbol. FSK modulation utilizes two tones to convey binary information,
and it is generated using two oscillators that are switched to transmit information.
Nevertheless, the bandwidth of FSK signals is greater than that of PSK modulation
due to the non-continuity of the phase.
QAM modulation uses both amplitude and phase of the signal to modulate sym-
bols and does not waste power in transmitting the carrier signal. Therefore, it uses
bandwidth eﬀiciently and is more effective than ASK modulation since the constella-
tion of symbols is centered.

redundancy and performing operations to detect and correct errors. Forward error
correction techniques are widely used, especially in scenarios where re-transmission
of a message is impossible. In such cases, it is better to correct errors in the received

Azimuth resolution
The resolution of a Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) system’s ability to distinguish be-
tween two targets in the cross-track direction is known as azimuth resolution. This is
determined by the system’s operating frequency and the size of the antenna aperture.
In general, higher operating frequencies and longer antenna apertures will improve
azimuth resolution. The higher frequency produces more radar signal cycles within a
given distance, allowing for finer spatial resolution. Meanwhile, a longer aperture en-
ables a more accurate measurement of the phase differences between signals reflected
from different points on the ground.
Azimuth resolution is typically expressed in meters and represents the minimum
separation between two targets that a SAR system can resolve. It is independent of
range and is what sets SAR apart. The only factor that restricts azimuth resolution
is the antenna length. For example, an antenna of 10 meters or less must be used
to achieve a 5-meter resolution which is the resolution aimed for according to the

5.1.2.3
Incidence angle
The angle at which the radar beam intersects with the ground surface is called the in-
cidence angle. This angle is formed between the perpendicular to the ground and the
radar line-of-sight at the point of reflection. SAR system performance is affected by
the incidence angle, including signal strength, resolution, and image quality. Higher
incidence angles lead to weaker signals due to increased atmospheric attenuation and
scattering, but they also improve resolution by reducing the along-track spacing be-
tween samples. On the other hand, lower incidence angles produce stronger signals
but lower resolution due to greater along-track spacing. The incidence angle is typ-
ically measured in degrees and can be calculated using antenna height, slant range,
and ground elevation variables. SAR systems are designed to operate at various in-
cidence angles for flexibility in different imaging scenarios. Varying incidence angles
impact the transmitted bandwidth and slant range. As the incidence angle increases,
the transmitted bandwidth decreases. The slant range also increases, causing greater
path loss and a corresponding decrease in sensitivity. To maintain the same incidence
angle, a lower bandwidth is required for ground range resolution, which reduces noise
bandwidth and increases sensitivity. Currently, smallSats like ICEYE use a relatively
low incidence angle of 25 degrees as used for this study as well.

5.2.1
System errors
A determination error is a discrepancy between an object or system’s actual position
or orientation and the estimated position or orientation calculated by a sensor or
algorithm. Determination errors can arise due to various factors such as sensor noise,
atmospheric disturbances, and Attitude and Orbit Control System (AOCS) errors.
Keeping these errors to a minimum is crucial for producing accurate and reliable
SAR data since they can significantly impact image quality. For the current mission,
the platform selected has a determination error of 200 arcsec.
Pointing error, also known as boresight error or alignment error, refers to the
difference between the intended and actual directions of the (SAR) antenna during
data acquisition. It is a type of determination error that can be caused by various
factors, such as flaws in the Attitude and Orbit Control System (AOCS), modifications
to the SAR antenna’s mechanical design, and outside disturbances like atmospheric
turbulence. Similar to the determination error, The mission platform determines the
pointing error for this mission and has been set at 250 arcsec.
5.2.2
Attitude Control
Star tracker: Spacecraft usually use star trackers to accurately determine their ori-
entation with respect to the stars. Star trackers use optical technology to recognize
star patterns in their field of view and compare them to a database of well-known stars
to determine the spacecraft’s precise attitude. This technology is useful for achieving
many mission goals, including aligning communications antennas and pointing scien-
tific instruments. Star trackers are a reliable type of sensor that use tiny cameras to
capture images of nearby stars.
Star trackers operate in two distinct modes. In the first mode, a lost-in-space
mode, the tracker does not need to know its current attitude beforehand. It searches
through the entire catalog to select the best attitude, which is not instantaneous due
to a large amount of data being compared. In the second mode, tracking mode, the
tracker is aware of its previous attitude, and it tracks stars that are currently in its
field of view while tracking new stars when they become available. This mode is much
faster and produces a constant attitude output, but the sensor cannot be interrupted
during this mode.

standard outlines relevant biases, including a static bias resulting from calibration
errors or launch alignment issues, limited field of view causing optical aberrations, and
temporal noise and pixel errors. Star trackers are typically large and heavy due to the
need for a big baffle to filter out stray light and special processing hardware. However,
some commercially available star trackers have a small weight and are effective. The
ST200 from AAC Clyde Space which has a weighing of only 42 g and achieves 30-200
arc-seconds accuracy is selected. [19]
Gyroscope: Gyroscopes are instruments used to measure or maintain orienta-
tion and angular velocity. They consist of a spinning rotor mounted in a three-axis
gimbal, which maintains its orientation in space, providing a reference point for mea-
suring changes in orientation or angular velocity. There are various types of gyros,
including optical, mechanical, and Micro-Electromechanical Systems (MEMS) gyros,
with different biases to consider, such as bias repeatability, bias stability, and bias
thermal sensitivity. The ECSS provides a standard defining gyro terminology and
performance. Although gyros are typically large, the telecom industry has profoundly
developed MEMS gyros, which are inexpensive and lightweight. However, their lower
stability and lack of space heritage make them controversial in space applications.
The GranStal Solutions GS-SFA-311A Fiber Optic Gyroscope is a three-axis FOG
with an RS422 interface that outputs angular velocity and attitude data in the three
orthogonal axes of the carrier. This FOG module has a flight heritage and was specif-
ically designed to be lightweight and small in size. The gyroscope itself weighs 1.1 kg
and has bias stability of 1.5◦
Sun sensors: Sun sensors are used to determine the spacecraft’s orientation with
respect to the sun. These sensors utilize an array of photodiodes or light-sensitive.
Components that generate an electrical current proportional to the intensity of light
when exposed to sunlight. By measuring the current flowing through each diode, the
sensor calculates the sun’s direction relative to the spacecraft’s orientation or pointing
direction. Sun sensors work with gyroscopes to ensure the spacecraft is pointed in the
correct direction. This is critical for spacecraft missions that require precise pointing,
such as Earth observation, communication, and navigation.
Sun sensors are simple, inexpensive attitude sensors that measure the sun’s angle
to determine the spacecraft’s attitude.
While they are reliable and can measure
at high angular velocities, they can only measure in 2-axis and with low accuracy.
They are ideal as backup sensors in safe mode to ensure accurate alignment of the
spacecraft’s antennas and solar panels. Sun sensors range from 14.1g to 61g in weight
and have an accuracy range of 0.1° to 5°. Despite requiring several sensors for the
lost-in-space mode, they are incredibly lightweight.
Magnetometer: Magnetometer works by measuring magnetic fields.
Earth’s
magnetic field strength and direction are not constant and vary depending on location.
The magnetometer on a satellite can measure these variations, which provide valuable
information about the satellite’s position and attitude. For example, sudden changes
in the magnetic field may indicate regions of intense radiation that could harm the
satellite’s electronics. Despite their reliability and affordability, magnetometers are

subject to inaccuracies due to the temporal variations in the magnetic field. It is
recommended to place magnetometers as far away from electronics as possible. This
is to reduce electromagnetic interference.
Reaction wheels: Reaction wheels consist of a motor-mounted rotating wheel
that can change the wheel’s speed or rotational direction. The conservation of angu-
lar momentum principle enables the spacecraft to experience an equal and opposite
torque when the rotating wheel experiences torque. Adjusting the wheel’s speed and
direction can precisely control the spacecraft’s attitude. Reaction wheels, when used
with other attitude control tools, such as thrusters or magnetic torques, can stabilize
three spacecraft axes. They are ideal for long-duration missions like remote sensing
or space telescopes because of their high effectiveness and ability to provide precise
control over extended periods.
A disadvantage of reaction wheels is that they may become saturated over time,
requiring resetting before additional torque can be produced. Extra wheels or other
attitude control tools can be used to help with this issue. Additionally, wear and tear
may decrease the eﬀiciency of reaction wheels over time. Reaction wheels use the con-
servation of angular momentum to control attitude by changing the flywheel’s angular
velocity, which applies torque to the spacecraft in the opposite direction. Since they
do not require expendable propellant, they are suitable for SmallSat. However, addi-
tional forms of attitude control are needed when the reaction wheels reach maximum
angular velocity.
5.2.3
Orbit Control
The Global Positioning System (GPS) is commonly used for satellite orbit control. It
uses signal time delays from satellites to calculate an object’s position and velocity.
GPS can provide precise orbit data to satellites in low Earth orbit. Integrated GPS
receivers are not the only option, and ground-based tracking is a viable alternative.
This information can be uploaded to the satellite during communication with the
ground station. The satellite’s orbit can as well be estimated using the clock, gyro-
scopes, and ephemeris data uploaded during the most recent communication session
with the ground station.

Data system
The amount of data produced by a radar system per unit of time, known as the data
rate, is a critical parameter in SAR imaging. It is determined by the number of data
bits or bytes sent or received every second. The data rate plays a crucial role in
determining the resolution, imaging swath width, and onboard storage requirements
of SAR systems. Higher data rates lead to more accurate measurements of the phase
and amplitude of received signals, enabling faster data acquisition and improved
resolution. However, larger areas take longer to collect data, resulting in narrower

imaging swath widths and greater onboard storage requirements. The data rate is
typically measured in bits per second. The data rate value depends on factors such as
operating frequency, aperture size, and pulse repetition frequency (PRF) in addition
to available bandwidth. Higher-frequency SAR systems with larger apertures and
higher PRFs usually require higher data rates due to their increased sensitivity and
faster acquisition times.
5.3.1
Quantization
The process of quantization in Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imaging involves con-
verting analog signals into digital signals with discrete numerical values. SAR systems
produce analog signals that need to be converted to digital for processing and storage.
The quantization process divides the potential signal amplitudes into distinct levels
or steps represented by digital values. The number of levels or steps is determined by
the bit depth of the analog-to-digital converter (ADC) used in the SAR system. A
16-bit ADC can represent 65,536 levels, whereas an 8-bit ADC can represent 256 lev-
els. The quantization process affects SAR system performance, including resolution
and dynamic range. Higher bit depth provides a more precise measurement of signal
amplitudes, enabling finer distinctions between signal levels and higher resolution.
However, it requires more storage capacity and can lead to reduced dynamic range
due to the limited number of signal levels. The data stream is split into an I and Q
channel using a sophisticated modulator. Currently, Sentinel-1 uses a 16-bit ADC,
providing each sample with a representation of 32 bits. Accordingly nb = 32
5.3.2
Sampling rate
The speed at which analog signals are converted into digital signals in a SAR system
is known as the analog-to-digital converter (ADC) sampling rate.
The sampling
rate is expressed as the number of samples collected per second. The SAR system’s
resolution, imaging swath width, and data volume are all affected by the sampling
rate. A higher sampling rate allows for more precise measurement of signal amplitudes
and higher resolution by enabling finer distinctions between adjacent signal levels.
However, a higher sampling rate may also result in a narrower imaging swath width
and require more storage space, as it takes longer to collect data over a smaller area.
The sampling rate is influenced by the operating frequency, pulse repetition fre-
quency (PRF), and available bandwidth. Higher-frequency SAR systems with higher
PRFs generally require higher sampling rates due to their increased sensitivity and
faster acquisition times. To avoid aliasing, the transmitted bandwidth Br must be
at least equal to the sampling frequency, fsr.

5.3.4
Data rates
Data rates refer to the amount of information produced and transmitted during a
Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) imaging mission. SAR systems are known for their
excellent sensitivity and resolution, which results in high data rates. To reduce the
data volume before transmission to the ground, SAR data is typically compressed,
filtered, and processed onboard the satellite. This is necessary as high data rates
present significant challenges in terms of data storage, processing, and transmission.
Effective data handling and processing methods, such as real-time compression, data
reduction, and eﬀicient data transmission schemes, are required to manage the high
data rates produced by SAR systems. These methods aid in reducing the amount
of data being transmitted while ensuring that the most important data is eﬀiciently
transmitted and processed.
For instance, SAR generates an average data rate of DR = 140.9 Mbit/s over
t = 4.34 s, or DR×t = 80.3 MB, to create a single image of size 50 ×22 km. However,
this data is not produced continuously but rather in bursts with an instantaneous data
rate of 576 Mbit/s.

Ground station
To meet the system’s requirement of providing real-time data from SAR scans, two

The ground

• Expanded Coverage By placing the ground stations at different locations
around the Earth, the satellite can always maintain contact with at least one of
the two ground stations. This expands the satellite’s coverage area and ensures
that there is no signal or data loss.
• Greater Reliability In case of equipment breakdown or maintenance require-
ments, having two ground stations provides redundancy. Both ground stations
can take over the operations and data collection if one goes offline.

level of service. This can improve the accuracy and dependability of the data
collected by the satellite.
• Increased Data Transfer Rates Having two ground stations allows the satel-
lite to transmit data simultaneously to both stations, which can increase data
transfer rates. This can be especially beneficial for satellites that require high
data transmission rates.
The first ground station is located in Svalbard, Norway, and the second is in
Gatineau, Canada. Both ground stations are well-equipped and have a reliable con-
nection, which is crucial for transmitting the data to the stakeholders in a timely
manner.

5.5.1
Power generation
The simulation created by GomSpace to calculate the power for the spacecraft system

energy is a function of power and time, and reflects the total amount of power used
over a given period. By considering the energy consumed over each orbit, we can
accurately determine the power needed to sustain the spacecraft’s operations during
that time.
To meet the power requirement outlined in Section 1, the power generated by

with the exact value dependent on the orbit. For the purpose of worst-case scenario
calculation, the calculation uses the lower limit value. Therefore, the energy generated
in each orbit can be calculated using the following Equation 5.24

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
Due to the nature of this project, many of the decisions that needed to be addressed
are already examined throughout the report.
However, there are still important
considerations to be taken into account. The discussion can be divided into mission-
technical, system-technical and feasibility.
6.1
Mission technical analysis
The study highlights the crucial role of practical design choices in the development
of functional spacecraft systems. This involves analyzing technical and geographical
limitations that may affect mission objectives and narrowing down the target size
to optimize resources and budgets for the detection system. Through a thorough
investigation of these limitations, mission designers can identify potential challenges
and develop effective strategies and solutions to overcome them, leading to a more
successful and eﬀicient satellite mission and helping achieve desired objectives.
Moreover, the study proposes a new mission focusing on Greenland’s territorial
waters and utilising a low-cost CubeSat with the required earth observation capa-
bilities. This mission is dedicated to a surveillance system that would provide data
to improve maritime safety and increase knowledge about dark ship movements and
their impact on the environment.
Regarding payload selection, the study emphasizes the importance of selecting
energy ranges that fit inside the atmospheric windows. Specifically, the visible, in-
frared, and radar wave regions are preferred because sensors for remote sensing mis-
sions must operate within these ranges to collect high-quality data about the Earth’s
surface without interference from the atmosphere.
When it comes to detecting dark ships, the study acknowledges the challenges of
using optical or infrared detection techniques, which may fail to spot ships that blend
in with the background water or appear as tiny, undetectable features. Instead, the
study recommends the use of SAR sensors that produce images using radar waves
rather than visible light or heat signatures. SAR sensors can function in a wider range
of weather and low-light situations, penetrate through clouds and other atmospheric
conditions, and provide high-resolution images with precise data on the ship’s position,
size, and speed.
Furthermore, SAR technology constantly improves, making it a
reliable choice for remote sensing applications in environments with poor visibility.

6.2
System technical analysis
The spacecraft’s design is based on a bus approach, utilizing the 12 U GomSpace
platform to provide greater flexibility in both spacecraft design and mission planning.
This approach allows to combine various components, dividing the spacecraft into
modular subsystems to tailor it to the needs of a specific mission. Additionally, the
bus approach design makes it easier to maintain and update individual subsystems,
as they can be replaced without affecting other components.
The study introduces a CubeSat-based Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) system
capable of detecting dark ships in the sea, independent of weather conditions and
capable of penetrating clouds using microwaves. The SAR payload’s technical specifi-
cations include an incidence angle of 25°, generating 80.3 MB of data while recording
images of 22 × 50 km. The SAR antenna measures 0.335 × 2 m, with a peak trans-
mitted power of 880 W and an average transmitted power of 114 W. The spacecraft’s
communication systems include a low data rate system for onboard communications
and a high data rate S-band radio for data downlink. To meet the spacecraft’s AOCS
requirements, star trackers, sun sensors, momentum wheels, and magnetometers are
utilized. Additionally, the spacecraft’s platform provides structural integrity, tem-
perature control, and protection from space radiation.
The choice of orbit significantly impacts the spacecraft’s performance, affecting
the altitude of measurements, lifetime of the spacecraft, and revisit time and ground
track while passing Greenland territorial waters. Assuming a starting altitude of 525
km, the estimated lifetime of the spacecraft is slightly over five years. The orbit’s
inclination is set at 81° to ensure it passes over Greenland’s territorial waters.
6.3
Feasibility
The project’s primary objective was to design and implement a comprehensive surveil-
lance system for monitoring the tutorial waters of Greenland. However, the simula-
tion conducted in subsection 4.1.4 revealed that the system would not be capable of
covering the entire area within a day. This could reduce the surveillance system’s
effectiveness, as the area is too vast to be covered by a single, small satellite in a short
time. Time is a critical factor in any surveillance system, and leaving areas uncov-
ered for extended periods may jeopardize the mission objective and lead to a loss of
stakeholder interest. Despite this, the system is technically feasible and could serve
alternative purposes or less ambitious surveillance objectives. Recommendations will
be presented for using the system, reducing the region of interest, or enhancing the
system to achieve the mission objective effectively.
Satellites Constellation To enhance satellite coverage, one feasible solution is to
utilize constellation satellites, which involve deploying multiple satellites at slightly
different inclinations to cover a larger area in less time, rather than relying on a
single satellite. Deploying multiple satellites can improve coverage and reduce the
time needed to cover the entire region.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The NewSpace era has brought about a multitude of companies producing hardware
specifically designed for SmallSat platforms. The increased competition has led to a
wide range of similar components available on the market, which can serve as inspi-
ration for designing hardware for a proposed spacecraft. In addition, technological
advancements have made many components smaller and better than before, making
it possible to create an EO SAR 12U CubeSat for ship detection.
For this project, the goal was to design a flexible detection system for ships that
could perform various remote sensing Earth observation tasks. A GOMspace 12U
platform was modified to be suitable for the system, with some in-built subsystems
and an 8U room for the payload. The payload was designed using a system engi-
neering methodology, outlining the subsystems, components, and functions for each
subsystem.
The SAR payload was determined to be the most effective option with the least
limitations, despite challenges related to the limited power available on the used
platform.
The payload was able to fit within the constraints of an 8U structure,
despite its large antenna.
The primary objective of the project was to create a comprehensive surveillance
system for monitoring Greenland’s territorial waters, but simulation results showed
that the system would not be able to cover the entire area within a day. Overcoming
this limitation is particularly challenging due to its classification as a physical lim-
itation. Recommendations were made for using the system to reduce the region of
interest or enhancing it to achieve the mission objective effectively.
This study provides valuable insights into practical design choices and the use
of SAR sensors to improve satellite coverage and accomplish mission objectives ef-
fectively. Future work could focus on enhancing the system or utilizing constellation
satellites for improved coverage. Overall, this project offers a low-cost CubeSat-based
SAR system for detecting dark ships in the sea, independent of weather conditions,
contributing to the field of remote sensing.
