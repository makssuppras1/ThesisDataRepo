Abstract
Bayesian methodology applied to deep learning promises desirable attributes such as
inherent uncertainty quantification and providing principled predictions. The com-
plexity of modern neural networks applied in Natural Language Processing limits the
use of Bayesian methods. Modeling a subset of the network parameters as stochastic
facilitates the utilization of Bayesian inference in large-scale problems.
This thesis presents an in-depth investigation into the implementation of partially
stochastic Bayesian neural networks across a series of posterior approximation meth-
ods and tasks. The investigation is two-part. Firstly, a study was conducted using
partial stochasticity in Bayesian inference methods applied to small-scale regression
problems. Secondly, a study of partial stochasticity on Natural Language Processing
(NLP) tasks was performed.
We present a thorough exploration of six different methods used for posterior infer-
ence. These are investigated as they represent a variety of modern inference methods,
ranging from the high-fidelity Hamiltonian Monte Carlo to the crude Mean-Field ap-
proximation of Variational inference. To investigate the eﬀicacy of these methods in
the setting of partially stochastic neural networks, we conduct a series of ramping
experiments in three different regression tasks. In these, we vary the percentage of
stochastic parameters in the model, thus ranging from the non-Bayesian maximum a
posteriori (MAP) solution to the fully Bayesian neural network in which all network
parameters are stochastic. We find that partial stochasticity across all modes of pos-
terior inference invariably outperforms the MAP solution. Furthermore, in almost
every method/dataset setting, a percentage of stochastic parameters can be selected
enabling partial stochasticity to outperform its fully Bayesian variant.
The initial exploration into partial stochasticity for different inference schemes was
performed to establish the feasibility of utilizing these methods in NLP. In the setting
of NLP, models are orders of magnitude larger than for the small-scale regression tasks.
Thus, feasibility is determined by both performance response to partial stochasticity
and by the computational requirements of the method. Thus, the second part of the
study revolves around applying the Laplace approximation and Stochastic Weight
Averaging - Gaussian (SWAG) in an NLP setting.
To establish the eﬀicacy of partial stochasticity through these two methods, we con-

the notion of uniform or homogenous positioning. We propose a framework for the
Laplace approximation wherein we select stochastic substructures in all linear map-

number of stochastic parameters while showing better or similar performance to the
fully stochastic versions and the highest-performing combination of stochastic linear
layers.
In conclusion, the research establishes the effectiveness of partial stochasticity in
Bayesian neural networks for NLP tasks.
It shows that methods like Laplace ap-
proximation and SWAG, when applied with a strategy of partial stochasticity, lead
to significant performance improvements over deterministic and fully stochastic ap-
proaches. Furthermore, we show that the performance increase induced by partial
stochasticity can be achieved for small percentages of stochastic parameters. The
thesis contributes to the field by providing insights into effective techniques for im-
plementing Bayesian neural networks in NLP, offering a promising avenue for future
research and practical applications.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Bayesian methods constitute a principled methodology for handling model uncer-
tainty, which is often neglected in modern neural networks [Yan+23].
However,
the inclusion of model uncertainty is essential to delivering trustworthy predictions.
Bayesian Machine Learning utilizes Bayes Theorem to include parameter uncertainty
by incorporating both prior notions on the parameter distribution and the evidence
provided by the data. It offers a robust and flexible methodology for handling un-
certainty and making predictions. The Bayesian framework has been used to solve
a broad selection of modeling problems; however, current Bayesian methods suffer
under the vast number of parameters in modern neural networks. This is due to slow
training, extensive memory requirements [Dax+22b], and sometimes even inadequate
performance [RJH18].
Natural Language Processing is a field of research where the introduction of deep learn-
ing has redefined the state-of-the-art, most notably through the transformer model
[Vas+17]. This has been achieved by introducing highly flexible model architectures
with an ever-increasing model complexity [Tou+23]. Though these models have dis-
played incredible progression recently, an issue remains to be solved: overconfidence.
For classification tasks, class probabilities tend to be pushed to the boundaries for
cases where the label might even be up for interpretation [Yan+23]. Hence, wrong
predictions are often made with high confidence, making it diﬀicult to incorporate the
models in safety-critical situations such as experimental design, financial markets, or
medical diagnosis. Transformer models used in these safety-critical decision-making
processes therefore demand heightened attention on calibration to avoid precarious
errors through, e.g., silent failures or direct misclassifications.
Focus on attaining improved model calibration in transformer models has steadily
increased as their application in the industry is expanding rapidly. Methods that
mimic uncertainty modeling, such as Monte-Carlo Dropout [GG16] and Temperature
Scaling [Guo+17], have been tested for pre-trained transformer models with varying
success [Xia+22]. Specific Bayesian methods have been tested on transformer mod-
els recently, including Mean-field Variation Inference applied to transformer models
[Xue+21; Cin+21], a posterior Laplace Approximation used on a low-rank adap-
tion to calibrate large language Models [Yan+23], Sparse Gaussian Processes [CL23],
Deep Ensembling [Xia+22], and Last-Layer Laplace Approximations applied to Distil-
==PAGE:12==
1 Introduction
2
BERT [Dax+22b]. The usage of these posterior approximation methods has generally
yielded improved calibration. Nonetheless, most still struggle to outperform simple
methods such as Temperature Scaling.
One possible solution to facilitate Bayesian posterior inference for Natural language
Processing is selecting a subset of the total network parameters.
In recent work,
subnetwork Inference has been proposed as a scalable methodology for computing
otherwise intractable posterior approximations [Dax+22a]. The framework has addi-
tionally been applied to small neural networks where subsets of stochastic parameters
have been shown to outperform fully Bayesian models [Sha+23]. The framework of
modeling Partially stochastic Bayesian Neural Networks can potentially make poste-
rior inference feasible for a much larger class of neural networks and across different
posterior approximation methods.
Problem formulation
In this thesis, an extensive exploration of the subnetwork framework is conducted.
To verify the validity of partially stochastic Bayesian neural networks, the superior
performance of stochastic subnetworks observed by Sharma et al.
[Sha+23] is re-
produced using the high-fidelity posterior approximation method Hamiltonian Monte
Carlo [Bet18].
In this thesis, we extend the experiment procedure to include six
different methods: Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), Stochastic Weight Averaging -
Gaussian (SWAG) [Mad+19], which has previously shown promising results for uncer-
tainty estimation in NLP [Tal+23], the Laplace approximation [Dax+22b; Mac92b]
for which subnetworks has been comprehensively studied, and Variational Inference
(VI) [BKM17] that is a fast and well-known method for posterior inference. Addi-
tionally, we include two node-based approaches [Dus+20; Tri+22], where auxiliary
stochastic parameters are introduced as an extension to the model and subsequently
estimated through VI.
These methods for posterior approximation were chosen because they are fairly rep-
resentative of the diverse array of approximation methods in existence.
HMC is
expected to cover the same type of behavior as would otherwise be exhibited by
a Markov-Chain-based sampling process [Bet18]. The Laplace approximation and
SWAG represent the class of ’post-hoc’ posterior approximation schemes. Further-
more, the Mean-Field VI approximation employed in this thesis may not necessarily
provide a complete picture of how all variational families in VI will perform in a
partially stochastic setting.
It may, however, yield a fair indication.
Lastly, the
two node-based methods represent a slightly different perspective on the induction of
stochasticity into the model. One where rather than attempting to approximate the
original posterior of the model parameters, we introduce supplementary parameters
over which posterior inference may perform better.

Research questions
While previous research [Sha+23; Dax+22b], has shed some light on the concept of
partial stochasticity, the area remains fairly unexplored. This thesis seeks to under-
stand and analyze the following three main topics.
I Does partial stochasticity offer improvement over deterministic and fully stochas-
tic variants across multiple posterior inference methods?
II Can partial stochasticity be successfully applied to large-scale transformer models
in the context of Natural Language Processing?
III How can we best select the stochastic parameter subset both in terms of optimal
performance and minimal computational requirements?
Question I
The first major research point is whether partial stochasticity offers improvement over
non-stochastic variants. If so, can it achieve comparable or even improved perfor-
mance over its fully stochastic variants? We perform a series of ramping experiments
to ascertain the effects of partial stochasticity across posterior inference methods pre-
viously mentioned. For fast experimentation, we consider three small-scale regression
tasks from the University of California, Irvine (UCI) Machine Learning Repository
[KLN]. This allows us to use relatively small neural networks, limiting the amount
of approximations that are otherwise needed due to computational constraints. We
perform several experiments varying the percentage of stochastic parameters in the
models across all posterior inference methods. The purpose is to establish the amount
of stochasticity required for each method to perform well. This is highly relevant, as
it will help us determine which methods are most readily applied to larger neural net-
works. Diagnostics over each method’s performance is done by means of the metrics:
Negative log-likelihood (NLL), Mean Squared Error (MSE), and Expected Calibration
Error (ECE), to weigh the advantages and disadvantages of applying the Bayesian
posterior inference frameworks.
Question II
To study partial stochasticity in the context of Natural Language Processing, we select
the two best-performing methods from the previous experiments that are most readily
applicable to large transformer models [Vas+17; San+20]. The chosen methods were
the Laplace approximation and SWAG, which both showed promising performance
in the previous experiments and have been tested in the context of Natural Language
Processing previously [Tal+23; Dax+22b]. To allow for agile experimentation, we
choose a single, moderately sized transformer architecture that still achieves high
performance [San+20]. However, even moderately sized networks pose computational
==PAGE:14==
1 Introduction
4
challenges for the Laplace approximation. Hence, we employ the Kronecker-factored
Approximate Curvature (KFAC) [MG20], which we show to introduce only small
performance losses on the UCI regression tasks. We explore the effects of partial
stochasticity, comparing it to the non-stochastic base model, the industry-standard
benchmark temperature scaling , and its fully Bayesian counterparts. We base the
experiments on three General Language Understanding Evaluation (GLUE) tasks
[Wan+19a] and an additional dataset for the sentiment analysis task [Maa+11]. The
main objective of the experiments is to ascertain if the concept of partial stochasticity
can be successfully applied to large-scale transformer models.
Question III
Stochastic subset selection is far from trivial. Selecting the parameters with the high-
est influence on the posterior predictive could conceivably be the optimal solution.
However, it is diﬀicult to reason in the space of the posterior predictive, as it requires
an approximation of the posterior distribution of the parameters. Additionally, in
most methods for posterior inference, the distribution over the stochastic subset will
depend on which parameters are included in this set. Thus, we turn our attention to
weight-space instead. For the small-scale regression results analyzed in question II.
We follow the maximum ℓ1-norm subnetwork selection strategy proposed by Sharma
et al. [Sha+23]. To motivate this subnetwork selection heuristics, we perform ex-
periments comparing the performance to a selection strategy based on the largest
marginal variances, outlined in [Dax+22b], and a randomized scheme. We find that
a parameter magnitude-based - ℓ1 - approach yielded the highest performance for
minimal stochasticity. In the NLP section we seek to extend the parameter ℓ1-norm
selection to an operator norm scheme. Where we conduct several experiments on
stochastic subset selection heuristics, most of these experiments indicate that a ho-
mogenous positioning across the model is preferable.
Thus, the findings on stochastic operator positioning from the NLP analysis are
combined with the parameter magnitude-based selection strategy.
We propose a
framework for selecting substructures in a KFAC factorization of network parameters
(Sparse-KFAC). The method significantly reduces the number of stochastic parame-
ters and memory capacity needed when calibrating transformer models for Natural
Language Processing with the Linearized Laplace Approximation. To show the gen-
eralizability of our findings on the Laplace approximation, we conduct additional
experiments on base models specifically designed to perform better in terms of cali-
bration.

2.3.3
Variational Inference
In Variational Inference (VI) the intractable posterior of a neural network’s param-
eters is approximated using a tractable proposal distribution where an optimization
problem can be solved such that the proposal distribution, q, closely approximates
the target distribution, p. This setup can be formalized as follows.
• Propose a Variational family Q of ”simple” distributions.
• Define a similarity measure D(q||p) that should relate the proposal and target
distributions to each other.
• An optimal proposal distribution q ∈Q should be found by minimizing the
similarity measure, such that q∗= arg minq∈Q D[q||p].
The approximate family should be chosen such that tractability and correctness of
approximation are weighed against each other. Free-form factorized can approximate
many distributions, but have slow convergence.
In this study, the popular fixed

A whole range of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods exists. The founda-

Markov Chains
A first-order Markov chain is a stochastic process that undergoes transitions from one
state to another, where the probability of the next state depends only on the current
state. Let zt be the state of the system at time t, the Markov property then gives
p(zt+1|zt, zt−1, . . . , z0) = p(zt+1|zt).
(2.43)
This means that the future state depends only on the present state and not on the
sequence of states that preceded it. The Transition kernel for a Markov Chain is
defined as
T(zt+1|zt) ≡p(zt+1|zt).
(2.44)
Where the exact specifications of the transition kernel depend on which algorithm
is used.
The goal here is to construct a Markov chain over states, such that the
distribution of the states is equivalent to the target distribution.

by the MAP parameters. I.e once the stochastic subset is chosen, those weights
are randomly initialized for further training.
• Additive- & Multiplicative node-based - Both are node-based methods,
initialized from a fully trained MAP solution. Both have significantly fewer
stochastic parameters than the other methods, and both introduce auxiliary
parameters into the model.
• SWAG & Laplace - Both are methods that are initialized from a fully trained
MAP model and approximate the posterior distribution of the parameters with
a Gaussian with location parameter close (SWAG) or equal (Laplace) to the
mode of the original network parameters.
We have primarily chosen to highlight the negative log-likelihood (NLL) for the dif-
ferent methods, as seen in Figures 3.3 to 3.5. The NLL can be viewed as a mixture
metric of the mean-squared error (MSE) and calibration metrics.

3.3.5
Model Fit and Calibration
In Section 3.3 the methods: HMC, SWAG, VI, Laplace, Additive- and Multiplicative
node-based were compared based on their performance as measured by the metric
Negative log-likelihood (2.14) evaluated on the test sets. NLL inherently considers
uncertainty and predictive performance and is a good overall measure of the perfor-
mance of a given model. Solely studying NLL can nevertheless lead to underinformed
conclusions. To further understand the trade-offs of each method, two other metrics,
MSE and ECE, are investigated.
Two important factors of the predictive capabilities of a probabilistic model are its
ability to predict the correct values (measured with MSE (2.17)), and how well it is
calibrated, meaning how well the predicted probabilities of a model corresponds to
the actual distribution (measured here by ECE (2.15)).
In Tables 3.6 to 3.8 the median MSE is displayed for the 3 UCI datasets over all
percentiles. HMC is similar to observations in Section 3.3 deemed the overall best-
performing model across different levels of partiality.

3.4
Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
Partially stochastic BNNs
In this chapter, we thoroughly analyze the effect of applying the partially stochastic
BNN framework presented in [Dax+22a; Sha+23] across various Bayesian posterior
approximation methods. This investigation is done to verify the validity of modeling
a subset of a neural network’s parameters as stochastic and achieving equal or even
improved performance compared with model stochasticity. Sharma et al.
[Sha+23]
show these results in a ramping experiment on three UCI datasets with an HMC pos-
terior approximation. The results observed by Sharma et al. are initially reproduced
in Figure 3.2. To further explore the effects of partial stochasticity. We carry out
ramping experiments by varying the size of the stochastic subset across the meth-
ods HMC, VI, Laplace Approximation (Laplace), SWAG, and Multiplicate/Additive
Node-based. The experiments are conducted across the three UCI datasets: Boston,
Energy, and Yacht.
From the NLL results presented in Figures 3.3 to 3.5, that the inclusion of a stochas-
tic subset yielded an improvement over the MAP solutions. This was evident across
nearly all dataset/method settings over varying percentages of stochasticity. Addi-
tionally, we observed that partial stochasticity often outperformed or showed perfor-
mance equal to its fully stochastic versions for most methods. Due to the number of
different methods analyzed in this thesis and the varying tendencies between them,
we shall now discuss them individually in a brief manner.
HMC
For HMC, we observe a steadily improving performance when increasing the size of
the stochastic subset {wS}. In Figure 3.3, the best-performing percentiles range be-
tween 5. and 23—percent of the total model parameters, all outperforming the fully
stochastic models.
As HMC samples from the true posterior, it can approximate
the distribution very accurately given enough samples, which can be observed clearly
in the NLL results where it outperforms all other presented methods Figures 3.4
and 3.5.
Interestingly when comparing HMC to Laplace, HMC requires a larger
subset before it reaches its optimum. This is especially evident in the Yacht and
Energy datasets. This might be attributed to the fact that HMC can capture the full
posterior complexity as opposed to Laplace which only captures Gaussian-like parts
of the posterior. Laplace captures the Gaussian features of the posterior using 1%
of the parameters with the largest ℓ1-norm, and then plateaus. HMC on the other
hand manages to reach lower NLL values when including more stochastic parameters.
We must comment on the surprising result that subnetwork HMC can outperform
full stochasticity. It is surprising since it has been shown that partially stochastic
==PAGE:60==
3.4 Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
50
models theoretically have the same predictive capabilities as their fully stochastic
versions. However, theoretically, there is little basis for increased performance. We
hypothesize that it is a consequence of the curse of dimensionality. The same number
of samples from the posterior are taken over all percentages of stochasticity. Since the
dimensionality of the parameter space is increasing, additional samples are needed
to represent the space to the same degree. Furthermore, since the samples in the
Markov chain are not i.i.d. and the Monte Carlo estimate of the posterior predictive
is improper, i.e. it does not break the curse of dimensionality.
Laplace
Partially stochastic variants of Laplace achieve predictive performance, surpassing
their fully stochastic counterparts. Laplace captures ’all’ attainable8 uncertainty with
very few parameters, often 1 percent of the available network parameters being the
best performing or on par with higher percentiles, which can be seen in Figure 3.5.
Laplace is a local approximation that captures uncertainty in the vicinity of the
posterior mode. The Gaussian defined by the curvature of the parameters at the mode
can capture symmetric, Gaussian-like information. However, if more uncertainty is
contained in skewed, multi-modal, or heavy-tailed parts of the distribution, Laplace
will struggle to capture this. It must be concluded then, as explained earlier, that
much of the Gaussian distributed uncertainty is contained in the MAP parameters
with the highest ℓ1-norm.
SWAG
We have observed that SWAG performs well in a partially stochastic setting. It is
3rd best on NLL on two out of three datasets when focusing on smaller percentiles,
Tables 3.3 to 3.5. Generally, the method is in the middle range regarding all three
metrics, NLL, MSE, and ECE. Several hyperparameters potentially have a large effect
on the performance of SWAG. In this study, we only optimize the learning rate/step
size component.
It could potentially yield significant improvements with a more
thorough search of the hyperparameter space.
Variational Inference
From Figures 3.3, 3.6 and 3.8 and Table 3.6, we observe that VI can have varying
success. This indicates that the simple family of mean-field Gaussian distributions
does not manage to incorporate uncertainty effectively. A drawback of utilizing VI
also presents itself, namely its tendency to prioritize uncertainty over predictive per-
formance. On the Boston dataset, VI effectively optimizes calibration and captures
uncertainty Table 3.9. Meanwhile, the MSE increases drastically Table 3.6. This
8All by Laplace attainable uncertainty. We do not argue that it captures the full posterior
==PAGE:61==
3.4 Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
51
trade-off is not ideal, as a better-calibrated model is only of interest if it retains most
of its predictive capabilities.
Node-based BNNs
Both node-based methods show a performance increase as the percentage of stochas-
tic parameters increases. The multiplicative node-based partial version performs sig-
nificantly better than the fully stochastic version. However, equal performance is
achieved on two datasets around the inclusion of 37 pct. of the stochastic parameters.
The additive tends to improve ’faster,’ and partial stochasticity outperforms full on
two datasets. This faster improvement can likely be attributed to the larger number
of parameters included at a lower ’percentage’ for the additive method Figure 3.4.
Both node-based methods display poor calibration, seen in Figure 3.8 compared to
the other methods. It is, however, important to recall that they contain significantly
fewer stochastic parameters than the other methods. In this study, VI is used for ap-
proximation of the posterior introduced by the auxiliary variables of the Node-based
methods. VI has a series of limitations, evident in Figure 3.3, and may adversely
affect the node-based methods. However, we did not observe a similar decrease in
predictive performance on the node-based methods as on VI, possibly due to the fact
that the underlying MAP solution remains invariant to the estimates on the stochas-
tic parameters.
It is possible that the node-based methods could show improved
performance under a different inference scheme. Node-based methods might be inves-
tigated in future work as methods for e.g. fine-tuning Large-language models similar
to Bayesian LoRA [Yan+23].
Subset selection heuristic
In this study, we follow Sharma et al.
[Sha+23] and select the stochastic subset
{wS} by their ℓ1-norm, where the largest norm is selected first. In Section 3.1.3, an
empirical comparison on UCI datasets is conducted between choosing the ℓ1-norm
[Sha+23] vs. marginal variance [Dax+22a]. In Figure 3.9 the ℓ1-norm subset selec-
tion heuristic is found to be significantly better on smaller percentiles of stochasticity.
Ideally, we would select the parameters with the highest impact on the predictive
posterior distribution. However, as it is diﬀicult to reason in function space, i.e., on
the model’s outputs, we have instead turned to reasoning on weight space. Daxberger
et al.
[Dax+22a] argue that the parameters with the largest marginal variances are
likely to have the largest impact on the posterior predictive distribution. However,
neural networks are underdetermined due to the large number of parameters relative
to the datasets [LDS89]. Hence, many weights will have little or no impact on the
final output. Therefore, the curvature around those weights will be relatively small,
leading to high marginal variances through the inverse Hessian. Furthermore, these
weights are unlikely to have large norms, as the prior term p(w) corresponds to ℓ2
regularization on the parameters. We do not argue that the optimal parameter se-
==PAGE:62==
3.4 Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
52
lection is from the ℓ1 norm. Indeed, methods used for model pruning [LDS89] may
yield improved results. However, we argue and demonstrate that in some instances,
it is preferable to a selection based on the largest marginal variances.
The maximum ℓ1-norm is applied throughout Section 3.3, showing promising conver-
gence across a wide selection of posterior approximation methods. To further validate
the selection heuristic, an ablation study compares ℓ1-norm to a random subset se-
lection in Figure 3.10. The findings are less conclusive compared with Figure 3.9.
However, we still observe that ℓ1-norm selection tend to outperform random selection
for small percentages of stochasticity, given no significant additional computational
overhead. This subset selection strategy is continued and extended on for transformer
models Chapter 4.
In Figure 3.11, we observe that for small percentiles of stochasticity distribution of
selected parameters is skewed towards the classifier layer. This substantiates the va-
lidity of including the last layer in a stochastic subset, as seen in [Dax+22b; Sha+23].
It should be noted that the absolute number of parameters is relatively small in the
last layer compared with the previous layers.
Method Scalability
HMC outperforms other methods in nearly all datasets and subset sizes configura-
tions, seen in e.g.
Tables 3.4 and 3.8.
However, a disadvantage of HMC is the
computational load, which restricts the method to problems with limited complexity.
The restrictions come in several formats, e.g. The sequential nature of HMC restricts
it from scalability through parallelization. An additional restriction comes from the
Leapfrog integration steps, which require numerous gradient evaluations for different
proposals. Gradient calculations, unfortunately, require both forward and backward
passes and are generally an approximation of the true gradient due to mini-batch
training. Additionally, the Leapfrog integrator uses a fixed step size and thus does
not adapt to the local geometry of the space. A fixed step size cannot adequately
capture the dynamics in areas with high curvature or complex features, leading to
poor sampling eﬀiciency. These restrictions and other limitations make HMC infeasi-
ble for complex posterior approximations such as Transformer models used in NLP.
Variational inference lacks certainty of retaining predictive capabilities and it is thus
left for others to study its usages in partially stochastic neural networks. The two
node-based methods, although optimized with VI, display promising results. Partic-
ularly when considering the relatively low number of stochastic parameters in those
methods. Like the other methods (except VI) they are trained in a two-stage manner.
I.e first a MAP solution must be found, and subsequently, the node-based posterior
approximation can be fitted. Thus, compared to a method like Laplace, only one
==PAGE:63==
3.4 Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
53
additional forward pass on the training data is required for fitting. The node-based
methods may require significantly more training time, as we introduce newly initial-
ized parameters, that may lie far from their true posterior.
SWAG possibly extends well to larger models. The fact that the space is searched
locally around the originally found solution means that training time can be limited.
Additionally, it does not have quadratic scaling of memory requirements, which is
ideal for larger models. Depending on available resources it can be altered e.g. by
changing the number of columns in the deviation matrix, to require less memory.
This is of course at the expense of a less reliable posterior approximation.
Thus
SWAG is a valid choice as a scalable method which will be tested in our experiments
on posterior approximation in the NLP setting.
Finally, Laplace is applied to NLP due to the limited number of parameters needed
for effective uncertainty modeling. Additionally, Hessian approximations like KFAC
allow for a sub-quadratic scaling of Laplace, which makes the approximation feasible
with far greater parameter subsets. Another feature of Laplace is its conservation
of predictions derived from its mode-centered approximation. The latter makes it
ideal in modern applications, where the certainty of predictive performance is very
important.

3.5
Conclusion
This chapter investigated the effects of introducing partial stochasticity in small neu-
ral networks for regression across the three University of California, Irvine (UCI)
datasets [KLN] for six different posterior approximation methods. The investigated
methods consisted of Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), Laplace Approximation,
Stochastic Weight Averaging - Gaussian (SWAG), Variational Inference (VI), and
Multiplicative- and Additive Node-based. We found that invariantly of the chosen
method, partial stochasticity improved performance when compared with the MAP
solution when measured in terms of negative log-likelihood (NLL). Further, partial
stochasticity outperformed or matched fully stochastic models for all methods except
SWAG. The high-fidelity posterior inference method, HMC, was shown to be supe-
rior. However, due to scalability issues, an extension of the method to NLP was not
possible.
The Laplace Approximation displays promising results across all three UCI datasets,
both as measured by the NLL and the expected calibration error (ECE). Since the
Laplace approximation does not alter the posterior mean, it did not alter the predic-
tive performance as measured by the mean squared error (MSE). Our investigations
showed that it requires a small stochastic fraction of the total parameters to effectively
model the inherent uncertainty in the network parameters. The Kronecker-factored
Approximate Curvature (KFAC), which allows the Laplace Approximation to be ex-
tended to larger problems, was shown to have minimal performance loss compared to
the full generalized Gauss-Newton (GGN) Laplace approximation. Additionally, we
showed that the Laplace approximation required the smallest stochastic subset for
performance increase among all methods. For SWAG, partial stochasticity improved
performance over the MAP solution. However, it presented as a more monotonic
increase towards full stochasticity, contrary to e.g. the Laplace approximation. Ad-
ditionally, it was the 3rd best-performing method after the Laplace approximation
and HMC.
VI displayed problematic trade-offs between calibration (ECE) and predictive perfor-
mance (MSE) on the UCI Boston dataset [DG12]. For most applications, maintaining
high predictive performance is preferable to good calibration. Some of these problems
could probably be corrected through enhanced model engineering. Multiplicative- and
Additive node-based showed varying results across the performance measures NLL,
MSE, and ECE. However, both methods are inherently partial, and even their fully
stochastic versions contain only approximately 10 pct. of the number of stochastic pa-
rameters as the other methods. Thus, when compared in terms of the absolute number
of stochastic parameters, the node-based methods exhibited high performance, com-
parable to the other methods.
We empirically showed that using ℓ1-norm as a subset selection heuristic proved ad-
vantageous across all methods and datasets. This paradigm outperformed a random
==PAGE:65==
3.5 Conclusion
55
selection strategy, and the strategy based on marginal variance, shown in [Dax+22a].
The four methods HMC, VI, Multiplicative- and Additive node-based, are not ex-
plored for our investigations on Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks. HMC is
computationally infeasible. VI showed unsuitable convergence properties. The scala-
bility of Multiplicative- and Additive node-based elicits an exploration into applica-
tions on larger models. However, this is left for further research. Finally, Laplace and
SWAG were selected for application on NLP tasks as they showed good performance
across all three datasets.

Natural Language Processing and Deep learning
Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a field of computer science concerned with ex-

from computational linguistics to build models that can solve tasks similar to human
inference on a natural language text. This can include tasks such as Question answer-

Natural

linguistic research, which gave models poor scalability and flexibility. The approxi-
mative nature of Machine Learning models has proved effective for NLP modeling,
with e.g., models like Hidden Markov models and N-gram models being prevalent for
tasks like Speech recognition and Machine translation [Blu04].
The field of NLP was greatly affected by the introduction of deep learning models,
especially transformer models, with their attention mechanism revolutionizing the ca-
pabilities of NLP models [Vas+17]. The applications of transformer models in NLP
are diverse and impactful. Machine translation has achieved high accuracy in trans-
lating text between languages. Sentiment classification categorizes opinions in text,
essential for e.g. marketing and customer service. Enhanced naturalness and under-
standing in chatbots and virtual assistants are now possible. Text summarization and
speech recognition, converting spoken language into text, are also notable application
areas, revolutionizing voice-controlled assistants and transcription services.
A theoretical introduction is done to understand the mechanism that allows Trans-
formers models to handle the complex domain of NLP effectively. We will introduce

4.1.1
Transformers
The concept of transformers was introduced by Vaswani et al. in their seminal 2017
paper ”Attention Is All You Need”[Vas+17].
The transformer model was a novel
way of handling sequential data processing, solely using an attention mechanism and
fully connected feed-forward neural networks. This deviated from the inherent recur-
rence of Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long-Short Term Memory models
(LSTMs) [She20]. The switch allowed for extensive parallelism and significantly im-
proved eﬀiciency and effectiveness in handling large amounts of sequential data i.e.
text.

4.1.2
BERT
Bidirectional Encoder Representations from transformers (BERT) [Dev+19] is a trans-
former encoder model using only the encoder part of the original transformer archi-
tecture. Unlike the original transformer model that handles the input directionally
==PAGE:70==
4.1 Natural Language Processing and Deep learning
60
(usually forward). BERT processes the entire sequence in a bidirectional manner,
thereby allowing the model to capture context from both directions (bidirectional
context). This leads to a richer understanding of the meaning of each word in its
sentence context. The encoder thus produces a representation of tokens that include
a deep contextualization of the token in relation to the sequence it is presented in.
BERT builds on the framework of first pre-training the transformer model with a
semi-supervised training mechanism.
Subsequently, a task-specific ’simple’ neural
network is appended to the encoder, and all model parameters are fine-tuned to the
task. This ’simple’ neural network will be referred to as the classification head for
the tasks presented in this thesis. The pre-training is done on a large corpus of text.
It involves two main tasks. The first is masked language modeling, where random
tokens are masked, and the model predicts the original input. The second is next sen-
tence prediction, where the model predicts whether a sentence pair consists of a pair
of adjacent sentences or a pair of unrelated sentences [Dev+19]. This pre-training
allows for a general-purpose encoder that can be fine-tuned to various tasks.
BERT extends on the static word embedding frameworks such as Word2Vec [Mik+13]
or GloVe [PSM14] which generates a vector representation of a word in a vocabulary.
Whereas these embeddings are static for a word independent of context, BERT allows
for context-dependent embeddings. This deep contextualization provides a signifi-
cantly more accurate representation of the actual variations in meaning associated
with a word in different contexts.
4.1.3
DistilBERT
DistilBERT [San+20] is a smaller and faster version of BERT, trained through a pro-
cess called knowledge distillation [HVD15]. This process involves training a smaller
model, the student (DistilBERT), to replicate the behavior of a larger, pre-trained
teacher model (BERT). DistilBERT follows the transformer architecture but with
fewer layers compared to BERT.
4.1.3.1

predictive capabilities are retained as best possible. The training objective used in
DistilBERT combines three losses. A cross-entropy loss is constructed between the
soft-target produced by Tm and the predictions made by Sm for the masked language

derstand textual relationships by discerning the logical relationships between phrases
or sentences, to have general world knowledge for example, inferring that ”a robin is
a bird” from the premise ”the robin is building a nest”.

4.4
Methods
This section introduces the methodology around DistilBERT as a partially stochastic
neural network. The treated tasks and datasets will be elaborated on, along with
the configuration of DistilBERT used as a baseline and MAP estimate for the par-
tially stochastic models. The effects of applying different levels of stochasticity are
investigated similarly to the exploration done Section 3.3. The posterior inference
methods introduced to these experiments are SWAG described in Section 2.3.5 and
the Laplace Posterior approximation explained in Section 2.3.1. Three different Nat-
ural Language Understanding (NLU) tasks are included in this study: Sentiment
classification, Paraphrase Identification, and Natural Language Inference explained
in Section 4.1.4. The GLUE datasets[Wan+19a] for the respective tasks are inves-
tigated. For the Sentiment classification task the IMDb dataset [Maa+11] will be
included too.
4.4.1
Datasets
SST-2 (Stanford Sentiment Treebank-2)
SST-2[Soc+13] is a sentiment analysis dataset. This dataset contains sentences from
movie reviews and their corresponding sentiment labels. The sentiment is binary:
positive or negative. The task is to determine the sentiment of a given sentence.
==PAGE:80==
4.4 Methods
70
MRPC (Microsoft Research Paraphrase Corpus)
MRPC[DB05] is a paraphrase identification dataset. It consists of sentence pairs auto-
matically extracted from online news sources, with annotations indicating whether the
sentences in each pair are semantically equivalent (i.e., paraphrases of each other).The
task is to predict whether each pair of sentences has the same meaning.
RTE (Recognizing Textual Entailment)
RTE[DGM06] is an NLI/textual entailment dataset. The RTE datasets, collected
over several challenges, consist of pairs of sentences (a premise and a hypothesis) and
a binary label indicating whether the hypothesis can be inferred from the premise.
The model must determine whether the hypothesis is true (entailment) or false (not
entailment) which is the two labels neutral and contradiction collapsed such that the
problem becomes a binary classification problem.
IMDb (Internet Movie Database)
IMDb[Maa+11] is a dataset compiled from the (Internet Movie Database). It con-
sists of movie reviews written by IMDb users. Similar to SST-2 the task is to perform
binary sentiment classification. Reviews are classified as either positive (if the user
liked the movie) or negative (if the user did not like the movie).
The GLUE NLU benchmark uses a hidden label policy to ensure that test data has
not mistakenly been included in the training process. In this study, swift development
was prioritized, and the provided development-sets, or validation sets, were treated as
test sets, in line with the methodology presented in [San+20]. The training set was
then split into train-validation with a 90/10% split. 5 train-val splits were created
for each dataset and used for estimating performance uncertainties. For the IMDb
dataset, the original test set is used as a test set. Again 5 train-validation splits are
created, now with a 80/20% split. The number of samples per split along with class
distributions for the 4 datasets can be seen in Table 4.2.

4.4.2.2
Laplace Approximation
The Laplace approximation framework utilized in this study is based on the laplace-torch
[Dax+22a] framework also applied in the exploration of Laplace Approximation done
in Chapter 3. We extend the framework to allow for partial stochasticity in the KFAC
approximation. We also include a patch to seamlessly incorporate Huggingface mod-
els4.
An implementation is created where entire neural network ”modules” are made stochas-
tic. ”Module” types include all linear or aﬀine mappings in the model, with the ex-
ception of Layer-Norm parameters Section 4.1.1.4. From the multi-head attention
blocks, these include the W Q, W K, W V , W O matrices along with their respective
biases. From the MLP components, it includes W I, W II, see (4.3) for further details.
An additional selection strategy, Sparse-KFAC (elaborated in Section 4.3), is imple-
mented where a more fine-grained selection is possible inside each module.
To optimize the prior precision we perform a sweep of possible values, these can be
seen in Table 4.5. We select the prior precision yielding the lowest NLL on the vali-
dation set, and use that to evaluate the test set. Due to computational restrictions,
the sweep is fairly coarse.

4.4.3
Experiments Overview
We define an experimental setup to thoroughly explore subnetwork inference for Nat-
ural Language Processing. The experiments are conducted across 3 different tasks -
Natural Language Inference, Paraphrase Identification, and Sentiment Classification,
described in Section 4.1.4 - and 4 datasets - RTE, MRPC, and (SST2 and IMDB),
described in Section 4.4.1.
The section includes a general overview of the experiments conducted. Additionally,
we conduct certain miscellaneous experiments for some of the datasets to explore
unexpected behavior. Since the general methodology of those does not deviate sig-
nificantly from the experiments outlined here, and for clarity, these will be defined
and explained when presented in the results section, Section 4.5. The validation set
is used to optimize inference method hyperparameters with the NLL as the perfor-
mance metric. For the Laplace approximation, this involves the prior precision, τ.
For SWAG, this involves the SGD learning rate, ˆη. In the following, module and
layer are used interchangeably to describe the parameters in an MLP or Attention
layer of the DistilBERT architecture.
1. Random ramping. Laplace /SWAG - This experiment randomly samples
a sequence of the model’s linear layers (modules) and sequentially adds these
to the stochastic subset. This is described in Section 4.2.1.
2. Min-Max operator norm ramping (MLP). Laplace/SWAG - In this
experiment, we choose the sequence as the set of linear modules sorted by
their operator norm.
Coincidentally, this corresponds to only including the
MLP components of the transformer as the operator norm distribution of these
exceeds that of the Attention layers. The experimental setup is described in
Section 4.2.2.
3. Min-Max operator norm (Attention). Laplace/SWAG - This is equiva-
lent to the previous experiment except that only the Attention layers are con-
sidered. These are still sorted, so we include the linear modules with the largest
operator norm first Section 4.2.2.
4. Sublayer ramping full model. Laplace/SWAG - For the Laplace experi-
ment, we choose fully connected substructures of all linear modules and make
them stochastic, proposed as Sparse-KFAC and described in Section 4.3. For
the SWAG experiment, we choose the weights with the largest ℓ1 norm and
include them in the stochastic subset. This is equivalent to the methodology in
Section 3.3.
5. Sublayer ramping specific modules. Laplace - The Sparse-KFAC strategy
is applied to a specific/predefined subset of linear modules (e.g. 2 MLP modules
with the highest operator norm) Section 4.3.

4.5.1
MAP models
All ramping experiments in the following sections are based on MAP estimates of the
DistilBERT model. Here MAP is used to describe the pre-trained DistilBERT, which
is additionally fine-tuned on the dataset at hand as described in Section 4.4. While
a standalone specific functional form prior is not applied when training the model,
regularization is handled through e.g. Dropout, Early stopping, decoupled weight
decay, and L2 regularization in AdamW. These combined enforce regularization and
loosely correspond to prior.
The pre-trained model is fine-tuned on the training set with additional early stop-
ping through evaluation on the validation set. A comprehensive exploration based on
a MAP estimate early stopped at accuracy (MAPacc) is done for the 3 GLUE tasks.
This is done to maximize the predictive performance of each model and then ”add”
calibration using Bayesian methodology. For completeness, a short investigation of
using a MAP estimate stopped using cross-entropy (MAPnll) is done in Section 4.5.6.
Here, we write nll as minimization of the cross-entropy loss can be shown to be equiv-
alent to minimizing the Negative log-likelihood. The fine-tuned MAP models perform
comparably to the original DistilBERT on the selected GLUE tasks [San+20]. In Ta-
ble 4.6, the three metrics Accuracy, NLL, and ECE are shown for the MAP models

Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
Findings in Laplace Approximation ramping experiments
In Section 4.5 the results of several schemes for selecting the stochastic subset were
presented across three GLUE tasks. The majority of the results were centered around
using the operator norm of the linear transforms in the model to guide the subset
selection. We considered the maximum operator norm for the MLP modules, the
maximum operator norm for the attention modules, and a random selection strat-
egy. The results can be seen in Figures 4.5 and 4.10. On 2 out of three datasets,
we found that selecting the highest operator norm for the MLP modules led to the
best performance for 1-3 stochastic modules. RTE was, in this case, the exception,
where the minimum operator norm led to significantly better performance, as seen
in Figure 4.12. However, we argue that the added performance from the last layer
Laplace approximation caused this performance difference. Combining the operator
norm strategy with LLLA outperformed the minimum ramping scheme Figure 4.11.
Perhaps surprisingly, we found the opposite tendency to be true for the attention
modules. Choosing the minimum operator norm repeatedly either outperformed or
performed equally to the maximum ramping scheme, at least for a ’small’ number of
stochastic modules. If we allow for more than 5 stochastic modules, choosing the mod-
ules at random showed the best performance across all three datasets. This indicates
that a more homogeneous distribution of the stochastic modules in the network is to
be preferred. However, exploring module selection with a more uniform distribution
in mind remains an object of further study.
Findings in Laplace Approximation with Sparse-KFAC
To look beyond the modular schemes, we performed Sparse-KFAC (S-KFAC) ramp-
ing experiments on all three datasets. In one experiment, stochastic parameters were
placed in all model modules, and in the other, we selected a small number of mod-
ules and densely placed the stochastic parameters in those. The results can be seen
in Figures 4.7 and 4.13. For the latter, we found that the performance converged
around the inclusion of approximately 25% of the parameters in the specified mod-
ules. From the UCI results, one might have expected even to see partiality within the
modules surpass full stochasticity in terms of NLL. However, this was not observed at
a significant level for either of the datasets. It might indicate that although approx.
25% stochasticity is suﬀicient to capture the expressivity of the selected modules. The
limiting factor is those modules’ ability to capture the expressivity of the entire model.
In the full model S-KFAC experiments, we found that on 2 out of 3 datasets, using
only a very small percentage of the models’ parameters in the stochastic subset out-
performed the full KFAC Figures 4.7 and 4.13. The lowest NLL was found on MRPC
==PAGE:112==
4.7 Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
102
and RTE with less than 10 pct. stochastic parameters. Furthermore, on MRPC, no
significant improvement is observed after including 0.46 pct. of the parameters (the
best observed was at 1.59 pct.). For RTE, it is at 3.39 pct. of the parameters (the best
observed was at 9.0 pct.), where even LLLA outperforms the fully stochastic version.
It is not a simple task reasoning on the S-KFAC performance. However, if we recall
the Laplace results for the UCI datasets [KLN], we note that there generally is a per-
formance decrease after including around 1 pct. of the parameters in the stochastic
subset. If we loosely argue that every module in the DistilBERT model corresponds
to the neural network used in Chapter 3. Then, using entire modules would cause
sub-optimal performance. Thus, S-KFAC on the full model takes around the ideal
amount of stochasticity for each module, thereby capturing all the uncertainty that
it can from the full model. However, without propagating, the errors were found to
be induced in the UCI models when the model stochasticity approaches 100 pct.
Findings in SWAG ramping experiments
For the operator norm ramping experiments, i.e. for the MLP and attention modules,
we found no clear tendencies in performance difference across the three datasets. In-
terestingly, random ramping generally achieves the lowest NLL and highest accuracy
Figures 4.8 and 4.14. Indicating that a more uniform spread of the stochastic mod-
ules is to be preferred, as we also observed for the Laplace experiments. Furthermore,
partial stochasticity outperformed their fully stochastic counterparts regarding NLL
and accuracy in all cases.
Most notable in the SWAG results was that the mod-
ular schemes outperformed the ’sublayer’ ℓ1 ramping scheme Figures 4.9 and 4.16.
Using the sublayer approach has additional drawbacks. In current implementations
[Pas+17] it is not - readily - possible not to update specific parameters during the
SGD iterations. Hence, those parameters need to be manually reset to their original
values. This significantly adds to the overhead of the training process. Similar slicing
is also required during inference, where all parameters must be reset - although many
simply go back to current values.
Investigating computational/memory requirements
We shall now briefly dwell on matters of computational- and memory requirements.
For the KFAC Laplace approximation, the memory requirements grow quadratically
with the width of the network. This stems from the Fisher block approximation in
(4.16). Even for a relatively small model such as Distilbert, this has a significant
impact, but it remains viable for a GPU with at least a few gigabytes of memory. For
larger LLMs such as Llama, [Tou+23], the intermediate sizes in the MLP modules
can range up to a width of 25,000. Thus, storing the Fisher block of a single MLP
module requires several gigabytes of memory. This represents an attractive feature of
==PAGE:113==
4.7 Discussion of Posterior Approximation Methods
103
S-KFAC, which allows us to maintain a relatively low ’width’ of the fully connected
stochastic components while retaining much or all of the expressivity of their fully
stochastic counterparts. To highlight the extent of the improvement with S-KFAC,
we consider the performance observed at approximately 1.5 pct of all parameters. In
this case, the number of parameters and the memory requirement is approximately
4 times lower than for a single MLP module. It is also a factor 1.5 smaller than a
single attention module. On all datasets, S-KFAC with 1.5 pct. outperforms single
modules.
Another advantage of the S-KFAC approach over the full model is the
possibility of parallelization at inference time. The product of each block with the
Jacobians can be computed independently of one another, then summed to estimate
the class ’temperatures’. We also note that the memory requirement grows linearly
with the number of stochastic modules and the sequence length. In these cases, linear
growth is usually a best-case scenario and, therefore, a positive attribute with KFAC.
For SWAG the memory requirements grow linearly with the number of stochastic
parameters and columns in the deviation matrix, generally making it slightly more
lightweight than Laplace.
Scalability considerations
We must also consider that the linearized Laplace approximation requires a back-
ward pass for all outputs of the final layer, i.e. the number of classes in classification
problems such as these. Unfortunately, this makes the approach unfeasible for e.g.
Seq2Seq tasks, where the number of outputs corresponds to the vocabulary size. Fur-
thermore, the problem extends beyond NLP, for instance, in object detection - and
classification - where fast inference might be required for autonomous vehicles. The
number of possible objects quickly becomes too large. The SWAG approximation
does not suffer this drawback, as we sample from the posterior distribution to yield
an MC estimate of the predictive posterior. However, in this study, we used 50 sam-
ples, leading to 50 forward passes and, therefore, slower inference than was the case
for Laplace. Additionally, the sampling process itself is time-consuming. Approxi-
mately 1500 batches were needed for the IMDb dataset to perform inference on the
full test set. This means that we sampled from the posterior approximately 75,000
times. Depending on which limitations might exist, it could partially be solved by
sampling the needed MC samples from the posterior and subsequently using those
samples to evaluate the entire dataset. This carries certain problems that we shall
not go into further.
Hyperparmeter tuning
Laplace and SWAG’s performance highly depends on certain hyperparameter choices.
For Laplace, it is primarily the choice of prior precision. In the SWAG method, most
==PAGE:114==

104
of the hyperparameters of the SGD optimization scheme, such as learning rate and
momentum, play important roles. For the experiments outlined in this thesis, we
have primarily chosen to address the choice of learning rate and set the momentum
constantly to 0.9 for both the UCI and NLP experiments. However, it is necessary
for SWAG to retrain and evaluate on a validation set to find the optimal learning
rate, which is quite time-consuming for an exhaustive sweep of possible learning rates.
Hence, we made the choice we only evaluated on 50 pct, of the whole validation set for
both Laplace and SWAG. Furthermore, we only train SWAG with 400 steps totaling
80 snapshots during the learning rate optimization process. This establishes a limit
to how well, or far, we can explore the posterior. It may also introduce a data bias if
the data samples are not suﬀiciently representative of the whole distribution.
Early stopping on Likelihood
In Section 4.5.6, we presented results for models that were early stopped with negative
log-likelihood as criteria rather than accuracy. We observed a much smaller differ-
ence between the performance of the MAP solutions and the Laplace approximations.
However, the same tendencies were present as for the accuracy-stopped ones. I.e we
saw that partial stochasticity outperformed the fully stochastic versions at similar
small amounts of stochasticity as previously observed. The decrease in NLL came at
the cost of predictive performance on all datasets except SST2. Most notably, the
NLL-stopped model regressed to random guessing on RTE seen in Table 4.6. We
argue that one should stop at the lowest NLL and the highest accuracy. If the final
accuracy on the test set is not significantly different between the two, utilize the
NLL stopped model, as the MAP solution is likely to have a lower NLL than either
of the Bayesian methods presented here would otherwise yield. To further improve
that model with respect to NLL by for example, using the Laplace approximation
with small percentages/number of modules of stochasticity will lead to further im-
provement. Identifying a weighting between the two metrics for early stopping in
combination with these methods remains a topic for further research.
Qualitative Analysis
Finally, we briefly touched on some qualitative analysis points in Section 4.6. We
presented cases that were wrongly classified and subsequently either corrected by
SWAG or made much more uncertain by Laplace. We note that these are cases that
require either background information or intuition to answer correctly. We might
not expect that the model possesses those things despite its vast (pre-) training data.
Additionally, there were cases where the wording was confusing and had labels that
were arguably up for interpretation. These observed ’corrections’ by the two methods
over the MAP solution represent some of the advantages we expect from Bayesian

methods. We can see from the MRPC and SST2 calibration curves Figures 4.18(a)

4.8
Conclusion
In the latter part of this thesis, we explored the effect of introducing partial stochas-
ticity in natural language processing with Transformer models.
We used two methods of approximating the posterior distribution of the stochastic
parameters: Stochastic Weight Averaging - Gaussian (SWAG), KFAC Laplace ap-
proximation and full GGN Laplace approximation for LLLA. We investigated this
effect across three General Language Understanding Evaluation (GLUE) [Wan+19b]
tasks. The investigated tasks were Sentiment Classification, Natural Language Infer-
ence, and Paraphrase Identification, with the datasets Stanford Sentiment Treebank
(SST2) [Soc+13], Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE) [DGM06], and Microsoft
Research Paraphrase Corpus (MRPC) [DB05] respectively. An improvement was ob-
served for both Laplace approximation and SWAG when introducing partial stochas-
ticity. This was evident on all three GLUE tasks. We also observed partial outperform
full stochasticity for both methods across all three datasets.
In the previous chapter investigating regression on the UCI datasets[KLN] Chapter 3,
we found that choosing the neural network parameters with the largest ℓ1-norm was
an effect heuristic when defining a stochastic subset. To further explore this con-
cept, we extend the notion of the ℓ1-norm to full linear layers (modules) and use the
modules with the largest operator norm ||T||2. An exploration using both posterior
approximation methods Laplace and SWAG, was conducted by selecting the modules
with minimum and maximum operator norm for both the attention and Multi-Layer
Perceptron (MLP) layers and comparing them with randomly selecting layers. Par-
ticularly for Laplace, we found that selecting the modules with the highest operator
norm tended to perform better for a small number of modules. We found that the
random module selection yielded the highest performance once approximately 25 pct
of the model layers were stochastic.
The Sparse-KFAC (S-KFAC) framework in the Laplace approximation was introduced
for selecting dense parts of the weight matrices to combine the methodology of sub-
layer stochasticity i.e, the ℓ1 selection strategy presented in Section 3.1.3, for which
the results in Section 3.3 were highly favorable. And the findings in this section Sec-
tion 4.5, that showed a homogenous positioning of the stochastic parameters might
be superior to more location-specific options, Section 4.2.2. S-KFAC was tested in
two settings: one where the dense substructures were selected from a subset of mod-
ules and another where the substructures were selected over the entire model. For
the first, We showed that NLL converged to the full stochasticity on the specified
modules, at approx. 25 pct. stochasticity. Contrary to previous results, we did not
observe a performance increase over the fully stochastic version.
For the second S-KFAC setting, where we select dense parts of the weight matrices
distributed over the entire model, we showed that this methodology outperformed full
stochasticity on 2 out of 3 tasks. Additionally, on the same tasks, S-KFAC was shown
==PAGE:117==
4.8 Conclusion
107
to be the highest-performing subset selection scheme while requiring significantly less
memory and parameters than the others.
Although partiality led to the highest performance for SWAG cases, similar tenden-
cies observed with the Laplace approximation did not emerge concerning subset se-
lection. However, partial stochasticity yielded improved calibration and predictive
performance. Combined with the low memory allocation needed for SWAG, it makes
it a highly attractive method for further applications.
Finally, we tested the eﬀicacy of these methods on already well-calibrated models.
These were early stopped using negative log-likelihood, as opposed to accuracy in the
previous findings. We showed that partial stochasticity using the Laplace approxima-
tion improved the maximum a posteriori (MAP) and fully stochastic solutions across
two sentiment classification datasets. However, these improvements were much less
dramatic than the previously presented results.
One of the few experiments that remain to be seen, and indeed is not present in this
study, is how these methods and schemes respond to out-of-distribution data. Final
conclusions on the real-world application of these methods are highly dependent on
how they might interact with less ideal data. We leave this as a subject of further
research.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this work, we explored the effects of partial stochasticity in several uses. First
across three small-scale regression tasks and later on three different Natural Lan-
guage Processing (NLP) classification tasks, with separate sizes of the underlying
model. For the regression tasks, we investigated the effects of partial stochasticity
over six different methods of posterior approximations, including Hamiltonian Monte
Carlo (HMC) as a high-fidelity benchmark. We found that partial stochasticity repeat-
edly yielded a significant performance increase over the maximum a posteriori (MAP)
solution across all modes of posterior approximation. Additionally, for all methods
except SWAG, partial stochasticity outperformed their corresponding fully stochas-
tic variants. However, this tendency was not present for all levels of stochasticity for
all methods. Hence, in practical applications, finding a subset that outperforms the
fully stochastic variant in practice requires ramping experiments. We also observed
that stochastic subset selection based on parameter norms tended to outperform both
a posterior variance-based and random selection for low percentages of stochasticity
using the Laplace approximation. We later attempted to extend to linear layers (mod-
ules) on transformer models using the operator norm. Selecting the ideal subset is
far from trivial and remains a subject of further research. Lastly, we found that the
Laplace approximation and Stochastic Weight Averaging - Gaussian (SWAG) were
the best-performing methods most readily extendable to larger models. The Laplace
approximation displayed superior performance for small percentages of stochasticity,
outperforming even HMC. SWAG was chosen due to its relatively good performance
and the possibility that it may improve predictive power. Both the Multiplicative and
Additive node-based methods showed promising results and have favorable properties
due to their inherent partiality and their low number of stochastic parameters. Addi-
tional research into their eﬀicacy on larger transformer models would be of interest.
We found that partial stochasticity consistently yielded significant improvement over
the MAP solution for the NLP tasks. For the Laplace approximation, we observed
that the results from the small-scale regression tasks at least partially extended to a
large transformer model. Indeed, we observed a significant performance increase in
terms of negative log-likelihood from the MAP solution to approximately one percent
of stochastic parameters. SWAG showed a more monotonic performance increase, in
line with the previous regression results. Most notably, for the transformer model, par-
tial stochasticity with SWAG outperformed its fully stochastic variants, a tendency
==PAGE:119==
5 Conclusion
109
that was not present in the regression tasks.
In fact, partially stochastic models
outperformed their fully Bayesian versions, for both of the posterior approximation
methods on NLP tasks.
We sought to extend the norm-based stochastic subset selection approach from the
regression tasks to larger models by considering operator norms rather than individ-
ual parameter norms. This was shown to perform on the same level as or better than
random selection for a small number of stochastic modules. We observed that random
selection outperformed the other approaches for a larger number of stochastic modules.
This indicated that a more homogenous positioning of stochastic parameters across
the model is preferable to selecting a distinct subset from e.g. the Multilayer Per-
ceptron (MLP) modules. This indication and the favorable results from the ℓ1-norm
selection strategy Section 3.3 led to a final framework. Rather than selecting specific
modules, we selected small, dense parts of each weight matrix (and bias) across the
entire model. The dense parts were selected based on an ℓ∞norm strategy, to ensure
that the parameters with the highest ℓ1 norm were made stochastic. The framework
was denoted Sparse-KFAC (S-KFAC) for the resulting highly sparse covariance struc-
ture. We showed that S-KFAC yielded equal to or higher performance than any of
the other schemes. It did so while generally requiring significantly fewer parameters
and less memory. This approach also showed promising results in terms of decreas-
ing the computational demands of a predefined selection of modules e.g. the ones
with the highest operator norm. These results support the hypothesis that spread-
ing the stochastic parameters throughout the model is better than a local positioning.
In the interest of more qualitative analysis, we showcase how the Laplace approxi-
mation and SWAG can effectively handle cases requiring nuanced understanding or
interpretation. Both methods demonstrate an ability to add uncertainty to borderline
cases while retaining high confidence in more clear-cut scenarios.
Finally, we can consider future work that arises from this thesis.
First, we only
investigated Laplace and SWAG as approximation methods for the NLP experiments
conducted. However, both node-based methods used in Chapter 3 showed promising
results.
Applying these methods in the setting of NLP could lead to interesting
findings. Additionally, selecting the optimal stochastic subset remains an unsolved
problem. We hypothesize, that combining our findings with methods used in model
pruning could produce robust and well-performing strategies.
The base model in
this thesis was the DistilBERT encoder model, with only approximately 60 million
parameters. Therefore, validating that these results extend to even larger models,
where the scale may be in the billions, is crucial for practical applications. Finally,
an interesting avenue of research could be to perform a more thorough analysis of the
semantic and syntactic structures in the sentences on which more uncertainty was
induced.
