Abstract
Two different censoring methods for partial duration series (PDS) are applied to observed
precipitation in Denmark from Climate Grid Denmark (CGD). Extreme value analysis
(EVA) confirms that their results differ, and that extreme events within Denmark shows
regional variation, favoring setting a constant number of extremes and not a regionally
common threshold.
EVA is conducted on present and future projections of precipitation simulated by 19
regional climate models (RCMs).
Model differences are analysed in various variables
describing extreme events, and climate factors relating the present and future describing
variables are calculated. It is established that the pattern observed in average annual
precipitation is visible in the minimum extremes for 24-hour duration.
Variograms are used as a geospatial tool to analyse regional variance. Individual models
display high estimation uncertainty and results in some complex empirical variograms. The
well-fitting modelled variograms show a tendency of highest spatial variance in the east-
west direction, and suggests that extreme precipitation patterns are spatially independent
at a range of around 200 km.
Average variograms reveals a tendency of overestimation of the spatial auto-correlation in
the RCMs compared to CGD. Climate factors (CFs) show that the intensity of extreme
precipitation will increase in the future, and that future regional differences will be similar
to the presently observed regional differences with high variation between east and west.
CFs have larger ranges for 24-hour duration than for 1-hour duration.
CF ranges of
around 100 km for extreme precipitation events with return periods below 10 years suggests
that recommending regionally distinguished CFs is favourable in optimising future climate
resiliency in local urban drainage systems.

1 Introduction
1.1
Background
Since the pre-industrial era, development in human activities such as economic and popu-
lation growth have lead to increased emissions of greenhouse gases (GHGs) (IPCC, 2014).
The concentration of GHGs in the lower part of the atmosphere are unprecedentedly high,
affecting the energy balance of the earth and thereby the climate system. This effect is
also known as the greenhouse effect. IPCC (2014) establishes that anthropogenic GHG
emissions are extremely likely to be the predominant cause of the global warming ob-
served since the middle of the 20th century. Other observed climate changes include more
extreme weather events with increased frequency and magnitude, and thereby a more
extreme climate. Climate model ensembles project that the climate will become more ex-
treme in the future (IPCC, 2014). Long-lasting changes in the climate system and a more
extreme climate can impact both people and ecosystems and expose them to great risks.
Climate mitigation and adaptation are therefore important tasks in ensuring resiliency.
With increasing population density and the following increase in built-up areas with im-
permeable surfaces in cities, urban planning is especially challenged by climate change.
Run-off from impermeable surfaces needs drainage systems to avoid flooding following pre-
cipitation events. Precipitation, and particularly the extreme kind, is therefore a climate
system component of great importance for urban planning and resilience. Urban drainage
systems are an integrated part of city infrastructure, and are planned with the prospect of
being useful for a long period. Knowledge of the projected future climate and especially
extreme precipitation is therefore essential in the dimensioning of urban drainage systems.
Climate factors (CFs) can relate current climate conditions with future ones through cli-
mate model simulation, and is therefore a great tool for calculation of design intensities
in urban drainage. In the national guidelines for estimating and applying CFs for ex-
treme precipitation in Denmark (Gregersen et al., 2014a), the CFs vary as a function of
return period. However, it is known that extreme precipitation also varies both spatially
within Denmark and with temporal resolution (duration). Extreme precipitation is a rel-
atively local phenomenon, and can show big regional differences. Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al.
(2006) establish a statistical significant difference between observed extreme precipitation
intensities in west and east Denmark, and Gregersen et al. (2014b) determines explana-
tory variables and contributors to regional variability in extreme events in Denmark for
different intensities.
However, further exploration of both present and future regional
patterns in extreme precipitation is necessary before possibly recommending application
of regionally distinguished CFs.
Climate models and ensembles are essential in projecting the future climate. The coor-
dinated regional climate downscaling experiment (CORDEX) aims to advance and coor-
dinate the science and application of regional climate downscaling through global part-
nerships (CORDEX, 2020). Since 2013 the European Domain of CORDEX have released
regional climate model (RCM) simulations with a relatively high spatial resolution, ulti-
mately creating an ensemble of model simulations over time. By using information from
a model ensemble, various uncertainties are diminished (Benestad et al., 2017). The high
spatial resolution enables modelling of relatively local extreme events, and thereby also

climate conditions to be used in drainage models, but encounter difficulties in generating
sub-daily precipitation realistically. The EURO-CORDEX ensemble with 1- and 24-hour

1.2
Study objective
This study aims to identify climatic changes in extreme precipitation in Denmark that
has previously been unidentifiable, focusing especially on geographical variation. Different
methods in extreme event analysis will be tested on observational data to establish possible
differences in their results. Estimation of local statistical parameters enables extrapolation
of extreme events and analysis of possible regional differences.
Subsequently, extreme
event analysis and statistical parameter estimation for modelled historical and projected
future precipitation data makes it possible to establish regional differences in model data.
Multiple RCMs provides a small model ensemble, where both individual models will be
compared and collective trends will be analysed.
Temporal resolutions (durations) of
both 1 and 24 hours in modelled data allows identification of spatial variation on different
temporal scales, as well as comparison between them. Furthermore observed and modelled
data are compared. CFs will be calculated for modelled data, and their regional variation
analysed.

2 Methodology
2.1
Climate modelling
The climate system is continually changing due to interactions between its components
and external factors. Climate models aim to quantify these interactions and the changes
they cause by mathematically defining the physical processes involved. The output of
climate models are numerical simulations of atmospheric and surface variables such as
temperature, wind and precipitation. They can be forced by measured climate data and
used to project both past and future climate conditions (Benestad et al., 2017).
General circulation models (GCMs) are models used to study the climate system on a
global scale. GCMs commonly have a spatial resolution of around 100-500 km. Regional
climate models (RCMs) commonly have a spatial resolution of around 5-50 km, and can re-
solve phenomena happening on smaller scales such as extreme precipitation events. GCMs
can be downscaled and used to drive RCMs. Physical processes and their mathematical
representation in the climate models can differ, which leads to a range of possible GCM
and RCM combinations (Benestad et al., 2017).
Climate projections are representations of various possible states of the climate system in
the future. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) use four different
representative concentration pathway (RCP) scenarios to project the future climate in
their latest reports (IPCC, 2014). The RCPs each represent a set of mitigation scenarios
resulting in a radiative forcing of 2.6, 4.5, 6.0 and 8.5 W × m−2 in the end of the 21st
century. The RCP8.5 scenario, which is used in this study, is often regarded the most
extreme scenario. In planning urban drainage systems, design intensities are based on
projected extreme events to ensure that flooding only happens rarely, and that the drainage
is useful for a long time. RCP8.5 projects high extreme events and helps ensuring that
design intensities are sufficiently high for almost any future scenario.
It is therefore of interest to assess the projected future climate compared to a reference
period (often the present climate). For this purpose, change factors can be useful. When
looking at a climate variable, the change factor can also be called a climate factor (CF).
It is defined as:
CF =
Vfuture
Vreference

where Vfuture is the projected future value of a specific variable and Vreference is the value
of the same variable in the reference period. If CF < 1, the model projects a decrease of
the variable in the future, and if CF > 1 it projects a future increase.
2.2
Extreme value theory
Extreme value theory or extreme value analysis (EVA) is the part of statistics involving
extreme deviations from the median of probability distributions. This analysis of precipi-
tation extremes focus on deviations with high values. The methodology for selecting which
values qualify as extreme in a series of precipitation data is explained in Section 2.2.1. A
parametric analysis is conducted by fitting a mathematical model of statistical probability
to the series of extreme precipitation data. The choice of statistical probability function
and the parametric analysis is described in Sections 2.2.2 and 2.2.3 respectively.

2.2.1
Selection of extremes
There are two main methodologies for selecting which precipitation values qualify as ex-
treme in a data series; the block maxima or annual maxima series (AMS) and peak-over-
threshold (POT) or partial duration series (PDS) (Coles et al., 2001). With the AMS
approach, a specific number of precipitation events are selected within a number of tem-

2.2.4
Goodness of fit
Using the fitted distribution for extrapolation presumes that it describes the PDS series
well. Therefore, the fit of the probability distribution to the PDS is evaluated by the
two-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. It determines if two datasets differ significantly,
and has the advantage of making no assumption about the distribution of data. The null
hypothesis is that the PDS and the fitted GPD are from the same continuous distribution,
and the alternative hypothesis is that they are from different continuous distributions.
For the two datasets having empirical cumulative distribution functions F1(z) and F2(z),
the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test statistic D equals the maximum absolute difference between
F1(z) and F2(z) for the same z. Visually, the test statistic is therefore the largest vertical
distance between the cumulative distribution graphs. The null hypothesis is rejected if
D > D(α), where D(α) is the critical value obtained by calculating the inverse of the
Kolmogorov distribution at the significance level α = 0.05.
2.3
Average precipitation
As Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al. (2006) and Gregersen et al. (2014a) show a correlation between
the average annual precipitation and the number of extreme events exceeding a threshold,
information on average precipitation could prove useful in identifying regional patterns in
extreme precipitation. Calculation of average annual precipitation is done by summation of
the daily precipitation within each calendar year, and subsequently averaging the resulting
yearly values.
2.4
Geostatistics and spatial analysis
Geostatistics is the part of statistics describing spatially distributed data by incorporating
the location, and is therefore useful in analysing regional variation. The geostatistical
analysis in this study focus on spatial dependence and follows the analysis outlined in
Isaaks and Srivastava (1989). One geostatistical tool which can be used to describe spatial
dependence of a dataset is the variogram.
An empirical variogram provides the basis
for fitting a theoretical variogram. The principles and use of empirical and theoretical
variograms are described below.

2.4.1
The empirical variogram
The empirical (also called experimental) variogram is based on data and their associated
locations. A variogram is used for measuring the dissimilarity between data points as
a function of distance. The ”first rule of geography” states that in general, two points

2.4.2
Modelling of the theoretical variogram
A theoretical variogram is the true variogram for a specific variable. Approximating the
theoretical variogram is done by fitting a model to an empirical variogram, enabling inter-
polation. The objective is to find the model that fits best to the empirical variogram. To-
wards infinity, theoretical variograms can show two different types of behaviour; seemingly
infinite (unbounded) or finite (bounded) spatial dependence in variance (Oliver & Web-

Nugget effect
Despite that in theory γ = 0 at h = 0, a discontinuity or vertical jump where γ > 0
at extremely small values of h can happen in practice. This phenomenon is called the
nugget effect. The variogram value at extremely small separation distances is called the
nugget. It is variability that cannot be explained by distance between the data points and
may be caused by for example estimation uncertainties and/or micro variability that is
not revealed/resolved at the particular scale (Isaaks & Srivastava, 1989; Nielsen, 2009).
A small nugget effect implies that most of the variability is resolved at the particular
scale, and might be explained by distance. A large nugget value means that the value
of data points near each other vary more than what can be explained by distance, and
that it is impossible to estimate spatial dependence between neighbouring points at the
particular scale. For observations/samples with irregularly scattered data, the minimum
distance between data points is essential for the nugget value, and for data in a grid it is
the scale/grid spacing that is important.
Sill and partial sill
The γ value will generally increase when the separation distance increases, as the values of
points further apart generally differs more. Eventually, the variogram will reach a plateau,
where an increase of h no longer leads to a corresponding increase of γ. The γ value of this
plateau is called the sill and it is the maximum observed variability in the data (within the
observed distance). In theory, the sill corresponds to the variance of the data as normally
estimated in statistics. The difference between the sill and the nugget value is the amount
of observed variation that can be explained by distance between data points and is called
the partial sill (sillP). A small nugget value and a large sill (and thereby a large partial
Geographical variation in climate model simulations of changes in extreme precipitation in Denmark
11
==PAGE:22==
sill) implies high spatial dependence. This is optimal for inference of values in unsampled
locations based on distance from points with known values.
Range
The separation distance where the variogram reaches the sill is called the range. It rep-
resents the distance where two points are unrelated/independent. A small range means
that the data is only spatially auto-correlated within a short distance, where a larger
range means that auto-correlation is also detected in points further apart. Analysing re-
gional differences, it is expected to see bounded behaviour and thereby a range within the
maximum distance of point pairs.
2.4.3
Evaluating modelled variogram fit
Normally, a variogram is modelled by choosing the individual model that seems to describe
the empirical variogram best (Isaaks & Srivastava, 1989). That guarantees a model that
is tailored to the specific empirical variogram, and further evaluation of the fit is rarely
necessary. However, when processing a high number of variograms, that becomes very
time consuming. A method allowing simultaneous screening of many variograms is there-
fore necessary. The modelled variogram fit is assessed to be insufficient if it has a very
low coefficient of determination (R2) in combination with one or several of the following
characteristics:
• A very high range
• A very low range (below the smallest distance between data points)
• A very high nugget value relative to the maximum variance
• A negative sillP (where the nugget value is higher than the sill)
• Bad visual fit

4.2
PDS censoring in CGD
Two different methods of selecting the extreme values in a precipitation series are evaluated
in this study, as presented in section 2.2.1. PDS censoring type I is used in the national
guidelines for estimating and applying CFs for extreme precipitation (Gregersen et al.,
2014a) and PDS censoring type II is applied to the RCM data by Schmith et al. (2020).
To compare these methods and their respective results, both methods are recreated with
the CGD data. Implementation of the two PDS censoring methods is described below.
For both censoring type I and II, independence of the extreme events included in the PDS
is ensured by requiring a minimum temporal separation between events. An independence
criteria of a 24 hour separation is chosen for the CGD data with a duration of 24 hours
in accordance with Berg et al. (2019). This independence criteria differs from the one
used by the Danish Water Pollution Committee (danish: IDA Spildevandskomiteen) in
the before mentioned national guidelines (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2006; Gregersen et al.,
2014a; Mikkelsen et al., 1999), where two events are considered independent if the time
period between them is above 24 hours (for a temporal resolution/duration of 24 hours).
Type I censoring
The threshold z0 is set to a constant that yields an overall average yearly number of
extreme values, µ, of 3 in accordance with Berg et al. (2019). With 22 years of data,
the regional average number of extremes should be 3 · 22 = 66. After a few trials, the
z0-value resulting in λ ≈3 and n ≈66 is found to be z0 ≥19.8 mm. Type I censoring is
also applied to the CGD data in Gregersen et al. (2014a). In regional studies using type
I censoring, the analysis can concentrate on the variability of µ and λ (Mikkelsen et al.,
1995).
Type II censoring
The average yearly number of extremes µ in each cell is set to 3 in accordance with Berg
et al. (2019). With 22 years of data, a total of 66 events are selected in each cell. This
censoring method is applied on the RCM data by Schmith et al. (2020). It allows a regional
analysis to concentrate on the variability of µ and z0.

5 Climate model results
In the following sections, results from EVA of the climate models using PDS censoring
type II is presented. The GPD parameters including the exceedance moments for both 24-
and 1-hour duration and the present and future period are presented, to assess regional
patterns in individual models. Extreme precipitation events with return periods of 0.5,
1 and 10 years are calculated from the GPD parameters, and model similarities and
differences in scales and patterns are assessed.
Climate factors between the historical
and future period is calculated for both parameters and extreme events and presented
below to have a measure for comparison of the periods. For maps with 24-hour duration
for the present period, corresponding results for CGD are included for comparison with
observations. Variograms are used to assess both omnidirectional and directional variance,
and determine the directions with the least and the most similarity. Finally, common
regional and directional trends for all models for present and future periods as well as CFs
are identified by calculating average variograms for both 24- and 1-hour duration.

5.3
Climate factors
It is of interest to assess the change in parameters and extreme events between the present
and the future. CFs are calculated as described in equation 2.1 for all cells in all models
for z0, α0 and α1 and for extreme events with T = 0.5, 1 and 10 years. CF figures not
presented in this section are found in appendix D. All maps visualising CFs displays values
below 1 (indicating a future decrease) in blue, values very close to 1 in light blue/turquoise
and the values above 1 (indicating a future increase) are expressed by the rest of the colour
scale.
5.3.1
Climate factors for parameters
All parameters show a general increase in future climate, as the majority of cells in all
models have CFs above 1. The future increase is most apparent in z0 (see figures D.1 and
D.2 in appendix D), where very few models project decreases in few cells. This means
that the minimum extreme events in the present climate will not be regarded as extreme
in the future, as the threshold defining extremes will shift. α0, describing the average
extremes (see figures D.3 and D.4), shows more cells with a projected decrease, but still
most cells have values above 1 projecting an increase. That indicates a general increase
in the intensity of events regarded extreme in the future. CFs for α1 (see figures D.3 and
D.4) also displays most CFs above 1, indicating that the variance of extreme events will
increase in the future, and their intensities will therefore vary more. The presented CFs and
assessments hereof are in accordance with results from IPCC (2014). No obvious regional
pattern emerges between the RCMs, but RCMs show identifiable individual patterns in
both z0, α0 and α1. These patterns are most distinct for 1-hour duration, which also holds
the largest spread of CFs for all three parameters.
5.3.2
Climate factors for extreme events
CFs for extreme events with return periods of 0.5, 1 and 10 years all show an overall
increase in a future climate with values predominantly above 1. Table 5.1 shows that CFs
for z10 with 1-hour duration is on average higher than with 24-hour duration. The same
observation applies for all the remaining CFs both for parameters and specific extreme
events. In figures 5.11 and 5.12 and table 5.1 it is observed that CFs for 1-hour duration
generally vary more than for 24-hour duration. This observation also applies for T = 0.5
and 1 years (see figures 5.9, 5.10 and D.9 and D.10 in appendix D). This is an indication
of higher uncertainty due to big variation in events of short duration and high intensity.
The before mentioned outliers partly above water in RCM 3 do not stand out in the CFs.
This indicates that the parameters and extreme events calculated for these areas deviates
for both the present and future climate, yielding CFs on the same scale as the remaining
cells. Nevertheless, CFs for these cells could be misrepresentative. In all analysed return
periods, GCM F seems to yield the highest CF for the respective RCM combinations.
RCM 3 stand out with the highest CF results for all RCMs. In combination, model 3F
yields the highest CFs, projecting the biggest change in extreme precipitation between the
present and future climate. The same is true for most parameters, suggesting that this
model projects the overall biggest increase of extreme events in the future.
In conclusion, it is not possible to detect a regional pattern in CFs by looking at individual
model maps. The individual models show different tendencies, but the majority project an
overall future increase in extreme precipitation. 1-hour duration on average shows higher
CFs than 24-hour duration, indicating that recommendation of different CFs for different
durations might be plausible, depending on the application.

5.4.2
Variogram model fit
Resulting modelled variograms are evaluated by their R2-value in combination with nugget,
sill, range and visual fit. The modelled theoretical variograms where the fitting algorithm
failed are excluded from the further analysis in this section. Additionally, some modelled
theoretical variograms have a very bad fit to the empirical variograms, for the same reason
as mentioned in section 4.2.4 with wave-like shaped empirical variograms. The empirical
variogram points with lowest distance has a high influence on the fit of the theoretical
variogram partly due to the assumption that these are the overall lowest values. When
that is not the case (mostly in directional variograms with less number of point-pairs), the
spherical model does not apply. This could result in the range and sill being misleading,
why these should be excluded.
However, the nugget of these variograms will still be
included in the further analysis, as the bad fit only affects the nugget value minimally.

5.5
Average spatial variation in climate models
The RCM data is located on different grids, and it is therefore not possible to compute
an overall average of all 19 models without interpolation. Instead, to assess whether the
models show a common regional variation, variograms are used as a tool to bypass the
specific locations of data and focus on distances. Creating an average model variogram
is possible by averaging the empirical variogram for all models. The average is calcu-
lated for each of the 14 distance bins for both omnidirectional and directional variograms
separately. A spherical model is fitted to the resulting average variogram, resulting in
one modelled theoretical variogram for each variable, period, duration and direction. All
resulting variograms and tables are found in appendix G.
5.5.1
Comparison with CGD
The resulting average model variograms for 24-hour duration in a present climate is com-
pared with variograms for CGD (see figure 5.21). Nugget values increase for increasing
return period.
This suggests that the higher return period, the more small-scale dis-
continuity is observed, and the harder it gets to describe the variable with that spatial
resolution. Nugget effects are similar but generally slightly bigger for models than for
CGD, except for z10. This means that some models show more small-scale discontinuity,
than observed in CGD, but that they on average are very similar to CGD. It can therefore
be concluded that the spatial resolution is sufficient for modelling extreme events with 24-
hour duration in the present climate, but that both uncertainty, small-scale discontinuity
and general variance increase for high return periods.
The majority of the variograms in figure 5.21 show spatial dependence of variance to some
degree, as they have a positive slope. For all variables, the E-W variogram is among the
highest, and in most cases has the highest variance and thereby also the highest sill. In
some cases, NE-SW is also higher than the remaining directions. N-S generally shows the
lowest variance, but still shows auto-correlation. Not all variograms reach a sill within the
maximum distance analysed, and therefore appear unbounded. For CGD, variograms for
z0 and all extreme events are bounded within the maximum distance, suggesting that there
is no correlation beyond the range. Especially variograms for the model average in the
E-W direction appear unbounded with ranges above 250 km. This suggests that almost all
E-W points within Denmark are spatially auto-correlated, and that it might not feasible
to regionally distinguish CFs. The difference between CGD and model ranges suggests
that they cannot recreate the variance of the observed variables, and that the variables in
reality might not be spatially auto-correlated for as long a distance as modelled. However,
the average range observed in section 5.4.3 of around 200 km is supported by this method,
as it shows the same magnitude of the overall range. A range smaller than the maximum
point pair distance implies that two regions separated with more than the range could be
spatially independent of each other, and that they therefore might be statistically different.

6 Discussion
This section includes discussion of the chosen methods, uncertainties and how they affect
the results. Additionally, application of the results and further analysis is discussed.
6.1
Censoring of partial duration series
As the two analysed PDS censoring methods result in big regional differences in the average
extreme values, it is necessary to consider which variable the analysis focus on. By focusing
on the average value of exceedances that show similar results in regional variation, the
PDS censoring type becomes almost negligent, when looking at events with return periods
up to 10 years. Only PDS censoring type II is used when analysing climate model data.
The advantages and results of type I are therefore not explored in modelled data, and
it is unknown whether the small dependency of regional clustering detected in type I for
CGD would have an effect on the modelled data. However, based on the results for CGD,
it is unlikely to cause big differences for extreme events with small return periods. For
return periods higher than 10 years, it is recommended to further analyse the extent of
local dependency of clustering within Denmark.
6.2
Climate model uncertainty and differences
In climate modelling, a limiting factor is often availability of data and time consumption
due to heavy computational load. The same goes for this study, where initial processing
and EVA are skipped, as it is possible to retrieve EVA results through Schmith et al. (2020).
The choice of models, data periods, RCP scenarios and methods are therefore limited to
the ones used in Schmith et al. (2020).
The opportunity of saving time in the initial
analysis therefore constrains the possibilities of the further analysis. It might be possible
to download additional model simulations, which could expand the model ensemble and
decrease uncertainty of the results. It might also be possible to download model data from
a period completely overlapping the observational data period, minimising uncertainty
when comparing observations and models. As the spatial variation does not show large
changes between the present and future period with the most extreme RCP scenario, it
is not relevant to investigate other RCP scenarios in this regard. However, for assessing
different CFs and their regional variation, it is very important to include results from all
relevant RCP scenarios.
The observed variability between models illustrates the uncertainty related to internal
climate variability and model estimation (Benestad et al., 2017). By using an ensemble
of models, both types of uncertainty are minimised. It is therefore preferred to look at a
common signal for the whole ensemble when interpreting results. Normally, interpolation
of the different model grids would enable calculation of averages. However, to preserve
local trends in individual cells, it is desirable to avoid interpolation in this study. Therefore
average variograms are used instead, bypassing the different grids. Another way to avoid
interpolation is to calculate an average for models with common grids.
However, this
method yields multiple results for smaller sub-ensembles with higher uncertainty, and
a common trend might be hard to identify. Also, the most different models might be
located on different grids, and it is therefore hard to assess whether an observed trend is
Geographical variation in climate model simulations of changes in extreme precipitation in Denmark
45
==PAGE:56==
representative for all GCMs and RCMs. It is expected that including additional models
in the ensemble could make the results more similar to the observations.
Although the overall signals of the whole ensemble are of importance, it is also essential
to know how the individual models differ. Obvious discrepancies and outliers needs as-
sessment and could be excluded from the analysis. However, without knowledge of the
reason behind the odd values, exclusion risks creating an unwanted bias in the overall
results. The possible discrepancy observed in RCM 3, that might be due to differences in
the definition of data above land/sea, can be taken care of by examining the underlying
modelling efforts and updating the land/sea definition in this study accordingly. The out-
liers are detectable in variograms and possibly affect the results causing higher variance
and misrepresentation. However, it could also be caused by a different physical definition
of extreme precipitation above sea in this RCM. In that case, it is unfeasible to exclude
these results, which would contradict the principles of including all model results in the
ensemble. Whichever is the reason for discrepancies, the necessary investigation exceeds
the scope of this project, and it is decided not to act on the observation.
6.3
Data normalisation for spatial analysis
A central issue in the analysis of spatial variation is the discussion of how to best illustrate
regional variance. No previous studies using variograms for different extreme precipitation
variables to this extent are found, and it is therefore difficult to determine best practices.
Without normalisation, inter-model variation dominates the variograms and results in big
differences in scale, and it is therefore very difficult to identify and compare directional
differences. Multiple methods of normalisation of data was tested, before a final method
was chosen. Normalisation of data before computing variograms by division of individual
dataset averages is deemed the best way of illustrating regional variance and still preserving
knowledge about inter-model variation.
Another way of normalising variograms is to
assume, that the maximum point of the omnidirectional variance corresponds to 100%,
so the directional variance is expressed in percent of the omnidirectional variance. This
way omnidirectional inter-model variation is excluded, and the focus of the analysis is
only on directional differences.
However, it is of interest to preserve some knowledge
about inter-model variation in the variograms, and therefore this method is not applied.
Other normalisation or data transformation methods might be useful as well, but further
assessment hereof exceeds the scope of this project. As CFs by definition are factors with
similar scale, normalisation of these is deemed unnecessary, and only parameter or extreme
event values are normalised.
6.4
Variogram modelling
Variogram modelling is most useful when done individually for a single variables, where
it is possible to thoroughly examine possible outliers and the fit of the chosen model. Un-
fortunately, the abundance of data in this in this study makes thoroughness for individual
variogram models impossible. The unbounded spherical model is chosen to represent all
analysed extreme precipitation variables, as it is the most commonly used model (Isaaks
& Srivastava, 1989; Oliver & Webster, 2007). However, it remains unknown if this model
actually describes extreme precipitation well compared to other types of models, or if the
different variables can be described by the same model. In the analysis, it is established
that some variograms show unbounded behaviour within the maximum considered range,
suggesting that an unbounded model might describe the phenomenon better.
The algorithm for fitting a model to variograms chosen for this study is relatively simple,
and has a limitation of using one single variogram model for fitting. Most bad or failed
46
Geographical variation in climate model simulations of changes in extreme precipitation in Denmark
==PAGE:57==
fits are due to fluctuation in the empirical variograms with an interim sill or apparent
multiple sills. The spherical model is unable to describe this type of variogram shape,
indicating that places further apart are more or less spatially auto-correlated than places
with smaller distance separation with no correlation. An alternative type of variogram
function (for example a periodic function (Oliver & Webster, 2007)) might perform better
in explaining this type of variogram shapes. Allowing an individual function type for each
variogram might also improve the resulting modelled theoretical variograms. However,
the variogram shapes differ a lot between models, and it is not feasible to assume complex
theoretical models.
Excluding badly fitting variograms increases uncertainty of the results, as the ensemble
size therefore differs for each direction, variable, duration and period. Certain models
might be over-represented creating biased results. This encourages averaging of empirical
variograms before fitting a model or other methods of analysing the overall signal of the
model ensemble.
The model variogram fit could possibly be improved by decreasing the maximum distance
considered as well. This would exclude some variance fluctuation in many variograms, and
thereby result in empirical variograms with only a single sill. However, it creates a risk
of excluding the range, and thereby considering the curvature as unbounded despite its
possibly bounded nature. Another approach to improve the modelled variograms could be
to divide Denmark in sub-regions, and thereby also decrease the maximum distance. With
a separate analysis for each region, empirical variograms might show clearer and possibly
more distinct shapes. Additionally, it could elaborate sub-regional variation tendencies
and provide insight on the suggested regional differences. However, one should be aware
of the number of point pairs arising from each sub-region, as too few pairs could cause the
shape of the empirical variogram to be erratic. The clustering of data points arising from
the layout of the coast of Denmark determines the number of points in each distance bin.
Although the variogram fitting algorithm accounts for the number of points in each bin,
it is likely to cause higher uncertainty for specific distances. A suggestion of sub-regions
could be Jylland, Fyn and Sjælland, as the E-W variance generally is highest and separate
variograms for these regions might show the biggest differences.
6.5
Spatial resolution
Data availability dictates the spatial resolution of the datasets used in this study, and
the highest resolution available is utilised. The nugget value contributes to the evaluation
of small-scale continuity and spatial variability, and thereby the assessment of how well
the spatial resolution represents each variable. Thus, higher nugget effect for extreme
precipitation with high return period suggests that an increased spatial resolution would
resolve the phenomenon better. This observation is in compliance with the understanding
and definition of very extreme events, that shows much more locally determined behaviour.
However, the amount of increased computational force and technical advance does not yet
support higher spatial resolution. Modelling extreme precipitation events with climate
models is therefore restricted, but still possible with an acceptable outcome for return
periods up to at least 10 years.
6.6
Regional variation and climate factors
The regional variation pattern identified in annual average precipitation and in both ob-
served and modelled z0 suggests that the directions with highest variation is E-W or
NE-SW. Examining the maps, a better guess of a direction with the ultimately highest
variance could be ENE-WSW. This particular direction is placed at the bin edge between
Geographical variation in climate model simulations of changes in extreme precipitation in Denmark
47
==PAGE:58==
E-W and NE-SW. Rotating the bins to center ENE-WSW would possibly result in higher
directional variance in that bin, and thereby allow the variograms to show additional nu-
ances of the regional pattern. The direction with biggest variation might therefore be
specified to be ENE-WSW by further investigation.
The intensity of extreme precipitation in Denmark is found to be likely to increase in the
future, in accordance with IPCC, 2014. This strengthens the reliability of the remaining
model results in this study. Future regional differences are found to be similar to the
presently observed regional differences with high variation in especially the E-W direction.
Spatial dependency is established for CFs, but overall directional differences are difficult
to identify. A range around 100 km strongly suggests that CFs separated by this distance
or more are independent, and CFs therefore should be regionally distinguished. However,
the implication of E-W as the best direction for separating the country in sub-regions is
unclear, as it shows higher ranges for some extreme precipitation variables. Before finally
recommending different CFs for different areas of the country, it is therefore necessary
to evaluate the best separation into sub-regions showing statistically different CFs and
perform statistical analysis of these areas specifically.
Recommended CFs are of high
significance in local urban drainage and affect the resilience of cities against flooding far
into the future.
6.7
Kriging
For further analysis, the variograms could be used for creating interpolated maps of CFs
by using kriging. The variograms are used as weighting in the interpolation between neigh-
bouring cells. Kriging presupposes geospatial correlation, and is therefore only plausible
for variables showing spatial auto-correlation. The detected differences in the directional
variograms suggests that CFs shows anisotropy, and kriging assuming isotropy as opposed
to anisotropy might therefore give different results.

7 Conclusion
This thesis identifies geographical variation in climatic changes in extreme precipitation
in Denmark. Two partial duration series censoring methods in extreme value analysis are
compared to assess their effect on the resulting extreme precipitation variables. Extreme
precipitation variables resulting from extreme event analysis of an ensemble of climate
models are analysed. Regional variation patterns are identified in both observed data and
a modelled present and future period for durations of 24 and 1 hour. Individual model
differences and collective trends in geospatial variation are analysed using variograms.
Local CFs are calculated for modelled data, and their regional variation is analysed for
both durations and multiple describing variables.
The analysis leads to the following main conclusions:
• The choice of PDS censoring type in EVA influences the regional pattern in average
extreme events greatly, due to differences in the local definition of what is considered
extreme. It affects the resulting extreme events locally, but not their overall regional
patterns for events with return periods of up to 10 years. PDS censoring type II is
recommended for regional studies.
• The regional variation pattern in average annual precipitation is recognised in the
variables describing observed extreme precipitation events. The observed extreme
events are most dissimilar in the E-W and partly the NE-SW direction, and most
similar in the N-S direction.
• The regional variation pattern in average annual precipitation is recognised in the
minimum extreme events for individual models, but is less apparent for the rest of
the variables describing extreme precipitation. Models with common RCMs describe
extreme precipitation more similarly than models with common GCMs.
• The spherical variogram model is too simple to describe the modelled variation of
extreme precipitation in individual climate models. However, it sufficiently describes
the model ensemble average for nearly all examined variables with both 24-hour
and 1-hour duration. With an ensemble including 19 climate models it is possible
to identify a common regional variation pattern similar to the observed. Extreme
precipitation is spatially auto-correlated in a decreasing range for increasing return
period. Variation is biggest in the E-W direction, strengthening the hypothesis of
significant differences in extreme precipitation in E and W. Modelled 1-hour extreme
precipitation shows more small-scale discontinuity and smaller ranges than 24-hour
extreme precipitation.
• CFs show that the intensity of extreme precipitation in Denmark will increase in the
future. Future regional differences will be similar to the presently observed regional
differences with high variation in especially the E-W direction.
CFs for extreme
precipitation are spatially dependant with a larger range for 24-hour duration than
1-hour duration. CFs are most similar in the N-S direction and varies the most in the
E-W direction, although directional differences are small. Recommending regionally
distinguished CFs requires additional statistical analysis for separate sub-regions.

B Parameter results
This appendix includes results of GPD parameters z0, σ and κ as well as exceedance
moments α0 and α1 for all models, periods and durations.
Exceedance moments are,
in this project, also considered parameters. Maps with parameter results are presented
followed by tables with statistical measures of each parameter. The parameters for all
19 models are plotted on separate maps. For plots with maps of parameters representing
the historical period for daily precipitation, a map with the GPD parameters for CGD is
included. Note that colour scales are common for all maps within each figure. The colour
scale limits are adjusted to highlight potential regional variation patterns, and does not
necessarily represent the minimum and maximum values of the datasets. To summarise
the results, minimum, maximum, average and variance values are calculated for each map.
Additionally the overall averages of the mentioned statistics for the modelled parameter
are calculated. CGD statistics are included in tables with 24-hour duration in a present
climate.
