Abstract
Cancer is currently the second leading cause of death globally with the incidence expected to increase.
The current method for treatment response monitoring is assessment of tumor size along with 18F-FDG
PET. As a metabolic response to treatment is seen much earlier than structural changes, molecular
imaging of cancer metabolism in vivo can provide important and critical information.
This thesis focuses on metabolic imaging using hyperpolarized 13C MRS measured in combination
with 18F-FDG PET (only brieﬂy considered) in a concept called hyperPET. The hyperpolarized 13C
MRS data was measured using two diﬀerent sequences, resulting in two diﬀerent data types. The aim
of this study is to perform noise reduction and evaluate its eﬀects on further analyses of the data.
In this thesis 32 data sets from dogs with spontaneous biopsy veriﬁed cancers were examined. These
were measured using an FID sequence resulting in a 2D data matrix. The noise levels were successfully
reduced using rank reduction by SVD, enhancing the underlying structure of the data.
The data
was reduced to ranks in the range 2 to 10, by evaluating the information in the matrices from the
decomposition. This resulted in SNR gains in the range 1.5 to 7. The reduced noise levels gave rise to
a gain of temporal information of the metabolites in addition to revealing metabolites previously buried
in noise. While the reduced noise levels improved model ﬁts of the data, the overall model parameters
showed little to no eﬀect of the noise reduction. Analyses involving alanine and bicarbonate were found
troubled by overﬁtting to the large pyruvate tails, why further analysis is needed to circumvent this
and get more precise reaction constant estimates.
Another 8 data sets from mice with ﬂank xenografts measured using an EPSI sequence were also
examined.
The EPSI sequence included 2 spatial dimensions resulting in a 4D data tensor.
The
noise levels were successfully reduced by truncation using TD, revealing underlying metabolites, most
commonly lactate. The data was truncated to rank [7, 7, 4, 4] resulting in SNR gains in the range 5
to 60. Artifacts were introduced by the truncation, but if these are disregarded the noise reduction is
likely constant in all data sets. Reaction constants (kPL) were estimated by the AUC ratio method
resulting in signiﬁcant improvements of the estimates, going from 2 to 17 reasonable estimates after
truncation. The correlation of the reaction constant with ex vivo normalized LDH and mean SUV
from 18F-FDG PET was analyzed showing no correlation before or after truncation, although slight
groupings were seen between the carcinomas and sarcomas.
The methods in this thesis successfully reduced the noise levels of the data, with the TD introducing
artifacts in some voxels after truncation. The use of this method in the future is therefore dependent
on the use.
Rank reduction by SVD can seamlessly be integrated in the everyday work ﬂow for
improvement of the data with no noticeable inﬂuence. TD is a more time consuming process and more
prone to complications, why the future use of this method is not as likely.

Introduction
3.1
Background
Currently, cancer is the second leading cause of death globally and is estimated to be responsible for 9.6
million deaths in 2018 [1]. In Denmark, it is estimated that about one third of the Danish population
will get cancer [2], and currently 37,200 people are diagnosed with cancer each year, with that number
expected going up in the years to come [3].
Among the current imaging methods for diagnosis of cancers is Positron Emission Tomography (PET),
along with structural imaging using Computed Tomography (CT) and Magnetic Resonance Imaging
(MRI). Treatment response is currently being monitored by assessing the tumor size along with 18F-
FDG PET. This reduction in tumor size in response to treatment may take weeks to develop, the
tumor may not decrease in size at all, or the tumor may even grow in size, all the while there’s still a
response to treatment [4]. In the two latter cases, assessment of tumor size is ineﬀective. In addition,
one issue with 18F-FDG PET is that it lacks sensitivity to certain tumor types and lacks speciﬁcity.
This means that 18F-FDG PET is unable to distinguish between high glucose uptake due to cancer,
inﬂammation or macrophage activity. It also has diﬃculty detecting tumors in areas with low glucose
uptake (e.g. the prostate) or areas with high background glucose uptake (e.g. the brain). Additionally,
PET exposes the patient to a radiation dose. Metabolic changes due to treatment can be seen much
earlier than structural changes, and a technique for medical imaging of metabolism is therefore desired
[4].
A technique with the ability to detect metabolites is Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR). The principle
of NMR relies on the spin properties of certain nuclei, which can be manipulated to obtain images
or spectra. MRI is built on this principle and is primarily used to obtain detailed 3D anatomical
images of the body, but can also be used to acquire functional images, such as diﬀusion weighted MRI
(DW-MRI).
MRI is widely used in the clinic, but the principle of NMR can also be used for Magnetic Resonance
Spectroscopy (MRS), where it can be used to investigate tissue chemistry in vivo [4]. Diﬀerent atoms
oscillate at a slightly diﬀerent frequencies due to the diﬀerences in the chemical environments the
atoms are in. Occurrence of diﬀerent compounds are then detectable and separable using Magnetic
Resonance Spectroscopy. Using this technique, the presence of certain molecules of interest can be
investigated.
Necessary for MRS of speciﬁc nuclides of interest is that they are abundant enough to be detected.
Only 1H are naturally abundant enough to be used in routine clinical evaluations, thus limiting the
technique to certain nuclides.
Additionally, the technique suﬀers from poor temporal and spatial
resolution, and due to the poor temporal resolution, the current use of MRS lack dynamic information
about the ﬂuxes of metabolites and instead show a steady state [4].
A solution to this problem is hyperpolarization. There are several hyperpolarization techniques, but
in this thesis focus will be on hyperpolarization using Dynamic Nuclear Polarization (DNP). DNP is
a technique to temporarily increase the polarization and thus get a signiﬁcant signal increase. DNP
has been shown to get a polarization increase above 10,000 times, shown by Ardenkjær et al [5].
This increase in signal strength enables imaging and spectra generation of previously diﬃcult nuclei,
most notably 13C troubled by a low abundance and gyromagnetic ratio. Carbon is one of the core
atoms in organic chemistry, and most compounds found in living creatures are therefore composed of
carbon to some extent. Therefore visualizing carbon has great inﬂuence on the potential use of MRS.
The increase in signal strength has also signiﬁcantly improved both spatial and temporal resolution,
with the temporal resolution now good enough to enable dynamic measurements of metabolites and
metabolite conversions [4].

Many nuclei have been hyperpolarized using DNP, and currently the most widely used substrate
is [1-13C]-pyruvate. Injection of intravenous hyperpolarized pyruvate results in increases in lactate,
alanine and bicarbonate. Studies have shown that lactate is elevated in tumor cells, an eﬀect known
as the Warburg eﬀect [4]. A method to visualize the Warburg eﬀect and investigate the presence of
these metabolites in vivo is therefore of interest.
While PET imaging has been shown to have some diﬃculty detecting certain types of cancers, most
notably brain tumors and prostate cancer, MRSI using a hyperpolarized [1-13C]-pyruvate substrate has
shown to identify these cancers. Thus, in these cases MRS potentially provides a routine diagnostic tool.
In addition, hyperpolarization also enables MRS to be used in detection of early treatment response,
and MRS then has a potential to provide a routine assessment of cancer therapy. The technique is still
fairly new and more work has to be done in this area of research, but proof of concept for tracking
early treatment response has been demonstrated in numerous studies [4].
At Rigshospitalet, Copenhagen forces have been combined in a concept called hyperPET, which is the
combined use of 18F-FDG with [1-13C]-pyruvate MRS. This combined imaging aims at providing better
comparison studies of hyperpolarized MRS against the current metabolic imaging standard and better
tumor phenotyping by combining the information from the two imaging modalities [6].
While hyperpolarization has increased the signal immensely, detection of metabolites in 13C MRS is still
limited by the noise of the measurements. Additionally, hyperpolarization is an artiﬁcial polarization
increase, so immediately after polarization is stopped, the polarization quickly returns to equilibrium.
The signal increase is then lost within a time scale of a few minutes. As the signal is decreased and
descends into the noise, the noise is also a limiting factor in regards to the amount of time the signal can
be detected. Reduction of noise in measurements could then possibly enable detection of compounds
with low signal and previously buried in noise, along with an increased amount of time for detection
of the metabolite signal.
A method suggested by Brender et al.
for reduction of noise in measurements is rank reduction
using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). A signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of nearly an order of
magnitude was achieved, resulting in a SNR for each scan higher than averaging over 450 scans. For 2
dimensional data obtained by FID sequences (non-imaging) decomposition is performed using SVD. For
4 dimensional data that includes additional spatial components (imaging) tensor rank decomposition
is used by Tucker Decomposition (TD) [7].
The reaction constant describing the rate of conversion from pyruvate to lactate has been proposed
by many as a possible biomarker for detection of cancer using 13C-MRS, with the estimation of this
currently requiring kinetic modelling of the data. Brender et al. demonstrated that the increased SNR

Theory
The following sections introduce cell metabolism and the changes in metabolism related to cancer. In
this report, the cellular reactions related to metabolism of pyruvate are investigated using MR and the
basic concepts of MR are therefore introduced along with hyperpolarization and theoretical concepts
used for data processing. Diagonalization of matrices is then described leading to the SVD theorem,
which is illustrated using an example data set. This is followed by a description of TD of data tensors,

4.1
Cell metabolism
The metabolism in cells consists of many pathways making up a unique network, where pathways are
a linked series of reactions taking place inside the cell. The reactions consist of diﬀerent metabolites in
the form of reactants, intermediates and products. Maintenance of the metabolic pathways is essential
to cell survival and cell homeostasis, and fundamental changes in the metabolism are often related to
disease [9].
Glucose metabolism in healthy cells is divided into two categories: anaerobic and aerobic respiration.
Anaerobic respiration is consumption of glucose without the use of oxygen, and is the metabolism of
glucose by glycolysis followed by lactate fermentation. This occurs when there is an insuﬃcient oxygen
supply. Aerobic respiration is consumption of glucose with use of oxygen, and is the metabolism of
glucose by glycolysis, the Citric Acid Cycle (CAC) and oxidative phosphorylation, also known as the
electron transport chain.
In glycolysis, metabolism of one glucose molecule results in 2 pyruvate molecules, and a net gain of
2 ATP and 2 NADH. Glycolysis produces ATP without the involvement of oxygen, where ATP is
one of the primary energy carriers in cells, and NADH serves as an electron carrier used in oxidative
phosphorylation [10]. In anaerobic respiration, the produced pyruvate is converted to lactate by lactate
fermentation and the lactate is then exported to the extracellular environment [10].
Aerobic glycolysis is the breakdown of glucose in the presence of oxygen to produce water, CO2
and ATP. After glycolysis, pyruvate is transported into the mitochondrial matrix.
Here, pyruvate
is converted into acetyl-CoA with 1 NADH released, and acetyl-CoA then enters the CAC cycle. The
CAC cycle is comprised of a number of reactions resulting in a gain of 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2 and
4 CO2. The electron carriers NADH and FADH2 are then used in the electron transport chain, where
the carried electrons combine with oxygen and protons to form water. During these reactions energy
is released, which is used to produce ATP.
By aerobic respiration, about 36 ATP molecules are produced per glucose molecule, whereas for
anaerobic respiration, just 2 ATP molecules are produced [10].
The most widely known hallmark of cancer metabolism is the increased consumption of glucose
compared to normal non-proliferating cells, where glucose serves as one of the main nutritional supplies
needed for cell proliferation. Studies by German physiologist Otto Warburg performed more than 90
years ago found that while cancer cells consumed massive amounts of glucose, which was converted to
pyruvate through glycolysis, the cancer cells did not take advantage of the high energy beneﬁts of the
CAC cycle. The cells instead converted pyruvate to large amounts of lactate, which was then exported
to the extracellular environment. This occurred in spite of the cells being exposed to suﬃcient amounts
of oxygen, which is necessary for exploiting the CAC cycle [9]. This change in metabolism is known as
aerobic glycolysis, as the cancer cells metabolize glucose to pyruvate (and lactate) even in the presence
of oxygen. This phenomenon is known as the Warburg eﬀect and is illustrated in Figure 3.

Magnetic Resonance
The following sub-sections introduce basic concepts of Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy, followed by
a brief description of dissolution Dynamic Nuclear Polarization. Finally, some theoretical concepts
used for processing of MR data are described.
Magnetic Resonance (MR) is commonly used in the clinic for structural imaging (MRI), where it
is used to examine structural changes needed for diagnosis or monitoring of a wide range of illnesses.
MRI is built on NMR, a spectroscopy technique that has long been used in organic chemistry for
investigation of organic compounds. MR spectroscopy in the clinic is not currently being used much,
but the use of spectroscopy in the clinic is expanding, called called MRS or MRSI.
As some of the following sections contain mathematical operations of data of diﬀerent types, to aid
understanding euler script will be used for denoting tensors (ex: A), bold face capital letters will denote
matrices (ex: A), bold face lower case letters will denote vectors (ex: a) and lower case and upper case
letters will denote scalars (ex: a or A).
4.2.1
Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) or Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS) rely on the property
nuclear spin angular momentum. While the name suggests that it comes from rotational motion of the
nucleus, this is actually not the case. It is simply an intrinsic property of the nucleus. Nuclear spin
angular momentum is denoted with quantum number I and can take both integer (0, 1, 2, ...) and
half integer (1/2, 3/2, ...) values [11]. In this project, only 13
6 C which has I = 1/2 will be considered.
Page 11 of 86
==PAGE:13==
Master thesis
s144320 / hnv579
Spin 1/2 nuclei split into two sub-levels, which are degenerate in the absence of a magnetic ﬁeld. When
immersed in a magnetic ﬁeld, the degeneracy is broken and the two levels separate in energy. This
splitting eﬀect is called the nuclear Zeeman splitting. The two sub-levels are m = +1/2 denoted the α
state or ’spin-up’ and m = −1/2 denoted the β state or ’spin-down’. The angular momentum of nuclei
is associated with a nuclear spin magnetic moment, meaning the nucleus generates a small magnetic
ﬁeld. When the nucleus is immersed in a magnetic ﬁeld, an interaction occurs between the nuclear
magnetic moment and the applied ﬁeld, with the energy of the interaction dependent on the angle
between the magnetic moment and the applied ﬁeld. The low energy arrangement is alignment of the
moment parallel to the direction of the magnetic ﬁeld and the high energy arrangement is alignment
of the moment opposite the direction of the magnetic ﬁeld [11].
In the absence of a magnetic ﬁeld, nuclei have a random orientation due to random thermal motion and
the net magnetization is therefore zero. When immersed in a magnetic ﬁeld, the interaction between
the magnetic ﬁeld and the nuclei generate a small net magnetization parallel to the ﬁeld, as this is
the low energy arrangement. This small net magnetization is called the equilibrium magnetization [11].
When nuclei are immersed in a magnetic ﬁeld, the magnetic moments begin to ’wobble’ around the
magnetic ﬁeld in a cone shape motion. This motion is called precession and the frequency of this motion
is called the larmor frequency. The larmor frequency is unique for each atomic nucleus and estimated
as ω0 = −γ B0, where ω0 is the angular frequency, γ the gyromagnetic ratio and B0 the magnetic
ﬁeld strength. The gyromagnetic ratio for 1H is γ1H = 267.5 106 rad−1 T−1 and the gyromagnetic
ratio for 13C is γ13C = 67.3 106 rad−1 T−1 [11]. Magnetic resonance measurements are performed by
applying radio frequency (RF) pulses at the larmor frequency of the nucleus of interest thereby ﬂipping
the direction of the magnetization 90◦and measuring the net magnetization. The nuclei then return
back to the equilibrium orientation with a relaxation time constant denoted T1 while emitting radio
waves. The measured signal is proportional to the polarization of the sample, which is proportional
to the diﬀerence between the amount of particles in the α state and the β state. At room and body
temperature this in the order of a few ppm.
Electrons are also magnetic and thus also generate a small magnetic ﬁeld that aﬀects the magnetic
ﬁeld felt by nearby nuclei. This change in magnetic ﬁeld strength means that the larmor frequency of
nearby nuclei is changed slightly. The exact frequency of each non-magnetically equivalent nucleus is
therefore dependent on the chemical environment of the nucleus. In NMR and MRS it is these small
diﬀerences in frequency that is visualized and used for identiﬁcation of metabolites. This is done by
fourier transforming the data thereby changing the axis from a time axis to a frequency axis.
As the exact frequencies of the nuclei are dependent on the magnetic ﬁeld strength, it is favorable
to convert the frequency axis to a relative frequency, since the relative frequency diﬀerences between
compounds are unchanged. This change of axis is called chemical shift (δ) and is measured in ppm.
This can be seen in equation (1), where Tetramethylsilane (TMS) is chosen as reference compound.
δ = ωsample −ωTMS

The sequences used to measure the data analyzed in this project are an FID sequence and an EPSI
sequence. The FID sequence does not use any spatial encoding and thus only show the spectral domain
and the temporal domain since multiple RF pulses are applied, with the time between pulses describing

diﬀerent volumes resulting in 4 dimensional data. The data is then constructed as an FID data set for
each spatial location.

4.2.2
dissolution Dynamic Nuclear Polarization
In this section, dissolution Dynamic Nuclear Polarization (dDNP) will be explained brieﬂy. A brief
description is chosen as dDNP is not the main focus of this thesis.
MRS suﬀers from low sensitivity, and while carbon is a nucleus of great interest in organic chemistry,
the sensitivity of 13C is very poor due to the low gyromagnetic ratio (1/4 relative to 1H) and very low
natural abundance of 13C (1.1% of natural carbon). The low sensitivity can be overcome by the use
of DNP, also called hyperpolarization. DNP is a technique to temporarily increase the polarization at
low temperatures (∼1 K, where the electron polarization approaches 100%). This is accomplished by
the use of microwave irradiation, which enables the transfer of polarization from electrons to nuclei.
In 2003 Ardenkjær et al demonstrated dissolution of the substrate using boiling water to bring the
substrate to the liquid state while preserving the enhanced polarization, denoted dissolution Dynamic
Nuclear Polarization (dDNP). This has enabled the use of hyperpolarization in in vitro and in vivo
applications. Using dDNP, an enormous signal enhancement was obtained resulting in an SNR gain of
a factor >10,000 [5]. The high increase in polarization is temporary and returns to equilibrium when
microwave irradiation is stopped. There are three mechanisms at which the signal of the substrate is
lost during measurement: i) T1-relaxation ii) RF excitation iii) metabolite conversion. As the enhanced
polarization is artiﬁcial, the polarized sample returns to equilibrium polarization with relaxation time
constant T1, and all signal increase will be lost within a few minutes. In addition to T1-relaxation,
the signal is also lost when an RF pulse is applied as the pulse reduces the diﬀerence between the α
and β state and thus some of the enhanced polarization is lost with each pulse. The ﬁnal reason for
substrate signal is the substrate conversion to other metabolites. If the metabolites synthesized from
the substrate are produced in suﬃcient quantity, these will then be visible in the data. Examples of
typical T1s for hyperpolarized metabolites in vivo can be seen in Table 1.

4.4
Tucker Decomposition
In this section, decomposition of tensors, also called multidimensional arrays, are described. First,
some tensor-related deﬁnitions and notations are introduced, leading up to the Tucker Decomposition
(TD). Note here that tensors in physics, such as stress tensors, are not the same as tensors in this thesis.
Tensors are arrays and thus just a collection of numbers.
The dimensions are dependent on the
data and can by deﬁnition have any dimension (non-negative). A zeroth order tensor is a scalar, a ﬁrst
order tensor is a vector, a second order tensor is a matrix, and tensors of order 3 or higher are called
higher-order tensors [22]. Usually when using the term tensor, it is implied that the data is of order 3
or higher, as the terms scalar, vector and matrix are usually used for orders 0 to 2. In this chapter, all
illustrations of tensors will be of 3rd order tensors, as these are easier visualized.
From the tensors, subarrays can be formed by ﬁxing all indexes but one. In matrices this corresponds
to the rows and columns, but for tensors the higher order analog is denoted ﬁbers. A mode-1 ﬁber is
a column, mode-2 a row and mode-3 a tube ﬁber [22]. These are illustrated for a 3rd order tensor in
Figure 12.
(a) Mode-1 (column) ﬁbers.
(b) Mode-2 (row) ﬁbers.

Tucker Decomposition (TD) is the decomposition of nth order tensors, where, n denotes the number
of dimensions. It was ﬁrst introduced by Tucker in 1963. TD is multidimensional analog to SVD, why
it is also called Higher Order Singular Value Decomposition (HOSVD). TD decomposes a nth order
tensor into a core tensor of the same dimensions as the original data multiplied with n factor matrices.
The resulting components after TD of a 3rd order tensor are shown in Figure 13.

B spans the second dimension and C the third dimension and so forth.
In the case of the data used in this thesis, the data is the 2 dimensional FID data and the 4 dimensional
EPSI data. The FID data is decomposed using SVD, and the EPSI data is then decomposed using
TD. The ﬁrst and second dimensions of the EPSI data are the two spatial components (x-position

this report, only a brief explanation is given.
PET is a functional imaging modality using β+-radioactive decay for imaging and measure of biochemical
processes in vivo. β+ decay is the radioactive decay of the nucleus with emission of a positron and a
neutrino. When a positron interacts with an electron, the two particles annihilate and simultaneously
produce two 511 keV gamma photons travelling in opposite directions.
Human tissue has a high
electron density, why the interaction occurs quickly after ≈1-4 mm, depending on the positron energy.
In PET it is these two 511 keV gamma photons that are detected by coincidence using a detector
ring surrounding the patient. From the detected photons, it is known that the annihilation occurred
somewhere on the Line Of Response (LOR) between the detected photons. The detection process is
illustrated in Figure 20 [24].

In PET imaging, desired molecules are labelled with a β+-radioactive isotope for tracking of certain
processes. The most commonly used tracer is 18F-FDG, which is a glucose analog used to image glucose
uptake in vivo. As PET provides little structural information about the tissue, it is commonly used in
combination with another imaging modality (CT or MRI).
The malignancy of a tumor is related to the glucose uptake, why 18F-FDG PET is used for a measure
of this. As the activity is related to a number of parameters (injected dose, weight, scan time) a
Standard Uptake Value (SUV) is used as a universal indicator, normalized to these parameters [24].

are representative for all data sets used in this project, and thus the results of the initial analysis are

• Possible changes in peak width are examined
• The residuals are examined

5.1.5
Main analysis
5.1.5.1
Rank reduction
After the initial analysis, the data is ready for the main analysis. As the initial analysis has already
investigated if an increase in signal-to-noise ratio is obtainable, the optimum amount of singular values
is then investigated by evaluating of the information in U and VT . Since the spectra are complex,
the matrices U and V are also complex, while the diagonal matrix Σ remain real. If the last columns
of U and V purely model noise, these should be normal distributed. Therefore for determining the
optimum amount of singular values, the function adtest in Matlab is used, which tests if the input is
normal distributed. The investigation starts at rank r = 15 and going towards rank r = 1. If either the
real or imaginary part of column k in U or column k V is not normal distributed with great certainty
(p = 5 · 10−4 corresponding to the lowest p-value of this function), the test is stopped and the rank is
reduced to r = k. These noisy last columns can be seen in Figure 10 on page 22 for the example data
set in section 4.3.5. The test of optimum rank for rank reduction is performed on all 32 data sets.
5.1.5.2
Signal-to-noise ratio
After the optimum rank has been determined, the eﬀect of the rank reduction is then examined. This
is ﬁrst done by assessing the gain in SNR and possible patterns related to the SNR gain. This involves
examining if the gain is constant throughout the data sets, dependent on the rank or original SNR.
It is ﬁnally examined if the reduced noise improves visibility of the metabolites and possibly enable
visualization of metabolites not visible in the original data due to high noise levels. The improved
visibility is evaluated qualitatively.
5.1.5.3
Improved metabolite detection
It is investigated if the noise reduction enables detection of metabolites previously hidden by the high
noise levels. For investigation of metabolites, the chemical shift of the peaks are essential. Therefore
the chemical shifts of the 4 detected metabolites are examined, using the chemical shift of pyruvate
as reference. The chemical shifts of lactate, pyruvate hydrate, alanine and bicarbonate are examined.
The absolute positions of the peaks may vary throughout the data sets, but the relative positions to
each other should be constant. Therefore the chemical shift of pyruvate is set to 171.1 ppm, found in
a chemical shift chart [28]. Using the measured peak distances, the positions of the 4 metabolites are
estimated.
After the peak positions have been clariﬁed, it is investigated if the rank reduction enables detection
of metabolites previously not visible in the data. This is done by qualitatively examining the spectra
before and after rank reduction.
Not all 32 data sets analyzed in this thesis are deemed eligible for further analysis through GLM
ﬁt, AUC estimates, and kinetic modelling of the data. Of the 32 examined data sets, 16 were deemed
eligible for further analysis. Data sets were deemed not eligible if no lactate, alanine or bicarbonate
was seen, if there was peak splitting, or if the data set was abnormal. Data sets were abnormal, if the
data showed inexplicable and physically impossible behaviour. Thus the following sections only apply
for the 16 data sets eligible for further analysis.
5.1.5.4

For extraction of metabolite quantities, a complex GLM is ﬁtted to the data. The quantity of each
metabolite at time t is proportional to the area under the metabolite peak at that given time. The
GLM is thus used to extract the metabolite peaks from the data. As the GLM is done for 1D data, a
separate GLM is ﬁtted for each time point, but with equal peak width for the metabolites at all time

5.2.3
Initial analysis
5.2.3.1
Rank reduction and optimum rank
TD is not an exact decomposition of the data, as opposed to SVD. When decomposing data according
to the SVD, the decomposition only has to be calculated once. Experiments with diﬀerent ranks can
be done by extracting diﬀerent amounts of singular values, but the calculated matrices do not change.
This is not the case for TD, since this decomposition is an approximation, and thus to experiment
with diﬀerent ranks the decomposition has to be recalculated every time the rank is changed to ﬁnd
the best rank r approximation [23].
TD is calculated in Matlab using the function tucker from the n-way toolbox for Matlab. In the
function the desired ranks are speciﬁed as [r1, r2, r3, r4], denoting the rank in the ﬁrst, second, third
and fourth dimension respectively. The options [2,2,2,2] are also given as input to the function, resulting
in an unconstrained decomposition of the data.
To ﬁnd the optimum combination of ranks, TD of the data must be calculated for all combinations
tested. Since the EPSI data is 4 dimensional, there are 4 separate rank truncations to be tested.
To reduce the parameter space, the two spatial ranks are assumed to be equal and the spectral and
temporal rank are assumed to be equal, reducing the parameter space to 2 rank truncations to be
tested. To ﬁnd the optimum combination of the two ranks, ranks 3 to 12 are examined for the spatial-
spatial rank, and ranks 3 to 25 are examined for the spectral-temporal rank. For each combination,
the variance explained, mean error and maximum error is saved. The optimum ranks are the ranks
in the combination with the lowest variance explained (indicating a low rank), lowest mean error
(indicating high rank) and lowest maximum error (indicating high rank), all scaled to the interval
[0,1]. As there are two indications towards high rank and just one towards low rank, the variance
explained is multiplied with a weighting factor of 2. For this analysis, the largest error and mean error
is found in the voxel grid [7:13,3:14], as this bounding box contains the signals from the mice, with the
remaining being noise, urea or artifacts.

As the concept of rank and rank reduction for data tensors with TD is more complicated than
decomposition using SVD, the optimum rank used for reduction is not exactly quantiﬁable. As the
variance explained, mean error and maximum error is used for rank investigation, these are combined
to form a surface used for ﬁnding the optimum combination of ranks. Since a low variance explained,
low maximum error and low mean error is desired, a valley is sought in this surface. The optimum
combination of ranks is found by the best combination of the three parameters, keeping in mind a
lower rank is generally preferred for largest noise reduction.

Quality check
After the ranks to be used for further analysis are chosen, a quality check is performed to ensure an
adequate model of the data is found. As a each data set contains 16 × 16 = 256 voxels, a few selected
voxels or spectra for each data set are examined, and the tendencies and analyses performed on these
selected voxels are assumed to be representative of all 256 voxels. For all data sets, the voxel containing
the largest signal is examined to ensure proper model of the data. In addition, the spectrum where
the largest residual is found is also examined. If the errors in this spectrum are deemed acceptable,
proper reconstruction for the entire data set is assumed.
While loss of information is not desired, introduction of false peaks can be even more catastrophic.
Therefore the largest negative error is also examined. All voxels and spectra are examined qualitatively.

Attempt 2: As a result of the poor phasing between lactate and pyruvate, other methods are tried
to solve the issue. The ﬁrst n points in the FID are shifted to the end of the FID and then attenuated
to the previous n points. This is attempted using n = 1, . . . , 10, and does not improve the phase
correction, but instead results in other poor phase corrections: inversion of the lactate peak, inversion
of alanine and pyruvate hydrate and other mixtures of poor phasing. This can be seen in Figure 75 in
the appendix.
Attempt 3: Phase correction of pyruvate and lactate separately is attempted as a ﬁnal attempt. Phase
correcting the lactate peak separately revealed the large baseline sensitivity of the ACME algorithm,
as even small baseline oﬀsets could result in an inversion of the lactate peak, or any phasing in between.
Based on the investigations above, it is concluded that the spectra cannot not be phase corrected
using a linear phase correction. This is likely due to other underlying causes (such as inhomogeneities,
eddy currents or others), but will not be investigated further, as it was deemed outside the scope of
this study.

Discussion
7.1
Summary of main ﬁndings
Increasing the use of hyperpolarized 13C-pyruvate for cancer diagnostics and treatment monitoring
is an overarching goal in this ﬁeld of research, why methods to improve the information from this
modality are of interest. In this project rank reduction of hyperpolarized [1-13C]-pyruvate MRS FID
and EPSI data by SVD and TD was performed. The goal of this analysis was noise reduction, as the
reduced noise levels could possibly enable extraction of more and more precise information from the
data. An optimum rank was determined for each data set resulting in successful reduction of the noise
levels. The eﬀect of noise reduction on reaction constant estimates were investigated, where the broad
trend of the analysis showed little to no eﬀect of the rank reduction on the FID data. For the EPSI
data large improvements were found after truncation, going from random and inaccurate results to
reasonable estimates. Finally the eﬀect of noise reduction on the correlation between kPL estimates
and 18F-FDG PET data and ex vivo LDH measurements was investigated. While the truncation did
aﬀect the data, no correlation was found before or after truncation.
7.2
The results
7.2.1
Rank reduction and SNR gain
7.2.1.1
FID data
In this study it was demonstrated that a noise reduction and therefore SNR gain was obtainable
through rank reduction by SVD. The method successfully reduced the noise levels and enhanced and
revealed underlying structure, resulting in SNR gains in the range 1.5 to 7. The correlation of SNR
gain with the reduced rank generally showed the SNR gain was larger for the lower ranks. In the data
used in this project up to 6 peaks can be detected, but not all peaks were seen in all data sets. It was
expected that the reduced rank be related to the amount of peaks in the data, which was established in
the data where the majority of the ranks were set in the range 2 to 6 - the same range as the amount of
peaks detected in the data sets. The correlation between SNR gain and original SNR was investigated,
where the larger SNR gains were generally seen for the lower original SNR. This is likely because the
data sets with the lowest original SNR have the largest room for improvement.
7.2.1.2
EPSI data
Truncation of the EPSI data by TD proved a little more diﬃcult than rank reduction by SVD, as
the TD is a model of the data and not an exact decomposition. As the decomposition had to be
recalculated every time the rank was changed, no clear optimum rank stood out. Additionally, there
were 4 dimensions to be truncated individually, complicating the calculation of optimum rank even
further. Based on the analysis conducted it was therefore chosen that all data sets were truncated to
the same rank for consistency.
Calculation of the SNR gain also proved diﬃcult in the EPSI data due to the small bandwidth,
since a noise range far from metabolite peaks could not be found. Therefore the ﬁrst or last frame
from voxel [1,1] was chosen for all data sets, where the SNR gain was estimated using the standard
deviation before and after noise reduction. The estimated SNR gains showed a large range spanning
from around 5 to 60. The data used for calculation of the original SNR was found to be similar for all
8 data sets, but peak artifacts were introduced by the truncation in 7 of the 8 data sets, contaminating
SNR measurements (see Figure 65). If these artifacts are disregarded, the truncated data also seem
similar for all 8 data sets, indicating the denoising eﬀect of the truncation was likely approximately
the same throughout all 8 data sets.

7.2.2
Peak detection
7.2.2.1
FID data
Improved metabolite detection was assessed by visually inspecting the data for new peaks. In the FID
data used in this project up to 6 peaks can be detected: pyruvate, lactate, alanine, pyruvate hydrate,
bicarbonate and urea. Here it was found that rank reduction revealed hidden metabolites in 24 of the
32 data sets. These peaks were all peaks visible in the sum-spectra of the original data, but these
could now be seen in the individual spectra after rank reduction. In the remaining 8 data sets, 2 of
these already contained all 6 metabolites, 3 showed abnormal and physically impossible metabolite
behaviour, and 2 were very poor quality and noisy data. This leaves just 1 data set for which no
obvious explanation could be found to explain why no additional information was obtained.
In 6 of the 32 data sets, a 7th peak was seen in the region between pyruvate and alanine.
The
peak was seen at chemical shift of 175.5 ppm in data set 23 (see Figure 42). Metabolism of pyruvate
is a well understood and investigated processes. It can either be converted to lactate, alanine or enter
the CAC cycle. In the CAC cycle the carbon labeled in [1-13C]-pyruvate is quickly converted to CO2
and thus no likely compound can be found here. Pyruvate is known to have several impurities, most
commonly the dimer parapyruvate located at 170 ppm. As the 7th peak is located at 175.5 ppm, this
is not the cause. Investigation by Harris et al. showed other impurities from the [1-13C]-pyruvate,
where one of these appear around 175 ppm [32].
In some of the data sets splitting of the pyruvate peak was seen. With the information above, this is
likely from the parapyruvate impurity of the pyruvate substrate.
7.2.2.2
EPSI data
In the EPSI data fewer peaks were found. For single time point spectra, pyruvate was seen in all data
sets in the original data but was the only recurring peak. Lactate was rarely visible in the original
data, but became visible in some voxels after truncation. In some data sets urea was visible in a few
voxels in both the original and truncated data. Alanine and pyruvate hydrate was visible in a few
voxels of data set 1 (the data set with the strongest signal), but no additional peaks were found at
reliable positions in the remaining 7 data sets. Peak artifacts were introduced in several data sets after
truncation, which were found at unreliable positions and were both positive and negative, supporting
the conclusion that these were artifacts and not real metabolite peaks.
If the original data was summed over the temporal dimension, lactate became visible in additional
voxels. Thus metabolites that could be seen in the sum spectra of the original data, could now be seen
in the individual spectra after truncation.
7.2.3
Estimation of metabolite quantities
7.2.3.1
FID data
A complex GLM was successfully ﬁtted to the data capturing the overall spectrum with small residuals.
Despite issues with the phase of the peaks during the phase correction attempt, the GLM managed
to capture the overall spectrum. As a quality check, a fake peak was included in the design matrix.
Investigation showed the model overﬁtted this peak to the noise in the spectrum, and examination of
the quantity curve of this fake peak showed a build up and loss, similar to that of a metabolite. The
noise peak was ﬁtted to the long pyruvate tail, complicating the distinction between real metabolites
and simply a reﬂection of pyruvate.
Examining the metabolites as a function of time, several of the alanine and bicarbonate quantity
curves showed a decline in quantity, then a rise and then a decline again (see Figure 49, 50 and 51).
These initial bumps in the quantity curves are likely also overﬁtting of the model to the pyruvate

tail. In the relevant ﬁgures, examination of the quantity curves showed this initial decline followed the
trajectory of the pyruvate quantity, conﬁrming the overﬁtting of the model to the pyruvate tail.
7.2.3.2
EPSI data
A GLM was also successfully ﬁtted to the EPSI data, although proper ﬁts of the model was only
possible if a Gaussian lineshape was used instead of a Lorentzian. From the model, imaging of the
spatial distribution of pyruvate and lactate was performed. In several of the data sets, a smear of
pyruvate was seen in the air to the left of the mice. While no pyruvate signal is expected in the air
surrounding the mice, the signal is likely from the tube used for transfer of the hyperpolarised substrate
into the tail veins of the mice.
7.2.4
Reaction constant estimates
7.2.4.1
FID data
The reaction constants for pyruvate to lactate, alanine and bicarbonate respectively were estimated
using kinetic modelling. From the modelling, R2 for the pyruvate ﬁts were generally unaﬀected by
rank reduction, but R2 generally increased for the lactate, alanine and bicarbonate ﬁts. Despite the
increase in R2, the model parameter estimates were largely unaﬀected by this.
For alanine and bicarbonate ﬁts, the overﬁtting to the pyruvate tail of the GLM complicated the kinetic
modelling of the data. As the data given to the kinetic modelling function is comprised of 2 distinct
time proﬁles, ﬁtting the data to a single proﬁle proved diﬃcult. The model does not know that the
initial decline is to be ignored, but instead ﬁnds a compromise with the smallest error. The resulting
model is then not an accurate model of the metabolite with an inaccurate estimate of the reaction
constant.
7.2.4.2
EPSI data
Due to poor temporal resolution, kinetic modelling was not attempted and the reaction constant from
pyruvate to lactate was instead estimated using the AUC ratio method. Investigation of the quantity
curves showed that lactate was not visible in the individual spectra of 22 of the 24 tumors, why
AUClactate estimated from the model ﬁts resulted in random lactate estimates that were both positive
and negative. This trend was then transferred onto the AUC ratio estimates and kPL estimates, why
these measurements from the original data are not accurate estimates, even if valid. After truncation,
lactate became visible in the quantity curves of 19 of 24 tumors, and thus reasonable estimates of
lactate could be found, under the assumption of proper representation of the data after truncation.
The changes in AUC ratio and kPL estimates seemed to be somewhat messy, with some estimates
increasing, some unchanged and some decreasing, all to diﬀerent degrees. This is likely due to the
random structure of AUClactate resulting in random estimates using the original data.
LDH is the enzyme catalyzing the conversion of pyruvate to lactate inside the cells, which is commonly
upregulated in cancer. As the lactate levels are directly dependent on LDH activity, a correlation
between lactate and the measured LDH levels are expected. However the data showed no correlation
between the two before or after truncation, even when separating in carcinomas and sarcomas.
For mean SUV and kPL no correlation was seen here either before or after truncation.
SUV is a
measure of glucose uptake, but glucose does not necessarily have to be converted to lactate, although
it is anticipated as the conversion of glucose to lactate is upregulated in cancers.
7.3
Earlier work in the ﬁeld
Noise reduction of 13C MRS data has been attempted a number of times with a variety of methods,
see short review in [7]. To my knowledge, the ﬁrst article describing noise reduction via rank reduction

==PAGE:73==
Master thesis

by SVD and TD was ﬁrst published online in February 2018 by Brender et al., why not much research
has been performed into noise reduction using this method. Brender demonstrated that after rank
reduction by SVD, an SNR gain of nearly an order of magnitude was obtained which enabled detection
of bicarbonate in the data. The SNR gain obtained translated into improved kinetic modelling, where
a 50 fold increase in the precision of kPG estimates was obtained, which is the reaction constant
describing the conversion from pyruvate to glutamate in the CAC cycle. The results shown by Brender
used a [1-13C]-pyruvate substrate for the SNR gains, but for the kinetic modelling a [2-13C]-pyruvate
substrate was used. It is not disclosed what improvements for kinetic modelling using the [1-13C]-
pyruvate substrate was found, nor was it disclosed what SNR gains resulted in the improved precision
of the kinetic modelling using the [2-13C]-pyruvate substrate.
While the SNR gains obtained from the analysis performed in this thesis showed considerably lower
SNR gains, with a mean gain between 2.5 and 3, improved metabolite detection was obtained from
the processing of the data. Improved metabolite detection was found in 24 of the 32 examined data
sets, with bicarbonate being the most frequent peak to appear. This conﬁrms the research of Brender
et al. that underlying metabolite peaks can be revealed using SVD.
The exact deﬁnition of the 50 fold improved precision found by Brender is not given, so it is assumed
that it is a 50 fold reduction in the standard deviation of the estimated forward reaction constants.
While the exact measure is not estimated for the data analyzed in this thesis, it was clear from the
ﬁgures that a 50 fold reduction in the standard deviation was not obtained for any of the models
performed in this project. Hardly any reductions in the standard deviation of kPL and kPA estimates
were seen, but as glutamate is a downstream intermediate product of the CAC cycle, the improved
precision is therefore likely best compared with the pyruvate to bicarbonate reaction, also from the
CAC cycle. In this report, the standard deviation of kPB estimates were in fact found to increase after
rank reduction - contradictory to the results by Brender. As the bicarbonate ﬁts were troubled by
overﬁtting to the pyruvate tail, it cannot be conluded whether this increase in the standard deviation
of kPB estimates were a result of the true bicarbonate signal or the overﬁtting of to the pyruvate tail.
The analysis performed by Brender using TD of the data used a glucose substrate measured with
a CSI sequence without hyperpolarization, why comparing the results of Brender with those found in
this study are complicated. In addition, the analysis performed by Brender is only described brieﬂy.
Brender uses a rank of [r1, r2, r3, r4] = [6, 6, 8, 8], while a rank of [r1, r2, r3, r4] = [7, 7, 4, 4] was used in
this thesis. The large diﬀerence in spectral and temporal rank is likely because of the substrate used,
as the glucose substrate is converted to a larger variety of metabolites than the pyruvate substrate. In
the article, only a single data set is mentioned that resulted in an SNR gain of an ≈31 fold increase.
The results in this thesis showed improvements up to an ≈60 fold increase in SNR, which resulted in
signiﬁcant improvements on kPL estimates.
Unpublished work performed by Clemmensen et al. has demonstrated a correlation between normalized
LDH and the lactate-to-muscle ratio in mice with non small-cell lung carcinoma [33]. The analysis
performed in this thesis showed no correlation between kPL and normalized LDH, but the carcinomas
generally seemed to contain more LDH than the sarcomas.
Research by Hansen et al. has demonstrated a possible correlation between kPL and mean SUV when
separating the data in carcinomas and sarcomas, estimated by the AUC ratio method on 2D FID
data [31]. Results by Hansen demonstrated the carcinomas generally showed higher mean SUV and
the sarcomas generally showed higher kPL estimates. No correlation between kPL and mean SUV was
found in this thesis, but the data appear to be somewhat grouped. The tendency from Hansen et
al is therefore only partly seen in the data examined in this project, as the sarcomas showed both
higher kPL and higher mean SUV. However the carcinomas were more aﬀected by outliers and thus
few measurements in the region examined.

7.4
Limitations
The study of this thesis suﬀered several limitations.
In the FID data large line widths were seen
especially of the pyruvate peak inﬂuencing the data.
This could be an eﬀect of poor shim of the
magnetic ﬁeld resulting in broadening of the peaks. This broadening especially aﬀected the alanine
and bicarbonate estimates - two small peaks in close proximity of the large pyruvate signal.
The
conclusions based on alanine and bicarbonate results are therefore of limited use.
The EPSI sequence used for data acquisition is a sequence primarily used for research.
Thus the
sequence is not used extensively and showed large ranges in data quality.
The EPSI data suﬀered issues during the alignment of the proton image with the EPSI data, as many
of the data sets showed the signal occurring at unreliable spatial locations under the mice, if directly
overlayed. The spectra were therefore shifted in integer steps for best visual ﬁt, but this method is very
uncertain and prone to error. The tumor voxel selection is based on manual inspection of the aligned
proton/EPSI image and several other parameters, thereby choosing the voxel best representing the
tumor. This selection process is therefore also uncertain, as it can be subject to human error. Another
limiting factor for the EPSI data was the large voxel size. The voxels were 5.0 × 5.0 × 16 mm, why it
was sometimes diﬃcult to ﬁnd a voxel just containing signal from the tumor.
For the TD of the EPSI data it was assumed that the two spatial ranks were identical and the spectral
and temporal rank were identical. Thus investigation of optimum rank for each dimension individually
could possibly result in another optimum rank and thus better reconstruction or noise reduction.
Last, there is no standard reference for reaction constant estimates as the true value is not known.
Here measured ex vivo LDH is the closest reference, which was only estimated for the EPSI data.
7.5
Future work
The work of this thesis demonstrates that additional research into the alanine and bicarbonate peaks
are needed to circumvent the overﬁtting of the GLM to get accurate estimates of kPA and kPB.
This study demonstrated artifacts introduced in the EPSI data as a result of the truncation using
TD. Therefore additional research into the truncation and its eﬀects are needed to ensure that the
peaks revealed by the truncation are reasonable estimates, and not a result of artifacts as well. As
there are 256 voxels in each data set to be examined, this is a large task why it was not performed in
full in this thesis.
Noise reduction by rank reduction using SVD investigated in this thesis showed a noise reduction
was possible. The potential future use of this technique is highly dependent on the context in which
it is to be used. For visual inspection of spectra (for example for peak identiﬁcation), the technique
proved valuable, but hardly any eﬀect from this technique was found in regards to kPL estimates. With
that in mind, performing the rank reduction is a very quick task with just an average laptop, why it
could easily be used to improve spectra with no noticeable inﬂuence in the everyday work ﬂow.
Noise reduction by truncation using TD was more tricky. Signiﬁcant improvements were found using
the TD, but this method was also more prone to introduction of artifacts in the data after truncation.
Either a more extensive analysis regarding the choice of rank for truncation is needed for future use or
the user of the algorithm therefore has to be aware of these artifacts and be able to distinguish between
true metabolite peaks and artifacts. The use of TD also cannot be incorporated into the everyday work
ﬂow as seamlessly as SVD, since investigation of optimum rank is a time consuming process in the order
of at least several minutes depending on the size of the parameter space investigated. This technique
is therefore more suited for research and not as likely for everyday use.

Conclusion
In this thesis, investigation of noise reduction through rank reduction by SVD (i.e. Singular Value
Decomposition) and TD (i.e. Tucker Decomposition) was investigated on diﬀerent data types. Rank
reduction by SVD on 2D FID data successfully reduced the noise levels and resulted in SNR gains in
the range 1.5 to 7, with a mean SNR gain between 2.5 and 3. An optimum rank for noise reduction was
estimated for all data sets individually and found in the range 2 to 10, with the a mean rank between
4.5 and 5. The reduced noise levels resulted in a gain of temporal information of the metabolites as
well as revealing metabolites previously buried in noise.
On The 4D EPSI data noise levels were also successfully reduced which resulted in SNR gains in the
range 5 to 60. The large range in SNR gain was likely a result of artifacts introduced in the data by
the truncation, and if these are disregarded a the noise reduction look similar in all data sets. The
reduced noise levels enabled visualization of lactate in most data sets, which was otherwise not easily
seen.
The eﬀect of noise reduction on reaction constants were investigated. For the FID data the reactions
from pyruvate to lactate, alanine and bicarbonate respectively was investigated by kinetic modelling
of the data before and after rank reduction. Here it was found that while improvements were seen for
R2 of the ﬁts, the model parameters were overall not aﬀected much by the rank reduction, showing
similar estimates before and after rank reduction. AUC estimates of the metabolites were also not
aﬀected much.
For the EPSI data the pyruvate to lactate reaction was investigated with the reaction constant being
estimated by the AUC ratio method. Considerable improvements were seen for the estimates of the
EPSI data, with just 2 reasonable estimates using the original data and 17 reasonable estimates after
truncation.
Finally, the correlation of kPL estimates from tumor voxels with mean SUV from 18F-FDG PET
data and ex vivo normalized LDH was investigated. Here no correlation between kPL and 18F-FDG
PET data and ex vivo LDH was seen, although slight groupings between carcinomas and sarcomas
were found after truncation.
Rank reduction by SVD and TD both successfully reduced the noise levels of the data, with the
TD introducing artifacts in some voxels after truncation. The use of this method in the future is
therefore dependent on the use. Rank reduction by SVD is a quick process and can thus seamlessly be
integrated in the everyday work ﬂow for improvement of the data with no noticeable inﬂuence. TD is
a more time consuming process and more prone to complications, why the future use of this method
is more suited for research than for everyday use. The future use of TD is therefore not as likely.

9.1
Lorentzian lineshapes and phases
Figure 73: Illustration of the eﬀect of a phase shift in the measured spectrum. In (a) the phase is φ = 0 and
the real part therefore contains the absorption mode lineshape and the imaginary part the dispersion mode. In
(b) the phase is φ = π/4 resulting in both the real and imaginary part being a mixture of the absorption and
dispersion mode. In (c) the phase is φ = π/2 with the real and imaginary part now switching places compared
to (a). In (d) the phase is φ = π resulting in an inversion of the spectrum in (a) [11].
Figure 74: Illustration of linear phase throughout the spectrum and how it is phased. In (a) the center peak
is properly phased and is in pure absorption mode. The phase error is often proportional to the oﬀset, why a
applying a linear phase correction with the frequency oﬀset (b) can phase the whole spectrum (c) [11].
