Abstract 
This report was commissioned to understand the fundamental lubrication properties of mucus layers for 
optimum operation of biomedical devices. In turn, it can provide many useful hints on how to tailor the surface 
properties of the medical devices for most favorable interaction with the biological tissue. For this study a 
selection of multivalent cations, including FeCl3, CaCl2, and KCl, two positively charged inorganic nanoparticles, 
gold and polystyrene and two types of charged surfactants were prepared. Pin-on-disk tribometry was used 
to investigate the lubricating properties of PGM upon mixing with selected substances. An array of analytical 
tools were employed to study the influence of these substances on the structural and conformational 
characteristics of PGM. The lubrication properties of PGM can be significantly improved by the addition of 
cationic nanoparticles like Ludoc Cl or charged surfactants like TTAB. The coefficient of friction decreased from 
around 1 to ca. 0.07 and 0.08 at the low speed of 0.25 mm/s for TTAB 164 and Ludox Cl 300 mg/mL, 
respectively.

1. Introduction 
Mucins are the large molecule proteins that comprise the main component of mucus; a biological hydrogel 
that protects and lubricates epithelial tissue surfaces found in humans and animals. Mucins are composed 
of a linear protein backbone chain with a deeply glycosylated center and a mostly un-glycosylated C- and 
N- termini. The amphiphilicity of mucins have given them significant interest in various bioengineering 
applications especially where slipperiness is required i.e. catheters, contact lenses, bio-lubrication etc. [1]. 
However, to fully utilize them a better understanding of their tribological properties is required and have 
therefor been the center of several studies. 
Mucin is commercially available such as bovine submaxillary mucin (BSM) and porcine gastric mucin 
(PGM). Researchers have studied both types and from several different angles, especially crosslinking to 
improve or enhance their properties. 
For example, interaction between PGM and chitosan (a mucoadhesive polymer) can lead to an improved 
lubrication and wear resistance of the adsorbed layer in comparison to PGM or chitosan layer alone. This 
is attributed to the electrostatic attraction, the compaction of PGM molecules and the durable cohesion 
of the mucin/polymer aggregates. However, due to low solubility of chitosan, this study was conducted 
with low polymer concentration and at an acidic setting of pH 3 [2]. Further studies on the interaction of 
PGM and amine-based polycations showed improvement in lubrication properties, especially with 
branched polyethylenmine (B-PEI). The improvement was categorized as two orders of magnitude 
reduction in the coefficient of friction (ca. 0.02) at neutral pH of 7.2, in comparison to PGM or B-PEI alone. 
It was attributed that the small hydrodynamic size and sphere like conformation of the B-PEI allowed 
tenacious complexation with PGM and fast adsorption and continuous reformation. The study has shown, 
that the PGM/B-PEI mixture increased the adsorbed mass slightly, however it does not constitute as a 
perquisite for effective lubrication, nor is the charge neutralization sufficient; yet the foremost factor is 
the relative size of PGM and the polycation, and the effective aggregation of the mixture [3]. 
Other studies have focused on the effects of metal ions on the structure of mucin showing the metal ions 
such as Ca2+ could coordinate with carbohydrates. Although the binding between a metal ion and a 
complex glycoprotein like mucin is difficult to study; it was found that low and high concentrations of Ca2+ 
would interact with the protein moiety of mucins. The experiments used two types of mucin, one from 
patients with pseudomyxoma peritonei and the other is the commercial bovine submaxillary gland mucin. 
It was also determined that CaCl2/mucin mixture would have a reduced hydrodynamic radius compared 
to mucin alone, indicating folding in the mucin chains to form more compact configurations [4]. A 
compaction of mucin layers was also found to occur with the presence of multivalent cations, hence 
signifying that these ions are bonding to the anionic sites of mucins [5]. 
Accordingly, it is of interest to discern how other substances such as multivalent cations, nanoparticles 
and surfactants may induce similar synergetic lubricating effects. The difference in size, structure, and 
chemistry of the chosen substances for this study is expected to provide insights on the generality of this 
approach. For this study a selection of multivalent cations, including FeCl3, CaCl2, and KCl, two positively 
charged inorganic nanoparticles, gold and polystyrene and two types of charged surfactants were 
prepared. Pin-on-disk tribometry was used to investigate the lubricating properties of PGM upon mixing 
with selected substances. An array of analytical tools were employed to study the influence of these 
substances on the structural and conformational characteristics of PGM.

2. Materials and Methods 
2.1 Mucins and Chemicals 
PGM (Type III: partially purified, bound sialic acid 0.5-1.5%) purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Denmark 
Aps, Brøndby, Denmark). All chemicals were laboratory grade and purchased also from Sigma Aldrich 
(Brøndby, Denmark). PGM and solutions were prepared at a concentration of 1 mg/mL in HEPES buffer 
(HEPES, 10 mM, pH 7.2). 
2.2 Dialysis of PGM 
PGM was dispersed in HEPES buffer (HEPES, 10 mM, no extra salts, pH 7.2) at a concentration of 1 
mg/mL (total 40 mL in a centrifuge tube). The PGM solution was stirred using a nutator (VWR) until 
the solution became homogeneous without visibly insoluble materials. Then, the solution was 
centrifuged at 6 × 103 rpm for 15 min (VWR). The precipitates were discarded and only soluble parts 
were used for further experiments. 
2.3 Dynamic light scattering (DLS) 
The hydrodynamic radii was determined with a Brookhaven ZetaPALS instrument (Brookhaven 
Instruments Corporation, Holtsville, NY) based on phase analysis light scattering via ZetaPALS particle 
sizing software. A 1 ml volume of sample solution was placed in a clean dry cuvette. The DLS 
instrument analyzed each sample for three runs at 5 min per run. The multiple runs resulted in a 
combined output reading for the hydrodynamic diameter, DH. The DLS software also used a dust filter 
algorithm based on standard deviation to exclude particles of unusually large size.  
2.4 Zeta potential measurements 
The zeta (ζ) potentials were found using the ZetaPALS instrument (Brookhaven Instruments 
Corporation, Holtsville, NY) using ζ potential analyzer software. Disposable cuvettes and electrode 
were cleaned using water, ethanol and then dried with nitrogen. About 1.5 mL of sample was placed 
in the cuvette and then placed in the machine carefully. 10 measurements were taken per sample. 
2.5 Optical waveguide lightmode spectroscopy (OWLS) 
OWLS experiments were carried out using an OWLS 210 Labelfree Biosensor system (Microvacuum 
Ltd, Budapest, Hungary). OWLS is based on grating-assisted in-coupling of He-Ne laser into a planar 
waveguide coating (200-nm thick Si0.25Ti0.75O2 waveguiding layer on 1 mm thick AF 45 glass, 
Microvacuum Ltd, Budapest, Hungary). 
The waveguides used for this study were coated with an ultrathin layer of PDMS, the PDMS base and 
curing agent (Sylgard 184 elastomer kit, Dow Corning, Midland, MI) were dissolved in hexane at a ratio 
of 10:3 where final concentration  is 0.5% w/w. The solution was spin coated on the waveguides at 
2000 rpm for 25 sec, and then cured in an oven at 70 °C overnight. 
The PDMS coated waveguides were exposed to HEPES until a stable baseline was obtained. To transfer 
the buffer solutions through the flow cell containing the OWLS waveguide surface a programmable 
syringe pump (Model 1000-NE, New Era Pump Systems, Inc., NY) was used. Upon baseline stabilization 
around 100 μL of sample solution was then injected through the loading loop. When the surface 
adsorption increased the pump was paused for around 30 min allowing the adsorption in static 
environment. Then the flow cell was rinsed again with HEPES buffer and the adsorbed mass density 
data was calculated according to de Feijter’s equations. The refractive index increment (dn/dc) values,

2.6 Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy 
Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy was used to observe changes into the conformation of the PGM, 
cations and nanoparticle molecules, and their mixtures. CD spectra were obtained by using a 
rectangular quartz cuvette with path lengths of 0.5 mm and 10 mm for far and near UV measurements 
respectively (Hellma GmbH & Co. KG, Mulheim, Germany) using a Chirascan spectrophometer 
(Applied Photophysics Ltd, Surrey, UK). 
2.7 Pin-on-disc tribometry and tribopair 
Triblogical investigations were carried out with a pin-on-disc tribometer (CSM, Peseux, Switzerland). 
A loaded pin was slid in contact with a rotating disc at controlled rotation speeds. The pin was slid 
over a fixed track (radius: 3 mm) under a load L = 1 N while varying the speed of rotation from 100 to 
0.25 mm/s. The Hertzian contact pressure was estimated to be 0.3 MPa based on the elasticity 
modulus of 2 MPa and Poisson’s ratio of 0.5 for PDMS. The friction force f was determined by a strain 
gauge. The coefficient of friction was calculated as μ = f/L. 
PDMS pins and discs were prepared as follows: the base fluid and cross-linker of Sylgard 184 elastomer 
kit (Dow Corning, Midland, Ml) were mixed at ratio of 10:1. A moderate vacuum was then applied to 
remove air trapped in the mixture. Discs were cast in machined aluminum plate mold with flat wells 
designed to the dimensions (30 mm diameter x 5 mm thickness) of the disc holder. For the discs, only 
the sides exposed to air during curing were used for tribological measurements. The pins were molded 
in a 96 microwell plate (NUNCLON Delta Surface, Roskilde, Denmark) in the same way described for 
the discs. The PDMS mixtures were then cured overnight at 80 °C.

3. Multivalent Cations and Nanoparticles 
Three types of cation solutions, namely FeCl3, CaCl2, and KCl, were prepared at the concentrations of 10 
mM, 100 mM, and 1 M, by dissolving in HEPES as well. Two types of cationic nanoparticle (NP) solutions, 
namely SiO2-NH2 (to be denoted as “SiO2-NH2” too) and SiO2-Aluminum (to be denoted as “Ludox Cl” 
hereafter) were prepared at the concentrations of 3 mg/mL, 30 mg/mL, and 300 mg/mL. The mixture 
solutions, namely PGM:SiO2-NH2, PGM:Ludox Cl, PGM:FeCl3, PGM:CaCl2 and PGM:KCl, were prepared by 
mixing PGM and cationic solutions at 9:1 (v/v) ratio for all cases. Thus, the concentration of cationic species 
in the mixture solutions was reduced to 1/10 of each stock solutions mentioned above. 
For the OWLS studies, the refractive index increment (dn/dc) values are 0.178, 0.446, 0.324 and 0.264 
for PGM, PGM:FeCl3 1M, PGM:Ludox Cl 300 mg/mL and PGM:SiO2-NH2 300 mg/mL respectively.

3.2 Conformational changes: CD spectroscopy 
To characterize the conformational features of proteins CD spectroscopy is used, and based on previous 
studies this tool may be applied further to observe conformational interactions of protein mixtures. 
Therefore CD spectroscopic measurements on PGM, PGM:FeCl3 1M, PGM:CaCl2 1 M, PGM:KCl 1 M, PGM: 
SiO2-NH2 300 mg/mL and PGM:Ludox Cl 300 mg/mL have been carried out to investigate the effect of these 
mixtures on the conformation of PGM (fig. 3-3). The far UV CD spectra reflects the secondary structure of 
proteins such as PGM. 
PGM showed a negative peak centered at around 205 nm, characteristic of an overall random coil peptide 
chromophores. The samples have shown that below 200 nm there was a large scatter of noise and hence 
were not presented in the spectra. The PGM:FeCl3 1M mixture is not represented in the near or far UV CD 
spectra due the large noise created from the presence of salts. None of the presented mixtures in the far 
UV CD spectra showed any conformational changes, hence it can be concluded that the secondary 
structure of the PGM remains as it is. The mixtures did not influence it. Higher concentrations of PGM: 
SiO2-NH2 300 mg/mL and PGM:Ludox Cl 300 mg/mL were also measured but are not presented in the 
spectra since they had a very similar (almost equal) effect and therefor are not shown. 
The near UV CD spectrum reflects the tertiary structure of proteins, specifically the region between 260 – 
280 nm are assigned to the aromatic side chains, present only in the C- and N- terminal regions of mucins 
[2]. Hence the large peak of PGM centered around 275 nm in the near UV CD spectrum is related to the 
tertiary structural features caused by hydrophobic moieties in the terminal regions. This presence allows 
for a straightforward tracking of conformational changes upon PGM interaction with multivalent cations 
and nanoparticles. In the case of multivalent cations there are no changes to the tertiary structure of the 
PGM, it is almost unchanged. However, in the case of both nanoparticles the SiO2-NH2 and Ludox Cl there 
is a clear shift in the PGM positive peak from around 275 nm to centering at around 305 nm, hence the 
nanoparticles have the most effect of on the tertiary structure of the PGM, while the secondary structure 
remains intact.

3.4 Friction measurements by pin-on-disc tribometry 
Fig. 3-5 shows the tribological properties of the PDMS/PDMS sliding interface in solutions of PGM and its 
mixtures with FeCl3, CaCl2, KCl2, SiO2-NH2 and Ludox Cl at PGM/cationic substances 9:1 ratio. 
The coefficient of friction for PGM is generally high, except for the very high-speed regime, but mixing 
with cationic substance may improve this property, as in the case of polycations, such as chitosan [2] or 
b-PEI [6]. The poor lubricating properties of PGM shown in Fig. 3-5 in this study are in agreement with the 
past studies at this concentration and pH. The coefficients of friction obtained for the mixture of 
PGM:multivalent cations were high and almost the same as the PGM values in the entire speed range. In 
the high speed regime (≥10 mm/s), all mixtures started to show a decrease of friction about two orders of 
magnitude. However, with the decrease in speed and the activation of the boundary lubrication mode all 
mixtures friction increased to almost that of PGM alone. On the other hand, for the case of PGM:NPs, the 
friction remained clearly lower than the PGM even in the low speed regime, which supports the boundary 
lubricating effects by the NPs. The lowest observed friction was for the SiO2-NH2 300 mg/mL where it 
remains at ca. 0.1.

5.2 Conformational changes: CD spectroscopy 
CD spectroscopic measurements on PGM, PGM:SDS 10 mM, PGM:SDS 100 mM, PGM:TTAB 16.4 mM and 
PGM:TTAB 164 mM have been carried out to investigate the effect of these mixtures on the conformation 
of PGM (fig. 5-3). The far UV CD spectra reflects the secondary structure of proteins such as PGM. 
PGM showed a negative peak centered at around 205 nm, characteristic of an overall random coil peptide 
chromophores. The samples have shown that below 200 nm there was a large scatter of noise and hence 
were not presented in the spectra. The PGM:TTAB 164 mM mixture is not represented in the far UV CD 
spectra due the large noise. None of the presented mixtures in the far UV CD spectra showed any 
conformational changes, hence it can be concluded that the secondary structure of the PGM remains as 
it is. The surfactants did not influence the secondary structure of PGM. 
The near UV CD spectrum reflects the tertiary structure of proteins, specifically the region between 260 – 
280 nm are assigned to the aromatic side chains, present only in the C- and N- terminal regions of mucins. 
Hence the large peak of PGM centered around 275 nm in the near UV CD spectrum is related to the tertiary 
structural features caused by hydrophobic moieties in the terminal regions. This presence allows for a 
straightforward tracking of conformational changes upon PGM interaction with surfactants. In the case of 
the SDS 10 mM and 100 mM there are no changes to the tertiary structure of the PGM, except for a slight 
weakening of the intensity, it is almost unchanged. However, in the case of PGM:TTAB 164 mM there is a 
clear shift in the PGM positive peak from around 275 nm to centering at around 285 nm, hence the TTAB’s 
have the most effect of on the tertiary structure of the PGM, while the secondary structure remains intact.

6. Discussion 
The interaction of PGM with multivalent cations or nanoparticles was examined eventually to assess its 
impact on the lubricating efficacy of PGM at PDMS/PDMS sliding interface in a neutral pH condition. Based 
on the changes in the hydrodynamic size distribution and zeta potentials, several mixtures indicated a 
strong interaction with PGM. However, none of the multivalent cations led to an improvement in the 
lubricating properties of PGM. Meanwhile, cationic nanoparticles employed in this study, SiO2-NH2 and 
Ludox-Cl, showed a clear improvement in the lubricating properties even in the boundary lubrication 
regime (i.e. slow speed regime). For example, the coefficient of friction decreased from around 1 to ca. 
0.13 and 0.08 at the low speed of 0.25 mm/s for SiO2-NH2 300 mg/mL and Ludox Cl 300 mg/mL, 
respectively. It is to note that despite the clear improvement in the lubricating properties of PGM upon 
mixing with the NPs in this study, its efficacy is not as good as the case of mixing PGM with polycations, 
such as chitosan [2] or b-PEI [6]. In those studies, the coefficient of friction remained ca. 0.02 to 0.04 over 
the entire speed range.  
Under the experimental conditions in this study, the multivalent cations and nanoparticles are all 
positively charged while the PGM is negatively charged as shown in Table 1. Thus, an electrostatic 
attraction is expected between the PGM and the cations or nanoparticles. However, contraction in the 
hydrodynamic diameter did not occur for all the samples. It was most significant for the PGM:SiO2-NH2 
300 mg/mL mixture, however, the charge of the mixture remained negatively charged, in opposite to the 
rest of the mixtures where the final zeta potentials  were slightly positive (with the exception of 
[PGM]/CaCl2 1 M] and [PGM]/KCl 1 M] where the charge remained negative). The positive shift in the zeta 
(ζ) potentials can be attributed to the surface charge compensation. 
The conformational changes as studied by CD spectroscopy have shown that the multivalent cations did 
not change the secondary or tertiary structure of the PGM. Unfortunately, the measurement for 
PGM:FeCl3, which showed the most clear change in DH among cations, was hindered due to too high noise 
caused from the FeCl3 solution. Meanwhile, the nanoparticles affected the tertiary structure of the PGM 
significantly, indicating that the local structure of the PGM surrounding the hydrophobic moieties at the 
terminal regions has changed. Based on less effective lubricating performance compared to polycations, 
it can be suggested that the nanoparticles did not act as a cross-linker but rather as a substrate where the 
negative end terminals of the PGM spread out and attached to the positive charges of the nanoparticles. 
At the same time, suppressed negative charges of PGM thanks to the interaction with positively charged 
NPs may have improved the stability of the PGM films at the tribological interface. 
The mass adsorption studies have shown that improved adsorbed mass is not a perquisite for enhanced 
lubricity, as shown by that the adsorbed mass of PGM:SiO2-NH2 300 mg/mL increased and the lubricity 
improved, however, the adsorbed mass of PGM:Ludox Cl 300 mg/mL decreased, yet its lubricity was also 
improved. However, charge neutralization, change in the hydrodynamic diameter and changes to the 
conformation of PGM combined create the most favorable conditions for improving lubricity. 
Au and PS particles did interact with PGM and did improve the lubricity to a certain extent. However, it 
was insignificant compared with other mixtures such as those shown in section 4 and 6 of this report. It 
was therefore decided not to pursue further studies of adsorption or conformational changes in the 
context of improving the lubricating properties of PGM.

The interaction of PGM with surfactants was examined while focusing on the lubricating efficiency of 
PGM:surfactant mixtures on  PDMS/PDMS sliding interface and at a neutral pH solution. Several mixtures 
showed a strong interaction with PGM, however, only the highest concentration of TTAB’s showed 
significant improvements in the lubricating properties in the boundary lubrication regime. The coefficient 
of friction decreased from around 1 to under 0.07 and 0.08 at the low speed of 0.25 mm/s for TTAB 164 
mM and TTAB 1.64 M respectively. 
Under the experimental conditions the surfactants and PGM are all negatively charged while the TTAB 164 
mM is positively charged as shown in table 6-1, an electrostatic interaction is expected between the PGM 
and the surfactants. However contraction in the hydrodynamic diameter did not occur for all the samples. 
It was most significant for the PGM:TTAB 16.4 mM and PGM:TTAB 164 mM mixture. The positive shift in 
the zeta (ζ) potentials can be attributed to the surface charge compensation. 
The conformational changes have shown that the SDS surfactants, did not change the secondary or tertiary 
structure of the PGM. This can be readily explained by the negatively charged characteristics of SDS. The 
TTAB has influenced the tertiary structure of the PGM indicating that the local structure of the PGM 
surrounding hydrophobic moieties has changed. Nevertheless, relatively weaker improvement of the 
lubricity of the PGM compared to polycations suggests that the surfactants’ capabilities to cross-linker 
PGM molecules is relatively weaker compared to polycations. 
The mass adsorption have shown that it is not a perquisite for enhanced lubricity, in the case of PGM:TTAB 
16.4 mM adsorption increased and the lubricity improved, however, the PGM:TTAB 164 mM showed a 
decrease in adsorption and yet lubricity was also improved. However, charge neutralization, change in the 
hydrodynamic diameter and changes to the conformation of PGM combined create the most favorable 
conditions for improving lubricity. 
7. Conclusion 
For this study a selection of multivalent cations, including FeCl3, CaCl2, and KCl, two positively charged 
inorganic nanoparticles, gold and polystyrene and two types of charged surfactants were prepared and 
mixed with PGM to study their synergetic lubricating effects. 
We have shown that the lubrication properties of PGM can be significantly improved by the addition of 
cationic nanoparticles like Ludoc Cl or charged surfactants like TTAB. The coefficient of friction decreased 
from around 1 to ca. 0.07 and 0.08 at the low speed of 0.25 mm/s for TTAB 164 and Ludox Cl 300 mg/mL, 
respectively. However, their efficacy is not as good as the case of mixing PGM with polycations. 
It was found that the nanoparticles did not act as a cross-linker but rather as a substrate where the 
negative end terminals of the PGM spread out and attached to the positive charges of the nanoparticles. 
On the other hand, the surfactants the surfactants’ capabilities to cross-linker PGM molecules is relatively 
weaker compared to polycations. 
To create the most favorable conditions for improving lubricity a combination of charge neutralization, 
change in the hydrodynamic diameter and changes to the conformation of PGM is required.
