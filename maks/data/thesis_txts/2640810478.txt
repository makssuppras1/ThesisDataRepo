Executive summary
Colored building integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) are expected to play an important
role in the green power transition. The recent development of new coloration tech-
nologies, in a field that lacks standardization, raise questions on how durable these
color technologies are, and how will they influence the BIPV products lifetime.
In this work, the issue is addressed experimentally through accelerated aging
testing. PV laminate samples are made combining c-Si cells with three coloration
technologies: Inkjet (ceramic inkjet printing on glass), Kromatix (spectrally selective
coatings), and Dansk Solenergi (dyed composites interlayers).
Real-life operating
condition are recreated in the lab using a damp-heat chamber and a UV-irradiation
chamber. Samples from each of the three groups, together with colorless references,
are divided in the two chambers and exposed to 1000 h of UV irradiation and damp-
heat following standard IEC 61215.
A spectral bidirectional reflectance distribution function measurement setup in
one plane is used for a colorimetry analysis that compare the sample color appear-
ance before and after accelerated aging. 180° peel-testing is used to measure the work
of adhesion of the encapsulant/coloration interface of aged and unaged samples.
Colorimetry results show no color difference perceivable to the human eye for any of
the technologies, meaning that no degradation occurs after UV exposure.
Peel-testing results proved to have high uncertainty. However, they suggest that
the coloration technology can enhance adhesion and therefore the sample durability.
Upon both type of aging, colored Kromatix samples show less loss of adhesion com-
pared to colorless standard PV glass. A layer of ceramic Inkjet color on polished
glass proved to increase adhesion compared to colorless glass. Moreover, most sam-
ples show higher degradation when exposed to damp-heat than to UV irradiation.
The comparison between the use of polyolefin elastomer (Danks Solenergy) and
ethylene-vinyl acetate (the rest of the samples) as encapsulant suggests that the
former one has lower risk of delamination hence leading to a longer lifetime.
In summary, none of the technologies showed signs of color degradation. Further-
more, the use of an EVA/coloration interface does not appear to increase the risk of
delamination, suggesting instead potential benefits in product lifetime.

1.1
Motivation
We are living in a critical moment of the history of mankind. The impact of human
activities on climate change needs to stop growing. The green transition of the en-
ergy sector urges targeted policies and scientific research. Solar photovoltaic (PV) is
expected to lead this transition becoming the power generation technology with the
highest installed capacity by 2035 [1]. To achieve this result, the use of PV power
plants needs to be supported by the use of PV in buildings. As shown in Figure
1.1, building applied PV (BAPV) and building integrated PV (BIPV) combined are
expecting to cover 30% of the global market by 2030, with the latter experiencing a
faster growth due to its use on renovated and newly constructed buildings.
Figure 1.1. Forecast of the global market share for different types of PV power production by
ITRPV [2]. Roof top refers to both BAPV and industrial buildings rooftops, while building
integrated refers to BIPV.
==PAGE:11==
4
1 Foreword
Colored BIPV is a visually attractive and land-free power generation solution that
can find a balance between eﬀiciency and aesthetics while profiting of building mate-
rial savings. The versatility of BIPV offers synergies with architecture needs, opening
to new market opportunities in need of sustainable solutions. For instance, a PV in-
stallation that matches the surrounding landscape would thrive in historical cities
like Bologna and Venice that have strict requirements for the coloration of the roof-
ing material. Nevertheless, PV color matching (e.g. with the surrounding façade) still
needs technological improvements in terms of precision, cost, and eﬀiciency. Recently,
research institutions and private companies developed various coloration techniques,
creating products that are now approaching the market.
BIPV products, being a hybrid of PV and building materials, find themselves
in the atypical position of belonging to both an innovative and a traditional sector.
There is an urge for standardization of production and testing of these products [3].
Unlike the straightforward assessment of power eﬀiciency, defined by PV standards,
durability assessment is more diﬀicult and not yet standardized. Confidence in the
product lifetime is fundamental for the end customer, since PV products lifetime is
usually limited to 25 years, while building (e.g. roofing) materials require longer life-
time.
While some research on BIPV power production can already be found, not much
is known about color durability and deterioration. Previous research ( [4], [5], [6])
deeply investigates PV modules components deterioration under outdoor weather
conditions as the yellowing and delamination of the encapsulant (EVA) layer of the PV
product [7]. Nevertheless, there are no relevant publications about the consequences
of having a surface contact between EVA and ceramic ink (or any other coloration
methods) instead of the usual EVA/glass surface.
Hence, the research questions of this thesis are:
• Will the added presence of the coloration layer accelerate/decelerate delamina-
tion, affecting the lifetime of the product?
• Will the BIPV product color degrade under UV irradiation at a pace that makes
it unsuitable for its purpose?
1.2
Approach and thesis structure
The research questions are addressed fabricating BIPV samples covered by three type
of colored glass from different manufacturers. Accelerated aging condition are recre-
ated in the lab and the samples are analyzed and intercompared.

2 Theoretical Background
Photovoltaic (PV) modules have gained popularity in recent years. These static
devices are composed of multiple layers, which are stacked and framed together. Fig-
ure 2.1 and Protocol 1 show a typical layer composition. in conventional panels, these
layers are sealed with an aluminum frame. Depending on the purpose, the frame may
not be necessary. In this thesis the investigation is focused on small frameless PV
laminates.
2.1.1
Colored BIPV
Building integrated photovoltaic (BIPV) add to the active role of the module also
the aesthetic and structural role of a building material.
Colored BIPV solutions
confirmed to enhance the appeal of PV products in the building market. There are
plentiful ways of obtaining coloration [8], that can be categorized in 4 groups:
1. Use of colored polymeric films instead of conventional encapsulant or backsheet.
2. colored or semi-transparent active layers, as transparent thin film PV, organic
solar cells, Grätzel PV cell among others.
3. Ink printing, coating or any other modification of the module front glass.
4. Thickness tuned Silicon nitride coatings on solar cells for color appearance, or
thin-film optical filters applied on the inside of the glass
5. Other structural solutions (e.g. plasmonic colors, that use resonant interactions
between light and metallic nanostructures, currently under development).
Recently, BIPV colored modules that mimic butterflies of the genus Morpho were
developed. This coloration is obtained profiting from wavelength specific constructive
interference: stacking special thin layers on the rear side of the glass creates a firm
color appearance, with low losses in terms of incoming light. A possible drawback
is that the colored samples are iridescent. Iridescence or goniochromism, is the phe-
nomenon of certain surfaces that appear to gradually change color as the angle of view
or the angle of illumination changes (e.g. soap bubbles, feathers, seashells as well as
some minerals) [9]. Nonetheless, iridescence can be exploited by architects to obtain
specific visual effects, as in the façade of the International School of Copenhagen in
Figure 2.2.
The use of inks (e.g. ceramic ink) eliminates the angle dependency of the color
at the cost of absorbed photons. Furthermore, the use of an ink or pigment in the
rear side of the glass might affect the adhesion and thereby the durability of the
modules [10], which introduces the need for revised assessments in the industry.
Printing of colored dots is a common method for coloration of BIPV modules, which
is perceived as a uniform color at higher viewing distances. Studies have been per-
formed [11] regarding the impact of dot size, coverage and transparency on the power

help to reduce the impact of iridescence [13].
Using photons to obtain coloration inevitably results in a loss of incoming light
and hence power. ”Pill box” theory [14] suggests a lower theoretical limit of incoming
light losses for a monochromatic BIPV solution, that ranges from 1.5% for dark blue
to 3.4% for red. Power losses below 8% have been found for monochromatic colors,
constructed from single ”pill boxes” of 40 nm width. Blue reflection spectra achieve
the lowest power losses, followed by green and red monochromatic colors. This is due
to the shape of the solar spectrum, the spectral response of photovoltaics (PV) cells,
and the sensitivity of the human eye, making long wavelength (red) photons the most
relevant ones for power generation [14]. Halme et. al. [15] found a link between solar
cell band-gap and color and eﬀiciency: for a c-Si cell (1.1 eV band-gap) yellow-green
is the most eﬀicient photovoltaic color, whereas highly saturated blue, red and purple
colors produce the lowest eﬀiciencies, when compared at equal brightness.
2.2
Colorimetry
2.2.1
Color perception
Colorimetry is the science and technology used to quantify and physically describe
the human color perception. It is similar to spectrophotometry, but is distinguished
by its interest in reducing spectra to the physical correlates of color perception, most
often the CIE 1931 XYZ color space tristimulus values and related quantities [16].
The human brain perceive colors by processing stimuli sensed by cones and rods in
the eye. Cones have a dominant sensation response to either green-red or yellow-blue,
while rods are sensitive to motion and degree of lightness [17].
When talking about lightness it is important not to confuse it with brightness:
lightness is the perceived level of a color relative to a stimulus appearing white (e.g.
D65 reference white), while brightness gives the perceived overall level of light emit-
ted by a color.
There is a perceived disconnection between the spectral composition and the color
appearance to the human eye. The principle of metamerism, for instance, refers to
the fact that two samples indistinguishable to the human eye may have different
spectrum. This confirms the diﬀiculty of representing accurately differences between
colors.

2.2.2
CIELab color space
With the intention of expressing color difference in this work, removing subjectivity
as much as possible, the CIELab (or CIEL*a*b*, not to be confused with Hunter’s
Lab) environment is chosen. This is a color space that expresses color as three values
(derived from the tristimulus), shown in the gamut in Figure 2.4. Perceptual light-
ness (L*) defines black at 0 and diffuse white at 100 (specular white can reach higher
values). The a* axis is relative to the green–red opponent colors, with negative values
toward green and positive values toward red. The b* axis represents the blue–yellow
opponents, with negative numbers toward blue and positive toward yellow. These
two coordinates range is technically unbounded, although is usually clamped at ±
128 [18], and for the use of this thesis it never exceeds ± 60.
(a)
(b)
Figure 2.4. (a) Visible CIELab gamut calculated with D65 reference white. (b) Three dimen-
sional CIELab space [18]: changes in saturation occur along the radii of the horizontal circles,
changes in hue occur along the circumferences, while changes in lightness occur vertically.
The non-linear relations for L*, a*, and b* are intended to mimic the nonlinear
response of the eye. Furthermore, these coordinates are well correlated to the human
eye, since a* and b* are inspired to how the cones perceive color, while L* represent
the lightness, as perceived by rods [18]. Therefore, uniform changes of components in
the CIELab color space aim to correspond to uniform changes in perceived color, so
the relative perceptual differences between any two colors in CIELab can be approx-
imated by treating each color as a point in a three-dimensional space and taking the
Euclidean distance (∆Eab) between them.
With the intention of expressing the difference between two colors, a more accu-
rate approximation can be done by using instead the ∆E00, also known as ∆E2000
or CIEDE2000. This was first proposed in 2001 and standardized in 2013 [19], when

2.3
Polymeric encapsulant
The encapsulant of a PV module is a polymeric foil that composes two of the five
layers of a PV laminate (see Figure 2.1).
Although every component impacts its
eﬀiciency and reliability, the encapsulant is of particular importance for the long-
term characteristics of a PV module. The encapsulant has to fulfill several different
functions simultaneously, all of which are essential to the proper functioning of the
module: it adheres to the components connecting them, provides protection to the so-
lar cells, maintains electrical insulation, secures an optimal optical coupling between
the glass and the solar cells, and lastly, it limits the penetration of humidity and
oxygen [24].

Even if there are many polymers that can be considered as alternative encapsulant
material - like polyvinyl butyral (PVB), thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU) - as of
now, cross-linked elastomeric ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) remains the dominating
encapsulant for PV modules [25]. Recently, polyolefin elastomer (POE) proved to
be a noteworthy alternative that may shift producers focus in the future [26], even
though EVA is expected to continue as the encapsulant market leader in the coming

2.3.1
Ethylene Vinyl Acetate
EVA is a copolymer of vinyl acetate (typically 28-33 wt%) [27] and ethylene (the
remaining fraction). It is composed by the polymer resin, the curing agent (usually
organic peroxide) and other additives (i.e. UV stabilizer, radical trapping stabilizer,
antioxidant agents). EVA is commercialized as rolls of roughly 0.5 mm thick film
that contains the whole components formulation.
Being a thermoset polymer, the curing agent is thermally activated during the PV
module lamination phase (usually happening close to 140°C). The cross-linking pro-
cess starts at temperatures between 120 and 130°C and is initiated via decomposition
of peroxides. These form free radicals that will in turn lead to the formation of cova-
lent bonds between polymer chains. The end of the polymer chains, that has diffused
into the adjacent surface, hardens while still being bound to the EVA, creating a
strong adhesive link between the surfaces (called diffusive bonding and illustrated in
Figure 2.7). In a macro-scale, curing transforms the initially opaque soft-foil in a
robust highly-transparent encapsulant.
Figure 2.7. EVA cross-linking adhesion process: heat (or UV+O2) activates the curing agent,
whose decomposition form free radicals. EVA chains that diffused in the adjacent surface
(e.g. a color layer) cross-link creating an interfacial bonding.
The degree of cross-linking is controlled by lamination time, lamination tempera-
ture (affecting the amount of peroxide activated per time unit) and initial peroxide
concentration. Soxhlet extraction - which is the determination of the gel content by
chemical solvent extraction at elevated temperatures (described in details in chap-
ter 3.1) - is a well-established and precise method for evaluating the cross-linking

degree [28], although it has the major drawback of being destructive and time con-
suming. Hirschl et. al. [29] carried out an extensive comparative study on sixteen
alternative methods to assess cross-linking degree. Results suggest that most meth-
ods still require improvements to be viable, with the exception of DSC (differential
scanning calorimetry), that provides data on residual amount of curing agent, as well
as Raman spectroscopy, identified as a potential new method for in-line cross-linking
degree measurements in PV industrial production.
2.3.2
EVA degradation
During the lamination of the PV modules, the peroxide catalyst is mostly depleted.
Incomplete cross-linking of the polymer may lead to residual (unconsumed) peroxide
catalyst which can then take part in further UV-initiated photo-chemical reactions
during the module service lifetime [30] (see Figure 2.7).
Discoloration
EVA is subject to gradual yellowing in PV modules exposed to the outdoor environ-
ment over the years, an inevitable phenomenon that can only be delayed. The cause
of EVA discoloration is mainly due to the degradation of the UV stabilizer additives in
the EVA compound [31] rather than UV-induced degradation of the polymer chains
themselves.
In fact, EVA resin does not normally absorb light in the UV-visible
spectral range passing through the front glass. In the case of a partially cured EVA,
discoloration under UV light occurs even faster than in the case of a fully cured EVA.
Acetic acid formation
While UV-induced yellowing of EVA leads to some minor power losses, the actual first
degradation stage is the formation of acetic acid (HaC). HaC is produced by hydrol-
ysis of EVA. Before curing, HaC formation occurs mainly at temperatures between
120°C and 150°C [32] that are reached during EVA manufacturing and lamination.
After curing, humidity is the main driver of HaC formation. HaC is usually volatile,
but in a PV module it is sealed between the glass and the backsheet.
HaC has corrosive effects on tin coating of the cell metallization. It consumes the
tin and exposes the underlying copper core of the cell interconnection, whose direct
contact with EVA causes a brown discoloration.
Furthermore, HaC facilitates the transport of Na+ from the front glass to the cells,
which significantly increases the likelihood of potential induced degradation (PID)
[33]. PID happens when an unusually high electrical potential (often between the
grounded frames and the PV cell it can reach up to 1000 V) drives the sodium ions to
==PAGE:23==
2.4 Encapsulant Adhesion
17
diffuse deep into the PN junction creating shunts that dramatically reduce the power
output.
For of these reasons, HaC plays a large role in PV degradation, and its inevitable
presence is the major disadvantage of EVA. However, it should be noted that a poly-
meric encapsulant like POE is less susceptible to the aforementioned degradation
mechanisms due to the lack of HaC. For instance, the absence of HaC combined with
lower polarity completely eliminates the risk of PID when using POE.
Delamination
Delamination is one cause of peak power loss in PV modules [34]. It can occur at any
of the interfaces within the PV module (e.g. glass/EVA, cell/EVA, contacts/EVA,
ribbon/EVA). Temperature, humidity, and UV light deteriorate the interfacial bond-
ing, then stress exerted at the interface from residual thermal stresses or from external
mechanical stress trigger the delamination.
Delamination creates new interfaces - e.g. glass/air and air/EVA in the case of
glass/EVA delamination, taken in consideration in this thesis. These two new inter-
faces cause reflection losses (Fresnel’s equations estimates an interfacial reflectance
of about 4% [35]). Moisture diffusing into the EVA can condense in the delaminated
area and accelerate the propagation of this area [36]. This leads to the formation
of pathways that accelerate the penetration of moisture.
Once the cell surface is
reached, moisture forms HaC and cause corrosion of metallic components. Due to
the corrosion-induced increase of internal series resistance and the optical losses, the
locally generated power decreases. This originates a current mismatch that, if signif-
icant, may trigger bypass diodes causing even further power losses. Lastly, an active
bypass diode increases the sub-string operative temperature further accelerating the
degradation rate.
2.4
Encapsulant Adhesion
Adhesion is the ability of dissimilar surfaces to cling to one another and is a fun-
damental necessity for PV modules. As previously outlined, adhesion strength of
polymeric encapsulants to the glass superstrates is a factor that can critically affect
the performance reliability and durability of modules. [37]. Furthermore, the higher
the adhesion force, the lower the chances of delamination [34].
There are five types of adhesion: mechanical, chemical, dispersive, electrostatic
and diffusive [38]. The one used in PV laminates is the latter. Diffusive forces are like
mechanical tethering at the molecular level. Diffusive bonding occurs when species
from one surface (EVA) penetrate an adjacent surface (glass/color layer) while still

18

being bound to the phase of their surface of origin. Adhesion is not a property of EVA
itself but of the system. Therefore, its measurement depends on many parameters
like temperature, time, speed, direction, relative moduli and surface dissipation [38].
Adhesion strength between EVA and glass, or any coloration technology on the
inner side of the glass, can be measured with a peel test (Figure 2.8). Peeling off
the EVA from the glass, the surface connection is broken, while the force needed is

A Soxhlet extractor (that takes its name from its inventor Franz von Soxhlet) is a
laboratory apparatus that extracts solid material recirculating a specific solvent. It
is made of glass (Figure 3.1) and it has three main parts: a percolator (boiler and

extracted, and a siphon mechanism (d), which periodically empties the thimble. The
apparatus, thanks to the continuous input of heat, minimizes the amount of needed
solvent using reflux: a technique where vapors condensate and return to the system
from which they evaporated.

Each sample quality is checked before, during and after accelerated aging in the

5.1.1
Electroluminescence
Electroluminescence (EL) testing consists in applying a reverse current through the
PV laminate and capture the emitted radiation in order to acquire spatial information
about photoactivity.
A BK Precision DC power supply is used to induce current (100% ISC) through the

has a Si-based detector that needs a lens filter which removes wavelengths in the
visible light range ( above 900 nm). The quantum eﬀiciency at the sample emission
range (from 950 nm to 1150 nm for c-Si) is very low, hence 30 seconds of exposure
time in a dark room are necessary.

5.2 Colorimetry: BDRF measurements

5.2.1
BDRF Setup
The setup was developed by the DTU Fotonik department and consist in a one plane
gonioreflectometer system (Figure 5.2 and 5.3).
The used light source is an EQ-99XFC laser driven light source (LDLS) from
Energetiq. The light is generated via a continuous wave laser that focuses onto a
proprietary bulb. The high heat generated by the laser converts the Xenon gas inside
the bulb into a plasma state. The plasma has a high UV component (190 - 400 nm),
which is why there is a UV filter mounted in the light path. The off-axis parabolic
(OAP) mirrors collimate the light into the sample. Collimation is necessary since the
test assumes that all photons are coming from a single direction. The light source
and the mirrors are cased in a black painted housing.
The device under test (DUT) is mounted on a rotation stage equipped with a DC
motor, controllable through a LabVIEW interface, that allows to change the light
angles of incidence (AOIs). A sensor collects the light reflected from the DUT. The
sensor used is a reflective collimator which consists in a housed OAP mirror with an
aperture diameter of 11 mm and a fiber connector. The sensor transfers the signal to
an Ocean Optics QE65000 spectrometer through the optical fiber. The sensor has its
own DC motor that allows it to rotate independently around the DUT. The sensor is
placed 7° below the horizontal plane of the light source. This allows it not to obstruct
and shade the light source when the light beam is perpendicular to the DUT (as can
be seen in Figure 5.3).

5.2.2
Experimental procedure
The setup is calibrated using as a reference a white disc slightly larger than the illu-
minated area. This, ideally, is a surface that shows close to perfect diffuse reflectance
(at least at low angles), acting as Lambertian reflector.
The following step consists in blocking the light source and perform a round of
measurements of the dark background noise at each sensor position. Ambient reflec-
tion will later be subtracted from the sample measurements. To avoid the detector
saturation, the spectrometer integration time is adjusted for each round of measure-
ments, ranging from 0.2 s to 10 s. This integration time is set at its lowest at specular
reflection angles, where most saturation-induced inaccuracies occur.
The BDRF measurements is performed varying the sample angle α in the inter-
val [0°; 75°] at steps of 15°. Measurements are done only in the positive direction
of angles since the negative side would not provide any new information due to the
Helmholtz reciprocity principle (see section 2.2.4).
For each sample rotation angle, β is varied in the interval [85°; -85°] with steps
of 5°. The spectrum is measured and integrated, from 300 nm (UV filter threshold)
to 980 nm. This is done for the above mentioned 37 positions of the sensor in a
semicircle around the sample, for each of the 6 sample positions, resulting in a total
of 222 spectrum measurements per sample.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion
This chapter contains an intercomparison of previously exposed results and a holistic
assessment.
7.1
Colorimetry
Kromatix colorimetry results show an absence of color degradation. Data points that
indicated possible perceived color degradation in the inkjet samples were found to
be associated with measurement inaccuracy. More specifically it was related to the
collimation at high relative view angles, and detector saturation at specular angles.
Dansk Solenergi colorimetry analysis showed no difference in color. ∆E00 scattered
values are caused by random changes in lightness, consequence of the heavily textured
front glass.
Overall, it can be concluded that none of the samples experienced color degrada-
tion after 1000 hours of UV exposure. This was to be expected considering the too
low dose of UV irradiation. BIPV products ideally aim to have a coloration that does
not experience major perceivable changes over a lifetime similar to other building ma-
terials (25-50 years). Therefore, the lack of perceived color degradation is a positive
attribute for these technologies. A greater UV dose would be necessary to compare
the durability of these three coloring solutions and to draw conclusions on the color
stability over the products lifetime.
7.2
BIPV laminate durability
7.2.1
Visual inspection
Both chambers have a humidity stress component, although this is much greater for
the damp-heat chamber (fixed 85%RH) than for the UV chamber (day/night dew
point cycle). While this led to a reduction of encapsulant adhesion in both cases,
visible degradation occurred only in samples exposed to damp-heat stress. Moisture
combined with the presence of acetic acid (HaC) caused evident metallization corro-
sion in Kromatix, Inkjet and reference samples, in agreement with similar studies on
==PAGE:63==
64
7 Discussion
EVA-based PV modules [4]. According to the literature, visible corrosion is not to be
expected in POE-based laminates since they do not have HaC as by-product. On this
matter, Dansk Solenergi sample (Grey_2L_DS) did not allow any visual assessment,
since view of the contacts is covered by the textured and dark-colored glass.
7.2.2
Adhesion between encapsulant and coloration layer
More information is obtained by comparing the work of adhesion measured by peel-
testing. In general, adhesion is drastically reduced after 1000 hours of exposure in
both accelerated aging chambers. Nevertheless, this reduction in adhesion has no con-
sequences on the performance of the samples, as no loss of power is registered. This
is in accordance with the theory presented in 2.3.2: even though reduced encapsulant
adhesion implies shorter module lifetime, due to high probability of delamination,
there is no significant loss of performance until delamination actually occurs. While
damp-heat induced loss of adhesion is in line with other studies, e.g. [5] [44], the in-
fluence of UV-induced degradation is consistently higher, especially considering that
the aging stress is based on a standard UV preconditioning test and not on the simul-
taneous exposure to damp-heat and UV light.
Inkjet samples comparison
The overall adhesion strength of Inkjet samples is consistently lower than all others.
The particular glass on which the ceramic ink is printed is considered a possible ex-
planation for this. Glaseksperten glass is unusually smooth and polished, and that is
known to be an obstacle to eﬀicient diffusive adhesion of a polymer. This hypothesis
is endorsed by comparing peel-test of the Glaseksperten and the Petraglas unaged
references, the latter resulting in three times greater work of adhesion.
When comparing Inkjet colored sample to the unaged colorless reference, it seems
that adding a layer of Inkjet ceramic ink on the rear side of a smooth glass enhances
EVA adhesion strength, and thus reduces the likelihood of delamination. This sug-
gests that a more extensive coloration area, which is known to reduce light penetration,
may have a compensating benefit on the durability of the module. Further research
on this topic is needed to draw solid conclusions, preferably by applying Inkjet ce-
ramic ink to standard PV glass such as Petraglass.
Type of climate chamber - UV/damp-heat exposure
A common observed trend for Kromatix and the reference samples is the higher loss of
adhesion after damp-heat exposure compared to UV light exposure. It is known that
damp-heat conditions reduce peeling strength and often trigger delamination [4]. How-
ever, an opposite trend is observed for inkjet samples, where a higher adhesion loss is
==PAGE:64==
7.2 BIPV laminate durability
65
observed after UV light exposure. This suggests that Inkjet sample are probably more
vulnerable to UV than other technologies. A possible explanation is that ink-based
colors have higher UV absorption than thin-film optical filters. Furthermore, ceramic
inks datasheets indicate a different chemical resistance to HaC depending on the spe-
cific color, suggesting the possibility of colored based UV-resistance. Sample Red_I,
aged in the UV chamber, has high resistance to HaC corrosion (Grade 1, appendix C),
while this could be different for the damp-heat aged Yellow_I, whose datasheet is not
available (e.g. Black, Green and Orange ink from the same manufacturer reported
a Grade 2 resistance). Finally, Kempe et al. [45] found that decomposition of UV
absorbers results in the formation of benzoic acid, that catalyses and accelerates the
de-bonding of EVA from the glass. The interaction of this acid with different inks is
a parameter that needs to be investigated. Nevertheless, the UV dose used in this
thesis is too low to have decomposed the UV absorbers. The strong loss in adhesion is
most likely due to a combination of temperature and moisture infiltration generated
by the dark/light cycles of the UV chamber (section 4.1).
Kromatix samples comparison
The relative durability of Kromatix samples is apparent, as they exhibited less ad-
hesion losses of the EVA-rear glass interface after both UV and damp-heat aging
compared to the standard Petraglas reference. This more durable bonding probably
derives from a more eﬀicient penetration of EVA polymeric chains in the plasma-
treated coloration multilayers. This suggests that the use of this type of glass in
BIPV products may increase the lifetime of the product and has more than just aes-
thetic benefits. Nevertheless, the advantages may be limited to the rear side of the
glass only, as the outer edges showed visible signs of deterioration. However, the use
of protective frames or other solutions (depending on the specific BIPV installation)
can address this issue.
Type of encapsulant - EVA and POE
The samples prefabricated by Dansk Solenergi were not built for an extensive peel
testing with the setup used in this work, so the single measurement that was per-
formed is not suﬀicient for a solid analysis. However, given the remarkably high peel
force recorded, it is presumable that a combination of the glass treatment and the use
of POE as encapsulant results in a stronger and more durable adhesion. Reducing
the risk of delamination, PID, and other HaC-related degradation mechanisms leads
to the hypothesis that the use of POE instead of EVA in BIPV products can have
a positive impact on their lifetime, narrowing the currently large gap between PV
products and building materials.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Below is shown a chronological summary of the procedure of this experimental work:
Protocol 2 Thesis work step-by-step
Lamination validation. Ensure an EVA cross-linking degree >75%.
Laminate preparation. Stacking layers: colored glass/EVA/PV cell/EVA/backsheet.
Accelerated aging. Three groups: unaged, 1000 h UV and 1000 h DH.
Characterization. IV-curves, EL and colorimetry before, during and after aging.
Destructive peel-test. Encapsulant adhesion measurements and final analysis.
The goal of this work was to shed light on the consequences of introducing a col-
oration technology on the rear side of the BIPV glass in terms of durability. This was
done investigating three different methods of obtaining coloration, implemented by
three manufacturers. Two types of accelerated aging were used to recreate real-life
working conditions, namely damp-heat and UV light exposure. Peel-test measure-
ments allowed to assess the adhesion work, a parameter strictly connected to the risk
of delamination, and thus to the product lifetime. Furthermore, the perceived color
of the samples and its potential degradation after 1000 hours of UV irradiation was
investigated by BDRF measurements.
Firstly, using the colored glass provided by the manufacturers (Glaseksperten and
SwissiINSO), BIPV laminates were prepared with a manual laminator. An encapsu-
lant (EVA) cross-linking degree above 75% was ensured using the Soxhlet extraction
method, which also allowed to determine the lamination time dependency of the EVA
gel content. Additional pre-made colored samples, which use POE instead of EVA,
were provided by Dansk Solenergi.
No color change perceptible to the human eye (∆E00 > 1) was observed after an
approximate UV dose of 10 KWh/m2. This means that none of the sample experi-
enced color degradation. To obtain more information about color durability and its
inter-comparison between technologies, a higher dose of UV irradiation is required.
==PAGE:66==
68
Conclusion
Ideally, only after such higher UV dose should the specimens be placed in a damp-
heat chamber to investigate any interactions between the degraded color layer and
the underlying encapsulant [5]. The time-frame of this master thesis compelled a
parallel exposure in the two chambers.
Appreciable reduction in adhesion was found in all aged samples. Among all sam-
ples, Kromatix ones experienced the lowest adhesion loss: in average 20% less than
the standard PV glass references. This suggests that their use plasma-processed inner
multilayered coating can give to a BIPV product enhanced durability other than a
pleasant colorful appearance.
The inner ceramic ink coloration layer of the Inkjet samples showed higher adhe-
sion compared to the unaged glass reference, even after accelerated aging. Since this
technique, which is known to reduce light penetration, is often applied by printing
colored dots, a choice of more extensive coloration area may have a compensating
benefit on the durability of the module. However, further testing with standard PV
glass instead of smooth Glaseksperten glass used in this work, is needed to confirm
this hypothesis. Sample Green_I cracked during the laser-cutting procedure. The
lack of these reference peel-test measurements inhibits adhesion loss intercomparisons
between Inkjet and the other technologies.
Dansk Solenergi samples, which achieve coloration alternating multiple POE and
color layers, are not fully compatible with the peel-testing setup, although partial re-
sults showed promising enhanced adhesion strength and durability of POE compared
to EVA.
In conclusion, none of the coloration technologies showed signs of degradation
after the administered UV dose.
Moreover, results showed that the introduction
of an EVA/coloration interface seems not to increase the risk of delamination of
the PV laminate, suggesting instead possible benefits in the lifetime of the product.
Deepening further research is needed.
Future work recommendations
Other than using higher UV doses and combining the two weathering chambers, the
effort following this work should be focused on measuring the EVA cross-linking de-
gree during and after accelerated aging. UV light combined with oxygen is known to
activate residual curing agent, starting a post-crosslinking reaction. For this purpose,
Soxhlet extraction must be replaced/paired with a non-destructive measurement (e.g.
Raman spectroscopy), which allows to repeat multiple measurements on the same
sample.
==PAGE:67==
Conclusion
69
Improvements to the peel-testing setup can be implemented by increasing the
number of peel-strips per sample, varying the peel angle to assess different interfacial
rupture mechanisms, and taking temperature into account. In fact, it is proven that
critical cracks opening for delamination are temperature dependent since the adhesive
energy of the EVA/glass interface decays with rising temperature [46]. Therefore, a
more insightful evaluation of the adhesion between EVA and glass/coloration layer
could be done performing peel-test at temperatures relevant to the BIPV application
and not just at room temperature. Finally, increasing the overall number of samples
and comparing the interaction of a glass coloration with various types of encapsulant
could lead to interesting results.
Future work may include the development of an algorithm that estimate the ac-
celeration factor of the aging stress induced by the the chambers, targeting both the
PV laminate and the color durability.
