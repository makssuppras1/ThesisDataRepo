Abstract 
The effect of initial water activity of acetonitrile (ACN), isohexane and methyl tert-butyl ether (MTBE) as 
reaction medium on CALA and isohexane as reaction medium for CALB was investigated. The investigation 
uses experimental enzyme kinetics of the esterification of ethanol and butyric acid to form the product ethyl 
butyrate. Ethyl butyrate is a short chain ester commonly used in the food industry. Furthermore, molecular 
dynamics (MD) simulations of CALA in either isohexane and ACN and CALB in isohexane were also performed 
to observe waters occupancy on the lipases at different water activities theoretically.  
The experimental results showed that the CALA in isohexane had the highest specific activity with ACN being 
second and MTBE having the lowest specific activity of the CALA experiments. Furthermore, CALB in 
isohexane had the largest specific activity of all the experiments and was found to also have higher specific 
activity than CALB in MTBE from other literature. The specific activity in general formed a trend of increasing 
specific activity with increasing water activity to a certain point. The specific activity then began to decrease 
at higher water activity. The results were deemed insufficient to make specific conclusions due to difficulties 
with inactivation of the enzyme and improper handling of the enzymes.  
The MD simulations were performed at 100, 200, 300 and 400 water molecules for CALA and CALB in 
isohexane. An additional simulation for CALA in ACN at 150 water molecules was added to better observe 
the water clusters occupancy with gradually increasing water activity. The simulations of CALA in ACN and 
CALB in isohexane showed little to no conformational changes with the increase of water activity. However, 
CALA in isohexane did show a larger conformational change of a cavity with the active site inside it, indicating 
interfacial activation. Which could support theoretically the higher specific activity of isohexane in the CALA 
experiments. However, it was uncertain whether this formed cavity was an outlier in the experiments and 
needed further investigation. Furthermore, the simulations of CALA in isohexane and CALB in isohexane 
needed further work to provide reliable results. The increasing water activities showed the occurrence and 
growth of water clusters near the active site for all simulations. Indicating theoretically the reason of the 
decrease in specific activity over higher initial water activity due to the water clusters might be blocking the 
substrate from entering the active site.

1 Introduction 
In the ever-growing chemical industry, biocatalysts have been recognized as a prominent tool to optimize 
different chemical processes, such as synthesis, degradation of waste, and large-scale production of biofuels 
from renewable resources. Biocatalysts or enzymes are widely utilized in the industry, in the production of 
various products such as pharmaceuticals, food ingredients, and chemicals among others [1]. Enzymes are 
mainly obtained through microbial organisms because of consistency of production, ease of conformational 
modification through mutations and increased stability due to the natural cellular environment. Enzymes are 
large molecules with the ability to greatly accelerate a specific reaction towards a product with high 
specificity, thus establishing their great use in the pharmaceutical industry because of the stereochemical 
purity [2][3].  
Enzymes have multiple functions, for example, hydrolases catalyze hydrolysis, which is used to degrade lipids 
and carbohydrates. Industrially, enzymes such as hydrolases and the subcategory lipases are often used in 
organic solvents, because of the advantages of shifting the reaction from hydrolysis towards esterification 
and increasing the enzymes thermostability [4]. 
However, enzymes natural environment is in aqueous solvents, and it is known that water is a necessity for 
the enzyme function. Water functions as a lubricant in non-aqueous media giving the enzyme its flexibility 
and therefore ability to catalyze a specific reaction [5]. In a previous study by Banik et al. of Candida 
antarctica lipase B (CALB) in methyl-tertbutyl-ether (MTBE) showed that some water increased the activity, 
but that at too high-water activities hindered the specific activity of the enzyme. To investigate further, 
molecular dynamics simulations with increasing water activities was performed by Banik et al. and showed 
that the water molecules would create a cluster near the active site, which could prevent the substrate from 
entering [4]. This creates impetus for investigating which specific water activity is required for optimal 
esterification [4][5]. 
In this study, the activity of CALB in isohexane and Candida antarctica lipase A (CALA) in isohexane, MTBE, 
and acetonitrile (ACN) are investigated. This will include characterization of the enzyme kinetics, 
esterification of butyric acid and ethanol into ethyl butyrate with either CALA or CALB in the mentioned 
solvents. Ethyl butyrate is a short chain ester used in the industry as a component for pineapple flavor [6]. 
Furthermore, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations will also be performed for CALA in isohexane and 
acetonitrile and CALB in isohexane, with increasing water activities to evaluate the initial water activities 
effect on the catalysis.

1.1 Enzyme structure and function 
Enzymes are large molecular biological catalysts, which accelerate a specific biochemical reaction. Enzymes 
are made of long, linear chains of amino acids, which fold into a characteristic three-dimensional(3-D) 
structure. The structure derives from a sequence of amino acids and gives each different chain unique 
properties. Therefore, if the protein is ever unfolded or denatured the catalytic abilities are lost [2]. The 
structure of enzymes can be divided into four structures, primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary 
structure seen on Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: The four degrees of structure that a protein can have. Primary structure is the sequence of amino acids a proteins peptide chains have. The 
secondary structure is formed by hydrogen bond interactions between the backbone of the polypeptide chain which forms alpha helix and beta 
pleated sheets. The tertiary structure consists of interactions from hydrogen bonds and disulfide bridges. The quaternary structure comes from the 
interactions between two or more individual polypeptide subunits coming together to form the protein. The image is acquired from [7]  
The primary structure is the amino acid sequence of the polypeptide chain connected with peptide bonds. 
From this sequence, the amino acids may interact with each other by hydrogen bonds between the amino 
and carboxyl groups of the backbone forming the secondary structure. The secondary structure has two main 
structures. The first structure is the 𝛼-helix that occurs when the chain circulates clockwise creating a spiral 
with hydrogen bonds occurring every fourth amino acid. The clockwise form is dictated by all naturally 
occurring amino acids being in the L-form instead of the D-form. The second structure is 𝛽-pleated sheets, 
which occurs when the polypeptide chain runs close with itself creating multiple hydrogen bonds between 
the chain which after several turns creates a sheet. The 𝛽-pleated sheets are formed in two different shapes 
dependent on the course of the chain. The chain can either have the same direction through the 𝛽-pleated 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
 
Page 11 of 124 
 
 
sheet, creating a parallel structure, or it can alternate every other turn causing the antiparallel structure, 
which is the more common of the two as seen in figure 2.  
 
 
Figure 2: Showing the three different structures comprising the main secondary structure. The alpha helix going counterclockwise with hydrogen 
bonds occurring every 4 amino acid and the beta pleated sheet in its parallel and antiparallel forms. The figure was made with PowerPoint and 
inspired by [8]. 
The tertiary structure consists of interactions between the residue parts of the amino acids. These 
interactions can be either hydrophobic, electrostatic, formed by hydrogen bonds or disulfide bridges forming 
the 3D structure of the protein. The last order of structure is the quaternary structure that is formed between 
the interactions of two or more individual polypeptide chains and is therefore not always present in all 
proteins [8][9]. 
Within the large complex structure of the enzyme, a small area is incorporated consisting of a few residues 
called the active site. The active site binds a specific substrate and catalyzes it towards a product. The 
enzymes specificity towards a single substrate derives from the active sites charge and shape. However, it is 
also the enzyme’s flexibility that causes its specificity. When the specific substrate is near the enzyme, the 
enzyme will form a complementary shape to accommodate the binding of the substrate to the active site. In 
general, when the substrate binds to the active site the enzyme will cause the reaction to become more 
favorable by positioning and interacting with the substrate to lower the activation energy [10]. These 
mechanisms can be seen in figure 3.

Due to enzymes high specificity and catalytic properties towards a certain substrate and reaction the 
enzymes have shown great promise in the industry. However, a challenge with many enzymes is their 
inability to tolerate some of the harsh environments in industrial facilities. This is due to the tendency of 
most enzymes to denature in too high heat or pH differing from their natural environment. Enzymes with 
high stability is therefore more sought after in the industry. This is often the case for microbial hydrolases 
and specifically its subcategory lipases [2][12].

1.2 Lipase catalyzed esterification reactions 
Short chain esters are a group of important molecules used in the cosmetic, food and beverage industry due 
to their property of adding fragrance and flavor to their products [13]. The ester, ethyl butyrate, is in high 
demand in the food industry because of its usage in synthetic pineapple flavor. To produce ethyl butyrate, 
either extracting it from natural sources or synthesis are the options available. The synthesis is normally a 
condensation reaction between ethanol and butyric acid also known as esterification. The reaction is 
normally catalyzed by a strong acid, besides butyric acid. The esterification reaction of ethyl butyrate can be 
seen below in figure 4 [6][14]. 
 
Figure 4: The reaction scheme between an alcohol and a carboxylic acid to form an ester in a condensation reaction with a catalyst. Image acquired 
from [14] and modified with a headline in PowerPoint. 
However, there are multiple challenges with acids as a catalyst because of difficulty with handling and 
corrosion issues [15]. Furthermore, esterification is a reversible reaction, which means by using an acid in 
aqueous solution one would force the equilibrium towards the reactants side reducing the yield of product 
for the reaction. It is typical for ester production to try and remove as much water as possible to favor the 
condensation reaction [16]. An alternative to the strong acid could be lipases. Lipases are a type of enzymes 
that are known to catalyze hydrolysis of lipids. Despite its nature of performing hydrolysis, it can also perform 
esterification with the lack of water in the system as enzymes catalyze the reaction towards equilibrium. By 
changing the reaction media from water to an organic solvent it would eliminate water from the system. 
Thus, pushing the equilibrium favoring esterification [16][17].  
With this approach a different challenge arises; water is the natural environment of lipases and is crucial for 
the lipase function. Water acts as a lubricant and is what makes enzymes flexible. Therefore, a certain amount 
of water is necessary for the enzymatic function [18]. Furthermore, most enzymes lose the majority of the 
enzymatic activity with the transition of reaction media [19]. However, microbial lipases have shown great 
biotechnological potential as they are stable in organic solvents, possess a high enantioselectivity and have 
a broader specificity towards organic substrates [2]. By using lipases as a catalyst, it allows the production to

the esterification reaction are Candida antarctica lipase A (CALA) and Candida antarctica lipase B (CALB) [20]. 
1.3 Candida antarctica Lipases 
Candida antarctica is a basidiomycetous yeast found in a lake in Antarctica. From Candida antarctica two 
lipases were isolated and purified named Candida antarctica lipase A and Candida antarctica lipase B (CALA 
and CALB) and are mainly expressed by Aspergillus Oryzae [20]. 
1.3.1 Lipase A 
CALA is a monomeric lipase consisting of a single chain with the length of 462 amino acids and a mass of 
49.3kDa. It has a pH optimum between 7 and 9 with an isoelectric point (pI) of 7.5. CALA has the enzyme 
commision(EC) number 3.1.1.3. CALA is thermostable up to 90oC but works optimally in the range of 40-70oC 
and is sold under the commercial name NovoCor AD L or Novozym 735 [21][22]. CALA has a preference of 
short chain triglycerides the length of 4-8 carbon atoms. CALA can interact with a wide range of substrates 
and can both catalyze condensation and hydrolysis reactions dependent on the environment. The sequence 
consists of a signal peptide, at the amino acids 1-21, and the main chain, at the amino acids 22-462 seen on 
figure 5 [20][21].

The mechanism in its name Ping Pong indicates that the substrate binding and product release occurs in a 
certain order. The Bi Bi is an indication that two substrates are used, and two products are formed [30]. A

1.3.3 CALA vs CALB 
As mentioned, CALA and CALB are both part of the 𝛼 /𝛽 hydrolase family and both have the active site 
catalytic triads of asp, ser and his. CALA does have a discrete lid consisting of the 𝛼 5-10 and CALB is suspected 
of having a lid-like structure in its structural 𝛼-5 and 𝛼-10. CALA is significantly larger at a mass of 49.3kDa in 
comparison to CALB 35.5kDa. CALA pI is at 7.5 whereas CALB is at 6.0. Both catalyzes condensation and 
hydrolysis reactions as they are both part of the hydrolase family and show activity in solvents because of 
their lipase properties [20][21][26]. 
1.3.4 Waters’ interaction with enzyme surfaces in solvents 
With the integration of solvents as reaction media for lipases it is important to consider how water acts when 
encountering hydrophobic chemicals. As mentioned earlier water is crucial for lubricating the lipase and 
providing the hydrophobic layer for interfacial activation [25][32]. Theoretically by MD simulations, when 
using solvents with different polarity it will affect how the water acts at the surface of the lipase. The solvents 
non-polar nature will force the water molecules towards polar areas. The surface area of a lipase consists of 
polar and non-polar patches which causes water to migrate towards areas with polar residues on the surface. 
In general, at low water activity the water forms a spanning network around the lipase at certain “hot spots”. 
At high water activity the hot spots grow or percolate and if they are too close to the active site, the water 
might prevent the substrate from entering [4][32].

1.4 Molecular Dynamics Simulations 
Molecular dynamics (MD) simulation is a method used to acquire information about a system made of 
molecules/atoms. MD simulations is used to analyze biomolecular systems, as this can provide detailed 
molecular complementary data to experimental results. It is difficult to acquire experimental data that 
describe the molecular interactions in a biomolecular system. MD is therefore quite useful as it may provide 
detailed information about the molecular interactions that experimental data can’t provide, however only 
theoretical. 
The principle of MD is to use an appropriate forcefield, initial positions (𝑟𝑖) and velocities (𝑣𝑖) to calculate the 
new velocities and positions of the atoms in the system. This is the generation of a phase space trajectory. It 
is also required to define the boundary conditions and statistical mechanics or ensembles. For a system of 𝑁 
atoms the following (1) may describe the force acted upon each individual atom:

1.4.2 Periodic boundary conditions 
When performing MD simulations, it is important to define the boundary conditions. In the first MD 
simulations a cubic box would be defined, and the atoms would move around in this box being reflected by 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
Page 24 of 124 
 
 
the wall. However, this does not represent reality as simulations is in the thousands of atoms whereas in 
laboratory scale it is at Avogadro’s number. In principle it would be the same as having a miniscule beaker 
filled with atoms which hardly reflect the reality as collisions would occur more frequent, than in reality. The 
frequent collisions would then disturb the systems properties which is known as finite-size effects. To 
overcome finite-size effects, periodic boundary conditions (PBC) was created [34].  
PBC is a method to extend the system created for the simulation without a box to constrain the system. The 
extension is simply infinite copies of itself displaced appropriately in all three cartesian directions creating a 
system where the original atoms can move freely between the copies. This is a more correct representation 
as the system now emulates bulk conditions where each atom is surrounded by approximately infinite atoms. 
The copies work by allowing original atoms to move out of the original box and into a copy box. Similarly, a 
copy atom then replaces the original atom in the original box from the opposite side. The spot will seem 
empty in the simulation as the copies aren’t shown in the simulation. In this way a more accurate simulation 
of the system can be made as there are no real boundary [34]. PBC conditions can be seen in figure 16. 
 
Figure 16: The periodic boundary conditions and how copy atoms may replace original atoms in the original box. The copy atoms are in light green 
and the original atoms in a bright blue. This figure was adapted from [34]. 
However, PBC doesn’t come without its faults. If one does not limit the distance of intermolecular 
interactions, two problems will occur. First the number of calculations required would be infinite and a MD 
simulation would therefore never compute. Second an atom would be able to interact multiple times with 
itself and other already accounted for atoms. This is unnatural and should not happen. To limit intermolecular 
interactions the distance or cutoff radius should at least be set so the atom cannot interact with itself. For a 
cube the cutoff should be less than half the length of the box shortest side 𝑟𝑐< 𝑙

1.4.4 Thermodynamic Ensembles 
When running molecular dynamics, it is preferable to keep some values constant. When integrating (1) into 
the system it will make an ensemble of constant volume V, atoms N and the total energy E also called the 
microcanonical ensemble. The problem with the NVE ensemble is that even though the total energy is 
constant, the kinetic and potential energy might deviate depending on the equilibrium of the system. This in 
turn means that the conditions of pressure and temperature is unknown in this kind of system until the run 
is performed. It can therefore be more favorable to use constant temperature (NVT) or also constant pressure 
(NPT) ensemble to have more control over the conditions in the system [33]. 
To control the temperature there are multiple different ways but only two will be mentioned here. First 
velocity scaling which is a very crude method. As the name implies it scales all the velocities by a factor every 
couple of steps in the simulation. The trajectory created does not sample the phase space which causes issues 
for MD simulations. A different method is using a thermostat such as the Langevin thermostat. The most 
common thermostats connect the system to a heat bath which will control the temperature of the system. 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
Page 27 of 124 
 
 
The coupling of a heat bath to the system allows for scaling of the velocities in the system as the system 
converges towards the heat bath temperature. Afterwards the heat bath will keep the temperature constant 
for the rest of the simulation. The NVT ensemble with a thermostat is useful when heating the system to a 
desired temperature [33][34].  
For constant pressure a similar concept is used where the rescaling occurs of the volume and not the 
velocities in a chosen barostat, such as the Monte Carlo barostat. The NPT ensemble is especially useful when 
wanting to produce comparable conditions to experimental conditions. The reasoning being that the 
temperature and pressure can be set comparable to experimental conditions [33]. 
1.4.5 Computational force saving 
As mentioned earlier, one of the biggest restrictions of MD simulations is the computational force. For each 
atom between each timestep, the distance between every other atom in the system, an evaluation of the 
forces acted upon the atom and then finally calculate the new velocities and positions for the given atom. 
This results in an exponentially increasing number of calculations for each additional atom in a MD 
simulation. Luckily there are multiple ways to constrain the system in a proper way to save computational 
time. 
When PBC is defined unto a system and a cutoff value is chosen one can choose a smaller cutoff value than 
required to limit the number of interactions between the atoms. By limiting the intermolecular interactions 
to an appropriately small cutoff value. An appropriate value would be when the intermolecular forces can be 
approximated to zero for the least inappropriate effect. With the help of Ewald summation for coulomb 
interactions the cutoff value can be quite small without too large fluctuations from reality [33].  
A different way to save computational time is trying to limit the fastest movements in the simulation. The 
timestep in MD simulations is required to be 10 times smaller than the fastest movement in the system. The 
reasoning being that if the timestep is too large one atom might come too close to a different atom causing 
large fluctuations in the system. The fastest movement in the system is going to be the oscillation of bonds 
between hydrogen and other atoms. One can restrain these with the SHAKE algorithm limiting these 
oscillations. This will slow down the movement enough to be able to choose a larger time step in the system. 
By having a larger timestep the amount of timesteps needed for the full run is reduced and therefore 
computational time is saved [33][34].

probably not being in their natural state. It was therefore to try and get the structures into a more natural 
conformity at a lower energy. The script used for minimization can be seen below in figure 20.

From figure 20 the parameter imin=1 makes it an energy minimization. Maxcyc is the maximum number of 
cycles run in the minimization step at 5000. Ncyc is the number of cycles run before the minimization goes 
from steepest descent to conjugate gradient. In total for each simulation, 3 different runs were made with 
varying ncyc at 1000, 1500 and 1800 which might give different results depending on local and global minima. 
This is the only setting throughout the minimization, heating, equilibration and production that is different 
between the 3 runs. The ntpr defines how often the progress should be printed, at 50 ntpr the progress is 
printed every 50 steps. The cut function defines at what maximum distance van der Waals forces, 
electrostatic forces and “off-diagonal” terms of the generalized Born interactions still affect each other and 
the method used for this is Particle Mesh Ewald (PME). This is set at 12Å to be in line with other literature 
[4][18][32]. Restraintmask specifies restraint molecules when ntr=1, which is the default value in any 
calculation. Restraint_wt is setting how much weight is applied to the restrained atoms limiting their 
movement [48].

A gamma value needs to be assigned gamma_ln=5.0 which is the collision frequency in ps-1. The ig value is 
the seed for a pseudo-random number generator and in the manual should be different each time. The value 
used was ig=-1, which is default as this value will base its seed on the time and date. Therefore, always will 
be different from a previous simulation. The ntc value defines the number of constraints applied to a system. 
The general algorithm is called SHAKE and with an ntc=2 it constrains all bonds involving hydrogen. For the 
most part ntf=ntc which is also the case here. The cut=12 value is mentioned under the minimization step. 
The ntb defines whether there are periodic boundaries or not. The ntb=1 sets the periodic boundaries to be 
in a constant volume. For iwrap=1 it constrains the size of the coordinates. Ioutfm is the formatting of 
coordinate and trajectory files, default ioutfm=1 which is binary and are chosen because the files are faster 
to read and write. The nmropt value is for whether the simulation should make NMR analysis. For nmropt=1 
it will read restraint and weight changes. For TYPE=’TEMP0’ because the simulation should be at constant 
temperature, this command tells the system to first perform the following heating.  ISTEP 1 and 2 define over 
how many steps a given change is applied. The change applied is defined by VALUE1 and 2. In this example 
at ISTEP1=0 then VALUE1=10.0 is in effect. At ISTEP2=150000 the VALUE2=298.15 should be in effect. Over 
these 150000 steps, the system will move closer to VALUE2 from 10K to 298.15K. TYPE=’END’ tells the system 
that when the temperature reaches VALUE2 at ISTEP2 the heating should be stopped, and temperature kept 
constant. The rest of the 350000 steps are used for the system to re-equilibrate itself before pressure 
constraints are set on the system in the next step [48].

2.3.1 Salt beaker preparation 
To prepare the salt beakers a beaker would be filled with 1 cm of water. Afterwards one of the salts were 
poured into the beakers until the solution was saturated. Additional salt was poured in until the mixture 
became a salt slush mixture. This was performed for each salt in individual beakers. These beakers were 
closed with some tin foil while not in use and reused in each experiment. 
2.3.2 Pre-equilibration 
Prior to initiating the reaction, the system needs to be pre-equilibrated to the correct initial water activity. 
The reaction mixture of butyric acid and one of the three solvents was prepared at a concentration of 150mM 
Butyric acid for CALA and 50mM for CALB. For each water activity a container was prepared with a magnet 
for stirring and a lid. The containers were weighed before and after the addition of reaction mixture and 
enzyme. Each container with the reaction mixture was then placed with the lid off in one of the prepared salt 
beakers. A container with organic solvent was also placed in each salt beaker for pre-equilibration to 
accommodate for any solvent evaporated. The salt beaker was then sealed with parafilm and tin foil with 
magnetic stirring at 400 rpm to pre-equilibrate the reaction to the proper water activity over roughly 24 
hours. Four small containers with a lid were also prepared with the same salt solutions. A small vial for pre-
equilibrating 2mL of ethanol for the reactions the next day was placed in each of the small containers as seen 
on figure 25.

3.2.2 Water activity analysis 
As previously described in the method section to find the water activity first the hypothetical RBound value 
needs to be defined in each simulation to find the bulk water. To define RBound one would need to find the 
distance where the average amount of water molecules is constant which can be done by plotting the 
average 𝑥𝑤 against the distance from the protein. Afterwards the amount of water molecules and the bulk 
water fraction 𝑥𝑤 at RBound can be found with an inhouse script. These values are found for the last 1000 
frames or 10ns of production from each simulation in accordance with other literature [4][32].  
The water activity analysis will be presented in the order of CALA in ACN, CALA in isohexane and CALB in

3.3 Relation between MD simulations and enzyme kinetic studies 
MD simulations have shown to be a valuable tool in theoretically understanding the mechanism taking place 
of water in different organic solvents. figures 55 and 31 show the water clusters at increasing number of 
water molecules and experimental specific activity over increasing water activity for CALA in ACN 
respectively. The trend of increasing specific activity from low water activity and decreasing at higher water 
activity seen for figure 31, can be supported by figure 55 with the increase in water cluster size near the 
active site corresponding to 400 water molecules or 𝑎𝑤=0.8042 potentially blocking the substrate. Even 
though this water activity does not correspond to aw(K2SO4)=0.97 the trend of growing water clusters can 
still be observed and will most likely occur at or near 0.97 water activity. 
The figures 57 and 34 show the water clusters at increasing number of water molecules and experimental 
specific activity over water activity for CALA in isohexane respectively. Here too the trend of increasing 
specific activity from low water activity 0.12-0.53 and decreasing at high water activity 0.97 for experiment 
1 figure 34 can be supported by figure 57. As figure 57 shows that water clusters appear near the active site 
as the number of water molecules increases. The growth of the water clusters near the active site is yet to 
be decided as the highest water activity available is 0.5999. However, this trend of growing water clusters at 
increasing water activities have been observed in this project and elsewhere [32]. Experiment 2 is ignored 
due to laboratory errors and the result showing no trend. 
The figures 59 and 36 show the water clusters at increasing number of water molecules and experimental 
specific activity over water activity for CALB in isohexane respectively. For figure 36 the trend of increasing 
specific activity at low water activity and decreasing at higher water activity for experiment 2 can be 
supported by figure 59. As figure 59 shows the water clusters and growth of the water clusters over increasing 
water molecules. Some of these water clusters appear near the active site at low water activity and grows as 
the number of water molecules increases. Figure 59 does not explain the trend of experiment 1 shown on 36 
as the specific activity continually increases with increasing water activity. The in-house results from table 17 
contradicts both these experiments as the specific activity was shown to decreases with increasing water 
activity. It is probably most likely that the in-house results trend of decreasing specific activity with increasing 
water activity is the most probable because the water clusters shown on figure 59 occurs close to the active 
site and is rapidly growing. These water clusters blocking the substrate from entering the active site might 
have an effect much earlier. There is also the case of the laboratory errors which makes the results 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
 
Page 95 of 124 
 
 
questionable. This trend of decrease with water activity might also be the case for CALA in isohexane but due 
to the laboratory errors it is unknown which trend is the most likely.

4 Conclusion 
The enzyme kinetic studies and MD simulations lead to the followin. The specific activity appears to be the 
highest for isohexane as reaction solvent, right after ACN and last MTBE for CALA. This may be supported 
theoretically by the simulations, as interfacial activation for CALA in isohexane for 400 water molecules seem 
to appear with the cavity. Further investigations are needed to validate this outlier. For CALB the results 
found a higher specific activity for isohexane than MTBE when compared to Banik et al. [4]. CALB in isohexane 
had the highest specific activity of all the experimental results gathered in this project. For CALA in either 
MTBE, ACN or isohexane a general trend was found as the specific activity would increase with increasing 
water activity until aw(Mg(NO3)2∙H2O)=0.53 where the specific activity would decrease towards 
aw(K2SO4)=0.97. This may also be supported by the theoretical simulations of CALA in either ACN or 
isohexane, showing water clusters appearing near the active site and growing with increasing water activity. 
The simulations were generally in order with other literature. However, the simulations of CALA in isohexane 
and CALB in isohexane would need a prolonged production of 50 ns to ensure convergence. For the most 
part the increase in water molecules had little to no effect on the structure. Except for the simulation of CALA 
in isohexane 400 water molecules. Furthermore, due to the errors of the experimental work it would be 
required to re-do all the enzyme kinetic experiments to ensure reliable results. This would be required with 
an altered protocol to ensure complete inactivation and newly purified proteins of CALA and CALB to correct 
the errors.
