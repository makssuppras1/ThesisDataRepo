Abstract
This thesis contains a study on the eﬀects of unsteady aerody-
namics upon the vibration of airfoil sections. The report begins with a review of
the unsteady aerodynamics and dynamic stall model used and the presentation of
some tests conducted to ensure the code developed is returning correct answers.
A review of the dynamics problem as related to the vibration and corresponding
damping of wind turbine blades which can lead to a instability condition is then
made using quasi-steady aerodynamics. The two methods that were used to
obtain the answer to the system of diﬀerential equations of the complete dynamic
model are then explained before the results of the simulations are presented and
discussed. The results are summarized in a series of maps where the aerodynamic
damping is given as a function of angle of attack and direction of vibration in
addition to the reduced frequency.

Introduction
Introduction
Mechanical vibrating systems often exhibit inherent damping characteristics that results
in acceptable levels of vibration. Structural damping usually comes from friction, internal
material properties or special devices designed to increase the mechanical damping available.
If a structure is immersed in a ﬂuid stream, aerodynamic forces can develop and couple
with the motion of the structure that is vibrating, in what is known as ﬂuid-structure
interaction. In many cases, the aerodynamic forces act in a way as to interfere and often
reduce the eﬀective damping that is acting on the structure, resulting in increased levels of
vibration or even, in extreme cases, when the eﬀective damping becomes negative, result
in divergent oscillations that can lead to catastrophic results in very short time. Examples
of ﬂuid-structure interaction include galloping of transmission lines due to ice build-up,
vibrations set up by Von Karman vortex trail and others.
Aircraft wings and tail surfaces may be subjected to one type of vibration instability
known as ﬂutter, studied under the broad area of aeroelasticity. The occurrence of stall-
induced vibrations or stall ﬂutter in aircraft propellers, helicopter rotors and in compressor
blades in gas turbines are another example of aeroelastic instability.
Stall-induced vibrations have also been experienced in wind turbine blades, with the ﬁrst
recorded measurements being made in the early 1980’s on stall-regulated wind turbines.
Since then, eﬀorts have been made to understand the problem in depth, see for example Ref.
[1], an introductory survey on the subject can be found in [2].
Most of the related research made use of quasi-steady aerodynamics, however, since the
problem involve both a non-steady ﬂuid ﬂow situation and operation at a stalled condition,
improvements can be expected if unsteady aerodynamics coupled with a realistic dynamic
stall model are used. While the development of unsteady aerodynamics reached a mature
state by the time of II World War, early studies on the dynamic stall phenomena were based
on experimental methods [3]; the development of analytical dynamic stall models began to
appear only decades later. A short review of current dynamic stall models can be found in
[4], a Risø National Laboratory development based on one of these models, intended for use
in wind turbine blade applications and described in [5] is used in the present report as a
complete dynamic stall model.
The purpose of this report is to study the eﬀect of using this unsteady aerodynamics
and dynamic stall model upon the calculation of aerodynamic forces which in turn are used
to determine the eﬀective damping acting on an airfoil section. Although stall-induced
vibrations are mainly associated to an edgewise direction of vibration, calculations are made
for the complete range of directions not only because this has an important eﬀect on the
aerodynamic damping but because blades can be intentionally designed to vibrate in certain
directions as to avoid the most unfavorable directions.

Review of the aerodynamic model
1. Review of the aerodynamic model
Introduction
When an aerodynamic section is undergoing arbitrary motions in a ﬂuid, quasi-steady
aerodynamic theories are unsatisfactory in describing the resulting aerodynamic forces. The
occurrence of ﬂutter in aircraft wings led the aeronautical engineering community to develop
theories capable of modeling the ﬂow in these cases, Theodorsen [8] was the ﬁrst to arrive at
a satisfactory solution back in 1930’s. Based on a potential ﬂow approximation, the theory
is limited to linear regions below stall, the extension into the stalled region was prompted by
the occurrence of stall ﬂutter in propellers, compressor blades and helicopter rotors, with
the ﬁrst analytical dynamic stall models appearing only in the late 1970’s.
The Beddoes-Leishman (B-L) unsteady aerodynamics and dynamic stall model described
in Risø-R-1354 [5] is used in this report. It is a formulation optimized for wind turbine
applications where compressibility eﬀects are not present and ﬂow separation initiated from
leading edge can be neglected due to the use of airfoils having relative thickness greater
than 15 percent. On the other hand, the model includes the eﬀects of unsteady lift due
to a time-varying ﬂow velocity, and is thus capable of analysing vibrations in lead-lag, for
example.
The model is based on a space-state formulation of the equations describing the unsteady
circulation around the airfoil in addition to the dynamics of the trailing-edge separation.
This Chapter presents a brief review of the B-L aerodynamic model in addition to some
tests that were made to make sure the implementation of the code was satisfactory.

Potential ﬂow
The unsteady lift on an airfoil performing harmonic motion can be determined by taking
into account the eﬀects of the vortex shedding history in the wake. The formulation proposed
by Theodorsen is based on a frequency response approach using special functions of the
Bessel type and is normally used in vibration problems of the ﬂutter type and in studies
dealing with response in random turbulence. A diﬀerent, time-based approach along the
lines developed by Wagner and K¨ussner is more suited to problems like the present one in
which a solution based on diﬀerential equations are sought. In any case, both Theodorsen
in 1934 and K¨ussner in 1936 showed that the vertical velocity at the three-quarter point
determines the circulation force on the airfoil undergoing oscillatory motions.

3. Stationary periodic solutions
Introduction
In this chapter the unsteady aerodynamic model described in Chapter 1 will be used
to determine the aerodynamic properties of an airfoil subjected to an oscillating motion
as described in Chapter 2 with the objective of determining the aerodynamic damping
properties of the airfoil section undergoing this motion. Although the motion itself is a
single degree of freedom lead-lag type, the angle formed in relation to the inﬂow direction
results additionally, in a continuous variation in angle of attack, inﬂuencing the resulting
aerodynamic properties like lift and drag and have to be feedback to the aerodynamic model.
Two methods were used to arrive at a solution for the state variables, which in turn
determines the unsteady aerodynamic coeﬃcients. The ﬁrst involved solving numerically
the set of ordinary diﬀerential equations and the second transforming the problem into an
equivalent boundary-value problem then solved using ﬁnite-diﬀerences techniques.
Time integration
The non-linearity of the problem requires the damping determination to be made based
on the work done by the resulting aerodynamic forces acting in the direction of motion over
the duration of a complete vibration cycle. If the resulting work done is negative, this means
that energy should be added to the system to keep the motion, meaning that the system
has positive damping, on the other hand if the work done is positive, this means that the
system has energy to release, resulting in an ever increasing amplitude of motion or negative
damping.

forces over one cycle and the damping coeﬃcient. This can be done in the following way,

Introduction
In this chapter the damping calculations based on the unsteady aerodynamics model are
presented. First, a series of preliminary results are presented having the objective of making
clear the methods used behind the ﬁnal calculations, then a series of curves showing the
variation of damping against the direction of vibration at a few selected angles of attack
are presented where both time integration (TI) and ﬁnite-diﬀerences (FD) methods used
are shown for comparison, the curve corresponding to the quasi-steady results is given for
reference and an explanation for the behavior of the damping curves is also given. Finally, a
series of maps showing the variation of damping at an extended range of angles of attack is
presented. It is hoped that these curves may be used as a guidance in actual design work.
Examples of resulting time simulations to show the variation of the force input Fx and the
resulting work performed by this force for 0 and 10 deg. angle of attack at diﬀerent direction
of vibrations θ are presented in Figures 4.1 and 4.2. Parameters used in this example were:
an amplitude a = 1 m, a chord c = 1 m, a frequency f = 1 Hz, and a inﬂow speed W0 = 80
m/s resulting in a reduced frequency k = 0.04. Results are presented for both calculation
methods, in the case of time integration the simulation was run during four cycles allowing
the state variables to arrive at suﬃciently converged values. The results obtained using
ﬁnite-diﬀerences are superposed on the ﬁnal cycle obtained with time integration showing
that the agreement obtained between the two methods was very good.
It is possible to see that the variation in force increases in magnitude as the direction of
vibration moves away from the rotor plane, or more precisely as the angle formed with the
steady state inﬂow angle increases, due to a greater variation in angle of attack and that the
ﬁnal accumulated work can sometimes be only a fraction of the maximum value reached at
the half-cycle region.
In the case of the time integration method it was necessary to zero the accumulated work
after each completed cycle in a way that in the end the work done on the cycle is based only
in the ﬁnal, converged values. Another remark in this connection, when working with the
time integration method is that a certain number of cycles must be simulated in order for
the equations to arrive at a steady-state condition in the sense that the state variables at
a given phase angle along the cycle must be the same among subsequent cycles. It should
be noted however, that this has no relation to the mechanical system having reached a
steady-state oscillation as the equations only describe the system oscillating at that speciﬁed
amplitude which is supposed to be constant during one cycle. In reality amplitudes are going
to decrease or increase depending if the damping is positive or negative, only if the damping
happens to be zero, the amplitudes will be constant along time.

Analysis of the results
The systematic generation of curves showing the aerodynamic damping coeﬃcient at
diﬀerent angles of attack α sweeping the whole range of directions of vibrations θ was made
initially using both time integration (TI) and ﬁnite-diﬀerences (FD) methods. While it
could be said that satisfactory results might be obtained using either method, convergence
problems were experienced when using the ﬁrst method, in cases the amplitude of vibration
fell below a certain amount, typically around a/c = 0.1, even in other situations, it was
often seen that convergence would not get improved when an increase in number of cycles
were attempted, several of the available Matlab ODE solvers working at various tolerances
were tested, but the behavior did not seem to change signiﬁcantly. Convergence criteria did
not apply when using the ﬁnite-diﬀerences (FD) method as a ﬁnal solution is obtained at
once, it was seen that the method would work at any level of amplitude tested, a/c = 0.1,
a/c = 0.01 or even a/c = 0.001 and that the solution was obtained much faster than with
the ﬁrst method. When using the FD method a grid comprising 1000 elements were used
through the calculations.
In Figures 4.9 to 4.20, curves show the damping results for angles of attack equal to 0, 10
and 20 deg. at increasing reduced frequencies, in each case the results obtained using both
TI and FD methods are compared with the results obtained previously using quasi-steady
aerodynamics. In most cases the agreement between the two methods was good with the
exception of some points within the 20 deg. angle of attack, reduced amplitude (a/c = 0.1)
curves.
Beginning with the lowest frequency, it can be seen that the damping values stay close to
those found using the linearization approach, then as the frequency is increased, a reduction
in damping takes place. In the ﬁrst group of ﬁgures, for an angle of attack equal to 0 deg.
damping values are found to be independent of the amplitude to chord ratio, suggesting the
problem to be linear as far as the airfoil is oscillating in the linear range of the lift curve.
A similar pattern is seen for an angle of attack of 10 deg., in this case however, some
inﬂuence from the amplitude is seen at higher frequencies, indicating that some non-linearity
is already taking place, indeed at higher frequencies, the variation in angle of attack may be
such as to cause part of the cycle to fall outside the linear range of the lift curve.
At an angle of attack of 20 deg. the regions in which the damping is negative decrease in
absolute magnitude as the frequency is increased, becoming less negative or even reversing
signal and becoming positive at directions of vibration θ suﬃcient high, as the whole damping
curve is shifted upwards.
The loss of damping at higher frequencies occurs chieﬂy due to the reduction in the
eﬀective lift curve slope that takes place as the frequency in increased. Since the lift curve
slope is a main contributing factor to the damping coeﬃcient achieved, especially at lower
angles of attack, a reduction in this parameter will reﬂect strongly in the ﬁnal result.
The reduction in lift curve slope veriﬁed when an airfoil is subjected to pitching oscillations
can be explained either in terms of the unsteady aerodynamic model based on the time-

A design criteria may be set up simply by avoiding regions in which the damping is
negative, knowing where the borderline of this limiting condition is located deﬁnes the
possible operating regions fulﬁlling this design criteria, Figures 4.35 and 4.36 shows the
variation of the neutral damping boundaries as a function of the reduced frequency. For the
range of angles of attack considered, it can be seen that below a certain angle of attack, in
this case about 3 deg. damping is always positive, between this and an angle of attack of
about 12 deg. a wedge shaped region centered around the rotor plane direction is formed,
in which damping is negative. According to what has been shown in Chapter 2, at lower
angles of attack positive damping comes mainly from the positive lift curve slope, the only
parameter with magnitude to decrease damping is the lift coeﬃcient itself but at lower angles
of attack it is too small to cancel the contribution from the lift curve slope, as the angle
of attack increases, its contribution will become the dominant factor bringing the damping
to negative values, since the damping is smaller close to the rotor plane, the inversion will
begin close to this area. Above this angle of attack the instability region widens and becomes
dependent on the vibration frequency when the direction of vibration assumes a negative
value, i.e., at the left side of Figure 4.35, the other boundary that runs close to the rotor
plane, i.e., at a direction of vibration close to zero, is much less aﬀected by the frequency.
This region can be seen in more detail in Figure 4.36 where only a narrow range of direction
of vibrations θ close to the rotor plane is shown.
During analysis of force and speed of vibration plots, it was veriﬁed that at the lower
angles of attack the resulting force and speed in the direction of vibration were always
out-of-phase at or close to 180 deg., and that this condition always lead to a negative ﬁnal
accumulated work. On the other hand, as the angle of attack is increased, for certain
directions of vibration θ, the resulting force and speed of vibration became in-phase at or
close to 0 deg. resulting in a positive ﬁnal accumulated work. Accordingly neutral damping
boundaries are characterized by having force and speed of vibration displaced by a 90 deg.
phase.

Conclusion
5. Conclusion
A study on the directional vibration of airfoil sections was made in which a complete
unsteady aerodynamics and dynamic stall model was used in place of the quasi-steady
aerodynamics. It was then possible to understand the eﬀects of the vibration frequency
upon the aerodynamic forces acting on the airfoil which in turn changed the damping
characteristics of the system.
Determination of the damping came from the evaluation of the energy gain or lost during
one vibration cycle. Two diﬀerent methods were used to solve the system of ODE describing
the problem, the resulting state variables were used to ﬁnd the resulting force acting in the
direction of motion which was then integrated to ﬁnd the work done.
From the two methods used in arrive at the solution, time integration and ﬁnite-diﬀerences,
the later was found to be more ﬂexible, faster and more precise and was thus used in the
generation of the maps that summarize the results of the ﬁndings.
It was determined that there is a sensible decrease in the aerodynamic damping as the
frequency is increased if the angle of attack is limited to the linear range of the lift curve
slope with the eﬀect being more pronounced as the direction of vibration moves away from
the rotor plane. At higher angles of attack the damping curve is shifted upwards bringing
portions of it to the positive range. The reason for the change in damping is due to the
reduction on the equivalent lift curve slope of an airfoil oscillating in pitch.
A series of maps where the aerodynamic damping is given as a function of angle of attack
and the direction of vibration, covering a range of reduced frequency from k = 0.04 to
k = 0.16, summarizes the results in graphic form.
Suggestions for future work include the addition of a second degree of freedom with the
inclusion of torsion, a systematic study of diﬀerent airfoil trying to correlate aerodynamic
characteristics with the resulting aerodynamic damping and use of speciﬁc indicial functions
φ tailored to each airfoil under study, since the one used was intended originally for ﬂat
plates.
