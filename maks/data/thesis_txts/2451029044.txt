Preface and acknowledgements
My Master’s thesis is structured as an article. Since it deals with an emerging ﬁeld within absolute sustainability,
the work done here may be interesting for other researchers in various ﬁelds of sustainability assessment, and the
aim is to submit this article to the Journal of Industrial Ecology. The article format, seems like a viable and concise
way of communicating to an interested audience. As a result of this decision, only the key ﬁndings of this work are
included in the article, while additional results and supporting information can be found in appendices to ensure
full transparency. There will be references to the supporting information throughout the article where relevant. The
literature review conducted has been summarised in a literature matrix and is attached in an Excel ﬁle in its entirety.

ﬁle. The thesis is for 30 ECTS and constitutes the ﬁnal leg of my MSc in Environmental Engineering. The work

Abstract
With human impacts on the Earth System increasing, action must be taken in order for the Planet to maintain
the climate stability that has characterized the Holocene. The Planetary Boundary framework assesses planetary
thresholds and human pressures in 9 environmental categories of assumed relevance for the planetary climate and
Earth system processes, also quantifying the safe operating space within each. However, when aiming to make the
concept operational for decision support it is unclear how this safe operating space should be shared between agents.
This study proposes a two step approach, where a pre-deﬁned global threshold such as a planetary boundary is ﬁrst
downscaled to the individual level using ethical allocation principles and next scaled up to a higher level with upscaling
methods, using the company level as an example (see ﬁgure 1).
For this purpose, I use two of the planetary boundaries; climate change and biogeochemical ﬂows, to show how
the choices of allocation principle and upscaling method impact the share of the safe operating space assigned to a
company, and thereby also the absolute sustainability status of the company in relation to a given planetary boundary.
I develop a framework to assess absolute sustainability at a company level within the planetary boundary framework
testing 4 allocation principles and 2 upscaling methods, using a case study based on the European dairy sector as proof
of concept. The case study shows that none of the tested scenarios assess the company to be absolutely sustainable,
although 5 of the 40 scenarios fall within the area of uncertainty of absolute sustainability. Further, I demonstrate
that the choice of upscaling method has larger inﬂuence on the results than the choice of allocation principle. A
sensitivity and uncertainty analysis shows that the inﬂuence of the method variables are of similar magnitude, and
that the results are reasonably robust to uncertainties.
Key words: absolute sustainability assessment, planetary boundaries, allocation principles, scaling, environmental
thresholds, safe operating space, ethics

Glossary and abbreviations
Absolute sustainability, absolutely sustainable Not just better but good enough in terms of acting within the
deﬁned safe operating space. Sustainability per se, in contrast to relative sustainability. In this study the focus is
solely on environmental sustainability, as deﬁned by the planetary boundaries.
Absolute sustainability assessment The assessment of whether an activity or an agent is absolutely sustainable,
given choices of global thresholds, allocation principles, and upscaling methods. In this paper the focus is solely on
environmental sustainability, as deﬁned by the planetary boundaries.
Absolute sustainability ratio The actual impact of an agent divided by the determined sustainable impact of the
same agent. The sustainable impact is deﬁned as the allocated share of the safe operating space in this study.
Agent Philosophical term for actor or stakeholder. Used as a general term when the agent is unspeciﬁed.
Allocation principle Ethical principle to determine the distribution of something between agents. Not to be confused
with the LCA term allocation. The ﬁrst step in the two step process proposed in this study, where the next step is
the upscaling method.
Carrying capacity The maximum sustained environmental intervention a natural system can withstand without
experiencing negative changes in structure or functioning that are diﬃcult or impossible to revert
Eco-eﬃciency The value of a product or service divided by its environmental impact.
Equal per capita The ethical view that every individual human has the same right to whatever is being shared.
Used as one of the proposed allocation principles in this study.
Final consumption expenditure An economic expression of consumer preference based on the percentage of income
spent on a product or service (FCE).
Global threshold A threshold within which it is deemed safe to operate and beyond which it is deemed unsafe, and
Earth system processes may not proceed as currently. In this study only environmental thresholds are considered.
Grandfathering The ethical view that rights to whatever is being shared should be shared based on the status quo.
Used as one of the proposed allocation principles in this study.
Green incentive An upscaling method developed in this study prioritising eco-eﬃcient agents.
Greenhouse gas (GHG) A gass contributing to global warming through the greenhouse eﬀect.
Historical responsibility The ethical view that agents with lower historical responsibility in terms of impacting
the Earth system should be favoured when sharing whatever is being shared. Used as one of the proposed allocation
principles in this study.
Holocene The current geological epoch of the Earth, supporting stable climate conditions as we know them.
IPCC The intergovernmental panel on climate change.
Life cycle assessment (LCA) A well established tool for assessing (relative) environmental sustainability of products
and services .
Monte Carlo simulation A method for conducting uncertainty analysis which consists of varying multiple input
parameters simultaneously within their respective uncertainty ranges to simulate the resulting probability distribution
of the output.
Planetary boundary (PB) A quantiﬁable global environmental threshold.
The planetary boundary framework
operates with nine environmental thresholds and associated boundaries.
ppm Parts per million. Used on volume basis as standard unit for concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere.
Polluter pays The principle that whoever is responsible for the pollution is also the one responsible for paying the
price for it (eg. the price of cleaning it up).
Precautionary principle A principle used to justify conservative or precautionary action based on the principle that
if a very undesirable outcome is possible, it is worth taking preventive actions despite uncertainty.
Prioritarianism The ethical view that those who are most in need should be prioritized when allocating shares, since
a beneﬁt to an individual has greater moral value the worse oﬀthe individual is.

Introduction
As global population and living standards surge, increasing human pressures on the environment threaten to alter the
Earth system as we know it (IPCC, 2018). In response, the urgency of a transition towards sustainable development
was underlined in the most recent IPCC report, the so called 1.5 °C report, calling for immediate, comprehensive,
and ambitious measures in order to keep global warming at 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels (IPCC, 2018). Global
warming, while often the main environmental impact addressed in environmental sustainability assessments, is just one
consequence of multiple human pressures on the Earth System. A more holistic view is provided by the the Planetary
Boundary (PB) framework, which quantiﬁes planetary thresholds and corresponding human pressures on 91 separate
but interlinked global environmental processes (see ﬁgure 8 in the supplementary material (SM) (Rockstr¨om et al.,
2009). Steﬀen et al. (2015) assess that staying within these boundaries will maintain the planet in the Holocene (the
state of the planet in which humans have developed and thrived so far), while violating one or more PBs increases the
risk of pushing the planet out of its current state (Steﬀen et al., 2015). Furthermore, the interconnectedness of the
PBs means that transgressing one boundary may mean transgressing others as well (Rockstr¨om et al., 2009), (Ryberg
et al., 2018). Confer (Ryberg et al., 2016), the PB framework is gaining increasing inﬂuence in the scientiﬁc literature
as well as in policy making and in industry and global organisations.
While the PBs were not originally introduced to be operationalized at smaller scales than the planet, but rather
to quantify and monitor the environmental state of the Earth, several attempts have been made at downscaling the
PBs in order to apply them for eg. policy - or local decision making (eg. (Ryberg et al., 2018), (Heck et al., 2018),
(Hossain et al., 2017), (McLaughlin, 2018), (Cooper og Dearing, 2019), (Teah et al., 2016), (Dao et al., 2018), see
literature review in the SM in tables 10 and 11). These attempts have mainly been directing their eﬀorts towards the
national or regional scale. However, a signiﬁcant group of agents has so far been largely overlooked in the literature
on operationalizing the PBs; namely companies. Companies can be signiﬁcant and swift drivers of change, as already
showcased by the large number committing to various sustainability reporting schemes such as The Gold Standard, the
Global Reporting Initiative (GRI), and ISO-standards. Further, companies can proﬁt from this shift as the demand
for environmentally sustainable products and services increases and companies compete to win market shares in their
ﬁeld. While downscaling the PBs to a national or regional level is often simply modelled in direct proportion to the
population in the given geographical area, downscaling to a company level requires further consideration.
However, the assessment of sustainability is often done in a relative manner - examples of methods for assessing
it are life cycle assessment and eco-eﬃciency, which quantify the environmental sustainability of similar products or
services in relation to each other. While one product may be more sustainable than the other, these assessments fail
to address whether the product is sustainable in the absolute sense of the term, (Bjørn og Hauschild, 2013) (Andersen
et al., 2019). What does it mean then, to be absolutely sustainable? This article argues that a company, or in fact
any agent, can only be absolutely sustainable if they do not exceed their share of the safe operating space deﬁned by
a quantitative set of thresholds, such as the PBs (Andersen et al., 2019). However, absolute sustainability assessment
is complicated by the string of normative choices which make up the backbone of the assessment and are crucial to
the results: how should we deﬁne the type or size of currency which is to be shared, making up the so called safe
operating space (SOS)? Once agreed upon, how should the SOS be distributed, between which actors, and on which
time scale? As noted by (Ryberg et al., 2018) and (Gardiner, 2010), such issues are interdisciplinary in their scope.
Aside from the quantitative framework provided by the PBs, the ethical framework plays an indisputable role in terms
of allocating shares of the safe operating space between agents - a key element in the downscaling process. The need
for further research in this ﬁeld has been stressed in recent literature (eg. (H¨ayh¨a et al., 2016), (Clift et al., 2017),
(McLaughlin, 2018), (Heck et al., 2018), (Ryberg et al., 2018)). The development of sustainability assessment is even
addressed as a systemic goal by the ﬁnal of the 169 targets behind the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals

development...”. Lack of regulation arguably leaves companies to share in a tragedy of the commons (Hardin, 1968)
type scenario where each company can claim as large a share of the SOS as they like or as possible. Allocation has
been attempted according to the ”grandfathering” scheme where existing actors receive rights to the SOS according to

facilitates the allocation of shares of the SOS.
This article addresses some of these needs by developing and testing a framework and associated methodology to
assess the absolute sustainability of a company in the framework of the PBs. The focus is on two of the PBs, namely
climate change and biogeochemical ﬂows, as well as on mapping the associated ethical challenges of allocating shares
of the identiﬁed SOS. The outcome is a framework and a tool which allows companies to assess their sustainability in
absolute terms in the light the PBs and a set of ethical allocation principles as well as methods for scaling up from
the individual. The consistent use of the developed framework would help guide companies towards managing their
environmental impacts in a manner that allows us to keep total environmental pressures within the boundaries of
absolute sustainability.
5
Framework for absolute sustainability assessment of companies
5.1
Overall methodology
This section demonstrates the steps which must be taken in order to conduct absolute environmental sustainabil-
ity assessment at company level with the developed framework, and highlights important choices and assumptions.
Throughout this study the focus is on environmental sustainability, with other interpretations of sustainability (eg.
economic or social) being outside the scope of this work. An overview of the assumptions made in this study can be
found in the SM. A conceptual model of the developed framework is provided in ﬁgure 2 for overview.
There are three overarching categories of choices to be made within the proposed assessment framework, shown
as the ﬁrst three vertical category boxes within the dashed line in ﬁgure 2. The ﬁrst category concerns the choice
of absolute thresholds against which the absolute sustainability assessment will be made. This could be some of the
United Nation’s SDGs or speciﬁc targets, the Paris agreement 2 °C goal, or, as in this case, the PBs. This study
focuses on two of the nine PBs (climate change and biogeochemical ﬂows). Once an absolute threshold is determined,
it is possible to determine a safe operating space and thus assess sustainability in terms of whether this threshold
is transgressed or not. The second category concerns the choice of allocation principle(s), including determining the
dimensions of allocation pattern, currency, target, scope, and time span. The allocation happens from the global to
the individual level, as will be justiﬁed in the following. The third category concerns the choice of upscaling level and
method. This category is relevant if the allocation of a safe operating space is to happen at any other level than the
individual, such as nation or sector. Here the company level has been chosen and the scaling method is based on ﬁnal
consumption expenditure (FCE) of the company’s customers, as will be elaborated in the following. Each category
will be examined in depth in the following sections. The choices made in each category are normative and crucial for
the results. Therefore, they must be explained and well documented for transparency.

modules and provide quantitative results based on the decisions and assumptions made as well as the input data and

In this study the underlying assumption is that no other agents than individual human beings have moral rights to
be allocated a share of whatever is being shared, eg. the SOS of a PB (Andersen et al., 2019). An ethical elaboration
is in order here. Why is sustainability such a widely accepted goal among humans? The frequently cited Brundtland
deﬁnition of sustainable development (UnitedNations, 1987) 2 is anthropocentric in the sense that it revolves around
the fulﬁllment of human needs. In other words sustainability is arguably not perceived as an intrinsic value in itself
but rather a goal by virtue of its ability to fulﬁl the needs of humans. While other views have also been defended in the
literature, such as the view that all fauna and ﬂora have rights along with humans (Naess, 2005), the anthropocentric
and more conventionally accepted motivation behind sustainable development is assumed in this study. Thus any
other agent, be it a nation, a product, or a company, can only obtain rights by virtue of the individual. Therefore, it
is deemed natural that the ﬁrst step in allocating shares of the SOS must always be to downscale to the level of the
individual.

Environmental thresholds: using the Planetary Boundaries to deﬁne the safe op-
erating space
Allocating rights to impact the Earth starts with deﬁning the safe operating space.
This can also be viewed as
the Earth’s carrying capacity, referring to the maximum sustained environmental intervention a natural system can
withstand without experiencing negative changes in structure or functioning that are diﬃcult or impossible to revert
(Bjørn og Hauschild, 2015). In this study the PB framework has been chosen to provide environmental thresholds. The
PBs provide a well established quantitative framework that deﬁnes thresholds before tipping points for 9 environmental
impact categories.
Since the Earth does not respond linearly to environmental impacts, and since there is some
uncertainty about when irreversible change has occurred, the lower bound of the range of uncertainty has been chosen
as the PB, guided by the precautionary principle (see table 1) (Rockstr¨om et al., 2009).
Both the climate change and biogeochemical ﬂows PBs have already been transgressed (see table 1 and ﬁgure 8
in SM), which is one reason why they are of critical interest. Climate change was also chosen because of its central
placement in the current political debate on sustainability, and the resulting data availability. Furthermore, climate
change is important because it is interlinked with multiple other PBs (such as ocean acidiﬁcation due to increased
carbon uptake in the oceans and loss of biodiversity due to lost habitats as a result of climate change). Biogeochemical
ﬂows include phosphorus (P) and nitrogen (N) ﬂows and was chosen because of the close link to agricultural industry,
which is essential for feeding a growing population. Following this, it was also chosen for being one of the planetary
boundaries most heavily impacted by human activity. Each of the PBs are associated with one or more measurable
control variables. The control variable for N is the industrial and intentional ﬁxation of N (Steﬀen et al., 2015). The
control variable for P regional is the P ﬂow from fertilizers to erodible soils, while it is the P ﬂow from freshwater
systems into the ocean for P global. These are anthropogenic impacts that come on top of the natural P and N ﬂows,

In line with (Ryberg et al., 2018) this study deﬁnes the SOS as the space between the PB and the pre-industrial
value (the natural background level). This deﬁnition ensures that the SOS is assigned in a consistent manner for all
PBs and is based on the assumption that if human impacts stay within the SOS, the PBs not already transgressed
will remain below the transgression threshold. For PBs which have already been transgressed, such as both climate
change and biogeochemical ﬂows, the underlying assumption is likewise that reducing the human impact on these PBs
to the space between the pre-industrial value and the PB will eventually bring the pressure below the PB. Note that
this assumption makes no guess at how long it would take to reach the PB at the given emission level. Climate models
project that global net emissions will need to be negative by no later than 2050 (IPCC, 2018) to achieve the Paris
goal of 2 °C — underlining the importance of a timely response to climate change. Furthermore, using the currently
remaining SOS, eg. the space between the current level and the PB, favours the already existing emitters as they
have already occupied part of the SOS (Ryberg et al., 2018). However, a beneﬁt of this deﬁnition of the SOS is that
negative SOSs are avoided, which would otherwise occur if the current level was taken into account for PBs which
have already been transgressed.
5.3
Allocation principles: sharing the safe operating space
How to share the SOS is widely discussed in the literature, for example by (Raworth, 2013), (Grasso, 2007), for a full
overview see the literature review in table 10 and 11 in SM. The simple reason that the issue of sharing is so crucial is
the fact that resources on Earth are limited (even if humans have been consuming them as if they were not) and need to
be shared to ensure that both current and future generations will be able to meet their needs in a sustainable manner.
There are some uncertainties relating to sharing the SOS into the future, both due to uncertainties in population
growth and prosperity, and in acknowledgement hereof, one can only guess at the needs of future generations or
Page 5 of 38
==PAGE:14==
Master’s Thesis in Environmental Engineering
Anjila Hjalsted
assume that they stay roughly the same as the needs expressed by consumption patterns today, as done in (Sandin
et al., 2015).
The process of allocating a speciﬁc share of the safe operating space is normative in the sense that it prescribes how
resources should be divided (Ryberg et al., 2018). Furthermore, the underlying assumption that we wish to preserve
the Earth in its Holocene state is arguably normative, and something we strive to uphold because it is the only state
that we know can support human life as we know it (Steﬀen og Staﬀord Smith, 2013).
In this article the term allocation principle is used to mean an overarching principle by which decisions about how
to allocate a given resource between agents can be made. In the literature allocation principle is used interchangeably
with the terms sharing principle (Ryberg et al., 2018), equity principle (H¨ayh¨a et al., 2016) and distributive justice in
the ﬁeld of political philosophy (Holtug and Lippert-Rasmussen, 2007). An allocation principle thus forms the ethical,
normative foundation on which the distribution of a chosen currency (this could be shares of the SOS, as it is here)
will be made and as such it is essential to spend some time choosing and understanding them. Distributive justice
concerns the just distribution of goods between agents. There are multiple dimensions of distributive justice, at least
ﬁve which have been deemed essential to this work. These include the pattern of distribution (eg. equality), the
currency (eg. welfare, money, CO2 emissions), the target (between whom is the distribution made? eg. individuals,
animals, companies, nations), the scope (eg. Europe, the World), and the time span (eg. inﬁnite, one generation).
Changing the variables in one or more of the mentioned dimensions results in diﬀerent allocation principles, while
some combinations of variables would probably be advocated by no-one. It is not the aim of this article to lay out
every possible allocation principle — that would indeed be diﬃcult and not very useful — but rather to identify and
underpin a handful of key allocation principles that can facilitate the downscaling of the two PBs in question.
5.3.1
Choice of allocation principle dimensions
For each of the suggested allocation principles presented in table 2 four of the ﬁve dimensions mentioned above are
kept ﬁxed for better comparability, namely: the currency: the unit of the PB in question (for example kg N/year
for nitrogen), the target: companies (via individuals), the scope: global, and the time span: until year 2100 for two
reasons; ﬁrstly the IPCC climate models often use 2100 as an endpoint for climate projections (IPCC, 2018) and
secondly, in year 2100 the children and/or grandchildren of the adult population today will still be alive, making it
easier to relate to for current decision makers. The remaining 1 dimension is left to be variable, namely the pattern
of distribution. A change in one or more dimensions can yield a change in the ﬁnal results. For instance, the ﬁnal
distribution is sensitive to whether the allocation happens in accordance with a principle of for example equality or
one of utilitarianism, as well as changes depending on the time span, with resulting allocation looking diﬀerent for
year 2050 than for year 2100, or even if we look 5 or 10 generations into the future.

5.3.3
Equal per capita
The equal per capita allocation principle is arguably the simplest of the 4 proposed here and it is a frequently used
and discussed allocation principle in the literature (Grasso, 2007), (Sandin et al., 2015),(Rao, 2014). It is also known
as the egalitarian principle (Greaker et al., 2013). The underlying moral implication is that any individual (living at
the time of the allocation) has the same right to the SOS. The intuition behind the equal per capita principle is easy
to follow and the allocation according to this principle is easy to perform since it only requires two variables, namely
the size of the SOS and the given population. This view, while easy to operationalize, has been criticized for being
much too simplistic and insuﬃcient as an allocation principle (Grasso, 2007), (Caney, 2009). It does not take into
account that a ”just” distribution is not neccesaroly an even one - if one person needs to consume more calories daily
than another, eg. due to sex or activity requirements of their daily life, is it still fair to allocate the same amount of
calories to both? Further, the equal per capita principle only takes into account individuals living at the time of the
allocation. However, future generations arguably also deserve to be taken into consideration. And arguably some of
the SOS has already been allocated to individuals who are no longer present - an issue not accounted for by the equal
per capita principle.
5.3.4
Grandfathering
The grandfathering allocation principle is based on the status quo in regards to emissions and impacts. In other
words, future emission rights are inherited based on a certain point in time (Sandin et al., 2015), (Grasso, 2007). The
grandfathering principle is often used in policy making due to its pragmatism (Caney, 2009).
Arguably, a downside to the grandfathering allocation principle is that it favours already existing companies and
large economies, thus making it diﬃcult for new companies or smaller economies to enter or expand on the market
as the SOS is already claimed by the existing companies. Furthermore it does not diﬀerentiate between companies
eg. based on any moral grounds, such as their contribution to sustainable development or attention to human rights.
Thus, a coal mining company would be assigned a higher share of the SOS than a wind power company given that
the wind power company starts out with lower emissions, which seems problematic. It is also worth noting that the
grandfathering allocation principle is sensitive to which year is chosen as the starting point.
5.3.5
Historical responsibility
The historical responsibility allocation principle proposed in this study is inspired by the polluter pays principle, which
is a widely known and applied concept in both the literature and policy making (Grasso, 2007). It is usually used
to express that the polluter (or impacter of the Earth system, in this case), has a duty to pay corresponding to its
own impact/pollution. However, when dealing with allocation principles, it is not the duty to pay but rather the
right to impact (or emit/pollute) which is being allocated to agents. With the historical responsibility principle the
polluter ”pays” by reducing impacts in proportion to national GDP, which is taken as a proxy. This is based on the
assumption that countries with a high GDP (compared to the global average) have impacted the Earth system more
than countries with low GDP, historically, which has brought them to to state of well being that they enjoy now
(Sandin et al., 2015).
The historical responsibility principle is also inspired by prioritarianism, an ethical viewpoint in political philosophy
aiming to prioritize those who are worst oﬀ(Holtug and Lippert-Rasmussen 2007). A way to make ”the polluter pay”
is by allocating a larger share of the SOS to low GDP countries than to high GDP countries, thus favouring poorer
and less developed nations. The diﬀerentiation could also be based on emissions per capita, at least for CO2 emissions
which is virtually linearly related to GDP per capita (see ﬁgure 9 in SM). An issue with this allocation principle is
arguably that countries with high GDP have adapted to a certain impact level and would have a comparatively harder
time reducing their impacts as drastically as this allocation principle would require. Further to the discussion is that

the right to emit and the duty for pay for emissions are not necessarily comparable or linked. For example, one may
suggest a principle in which there is no limit to how much an actor may emit, as long as the price for emitting is
paid for — which is a diﬀerent albeit important issue and another discussion altogether. As with the grandfathering
allocation principle the historical responsibility principle is sensitive to the choice of start year.
5.3.6
Suﬃcientarianism
The suﬃcientarianism allocation principle aims to supply each individual with suﬃcient of the SOS (Nielsen, 2017).
The ethical view of suﬃcientarianism does not conclude on what to do once this is achieved, but in this study any
remaining share of the SOS is divided equally between the global population, as in equal per capita if this comes
to pass. This allocation principle allocates based on needs, without regard for current impacts — contrary to eg.
the grandfathering principle. The suﬃcientarianism allocation principles allocates based on needs, in line with the
deﬁnition of sustainability based on the fulﬁlment of human needs. However, determining what is ”suﬃcient” is a
very diﬃcult if not impossible task. It is arguably sensitive both to geographical location (people living further from
equator require more energy for heating) and cultural diﬀerences among other things. Agreeing upon needs seems a
diﬃcult matter. The result of any allocation based on this principle would be dependent on the determined ”suﬃcient”
share of the SOS, which is a highly normative and debatable discussion with few or no clear indicators. The allocation
principle is included in the framework none the less, if only to prove that, as of now, it is too arbitrary to yield any
useful results. However, with further research, it may prove operational.
Another issue arises if there are not ”suﬃcient” shares for everyone within the SOS. The proposed solution according
to Caney is then to distribute by means of the equal per capita allocation principle (Caney, 2009).

5.4.1
Final consumption expenditure
To scale to a company level, the right to a share of the SOS must ﬁrst be anchored to the individual’s right. A just
way of doing so is arguably in proportion to how many customers the company serves. All else being equal, it seems
fair that a company serving a larger number of customers deserves a proportionally larger share of the safe operating
space (SOS) and vice versa, since the number of customers can be taken to express the overall preference given to
the company by individuals. By determining this preference of the average customer of the company, an amount of
personal equivalents can be estimated for the company, and a share of the SOS equivalent to this number of people
can be assigned to the company. This can be estimated in a number of ways. For companies that sell food products,
for instance, their right to a share of an individual’s right could be dependent on how much of the individuals daily
calorie requirement was met by the products of the company. It could even be broken down to the amount of protein
and various nutrients provided, eg. valuing healthy food over unhealthy food. However, this method disregards the
Page 10 of 38
==PAGE:19==

individual’s preference for anything else than food. Thus this article suggests a more holistic approach based on ﬁnal
consumption expenditure (FCE), namely the spending patterns of the individual. This is a typical and simple way to
determine a consumer’s preference for a product, often used by economists. However, another step is needed beyond
considering the company’s end customers and their preference for the type of product that the company produces.
Taking dairy products as an example, it is too strong an assumption that an average consumer will buy all of her
dairy products from the dairy company in question. Thus a market share must be determined to deﬁne how large a
share of the dairy products she buys are provided by the company in question (in this study, 10% is assumed). So the
FCE upscaling method is based on customers, their preference for the product type and the market share which that
company has for the product type (see equation in table 5).
It is worth noticing that the FCE upscaling method resembles grandfathering to some degree in the sense that it
bases its allocation on the status quo in terms of consumer preference for existing companies and products.
5.4.2
Green incentive
The green incentive (GI) upscaling method is based on the FCE method, multiplying it with a factor favouring ”green”
companies. The GI upscaling factor itself (see table 5 for equation) is based on an initial equal per capita division and
then weighted to favour a certain trait, similar to the historical responsibility allocation principle which in turn favours
depending on low GDP. In this case the scaling factor favours companies who have a high turnover:impact ratio. In
other words it favours companies that have a large turnover and small impacts comparatively - the scaling factor is
normalised with an average turnover:impact ratio for the given sector and potentially region (see SM for assumptions
made about the normalization factors used in this study). In practise this means that two clothes companies with
the same turnover could have diﬀerent shares of the SOS assigned to them based on their emissions: the one with
lower emissions would be assigned the bigger share, given it’s larger turnover:impact ratio. This upscaling method
is designed to provide an incentive for companies to continue striving to boost turnover, while also reducing impacts
on the PBs. This concept is also known as improving the eco-eﬃciency of a company (Bjørn og Hauschild, 2013). A
feature of the way the GI principle is constructed is that the basic principle behind the GI upscaling method could be
used to favour and disfavour based on other things than eco-eﬃciency as well, such as ratios of food production by
weight per impact or sick days taken by the employees per impact.
A limitation of this method is that to be accurate and up to date, it requires a large ﬂow of information, especially
given that the turnover and impacts of companies change continuously, and the normalisation factors would also need
to be updated accordingly. While the demand for data may make this approach unrealistic to apply as it is, further
research in this direction could be interesting. Also, as with the historical responsibility allocation principle, the GI
principle does not guarantee that the sum of the allocated shares stays within the deﬁned SOS. Therefore, a scaling
constant β has been derived and is given in equation 2. Multiplying with this constant ensures that the sum of all the
allocated shares based on this upscaling method will stay within the SOS. Due to a lack of data β has been omitted
in this study, but with substantial data collection if could be implemented in further research.

Assessment of absolute sustainability
Based on the given data and the chosen thresholds, allocation principles, and scaling methods, the allocation tool
answers the question of whether the company is absolutely sustainable or not. The result is given as a ratio indicating
how close to absolutely sustainable the company is: Whether or not a company can call itself absolutely sustainable
within a PB is quite simply a question of whether the company uses more or less than its allocated share of the
identiﬁed SOS. This can be calculated as sustainability ratios as shown in equations 4 and 5.

safe operating space (SoSOS) assigned to the company. This method of assessment is also proposed elsewhere in the
literature ((Ryberg et al., 2018), (Andersen et al., 2019)).
The ratio between the actual impact and the allocated SoSOS is thus an estimation of how sustainable a company
is, in absolute terms. Equations 4 and 5 are used to assess absolute sustainability independently of the allocation
principle and scaling method. As the actual use of the SOS (the impact of the company) stays the same (at least for
the reference year of choice), the interesting variable in this equation is the allocated SoSOS, which depends on the
allocation principle and the scaling method, as will be discussed in the following.
5.7
Proof of concept: A case study
The global demand for food is expected to double by 2050 (FAO, 2019). In 2007, 4% of global GHG emissions (ca. 2
million tons of CO2-eq) could be attributed to the dairy sector, which was estimated at a market value of 330 billion
USD in 2014 (FAO, 2019). FAO also estimates that more than 80% of the World’s population consume dairy products
regularly(FAO, 2019).
For this study, a made up dairy sector company has been used as case study for proof of concept of the developed
framework. The case company has 20 million customers, amounting to 2.69% of Europe’s population. For simplicity,
the company’s production operates only within Europe, and the customers are all assumed to be within Europe, evenly
spread out across the continent, allowing the use of average data for European climate change, N, and P impacts as
well as GDP (see assumptions made in this regard in table 17 and data used in table 15 in SM). The case company’s
emissions of GHGs are based on publicly available data for large dairy companies in order to be representative. The
case company’s emissions of N and P have been modelled to account for a tenth of the N and P used for agricultural
purposes in Europe according to FAOstat (FAO, 2019), due to lacking public information on nutrient use of dairy
companies. The background data, such as global population and GDP, used and its sources are listed in table 14 in
SM. Overall these numbers are not deemed very robust, but the point here is to provide a proof of concept, not to
create the perfect make believe dairy company. The numbers used can be determined on company level when applying
the framework and the values used in this study serve only for the proof of concept. This also implies that the results
based on the data used should be taken as a proof of concept and not a determination of the absolute sustainability
status of dairy production in Europe.

no solid reasoning for how to determine what is ”suﬃcient” is available. However, the underlying idea is ethically
meaningful and more research into operationalizing it is called for.

Variability between upscaling methods
Figure 5 shows that the diﬀerences between the allocation principles are of lower orders of magnitude than the
diﬀerences between the two upscaling methods. Thus the results seem to be less sensitive to the choice of allocation
principle than to the choice of upscaling method. Further, this diﬀerence is more prominent for climate change than
for biogeochemical ﬂows which can be seen in ﬁgure 5 where the diﬀerence between the outlined green bars and the
non-outlined green bars next to them (climate change) is larger than the diﬀerence between the outlined yellow bars
and the non-outlined yellow bars next to them (biogeochemical ﬂows). The GI upscaling principle is based on a
normalised scaling factor determined by the turnover to impact ratio of the company relative to the global average for
the given sector, as described in section 5.4.2. The observed diﬀerence in the results indicates that the case company
is more eco-eﬃcient in terms of GHG emissions than N and P emissions, both relative to the global average.
In terms of the upscaling method, ﬁgure 5 shows that adding the normalised eco-eﬃciency scaling factor, GI,
brings the case company closer to being absolutely sustainable than by uscpaling with the FCE-only method for all
PB-thresholds except P global. This indicates that the case company is more eco-eﬃcient than the world average,
which was used as a normalisation factor for the green incentive scaling principle. Had the case company had either a
lower turnover or a higher impact, enough to make it less eco-eﬃcient than the world average, the eﬀect would have
been reversed. More eco-eﬃcient companies are allocated larger shares of the SOS than less eco-eﬃcient companies,
which can seem arbitrary since they need less of the SOS to operate in the ﬁrst place, given their lower than average
impact compared to turnover. However, this way of allocating can create an incentive for companies to increase their
eco-eﬃciency, which in turn will decrease the global impact of companies. In principle, any excess shares of the SOS
could be traded to further beneﬁt the company (trading shares falls outside the scope of this article, but could be
interesting for future research).
In this study the case company’s turnover:impact ratio is normalised by the global average turnover:impact ratio
for the dairy sector. However, a more local normalisation factor could also be used if instead of comparing to the
global market, one wanted to compare to for example the European market, or the Indian market, or another market
of interest.
Arguably, a limitation of using FCE as a proxy for consumer preference is that products which are non essential to
basic human needs may be given disproportional preference in regions with high prosperity, thus apparently justifying
their existence over products such as food and water. A solution to this could be to model FCE in a world where
all consumption is already sustainable (Marchman et al 2019, in review). An example of this could be the ”steady
state economy” explored by (Fanning og O’Neill, 2016), maintained within shares of planetary boundaries. In other
words, the approach taken in this study leaves out any question about the importance or moral status of a product
in itself — however, that is a discussion for another paper. Here all products and services are assumed to be of the
same importance and moral worth 3.

6.4
Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis
In order to further assess the case study results and provide recommendations based on them, a sensitivity and
uncertainty analysis is performed as shown in the framework in ﬁgure 2.
The objective of the sensitivity analysis is to quantify the eﬀect of a change in a single input parameter on the
output (the result), varying one input parameter at a time. This method is often used to identify key parameters in
the calculations as a means of testing model robustness.
However, in this case, the output is always either proportional or inversely proportional to the input parameter
(with the exception of suﬃcientarianism where an equal per capita allocation can be added in case of surplus shares)
it is to be expected that the inﬂuence of each parameter on the ﬁnal result will be similar (not as if fx. a parameter
had been squared in an equation, giving it much larger weight). This also goes for the two upscaling methods from
the individual to the company level, as these are multiplicative factors. The hypothesis that all input parameters are
of roughly the same importance to the output is veriﬁed in table 22, showing that the relative sensitivity coeﬃcients
(RSC) are ≈1. These are calculated as follows:

Furthermore, uncertainty analysis was conducted using Monte Carlo simulation to assess the eﬀects of the un-
certainty connected with some of the input data to the framework calculations.
The equal per capita allocation
principle was chosen to demonstrate the applicability of Monte Carlo simulation on the case study. Monte Carlo
simulation, unlike the sensitivity analysis mentioned above, varies multiple input parameters at the same time, basing
the calculations on the mean as well as the standard variation of the input parameters.
These are the results of the Monte Carlo simulation performed with 10,000 simulation runs for each allocation
principle. Each simulation generates a random result within the set boundaries based on the mean and standard
deviation. The mean input values are chosen to be the values used for the original calculations, namely the world
population of 7.71 billion people, and the lower bounds of the PBs (see table 1). The standard deviation for the world
population is assumed to be 1%, amounting to an uncertainty of +-7.71 million people. The uncertainty of the various
SOSs is assumed to be 10% of the PB. The objective of the uncertainty analysis is to determine to which degree the
uncertainty of the input parameters aﬀects the results, which in this case are the allocated shares of the SOS. Figure
7 shows the probability distribution of allocated shares of the SOS for climate change, while the allocated shares of
the SOS for biogeochemical ﬂows are shown in SM in ﬁgure 12.
The allocated SoSOS for climate change with the atmospheric concentration control variable ranges from 0.8-1.21
tons CO2 per year per capita, while the stricter boundary for climate change, namely radiative forcing, allocates SoSOSs
within the range of 0.44-0.63 tons CO2 per year per capita (see ﬁgure 7). Ranges for the SoSOSs for biogeochemical
ﬂows can be found in ﬁgure 12, and table 23 provides the averages as well as maximum and minimum values for
the 10,000 simulations for the SOS of each PB, in SM. Taking these uncertainty ranges for the results into account,
the uncertainty can be factored into the recommendations provided to the case company. In eﬀect the most likely
allocated SoSOS to the individual using the equal per capita allocation principle is 0.98 tons CO2 per year per capita
for the atmospheric concentration threshold, but it could also be as low as 0.8 or as high as 1.21 tons CO2 per year
per capita, which arguably would make a diﬀerence. This is an uncertainty range of 0.41 tons, while the uncertainty
range of the radiative forcing threshold SoSOSs is smaller (0.19 tons) due to the smaller original input value.
Monte Carlo analysis can be used to vary multiple input parameters to diﬀerent degrees for diﬀerent scenarios, such
as for diﬀerent projections of future population or for diﬀerent values for planetary thresholds. The ﬁgures provided
here serve as a proof of concept of this part of the absolute sustainability assessment framework. When using the
framework, it is important to make assumptions about data uncertainty that are well informed in order to use the
results as a guide for giving recommendations, and to reduce the uncertainty ranges where possible.

6.5
Recommendations
The ASRs in ﬁgure 5 can be a guide to the company’s eﬀorts, where the size of the ratio could be a guideline. If
attributing equal weight to each of the applied thresholds, the threshold with the largest ASR could be the one to
tackle ﬁrst or spend the most resources on. Using this approach on the case company results, focusing eﬀorts on
biogeochemical ﬂows ﬁrst could be justiﬁed by the fact that these ASRs are the highest when giving equal weight
to both upscaling methods across all 4 allocation principles. Another way of prioritizing could be to prioritize any
thresholds which do not have ASRs of or below 1, in which case biogeochemical ﬂows would also be prioritized for the
case company.
A potential drawback of using multiple scenarios to assess absolute sustainability of a company could be that when
presented with the varying ASRs, it could be tempting for a company to pick and focus on the combination which
yielded the ASRs closest to or below 1. However, this strategy is discouraged since the idea of including multiple
allocation principles and upscaling principles was not to provide an array from which to pick but rather to provide a
nuanced view on the issue of the company’s absolute sustainability status.
While the framework developed here can be used to identify areas to focus on, how to reduce impacts is the next
step to be considered by the company, and many strong and well established assessment tools exist to aid with this
(such as hot spot analysis and LCA in general). A general recommendation when using this framework is to ﬁrst
identify focus areas in which to reduce, and then propose scenarios where action to reduce impact within a given
PB has been taken. It is then possible to use the framework to calculate new ASRs and compare how much closer
to being absolutely sustainable the company would be after the proposed action was taken. To demonstrate this,
a sensitivity analysis was conducted where a hypothetical impact reduction of 90% has been implemented and new
ASRs calculated on the new case company impact data, shown in ﬁgure 10 in SM. Mathematically unsurprisingly,
the ﬁgure shows that the ASRs for all allocation principles and the FCE upscaling method have been reduced to a
tenth of their original size, yet still not achieving absolute sustainability. The main conclusion to be drawn from this
Page 19 of 38
==PAGE:28==

is that even with an impressive (and diﬃcult to achieve) 90% reduction on overall impacts, the case company would
still not be absolutely sustainable. In the light of this, perhaps the company should consider the eﬀectiveness of its
business, rather than the eﬃciency (Hauschild, 2015). While this could be considered drastic, it may be the only
way the achieve absolute sustainability, and it could provide valuable input to future strategic considerations for the
company. A way of doing this could be to shift from producing cow’s milk to producing oat milk or another type of

6.6
Expanding the framework to other PBs
A strength of the framework developed here is its versatility, eg. how it can be expanded to include other PBs than
the 2 chosen here. In fact it can operate with any quantitative boundary or threshold of interest, such as the 1,5 or
the 2 °C goal in the Paris Agreement or speciﬁc, quantiﬁable targets within the SDG framework. The underlying
assessment framework remains the same (as introduced in ﬁgure 2). Each PB is given in its own units, and the amount
of the SOS already claimed by humanity is relative to the unit of the PB in question, as shown in ﬁgure 8 in SM.
For the PBs which have quantiﬁed boundaries, the same methodology can be applied given that impact data from
the company in question is available. The issue of data availability likely poses the largest issue when expanding the
framework to other PBs, since not all agents, be it individuals, companies, or other agents, have access to reliable
data about their own impact on eg. biogeochemical ﬂows or land-system change. Data availability is another reason
for choosing to allocate on a company level rather than an individual level, seeing as companies can more easily be
held responsible for and can more easily obtain data about their impacts and emissions than individuals can. Three of
the PBs either do not have indicators (biodiversity) or are not quantiﬁed (biodiversity, aerosol loading, novel entities),
and hence cannot, as of now, be used as quantitative thresholds to perform absolute sustainability assessment on.
Further, it is arguably beneﬁcial to include as many PBs in the absolute sustainability assessment as possible in order
to avoid problem shifting between the PBs.

6.7
Expanding the framework to other companies or agents
Another strength of this framework is its anchoring point in the individual, making it feasible to scale from the
individual to technically any other level, such as household, nation, company, sector or product level (see table 4).
This is done by allocating shares of the SOS to the chosen agent or level by right of the individuals aﬃliated with
it. For a nation, this would be its inhabitants. For a company or a product, this would be based on the number
of individual customers as well as their preference for the company or product compared to their other preferences,
determined by local FCE data. Since the allocation principles proposed in this framework scale to the individual level,
scaling to any other level is ”merely” a question of multiplying the individual’s assigned share with an appropriate
scaling factor. The less straight forward task is naturally deriving and justifying this scaling factor. It is essential
that the derivation is well justiﬁed and transparent. For a company that operates outside of Europe’s boarders the
extended geographical scope should be taken into account. This can be done by assigning shares of the companies
activities to certain countries and using data for all nations proportionally in the equations for allocation.
7
Conclusion and outlook
In the light of increasing human pressures on the Earth system, the issue of sharing in the light of scarcity is more
pressing than ever. It poses a fundamentally ethical problem, which, however, needs to be approached technically
from a natural science background as well as morally to be solved in practice. The concept of absolute sustainability
assessment constitutes a strong tool in regard to determining how to share the safe environmental operating space.
This study proposes a two step approach, where a pre-deﬁned global threshold such as a planetary boundary is ﬁrst
downscaled to the individual level using ethical allocation principles and next scaled up to a higher level with upscaling
methods, using the company level as an example. This study takes the ethical standpoint that individuals are the
only agents with rights to shares of the safe operating space.
To demonstrate the applicability of the developed framework, two of the planetary boundaries; climate change
and biogeochemical ﬂows, were selected to show how the choices of allocation principle and upscaling method impact
the share of the safe operating space assigned to a company. The safe operating space was deﬁned as the pollu-
tion space between the planetary boundary and the natural background level of the same planetary boundary. In
this study a framework was developed with which to assess absolute sustainability at the company level within the
planetary boundary framework testing 4 allocation principles and 2 upscaling methods, using a case study based on
the European dairy sector as proof of concept. Three of the proposed ethical allocation principles (equal per capita,
grandfathering, and historical responsibility) are deemed operational while one (suﬃcientarianism) requires further
work to be operationalized. The case study results show that none of the combinations of the allocation principles and
upscaling methods assess the company to be absolutely sustainable, although 5 of the 40 scenarios fall within the area
of uncertainty of absolute sustainability. Further, it was demonstrated that the choice of upscaling method is of larger
inﬂuence on the results than the choice of allocation principle. A sensitivity and uncertainty analysis shows that the
inﬂuence of the method variables are of similar magnitude, and that the results are reasonably robust to uncertainties.
The framework developed in this study can be used to prioritize interventions for impact reduction at company
level, and can serve as a means to plan for documented sustainable development and hence contribute to keeping the
planet in the Holocene by reducing human pressures on the Earth system. For a list of recommendations based on
the work done in this study, please see table 24 in SM.
In 2011, 82% of the World’s 500 largest companies produced some kind of social and environmental sustainability
reporting (Antonini og Larrinaga, 2017). Regrettably, sustainability reporting is far from the same as being sustainable,
and green washing is far too easy when the operating space and/or environmental thresholds are either unclear,
undeﬁned, or unknown.
Absolute sustainability assessment poses a holistic solution to this, with the framework
developed here as an example of how to do it.

List of general assumptions:
• Planetary processes happen on much larger time scales than company processes and the trajectories are not
linear.
• Climate change PB: control variables of ppm and w/m2 are not useful to companies, who need a unit of ton
co2-eq to identify with. Translation between these is important and not trivial, but the normalisation references
of Bjørn and Hauschild 2015 are used.
• For all transgressed PBs: assume that emissions kept within the SOS will at some point bring the levels back
below the SOS boundary (as in Ryberg 2018).
• Lower PB bound chosen: in line with precautionary principle (and original PB framework).
• Geographical scope: strongly dependent on this, eg Europe’s GDP and emissions diﬀer a lot from India’s, and
geography speciﬁc data is included in the allocation principles proposed.
• Anthropogenic motivation for sustainable development.
• Assume thresholds proposed by PB framework authors. Fang et al 2015: The diﬃculty in predicting thresholds
is a major challenge to ESA.
• Assume that data is available at company level – although I spent months trying to get data from Arla without
luck.
• Assume that all impacts world wide can be attributed to a company of sorts (so the sum of all the company’s
impacts is the global impact).
• Assume heterogenic nations. Do not consider inequality within a nation. (eg only look at average national GDP)
(Rao 2014).
• Focus on environmental sustainability, not social or economic.
• Large internal variability in Europe! For emissions and GDP and FCE.
