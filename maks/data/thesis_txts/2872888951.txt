Abstract
This thesis presents the development and evaluation of a multi-sensor fusion framework
for robust localization in agricultural robotics. The system integrates GNSS, IMU, and 4D
radar odometry to address the limitations of single-sensor approaches under challenging
field conditions, including GNSS outages and signal degradation. The fusion framework
was implemented in ROS2 and validated using real-world datasets collected with com-
mercial agricultural platforms. Quantitative results demonstrate that sensor fusion sig-
nificantly improves localization accuracy and trajectory stability compared to standalone
GNSS or radar odometry. The system is able to maintain reliable pose estimates during
GNSS faults and provides consistent 6-DOF trajectories even in adverse environments.
These findings support the use of multi-sensor fusion as a practical solution for resilient
and precise localization in autonomous agricultural vehicles.

Introduction
In recent years, there has been an increasing need for robust odometry and mapping sys-
tems due to the desire of automating repetitive tasks such as plowing, mowing, or tilling
the field. Accurate localization, which involves continuously tracking a vehicle’s position
and orientation, has therefore become crucial for mobile robots in such environments. Re-
liable self-localization helps robots navigate independently, avoid obstacles, and perform
accurate tracking tasks. Maintaining consistent position data ensures these tasks can
be completed successfully, and more importantly, safely, minimizing any risk of harm to
individuals.[1]
In autonomous driving, odometry provides essential support for navigation, particularly
in environments where Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) signals may be weak,
blocked, or intentionally disrupted. GNSS signals are vulnerable to interference, including
intentional jamming and spoofing, especially amid geopolitical tensions [2]. This vulnera-
bility has encouraged the development of multi-sensor fusion approaches capable of reli-
able localization without depending solely on GNSS, but rather as a fused system where
GNSS measurements are combined with local odometry sensors to further improve the
overall positioning of the vehicles.
Integrated navigation systems that combine Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS)
with Inertial Navigation Systems (INS) have become widely adopted for vehicle localiza-
tion, as they exploit the complementary strengths of both technologies. In these systems,
GNSS provides global position fixes with high absolute accuracy when satellite signals
are available, while the INS offers continuous position and attitude estimates by integrat-
ing sensor data from accelerometers and gyroscopes. Several critical limitations affect
GNSS/INS integrated systems, particularly in real-world environments such as those en-
countered by agricultural robots. GNSS signals are highly sensitive to signal blockage
from vegetation, buildings, and adverse atmospheric conditions. When GNSS measure-
ments become unavailable or unreliable, the integrated system relies solely on the inertial
navigation system, whose errors rapidly accumulate over time due to sensor biases and
drift. Furthermore, GNSS data may be disrupted by multipath effects, ionospheric and
tropospheric delays, or interference, resulting in outliers or sudden jumps in the position
estimate. The performance of the entire integrated system is also limited by static and
dynamic errors inherent in inertial sensors, such as bias, misalignment, and noise. As

a result, maintaining accurate and robust localization requires new multi-sensor fusion
approaches that can compensate for these individual weaknesses [3][4][5].
Modern odometry systems process sequential data from sensors like LiDAR, Radar, or
Inertial Measurement Units (IMU) to calculate the vehicle’s position and orientation. When
combined with mapping capabilities, these systems can create detailed maps of the sur-
rounding environment, including 2D grids and 3D point clouds. Such maps facilitate effi-
cient navigation, decision-making, and the ability to adapt quickly to changes in the envi-
ronment. Despite recent improvements in odometry systems, agricultural areas present
different struggles for the sensor. LiDAR-based systems present severe degradation in
their performance when faced with adverse weather conditions such as fog or heavy rain
[6], whereas cameras also underperform when visual occlusion such as smoke, dust, or
rain is present.
As a response to the limitations of conventional sensors, 4D mmWave radar technologies
have emerged as a robust solution for autonomous driving in adverse weather and low-
visibility conditions [7]. Operating in the 30–300 GHz frequency range, these radars are
inherently resilient to rain, fog, and smoke, as their radio waves are less susceptible to
scattering by water droplets or airborne particles. In contrast to LiDAR and camera sys-
tems, which often experience significant performance degradation under such conditions,
4D mmWave radars maintain reliable detection and ranging capabilities. Furthermore,
these sensors offer a substantial cost advantage, with hardware costs that are orders of
magnitude lower than LiDAR systems, making them a practical and scalable choice for
agricultural robotics [8].
This research has been developed in collaboration with AgriRobot, a company specializ-
ing in certified safety and autonomy solutions for agricultural field robots. Its mission is
to enable fully autonomous farming operations, removing the need for continuous human
oversight. AgriRobot utilizes advanced 3D sensor technology, including radars, lidars,
and cameras, as well as GNSS receivers, to provide robust obstacle detection and ensure
safe operations in challenging agricultural environments, meeting stringent international
safety standards.
To address the specific operational demands of agricultural robotics, AgriRobot has identi-
fied several key requirements for next-generation localization systems. Most importantly,
the system must maintain reliable and high-precision positioning, even during periods
of partial or total GNSS signal loss, such as those caused by vegetation, buildings, or
inclement weather, which could potentially cause the GNSS receiver to lose partial or to-
tal communication with the vehicle. The solution should extract the strengths of onboard
radar and inertial sensors to compensate for these outages and reduce overall positioning
drift. In addition, while the primary focus is on robust localization, the capability to record
field boundaries as three-dimensional maps and to enable localization within these maps
is considered a valuable enhancement that could support repeated and precise navigation
in the future. This task could take advantage of the final output of the odometry algorithm,
which will output the full pose and orientation of the system, allowing for the recording of
complete maps. These requirements reflect both the practical challenges encountered
in agricultural environments and the need for scalable, robust, and certifiable solutions in
the automation of farming operations.

1.3
State of the art
A wide range of recent research explores radar-inertial odometry and SLAM systems
aimed at improving localization in environments where GNSS signals are unreliable. The
method proposed in [9] combines radar scan matching and inertial measurements within
an EKF framework, incorporating online calibration to reduce long-term drift. In [10], the
authors present a real-time odometry and mapping approach that integrates 4D imaging
radar and inertial measurements. It robustly handles noise, multipath effects, and dynamic
objects by using the Graduated Non-Convexity (GNC) method for ego-velocity estima-
tion. Radar scan points are matched to a maintained submap using a distribution-to-multi-
distribution matching strategy, enhancing reliability. An iterative Extended Kalman Filter
(EKF) fuses radar-derived ego-velocity and inertial data, providing precise 3D localization.
The system in [11] introduces a continuous-time optimization strategy for radar-inertial fu-
sion, allowing for tightly coupled integration of Doppler and inertial data. Similarly, [12]
presents a tightly coupled EKF that directly incorporates radar measurements and uses
multi-stage preprocessing to filter noise and identify static points for reliable matching. A
sliding-window graph-based method with ego-velocity estimation from Doppler radar is de-
scribed in [13], offering fixed computational cost and improved robustness through motion
constraints. The approach in [14] removes the dependency on IMU altogether by relying
on Doppler and RCS data, applying polar-grid voxelization and weighted scan matching to
overcome radar sparsity. The dead reckoning structure in [15] uses radar-derived velocity
and gyroscope data for time updates, with radar scan matching contributing to correction
steps in a filter-based framework. Finally, [16] proposes a full SLAM pipeline for 4D radar,
combining front-end odometry using GICP, loop closure detection, and back-end pose
graph optimization, demonstrating its scalability in large-scale field experiments.
All of the previously mentioned methods do not integrate GNSS into the system output, but
rather act as a previous step either using radar on its own, or fusing it with inertial sensors.
Papers fusing all three sensors, such as [17], an improvement on [10], in which tightly
coupled IMU measurements, Doppler observation information and point cloud matching
information from 4D radar by iterative extended Kalman filtering (iEKF) method are used
to obtain local odometry, introducing GNSS RTK and loop closure information as global
observations to correct the inherent drift of continuous, local odometry. The authors in
[18] present a version of a proprietary software, AUTO, which consists of a real-time nav-
igation system that integrates INS, RTK GNSS, odometer and radar sensors alongside
a previously built high-definition map to provide precise localization by performing a tight
nonlinear integration of the radar and maps alongside the GNSS and INS system. Doer
et al. [19] extend their previous radar-inertial odometry for UAVs [9] by fusing GNSS mea-

The project strategy was established following a review of the state of the art and an
assessment of available resources. The first phase focuses on generating local odometry

• Radar4Motion: A radar-only odometry approach relying on scan matching and

• iRIOM: A tightly coupled radar-inertial odometry method, combining radar detec-

• DeRO: A dead-reckoning pipeline fusing radar-derived velocities and gyroscope

3
Radar odometry
Accurate short term pose estimation is essential whenever GNSS information is degraded
or unavailable, as may occur in agricultural environments characterised by dense foliage,
airborne dust, or light precipitation. 4D mmWave imaging radars are well suited for this
problem domain: independent of ambient illumination, resilient to most weather phenom-
ena, and delivering distance, speed, horizontal angle, and elevation angle datapoints.
As detailed in Chapter 1.3, three radar-based odometry algorithms were selected for im-
plementation and analysis. Radar4Motion [14] operates solely on radar data, performing
scan matching on a polar-grid voxelisation in which Doppler velocity and radar-cross-
section weights alleviate point-cloud sparsity. iRIOM [10] combines imaging-radar de-
tections with inertial measurements: ego velocity is first estimated via the Graduated
Non-Convexity scheme, after which scans are registered to a rolling sub-map through
distribution-to-distribution matching. Finally, DeRO [15] implements a lightweight dead-
reckoning pipeline that propagates radar-derived velocities and gyroscope rates in a de-
layed state extended Kalman filter, injecting scan-matching residuals as periodic correc-
tions. This analysis will be preceded by a short introduction on radar technology, a brief
description of the math behind the measurements and the applicability of 4D mmWave
sensors for sensor odometry.
3.1
Radar technology
Radar (Radio Detection and Ranging) employs electromagnetic waves, typically in the
microwave or millimetre-wave bands, to determine the position, velocity, and characteris-
tics of objects by transmitting signals and analysing their echoes. Its inherent resilience
to adverse weather and poor illumination, together with its direct Doppler sensing capa-
bility, makes radar an essential modality for robust outdoor localization and mapping in
agricultural robotics.
3.1.1

and a fraction of the wave reflects back toward the radar. A receiving antenna captures

3.2
Related work
This section reviews three influential algorithms that form the basis for the radar-based
odometry and sensor fusion approaches explored in this thesis. Each method exemplifies
a distinct philosophy for utilizing radar data, as a standalone sensor or in combination
with inertial measurements, to achieve robust vehicle localization in environments where
GNSS may be unreliable or unavailable.
3.2.1
Radar4Motion
Radar4Motion is a radar-only odometry method that does not require any inertial sensors.
It leverages modern 4D imaging radars, which provide 3D spatial information (x, y, z),
Doppler velocities, and radar cross-section (RCS) for each detected point. The method
follows three main stages, summarized in the flowchart provided in Figure 3.4.
Figure 3.4: Flowchart for Radar4Motion Algorithm. Adapted from [14]
The first stage is ego-motion estimation using Doppler measurements. Here, the vehicle’s
movement is estimated from frame to frame using only the Doppler velocities of radar
points. Each static object in the environment produces a point return, and the Doppler
effect reveals how quickly that object appears to move relative to the radar. If the object
is static, this value directly measures the ego-motion of the vehicle. To deal with mov-
ing objects, which could corrupt the estimate, the algorithm uses a robust M-estimator
that minimizes the influence of outliers. Specifically, the method applies Tukey’s biweight
function to reduce the weight of points that do not match the dominant motion pattern[14].
This leads to a weighted least squares solution for the vehicle velocity, using the previous
frame’s motion as a starting point and iteratively refining the result. The method also esti-

mates the yaw rate using a simple kinematic constraint based on the position of the radar
on the vehicle, without needing an IMU.
In the next stage, the algorithm extracts robust features from the radar point cloud. Radar
measurements are often sparse and noisy, so the point cloud is divided into a polar grid,
based on range and angle. In each grid cell, only the points with the highest RCS values
are retained. RCS represents how strongly an object reflects radar signals, so this step
selects stable and distinctive features, such as posts or building corners, while maintaining
even spatial coverage.
From here, the algorithm accumulates several recent scans into a local point cloud using
the estimated ego-motion. This creates a denser cloud, improving robustness for match-
ing. The accumulated scans are then matched against a submap, built from previous
frames, using an ICP-like registration method. During registration, each point correspon-
dence is weighted according to both distance and RCS, so that highly reflective and well-
aligned points have greater influence on the pose estimate. This approach helps manage
the sparsity of radar data and increases the reliability of scan matching. ICP (Iterative
Closest Point) registration works by iteratively matching each point in the current scan
to its nearest neighbor in the submap and then computing the transformation that best
aligns the two sets of points. This process repeats until the alignment converges or a set
number of iterations is reached. An example of ICP can be seen in Figure 3.5, where two
pointclouds not aligned become so after performing the registration.
Figure 3.5: Example of ICP being performed on two pointclouds. Adapted from [27]
Radar4Motion is effective in structured environments with strong static reflectors, espe-
cially when the radar faces forward and the vehicle follows mostly straight paths. However,
the method can be sensitive to strong rotations, non-standard radar mounting, or poor fea-
ture distribution. If there are few reliable reflectors or large unmodeled rotations, accuracy
can degrade. Careful calibration between the radar and vehicle frame is also important
for optimal results.
3.2.2
iRIOM: Radar-Inertial Odometry Methodology
The iRIOM algorithm fuses data from a 4D imaging radar and a six-degree-of-freedom IMU
to estimate the full pose of a vehicle in real time. The method is designed to provide robust
odometry even when GNSS is unavailable or unreliable. The core pipeline includes radar
point cloud filtering, IMU-based state prediction, Doppler-based ego-velocity estimation,
and scan-to-submap matching, all fused within an extended Kalman filter. Figure 3.6
shows the overall structure from the proposed method, adapted from [10].
The process starts by preprocessing the radar point cloud. Since radar data is often
sparse and noisy, points are filtered by local spatial density and intensity consistency.
Only points with enough neighbors and low local range variance are kept, which helps to

suppress outliers and noise.
With a cleaner point cloud, the system predicts the platform’s motion using IMU data.
The IMU readings are bias-corrected and integrated to estimate position, velocity, and
orientation. Radar Doppler measurements are then used to estimate the vehicle’s velocity
by projecting the expected motion onto each detection direction. To focus on static scene
features and reject dynamic outliers, the algorithm uses a robust optimization scheme that
downweights inconsistent measurements.
Finally, the method registers each radar scan against a submap built from recent scans.
For each point, the algorithm finds the closest neighbors in the submap and matches to
their centroid, taking into account the local covariance to improve robustness. Both the
Doppler-based velocity estimate and the scan matching constraints are fused with the
IMU state in an extended Kalman filter, providing a refined pose estimate. As a result,
iRIOM achieves reliable and accurate odometry and mapping in complex environments
using only radar and IMU data.

4
GNSS/IMU/Radar Odometry Fusion
In dense foliage, airborne dust, or adverse weather conditions, common in agricultural
settings, GNSS signals frequently attenuate or lose lock, undermining reliable position-
ing [28]. Low-cost MEMS IMUs supply high-rate angular and linear measurements but
drift unbounded in the absence of external corrections [29]. Millimetre-wave 4D imaging
radar, by contrast, remains resilient to most weather and lighting conditions, delivering
short-term motion estimates via Doppler processing and scan matching [30]. Fusion of
GNSS for absolute, long-term accuracy, IMU for high-frequency dynamics, and radar for
robustness during GNSS outages sustains accurate odometry even when satellite signals
fail.
As discussed in Chapter 1.3, several GNSS/radar/IMU fusion systems have been pro-
posed in recent years. Among them, G-iRIOM [17] extends the iRIOM [10] framework
by tightly integrating GNSS with 4D radar and IMU data; however, since iRIOM was thor-
oughly evaluated in this thesis and produced unsatisfactory results, its successor G-iRIOM
was not considered for further investigation. GNSS-RIO [19] is another notable approach
that combines all three modalities and has demonstrated robust performance in aerial
robotics, but it was not adopted here due to differences in application domain and soft-
ware compatibility with the project’s ROS2-based system.
4.1
Multi-Sensor Fusion Architecture
In light of the previous considerations and the lack of robust, domain-specific solutions,
this project adopts general-purpose sensor fusion frameworks to integrate GNSS, radar,
and IMU data. The robot_localization package, which implements an Extended Kalman
Filter (EKF), and the fuse package, which is based on factor-graph optimization (FGO),
were selected for this purpose. Both frameworks provide the flexibility to combine multiple
sensor streams in real time and support modular algorithm development for agricultural
robotics. The following sections describe the architecture and core components of each
framework.
4.1.1
Architecture and Components of robot_localization
The robot_localization package provides a robust general-purpose nonlinear state es-
timation approach for mobile robotics by employing Extended and Unscented Kalman
Filters (EKF/UKF). Specifically, it integrates sensor data in 3D using two primary ROS

Kalman filtering is a state estimation technique that provides an optimal recursive solu-
tion for linear systems with Gaussian noise [32]. Introduced by R. E. Kalman in 1960, the
Kalman Filter (KF) predicts the internal state of a dynamic system and corrects that predic-
tion using noisy sensor measurements. In mobile robotics, including agricultural robots,
KFs are widely used for sensor fusion, integrating data from sources such as IMUs, GPS,
wheel odometry, and radar sensors to estimate a robot’s pose (position, orientation) over
time [33]. By accounting for sensor uncertainties, the KF produces state estimates with
minimized mean-square error [34], crucial for navigation in real-world environments.
The KF operates on a two-step cycle of prediction and update. In the prediction step, the
filter uses a process model to project the previous state estimate forward in time, given

enabling robust integration of multiple noisy sensor streams into accurate pose estimates
critical for autonomous operations in agriculture and beyond [33, 35].

the nonlinear system and sensor models around the current best state estimate. The pack-
age manages asynchronous sensor streams through internal buffering, ensuring state
estimates are updated continuously at a fixed rate.

By default, each EKF maintains a 15-dimensional state vector including 3D position, orien-

allows fusing diverse sensor inputs such as odometry, IMU, GPS, and radar data, with
customizable selection of individual state variables per sensor (e.g., yaw-only from IMU).
A common best-practice deployment involves two concurrent EKFs: a local filter integrat-

pose estimates, and a global filter correcting long-term drift through absolute positioning
sensors like GPS [36]. While this architecture isolates global sensor discontinuities, it
requires careful initialization and an external odometry source to function optimally.
Despite its wide use, robot_localization has known constraints: the fixed 15-state di-
mensionality limits adaptability without custom code modifications, and the absence of
plugin support complicates integrating novel sensor models. As a single-step linearization
EKF/UKF, it is not designed for advanced smoothing or loop closure corrections, introduc-
ing potential limitations for complex scenarios.
4.1.2
Architecture and Components of fuse
The fuse framework implements state estimation as nonlinear least-squares optimiza-
tion over a factor graph, relying on the Ceres Solver for efficient and robust solution of
large-scale problems. It is structured around a central optimizer node and three plugin
categories, sensor models, motion models, and publishers, that interact via well-defined
transaction interfaces. Incoming sensor data are converted into constraints and variables,
motion models supply kinematic priors, and publishers disseminate optimized results. A
configurable smoothing window (fixed-lag or full trajectory) bounds computational effort
while preserving the ability to relinearize past measurements. All components are loaded
at runtime via YAML or ROS parameters, enabling modular extension without modifying
core code.
Sensor model plugins serve as the front line for incoming data. Each plugin subscribes
to one or more sensor streams, preprocesses raw measurements, and converts them
into constraint transactions. A transaction may introduce new variable nodes (for exam-
ple, a pose at a fresh timestamp or a newly observed landmark) and associated error
functions. These error functions capture the discrepancy between predicted and actual
measurements and are equipped with weighting (information) matrices and optional ro-
bust loss terms to down-weight outliers. Transactions are forwarded asynchronously to
the optimizer for integration into the graph.
Motion model plugins supply kinematic and dynamic priors that bind successive state
nodes in time. Whenever the optimizer incorporates a new state variable, typically with a
timestamp yet to be connected, the motion model is queried to generate one or more con-
straints that encode expected evolution (such as a constant-velocity or unicycle model).
These constraints replace the prediction step of classical filters, enforcing smooth transi-
tions between states and ensuring temporal coherence across the factor graph without
hard-coded assumptions.
The optimizer node orchestrates the fusion process. Running at a user-defined frequency,
it consumes all pending transactions, augments the graph with motion priors, and invokes
Ceres to solve the aggregated nonlinear problem. Upon convergence, variables that have
fallen outside the smoothing window are marginalized to keep problem size bounded, pre-
serving essential information while discarding outdated nodes. This loop naturally accom-
modates multi-rate and out-of-order measurements, with relinearization of all constraints
at each step enabling robust handling of nonlinearities and delayed observations.

output logic allows for flexible downstream integration, from visualization tools to control
loops, without altering the core optimization engine.

Protection Levels and Integrity Monitoring in Sensor
Fusion
Reliable localization in autonomous vehicles requires not only high accuracy but also for-
mal integrity guarantees: statistical bounds ensuring the true position error rarely exceeds
a user-defined threshold. In this context, a protection level (PL) provides a real-time, prob-
abilistic bound on navigation error, ensuring the system can alert or react before safety is
compromised.
Integrity monitoring originated in aviation, with frameworks such as Receiver Autonomous
Integrity Monitoring (RAIM) for GNSS, which introduced the operational logic of compar-
ing the estimated PL against an alert limit (AL). If the PL surpasses the AL, navigation
is declared unavailable; if the actual error exceeds the AL undetected, the result is Haz-
ardously Misleading Information (HMI) [37]. For ground vehicles, integrity monitoring has
adapted to cope with more challenging conditions, including multipath, outages, and sen-
sor degradation.
Classically, PLs in GNSS-only or loosely-coupled systems are computed from measure-
ment redundancy and snapshot geometry. However, as multi-sensor fusion and dynamic
estimation become standard, integrity must also reflect the temporal evolution of state
errors. In tightly coupled GNSS/IMU systems, Lee et al. [38] derived protection level al-
gorithms for extended Kalman filters (EKF) that rigorously account for both measurement
and process noise, as well as worst-case impacts of IMU faults. Their approach combines
the filter’s evolving covariance with sensitivity matrices that capture the effect of potential
sensor failures, providing real-time bounds with explicit integrity risk guarantees.
With the adoption of factor graph optimization (FGO) for navigation, the integrity paradigm
has expanded further. Tian et al. [39] proposed the first general approach for computing
protection levels in FGO-based fusion, using the graph’s information matrix and the lin-
earized residuals from all measurements to bound position error, including multiple simul-
taneous sensor faults. Their results, validated on a range of sensor types, show that the
computed PLs reliably contain true errors in all tested scenarios.
Significant challenges remain. Most published techniques focus on GNSS and IMU, with
little attention to radar or radar-odometry faults. Nayak and Barth [40] highlight the need
for integrity frameworks that address these novel modalities, especially for connected
and automated vehicle (CAV) applications, as well as practical methods for real-time PL
computation under non-ideal data and noise conditions.
Formal protection level methods exist for both Kalman and factor-graph-based fusion, en-
abling robust integrity monitoring in principle. Nevertheless, their real-world effectiveness,
particularly for new sensor types and evolving operational demands, is yet to be fully re-
searched [40].

Summary
This chapter has examined practical approaches for fusing GNSS, IMU, and radar odome-
try streams, with a focus on modular, real-time frameworks compatible with ROS2-based
agricultural robots. Two general-purpose sensor fusion packages, robot_localization
(EKF-based) and fuse (factor-graph-based), were implemented and adapted to accom-
modate the specific sensor configurations of this project, including the development of
new plugins for 3D motion and GNSS integration.
Through a series of integration and validation experiments, it was demonstrated that rely-
ing solely on GNSS data yields position estimates that track ground truth, but fails to re-
cover meaningful orientation, due to the absence of heading information from the receiver.
Augmenting the fusion with radar–IMU odometry resolves this issue, providing accurate
roll, pitch, and yaw estimates across diverse test trajectories. Both robot_localization
and fuse produced highly consistent and precise results, with negligible differences in
pose error.
Beyond accuracy, recent work on integrity monitoring has introduced Kalman-Integrated
Protection Levels (KIPL), which allow the EKF-based fusion to provide real-time statistical
bounds on position error. This enables not only high-precision odometry but also formal
integrity guarantees, which are essential for safety-critical agricultural applications.
These results confirm that both EKF- and factor-graph-based architectures can effectively
fuse all three modalities in real-time, supporting robust and accurate multi-sensor odom-
etry under field conditions. Further analysis in the next chapter will focus on the compar-

5.1
Introduction
This chapter presents the experimental results obtained by evaluating the multi-sensor
fusion framework developed for integrating GNSS, IMU, and radar odometry. The primary
aim of these experiments was to assess the system’s capability to mitigate positioning
inaccuracies typically observed when GNSS receivers report high covariance or exhibit
sudden, unrealistic position jumps in open-field scenarios.
Two distinct test configurations were employed: one using the IMU from a NovAtel system,
and another utilizing the IMU from a SICK LiDAR sensor. Both scenarios involved open-
field trajectories designed to replicate typical agricultural vehicle operations, as well as
some trajectories under dense foliage or tree canopies to further assess the performance.
The system performance was quantitatively assessed using the Absolute Pose Error
(APE) metric. Ground truth data for evaluation purposes were obtained from two reli-
able sources: a NovAtel Smart7 RTK-GNSS/IMU fusion system for the first configuration,
and an RTK dual-antenna ComNav K827 system for the second configuration.
The results demonstrate the benefits of sensor fusion, highlighting significant improve-
ments in localization accuracy and stability compared to individual sensor modalities alone.

5.4
GNSS Outages
In addition to the well-known limitations of GNSS receivers in secluded or obstructed en-
vironments, such as large position errors caused by multipath effects, canopy cover, or
atmospheric disturbances, practical field deployments may encounter more acute failures.
For example, GNSS sensors can intermittently stop producing valid data, or communica-
tion between the receiver and the robot may temporarily drop out. These issues result
in intervals during which no GNSS information is available, regardless of the underlying
quality of satellite reception.
To evaluate the system’s resilience to such outages, experiments were conducted in which
GNSS messages were deliberately and randomly dropped for short durations. This ap-
proach simulates not only poor satellite visibility, but also the impact of temporary sensor
or communication failures. During these simulated outages, the localization system re-
ceives no GNSS input and must rely solely on local sensors such as odometry and IMU
to propagate the robot’s state.

5.5
Mapping
An additional advantage of fusing GNSS, IMU, and radar odometry is the ability to con-
struct spatial maps of the environment during normal operation. In this work, mapping
was conducted exclusively with the Fendt 716 platform, testing both front-mounted radar
sensors and the fused odometry solution.
The mapping process proceeds as follows. Each incoming radar pointcloud is first trans-
formed from the sensor frame to the vehicle frame using the known static calibration. The
points are then further transformed into the global odometry frame using the most recent
fused pose estimate, ensuring all measurements are accumulated in a consistent global
reference.
To ensure the quality of the reconstructed map, several filtering steps are applied. Points
with signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) below a specified threshold are discarded, as are those
falling outside a plausible height range. Additional filtering is performed by requiring each
point to have a minimum number of neighbours within a local radius or a sufficiently low
local variance, which removes isolated or noisy detections. The resulting filtered points
are accumulated into a global pointcloud.
As the mapping process continues, the accumulated pointcloud is periodically voxelised
with a 2 cm resolution. This down-sampling reduces redundancy, limits memory growth,
and preserves salient spatial features. The resulting map, published in real time as a
PointCloud2 topic, captures tree lines, field boundaries, and major obstacles, provided
the odometry remains reliable. This approach enables effective radar-based mapping
for agricultural environments and illustrates the practical benefits of multi-sensor fusion
beyond localization alone.

fused odometry keeps the warehouse and adjacent yard sharply aligned, and the radar
returns clearly delineate building edges and open spaces. Note that the radar pointclouds
mainly map those reflections with high SNR, so further, noisier points are not included.

5.6
Test result analysis
The experiments on the reproducible AgriRobot setup demonstrate that all fusion ap-
proaches, fuse, robot_localization, and standalone GNSS, achieve similar overall error
statistics in open-field conditions with high-quality GNSS input. However, during periods
of increased GNSS covariance or short-term drift, the advantage of sensor fusion be-
comes clear: while no filter can fully correct for large simultaneous errors in both GNSS
and radar odometry, the fusion algorithms maintain overall trajectory stability and prevent
50
Multi-Sensor Fusion with GNSS, IMU, Radar to improve localization and integrity in agriculture robotics
==PAGE:59==
implausible jumps, particularly in orientation estimation.
The second campaign, performed in a more challenging agricultural scenario, further
highlights the strengths and limitations of the fusion framework. When the Septentrio
GNSS solution fails (large covariance and position jump), the fuse filter remains clos-
est to the ground truth, with errors around 10 cm, while both standalone GNSS and
robot_localization can exceed 1 m error. This confirms that loosely coupled fusion
mitigates GNSS faults better than any single sensor or naive filter. However, radar odom-
etry drift remains a problem in long occluded segments. When both GNSS and radar
are degraded, none of the filters can compensate. RMSE alone is not sufficient to evalu-
ate performance: while the Septentrio may show low error due to noise aligning with the
reference, only the fusion output stays physically plausible during stationary periods.
In addition to localization, the results demonstrate the value of sensor fusion for real-
time mapping. The fused pose estimate enables consistent global alignment of radar
pointclouds, allowing for the construction of accurate 3D maps even during periods of
GNSS degradation. The resulting maps, such as those generated on the Fendt 716 and
in circular trajectories, reveal detailed features like warehouse boundaries, field edges,
and vegetation clusters. This spatial consistency would not be possible with single-sensor
odometry alone, further illustrating the practical benefits of robust multi-sensor fusion in
agricultural environments.
5.7
Future works
The following areas are recommended for future development:
• Implement tightly coupled fusion (use raw GNSS and radar measurements instead
of post-processed odometry).
• Extend the to a full SLAM system, localizing within the generated map.
• Test in a wider range of environments, especially with longer GNSS occlusion, high
multipath, and dynamic scenes.
• Add quantitative reliability/integrity metrics for the fused pose.
• Reduce radar drift with loop closure or map-based corrections.
• Improve sensor calibration, especially between radar and IMU.
5.8
Summary
The fusion system clearly improves pose estimation in GNSS-challenged scenarios, with
fuse consistently outperforming other methods in both accuracy and trajectory stability.
Mapping results further demonstrate that robust sensor fusion enables reliable global
alignment of radar pointclouds, producing accurate and consistent maps of the environ-
ment. Key limitations remain, including radar drift and reduced robustness when all sen-
sors degrade. Continued work on tighter fusion, SLAM integration, and robust evaluation
in more varied and challenging scenarios will be essential for fully reliable deployment in
the field.

Conclusion
This thesis addressed robust localization for agricultural robotics by fusing GNSS, IMU,
and radar odometry. A modular, ROS2-based framework was developed and validated
on multiple field datasets using both loosely coupled filters and custom extensions for 3D
pose fusion.
The main findings are:
• The fusion approach significantly improves localization accuracy and stability com-
pared to standalone GNSS or radar odometry, especially during GNSS outages or
covariance jumps.
• The proposed system reliably suppresses large GNSS errors and maintains plausi-
ble trajectory estimates when single-sensor methods fail.
• Radar odometry provides valuable short-term motion during GNSS degradation but
remains sensitive to drift in extended occlusions.
• Adding radar and IMU odometry resolves the lack of orientation in low-cost GNSS
receivers, yielding a consistent 6-DOF pose for the vehicle.
• The framework performs in real time and is compatible with standard field hardware
and embedded platforms.
In addition to localization, the developed framework enabled mapping of field boundaries
and operational areas as three-dimensional representations, employing the fused sensor
data to create consistent and accurate maps. These mapping capabilities were validated
in multiple field scenarios and proved valuable for recording trajectories, supporting po-
tential future use cases such as repeated path-following and autonomous coverage of
large fields.
Moreover, the system was extensively tested under simulated and real GNSS outage
conditions, including scenarios where the vehicle passed under dense tree cover or ex-
perienced intentional signal degradation. During these events, the fusion of radar and
IMU data allowed the platform to maintain reliable pose estimates and trajectory stabil-
ity, effectively bridging periods of poor or lost GNSS coverage. The results confirmed
Multi-Sensor Fusion with GNSS, IMU, Radar to improve localization and integrity in agriculture robotics
53
==PAGE:62==
that the proposed multi-sensor fusion approach not only improves steady-state localiza-
tion, but also provides crucial resilience against the types of GNSS disruptions commonly
encountered in agricultural environments.
The results align with the thesis objectives: increasing localization robustness, quantifying
performance under real agricultural conditions, and validating sensor fusion as a practical
solution for autonomous farming. Limitations remain in long-term radar drift, degraded per-
formance when all sensors fail simultaneously, and the absence of a fully tightly-coupled
fusion pipeline. These points are discussed as future work in Chapter 5.
In summary, this work demonstrates that multi-sensor fusion is a viable and effective strat-
egy for resilient localization in agricultural robotics, meeting the stated goals and providing
a solid baseline for further development.

from 2D to 3D
This appendix outlines the development of new plugin routines that extend the fuse sensor-
model framework from two-dimensional to full six-degree-of-freedom operation. Rather
than altering the existing 2D code, new methods were introduced to handle 3D transforms,
instantiate three-dimensional state variables, propagate and validate expanded covari-
ance matrices, and generate appropriate graph constraints. These additions enable the
optimizer to natively process radar–IMU odometry in 3D, providing consistent pose esti-
mation with full uncertainty representation. Note that this is not a comprehensive list of
all the changes performed, but rather a showcase of the type of changes that had to be
made in order to enable 3D analysis of the data.
