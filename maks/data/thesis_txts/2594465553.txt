Abstract 
The buildings sector is the largest energy-demanding and polluting sector in the global economy. The 
implementation of nearly Zero Energy Buildings contributes to reduce energy use and climate change. To 
support this new type of buildings construction, there is the need for educating professionals with the required 
skills and competencies on sustainable buildings. Developed countries have developed energy efficiency skills 
and updated their university programmes. However, these also need to be transferred to emerging economies 
where there is a large scope for the interventions in the future. This thesis will focus on the review of existing 
academic curricula related to nZEB across Europe and what Argentine university programmes can acquire. 
Content analysis, based on nZEB skills, is used to generate and analyse the contents of the courses from eight 
engineering programmes in Europe and three programmes in Argentina. The European engineering curriculum 
is revealed to have integrated sustainable buildings courses, which they can be transferred to Argentinian 
education. The research is an effort to provide suggestions of new university programmes and courses based on 
the country energy-efficient needs in buildings. It also presents a methodology for analysing nZEB content in 
university curriculum and contributes to the continuing sustainability education discourse.

1.Introduction 
1. Introduction 
1.1. Background 
Actual patterns of energy supply and use are unsustainable economically, environmentally and socially. Despite 
efforts to reduce GHG emissions, the world’s energy supply still is by and large as carbon-intensive as it was two 
decades ago (IEA (a), 2019). Without decisive actions and a population growth projection, energy-related 
emissions of carbon dioxide will be more than double by 2050. This massive energy use and consequent high 
pollution have changed the climate patterns which they have already affected the lives of communities, eco-
systems and economies all over the world. Additionally, the dependency on a limited energy source as fossil 
fuels and higher energy demand have triggered concerns over the security of the supplies (IEA, 2013). Thus, one 
of the measures to be placed is the transformation of the energy industry with a view to accelerating the 
reduction of global greenhouse gas emissions to provide a sustainable future habitat. The Paris Agreement aims 
to limit the rise in the average global temperature to less than 2°C above pre-industrial levels to pursue efforts 
to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels (UN, 2015). 
Within this context, the buildings sector is the largest energy-demanding sector in the global economy. Including 
execution and operation phases, the building sector constitutes 36% of the total global energy demand and 39% 
of global carbon dioxide emissions (UNEP, 2017). Also, buildings consume 55% of global electricity, what it 
requires high capacity and infrastructure for the power sector,  which is critical in some countries (IEA (a), 2019). 
During the last two decades, the rise in the use of electricity in buildings, especially in developing countries in 
temperate regions, has been driven by an increase in demand for cooling systems. Globally, cooling services 
account for 15% of electricity consumption, getting to maximum consumption peaks up to 50% on hot days in 
some countries. Without efficiency gains, electricity demand for cooling could be more than double by 2040, 
having emerging economies larger number (IEA (a), 2019). Heating is also the other main energy share on a 
building, representing 47% of the total (IEA (b), 2019). Additionally, the building sector is to grow, due to an 
increase of population by 2.5 billion by 2050 and likely higher living standards. Consequently, such an increase 
will be extra pressure on the energy system (IEO, 2019).  
Strategies and policies on buildings must deliver measures to change the present impact on energy use and the 
environment. The transformation to an energy-efficient and low-carbon buildings is crucial to achieving global 
ambitions to mitigate climate change. If new near-zero energy construction and deep renovations in existing 
buildings take place during the next decade, the sector’s energy consumption can decrease by 35% to 2050, yet 
with double of global floor area (IEA (a), 2019). Technologies already exist that would allow the buildings sector 
to be more energy-efficient and sustainable (IEA (c), 2019). Space heating can be improved by better insulation, 
refurbishments of old buildings, design considerations and more efficient equipment like heat pumps. A similar 
strategy to cut down cooling energy demand could be applied by insulation and better energy performance

This new concept of buildings is already under implementation. For instance, Europe is witnessing a deep

possess the required sustainable knowledge to be able to deliver effective results following energy efficiency 
requirements (WBCSD, 2008). In previous studies, the shortage of sustainable knowledge has been identified in 
a number of countries as a major obstacle in implementing national strategies to cut GHG or address 
environmental changes (ILO, 2011). Thus, education is in the core of the green transition for confronting the 
environmental challenges from the buildings sector. The gaining of knowledge on nearly Zero Energy Buildings 
is crucial for public institutions, investors, clients, existing and new professionals.  
European buildings sector transformation has already started to deliver positive results. Such interest in energy 
savings have been present since the time of the oil crises of the 1970s when the security supply was the driver 
for shifting the energy policy dynamics and not the today’s environmental crusade (Lauber, 2015). PassiveHouse 
institute has been one of the most prominent nZEB promoters by spreading the required awareness in the whole 
buildings industry to provoke the reaction in the institutional and political level (PHI, 2016). In 2002, the 
European Commission launched the Energy Building Performance Directive, which addresses and regulates the 
implementation of nearly Zero Energy Buildings across the Member States in Europe (European Commission, 
2002). This directive has encouraged the upgrade of national building codes to make mandatory energy 
optimization in built environments (EPBD, 2020). One result is observed in the energy use in households’ sector, 
which shows a reduction of end-use energy consumption per capita of 5.55% since 1990 and 2016 (EIA (f), 2019). 
The biggest progress has been seen in heating space energy consumption. States like Germany, France and the 
United Kingdom have experienced reductions of over 30% since 2000 (IEA (b), 2019). Despite this, today the

Nowadays, 86 % of the world population lives in developing countries where also the population growth is going

manner (UNDP, 2020). The aim is to raise the standards of the existing and new building construction following 
energy efficiency and living conditions requirements. 
A clear example is seen in cooling building services. This utility has been increasingly installed for the last two 
decades in countries with very large populations as China, India, Brazil, Mexico and Indonesia (IEA (d), 2019). 
People of these countries have proved an enhancement of their purchasing power which is reflected in the 
acquisition of systems to increase their living conditions (World Bank, 2019). In 2018, there were a total number 
of 1,76 billion of air conditioning units worldwide, which two-thirds of them were installed in buildings in 
developing countries. However, the typical unit bought has an average Seasonal Energy Efficiency Rate (SEER) 
of AC units under 3.5. With a little higher investment, the user could purchase devices with cooling efficiency 
rates that are 50-70% better. Without any efficiency improvement, the cooling demand could increase by 60% 
globally by 2030 (IEA (d), 2019). 
One country which exemplifies this trend in the developing world is Argentina. In 2018, buildings consumed the 
33% of the total energy consumption of the country (Gobierno de Argentina, 2019) which represented a 20,8% 
of the GHG of the country (Republica Argentina, 2019). Also, almost a third of the population lives in a deficient 
house (Granero et al, 2019). Since 1990 to 2017, end-use energy consumption per capita increased by 154% (IEA 
(e), 2019). This situation is expected to worsen due to the population rise by 23% in the coming three decades 
(UN, 2019). An average Argentinian dwelling can be described as having walls with U-values of 3–4 W/m2 K, 
poorly-insulated roofs and floors, one-glazing windows, large infiltration rates and low-efficiency appliances 
(González, 2013). This low-quality and deficit housing status drive to the need of changing the way of designing, 
renovating and producing new properties.  
Argentina has initiated this transition process by the creation of national commissions and legal framework 
following the Paris Agreement (Secretaria de Vivienda, 2019). However, the country presents several barriers 
which impede the entire transformation of the buildings sector. Among several obstacles, the knowledge of 
energy saving in buildings professionals is low. This is caused by the lack of nZEB teaching in the existing 
academic curricula for engineers (Schwarz, 2017). Then, one of the measures to take is to update engineering 
study programmes to form professionals with the required competencies on green building construction. The 
assessment of European university programmes can contribute to provide examples of relevant courses related 
to energy-efficient measures applicable in the Argentine buildings sector.

1.Introduction 
1.2. Problem statement 
The need for sustainable development has changed the entire buildings sector across Europe. This change has 
implicated the integration of energy efficiency knowledge in education programmes. For instance, Engineering 
schools have updated their old academic curricula and developed new programs following sustainable 
requirements. As a result, there are engineers in today’s market working on projects where factors of energy 
savings, indoor air quality and environmental concerns are included. However, the situation in Argentina is 
different. There is a scarcity of professionals with the proper knowledge and skills on nZEB. The present 
university system does not generate this type of professionals and forces to students to acquire this knowledge 
through seminars, online or abroad. This fact hinders a complete green transformation across the entire sector, 
and eventually, to deliver sustainable building projects. Therefore, despite some minor changes already 
introduced, the current academic curricula in Technical Universities across Argentina should integrate and cover 
nZEB concept. Examples extracted from European Engineering Schools can guide to Argentinian universities in 
its own academic transition process. 
 
 
 
 
1.3. Research questions 
The main research question is: Which are the current academic curricula in European universities for engineers 
regarding energy efficiency in buildings and what Argentina can learn? 
Additionally, this master thesis lies on the next six research sub-questions: 
1. What is a nearly Zero Energy Building, nZEB? 
2. Which are the skills required for engineers concerning nZEBs? 
3. What are the requirements for energy-savings construction in Argentina? 
4. What are the contents of the courses in relevant European universities regarding nZEBs? 
5. What is the current context of energy building performance and nZEB teaching in Argentina? 
6. Which are the potential gains for the present Argentinian academic curricula?

2. nZEB in Europe 
2. nZEB in Europe   
The chapter will first clarify the concept of nearly Zero Energy Building nZEB, which European Commission 
addresses it through Energy Performance Building Directive. This directive provides nZEB definition and 
regulations to promote the implementation of energy-efficient measures in indoor spaces. This first part of the

2.1. European legal framework for nZEB: Energy Building Performance 
Directive (EPBD) 
Currently, there are many ways to name or address the fact of energy efficiency in buildings. Several public and 
private institutions provide their own specific definition and requirements for sustainable buildings. Among 
these institutions, the European Commission established Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) to 
articulate any plan, action and innovation regarding energy consumption in buildings, along with the reduction 
of greenhouse gases emissions. By the implementation of nZEB concept, it is expected to achieve by 2050 a high 
energy efficiency performance, to decarbonize building stock, to create long term stable environment for 
investments and to allow consumers and business to have better access to systems to save energy and money. 
EPBD defines the concept of a nZEB as “‘nearly zero-energy building’ means a building that has a very high energy 
performance, as determined in accordance with Annex I. The nearly zero or very low amount of energy required 
should be covered to a very significant extent by energy from renewable sources, including energy from 
renewable sources produced on-site or nearby” (European Commission, 2010). As it is described in the Annex I, 
an nearly Zero Energy Building takes into consideration aspects of thermal capacity, insulation, passive heating, 
cooling elements, thermal bridges, heating installation, hot water supply, air-conditioning installations, natural 
and mechanical ventilation which may include air-tightness, built-in lighting installation, positioning and 
orientation of the building, passive solar systems and solar protection, indoor comfort conditions and internal 
loads. 
The first EPBD version was published in 2002. Then, in 2010, this interest was recast and amended in 2018, which 
Annex 1 introduced the obligation of: “Member States shall describe their national calculation methodology 
following the national annexes of the overarching standards, namely ISO 52000-1, 52003-1, 52010-1, 52016-1, 
and 52018-1, developed under mandate M/480 given to the European Committee for Standardisation (CEN). This 
provision shall not constitute a legal codification of those standards.” (European Parlament, 2018). This meant 
that Member States have had to describe the energy calculation methodology explaining where and why they 
deviate from EPBD Standards. The last progress of Energy Performance Building Directive has been to deliver 
Commission Recommendation (EU) 2019/786 of 8 May 2019 on building renovation and Commission 
Recommendation (EU) 2019/1019 of 7 June 2019 on building modernisation (EPBD, 2020).  
EPBD provides a package of standards is made of a collection of CEN Standards and five overarching EN ISO 
52000 family of standards. Each of these describes an important chapter in the evaluation of energy 
performance building:

For the last two decades, EPBD has boosted the integration of nZEB policies in every Member State. A 
characteristic in this implementation process is the variability of nZEB definition among European countries. This 
variance comes from different interpretations of building category classification, inclusion of different energy 
uses in nZEBs and system boundary generation for the renewable energy source. In addition, primary energy 
thresholds and calculation methodologies differ across the Member States. Despite of that, national energy

it is described inside the same Article 9(1) of the EPBD part a, “by 31 December 2020, all new buildings are nearly 
zero-energy buildings” (European Commission, 2010). 
The revision of EPBD serves to identify the concept of nearly Zero Energy Buildings. The nZEB definition 
establishes new features to consider in a building. The building energy consumption, building elements, indoor 
space conditions and technical building systems are factors included in the building design process. Additionally, 
it shows the evolution of nZEB and its implementation across European Union for the last two decades. These 
changes in the regulations have enabled the transformational change in the buildings sector across Europe. For 
this new generation of buildings, there was the need of supplying a new type of engineers to the labour market 
to support the new regulations. Thus, the higher education system was adapted to this need to generate this

2.3. The current scene of engineering education related to nZEB in Europe 
As it is mentioned earlier, EPBD has pushed to nZEB implementation across Europe. One of the main changes is 
observed in the higher education system. The availability of courses and programmes related to energy-efficient 
buildings in European universities is a reality. The educational need for building professionals is essential to the 
success of the nZEB implementation. Universities in Denmark, Ireland and the United Kingdom have the highest 
number of courses on EEB, which is directly linked with a higher implementation on nZEB policies (Tobriska et 
al., 2018). 
However, engineering education across Europe is diverse and has two different pedagogies approaches. “Theory 
driven” educational programmes are to respond to technical challenges by reaffirming the scientific knowledge. 
The engineer is extensively trained in various natural sciences and mathematics related to her/his field and tends 
to turn scientific facts into technical solutions. Traditionally, these academic curricula are more teacher-centred 
and provide more general than specialized knowledge. This academic mindset can be seen across France and 
Southern Europe. On the other hand, “Practice-driven” university programmes are to deliver engineers with a 
deeper knowledge of any specific science or field. This approach provides a flexible adaptation to a wide range 
of present-day problems, and eventually, being closer to the needs of the industry. In these programmes, the 
student is the main protagonist and has higher freedom to compose her/his own academic pathway. Central 
and Northern Europe has this approach integrated in their engineering educational programmes (Jamison & 
Heymann, 2012). Therefore, these two different academic views originate different study plan structures with 
the same target of forming EEB engineers.  
Despite of these different approaches and cultures, European Universities have several common characteristics. 
In 1998, the European Higher Education Area and Bologna Process were implemented to systematize some 
patterns in the tertiary educational level. This agreement brought the opportunity to reform the academic 
structure as three-cycle degree structure (bachelor, master’s, doctorate) and adopt share tool as European 
Credits Transfer System (ECTS) and guidelines for Quality Assurance (EUA, 2020). Concerning engineering 
education offered, there are 3- or 4-years bachelors, 1- or 2-years master’s degree and 2- or 3-years PhD 
programmes.

3.1. Argentinian climates and their energy building performance 
requirements 
Argentina is the 8th largest county in the world. The land is shaped like an inverted triangle with a base of 1,420 
km and 3,800 km from the subtropical north to the subantarctic south. The country can be classified in four 
major regions: Patagonia, La Pampa, the Andes, and the North. It encloses many different types of landscapes 
and climatic zones: plains, tundra, deserts, forests, jungles, mountains, large rivers and a large ocean shoreline 
(Britannica, 2020). Therefore, Argentine climate is very varied, and it depends on the location and geographic 
features of the site. The Argentinian Building Code “IRAM 11603 Thermal conditioning of buildings- 
Bioenvironmental classification of Republica Argentina” contemplates six different specific climates. These types 
of climate encompass from subtropical to subantarctic weather conditions and present different challenges and 
assumptions in order to design a building.  
The Zone I or “very hot climate” is in the northern part and the hot weather is predominant, with less than 390 
heating degrees days. In the summer season, the whole area temperature exceeds 34°C, presents a mean 
temperature of 26°C and thermal amplitude 15°C. During the winter period, the coldest month presents higher 
temperatures than 12°C. To avoid overheating events, the design shall contemplate: 
• 
Optimal orientations for windows are Northwest, North, Northeast, Southwest, South and Southeast. 
• 
All surfaces must be protected against the solar radiation and painted with pale colours. 
• 
East and west wall should be minimized and windows with minimum area, prioritizing South- and North-
face windows. 
• 
Shading systems should be placed in building façades towards Southwest, West, Northwest, North, 
Northeast, East and Southeast. 
• 
Project corbel in the Northwest, North and Northeast side in order to protect vertical surfaces and walls. 
• 
Integration curtains and shutters in every window. 
• 
Integration canopies and/or other solar shade systems alike in the North orientation. 
• 
Vegetation in front of the walls in order to hinder the solar radiation incidence. 
• 
Disposition of adjacent buildings by having a generous space between them to enable the natural 
ventilation into the building. 
The zone II or “hot climate” has between 390 and 780 heating degree days. In the summer season, the maximum 
temperature can exceed 30°C, the mean temperature is between of 20°C and 24°C and thermal amplitude is 
16°C. During the winter period, the mean temperature varies between 8°C and 12°C. The climatic conditions are 
less severe than Zone I although the same assumptions earlier before are to be considered. Additionally, the 
design should contemplate: 
• 
Optimal orientations for windows are North and South. 
• 
Houses and outdoor spaces shall be dimensioned in order to optimize the solar gain during wintertime.

More than the 90% of Argentine population lives where there is a temperate climate and high solar irradiation

design should prioritize low-cost strategies to mitigate overheating phenomena. Aspects like location, building 
orientation, higher insulation, window position, window-wall rate, types of glazing with special regard on Solar 
Heating Gain Rate (SGHR) and shading systems should be considered in the design of a sustainable building

Among these measures, high-performance windows can turn into a costly solution and eventually to have a poor 
degree of implementation in a low-purchasing- power country like Argentina. The rest of energy efficiency 
strategies are costless or very low budget and represents significant energy savings opportunities (Gastine & 
Pattini, 2019).  
However, recent researches state the importance of combining envelope building, passive ventilation strategies 
and heat recovery units for energy savings without mentioning windows positioning and building orientation. 
Investigations dealing with soft climates show that a doubled pan glazing would be sufficient against costly triple 
glazing window solution advised in old passive house guidelines. The heating demand would increase 6% but 
solar gains would increase 25%. This, in combination with passive ventilation strategies like crossed natural 
ventilation and night cooling, would decrease the total energy demand in 26% during spring, summer and 
autumn. This implies the use of heat recovery units only in cold days and winter season (Crespo Sanchez et at, 
2019).  
In Southern and mountainous areas of Argentina, the saving energy measures should aim at envelope design 
and efficient heating systems (Andersen et al, 2016). In addition, the orientation of the building, windows 
position and location of adjacent buildings to stop the wind are aspects to be regarded. 
Thus, the design of nZEB should not focus only on sustainable architectural design aspects but to integrate 
systems as efficient heating installations, heat recovery units, building envelope improvement and to apply 
passive strategies as natural ventilation. The latest advances have brought economical and more efficient 
systems which its integration on EEB design should be studied for the benefit of Argentine buildings case.

5.1. Overview of the building sector in Argentina 
As earlier mentioned in the introduction, buildings in Argentinian consumed the 33% of the total final energy 
during the year of 2018 (Gobierno de Argentina, 2019) which represented the 20,8% of the GHG of the country 
(Republica Argentina, 2019). These numbers of energy and GHG do not show high impacts because there is a 
large population with low incomes which accounts for more than 40 % of households. This group has a lack of 
access to natural gas, using other energy sources as bottled gas, firewood, charcoal, diesel, and electricity. 
(González, 2013). 
The Paris Agreement of 2015 promoted a revision in 2016 of national plans already launched a year early. This 
update modified the requirements to more ambitious targets. The goal is to not exceed the total emissions of 
483 MtCO2e in 2030 by adding several measures. Additionally, it includes more ambitious unconditional 
emission limit to 369 MtCO2e in 2030. This is equivalent to a 3% increase above 2010 levels and 38% above 1990 
levels. Contribucion Nacional Determinada (NCD) launched this plan including a large set of measures in all 
sectors, including the buildings sector (Ministerio de medio ambiente y desarrollo sostenible de Argentina, 
2017).

The main energy sources in buildings are the same for the energy matrix (Salvetti et al, 2013). The supply of 
energy in the Argentinian Republic lies on fossil fuels for 87%, where natural gas is the 58%, petroleum a 28% 
and coal is 1% (Subsecretaría de Planeamiento Energético, 2019). The last decade, Argentina has been 
constrained by the energy deficit and the need of buying fossil fuel in the international market besides being 
Argentina an oil and gas producer. In 2019, Argentina was able to reach almost the energy equilibrium 
presenting a low deficit of 72 million of dollars. The reasons of these good news do not lie in the results of energy 
efficiency measures but the higher production and investments on new exploitation areas (Terzaghi, 2020). Since 
1990 to 2017, end-use energy consumption per capita in buildings increased a 154% (IEA, 2019). This increase 
can be broken down in two: one part of this increase has been caused by the lowering energy efficiency in 
buildings; the other part because of living conditions have increased by using 62,81% more of electricity (Chévez 
et al, 2017). Moreover, if there are not changes in building energy use, this situation will worsen due to the 
population rise by 23% in the coming three decades (UN, 2019). 
In general, buildings in Argentina show a poor thermal behaviour. Most of the residential houses could save 
between 3 and 5 times of heating consumption by the implementation of some ECMs like thermal insulation. 
Also, the indoor climate presents a low quality and building equipment are low energy efficient (González, 2013). 
Thus, it turns out that building are unhealthy environments and present high energy consumptions. This 
situation is caused by three elements: energy subsidy policies along the last decades, predominant construction 
mindset by building cheaply without any energy saving considerations and integration of low efficient 
equipment in the buildings.  
Subsidies have been present on the natural gas and electricity policies during the last three decades, being at 
the top of the agenda of any government. These public incentives were introduced in the decade of the 90’s in 
a low basis until the Corralito crisis hit Argentinian economy in 2001. To cope with this recession, one measure 
taken on the energy chapter was to increase the subvention rates to alleviate the monthly expenses of middle 
and low classes. Particularly, gas natural was the most promoted form of energy. Since 2003 and for one decade, 
the price per unit of energy of residential gas was 10–20 times lower than diesel and 10–20 times lower than 
electricity. Such price policy implied that customers obtained low gas invoices what made them not to be aware 
of their energy use, and eventually leading to higher energy consumptions. This contributed to users to not host 
any much-needed energy efficiency measure inside the built environment. During that decade,  the cost of 
implementing any ECMs equated to about 50 years of residential heating costs at current gas prices, but 3 years 
if European gas fees had been considered (González, 2013). Additionally, these subsidies were implemented 
without any socioeconomic differentiation, not considering the income of the classes neither the access to 
natural gas. The natural gas network accounts for the 60% of houses, which belong to middle- and high-income 
householders, leaving without possibility of gas access to poorer sectors. Thus, benefits of such public support 
were enjoyed by the one who did not need it, provoking that the most vulnerable part of the population paying 
5-15 times more for other type of energy source to obtain the same amount of energy. In 2009, the Government 
passed a bill to reduce the subsidies provoking social unrest and eventually it was withdrawn. Since 2010, every 
Province Government ordered to energy companies to reflect a complete information of the energy used, the 
number of subsidies involved and a comparison with other countries payments for the same amount of money 
(González, 2013).  Nowadays, with the change of Government, energy subsidies policy is to change its direction 
again. Macri’s office reduced from 86% to 4% the presence of subvention for natural gas householders. The new 
government has presented a subsidy proposal which represents the 47% of the final natural gas price (Serrichio, 
2019). 
The second main cause of the low energy building performance lies in the low thermal quality due to cheaper 
construction methods and practices along the last century. This low quality has been directly linked with the 
permanent housing deficit (Granero et al, 2019). On the one hand, the State has been developing social housing 
since the 70’s with the aim to meet the demand of families in need of a roof by only considering the quantity 
and the budget without any other regards as the energy use and air indoor quality. An example is found in the

Plan Eva Peron chalet from the middle of decade of 40’s until the end of the decade of 70’s. During the first two 
decades of this type of building being constructed, the quality of this social house typology was high, particularly 
in the basis of the envelope design. However, the need to response to a higher demand of houses changed this 
tendency after the 70’s. The factors of costs, construction duration, lower requirements, poorer materials, larger 
quantity and rationality would dominate the design phase. Walls would have a thickness of 30 cm before 
declining to 15 cm.  The same occurred to other kind of edifices like Casa Cajon FO.NA.VI and Duplex estatal 
(Rosenfeld & Czajkowski, 1992). On the other hand, the private sector was not behind and carried out the same 
practice despite of the presence of building thermal quality in building regulations since the late 70’s. This 
tendency of low thermal requirements reached to the point that a revision of Argentine building code in 1986 
hosted the lowering of energy performance requisites (Salvetti et al, 2013). However, building code embraced 
again the ambition on cutting down the energy consumption in buildings, although these requirements are 
contemplated as recommendations and not mandatory actions (Czajkowski, 2015). This little attention for 
energy savings from the recent past is reflected in many examples across the country and its different climatic 
zones.  Cold regions could save more in fuels for heating, while temperate and warm zones would also save 
electricity due to less cooling loads. For instance, in the South of the country, in Bariloche, the overuse of heating 
leads to, in terms on environmental impact, 8 tons of CO2/year per household in a house of 100 m2. This could 
be reduced to the half by minor refurbishments measures. In La Pampa, with a similar situation considering 
heating and cooling needs, a decrease to 2-3 tons CO2/year per household. Such situation puts the energy matrix 
on the edge, especially during hard winters that provokes the paralysation of part of the industry in order to 
supply energy to the residential sector (González, 2013).  
Currently, the third of the families in Argentina, around 3 million and a half, live in a deficient house. Since 2001, 
this tendency has worsened because the housing deficit has been increasing. Again, the quantity request is 
prioritized against the qualitative demands. Additionally, the Provincial Governments allocates less resources to 
cope this situation due to a budget diminution which it is more aggravated in less developed areas. This present 
deficit provokes the overcrowd of houses that leads to worse indoor climate conditions (Granero et al, 2019). 
This situation is less favourable when a householder does not have access to natural gas and is obliged to use 
another form of energy like firewood, bottled gas and/or electricity which are not enough to warm the place 
deriving to temperatures between 10 to 15 C. These households are more prone to fire incidents, coming either 
from firewood or insufficient electrical installation not adapted to higher power demand due to the high heating 
consumption (González, 2013). 
The third reason consists in the increasing electrical demand for domestic appliances with poor energy 
performance.  For the last two decades, an average Argentinian household has been equipped with a larger 
number of devices in order to improve its living conditions without considering energy efficiency. This is more 
notorious in warm areas where the use of AC is essential, and houses tend to have a big extension and deficient 
building envelope. This fact has provoked the increase of electrical demand per householder by 62,81% since 
1995 to 2014 (Chévez et al, 2017). 
The need of acting in the buildings sector by implementing ECMs is primordial. The potential of energy savings 
with moderate refurbishments are between 30-50% (González, 2013). This would release part of the national 
budget dedicated to subsidies, generate a positive energy balance and comply with the Paris Agreement. For 
this target, the public initiative is key in this transition. The Housing Secretary launched a dwelling national plan 
in 2017 addressing the need of delivering houses to cope the room deficit added to sustainable requisites. This 
is included in the “Estandares minimos de calidad para viviendas de interes social” (Standards for minimum 
quality for social housing). These standards have introduced the novelty of making compulsory the compliance 
of IRAM codes in any new social housing project. Additionally, the programme called Semaforo de Vivienda 
Sustentable has been created to provide guidelines on the phases of design, construction and evaluation 
(Secretaria de Vivienda, 2019).

5.2.4. Summary  
In conclusion, university curricula in Argentina shows a very poor integration of nZEB concept. It can be observed 
that bachelor programmes barely contemplate sustainable construction. Study plans in Civil and 
Electromechanical engineering do not address nZEB in their study plans, with the minor exception of UNLP Civil 
Engineering. In the case of UBA, the Architecture programme has two introduction courses concerning nZEB, 
although they do not have a mandatory character. At master’s education level, the presence of nZEB education 
is higher. There are three programmes whose academic curricula includes in some extent EEB. They combine 
nZEB learning with other areas of expertise like Architecture, Urbanism and renewable energy sources. 
Additionally, these study programmes are only imparted in three schools in the entire country. 
 
 
 
 
5.3. Comparison with European academic curricula 
As it is reflected in the previous subchapter, current Argentinian academic scene does not perform suitably 
concerning nZEB teaching. Essentially, there are few examples and it can be stated that programmes and courses 
concerning energy efficiency in buildings do not exist in a broad sense. By comparing with Europe, it can be 
observed an academic gap in the nZEB teaching in Argentina.  
At the level of bachelor, the foregoing European universities provide sustainable construction education with 
two different pedagogy approaches. TUM, Chalmers and UPM imparts some mandatory courses and then, offers 
elective courses or a proper specialization inside the same degree to get deeper sight on the topic. On the other 
hand, DTU has a specific entire bachelor dedicated to energy savings and indoor climate. However, Argentinian 
bachelor curricula have very few courses on nZEB. These courses are not mandatory and have been launched by 
the initiative of single academics. 
At the master’s level, Argentinian academic curricula show a higher nZEB integration. However, those three 
programmes are not fully dedicated to energy efficiency in buildings and do not offer a high insight on this topic.   
Therefore, the comparison between European and Argentinian university programmes brings the opportunity 
to define the gaps in the academic curricula, learn from the European study cases and enhance the present study 
programmes in Argentina. This university system improvement shall contribute to mitigate the lack of 
professionals related to this new generation of buildings in the country of Argentina.

by not forming professionals with the required knowledge about sustainable buildings.  In the previous chapter,

as passive ventilation, heat recovery units, efficient heating systems in cold areas, building envelope 
improvement and sustainable architectural aspects as location, building orientation, windows position, window-
wall rate and shading systems should be present in any rehabilitation and new building project. These measures

5.4.3. Implementation barriers for redesigned academic proposal  
The present Argentinian university system may present resiliencies to academic curricula changes or new study 
plan approvals. According experts consulted in Argentina, there is a barrier in the level of organizational 
management in Argentinian universities. To modify any existing programme or insert any new study plan, any 
school must elaborate its proposal. Later, the CONEAU (National Commission for University Evaluation and 
Accreditation) approves and eventually accredits the new curricula.  This implies a slow bureaucratic process 
and can last more than 3 years; and eventually, it turns out in a long period for starting to generate nZEB 
engineers. Thus, there is the need for inserting proposed changes at the earliest. In the short term, elective 
courses for bachelor’s degree could be created. These courses approval should not be complicated because they 
are optional and not part of the main core of the existing academic curricula. In the medium term, the suggested 
new master’s programme, due to its novelty and no need of restructuring any existing programme, could be 
implemented. In the long term, mandatory courses in the bachelor’s level should come into effect. These courses 
would have to change the existing and rigid engineering programmes of Civil engineering bachelor, what it 
implies a long approval process.

6.Conclusions and further research  
6. Conclusions and further research 
6.1. Main findings 
The objective of this master thesis has been to review the engineering education related to sustainable buildings 
in Europe and to propose changes in existing Argentinian academic curricula programmes. By using examples 
from the European higher education related to energy-efficient buildings, Argentinian universities can introduce 
courses and new programmes in their educational offer to generate engineers capable to deal with nearly Zero 
Energy Building projects. This will contribute to the required transformation of the buildings sector. Through the 
presented foregoing work, several conclusions can be drawn to answer the research questions.  
What is a nearly Zero Energy Building, nZEB? 
The European Union, through the Energy Building Performance Directive EPBD, defines a nearly Zero Energy 
Building as a building that has a very high energy performance. To achieve this goal, an nZEB considers aspects 
of thermal capacity, insulation, passive heating, cooling elements, thermal bridges, heating installation, hot 
water supply, air-conditioning installations, natural and mechanical ventilation which may include air-tightness, 
built-in lighting installation, positioning and orientation of the building, passive solar systems and solar 
protection, indoor climatic conditions and internal loads. 
Which are the skills required for engineers concerning nZEBs? 
This new concept of building implies to building engineers the acquisition of additional skills to deal with energy 
efficiency, indoor climate and environmental issues. These new competencies cover energy building 
performance, standards and building certifications, sustainable architectural design, indoor comfort, envelope 
design, ventilation systems, heating and cooling systems, domestic hot water, lighting and acoustics. 
What are the requirements for energy savings construction in Argentina? 
Most Argentinians live in areas with temperate climate and high solar irradiation levels and have a low 
purchasing power. Hence, the building design must include low-cost strategies to mitigate overheating 
phenomena. The combination of sustainable architectural aspects as location, building orientation, windows 
position, window-wall rate and shading systems with building envelope improvement, passive ventilation 
strategies (vents to allow crossed natural ventilation and night cooling) and heat recovery units in active 
ventilation systems should be considered when nZEB project is being conceived.  On the other hand, EEBs in 
cold areas should integrate efficient heating systems, building envelope improvement and sustainable 
architectural design aspects. 
What are the courses contents in relevant European universities regarding nZEBs? 
Selected programs reflect the integration of nZEB concept in the academic curricula across Europe. Courses 
cover concepts on energy building performance, standards and building certifications, sustainable architectural 
design, indoor comfort, envelope design, ventilation systems, heating and cooling systems, domestic hot water, 
lighting and acoustics. However, it is observed that the depth of teaching depends on the pedagogy strategy and 
programme duration. Theory-based engineering programmes tend to have a larger number of fields reaching a 
lower degree of specialization. This is the case of UPM and in a lower extent, TUM. On the other side, Chalmers 
and specially DTU follow a practice-based pedagogy. Students obtain a deeper knowledge on one single 
discipline. Comparing selected programmes, DTU bachelor programme stands over the rest because of larger 
duration and dedicated study plan. The rest of study plans combines nZEB teaching with other civil engineering 
topics, imparting some mandatory courses and specialization branch in the last year. In the level of master’s 
degree, nZEB is addressed in a higher extent in every university. Once more, DTU offers an exclusive and fully 
focus study programme for this topic. The rest of graduate programmes blends nZEB with other disciplines as

What is the current context of energy building performance and nZEB teaching in Argentina? 
In Argentina, energy efficiency in buildings presents a poor performance and worsening tendency. This situation 
has been caused by highly subsidized energy policies, poor construction quality and low energy efficient 
appliances. By embracing saving energy measures in buildings, the country could decrease the budget dedicated 
to energy subventions, reach a positive energy balance and comply with the Paris Agreement. One of the main 
barriers of nZEB implementation is the lack of academic curricula to generate engineers with knowledge in this 
discipline. Existing study plans in Architecture, Civil engineering and Electromechanical engineering do not 
include sustainable buildings in their study plans, with some minor exceptions. 
Which are the potential gains for the present Argentinian academic curricula? 
Argentine engineering academic scene must be redesigned to integrate nZEB concept. Proposals included in this 
research are based on course contents extracted from European academic curricula. These concepts respond to 
needs and solutions from the selected countries in Europe, which might not relate completely to Argentine 
context. Thus, these proposals must be taken as general examples on how these concepts and competencies 
can contribute to educate engineers in Argentina. At the bachelor’s level, nZEB could be treated like another 
traditional discipline as Structures or Hydraulics. The study plan would incorporate mandatory courses to ensure 
that every student has a minimum knowledge on this field. Later, a new specialization branch with several 
elective courses and final bachelor’s project would be offered. At the master’s level, a new and entire dedicated 
master programme on nZEB could be developed, without the integration of any other discipline. Every skill 
required for nZEB professionals has a specific course.  
For this academic upgrade, the long accreditation process appears as the main implementation barrier. The 
elaboration of internal proposals and the final approval from CONEAU (National Commission for University 
Evaluation and Accreditation) might take more than three years. Then, to proceed with a quicker and more 
effective implementation, mentioned suggestions are scheduled.  In the short term, elective courses for 
bachelor’s degree can be created. This courses’ approval should not be complicated because they are optional 
and not compulsory courses. In the medium term, the suggested new master’s programme, due to its novelty 
and no need of restructuring any existing programme, could be implemented. In the long term, mandatory 
courses in the bachelor’s level should take place. These courses would have to change the existing and rigid 
engineering study structure of Civil engineering, what it implies a long approval process.  
 
 
6.2. Potential further investigations 
Based on this research of the comprehensive topic of European academic curricula review in Europe and 
Argentina, there are opportunities for additional investigations. 
The first recommendation is evaluating how European academic curricula is updated to the latest nZEB research 
findings. The building industry is characterized to embrace any change slowly, and energy efficient buildings is 
another example. Despite of seeming a new idea, the passive house concept has been developed since the 
1970’s decade. The first investigations pooled many aspects to design a sustainable building that, the latest do 
not prioritize. Innovation on construction materials, efficient building systems and simulation computer 
programs have brought the opportunity to change previous nZEB design strategies. New advances on heat 
recovery devices, efficient pumps, insulation products and glazing windows are more energy-efficient measures

The second suggestion is investigating in detail energy saving measures for every zone in Argentina. As 
mentioned in the first recommendation, new construction materials, efficient building systems and simulation 
computer programs have contributed to reshape the EEB conception. These would allow to develop the required 
knowledge to base a more precise and effective academic curricula. Additionally, this research should be aware

Finally, the third recommendation deals with changes in the traditional engineering education pedagogy in 
Argentina. Sustainability introduces a new kind of engineer, capable to understand engineering within society, 
economy and environment. This new horizon brings new abilities to work in multidisciplinary teams and find 
holistic solutions. Traditional engineering teaching is marked by teaching-centred lectures, exam evaluation and 
few electives courses and cannot answer to these needs. However, project-based learning involves students in 
complex real-life problems. Students combine theoretical lectures with group work to analyse and solve a 
problem. This method provides students a first touch to professional life and offers an opportunity to learn 
about themselves.

science, it encompasses all related aspects, such as the origin and 
generation, spreading, influencing and analysis of sound, its 
perception by hearing and its effect on people.
